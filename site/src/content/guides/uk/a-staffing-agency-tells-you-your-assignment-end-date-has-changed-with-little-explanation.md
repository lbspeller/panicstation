# What to do if… a staffing agency tells you your assignment end date has changed with little explanation (UK)

## Short answer
Send a written request for the specific reason for the meeting and a summary of any allegations. If the meeting is a formal disciplinary hearing rather than just an investigation, you have a legal right to see the evidence and the specific charges in writing before the meeting takes place.

## Do not do these things
- Do not speculate or volunteer information about topics that have not been raised.
- Do not admit to any misconduct just to end the tension of the meeting.
- Do not sign any "minutes," "notes," or "witness statements" immediately if you have not read them or if they contain errors.
- Do not record the meeting covertly; this can be grounds for separate disciplinary action.
- Do not delete, move, or alter workplace files, emails, or messages in an attempt to "clean up."
- Do not discuss the matter with colleagues, as this may be viewed as interfering with the investigation.

## What to do now
1. **Send a written request for clarification immediately.** Use email to ensure there is a time-stamped record. State that you want to cooperate fully but need the following information to prepare:
   - the specific nature of the meeting (e.g., is it a "fact-finding investigation" or a "formal disciplinary hearing")
   - a summary of the concerns or allegations being discussed
   - the names of everyone who will be attending
   - any documents or evidence they intend to refer to
   - a copy of the company's disciplinary and investigation policy
2. **Determine the status of the meeting.** If the meeting is described as a "disciplinary hearing" (where a sanction could be issued), you have a legal right to receive the allegations and evidence in writing with "reasonable notice" to prepare.
3. **Verify your right to a companion.**
   - **For a formal disciplinary or grievance hearing:** You have a statutory right to be accompanied by a trade union representative or a work colleague.
   - **For an investigation meeting:** There is no automatic statutory right, but you should check your employment contract as it may grant this right. 
   - **Disability exception:** If you have a disability (including mental health conditions or neurodiversity), you may have a legal right to a companion as a "reasonable adjustment" under the Equality Act.
4. **Request a postponement if notice is insufficient.** If you are given less than 24–48 hours' notice or have not received the evidence needed to respond, formally request that the meeting be rescheduled to allow for "adequate preparation."
5. **Prepare a private timeline of events.** Write down dates, times, and names related to any potential issues you suspect might be raised. Keep this document for your own use; do not hand it over unless you choose to.
6. **State your position clearly at the start of the meeting.** If the meeting begins and the employer still refuses to provide details of the allegation, state the following:
   - "I am happy to cooperate, but I cannot provide a meaningful response to unknown allegations."
   - "I am requesting that we pause this meeting until I have been provided with a written summary and time to review it."
7. **Take your own notes during the session.** Record what is asked and what you answer. If you need a moment to think, ask for a brief adjournment to get a glass of water or use the restroom.
8. **Provide "open" answers for unverified facts.** If you are unsure of a detail, use phrases like "I would need to check my records to be certain" or "I don't recall the specific date." Avoid guessing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to resign or take legal action today.
- You do not need to provide a full written defense statement before the meeting has even happened.
- You do not need to decide on a solicitor or legal representative immediately, as they are generally not permitted to attend internal workplace meetings in the UK.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel anxious when called to an unexplained meeting. Requesting the allegations in writing and asking for reasonable notice are standard procedural steps, not signs of guilt. These requests help ensure the process is transparent and fair for everyone involved.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the initial invitation and meeting. If the process escalates to a formal disciplinary outcome or dismissal, you will need to follow the specific appeal procedures outlined in the Acas Code of Practice.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment rights can vary based on your length of service, your specific contract, and your employment status (worker vs. employee). If you are a member of a trade union, contact them immediately. You can also contact Acas (the Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service) for free, impartial advice on your specific situation.
