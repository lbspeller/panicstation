# What to do if… your boot or trunk release fails and you cannot access urgent items you need (UK)

## Short answer
Park safely, attempt interior access by folding the rear seats, and use the emergency manual release if available. If the items are life-critical medication, contact NHS 111 or 999 immediately rather than attempting mechanical repairs.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to pry the boot open with screwdrivers or crowbars.
- Do not stop on a hard shoulder or live lane to troubleshoot.
- Do not leave children or pets inside a locked vehicle while seeking help.
- Do not repeatedly trigger the electronic release if the battery is weak.
- Do not climb over seats while the vehicle is in motion or on a steep incline.

## What to do now
1. **Move the vehicle to a safe, legal parking location.**  
   **Ensure the handbrake is engaged and hazard lights are active if you are on a public road.** If you are working near traffic, wear a high-visibility jacket and place a warning triangle behind the vehicle as per 2026 Highway Code expectations.

2. **Assess the urgency of the locked items immediately.**  
   **Determine if the contents include time-critical medication, medical equipment, or life-sustaining supplies.** 
   - If someone's health will deteriorate within the hour without the items, call 999. 
   - For urgent but not life-threatening medication needs, call NHS 111 or use the NHS 111 online emergency prescription service.

3. **Check for a physical key entry point.**  
   **Examine the boot lid or tailgate for a traditional key cylinder, which may be hidden under a plastic cap or near the handle.** Use the physical emergency blade found inside your electronic key fob to attempt a manual unlock.

4. **Attempt to access the boot from the vehicle interior.**  
   **Fold down the rear passenger seats to reach into the boot space.** 
   - If the items are within reach, retrieve them and exit the vehicle. 
   - Look for a small plastic cover or a lever on the interior of the boot latch; many UK models have a manual emergency release intended for internal use.

5. **Verify the vehicle's locking status and settings.**  
   **Check if the "valet mode" or "boot lock" switch (often located in the glovebox or on the driver's door) has been accidentally activated.** Ensure all doors are fully unlocked using the central locking button on the dashboard, not just the key fob.

6. **Contact professional assistance if non-destructive methods fail.**  
   **Call your breakdown provider or a certified auto locksmith if the items remain inaccessible.** 
   - You must have your V5C logbook (vehicle registration document) and a government-issued photo ID (driver’s licence or passport) ready to prove ownership.
   - Inform the provider specifically that you have urgent medical or safety items locked inside to potentially prioritise your call-out.

7. **Use emergency services for trapped persons.**  
   **Call 999 immediately if a person or animal is trapped inside the boot or vehicle and cannot be released.** Do not attempt to smash windows unless instructed by emergency operators, as falling glass can cause injury in confined spaces.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the mechanical cause of the failure (e.g., faulty actuator or snapped cable) right now.
- You do not need to attempt to dismantle the interior trim or tailgate panelling.
- You do not need to decide on a permanent repair or replacement parts while at the roadside.

## Important reassurance
Mechanical and electronic latch failures are common and do not usually indicate a total vehicle breakdown. Most modern vehicles have a manual override or interior access path specifically designed for this situation. Professional locksmiths can typically gain access without causing permanent damage to the locking mechanism.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for accessing urgent items. It does not cover mechanical repair, wiring diagnostics, or permanent lock replacement.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace the specific instructions in your vehicle’s owner manual. If you are in a high-risk area or on a motorway, prioritize personal safety over property recovery and contact the police via 101 (non-emergency) or 999 (emergency) as appropriate.
