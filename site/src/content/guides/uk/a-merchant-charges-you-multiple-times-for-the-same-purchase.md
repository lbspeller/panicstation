# What to do if… a merchant charges you multiple times for the same purchase (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the merchant immediately to request a refund for the duplicate transaction; if they refuse or do not respond, contact your bank to initiate a chargeback or a Section 75 claim. Check your banking app to see if the second charge is "pending," as duplicate authorizations often expire and disappear automatically within 7 to 30 days.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a "pending" charge is permanent — these are often temporary holds that the bank will release if the merchant does not claim the funds.
- Do not cancel your card unless you suspect the merchant is fraudulent or your details were stolen — a simple processing error does not require a new card.
- Do not delay action if the extra charge puts you at risk of an overdraft or missed bills.
- Do not ignore the merchant's refund policy — you must usually give them one chance to fix the error before the bank will accept a dispute.
- Do not send original physical receipts through the post — provide clear digital photos or scans instead.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact status of each transaction in your banking app or statement.**
   - Note whether each entry is marked as **pending** (funds reserved but not yet sent) or **completed/posted** (funds have left the account).
   - Record the exact date, time, and unique transaction ID or merchant reference for every duplicate.

2. **Wait up to 7 days if the extra charge is still pending.**
   - Most duplicate authorizations for standard retail purchases expire and drop off your "available balance" within a week.
   - For hotels or car rentals, this window can extend up to 30 days.

3. **Request an immediate refund from the merchant if the duplicate has "posted" or "completed."**
   - Contact the merchant via email or their official help channel so you have a written record.
   - Provide your original order number and screenshots showing the two identical charges.
   - Ask for a "refund of a duplicate transaction" and request a timeframe for the funds to return to your account.

4. **Initiate a Card Dispute (Chargeback) if the merchant does not resolve the issue within 15 days.**
   - Contact your bank or card issuer and state you wish to "dispute a duplicate transaction."
   - Provide proof that you attempted to contact the merchant first.
   - **Note for Mastercard users:** There is generally a minimum claim limit of £10 for chargebacks.
   - Most chargeback claims must be started within 120 days of the transaction.

5. **Submit a Section 75 claim if the purchase cost more than £100 and you used a credit card.**
   - If the merchant has gone bust or is being obstructive, use Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974.
   - This makes the credit provider legally "jointly and severally liable" for the error.
   - This applies even if you only paid a small deposit for the item on the credit card.

6. **Request an "Immediate Refund" under the Direct Debit Guarantee for non-card payments.**
   - If the money was taken via Direct Debit (not a card payment), tell your bank there has been an "error" or "duplicate collection."
   - Under the Guarantee, the bank must refund you the full amount immediately into your account.

7. **Escalate to a formal complaint if the bank refuses to help.**
   - If the bank rejects your dispute, ask for a "Final Response Letter."
   - For payment service errors (like duplicates), banks must usually respond to formal complaints within 15 working days.
   - If unresolved, take the case to the **Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS)**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to switch banks or merchants right now.
- You do not need to file a police report (Action Fraud) unless you believe the merchant is a "scam" site rather than a legitimate business with a technical error.
- You can wait to post public reviews until you have given the merchant a reasonable window (3–5 days) to respond to your private refund request.

## Important reassurance
Duplicate charges are one of the most common technical glitches in the UK payment system, often caused by a brief loss of signal at a card terminal or a page refresh during an online checkout. UK consumer protections, including the Direct Debit Guarantee and the Chargeback scheme, are specifically designed to reverse these errors. In the vast majority of cases, the money is recovered in full.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and the first steps for reclaiming funds from UK-based merchants or via UK-issued cards. It does not cover long-term credit score repair or complex disputes involving overseas merchants with no UK presence.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Individual bank policies and card scheme rules (Visa, Mastercard, Amex) may vary slightly. Ensure you maintain a log of all communications with the merchant and your bank.
