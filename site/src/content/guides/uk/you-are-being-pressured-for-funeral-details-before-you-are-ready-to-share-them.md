# What to do if… you are being pressured for funeral details before you are ready to share them (UK)

## Short answer
Use one calm holding reply: “Thank you for your message. I’m not ready to share funeral details yet. We’ll update people when we can.” Then reduce the noise by using one update route, or one trusted person, instead of replying to everyone separately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel you have to answer every message individually or immediately.
- Do not share the date, time, place, livestream link, or donation link widely just to stop people asking.
- Do not explain the death, family decisions, or funeral plans by text if it is draining you.
- Do not click unknown links, send money, or confirm personal details in unexpected messages about the funeral.
- Do not let arguments about who should be told happen late at night, while travelling, or straight after a difficult call.
- Do not make a public funeral notice or social media post before you are ready.

## What to do now
1. Send one holding message and stop there.  
   “Thank you for your message. I’m not ready to share funeral details yet. If there is an update, we’ll let people know when we can.”  
   You do not need to personalise it.

2. Choose one update route for later.  
   Use one group chat, one broadcast message, one email, or one trusted person. When new requests come in, reply: “Please wait for updates through that route.”

3. Ask someone to be a message buffer for the next day or two.  
   Send them: “Can you handle questions about funeral details for 48 hours? I’m not ready to reply.” They can answer from their own phone or tell you only about anything urgent.

4. Set a temporary privacy rule for today.  
   For example: “No date, time, place, livestream link, or donation link is being shared until we have agreed it.” This can be changed later.

5. If you do share details with a small group, say they are not for forwarding.  
   Use plain wording such as: “Please do not pass these details on or post them online. We are keeping this private for now.”

6. Protect against funeral-related scams.  
   If a message asks for urgent payment, bank details, a paid livestream link, or personal information, do not reply in that thread. Check using paperwork you already have, a phone number you already know, or the funeral director’s official website found independently.

7. Reduce incoming messages for a set period.  
   Put your phone on Do Not Disturb, allow only key contacts through, and choose one or two times to check messages. You are allowed to be unavailable.

8. Keep official admin separate from social updates.  
   If the person who died was living in England, Scotland or Wales, Tell Us Once can usually be used after the death is registered, or after a coroner’s interim or final certificate, to tell most government organisations in one go. It is not available if the person was living in Northern Ireland, where nidirect guidance explains who may need to be told.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today who should receive funeral details first.
- You do not have to write a full announcement now.
- You do not have to reply to every condolence message now, or at all.
- You do not have to publish an obituary, funeral notice, or social media post to be doing things properly.
- You do not have to settle disagreements about arrangements by text.

## Important reassurance
Feeling overwhelmed by messages, even kind ones, is common after a death. Saying “I’m not ready to share details yet” is a reasonable boundary, not a failure or an insult.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for handling requests for funeral details when you need time and privacy. Later decisions about family disagreements, funeral arrangements, estate issues, or official duties may need help from a funeral director, registrar, solicitor, bereavement service, or another trusted support person.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel unable to cope or you are in a mental health crisis, use NHS urgent mental health help. If you are in immediate danger, call 999; if messages are threatening or persistent, contact the police.
