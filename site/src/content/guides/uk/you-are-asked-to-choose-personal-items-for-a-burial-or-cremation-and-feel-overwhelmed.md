# What to do if… you are asked to choose personal items for a burial or cremation and feel overwhelmed (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before anything is placed in the coffin. Ask the funeral director or crematorium what is allowed, then choose none, one, or a very small number of items you would be at peace with not getting back.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t try to choose the “perfect” items or represent everyone’s feelings at once.
- Don’t put in valuables, jewellery, letters, photos, soft toys, clothing, glass, plastic, PVC items, electronics, batteries, or pressurised items without asking whether they are allowed.
- Don’t place anything irreplaceable in the coffin unless you understand that it may not be recoverable after burial or cremation.
- Don’t agree because someone is pressuring you in the moment; ask for the exact deadline and a short pause.
- Don’t turn this into a full family meeting if that is making the decision harder.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for a short pause and the exact deadline.** Say: “I’m overwhelmed. Please tell me the last time items can be added or removed, and who I confirm this with.”
2. **Confirm whether it is burial or cremation.** Say: “Is this for burial or cremation, and are there different restrictions for the items?”
3. **Ask the direct safety question before choosing.** Say: “Please tell me what cannot go in the coffin for this crematorium, cemetery, or burial ground.”
4. **Use a small decision rule.** Choose no items, one item, or up to three small items only if each one has been confirmed as acceptable.
5. **If you cannot think, ask about simple low-risk options.** Say: “Would a single paper letter, a printed photo, or a small natural-fibre item be allowed here?” Use only what they confirm.
6. **Separate valuables from memory.** If jewellery, watches, medals, or other valuables are involved, ask: “Can this be removed and returned to the family before the final handover, burial, or cremation?”
7. **If family members disagree, split the choice into two groups.**
   - Items that go in the coffin, only if confirmed acceptable.
   - Items that stay with the family, can be displayed at the service, or can be kept for later.
8. **Write down the final decision.** Note the exact items, who confirmed they were allowed, and the deadline. This helps stop second-guessing and avoids confusion later.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain what the items mean to everyone right now.
- You do not need to choose something for every family member today.
- You do not need to settle wider family disagreements before making the immediate item decision.
- You do not need to decide future memorials, ashes choices, keepsakes, or rituals in this moment.

## Important reassurance
Feeling unable to choose is a normal response to grief and shock. Choosing nothing, or choosing one simple item that is allowed, can still be a careful and loving decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce pressure and prevent irreversible mistakes in the next hours or day. Later decisions about memorials, keepsakes, ashes, family disagreements, or estate property may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, funeral-directing, or other professional advice. Rules and practical deadlines vary by crematorium, cemetery, burial ground, funeral director, and where in the UK the service takes place, so confirm directly with the person arranging the service.
