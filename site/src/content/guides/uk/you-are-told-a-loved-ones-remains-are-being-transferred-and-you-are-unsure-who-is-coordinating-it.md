# What to do if… you are told a loved one’s remains are being transferred and you are unsure who is coordinating it (UK)

## Short answer
Before agreeing to anything, contact the current custodian using a trusted main number and ask them to confirm who has the body, who is authorised to give instructions, who is collecting, and where the remains are going.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree to a transfer if you do not know where the remains are going, who is collecting, and who has authorised release or collection.
- Don’t rely on “someone will call you” without getting a named person, direct number, and case or reference number where one exists.
- Don’t share passport scans, bank details, card details, or make urgent payments to an unknown caller.
- Don’t assume the hospital, mortuary, coroner, and funeral director are interchangeable; each may have a different role.
- Don’t let several relatives give separate instructions to different offices if you can avoid it.
- Don’t finalise funeral arrangements if the death is with a coroner, or with the procurator fiscal in Scotland, until you have been told the body can be released.

## What to do now
1. Make a quick fact sheet and call log. Write the person’s full name, date of birth, date and place of death, your relationship to them, and every call time, name, role, number, and answer you are given.

2. Confirm who currently has physical custody. Ask: “Are they in a hospital mortuary, public mortuary, coroner’s mortuary, funeral director’s care, or somewhere else?” If you are unsure, call the hospital main switchboard, care home, police contact, local coroner contact, Northern Ireland Coroners Service, or procurator fiscal contact in Scotland using a number from an official website or paperwork you already trust.

3. Ask the current custodian these exact questions:
   - “Who is recorded as the family contact or person giving instructions?”
   - “Has a funeral director or undertaker been named to collect? If yes, which one?”
   - “What transfer is planned, from where to where, and who authorised it?”
   - “Can you record that there is uncertainty and that I am asking for no release to an unconfirmed person or destination?”

4. If a coroner, coroner’s officer, or procurator fiscal contact is involved, ask what stage the case is at. Say: “Has the body been released for funeral arrangements yet? If not, what is still pending, and who will tell us when release is allowed?”

5. Nominate one temporary family coordinator for today. Tell the custodian: “Please record that updates should go through [name] on [phone number] while we confirm the arrangements.”

6. If a funeral director has already been chosen, verify it directly. Call the funeral director using a number from their official website or paperwork, ask whether they have been instructed to collect, and ask them to confirm the destination and the person who gave instructions.

7. If no funeral director has been chosen, say this clearly to the custodian: “No funeral director has been appointed yet. Please do not release to a funeral director or move the body for funeral arrangements until we confirm the named firm and destination.”

8. If someone says transport is already booked, do not argue with the caller. Ask for the organisation name, collector name, destination, and reference, then independently call the current custodian and ask whether that arrangement is recorded and authorised.

9. If relatives disagree about who can decide, slow the transfer down. Tell the custodian: “There is a family dispute about who is authorised. Please record this and tell us what documentation you need before any release.”

## What can wait
- You usually do not need to decide the service details, coffin or urn choice, flowers, notices, music, readings, or catering right now.
- You usually do not need to use Tell Us Once, contact banks, contact pension providers, or deal with estate paperwork before you have stabilised who is coordinating release and collection.
- You do not need to resolve every family disagreement today; the immediate task is to prevent an unverified transfer and create one contact point.

## Important reassurance
This kind of confusion can happen when hospitals, mortuaries, coroners, procurators fiscal, funeral directors, and family members are all involved. Asking for names, roles, a reference number, the collection authority, and the destination is normal and reasonable.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to establish custody, authorisation, and who is coordinating any transfer. Later decisions about registration, funeral planning, paying for a funeral, disputes, or the estate may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information for the UK, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Local procedures vary, especially where a coroner or the procurator fiscal is involved, so ask the current custodian what applies in your situation.
