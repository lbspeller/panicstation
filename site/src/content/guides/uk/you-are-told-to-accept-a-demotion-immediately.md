# What to do if… you are told to accept a demotion immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Do not agree or sign on the spot. Ask for the proposed demotion in writing and ask for time to consider it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign anything, including an “acknowledgement”, if you are not sure what it changes.
- Do not say “I accept” verbally just to end the meeting.
- Do not resign on the spot.
- Do not rely on “it’s temporary” promises unless the temporary terms are written down.
- Do not send angry messages or post about it publicly; keep communications factual.
- Do not secretly record meetings unless you have checked your workplace rules and got advice.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the full proposal in writing before you respond.** Ask them to set out:
   - the new job title, reporting line, duties, location, and working pattern
   - the new pay, benefits, bonus, commission, or grade impact
   - the proposed start date
   - whether the demotion is temporary or permanent
   - the reason for the change
   - whether it is linked to performance, disciplinary action, a grievance, redundancy, or reorganisation
   - what they say will happen if you do not accept

2. **Say clearly that you are not accepting immediately.** You can say:
   - “I’m not agreeing today. Please send the full terms in writing and I’ll respond in writing after I’ve reviewed them.”

3. **Create a same-day written record.** Email a brief recap saying:
   - you were asked to accept a demotion immediately
   - you have asked for the proposed terms in writing
   - you need time to consider the proposal
   - you are not agreeing to any change yet

4. **Collect the documents that matter.** Put these in one place:
   - your contract and any later contract changes
   - your current job description, grade, title, and pay information
   - any handbook or policy referred to in your contract
   - any disciplinary, grievance, restructure, or performance documents
   - any collective agreement if one applies to your workplace
   - any flexibility or variation clause your employer may rely on

5. **Ask what process you are in.**
   - If it is a disciplinary hearing, ask for the allegations, evidence, possible outcomes, and your right to be accompanied by a trade union representative or work colleague.
   - If it is a grievance meeting about a legal or contractual duty, you may have the right to be accompanied; ask for this in writing.
   - If it is an appeal hearing, ask for the appeal process and your right to be accompanied.
   - If it is an investigatory meeting, you may not have a legal right to be accompanied, but you can still ask for a companion, written notes, and a pause to get advice.

6. **If they call the demotion a disciplinary outcome, ask for the formal outcome and appeal route.**
   - Ask for the decision, reasons, evidence relied on, sanction, and appeal deadline in writing.
   - If you disagree, send a short written appeal within the deadline they give, even if you add more detail later.

7. **If they try to impose the change anyway, object in writing.**
   - Write that you do not agree to the demotion or any pay cut and want the issue resolved.
   - If you keep working while disputing it, say you are working under protest for a short time while you get advice or try to resolve it.
   - Repeat your objection regularly while the imposed change continues, especially when duties or pay are affected.

8. **Check your next payslip and written pay information.**
   - If pay drops or deductions appear, raise it in writing with HR or payroll immediately.
   - Keep copies of payslips, pay notices, emails, letters, and meeting notes.

9. **Use early support if you can.**
   - If you are in a union, contact your rep and ask them to attend future meetings where allowed.
   - If you are not in a union, contact Acas for guidance on contract changes, disciplinary process, and next steps.
   - If you are in Northern Ireland, check the Labour Relations Agency route through GOV.UK or nidirect.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you accept the demotion long-term.
- You do not need to resign, threaten legal action, or write a long rebuttal tonight.
- You do not need to negotiate the best deal right now.
- You do not need to prove the whole history immediately; first get the terms, dates, reasons, and process clear in writing.

## Important reassurance
Being pushed to decide instantly can make anyone freeze. Asking for written terms, not agreeing on the spot, and making a calm record are protective first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to slow the situation down and preserve options. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if pay is reduced, a disciplinary sanction is imposed, a contract change is forced, or you are considering resignation or a legal claim.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Employment rights depend on your contract, workplace policies, union agreements, employment status, location within the UK, and the facts. For personalised help, consider Acas, the Labour Relations Agency in Northern Ireland, a trade union representative, Citizens Advice, or a qualified employment adviser.
