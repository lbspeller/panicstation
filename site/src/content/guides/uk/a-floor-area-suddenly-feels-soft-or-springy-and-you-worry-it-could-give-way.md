# What to do if… a floor area suddenly feels soft or springy and you worry it could give way (UK)

## Short answer
Stop using that part of the floor immediately, keep people out of the room, and do not add weight to it. If the floor is dropping, cracking, or could collapse right now, leave the area and call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t test it by bouncing, stamping, jumping, or adding weight.
- Don’t move heavy items such as beds, wardrobes, appliances, or boxes across the suspect area.
- Keep children and pets out of the room.
- Don’t pull up boards, cut holes, or lift carpet over the weak area unless a competent professional tells you it is safe.
- Don’t go into the room underneath the suspect area to inspect the ceiling.
- Don’t ignore new warning signs such as loud cracking, a worsening sag, tilting furniture, spreading cracks, or doors suddenly sticking nearby.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the load.** Ask everyone to step back the way they came and stop walking on the soft or springy patch.
2. **Make a no-go zone.** Close the door, put a clear note on it, and block access if you can do that without stepping on the suspect floor. If the area is in a corridor, route people another way.
3. **Leave and call 999 if there may be immediate danger.** Treat it as immediate danger if the floor is visibly dropping, there are loud cracking sounds, furniture is tilting, cracks are spreading, or the give is sudden and severe.
4. **Warn anyone below or next to the area if you can do it safely.** In a flat, maisonette, or shared building, phone or message the neighbour below or the building manager and ask them to avoid the area directly underneath. Do not go underneath the weak area yourself.
5. **Keep nearby vibration and weight down.** Do not run nearby, move furniture nearby, or use the room directly below the suspect area until it has been checked.
6. **If water may be involved, stop adding water only if it is safe.** Do not use the bath, shower, sink, washing machine, or dishwasher near that area. If you can reach the stopcock without crossing the suspect floor, consider turning off the water. If water is near electrics, do not touch affected switches or sockets; leave the area and ask for urgent help.
7. **Report it through the right make-safe route.**
   - In England and Wales, you can report a dangerous building or structure to your local council.
   - In Scotland, contact your local authority’s Building Standards or emergency out-of-hours route if available.
   - In Northern Ireland, contact your local district council or Building Control route.
   - Anywhere in the UK, call 999 if there is immediate risk to life or injury.
8. **Use the owner or manager channel as urgent.**
   - If you rent, report it to your landlord, letting agent, council landlord, or housing association as urgent and ask what they want you to avoid until it is inspected.
   - If you own a flat or the issue may affect other residents, contact the managing agent, freeholder, factor, or residents’ management company.
   - If you own the whole property, contact your insurer or an urgent building professional route and say the floor may be unsafe to walk on.
9. **Ask for an urgent safety assessment.** Ask for the floor structure to be checked by a competent building professional, such as a structural engineer or suitably qualified contractor, and ask whether temporary propping, exclusion, or other make-safe action is needed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the cause right now, such as rot, a leak, insect damage, failed joists, or a support problem.
- You do not need to decide on repairs, quotes, contractors, decoration, or costs today.
- You do not need perfect evidence. A few clear photos and notes from a safe place are enough for now.
- You do not need to argue about responsibility before the area has been made safe.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to treat a suddenly soft or springy floor as a safety issue, even if nothing has collapsed. Keeping people away, avoiding extra load, and asking for urgent make-safe help is a calm and proportionate first response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: avoid injury, avoid making the floor worse, and get the right people involved. Later decisions about repairs, liability, insurance, tenancy rights, or service charges may need specialist advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not a professional inspection, legal advice, financial advice, or emergency-service assessment. If you think anyone is at immediate risk, move to a safer place and contact emergency services.
