# What to do if… a lab or clinic travel certificate has the wrong name or date of birth (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the issuing lab or clinic immediately and ask for a corrected reissued certificate that matches your passport before you travel. Do not alter the certificate yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not edit the certificate yourself, even for a small spelling or date correction.
- Do not ask a friend, print shop, or online forum to alter the file.
- Do not assume airline staff, check-in staff, or border officials will ignore a name or date of birth mismatch if the certificate is required.
- Do not rely on handwritten corrections or crossed-out text unless the issuer confirms in writing that this is their valid correction process.
- Do not cancel travel in a rush before you have tried the issuer’s fastest correction or replacement route.
- Do not post the certificate online or send it to strangers for advice; it contains sensitive personal data.

## What to do now
1. **Compare the certificate with your passport.** Check the exact spelling of your given names, surname, date of birth, passport number if shown, and any booking name that the airline or travel provider will check.
2. **Write down the exact mismatch.** For example: missing middle name, surname spelling error, wrong year of birth, or day and month swapped.
3. **Contact the issuer by the fastest route.** Phone first if possible, then follow up in writing. Say that the certificate is for travel, the identity details are wrong, and you need a corrected reissued certificate that matches your passport.
4. **Ask for a full reissue, not an informal edit.** Ask the lab or clinic to regenerate the certificate through its usual system so any QR code, reference number, portal check, signature, stamp, or verification link still works.
5. **Give the issuer the correct details securely.** Use the clinic’s official portal, upload link, or a verified email address from your booking confirmation or the clinic’s website. Send only what they need, usually your passport photo page and booking reference if relevant.
6. **Check the corrected certificate before you leave the call or email thread.** Confirm the spelling, date of birth, certificate date, test or vaccination details, QR code or reference number, and PDF download all match what you need.
7. **If the issuer says the error comes from an NHS record or app-linked record, ask who holds that record.** Contact the NHS service, GP surgery, or clinic that holds the factual identity details and ask how to correct the inaccurate name or date of birth.
8. **If a corrected reissue is not possible in time, ask for the quickest valid replacement route.** This may mean a repeat test, repeat assessment, or a new certificate with the correct identity details entered at booking and checked at arrival.
9. **If the document is a yellow fever International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis, ask a currently active Yellow Fever Vaccination Centre for advice.** A replacement may be possible where there is an accurate record of the previous vaccination, but reissue is at the centre’s discretion.
10. **Keep a clear audit trail.** Save the original certificate, your correction request, any written replies, the corrected certificate, and a screenshot of any verification page if the certificate has one.
11. **If the issuer refuses to correct an obvious factual error, escalate calmly.** Ask for the clinic manager, records or administration lead, and the formal process for correcting inaccurate personal data.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a formal complaint.
- You do not need to work out compensation, insurance, or refund arguments while you are still trying to get the document corrected.
- You do not need to prove whose fault the error was; focus on getting a certificate that matches your passport and can be checked.
- You do not need to contact border agencies unless your airline, travel provider, or official destination guidance specifically tells you to.

## Important reassurance
A wrong name or date of birth on a travel certificate is frightening when travel is close, but it is often fixable if the issuer can reissue it quickly. The safest immediate aim is simple: get an issuer-generated corrected certificate that matches your passport and still verifies.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and reduce the chance of being refused travel because of a document mismatch. Later decisions about complaints, missed travel, insurance, refunds, or data rights may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, travel, or professional advice. Certificate acceptance can vary by destination, carrier, document type, and verification system, so check the relevant travel rules and prioritise an official corrected reissue from the issuer.
