# What to do if… you experience a pregnancy loss or newborn death and officials are asking for decisions (UK)

## Short answer
Ask for a named bereavement contact, such as a bereavement midwife, nurse, doctor, or hospital bereavement office, before you answer detailed questions or sign forms. It is reasonable to say: “I am in shock. Please slow down, write down what is needed, and tell me what can wait.”

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign consent forms for a post-mortem, tests, tissue retention, release arrangements, information sharing, or funeral arrangements until someone has explained what is optional, what is required, and what can wait.
- Do not agree to give a detailed account immediately if you are in shock; ask for a pause and for a trusted person to be present.
- Do not assume you must decide immediately about seeing, holding, photographs, keepsakes, post-mortem, funeral arrangements, or registration; ask what can be delayed and for how long.
- Do not let clothing, blankets, ID bands, cot cards, scan pictures, or other baby items be discarded without asking staff to store them safely while you decide.
- Do not try to manage repeated conversations alone; ask for one lead contact and one written plan.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for one named point of contact and a private space.** Say: “I need the bereavement midwife, nurse, doctor, or bereavement office now, and somewhere private. Please pause detailed questions until they are here.”

2. **Ask each person to identify their role and the reason for the question.** Say: “What is your name and role? What decision are you asking for today? What happens if I do not decide today?”

3. **Ask whether any official review or investigation is involved.** Say: “Is this being referred to a coroner, or in Scotland the Procurator Fiscal? What changes while that decision is being made?” If you are in England or Wales and the death is not being investigated by a coroner, you can also ask whether a Medical Examiner’s office will contact you about the cause of death and any concerns you may have.

4. **Ask for a written list before you choose anything.** Request: “Please write down the options, what is mandatory, what is optional, and the timeframes. I cannot process this verbally right now.”

5. **If post-mortem, examinations, or tests are mentioned, slow the conversation down.** Ask:
   - “Is this being directed by a coroner or Procurator Fiscal, or is it optional and based on consent?”
   - “What exactly would be done, and what would not be done?”
   - “Who will explain the results, and how can we ask questions later?”
   - “Can we have time to think before signing anything?”

6. **Ask staff to preserve choices about seeing your baby and keepsakes.** If you are unsure, say: “Please tell me what options exist, such as seeing or holding, photographs, handprints, footprints, ID bands, cot cards, clothing, or a blanket. Please store anything safely so I can decide later.”

7. **Ask what paperwork applies, without trying to complete it all now.**
   - For a newborn death, ask what the hospital or doctor will provide and when you can register the death.
   - For a stillbirth from 24 weeks, ask what document or evidence the hospital or midwife will provide and how to contact the register office. Ask the bereavement contact or register office for the deadline in your part of the UK.
   - For an earlier pregnancy loss, ask what records or written confirmation the hospital can provide. In England, if the loss was before 24 weeks, you may be able to request an optional baby loss certificate later.

8. **Ask how your baby will be cared for and identified.** Say: “Please explain where my baby will be kept, how they will be identified, who can access them, and how any transfer or release works when we are ready.”

9. **Bring in a trusted person for conversations and forms.** Ask staff to phone the person you choose and to let them be present, on speakerphone if needed.

10. **Write down three anchors before answering anything else.** Record the lead contact’s name and number, the decisions being asked today, and what can wait until tomorrow or another agreed day.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a funeral director, burial, cremation, service details, music, wording, or announcements in this moment.
- You do not need to decide now what to tell extended family, friends, employers, school, or social media.
- You do not need to decide immediately about keepsakes; ask staff to preserve options safely if you are unsure.
- You do not need to understand every form at once; ask who requires it, why it is needed, what happens if it waits, and whether your bereavement contact can go through it with you later.
- You do not need to make complaints, inquest decisions, benefits decisions, workplace decisions, or longer-term support decisions while you are still trying to get through the first conversations.

## Important reassurance
Being unable to answer clearly, forgetting details, crying, shaking, feeling numb, or feeling blank can happen after a severe shock. It is reasonable to ask people to slow down, repeat themselves, write things down, and let someone you trust sit with you.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment hospital staff, registrars, medical examiner staff, coroners’ officers, the Procurator Fiscal process, or other officials start asking what happens next. Later decisions about registration, post-mortem, funeral arrangements, inquests or investigations, complaints, benefits, leave from work, and specialist bereavement support can be handled after you have a named contact and a clearer written plan.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes vary across the UK and may change depending on whether a coroner, or in Scotland the Procurator Fiscal, is involved, and whether a Medical Examiner review applies in England or Wales. Ask what is mandatory, what is optional, what can be delayed, and who your named contact is for follow-up.
