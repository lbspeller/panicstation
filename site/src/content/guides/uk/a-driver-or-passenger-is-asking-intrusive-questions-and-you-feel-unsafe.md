# What to do if… a driver or passenger is asking intrusive questions and you feel unsafe (UK)

## Short answer
Stop giving personal information and move into safety mode: contact someone you trust, share your location or trip if you can, and end the ride at a busy, well-lit public place if your unease increases.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t share your exact home address, whether you live alone, your workplace, your routine, or where you are going after this.
- Don’t hand over your phone, unlock it for them, or let them add themselves on social media.
- Don’t agree to detours, a quick stop, or changing the drop-off to somewhere quieter.
- Don’t argue, insult, or try to prove a point if you sense escalation; aim for a calm exit, not a confrontation.
- Don’t get out somewhere isolated just to end the conversation; prioritise a public, staffed place.

## What to do now
1. **Give one plain boundary, then stop feeding the questions.**  
   Use a short line like: “I don’t share personal details.” After that, use boring answers such as “not sure”, “can’t talk right now”, or “I need to make a call”.

2. **Create immediate accountability.**  
   Call or message someone you trust if it is safe to do so. Say or write: “I’m in a car heading to [general area]. I’ll message when I’m out.” Share your live location or trip status if you can do it without drawing attention.

3. **Change the end point before you reach your door.**  
   Ask to be dropped at a busy, well-lit public place such as a petrol station, supermarket, hotel lobby, train station, or staffed transport hub. A simple line is enough: “Please drop me here.”

4. **Use app safety tools if this is a booked ride or private hire trip.**  
   Open the app and use trip-sharing, trusted contact, emergency help, or support tools if available. Keep communication inside the app rather than giving your personal phone number.

5. **If this is happening on the rail network and it is not an emergency, text British Transport Police on 61016.**  
   Include what is happening, where you are, and the time. If someone is in immediate danger, call 999 instead.

6. **If you think you are in immediate danger, call 999.**  
   If you cannot speak, stay on the line and follow the operator’s instructions. From a mobile, you may be prompted to press 55 or tap to be put through to police call handlers. If you do not respond, the call could be ended.

7. **Once you are out, do not go straight to a secluded final destination.**  
   Go into a staffed place for a few minutes. From there, arrange a safer next step, such as another ride, a lift from someone you trust, or a route with staff and other people nearby.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to complain, report formally, or prove what happened.
- You can collect details later, such as screenshots of trip information, the vehicle registration, the time, the location, and a short note of what was said.
- You do not need to decide whether you overreacted. If the questions felt intrusive and escalating, getting to a safer place is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling alarmed by intrusive, escalating questions is a normal safety response. You are allowed to be brief, awkward, repetitive, or quiet if it helps you get to a safer place; your priority is to exit safely, not to manage someone else’s feelings.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on safely ending the interaction and buying time. If the person has your details, keeps contacting you, follows you, or you remain worried, later decisions may need specialist help from the police, transport staff, the ride app, or taxi and private hire licensing support.

## Important note
This is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, prioritise getting to a safer place and contacting emergency services.
