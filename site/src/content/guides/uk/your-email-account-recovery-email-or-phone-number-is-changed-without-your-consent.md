# What to do if… your email account recovery email or phone number is changed without your consent (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all outbound sending immediately and sign in to your account through the provider's official, manually-typed website to change your password and revoke any unauthorized forwarding rules or apps.

## Do not do these things
- Don't click any "Verify" or "Unblock" links contained within the warning email itself.
- Don't attempt to "test" the block by sending multiple emails to yourself or others.
- Don't ignore the warning if you still have access; outgoing blocks are often the first sign of a silent account takeover.
- Don't use the same "compromised" password for any other service, especially banking or social media.
- Don't assume the block is a technical glitch without checking your "Sent" folder for messages you did not write.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the account and sign in securely.**  
   **Close all mail apps on your phone and computer to stop automated syncing.** Open a fresh browser window and type your email provider's address (e.g., outlook.com, gmail.com) directly into the address bar. Do not use search engine results or links from the alert.
2. **Update your credentials and force a logout.**  
   **Change your password immediately to a strong, unique phrase consisting of three random words.** Once changed, select the option to "Sign out of all other sessions" or "Forget all devices" to kick any active intruders out of your account.
3. **Audit for "Persistence" in your mailbox settings.**  
   **Check your account settings specifically for "Rules," "Filters," or "Forwarding."**  
   - Delete any rules that move incoming mail to "Archive," "Junk," or "Deleted Items" automatically.
   - Remove any forwarding addresses that you did not personally authorize.
   - Check the "Auto-reply" or "Out of Office" section for phishing links added by attackers.
4. **Revoke connected apps and API access.**  
   **Go to the "Security" or "Permissions" tab and review "Third-party apps with account access."** Remove any apps you do not recognise or no longer use. Delete any "App Passwords" that were generated for older devices, as these can bypass two-step verification.
5. **Enable or reset Two-Step Verification (2SV).**  
   **Turn on 2SV if it was off, or reset the "trusted devices" if it was already on.** If an attacker had access, they may have added their own device as "trusted."
6. **Clear browser data to remove session tokens.**  
   **Clear your browser’s cache and cookies completely.** This prevents "Session Hijacking," where an attacker uses a stolen "token" to stay logged in even after a password change.
7. **Report the incident to the authorities.**  
   **Report the suspicious activity to the NCSC via their official channels.**
   - Forward the warning email (if you suspect it is phishing) to report@phishing.gov.uk.
   - If you received a suspicious text message, forward it to 7726.
8. **File a formal report if data or money was lost.**  
   **In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use the "Report Fraud" service (formerly Action Fraud) online or by calling 0300 123 2040.** In Scotland, report the cybercrime to Police Scotland by calling 101.
9. **Notify your contacts using a separate method.**  
   **Send a brief message via text or a different account to frequent contacts.** State: "My email was recently compromised; please ignore and delete any unusual links or attachments sent from my address in the last 24 hours."

## What can wait
- Changing passwords for every single unrelated website (focus only on your email and banking for the first hour).
- Running a full "deep" virus scan (a quick scan is sufficient while you secure the account itself).
- Formatting or "wiping" your devices (unless a scan confirms active malware).

## Important reassurance
Email providers use automated "rate-limiting" and "unusual activity" blocks to protect you. Receiving this warning often means the provider's security system successfully intervened before significant damage occurred. Most blocks are temporary and are lifted once you demonstrate control by completing the provider's official security check.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an individual's personal email account. If this involves a corporate or government email system, you must follow your organization’s internal IT security reporting protocol immediately after changing your password.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional cybersecurity advice. If you have lost significant funds or sensitive corporate data, you should consult with a professional security specialist or your legal representative. Always prioritize instructions provided by your official email service provider during their recovery workflow.
