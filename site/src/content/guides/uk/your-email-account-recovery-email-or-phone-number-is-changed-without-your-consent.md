# What to do if… your email account recovery email or phone number is changed without your consent (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as possible account takeover. Use the email provider’s official recovery route from a device you trust, then secure the account by changing the password, signing out other sessions, restoring your recovery details, and turning on 2-step verification.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use links in “security alert” emails or texts to sign in; type the provider’s address yourself or use the official app.
- Don’t keep guessing passwords or codes if recovery is failing; repeated failed attempts can make some recovery flows harder.
- Don’t contact unknown recovery emails or phone numbers shown on the account.
- Don’t pay anyone in direct messages, adverts, WhatsApp, Telegram, or search results who claims they can recover the account.
- Don’t assume it is fixed after one password change; attackers may have added forwarding, mailbox rules, connected apps, or extra sessions.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer setup.** Use a device you trust and keep updated. If you think your phone number or phone may be compromised, use a trusted computer instead.
2. **Start with the provider’s official recovery route.** Use the provider’s “recover account”, “hacked account”, or “can’t sign in” help page. If you can still sign in, go straight to the account’s security settings and check what changed.
3. **If you get back in, secure the account in this order.**
   1. Change the password to a strong, unique password you have not used anywhere else.
   2. Choose the option to sign out of other devices or sessions, if the provider offers it.
   3. Remove any recovery email address or phone number you do not control.
   4. Add back only recovery details you control.
   5. Turn on 2-step verification and save any backup or recovery codes somewhere offline.
4. **Check inside the mailbox for ways the attacker could stay hidden.**
   - Look for forwarding addresses you did not set.
   - Check filters or rules that auto-forward, auto-delete, archive, or hide messages.
   - Check delegated access, shared mailbox access, and connected apps if your provider offers these settings.
   - Review recent security activity and remove unknown devices, sessions, or app access.
5. **Protect the accounts that depend on this email for password resets.** Start with banking, payment apps, government services, shopping accounts, your mobile provider, cloud storage, and social media. For each one, check that the recovery email and phone number still belong to you, then change any reused or suspicious password.
6. **If codes are arriving by SMS and you do not trust your phone number, contact your mobile network.** Use the number on your bill, SIM packaging, or the network’s official website. Ask them to check for a SIM swap, number port, new SIM order, or unauthorised account changes, and ask what extra account security they can add.
7. **If money was lost or personal information was used, report it.** In England, Wales and Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud. In Scotland, report to Police Scotland by calling 101 for non-emergency crime reporting.
8. **If a suspicious email may have led to this, report the email.** Forward it to report@phishing.gov.uk if you can do so safely. Do not click links in the suspicious message.

## What can wait
- You do not need to know exactly how they got in before you secure the account.
- You do not need to contact the attacker or reply to any message they sent.
- You do not need to clean up every old online account right now; secure the email account and the most sensitive reset-linked accounts first.
- You do not need to delete the email account today unless the provider confirms it cannot be secured.

## Important reassurance
This can happen to careful people, especially when recovery details are changed quickly. Taking the steps in order can reduce further damage even if you feel behind.

## Scope note
These are first steps to regain control and limit harm. If the provider recovery route fails, or if work systems, money, identity documents, or multiple accounts are involved, later decisions may need specialist technical, fraud, legal, or workplace support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. If a crime is ongoing or someone is in immediate danger, call 999.
