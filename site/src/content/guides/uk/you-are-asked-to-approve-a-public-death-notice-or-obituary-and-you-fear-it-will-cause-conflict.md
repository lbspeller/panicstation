# What to do if… you are asked to approve a public death notice or obituary and you fear it will cause conflict (UK)

## Short answer
Do not approve the notice while you feel rushed or pressured. Ask for publication to be held until you know what type of notice it is, who is responsible for it, and whether a short, neutral version can be used.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t approve wording just to end the pressure if you think it may cause avoidable harm.
- Don’t use the notice to settle old arguments, hint at blame, or send a message to one person.
- Don’t add avoidable private details, such as a home address, personal phone number, personal email address, or family bank details.
- Don’t debate the wording in a large group chat if screenshots, pile-ons, or misunderstandings are likely.
- Don’t assume a family death notice, an obituary, an online memorial post, and a deceased estates notice are the same thing.

## What to do now
1. **Ask what kind of notice this is.**  
   Ask where it will appear: a newspaper, funeral director’s website, online memorial page, social media post, church notice, crematorium notice, or The Gazette. Ask who is submitting it and what deadline the publisher has given.

2. **If it is an estate or creditor notice, do not rewrite it as a family tribute.**  
   If someone says it is “for creditors”, “for probate”, “for the estate”, or “for The Gazette”, ask who the executor, administrator, or solicitor handling the estate is. Ask what they need from you, if anything, before you approve wording.

3. **Ask for one named contact and a short hold.**  
   Send one calm message: “I’m not able to approve this wording yet. Please hold publication until one neutral version is confirmed and one person sends the final text.” If there is a publisher deadline, ask for the latest safe time for edits.

4. **Move to a minimum neutral version.**  
   Aim for only what is needed: the person’s name, a broad location such as town or county, and service or donation details if those are already settled. If family relationships are the flashpoint, use wording such as “loved and missed by family and friends” rather than a long list of relatives.

5. **If names or relationships are contested, reduce the list rather than ranking people.**  
   Consider using no survivor list, a very short relationship list, or first names only if the publisher allows it. Avoid wording that looks like a correction, punishment, exclusion, or public judgement.

6. **Use the funeral director or publisher as a buffer where possible.**  
   If a funeral director is involved, ask whether they can receive the final text from one agreed person and submit it to the publisher. This can stop the approval process becoming a direct family power struggle.

7. **Protect privacy and reduce funeral scam risk.**  
   Use the funeral director, church, crematorium, or charity route for public contact details. Avoid public family bank details. If there is a livestream or donation link, check it with the funeral director or named charity before it is shared.

8. **Keep a clean record of the final wording.**  
   Save the version you approved, who sent it, the time it was sent, and any publisher confirmation. Keep it factual and brief.

9. **If you are being threatened or harassed, stop negotiating by message.**  
   Save the messages. If there is immediate danger or someone is threatening violence, call 999. For non-emergency harassment or threats, use 101 or the relevant police online reporting route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle inheritance questions, family status disputes, or old relationship conflicts right now.
- You do not need to write a full life story today; a longer tribute can wait or be shared privately.
- You do not need to decide who is right about every name or relationship before preventing a public wording mistake.

## Important reassurance
A public notice can feel permanent, especially when grief and family tension are already high. Taking a short pause, reducing detail, and using neutral wording is a reasonable way to prevent new harm.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce immediate conflict and avoid an irreversible publication mistake. Later decisions about estate responsibility, harassment, funeral arrangements, or family disputes may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Publisher policies and estate responsibilities vary, so ask the publisher, funeral director, executor, administrator, or a suitable professional what is required in your situation.
