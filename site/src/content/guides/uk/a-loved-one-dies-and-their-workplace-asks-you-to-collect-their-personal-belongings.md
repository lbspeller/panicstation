# What to do if… a loved one dies and their workplace asks you to collect their personal belongings (UK)

## Short answer
Ask the workplace to secure and hold everything, and to not dispose of anything, while you confirm who is authorised to collect. If they press for an immediate decision, use: “Please hold everything securely; I will confirm collection arrangements when I can.”

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to disposal of personal items just because they look unimportant.
- Do not let several people collect items informally if there could be disagreement later about what was taken.
- Do not give a shipping address until you know what is being sent, how it will be packed, and how it will be tracked.
- Do not share passwords or ask staff to access personal accounts.
- Do not assume the workplace can release everything to any family member; they may need to check who has authority.
- Do not mix personal belongings with company property such as laptops, phones, keys, ID badges, access cards, uniforms, or tools.

## What to do now
1. Send a holding message in writing if you can: “Please keep all belongings safe and unchanged. Please do not dispose of anything. I will confirm collection arrangements when I can.”
2. Ask for one named contact, such as someone in HR or a senior manager, so you are not dealing with several people at once.
3. Ask them to secure the items now. Include drawers, lockers, coat storage, tool cabinets, bags kept on site, and any private storage area they know about.
4. Ask for a written inventory of the items, and photos if they can provide them. Ask where the items will be stored and who will have access.
5. Ask them to separate personal belongings from company property. Say that company property can be retained by the employer for security or IT reasons, while personal items are held for collection.
6. Ask what they need before releasing the belongings. They may ask for proof of death and may need to release items to the personal representative, meaning the executor or administrator, or to someone that person authorises.
7. If you do not yet know who is authorised, say: “We are identifying the personal representative. For now, please hold everything securely and do not release or dispose of anything.”
8. If collecting in person, ask for a private appointment time. Bring photo ID, check the items against the inventory, and ask for a simple signed receipt or handover list.
9. Keep a small record: date, time, who you spoke to, what was agreed, where the items are stored, and any follow-up agreed in writing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today what should happen to every item.
- You do not need to settle family decisions about who keeps what today.
- You do not need to resolve probate, pensions, benefits, final pay, or tax before asking the workplace to hold and document the belongings.
- You do not need to answer questions about funeral arrangements or workplace announcements unless you want to.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel blank or pressured in the first days after a death. “Hold securely, document, and hand over carefully” is a safe temporary answer when you are not ready to decide more.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate belongings decision only. Later decisions about the estate, employment benefits, pay, pensions, tax, or disputes may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Workplace policies and document checks can vary, so keep requests in writing and use specialist advice if there is disagreement, uncertainty about authority, or concern that items could go missing.
