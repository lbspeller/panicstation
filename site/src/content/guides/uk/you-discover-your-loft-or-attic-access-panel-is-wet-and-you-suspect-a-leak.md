# What to do if… you discover your loft or attic access panel is wet and you suspect a leak (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the wet loft or attic access panel as possible water ingress until it has been checked. Keep away from anything electrical near the wet area, isolate power only if you can do that safely, and arrange a prompt check of the loft, roof, pipes, or tank.

## Do not do these things
- Do not touch light fittings, switches, sockets, cables, or the consumer unit if they may be wet.
- Do not go into the loft if it is slippery, dark, unsafe, or not properly boarded.
- Do not step on insulation or ceiling plasterboard.
- Do not puncture the ceiling, pull down wet plasterboard, or force a swollen hatch.
- Do not run a fan, heater, or dehumidifier near the affected area if water may have reached electrics.
- Do not climb onto the roof.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safer first.**
   - Keep people and pets away from the wet hatch, the floor below it, and anything electrical nearby.
   - If there is arcing, sparking, a burning smell, smoke, or water coming through a light fitting, leave the area and call 999 or 112.
   - If you can reach the consumer unit without touching wet surfaces, switch off the affected circuit. If you are not sure which circuit is affected, use the main switch only if it is safe to do so.
   - If the consumer unit or fuse board may be wet, do not touch it. Contact a registered electrician.

2. **Look from the hatch only if it is safe.**
   - Use a torch from below or from the hatch opening.
   - Look for active dripping, a spreading wet patch, water trails, damp insulation, a musty smell, or daylight showing through roof damage.
   - Stop if the hatch, ladder, ceiling, or loft area looks unsafe.

3. **Check visible internal sources without entering the loft.**
   - If you can see a cold-water storage cistern, header tank, overflow, or pipework from the hatch, look for water tracking from that area.
   - If water appears to be coming from a tank, pipe, overflow, or heating-related pipework, contact a plumber.
   - If water appears after heavy rain, strong wind, snow, or ice, contact a roofer or suitable building repair professional.

4. **Contain drips without putting yourself under risk.**
   - Put a bucket or bowl under drips if it is not under a light fitting or other electrical item.
   - Put towels around the wet area to catch splashes, but keep them away from sockets, cables, and light fittings.
   - Move cardboard, fabrics, electronics, and stored items away from the damp area if you can do so safely.

5. **Record what you can see.**
   - Take photos or a short video of the wet hatch, ceiling stain, drips, and any visible water trail.
   - Note when you first noticed it and whether it followed heavy rain, strong wind, snow, ice, or a very cold night.
   - Keep any damaged items aside until you have photos.

6. **Get the right help.**
   - If there is active dripping, sagging plaster, spreading staining, or any possible electrical contact, treat it as urgent.
   - If you own the home, arrange a roof or building inspection, and use a plumber if the visible source may be a tank or pipe.
   - If you rent, report it to your landlord, letting agent, or housing provider straight away as possible water ingress, roof leak, pipe leak, or electrical risk. Send the photos and ask how emergency repairs should be handled.
   - If water may have reached wiring, light fittings, sockets, or the consumer unit, do not restore power to the affected area until a registered electrician says it is safe.

7. **Limit moisture spread only after electrical risk is controlled.**
   - Open windows in the affected room if practical and safe.
   - Keep the hatch closed if opening it makes dripping or damp air spread into the room.
   - Do not start drying, cleaning, or mould treatment until the source of the water has been identified or made safe.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it is condensation, a roof leak, a pipe leak, or a tank problem.
- You do not need to replace insulation, redecorate, treat mould, or plan ventilation changes until the water source is found.
- You do not need to sort every insurance or repair detail before the area is safe, photographed, and contained. Check policy wording when you are calmer.

## Important reassurance
A wet loft hatch can be the first visible sign of a small leak, condensation, or water tracking from somewhere nearby. Acting cautiously does not mean the worst has happened; it means you are reducing electrical risk and preventing avoidable damage while the source is checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A roofer, plumber, landlord repair team, insurer, surveyor, or registered electrician may be needed later depending on where the moisture is coming from.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, building, electrical, or professional advice. If there is fire, smoke, arcing, sparking, a burning smell, or water coming through a light fitting, leave the area and call 999 or 112.
