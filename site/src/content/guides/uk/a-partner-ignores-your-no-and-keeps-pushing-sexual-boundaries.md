# What to do if… a partner ignores your no and keeps pushing sexual boundaries (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause and stop the interaction if you can. Then contact specialist sexual violence support, even if you are unsure what counts or what you want to do next.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay in a private or isolated space with them if you feel your boundaries may be pushed again.
- Do not keep explaining your no in the moment to try to make them understand.
- Do not agree to anything sexual just to end the pressure or keep the peace.
- Do not confront them today if that could make you less safe.
- Do not delete messages or notes in a panic if you might want clarity later.
- Do not assume you must report to police to get support or medical help.

## What to do now
1. **Create immediate distance.** End the interaction and move to a safer room or place, ideally somewhere with a lock, other people nearby, or an easy exit. If you can, take your phone, keys, and any essential medication.
2. **Use one short line, then act.** You could say: “I said no. Stop.” You could also say: “This is not up for discussion.” Then leave the room, end the call, ask them to leave, or ask someone else to come to you.
3. **Bring in another person quickly.** Text or call someone you trust with a simple request: “Can you call me now?” or “Can you come and get me?” If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
4. **Make the next few hours safer.** If you live together or they can access your space, consider staying with a friend, family member, or somewhere they cannot enter freely. If you cannot leave, keep your phone charged, stay near an exit if possible, and avoid being alone with them behind a closed door.
5. **Contact specialist support without committing to any next step.** In England and Wales, if you are 16 or over, you can contact the 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line by phone or online chat. In Scotland, Rape Crisis Scotland and NHS Scotland SARCS can help. In Northern Ireland, The Rowan and nidirect routes can help.
6. **If sexual contact happened and you are worried about health, ask for medical help.** Sexual assault referral or response services can usually help with injuries, STI concerns, pregnancy prevention options, and choices about forensic evidence. You can ask for support even if you do not want police involvement.
7. **If you need urgent medical advice but it is not an emergency, use the local urgent health advice route.** This may be NHS 111 where available, NHS 24 in Scotland, or the relevant urgent out-of-hours service in Northern Ireland. If you are in immediate danger or need emergency medical help, call 999.
8. **Make a short private memory note if it is safe.** Write the date and time, what you said, what they did, any witnesses, and any relevant messages. Keep it somewhere only you can access, not on a shared device or account.
9. **If you may want to report later, avoid deleting messages or washing clothing if this is easy and safe.** If you have already washed, changed, eaten, drunk, or deleted something, you can still ask for support and advice.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this is abuse, assault, or a crime.
- You do not need to decide today whether to report to police.
- You do not need to confront them, negotiate rules, or fix the relationship now.
- You do not need to gather perfect evidence or write a full account.
- You do not need to tell everyone what happened; one safe person or one support service is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
If someone keeps pushing after you have said no, it is reasonable to treat that as a serious boundary problem and get support. Freezing, going quiet, changing your mind under pressure, or giving in to make it stop can happen when someone feels unsafe. You are allowed to slow everything down and put your safety first.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: to stop the pressure, get you safer, and connect you to specialist support. Later decisions about housing, the relationship, reporting, legal options, or ongoing support can be made with specialist help and without rush.

## Important note
This guide provides general first-step safety and support information. It is not legal, medical, counselling, therapeutic, or other professional advice.
