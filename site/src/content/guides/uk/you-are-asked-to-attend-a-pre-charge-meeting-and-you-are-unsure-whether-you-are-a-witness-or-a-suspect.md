# What to do if… you are asked to attend a pre-charge meeting and you are unsure whether you are a witness or a suspect (UK)

## Short answer
Request written clarification of your status as either a witness or a suspect and do not attend any meeting until you have secured independent legal advice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a "voluntary" or "informal" meeting carries no legal risk or cannot lead to a charge.
- Do not attend the meeting alone or without a solicitor if there is any uncertainty regarding your status.
- Do not provide written accounts, timelines, or verbal explanations before speaking to a legal representative.
- Do not guess, speculate, or fill in memory gaps if questioned by an officer.
- Do not discuss the investigation with other potential witnesses, colleagues, or on social media platforms.
- Do not volunteer access to your mobile phone, social media accounts, or personal data under pressure.

## What to do now
1. **Determine which UK jurisdiction has authority over the matter.** **Ask the officer specifically if the investigation is being conducted under the laws of England and Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland.**
   - In Scotland, you have specific rights under the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Act 2016, including the right to a "Letter of Rights" before any voluntary interview.
   - In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, the Police and Criminal Evidence Act (PACE) codes apply.

2. **Request the specific details of the meeting in writing.** **Ask the officer to confirm via email or letter whether you are being treated as a witness or a suspect.**
   - Request the name of the offence being investigated and the date/location of the alleged incident.
   - Ask if the meeting will be an "interview under caution" or "pre-charge engagement."

3. **Secure independent legal advice before agreeing to a date or time.** **In England and Wales, you are entitled to free legal advice for any police interview, including a voluntary one.**
   - If you do not have a solicitor, tell the officer you require the "duty solicitor" for the interview.
   - If you are in Scotland, you have a statutory right to a solicitor during any interview if you are a suspect.

4. **Confirm if you are legally required to have an "appropriate adult" present.** **If you are under 18 or have a mental health condition or learning disability, the police must arrange an appropriate adult to support you.**
   - For anyone under 18, this is a mandatory legal safeguard that cannot be waived.
   - For vulnerable adults, the police must appoint one as soon as they identify a need.

5. **Establish if the meeting is "pre-charge engagement" or an interview.** **Pre-charge engagement is a specific, voluntary process for suspects that occurs after a first interview to discuss evidence before a charging decision.**
   - Your solicitor must agree to this process; do not agree to it on your own.
   - If it is an interview, it must be conducted under caution if you are a suspect.

6. **Decline "informal chats" or off-the-record discussions.** **Tell the officer: "I am willing to assist, but I will only participate in a formal meeting once I have received legal advice and my solicitor is present."**
   - Be aware that anything you say in a "casual" setting can still be recorded in an officer's notebook and used as evidence.

7. **Verify the recording and transcription procedures for the meeting.** **Ask if the meeting will be audio or video recorded and how you can obtain a copy of the transcript.**
   - If you are a witness, you may be asked to sign a section 9 statement (England / Wales) or a witness statement; do not sign this until your solicitor has reviewed it.

8. **Create a private, factual log of all police contact.** **Write down the names, ranks, and collar numbers of any officers who contact you, along with the date, time, and summary of the conversation.**
   - Keep this log private for your solicitor only.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to provide a full statement or "clear your name."
- You do not need to provide access to digital devices or medical records immediately.
- You can wait for your solicitor to receive "disclosure" (the details of the evidence against you) before responding to any allegations.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel anxious or confused when the police use ambiguous terms like "pre-charge meeting." Taking a moment to secure legal advice is a standard procedural step and does not imply guilt or lack of cooperation. Slowing the process down ensures that your rights are protected.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for initial police contact. Long-term legal strategy and decisions regarding evidence must be handled by a qualified solicitor.

## Important note
This information is general and does not constitute legal advice. Procedures vary significantly between the different nations of the UK. If you are unsure of your status, the safest default is to assume you are a suspect and exercise your right to legal representation.
