# What to do if… you notice work calendar entries disappearing and you suspect interference (UK)

## Short answer
Confirm receipt of the notice in writing, request the full evidence pack including specific allegations, and identify a colleague or trade union representative to accompany you to the hearing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the letter or fail to attend the hearing without providing a valid reason.
- Do not resign immediately to avoid the process, as this may forfeit your right to claim unfair dismissal.
- Do not admit to allegations or offer detailed explanations in your initial acknowledgment.
- Do not delete emails, alter logs, or remove company files in an attempt to "clear" your name.
- Do not discuss the details of the case with colleagues or on social media platforms.
- Do not attend the hearing alone if you have access to a statutory companion.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the physical or digital notice immediately.** Save a copy of the letter or email to a personal device or print it. Note the specific date, time, and location of the hearing, and identify the name of the person chairing the meeting.
2. **Request the full evidence pack.** Reply to the sender (usually HR or a manager) asking for all documents the employer intends to rely on.
   - copies of witness statements
   - relevant emails or data logs
   - the specific workplace policy or rule allegedly breached
   - confirmation of the potential outcomes (e.g., first warning, final warning, or dismissal)
3. **Appoint a statutory companion.** You have a legal right under the Employment Relations Act 1999 to be accompanied by a work colleague, a trade union representative, or a trade union official. Contact your union branch immediately if you are a member.
4. **Request a postponement if your companion is unavailable.** If your chosen companion cannot attend the scheduled time, propose an alternative time.
   - The proposed time must be reasonable.
   - It must fall within five working days beginning with the day after the day originally proposed by the employer.
5. **Establish a factual timeline of events.** Write down a private, chronological account of the situation for your own reference. Include dates, times, locations, and the names of anyone else who was present. 
6. **Identify your own supporting evidence.** Gather any documents already in your possession that support your account, such as rotas, training records, or performance reviews. Do not download or "data dump" confidential company information that you do not normally have access to.
7. **Ask for reasonable adjustments for the hearing.** If you have a disability, neurodivergence, or a health condition that affects your ability to participate, request adjustments now. This could include extra breaks, the hearing being held in a neutral location, or receiving questions in writing beforehand.
8. **Clarify the role of your companion.** Ensure you and your companion understand that they can put your case forward, sum up the case, and respond to views expressed, but they cannot answer questions on your behalf.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide your full defense or witness statements until you have reviewed the employer's evidence.
- You do not need to decide whether to appeal until after a formal decision has been issued in writing.
- You do not need to hire a private solicitor to attend the hearing, as they do not have a statutory right to attend internal meetings unless your contract specifically allows it.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a disciplinary notice is an invitation to a meeting, not a final verdict. The Acas Code of Practice exists to ensure that the process is fair and that you have a chance to state your case before any decision is made. Nervousness or confusion is a normal response to workplace stress; taking a moment to organize your documents is the best way to handle this.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for receiving a formal disciplinary notice in the UK. It does not cover the conduct of the hearing itself, the appeal process, or legal claims for unfair dismissal.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment rights can depend on your specific contract, length of service, and employment status (employee vs worker). For specific advice, contact Acas, your trade union, or a qualified employment law professional.
