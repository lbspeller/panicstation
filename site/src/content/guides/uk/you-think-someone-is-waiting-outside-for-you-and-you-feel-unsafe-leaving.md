# What to do if… you think someone is waiting outside for you and you feel unsafe leaving (UK)

## Short answer
Do not leave alone. Move away from the exit, stay with staff or other people, and ask venue or building staff to help you leave safely. Call 999 now if you think harm is likely or someone is in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not slip out alone to test whether they are really waiting.
- Do not confront them or try to film them at close range.
- Do not go to a car park, side exit, stairwell, or quiet corridor alone to check.
- Do not post your location, exit route, or transport plan where it could be seen by the wrong person.
- Do not go straight home if you think you are being followed.

## What to do now
1. **Move away from the exit and get to a staffed area.** Go to the bar, reception, security desk, box office, front desk, or another place where staff and other people are present. Ask to wait somewhere away from doors and windows if possible.
2. **Tell staff clearly what you need.** Say: “I think someone is waiting outside for me and I do not feel safe leaving.” Ask for:
   - a manager or security person now,
   - a safe place to wait away from the exit,
   - an escort to your taxi, car, bus stop, station, or pickup point,
   - staff to watch the exit while you leave,
   - a different exit if one is available and safe.
3. **Use a discreet venue scheme only if it helps.** Some participating venues use a help phrase such as “Ask for Angela”. If you do not know whether the venue uses it, ask staff directly for help instead.
4. **Call police if you feel in immediate danger.**
   - If a crime is happening now, someone is threatening violence, or you think harm is likely, call 999.
   - If you cannot speak on a mobile 999 call, stay on the line, cough or tap if you safely can, and press 55 when prompted.
5. **Change the leaving situation, not your courage level.** Leave only when at least one safer condition is in place:
   - you are leaving with staff, security, or a group,
   - you are using a different safe exit,
   - your transport is ready and you can go straight to it,
   - staff can watch from inside until you are away.
6. **Make leaving one supported move.** Go directly to your transport with the escort or group. If using a taxi or private hire vehicle, wait inside until it arrives, check the vehicle details before stepping outside, and get in only when you can leave immediately.
7. **If you think you are being followed after leaving, do not go home.** Go to a busy, well-lit public place such as a staffed shop, transport hub, hotel reception, police station front counter if nearby, or another open venue. Call 999 if you feel at risk.
8. **Capture key details without increasing risk.** From inside, or through staff, note the time, where the person is standing, what they look like, what they are wearing, and any vehicle details you can safely see. Ask staff to make an incident note and preserve relevant CCTV if they can.
9. **Tell one trusted person once you are safer.** Share where you are now, where you are going next, and what you want them to do, such as staying on the phone until you are inside somewhere safe.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether this counts as stalking, harassment, a misunderstanding, or something else.
- Deciding whether to make a formal report, unless there is immediate danger now.
- Writing a full timeline; a few accurate notes and times are enough for the moment.
- Longer-term decisions about routes, routines, work, school, housing, or future contact.

## Important reassurance
You do not need proof to ask staff for help, wait inside, change your exit plan, or call 999 if you feel at risk. Treating the situation as real until you are safely away is a reasonable safety step.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes to hours. If this is part of a repeated pattern, later decisions may need specialist safety, stalking, legal, workplace, housing, or other professional help.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice, and it is not a substitute for emergency services.
