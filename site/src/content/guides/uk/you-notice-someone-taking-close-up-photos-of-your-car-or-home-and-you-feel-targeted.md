# What to do if… you notice someone taking close-up photos of your car or home and you feel targeted (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer position first, such as inside, behind a locked door, or near other people. Call **999** if you feel in immediate danger or a crime is in progress; otherwise call **101** or report it to your local police force online.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront, chase, or try to block the person with your car.
- Do not go outside alone to get a better look if you feel uneasy.
- Do not post their photo, your address, or your plate details on social media.
- Do not touch anything suspicious on your car or property, such as an unknown device or tag; leave it in place and report it.
- Do not dismiss it if you feel targeted, it repeats, or the person reacts aggressively when noticed.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safety buffer.** Go indoors or into a staffed, busy place. Lock doors and keep a view only if you can do that safely. If you are in your car, keep the doors locked and drive to a busy, well-lit location with people rather than going straight home if you feel uneasy.
2. **Decide whether this is an emergency.** Call **999** if you feel threatened, the person is approaching you, trying doors, you think a crime is in progress, or you cannot get to safety. Otherwise call **101** or use your local police force’s online reporting route.
   - **If you need 999 but cannot speak:** stay on the line and follow the operator’s prompts. From a mobile, you may be asked to **press 55** or tap when prompted.
3. **Record what you can safely.** From a safe place, note the time, exact location, what they photographed, what they looked like, any vehicle make, colour or registration, and the direction they went. Only take a discreet photo or video if it does not increase risk, such as from indoors.
4. **Preserve your evidence after you are secure.** Save doorbell or CCTV clips promptly if you can. Write a short timeline while it is fresh. Keep originals and avoid editing them.
5. **Reduce immediate opportunity.** Close curtains or blinds at the front, turn on exterior lights, and check doors, windows and outbuildings. Move visible valuables away from windows. Later, only if the area is clear and it feels safe, move your car to a driveway, garage, or more visible well-lit spot.
6. **Make an official record.** When you report it, ask for the incident or crime reference number. If it happens again, quote that reference so the reports are clearly linked.
7. **Tell one trusted person nearby.** Let household members know not to open the door to strangers right now. If you trust a neighbour, ask them to save any camera footage covering the street or driveway for the same time window.

## What can wait
- Working out why they did it or trying to identify them yourself.
- Changing locks, installing cameras, or buying security equipment.
- Contacting insurers or landlords, unless there is damage or attempted entry.
- Writing a long statement; a short factual log is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel shaken by this. Taking photos can have innocent explanations, but you do not need to solve the motive right now. Your priority is to stay safe, document what happened, and create an official record so you are not handling it alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety and preserving options. If this repeats, becomes harassment, or you feel personally targeted, later decisions may need help from police or specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call **999**. If you are unsure, it is still appropriate to report suspicious activity and ask what to do next.
