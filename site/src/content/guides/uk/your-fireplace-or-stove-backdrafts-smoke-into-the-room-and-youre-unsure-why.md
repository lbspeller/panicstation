# What to do if… your fireplace or stove backdrafts smoke into the room and you’re unsure why (UK)

## Short answer
Stop using the appliance immediately, open windows briefly as you exit, and move everyone to fresh air to prevent carbon monoxide poisoning.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep the fire burning to "see if it clears" or attempt to relight it.
- Do not look directly up the chimney or flue, as falling embers or hot soot can cause eye injuries.
- Do not close internal doors or vents to trap the smell, as this can concentrate dangerous gases.
- Do not ignore symptoms like headache, nausea, or dizziness, even if you do not see smoke.
- Do not run kitchen or bathroom extractor fans, as these can pull more smoke into the room by creating negative pressure.
- Do not attempt to use a vacuum cleaner to clear soot while the area is still hot or smoky.

## What to do now
1. **Stop interacting with the fuel and isolate the appliance if safe.** Close the stove door or the fireplace damper if the handle is cool to the touch.
   - If it is a gas fireplace, turn the control knob to the "off" position.
   - If the appliance is too hot to touch or flames are uncontrolled, leave it alone and evacuate.
2. **Open windows and doors as you move toward the exit.** Create a brief cross-breeze to clear the immediate build-up of smoke, then leave the room.
3. **Move all occupants and pets into fresh air immediately.** Do not wait to see if the smoke dissipates on its own.
4. **Call 999 if anyone is experiencing severe symptoms.** 
   - Symptoms include collapse, chest pain, sudden confusion, or loss of consciousness.
   - For milder symptoms where you suspect carbon monoxide exposure, call NHS 111.
5. **Report a potential gas or carbon monoxide emergency if applicable.** Call the **National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999** from outside the property if you suspect a CO leak or if you smell unignited gas.
6. **Check for signs of a chimney fire from the outside.** Look for sparks, dense black smoke, or a roaring sound coming from the chimney stack.
   - If you see these signs, call 999 and request the Fire and Rescue Service.
7. **Ensure no extractor fans or tumble dryers are running.** Switch off any mechanical ventilation that might be pulling air out of the house.
8. **Stay out of the affected rooms until the smoke has fully cleared.** Do not re-enter to clean or investigate until the air is fresh and the appliance is cold.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific cause (e.g., wind direction, cold flue, or blockage) right now.
- You do not need to clean soot or ash from furniture or carpets immediately.
- You do not need to contact your insurance provider until the property is safe and a professional has inspected the system.

## Important reassurance
It is common for fireplaces to backdraft due to sudden changes in air pressure or weather conditions. Feeling lightheaded or startled is a normal reaction to smoke inhalation and the sound of a CO alarm; these physical responses do not mean you have failed to manage the fire correctly.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate safety. It does not cover the technical repair of flues, chimney sweeping, or the diagnosis of structural ventilation issues, which require a qualified professional.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization only and is not a substitute for emergency services or professional technical advice. If a carbon monoxide alarm sounds or if anyone feels unwell, you must prioritize evacuation and professional medical or emergency assessment. Do not use the appliance again until it has been certified safe by a Gas Safe engineer or a HETAS-registered professional.
