# What to do if… a payment arrangement has been cancelled and the full balance is due (UK)

## Short answer
Do not panic-pay through a link or new number in the notice. Check it is genuine using a trusted contact route, then contact the creditor or payment-plan provider as soon as you safely can to ask why it was cancelled and whether they will pause action while the plan is corrected or replaced in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay using a link, QR code, or new phone number in the notice until you have checked it against a trusted source, such as a recent statement, your online account, or the organisation’s official website.
- Do not ignore the notice; it may be time-sensitive.
- Do not agree to a repayment amount you cannot keep up with just to stop the stress.
- Do not take high-cost borrowing to make a lump-sum payment without speaking to a free debt adviser first.
- Do not send card or bank details by email, text, or to someone who contacted you unexpectedly.
- Do not assume it is your fault; failed payments can happen because of bank issues, admin errors, changed payment dates, or a Direct Debit problem.

## What to do now
1. **Check the notice is safe to act on.**
   - Pause before clicking anything.
   - Find a trusted contact route for the creditor or payment-plan provider.
   - Use a recent statement, your existing online account, your payment-plan paperwork, or the organisation’s official website.
   - Do not use contact details from the notice until you have checked them.

2. **Identify what kind of debt or bill it is.**
   - Check whether the notice is about a loan, credit card, overdraft, catalogue account, utility bill, rent, mortgage, council tax, HMRC, court fine, or another debt.
   - If it is rent, mortgage, council tax, gas, electricity, court fines, or another debt where non-payment could affect your home, essential services, goods, or liberty, treat it as urgent and contact a free debt adviser.

3. **Check what happened to the last payment.**
   - Look in your bank app or statement to see whether the payment left your account, was returned, was taken late, or was taken for the wrong amount.
   - Find the notice, the original payment arrangement, the last two or three payment confirmations, and any bank evidence of a failed or incorrect payment.
   - Note the date the arrangement was cancelled and any deadline in the notice.

4. **Contact the creditor or plan provider and ask for the reason in plain terms.**
   - Ask what exactly triggered the cancellation: missed payment, late payment, review date, admin error, failed Direct Debit, changed circumstances, or something else.
   - Ask them to explain the current balance, any interest or charges added, and what would be needed to restart the arrangement.
   - Ask for a temporary hold on collection or enforcement action while the issue is checked.
   - Ask for any hold, restarted plan, or new payment arrangement to be confirmed in writing.

5. **If the payment failed because of a Direct Debit problem, contact your bank.**
   - If a Direct Debit was taken on the wrong date, for the wrong amount, or without authority, ask your bank or building society about the Direct Debit Guarantee.
   - Get a reference number for the bank contact.
   - Tell the creditor that you are checking a Direct Debit issue and ask them to pause action while it is corrected.
   - If you disagree with the bill itself, raise that with the company as a complaint or billing dispute as well.

6. **If you can afford the old arrangement, ask to reinstate it.**
   - Say the amount and date you can restart.
   - Ask whether they will remove or reduce any charges caused by an error or failed collection.
   - Ask them not to treat a corrected payment problem as a refusal to pay.

7. **If you cannot afford the old arrangement, make an affordable holding offer.**
   - Work out what you can safely pay after rent or mortgage, council tax, utilities, food, travel, and other essentials.
   - Offer only what you can keep paying.
   - Ask them to freeze or reduce further interest and charges while a new affordable plan is considered.
   - If it is a priority debt, get free debt advice before relying on a token payment.

8. **Get free debt advice quickly if the balance is large, the debt is a priority debt, or the creditor will not pause.**
   - Contact a free UK debt advice service and say: “My payment arrangement was cancelled and they are asking for the full balance.”
   - Ask what to do before making a lump-sum payment or accepting a new plan.
   - If you live in England or Wales, ask whether Breathing Space could be suitable. A debt adviser must start it if it is appropriate.
   - If you live in Scotland or Northern Ireland, ask the adviser about the debt options and protections that apply where you live.

9. **If the creditor may have acted unfairly, start a complaint record.**
   - Make a formal complaint to the organisation and keep a copy.
   - Include what happened, what you asked for, what evidence you have, and what would put it right.
   - If it is a regulated financial business and the complaint is not resolved, ask the Financial Ombudsman Service whether it can look at it.

10. **Keep one simple record.**
   - Write down the date and time of each call, the name or department you spoke to, and what was agreed.
   - Save letters, emails, screenshots, payment confirmations, and complaint references.
   - After any phone call, send a short message confirming what you understood was agreed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a long-term debt solution today.
- You do not need to decide today whether to use a debt management plan, IVA, bankruptcy, debt relief order, or any other formal option.
- You do not need to fix your credit file right now.
- You do not need perfect paperwork before asking for a hold; you can say you are gathering documents and need time to check what happened.

## Important reassurance
A full-balance notice can feel like a cliff edge, but it does not mean you have to make a rushed payment today. Verifying the notice, finding out why the arrangement stopped, and asking for a written hold or replacement plan can buy time and reduce avoidable escalation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the situation, avoid scam or panic payments, and create a clear paper trail. Later decisions may need help from a free debt adviser, a regulated financial complaints route, or another specialist depending on the type of debt.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, debt, or professional advice. If you are at risk of losing your home, essential services, essential goods, or facing court or enforcement action, get urgent help from a free debt adviser or a qualified professional.
