# What to do if… you need to update your child’s emergency contacts and pick-up list after a death (UK)

## Short answer
Start with the place responsible for your child right now, such as school, nursery, childminder, or club, and ask them to update today’s emergency contacts and authorised collection list.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume staff will change the record just because they know about the death.
- Don’t rely only on an informal text to one staff member; ask for the official contact and pick-up record to be updated.
- Don’t send a new adult to collect the child without warning the setting first.
- Don’t give a new collection password or code word to more people than necessary.
- Don’t try to solve long-term guardianship, wills, or court questions just to fix today’s contact list.
- Don’t give detailed information about the death if the setting only needs enough information to keep the child safe.

## What to do now

1. **Update the place holding your child today.**  
   Call the office or main contact point and say: “A listed emergency contact has died. I need to update the emergency contacts and authorised collectors today.”

2. **Ask for the specific records to be changed.**  
   Ask them to remove the deceased person, add at least two reachable contacts if possible, update authorised collectors, and change any collection password or code word the setting uses.

3. **Give a clear fallback plan.**  
   For each contact, give their full name, relationship to the child, phone number, and whether they can actually collect the child. If someone is only available by phone, say that clearly.

4. **Check what proof the setting needs today.**  
   Ask: “What will your staff accept today so they can release my child to the right adult?” They may ask for photo ID at pick-up, a signed form, an email from a registered parent or carer, or another check under their own procedure.

5. **If the usual main carer has changed, flag this as urgent and practical.**  
   Say: “The normal carer arrangements have changed suddenly. Please tell the safeguarding lead or manager, and tell me what you need on file for today.” Keep the focus on who can be contacted and who can collect.

6. **If someone should not collect the child, tell the setting directly.**  
   Ask what information or documents they need before they can act on that request. Schools and childcare settings may need to follow their own safeguarding, parental responsibility, and court-order procedures.

7. **Update health contact routes if the deceased person was listed.**  
   Contact the child’s GP practice and ask how to update contact or emergency contact details on the child’s record. If you already manage the child’s services through an NHS account or the NHS App, check whether the relevant detail can be changed there, but ask the GP practice if you are unsure.

8. **Use one copy-paste contact block.**  
   Write one short note with the child’s name, date of birth, your phone number, backup contacts, authorised collectors, and any new collection password. Reuse it so you do not have to keep retyping details while overwhelmed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide long-term guardianship, schooling, wills, or family court steps just to update emergency contacts.
- You do not need to update every organisation today; start with anyone supervising your child today or this week.
- You do not need to explain the death in detail; “a close family bereavement” is usually enough for urgent admin.
- You do not need to make the contact list perfect now; it only needs to be safe enough for the next few days.

## Important reassurance
This kind of admin can feel harsh immediately after a death, but it is a protective step for your child. It is normal to feel foggy, so a short script and one reusable contact note are enough.

## Scope note
These are first steps for urgent contact and pick-up safety only. If the death changes who will care for the child longer-term, later decisions may need specialist family, legal, school, or bereavement support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. Processes vary between schools, childcare settings, GP practices, and family circumstances. If anyone is in immediate danger, call 999; if there is a setting-related safety concern, ask for the safeguarding lead or manager.
