# What to do if… you are asked to leave a crowded bus or train because it is at capacity (UK)

## Short answer
Step away from the doors, platform edge, or boarding queue, then get a clear next authorised option from staff, the operator, or official travel information before you board something different.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to re-board against staff or crowd-control instructions.
- Do not argue at the doors, platform edge, or kerbside where people are still trying to move.
- Do not assume your ticket will work on a different train operator, route, bus, or tram without checking.
- Do not delete your ticket, booking confirmation, app notice, receipt, or delay message.
- Do not split up from children, dependants, or anyone you are travelling with just to catch the next service.
- Do not buy an expensive replacement ticket while still in the crowd unless you have no safer practical option.

## What to do now
1. Move to a safer pause point: step away from the doors, platform edge, road edge, or boarding queue, keep hold of your phone, ticket and bag, and regroup with anyone travelling with you.

2. Ask for the next authorised option. Use one clear sentence: “I was asked to leave because this service is full. What is the next service I can take to reach [destination], and will my ticket be accepted?”

3. If staff are nearby, ask them to be specific: the next train, bus, tram, platform, stop, route, operator, or interchange; whether your ticket is accepted; and what to do if you cannot travel soon.

4. If no staff are available, check official operator information, departure screens, station or stop notices, the operator app, National Rail, Traveline, or the local transport authority where relevant. Look for words such as “ticket acceptance”, “next permitted train”, “replacement service”, “major disruption”, or “do not travel”.

5. If you have an Advance train ticket or a ticket for a booked service, do not assume you can use a different train or operator. Check whether disruption arrangements allow it, or ask staff before boarding.

6. Keep a quick evidence note: time, place, route or service number if known, what you were told, and whether you were unable to board or were asked to leave. Keep screenshots of official messages if you can do that safely.

7. Choose the route that keeps you moving with the least uncertainty: the next authorised service, a confirmed alternative route, or a staffed waiting point where you can ask again. If the crowd still feels unsafe, move to a help point, ticket office, staffed barrier, or less congested area first.

8. If you cannot continue soon, ask the operator or ticket retailer what your immediate options are for changing the journey, refunding an unused ticket, or claiming later if you arrive late. The answer can depend on the ticket, operator and disruption arrangements.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide in the crowd whether to complain, claim compensation, or challenge the decision.
- You do not need to work out the perfect or cheapest route while people are pushing to board.
- You do not need to explain the whole situation repeatedly. A short note of what happened is enough for now.
- Refunds, compensation and complaints can usually wait until you are moving again or safely paused.

## Important reassurance
Being asked to leave or wait because a service is full can feel personal, especially when you urgently need to travel. Treat it as a crowd-safety situation first: get out of the pressure point, confirm what you can use next, and keep your ticket evidence.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes only. Later decisions about refunds, compensation, missed connections, complaints, accessibility support, or extra costs may need help from the operator, ticket retailer, passenger body, regulator, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, professional or transport-contract advice. Ticket validity, refunds, compensation and alternative travel depend on the ticket type, operator, route, disruption arrangements and any crowd-control instructions in force, especially for Advance tickets or different operators.
