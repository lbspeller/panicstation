# What to do if… a lender or subscription service pulls more money than you authorised from your account (UK)

## Short answer
Stop any further pulls through your bank or card issuer first. Then ask for the right refund route: a Direct Debit error can be refunded by your bank under the Direct Debit Guarantee, while a recurring card payment or continuous payment authority should be handled by your card issuer as a wrong or unauthorised payment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume you must deal with the company first. Your bank or card issuer can usually stop repeats, and may be able to refund or start the dispute route right away.
- Do not cancel your card or close your account as your first move unless your bank tells you to. A new card does not always stop a recurring card payment, and it can make refunds and essential payments harder to manage.
- Do not accept “we can’t stop it” from your bank or card issuer. Be clear that you are cancelling the Direct Debit or withdrawing consent for the recurring card payment.
- Do not get drawn into long arguments with the company right now. Your priority is stopping repeats and starting the refund route.
- Do not ignore overdraft fees, missed bills, or bounced essentials caused by the wrong payment. Raise these with your bank once the payment is logged as wrong or unauthorised.
- If you think your card, banking, or login details are compromised beyond this one company, do not keep using the same details. Freeze the card if you can, contact your bank immediately, and follow its fraud instructions.

## What to do now
1. **Identify what type of payment it was.**
   - In your banking app or statement, look for wording such as **Direct Debit**, **card payment**, **recurring card payment**, or **continuous payment authority**.
   - Screenshot the entry and note the merchant name, date, time, amount, and any reference.

2. **Stop further payments using the right route.**
   - **If it was a Direct Debit:** cancel the Direct Debit with your bank or building society through the app, phone, branch, or secure message.
   - **If it was a recurring card payment or continuous payment authority:** tell your card issuer that you are **withdrawing consent** and want **all future payments to this merchant stopped**.
   - For a future recurring card payment, ask by the end of the business day before the next payment is due where possible. If it is later than that, still ask now so repeats are stopped.

3. **Start the refund route with your bank or card issuer.**
   - **Direct Debit:** say, “This Direct Debit was taken for the wrong amount, on the wrong date, twice, or without authority. I want a refund under the Direct Debit Guarantee.”
   - **Recurring card payment or CPA:** say, “I did not authorise this amount, or I had already cancelled or withdrawn consent. Please stop the authority, treat this as a wrong or unauthorised payment, and tell me the refund or dispute route.”

4. **Tell the lender or subscription service in writing.**
   - Send a short message: “You took £X on DATE, but I authorised £Y / I cancelled on DATE. Do not take any further payments. Please confirm the account or subscription status and refund steps.”
   - Save the sent message, screenshots, chat logs, cancellation confirmation, and any case number.

5. **If this was a lender and you still owe money, prevent an accidental missed-payment problem.**
   - Ask the lender in writing for the correct next payment amount and date.
   - Ask how to pay manually while the automatic payment is paused.
   - Ask them to correct any internal notes if the over-collection caused you to cancel the payment authority.

6. **Protect the next 48 hours of essential money movement.**
   - Turn on bank alerts for low balance and new transactions.
   - If the wrong payment made you overdrawn or will affect rent, mortgage, utilities, food, travel, childcare, or other essentials, contact your bank and ask for help preventing knock-on harm.
   - Ask the bank to consider refunding or waiving charges linked to the wrong or unauthorised payment.

7. **If the bank or card issuer will not help, escalate cleanly.**
   - Make a formal complaint to the bank or card issuer with a short timeline, screenshots, and the exact outcome you want.
   - If you receive a final response you disagree with, you can usually take the complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service within six months of the date on that final response.
   - If the bank or card issuer has not responded within eight weeks, you can usually contact the Financial Ombudsman Service without waiting longer.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to keep or cancel the underlying contract beyond stopping the payment mechanism.
- Switching banks, changing card numbers, or rebuilding every payment setup.
- Writing a long complaint narrative. A short timeline and screenshots are enough right now.
- Arguing about compensation beyond the immediate stabilising issues: getting the money back, stopping repeats, and undoing fees.

## Important reassurance
Wrong amounts, duplicate pulls, price changes applied wrongly, and payment authorities continuing after cancellation can happen. You do not need to solve the whole dispute today; stopping repeats and starting the refund route is the stabilising step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about what you owe, cancellation terms, complaint outcomes, credit-file impact, or debt support may need specialist help once your account is stable.
