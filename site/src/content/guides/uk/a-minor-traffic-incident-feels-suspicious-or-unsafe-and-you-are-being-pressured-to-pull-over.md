# What to do if… a minor traffic incident feels suspicious or unsafe and you are being pressured to pull over (UK)

## Short answer
Do not stop in an isolated place to “sort it out”. Stop only when it is safe to do so, ideally somewhere public, well-lit and easy to leave, and call 999 if you are in danger or being threatened.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t follow them to a side street, lay-by, quiet car park, or another place they choose.
- Don’t get out of your vehicle if their behaviour feels aggressive, controlling, or wrong.
- Don’t hand over your phone, bank card, cash, PIN, or online banking app to “settle it”.
- Don’t pull in so tightly that you cannot drive away if the situation worsens.
- Don’t drive home if you think you are being followed; go to a busy public place or follow 999 instructions.
- Don’t argue about fault at the roadside or make admissions because you feel pressured.
- Don’t use a hand-held phone while driving unless you need to call 999 or 112 in a genuine emergency and it is unsafe or impractical to stop.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Keep driving at a normal, legal speed until you can stop somewhere safer. Use your indicators and hazard lights only when appropriate, and avoid sudden moves that could cause another collision.
2. **Choose the safest available stopping place.** Aim for a busy petrol station, supermarket car park, service area, or another public place with people, lighting and cameras. On a motorway or high-speed road, use a service area or another place of relative safety if you can.
3. **If someone is in danger, call 999.** Use hands-free if available, ask a passenger to call, or use a hand-held phone only if it is a genuine emergency and unsafe or impractical to stop. Say you have had a minor collision, the other driver is trying to make you stop somewhere you do not feel safe, and give your location, direction of travel, and the other vehicle’s registration if you can.
4. **If it is not an emergency, call 101 once safely stopped.** Explain that you could not safely exchange details at the scene because the other driver’s behaviour felt threatening or suspicious. Follow the call-handler’s instructions; many police forces also have online road-traffic reporting.
5. **If you stop, stay ready to leave.** Keep doors locked, windows mostly up, and park so you are not boxed in. If you choose to speak, open the window only a small amount.
6. **Capture the basics without a confrontation.** From inside your vehicle if possible, note the other vehicle’s registration, make, model, colour, location, time, and a short description of the driver. If you have dashcam, keep it recording and save the clip when you are safe.
7. **Meet collision duties in the safest way available.** If it is safe, exchange names, addresses, vehicle registration numbers, and the owner’s name and address if the vehicle is not yours. Provide insurance details if requested.
8. **If you could not give details at the time, report it promptly.** Report the collision to police as soon as reasonably practicable and, in any case, within 24 hours. Clearly say that you left the immediate spot because you did not feel safe.
9. **If they follow you or try to box you in, treat it as urgent.** Stay in the vehicle, call 999, keep moving only if it is safer than stopping, and follow police instructions.

## What can wait
- Deciding or arguing about who was at fault.
- Calling your insurer until you are safe, unless police or your insurer’s emergency line is the safest next call.
- Repair quotes, full written statements, courtesy cars, and detailed damage photos.
- Replying to messages or calls from the other driver while you still feel unsafe.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to prioritise immediate safety over a roadside discussion with a stranger. You can still report what happened and deal with insurance after you are somewhere safe.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the moment and reduce immediate risk. Insurance, liability, police reporting details, and any later legal questions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, insurance, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If someone is seriously injured, someone is in danger, a crime is in progress, or the collision has created a dangerous road obstruction, call 999.
