# What to do if… your authenticator app shows accounts missing after an update or phone change (UK)

## Short answer
Do not delete, reset, clear data, or reinstall the authenticator app yet. First, check whether you still have the old phone, app backup, sync, transfer, or recovery codes, then protect your email account before changing anything else.

## Do not do these things
- Do not tap "reset", "clear data", "remove all accounts", or uninstall and reinstall the app until you have checked restore, sync, or transfer options.
- Do not factory reset the old phone if it might still contain working codes.
- Do not turn off two-step verification in a panic unless you are already inside the real account settings and have another safe sign-in method ready.
- Do not use help links from unexpected emails or texts about your accounts; open the service by typing its address or using a known bookmark.
- Do not assume it is only an app bug if you also lost mobile signal, received SIM change messages, or saw password reset emails you did not request.

## What to do now
1. Put both phones somewhere safe and keep them powered.
   - Put the new phone and old phone on charge.
   - Do not factory reset, trade in, wipe, or give away the old phone yet.
   - Set or keep a screen lock on both phones.

2. Check the authenticator app before reinstalling it.
   - Open the app settings and look for backup, sync, restore, transfer, or export options.
   - If the app offers a restore prompt, use that before manually adding new accounts.
   - If the app shows an account sign-in option, sign in only if you are sure it is the official app and your own account.

3. Use the right recovery route for the app you were using.
   - For Microsoft Authenticator, look for "Restore from backup" or "Begin recovery". Microsoft says backup and restore only works on the same device type, so an iPhone backup cannot be restored to Android, and an Android backup cannot be restored to iPhone.
   - For Google Authenticator, if you still have the old phone, use Transfer accounts: export on the old phone and scan the QR code on the new phone. If you previously used Google Account sync, sign in to the same Google Account and check whether the codes reappear.
   - For Authy, try restoring access with the phone number used for Authy. If you used Authy backups, you may still need the backup password; that password or key usually cannot be recovered or reset.

4. If the old phone still has working codes, use it as the lifeline.
   - Use it to sign in to your main email account first.
   - In that email account's security settings, add a safe second sign-in method, such as backup codes, another authenticator, or a security key.
   - Then repeat this for your password manager, mobile network account, banking or financial accounts, and other important accounts.

5. Use each service's official fallback options.
   - On the sign-in screen, look for backup codes, use another method, security key, text or call code, or account recovery.
   - Do not guess codes repeatedly if the service warns that attempts are limited.
   - Once you regain access, go straight to security settings and add the new authenticator before signing out.

6. Check for signs that someone else may be in your accounts.
   - In your email account, look for unexpected password reset emails, new device sign-ins, changed recovery details, or new forwarding rules and filters.
   - If anything looks wrong, change the email password, sign out other sessions, and review recovery phone numbers and email addresses.
   - Then check the accounts that use that email address for password resets.

7. If you suspect a SIM swap or number port, treat it as urgent.
   - Warning signs include sudden "No Service", SIM change messages, porting messages, or being unable to receive calls or texts.
   - Contact your mobile network provider using its official website, app, or a known customer service number, and ask them to secure the account and check whether a SIM swap or number port has happened.
   - If money has been taken or fraud has occurred in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report it to Action Fraud through Report Fraud. In Scotland, contact Police Scotland by calling 101 unless there is an emergency.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose the perfect authenticator app today.
- You do not need to reorganise every login right now.
- You do not need to reset every password at once unless there are signs of compromise.
- You do not need to decide today whether to use an app, SMS, passkeys, or security keys long term.
- You can deal with low-value accounts after email, phone, password manager, banking, and work access are stable.

## Important reassurance
This can happen after updates, phone changes, backup changes, or app sign-in changes. Missing codes do not automatically mean your accounts are gone or hacked; moving slowly gives you the best chance of restoring access without wiping the remaining recovery routes.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise access and prevent irreversible mistakes. Work, banking, government, regulated, or high-security accounts may need their own support process or identity checks before two-step verification can be reset.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, professional security, or technical support advice. Use official support channels for each affected service, especially if you think someone else may be accessing your accounts.
