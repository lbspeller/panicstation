# What to do if… you notice chest discomfort plus new jaw, shoulder, or arm pain without a clear cause (UK)

## Short answer
Call **999** (or **112**) for an ambulance immediately; this specific combination of symptoms requires emergency medical assessment even if the discomfort feels mild.

## Do not do these things
- Do not **drive yourself to the hospital** or ask a friend to drive you unless the emergency operator explicitly instructs you to do so.
- Do not **wait to see if the symptoms pass**, even if you think it might just be indigestion, muscle strain, or anxiety.
- Do not **perform any physical activity**, such as walking up stairs, finishing chores, or pace the room.
- Do not **take any new medication or painkillers** to "test" the pain, with the exception of aspirin if advised.
- Do not **take someone else's heart medication**, such as GTN sprays or tablets, if they were not specifically prescribed to you.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all physical activity and sit on the floor** with your head and shoulders supported and your knees bent (the W-position).
   - This position reduces the workload on your heart and provides stability if you become lightheaded.
2. **Call 999 (or 112) immediately** and state clearly: "**I have chest discomfort with new pain in my [jaw / shoulder / arm]**."
   - Put your phone on **speaker mode** so you can talk to the operator while remaining still.
3. **Slowly chew and swallow one adult-sized aspirin tablet (300mg)** if you have one available and are not allergic.
   - Do **not** give aspirin to anyone under the age of 16.
   - If you are unsure about an allergy or have been told to avoid aspirin, wait for the ambulance.
4. **Unlock your front door** and, if another person is present, ask them to wait outside to flag down the paramedics.
5. **Ask a bystander to locate the nearest Automated External Defibrillator (AED)** if you are in a public place.
   - Do not go to find it yourself; stay in the W-position on the floor.
6. **Use your own prescribed angina medication** (such as GTN spray or tablets) if you have a pre-existing diagnosis.
   - Follow your specific medical instructions for dosing (typically one dose, wait 5 minutes, then a second dose if pain persists).
7. **Gather your current medications or a prescription list** to show the paramedics when they arrive.
   - Include the time your symptoms first started.
8. **Follow the operator's instructions for CPR** if the person becomes unresponsive and stops breathing normally.
   - The operator will talk you through the correct rhythm and depth for chest compressions.

## What can wait
- Deciding if this is "definitely" a heart attack; that is for the clinical team to determine.
- Calling your GP surgery or NHS 111, as they will likely refer you to 999 for these specific symptoms.
- Packing a hospital bag, finishing work tasks, or tidying the house.
- Notifying family members or employers until after the ambulance has been called and you are stabilized.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for people to feel like they are "making a fuss" or to mistake these symptoms for indigestion. Paramedics and A&E staff prefer to be called for a false alarm than to arrive too late. Hesitation is a normal response, but calling 999 is the safest action when pain spreads from the chest.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Emergency responders and hospital clinicians will provide the necessary diagnostic tests, such as an ECG and blood tests, to determine the cause of your symptoms.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and does not replace professional medical diagnosis. If you are experiencing these symptoms now, your priority is contacting emergency services via 999 or 112.
