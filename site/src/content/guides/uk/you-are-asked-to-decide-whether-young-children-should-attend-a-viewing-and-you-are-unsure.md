# What to do if… you are asked to decide whether young children should attend a viewing and you are unsure (UK)

## Short answer
You do not have to decide immediately. Request details from the funeral director about the setting, prepare the child with honest, non-euphemistic facts, and ensure they have a dedicated adult "anchor" and a clear exit plan.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use euphemisms like "sleeping" or "gone away" as young children may expect the person to wake up or return.
- Do not present the viewing as a duty or a way to make other adults feel better.
- Do not force a child to touch the body or move closer than they are comfortable with.
- Do not allow other family members to pressure or debate the decision in front of the child.
- Do not enter the room without a "leave anytime" signal that the child knows you will honor instantly.
- Do not let the child enter alone or with an adult who is too emotionally overwhelmed to focus on the child's needs.

## What to do now
1. **Request a safe delay to gather information and lower the pressure.** Tell the funeral director or hospital staff that you need time to consider the child's needs and cannot decide this minute.
   - Ask for a later time slot or an early morning appointment when the building is quiet.
2. **Obtain concrete sensory details about the room and the person's appearance.** Ask the funeral director exactly what the child will see, including whether the coffin is open, what clothes the person is wearing, and if there are any visible medical marks or changes.
   - Confirm if the room is cold (due to refrigeration) so you can explain the temperature to the child.
3. **Appoint a dedicated "anchor adult" for each child attending.** This person's sole responsibility is to monitor the child and leave the room with them immediately if the child uses their exit signal.
   - If you are the primary bereaved person, choose a trusted friend or relative who is less emotionally impacted to be this anchor.
4. **Prepare the child with a simple, factual preview of what death means.** Explain that the body has stopped working, does not feel pain or cold, and cannot breathe or move.
   - Use plain language: "Their heart has stopped, and they are dead. Their body is kept in a special cool room so it stays safe."
5. **Offer the child a range of "micro-choices" rather than a single yes or no.** Explain that they can choose to go to the building but stay in the waiting room, stand at the doorway, or go right up to the coffin.
   - Reassure them that changing their mind at the very last second is perfectly acceptable.
6. **Establish a silent "exit signal" that requires no explanation.** Agree on a hand squeeze or a specific word that means "I want to leave now."
   - Promise the child: "If you use the signal, we walk out immediately, and no one will be cross or ask why."
7. **Consult with the Medical Examiner or Coroner if the death was sudden.** If a post-mortem is required, viewing times may be restricted or specific conditions may apply; confirm these details before involving the child.
8. **Bring a small physical item to help the child feel involved.** A drawing, a letter, or a small toy can be placed in or near the coffin if the child wishes to "give" something as a goodbye.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if the child is attending the full funeral service yet.
- You do not need to justify your decision to extended family members who may have different cultural or personal views.
- You do not need to worry if this will be the child's "final memory"; children often balance these images with memories of the person alive.
- You do not need to find "perfect" answers to big questions about the afterlife right now; "I don't know" is a valid response.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel protective and hesitant. UK guidelines, including the 2026 RSHE curriculum in schools, increasingly recognize that including children in grief processes—when done with preparation and choice—can reduce their anxiety about the unknown. Your hesitation shows you are prioritizing their emotional safety.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate logistical and preparatory steps for a viewing only. It is not a substitute for long-term bereavement counseling or professional psychological support.

## Important note
If you are concerned about a child's reaction or if the death was particularly traumatic, contact a specialist service like Winston’s Wish or Child Bereavement UK for tailored advice. In an immediate emotional crisis, contact your GP or call 111.
