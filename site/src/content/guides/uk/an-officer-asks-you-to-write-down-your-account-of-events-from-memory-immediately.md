# What to do if… an officer asks you to write down your account of events “from memory” immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and request free legal advice before you write or sign any document. If you cannot access a solicitor immediately, do not attempt to "fill in gaps" or guess details; state clearly that you are not able to provide a written account at this time.

## Do not do these things
- Don't write an "on the spot" narrative or timeline just to satisfy the officer or stop the pressure.
- Don't include "best guesses" for times, distances, or the sequence of events.
- Don't sign any document you have not read slowly and confirmed to be 100% accurate.
- Don't allow an officer to paraphrase your words or "tidy up" your account on the page.
- Don't hand over your phone or personal notes to "refresh your memory" without legal advice.
- Don't assume you must provide a written account just because you are a witness rather than a suspect.

## What to do now
1. **Determine your legal status and why the account is being requested.** Ask the officer: "Am I a suspect or a witness? Am I under arrest, detained, or here voluntarily?"
   - If you are a suspect, you must be cautioned before being asked for an account.
   - If you are a witness, you still have the right to provide your statement at a later time when your memory is clearer.
2. **Request free and independent legal advice before writing anything.** Say: "I want to speak with a solicitor before I make a written statement or sign any account of events."
   - In a police station, ask for the duty solicitor. 
   - If at the roadside or a home visit for a voluntary interview, the police must still facilitate your right to legal advice if you are under caution.
3. **Provide a clear "holding line" if pressured for an immediate response.** Say: "I am not providing a written account from memory at this moment; I require legal advice first."
   - If they insist you sign a pocketbook or paper to "confirm you were asked," write only: "I request legal advice before making or signing a statement."
   - Add the date, time, and your signature only to that specific sentence.
4. **Listen carefully to the caution if one is given.** If the officer says, "You do not have to say anything...", this applies to written accounts as well as spoken words.
   - Confirm you understand the caution but reiterate your request for a solicitor before writing.
5. **Clarify that you are not refusing to cooperate, but are ensuring accuracy.** Say: "I am happy to cooperate, but I need to ensure my account is accurate, which requires legal advice and a clearer head."
6. **Identify yourself as a vulnerable person if applicable.** If you are under 18, have a mental health condition, or a learning disability, state this immediately and ask for an "appropriate adult."
   - Under PACE Code C, vulnerable persons and juveniles must have an appropriate adult present during interviews and when providing certain written statements.
7. **Record the officer's details and the time of the request.** Write down the officer's name, shoulder number, and station for your own records.
8. **If you are a witness, ask for the "witness care" information.** Request a copy of the Witness Charter or information on when you can provide a formal statement later.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a complete narrative of the event right now.
- You do not need to resolve "gaps" in your memory or provide exact timings today.
- You do not need to decide if you will eventually go to court or provide a full witness statement.
- You do not need to "prove" you have nothing to hide by writing while in shock.

## Important reassurance
It is scientifically normal for memory to be fragmented or "patchy" immediately after a high-stress event. Requesting a pause and legal advice is a standard procedural right in the UK and does not imply guilt or a lack of cooperation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate pressure of being asked for a written account. Long-term decisions regarding formal statements (Section 9 or Section 137 statements) or interview strategies should only be made after full consultation with a qualified solicitor.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. The rights of suspects and witnesses are governed by the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 (PACE) and its Codes of Practice. Procedures may differ in Scotland or Northern Ireland.
