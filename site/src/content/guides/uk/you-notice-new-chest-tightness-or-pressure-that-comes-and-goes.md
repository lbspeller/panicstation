# What to do if… you notice new chest tightness or pressure that comes and goes (UK)

## Short answer
Call **999** immediately for an ambulance if the tightness is happening now, lasts more than 15 minutes, or is accompanied by breathlessness, sweating, or pain spreading to your arms, neck, jaw, or back.

## Do not do these things
- Do not **drive yourself** or ask someone else to drive you to the hospital; wait for an ambulance with paramedics.
- Do not **exercise, walk around, or perform physical tests** to see if the symptoms return.
- Do not **wait for the next episode** to see if it gets worse if this is a new or unusual sensation for you.
- Do not **assume the cause** is indigestion, anxiety, or a muscle strain without a professional clinical assessment.
- Do not **take aspirin if you have a known allergy** or have been specifically told by a doctor never to take it.
- Do not **stop taking usual prescribed medications** unless instructed by the emergency operator or a clinician.

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 immediately if symptoms are active or severe.**
   - Request an ambulance if the pressure is **crushing, heavy, or vice-like**.
   - Call if the discomfort **spreads** to the arms, jaw, neck, back, or stomach.
   - Call if you feel **sick, sweaty, short of breath, or light-headed**.
2. **Adopt the "W" sitting position while waiting for help.**
   - **Sit on the floor** with your back and shoulders well-supported by a wall or sturdy furniture.
   - **Bend your knees** and keep your head and shoulders up to ease the strain on your heart.
3. **Chew one 300mg aspirin tablet if available.**
   - Do this **only if you are not allergic** to aspirin and it is within immediate reach.
   - **Chew the tablet slowly** before swallowing to help it enter the bloodstream faster.
4. **Follow the "5-minute rule" if you have prescribed GTN medication.**
   - **Take one dose** (spray or tablet) as soon as the tightness starts and rest.
   - **Wait 5 minutes**; if the pain persists, take a second dose.
   - **Call 999 immediately** if the pain has not gone 5 minutes after the second dose.
5. **Prepare your environment for paramedics.**
   - **Unlock the front door** so emergency services can enter without delay.
   - **Move pets** to a separate room and turn on outside lights if it is dark.
   - **Keep your phone close** and do not hang up if the operator asks you to stay on the line.
6. **Gather essential medical information.**
   - Have your **medication list or physical packets** ready to show the crew.
   - Identify any **allergies**, especially to medications or latex.
7. **Contact NHS 111 if symptoms have passed but were new today.**
   - If the tightness lasted only a few minutes and you feel okay now, you still require a **same-day urgent assessment**.
   - Tell the operator you had **"new, intermittent chest tightness/pressure"**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific cause (angina vs. heart attack vs. other) yourself.
- You do not need to notify your employer or cancel non-urgent appointments until you have been assessed.
- You do not need to look up survival statistics or similar stories online.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel hesitant about calling emergency services when symptoms come and go. Paramedics and doctors prefer to assess a "false alarm" than to treat a delayed, serious cardiac event. Sudden tightness is a physical signal that requires a professional response, regardless of whether it feels "severe" at every moment.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and escalation advice only. It does not replace clinical diagnosis or long-term management plans provided by the NHS.

## Important note
This information is for guidance in acute situations and does not constitute medical advice. If you suspect a heart attack or are in significant distress, call **999** immediately.
