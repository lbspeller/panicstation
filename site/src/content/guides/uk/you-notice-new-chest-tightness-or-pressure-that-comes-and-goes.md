# What to do if… you notice new chest tightness or pressure that comes and goes (UK)

## Short answer
Treat new chest tightness or pressure as potentially serious. If it is happening now, does not go away, keeps coming back, spreads to your arm, neck, jaw, back or stomach, or comes with breathlessness, sweating, sickness, light-headedness, collapse, or feeling very unwell, call **999** for an ambulance.

## Do not do these things
- Do not **drive yourself** to A&E if this could be heart- or lung-related.
- Do not exercise, rush around, or “walk it off” to test yourself.
- Do not wait for another episode if this is **new**, worsening, happening at rest, or keeps returning.
- Do not assume it is “just anxiety” or “just indigestion” if it feels unusual for you.
- Do not take new or extra medicines, alcohol, energy drinks, or recreational drugs to see if it passes; keep taking your usual prescribed medicines unless a clinician tells you not to.
- Do not ignore advice from **999**, **111**, or a clinician because the symptoms have eased.

## What to do now
1. **Check if you need 999 now.** Call **999** for an ambulance if any apply:
   - The tightness or pressure is happening now and does not go away.
   - It keeps coming back, feels tight, squeezing, heavy, or unusual for you.
   - It spreads to your arm, neck, jaw, back, shoulder, or stomach.
   - You feel short of breath, sweaty, sick, light-headed, faint, confused, very weak, or very unwell.
   - You have severe difficulty breathing, have collapsed, or someone with you is not responding normally.
   - You have diagnosed angina and the pain does not stop after rest and your prescribed angina medicine, or this episode is worse, different, more frequent, or happening at rest.

2. **If you call 999, make it easier for help to reach you.**
   - Sit and rest in the most comfortable position; keep activity minimal.
   - Unlock the door if you can do so safely, or ask someone else to.
   - Put pets away, keep your phone nearby, and stay on the line if told to.
   - Have your medication list, allergies, key conditions, inhalers, and prescribed **GTN** spray or tablets nearby.

3. **Use medicines only in the safer way.**
   - If you think this may be a heart attack and you are waiting for emergency help, take **300mg aspirin** if you have it; do not take aspirin if you are allergic to it, under 16, or have been told to avoid it.
   - If you have **prescribed GTN** for angina, use it only as you have been instructed.
   - Do not take someone else’s medicines.

4. **If symptoms are not severe right now but are new or recurrent today, get urgent same-day advice.**
   - Call **111** where that is your urgent-care route, or use your local urgent-care route if 111 is not available where you are.
   - Say clearly: “I have new chest tightness or pressure that comes and goes.”
   - If symptoms return, worsen, or you feel unsafe waiting, call **999**.

5. **Write down a short episode note if you can do it without delaying help.**
   - Time it started.
   - How long it lasted.
   - Whether it began at rest, with exertion, after eating, or during stress.
   - Where you felt it and whether it spread.
   - Any breathlessness, sweating, sickness, dizziness, faintness, palpitations, or unusual weakness.

6. **If you are alone, tell someone nearby.**
   - Call or message a trusted person to stay with you or be ready to let ambulance staff in.
   - Keep your phone charged and close.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to search symptoms online, compare stories, or do home “tests” such as stairs, press-ups, cold water, or checking whether exercise makes it worse.
- Work, messages, chores, and non-urgent appointments can wait until you have been assessed or advised what to do next.

## Important reassurance
It is common to doubt yourself when chest symptoms come and go. Getting urgent advice for **new** chest tightness or pressure is a sensible safety step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps to keep you safe and help you reach the right service. Later decisions about tests, referrals, medicines, or treatment may need assessment by a clinician.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical diagnosis or personal medical advice. If you think you might be having a medical emergency, call **999**.
