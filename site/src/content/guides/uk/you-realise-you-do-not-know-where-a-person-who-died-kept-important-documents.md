# What to do if… you realise you do not know where a person who died kept important documents (UK)

## Short answer
Stabilize the physical site by gathering visible paperwork into one container, then systematically check for professional contact details (solicitors or banks) who may hold the original will and deeds.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t discard any mail, receipts, or "boring" letters as they often contain reference numbers for lost accounts.
- Don’t send original documents (like the will or death certificate) through the post unless using a tracked service to a verified institution.
- Don’t attempt to guess digital passwords or "hack" into computers / phones, as repeated failed attempts may permanently lock the device.
- Don’t distribute any property or money until the valid will is found or the rules of intestacy are confirmed.
- Don’t share images of sensitive documents on social media or public forums when asking for help.

## What to do now
1. **Gather all visible documents from the immediate environment into a single, secure box.** 
   Scan desks, bedside tables, and common storage areas without "tidying" or re-filing; maintaining the original context (e.g., which papers were grouped together) can help identify their filing logic.

2. **Check for a "Solicitor’s Letter" or business cards in the home.** 
   **The fastest way to locate a will or deeds is often identifying the firm that handled the person's last house purchase or probate for a spouse.**
   - Look for letterheads from law firms, banks, or financial advisors.
   - Contact these firms directly and ask if they hold a "Last Will and Testament" or "Title Deeds" for the deceased.

3. **Search for "The National Will Register" (Certainty) to see if a will was registered voluntarily.**
   **This private database can often link you to the solicitor holding the document even if you have no record of the firm's name.**
   - You will need to provide the deceased's full name, date of birth, and date of death.

4. **Apply for a Special Circumstances Redirection with Royal Mail if you cannot access the property's post.**
   **This ensures that upcoming bank statements or policy renewals are sent to your address rather than sitting in an empty home.**
   - This application must be made in person at a Post Office or via post; it cannot be completed online.
   - You must provide a death certificate and proof that you are the executor or administrator.

5. **Use the "Tell Us Once" service for deaths in England, Scotland, or Wales.**
   **Once you have registered the death, this service notifies multiple government departments (DWP, HMRC, DVLA, Passport Office) in one action.**
   - This service is not available for deaths in Northern Ireland; you must contact the "Bereavement Service" in NI instead.

6. **Search official probate records if you suspect the will has already been processed.**
   **In England and Wales, you can search the government’s probate database; in Scotland, contact the Sheriff Clerk; in Northern Ireland, use the Probate Registry in Belfast.**
   - For England and Wales, copies of a proved will cost £16 (2026 fee).

7. **Verify property ownership via the relevant Land Registry for your jurisdiction.**
   **If you cannot find deeds, you can purchase an official copy of the title register to confirm who owns the property and if there is a mortgage.**
   - England and Wales: HM Land Registry.
   - Scotland: Registers of Scotland.
   - Northern Ireland: Land & Property Services (LPS).

## What can wait
- You do not need to find every single utility bill or minor receipt today.
- You do not need to close all bank accounts immediately; notifying them of the death is the first step.
- You do not need to decide on the sale of the house or distribution of heirlooms until the legal authority (Executor/Administrator) is officially confirmed.

## Important reassurance
It is common for paperwork to be disorganized or hidden, particularly if the person was unwell or very private. Most institutions (banks, insurers, and government bodies) have established "Bereavement Teams" specifically trained to help you reconstruct these records when the originals are missing.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for locating documents. It does not cover the legal process of probate, inheritance tax calculations, or resolving disputes between family members.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance only and is not legal advice. If you cannot find a will and the estate is complex, or if there is conflict regarding who should search for documents, consult a solicitor specializing in probate and estates.
