# What to do if… you find water collecting under a tiled floor and you suspect a leak beneath the tiles (UK)

## Short answer
Shut off the main internal water stopcock immediately, turn off any underfloor heating, and isolate the area to prevent slips or electrical contact.

## Do not do these things
- Don't run taps, showers, or appliances to "test" where the water is coming from.
- Don't switch on underfloor heating to "dry out" the floor, as this can worsen the leak or cause electrical shorts.
- Don't attempt to pry up tiles while the water is still active or while standing in a wet area.
- Don't use vacuum cleaners, dehumidifiers, or heaters in the immediate wet area if there is any chance water has reached electrical floor circuits.
- Don't ignore the leak if the water is warm; this indicates a leak on the hot water or heating system which may cause faster damage.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the immediate area.** Keep children and pets away from the wet tiles.
   - Treat the floor as an immediate slip hazard. 
   - Note if any tiles are loose, "crunchy," or "spongy," as these may move unexpectedly.
2. **Shut off the internal water supply.** **Locate your main internal stopcock (usually under the kitchen sink, in a utility room, or under the stairs) and turn it fully clockwise.**
   - Once fully closed, turn it back anti-clockwise by a half-turn to prevent the valve from seizing.
   - Open a cold tap to confirm the water flow has stopped.
3. **Turn off the boiler or water heater.** **Switch off your heating system and hot water to prevent the appliance from running while the water supply is isolated.**
   - If the leak is warm, the source is likely a hot water pipe or a central heating loop.
4. **Isolate the electricity (if safe).** **If water is near floor-level sockets, appliances, or underfloor heating controls, go to your consumer unit (fuse box) and switch off the relevant circuit.**
   - Only do this if you can reach the consumer unit from a completely dry position.
   - If there is any sign of water inside the consumer unit, or if it is buzzing or sparking, do not touch it. Call **105** for the network operator (Great Britain) or **03457 643643** (Northern Ireland).
5. **Report to the water company if necessary.** **If water is coming up from the floor before it reaches your internal stopcock, contact your local water supplier's emergency line.**
   - They are responsible for the communication pipe up to your property boundary.
6. **Notify others in shared buildings.** **If you live in a flat or terraced house, inform your neighbours or the building manager immediately.**
   - Water under floors often travels along joists and can emerge through ceilings in the property below.
7. **Document the evidence.** **Take clear photos or videos of the water appearing through the grout, any lifting tiles, and the general area.**
   - This is required for insurance claims and helps the plumber identify the likely source without unnecessary demolition.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a replacement floor or tiles today.
- You do not need to identify the exact pipe responsible; that is the role of a professional with leak detection equipment.
- You do not need to clean or scrub the grout until the leak is professionally resolved.

## Important reassurance
Hidden leaks are stressful because the damage is not immediately visible, but the most important step—stopping the flow of water—is already within your control. Once the stopcock is closed, the immediate threat of further flooding is paused.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Resolving a leak beneath tiles often requires a plumber or a specialist leak detection service to avoid unnecessary damage to the subfloor or heating systems.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and is not a substitute for professional plumbing or electrical advice. If you cannot safely access your stopcock or consumer unit, prioritize personal safety and call an emergency professional immediately.
