# What to do if… you suspect someone is trying to lure a child or vulnerable person nearby and you are at a safe distance (UK)

## Short answer
**Call 999 immediately** to report a suspected crime in progress and a potential threat to life. Maintain a safe distance and provide the police with precise location details and descriptions of the individuals and any vehicles involved.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront the suspect or attempt to "test" their intentions as this may cause them to flee with the victim or escalate to violence.
- Don’t shout or create a scene that could cause the child or adult at risk to panic or run into traffic.
- Don’t attempt to physically intervene or block a vehicle unless you are certain you can do so without increasing the danger to the victim or yourself.
- Don’t delay calling 999 because you are unsure of the person's motives; the police are trained to assess these risks.
- Don’t post photos, videos, or descriptions on social media, as this can alert the suspect or compromise future legal proceedings and police operations.

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 and state that you are witnessing a suspected abduction or luring attempt.**
   - Tell the operator your exact location using landmarks, street names, or a digital location app.
   - Specify if the victim appears to be a child or an adult at risk (vulnerable person).
   - Inform the operator that you are currently at a safe distance and observing.
2. **Move to a secure vantage point while keeping the individuals in sight.**
   - **Position yourself where you have a clear line of sight but are not easily noticed by the suspect.**
   - Use natural cover like a shop window, a parked car, or a doorway.
   - If you are in a managed space (like a shopping centre or station), alert on-site security or staff quietly while staying on the phone with the police.
3. **Provide a detailed "description of person" to the operator.**
   - **Focus on permanent or distinctive features that cannot be easily changed.**
   - Include height, build, hair colour, facial hair, and clothing (specifically outer layers like jackets or hats).
   - Note any distinctive items like bags, umbrellas, or pets they may be using to entice the victim.
4. **Identify and record vehicle details if applicable.**
   - **Prioritise the number plate (registration), make, model, and colour of any vehicle the suspect is using.**
   - Note the direction of travel if the vehicle begins to move.
   - Identify any unique damage, stickers, or logos on the vehicle.
5. **Observe the interaction without making your presence known.**
   - **Note specific "luring" behaviours, such as offering items, physical guiding, or the victim appearing confused or resistant.**
   - If the victim is an adult at risk, look for signs of coercion or the suspect taking advantage of their confusion.
   - Stay on the line until the police arrive or the operator tells you to hang up.
6. **Identify other witnesses in the immediate area.**
   - **Ask nearby adults to stay until police arrive to provide their own accounts.**
   - Do not let this distract you from keeping eyes on the suspect.

## What can wait
- Confirming the relationship between the individuals — the police will verify if they are family or strangers.
- Checking for "missing person" alerts online — your immediate report is more vital for a fast response.
- Reviewing your own phone footage — keep watching the scene; you can provide the digital evidence to officers later.
- Contacting social services or local councils — the police will initiate the multi-agency safeguarding protocols (MACPTs) once the immediate danger is managed.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of "spectator anxiety" or worry that you are overreacting. In the UK, police treat reports involving children and adults at risk with the highest priority. Providing a professional assessment is their responsibility; your responsibility is simply to report what you see so they can keep people safe.

## Scope note
This guide focuses on the immediate minutes of witnessing a potential luring or abduction from a distance. It does not cover long-term grooming investigations or how to talk to a victim after an event has concluded.

## Important note
This information is for immediate crisis stabilization and does not constitute legal advice. Under the 2026 Crime and Policing Bill, certain professionals in regulated activities have a statutory duty to report concerns of child sexual abuse. If you believe a life is in danger, always call **999**.
