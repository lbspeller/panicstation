# What to do if… your final paycheck is delayed and you are told it will be sorted later (UK)

## Short answer
Final pay should usually arrive on your normal payday. If that date has passed, ask payroll or HR today for the final payslip, an itemised breakdown, the confirmed payment date, and urgent escalation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not accept “we’ll sort it later” without a specific payment date, amount, method, and named person confirming it in writing.
- Do not sign anything saying you have been “paid in full” or agreeing to deductions you do not understand.
- Do not let it drift for weeks; employment tribunal time limits are strict and are usually 3 months minus 1 day for most claims, although some claims differ.
- Do not argue publicly on social media or review sites while you are trying to get payroll to fix it; keep messages calm and factual.
- Do not hand over original documents such as your contract or payslips. Keep originals and share copies or screenshots only.

## What to do now
1. **Anchor the issue to your normal payday.**  
   If your normal payday has not happened yet, ask payroll to confirm your final pay will be included in that run. If your normal payday has passed, treat it as overdue wages.

2. **Send one clear written message to payroll or HR.** Include:
   - Your full name, employee or payroll number, last working day, and normal payday
   - “Final pay not received” or “final pay appears short”
   - The amount you believe is missing, if you can estimate it
   - What the missing pay relates to, such as basic pay to your last day, holiday pay, notice pay, expenses, commission, or bonus
   - A request for the final payslip, an itemised breakdown of earnings and deductions, the confirmed payment date, and how it will be paid

3. **Turn “sorted later” into a concrete commitment.**  
   If the reply is vague, write back: “Please confirm the date it will be paid, the amount expected, and who has approved the payment.” If you are struggling to cover essentials, ask whether they can make an urgent manual payment rather than waiting for the next payroll run.

4. **Ask for the leaver documents.**  
   Request your P45, or written confirmation of when it will be issued, as well as the final payslip. This can help show whether your leaver process has been completed.

5. **Save proof while it is fresh.** Keep copies of:
   - Your contract or offer letter and any pay, bonus, commission, or holiday terms
   - Your last 2 or 3 payslips
   - Your bank entry showing the last payment you did receive
   - Timesheets, rota screenshots, overtime approvals, and expense approvals
   - Your resignation acceptance, dismissal letter, termination letter, or HR email confirming your leaving date
   - Every message where payroll or HR says the payment will be sorted later

6. **Escalate inside the employer if payroll will not give a clear date.**  
   Forward your written message to a senior HR contact, your former manager, or whoever handled your exit. Say: “This is now overdue. Please escalate for urgent payment and confirm today the amount due, the payment date, and the reason for any deduction.”

7. **Move to the formal track if it is still not resolved.**  
   Send a short written complaint or grievance, if the employer’s process allows it. State what you think is owed, when it should have been paid, what documents you need, and the date you want a reply. Contact Acas promptly if you may need to make a claim; you usually need to notify Acas before making an employment tribunal claim, unless an exemption applies, and time limits can be complicated.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a tribunal claim; first get the employer’s written position and a firm payment date.
- You do not need a perfect calculation before escalating; a reasonable estimate plus your records is enough to start.
- You do not need to argue about blame; keep the focus on dates, amounts, documents, and payment.
- You do not need to send every record at once; keep your evidence safe and share only what is needed.

## Important reassurance
Payroll delays do happen, but “we’ll sort it later” can become open-ended unless you pin down the amount, date, and person responsible. Asking for a final payslip, breakdown, and escalation is normal and reasonable.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stop the delay, get a clear answer, and protect your position. Later decisions about Acas, tribunal claims, court action, or representation may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, employment, or professional advice. Rights, routes, and deadlines depend on your situation and on what is unpaid, such as wages, holiday pay, notice pay, commission, bonus, or expenses.
