# What to do if… you find a locked safe or strongbox and suspect vital documents are inside (UK)

## Short answer
Do not force it open. Treat the safe or strongbox as part of the estate, secure it where it is, and let the executor, administrator, or person properly dealing with the estate arrange a recorded, controlled opening.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drill, pry, cut, or use heat; you can destroy wills, certificates, insurance papers, deeds, or other documents.
- Do not remove the safe from the property unless it is needed for immediate security and the person properly dealing with the estate knows.
- Do not keep trying random combinations; some locks can jam or lock out after repeated attempts.
- Do not share possible codes, keys, photos, or locations widely.
- Do not empty, rearrange, or throw away papers found near the safe.
- Do not let one person open it alone if there is any family tension or uncertainty about authority.

## What to do now
1. **Make the safe secure without turning it into a confrontation.** Leave it where it is if you can, keep the room or property secure, and note who is present.
2. **Record what you found before moving anything.** Take clear photos of the safe in place, the lock, hinges, make or model label, serial number, nearby keys, key tags, envelopes, notebooks, and any signs of damage or previous tampering.
3. **Tell the right estate person early.** If there is a will naming executors, contact them. If there is no known will, or nobody is clearly able to act yet, treat the safe as preserve-unopened for now until the executor, administrator, likely estate representative, or a solicitor can advise.
4. **Do one brief check for the obvious access route, then stop.** Check only obvious nearby places such as labelled keyrings, a file of house papers, a safe manual, a receipt, or a diary note mentioning a code. If nothing is obvious within a few minutes, stop searching.
5. **If you suspect crime or a serious dispute, preserve it as found.** If the safe looks forced, documents appear missing, or conflict is escalating, do not open it. For immediate danger or a crime in progress, call 999. For non-emergency concerns, report it online or call 101.
6. **Arrange a controlled opening if authority is clear.** Use a reputable safe locksmith or safe engineer, ask for identification and a written job record, and ask whether non-destructive opening is possible first.
7. **Have witnesses and keep a simple record.** Ideally have the executor, administrator, or person properly dealing with the estate and one other adult present. Photograph or video the opening and the contents as they first appear, then make a dated inventory of documents found.
8. **Protect documents as documents, not as personal possessions.** Put papers flat in a folder or envelope, label it with the date, time, address, and names of people present, and give it to the person properly dealing with the estate.
9. **If it is a bank or storage facility safe-deposit box, do not try workarounds.** Contact the bank or provider and ask what proof of death and estate authority they need.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who inherits anything.
- You do not need to value the contents today.
- You do not need to contact every bank or insurer just because you found the safe.
- You do not need to resolve probate, confirmation, letters of administration, or every estate form tonight.
- You do not need to argue about who was “meant” to have the papers; the immediate task is to preserve them and keep a record.

## Important reassurance
Finding a locked safe during bereavement can feel urgent and suspicious, but slowing down usually protects everyone. A secure, witnessed, recorded opening makes it more likely that any vital documents stay usable and less likely that anyone is accused of hiding or interfering with them.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a solicitor, the relevant probate or confirmation service, a bank bereavement team, the police, or a locksmith depending on what is found and where in the UK the estate is being handled.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, security, locksmith, or other professional advice. If authority is unclear, a will may be inside, the estate is disputed, or you suspect theft or tampering, preserve the safe unopened and get appropriate professional guidance before proceeding.
