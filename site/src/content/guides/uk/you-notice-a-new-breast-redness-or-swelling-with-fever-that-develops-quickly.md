# What to do if… you notice a new breast redness or swelling with fever that develops quickly (UK)

## Short answer
Get same-day medical advice. A breast that becomes red, swollen, warm, painful, or lumpy with fever or feeling unwell should be assessed quickly.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait several days if you have fever, fast-spreading redness, or feel unwell.
- Do not squeeze, deeply massage, or try to push out a lump.
- Do not start leftover antibiotics or someone else’s antibiotics.
- If you are breastfeeding, do not abruptly stop feeding or expressing because you are scared.
- Do not try to drain a lump yourself, including with a needle.
- Do not keep applying firm pressure if it makes pain, swelling, or throbbing worse.

## What to do now
1. **Get same-day NHS help.**  
   If you can contact your **GP practice**, ask for an **urgent same-day appointment** for “rapid breast redness or swelling with fever.” If you cannot get through or it is out of hours, use the urgent-care route for where you are: in England, use **NHS 111 online** or call **111**; in Wales, use **NHS 111 Wales**; in Scotland, call **NHS 24 on 111**; in Northern Ireland, contact your **GP out-of-hours service**.

2. **Use emergency care now if you have sepsis warning signs.**  
   Call **999** or go to **A&E** if you are acting confused, have slurred speech, have severe difficulty breathing or very fast breathing, your skin, lips, or tongue look blue, grey, pale, or blotchy, or you are rapidly getting worse. Do not drive yourself to A&E; call 999 or ask someone else to drive you.

3. **If you are breastfeeding or recently breastfeeding, keep milk moving gently while you arrange care.**  
   Continue feeding or expressing **as you normally would** if you can tolerate it. Use gentle milk removal only; avoid forceful pumping, hard pressing, or aggressive massage.

4. **Use comfort measures without overdoing them.**  
   Use a cold compress for pain, or gentle warmth briefly before feeding if it helps milk flow. Stop heat if it increases swelling, throbbing, or discomfort. Use pain or fever relief you can safely take, follow the packet instructions, and avoid anything you have been told not to use.

5. **Check for a lump or spreading redness without repeated pressing.**  
   Look once in good light and note whether there is a firm lump, swelling, nipple discharge, a skin break, a piercing, a recent injury, or redness that is spreading. Do not keep prodding the area.

6. **Write down the details the clinician will ask for.**  
   Note when symptoms started, your highest temperature, whether you are breastfeeding, pregnant, or recently postpartum, whether you have breast implants, and whether you have already taken any antibiotics or pain relief.

7. **If you are already on antibiotics, seek urgent review if you are worse or not improving.**  
   Do not push through worsening symptoms. Ask for urgent medical review if symptoms get worse at any point or do not start to improve within 48 hours of starting antibiotics.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the exact cause right now; the priority is same-day assessment and safe treatment.
- You do not need to decide about scans, drainage, or antibiotics before you have been assessed.
- You do not need to change breastfeeding routines beyond gentle feeding or expressing unless a clinician gives you specific advice.
- You do not need to do repeated self-checking or heavy pressing on the area.

## Important reassurance
This can feel frightening because it can come on fast and fever can make everything feel more intense. Many causes are treatable, and getting same-day help is the safest way to reduce risk and get relief sooner.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours. After you are assessed, you may need treatment such as antibiotics, breastfeeding support, imaging, or drainage, depending on what the clinician finds.

## Important note
This is general first-step information, not a diagnosis and not medical, therapeutic, legal, financial, or other professional advice. If you feel rapidly worse or have severe symptoms, treat it as urgent and use emergency services.
