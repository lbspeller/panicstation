# What to do if… you notice a new breast redness or swelling with fever that develops quickly (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your GP for an urgent same-day appointment or call NHS 111 immediately. Fast-developing breast redness with fever requires rapid assessment to rule out or treat serious infection or abscess.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait more than 24 hours to seek medical advice if you have a fever.
- Do not squeeze, poke, or try to drain any lumps yourself.
- Do not use old antibiotics or medication prescribed for someone else.
- Do not stop breastfeeding or expressing suddenly if you are currently lactating.
- Do not apply direct ice or excessive heat to the skin without a protective cloth layer.

## What to do now
1. **Request an urgent same-day GP appointment or use NHS 111.**
   - Tell the receptionist you have "rapid breast swelling, redness, and a fever."
   - If your GP is closed, call **111** or visit **111.nhs.uk** for urgent triage.
   - If you are not breastfeeding, emphasize this to the clinician as non-lactational mastitis always requires immediate antibiotic review.
2. **Seek emergency care if you show signs of sepsis.**
   - Call **999** or go to **A&E** if you experience:
     - Confusion, slurred speech, or disorientation.
     - Difficulty breathing or breathing very fast.
     - Skin, lips, or tongue that look blue, grey, pale, or blotchy (on brown/black skin, check palms or soles).
     - A rash that does not fade when a glass is rolled over it.
3. **Keep milk moving gently if you are breastfeeding.**
   - **Continue to feed or express** from the affected side as often as the baby wants or as you usually would.
   - **Check your baby’s positioning and attachment**; contact a midwife or the **National Breastfeeding Helpline (0300 100 0212)** for support.
4. **Identify specific sensory cues for the doctor.**
   - Check if the redness is wedge-shaped or spreading.
   - Note if the skin is dimpled like orange peel (peau d'orange) or if the nipple has changed shape.
   - Look for skin colour changes; on darker skin, look for areas that appear darker, purple, or brown rather than red.
5. **Apply temperature-based comfort measures carefully.**
   - **Use a warm, damp cloth** before feeding or expressing to help milk flow.
   - **Use a cold compress** (like a cloth soaked in cold water) after feeding for 10-15 minutes to reduce swelling and pain.
6. **Use simple pain relief as directed.**
   - **Take paracetamol or ibuprofen** if you can safely take them (check the packet) to manage fever and pain.
   - **Avoid aspirin** if you are breastfeeding.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause (infection vs. inflammation) yourself.
- You do not need to visit a pharmacy for "over-the-counter" cures; medical assessment is the priority.
- You do not need to worry about the safety of your milk; breastfeeding with mastitis is safe for the baby.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel very unwell or "flu-ey" when breast inflammation occurs. Symptoms often appear suddenly and can feel overwhelming, but most cases respond quickly once the correct treatment or drainage plan is started by a professional.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hours following the onset of symptoms. Long-term management, such as specific antibiotic courses or ultrasound referrals, must be directed by a clinician.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not replace professional medical diagnosis. If symptoms worsen rapidly, treat the situation as an emergency and contact 999.
