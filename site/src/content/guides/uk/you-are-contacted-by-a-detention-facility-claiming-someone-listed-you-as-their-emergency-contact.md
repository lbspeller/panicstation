# What to do if… you are contacted by a detention facility claiming someone listed you as their emergency contact (UK)

## Short answer
End the incoming call and verify it through an official number or website you find yourself. Do not share personal details, travel, or send money because of an unexpected call.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money for fees, fines, bail, release costs, transport, phone credit, vouchers, or crypto because of an unexpected call.
- Do not trust caller ID, a voicemail number, WhatsApp messages, or a reference number as proof the call is real.
- Do not give your address, date of birth, bank details, ID photos, or login codes to “confirm” you are the emergency contact.
- Do not travel to a police station, prison, immigration removal centre, or other site until you have verified the facility and the request.
- Do not agree to collect property, pass on messages, or contact other people until you know what is being asked and why.
- Do not post about it publicly while you are still unsure whether the contact is genuine.

## What to do now
1. **End the incoming call and take control of verification.**  
   Say: “I can’t discuss this on an incoming call. I’ll call back through the official switchboard.” Then hang up.

2. **Verify the organisation using an official route you locate yourself.**
   - If they said **police custody or police station**, call 101 or use the local police force’s published contact route and ask whether the contact was genuine.
   - If they said **prison**, use the official prison service pages for the part of the UK named by the caller: GOV.UK for prisons in England and Wales, the Scottish Prison Service for prisons in Scotland, or the Northern Ireland Department of Justice prisons pages.
   - If they said **immigration removal centre**, use GOV.UK’s immigration removal centre pages to find the centre’s published contact details, then call that number.

3. **Ask only for the minimum needed to confirm what is happening.**  
   Staff may be limited in what they can tell you. Ask:
   - “Has someone there listed this number as an emergency contact?”
   - “What name do you have for them, if you are able to share it?”
   - “What exactly are you asking me to do right now?”
   If they cannot confirm enough, ask: “Can you pass them a message to contact me through approved channels?”

4. **If you do not recognise the person, or you do not want involvement, say so clearly.**  
   Script: “I don’t know this person, or I can’t act as an emergency contact. Please remove my details and do not share my information with them.”

5. **If you are receiving unwanted contact from someone in prison, use the stop-contact route.**  
   Use the Unwanted Prisoner Contact Service if contact from a prisoner feels unwanted, harassing, coercive, or unsafe.

6. **If the call seems genuine and you are worried about immediate welfare, keep it to a welfare concern.**  
   Call the published switchboard for the prison or detention centre and say: “This is an urgent welfare concern about an individual.” Ask for the duty governor, safer custody team, welfare contact, or the equivalent contact for that setting.

7. **If the call involved money, secrecy, threats, or pressure to stay on the phone, treat it as a scam.**
   - Stop engaging.
   - Report it through Report Fraud if the service applies where you are; if it directs you to 101, use 101.
   - If you shared bank details or sent money, contact your bank immediately using the number on your card or in your banking app.

8. **If it is real and it is someone you know, keep your role small and practical at first.**  
   Ask what specific immediate action is needed, such as confirming a medical allergy, receiving a message, or arranging a scheduled property drop-off through the official process. If the detained person needs legal advice, they can ask for a solicitor; you do not need to fix that during the first call.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to become an ongoing support person.
- You do not need to contact their employer, landlord, family, or friends immediately.
- You do not need to travel, sign anything, share documents, or collect property right now.
- You do not need to keep answering calls from numbers you have not verified.

## Important reassurance
This can feel urgent and alarming even when the contact is genuine, and it is also a situation scammers can exploit. Slowing down and calling back through official routes is a normal, safe response.

## Scope note
These are first steps to verify the contact, prevent scams, and avoid accidental disclosures. Later decisions about visits, property, safeguarding, legal support, or ongoing contact may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information for immediate harm-prevention and verification, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Staff may be restricted in what they can disclose. If you believe a crime is happening now or someone is in immediate danger, call 999.
