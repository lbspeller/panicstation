# What to do if… your paycheck is late because of a payroll failure (UK)

## Short answer
Get the new payment date and the amount confirmed in writing, and ask for an emergency payment today if rent, food, travel, bills, or bank charges are at risk.

## Do not do these things
- Do not rely on a verbal “it’ll be sorted” with no date, amount, and person responsible in writing.
- Do not accept “when it’s fixed” as the only answer; ask for a specific date and what happens if the payment fails again.
- Do not quit, walk out, or stop turning up in the heat of the moment without checking the effect on your pay and job.
- Do not agree to be paid “off the books” or in a way that avoids a payslip, tax, or National Insurance.
- Do not take high-cost credit in a panic before asking your employer for an emergency payment and asking bill providers for breathing space.

## What to do now
1. **Check what should have happened.** Look at your contract, written statement, rota, timesheets, and last payslip. Note the agreed payday, the pay period, hours worked, expected gross pay, expected deductions, and expected net pay.

2. **Get the employer’s plan in writing today.** Ask payroll, HR, or your manager for a written message that states:
   - the reason your pay is late
   - the new payment date
   - the amount you should receive
   - whether it will be one payment or split payments
   - who to contact if it does not arrive

3. **Ask for an emergency payment if essentials are at risk.** Say plainly what you need and why, for example: “I need £X today because rent, food, travel, or a direct debit is due before the delayed wages arrive.” Ask whether they can make a manual bank transfer or another off-cycle payment, and ask who can authorise it.

4. **Check for simple payment problems.** If you recently changed bank accounts, check the details payroll holds. Ask whether the failed payroll run will be re-tried, whether any payment could be duplicated, and whether any advance will later be deducted from your next pay.

5. **Create a short paper trail for charges.** If late pay may cause overdraft fees, late rent fees, missed direct debits, or other charges, tell payroll or HR in writing now. Keep bank messages, landlord or bill-provider messages, and screenshots showing the charge and the date.

6. **Contact anyone who may take money before you are paid.** If a rent payment, loan, utility bill, childcare payment, travel cost, or direct debit is due, contact the provider before the payment fails where possible. Say your wages are delayed because payroll failed, give the expected new pay date, and ask for a short hold or fee waiver.

7. **Ask for and check your payslip.** In the UK, payslips must usually be provided on or before payday. When you can see it, check hours, rate, deductions, pension, tax, National Insurance, and any entry described as an advance, repayment, correction, or adjustment.

8. **If the pay is missing, short, or keeps changing, say that clearly in writing.** Write a short message saying what you believe is owed, why, and by when you need a response. Attach or list the evidence you have, such as your contract, payslip, rota, timesheet, or written pay message.

9. **If non-payment or underpayment means you think you have not been paid at least the National Minimum Wage, use the GOV.UK pay and work rights route.** You can also contact Acas for advice about pay and workplace rights.

10. **Watch the external time limit without trying to solve it tonight.** For an unpaid wages employment tribunal claim, the time limit can be as short as 3 months minus 1 day from the date you should have been paid. Note the missed payday in your calendar and get advice promptly if the money is not paid.

11. **If the employer may be unable to pay at all, protect your records.** If you hear about insolvency, administration, closure, or “no money to pay wages,” save copies of payslips, your contract, timesheets, rota, bank statements showing non-payment, and written messages from the employer. There may be a GOV.UK route to claim some money owed if the employer is unable to pay, but eligibility depends on your situation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to take legal action; first secure written confirmation and ask for an emergency payment if needed.
- You do not need to write a long complaint right now; a short written record and a clear request are enough.
- You do not need to argue about whether payroll, the bank, or a payroll provider caused the failure; focus on the payment date, the amount, emergency support, and evidence.
- You do not need to calculate every possible loss immediately; start by saving proof of any fees, missed payments, or extra costs as they happen.

## Important reassurance
Late pay can feel frightening because it threatens basic stability. Asking for a written date, an emergency payment, and help with charges is a normal and reasonable response to a payroll failure.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise today, reduce avoidable fees, and create a clear record. If the pay does not arrive, the amount is wrong, the delay repeats, or the employer may be unable to pay, later decisions may need specialist employment, debt, or union advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Your options can depend on your contract, work status, pay arrangements, and the reason payment failed.
