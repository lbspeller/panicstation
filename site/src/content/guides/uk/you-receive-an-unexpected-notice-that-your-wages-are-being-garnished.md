# What to do if… you receive an unexpected notice that your wages are being garnished (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the notice before paying anyone or sharing personal details. Get the issuing body, case reference, and deduction start date from payroll or the paperwork, then contact the issuer using independent official contact details.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay, click links, call numbers, or share ID details just because the message sounds official.
- Don’t assume payroll can cancel deductions because you ask them to; they usually need an official instruction from the court or enforcement body.
- Don’t ignore it because it feels wrong; acting quickly can protect your options.
- Don’t quit your job in panic; it usually does not remove the underlying order and can make the situation harder.
- Don’t guess which form or procedure applies until you know which UK nation and which issuing body are involved.

## What to do now
1. **Get the exact document payroll received.** Ask payroll for a copy of the order or notice, plus the date they received it and when deductions are due to start.

2. **Identify which UK route it appears to be.** Look for:
   - **England or Wales:** terms such as County Court, attachment of earnings order, N56, judgment, or CCJ.
   - **Scotland:** terms such as earnings arrestment, wage arrestment, charge for payment, charge to pay, decree, or sheriff officers.
   - **Northern Ireland:** terms such as Enforcement of Judgments Office, EJO, or attachment of earnings order.

3. **Write down the core details before you call anyone.** Note the issuing body, case number, creditor or claimant name, amount, deduction start date, and any deadline for replying or objecting.

4. **Verify the order using independent official contact details.** Use an official court or government website rather than contact details in an unexpected message. Ask: “Does this case number exist, what order has been made, and what is the deadline for me to respond?”

5. **Ask how you were notified about the original case.** Ask for copies of the judgment, decree, attachment of earnings order, earnings arrestment paperwork, or EJO order, and ask what address or contact route was used for the earlier papers.

6. **If you did not know about the case, say that clearly and ask about the challenge route.**
   - In **England and Wales**, ask the court about applying to set aside a default judgment if you never received the claim or judgment papers. Form N244 is commonly used for an application notice.
   - In **Scotland**, ask for advice quickly if you did not receive the charge for payment, charge to pay, decree, or Debt Advice and Information Package.
   - In **Northern Ireland**, ask the EJO what review, objection, or court route may apply if you did not know about the judgment or enforcement.

7. **If deductions may start before the issue is sorted, ask what can pause them.** Filing a form or making a complaint may not stop deductions by itself. Ask the court, EJO, or adviser whether you need an urgent suspension, stay, review, objection, or variation request.

8. **If the debt may be yours but the deduction is unaffordable, ask about reducing, suspending, or varying the deduction.**
   - In **England and Wales**, ask the court or a debt adviser whether you need to reply on form N56, apply to reduce or vary an existing attachment of earnings order using N244, or deal separately with the underlying CCJ.
   - In **Scotland**, ask a debt adviser what options may apply to an earnings arrestment and whether the correct notices were served.
   - In **Northern Ireland**, ask the EJO how to give up-to-date income and spending details and whether the order can be reviewed.

9. **Check the two common mistake points.**
   - **Old address:** ask what address was used and update your address with the court, creditor, or enforcement body.
   - **Wrong person or mistaken identity:** compare the full name, address, date of birth if shown, employer, and debt details. Tell payroll and the issuer promptly if the order may not be for you.

10. **Get free debt or legal advice before sending a form if you can do so without missing a deadline.** Say: “My employer has received an order to deduct from my wages, but I did not know about the case. I need to verify the order and find the quickest way to challenge, suspend, or vary it.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to dispute the whole debt or negotiate payment; first confirm what order exists.
- You do not need to explain your full personal situation to your employer; payroll mainly needs official documents and updates.
- You do not need to make a panic payment to a collector until you have confirmed the issuer, case reference, and whether the debt is yours.
- You do not need to solve every debt at once; focus on this order, any deadline, and keeping essential bills covered.

## Important reassurance
A surprise wage deduction notice can happen because papers went to an old address, the debt was misidentified, or a judgment was made before you knew about it. The next step is not to panic or argue with payroll; it is to verify the order, get the documents, and use the correct route for your UK nation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the situation, check whether the order is real, and avoid irreversible mistakes. Later decisions may need debt advice, legal advice, or help from the relevant court or enforcement body.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. UK processes differ between England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, and the right route depends on the type of debt and order.
