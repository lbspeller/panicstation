# What to do if… you return to your vehicle and something looks “off” as if someone may have accessed it (UK)

## Short answer
Create immediate physical distance by moving to a well-lit, populated area and observe the vehicle from a safe vantage point. Call 999 if you believe a crime is in progress or you feel physically threatened; otherwise, report the incident via 101 or online.

## Do not do these things
- Do not enter the vehicle or touch the door handles if you suspect someone is still inside or nearby.
- Do not confront anyone observed loitering near the vehicle.
- Do not touch or move unfamiliar objects found on or inside the car, such as wires, bags, or devices.
- Do not attempt to drive if there are signs of mechanical tampering, such as cut lines, loose wheels, or a strong smell of fuel.
- Do not clean the interior or "tidy up" before the police have advised you on evidence preservation.
- Do not ignore your "gut feeling" that the situation is unsafe; prioritize your physical person over the vehicle.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe pause point immediately.** Walk away from the vehicle toward a staffed building, shop entrance, or a well-lit area with other people.
2. **Observe from a distance to confirm the state of the vehicle.** Look for visible signs of entry without approaching the car.
   - Smashed glass on the ground or seat.
   - A door or boot that is not fully closed.
   - Interior lights that are currently illuminated.
   - Anyone watching the car or walking away as you arrived.
3. **Call 999 if the threat is immediate.** Use this number if:
   - You see someone inside or exiting your car.
   - You believe you are being followed or watched.
   - The crime has "just happened" (within the last few minutes) and the suspect may be nearby.
   - If you cannot speak, call 999 and press "55" when prompted to indicate an emergency.
4. **Report the incident via 101 or online for non-emergencies.** If the vehicle was clearly accessed earlier and there is no immediate danger, use the non-emergency route to obtain a Crime Reference Number (CRN).
5. **Request a safety escort if in a managed car park.** Ask security staff or a trusted person to accompany you back to the vehicle once you have confirmed it is safe to approach.
6. **Perform a "no-touch" visual inspection with your escort.** Check the exterior for tampering while staying outside the vehicle.
   - Verify if your keys or fob still function correctly.
   - Check if the central locking appears to have been jammed (electronic interference).
   - Look for items that do not belong to you inside the cabin.
7. **Secure your personal data and property.** If the vehicle is safe to enter but has been searched, check for the absence of:
   - Vehicle logbook (V5C) or insurance documents.
   - House keys or garage remotes.
   - Electronic devices or wallets.

## What can wait
- You do not need to conduct a full inventory of every missing item right now.
- You do not need to contact your insurance company until you are in a secure, private location.
- You do not need to decide on permanent repairs or lock replacements in this moment.
- You do not need to review dashcam or CCTV footage immediately; the police or car park operators will handle this request.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sudden surge of adrenaline, shakiness, or "tunnel vision" when you find your property has been disturbed. This reaction is a standard physiological response to a perceived threat. Taking a moment to step away and breathe in a safe environment is the most effective way to regain clarity.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate physical safety and the first steps of reporting. It does not cover forensic evidence collection, insurance claim procedures, or long-term vehicle security upgrades.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. In any situation where you feel your life or safety is at risk, contact 999 immediately.
