# What to do if… someone you are messaging suddenly pressures you to send nude photos to build trust (UK)

## Short answer
Do not send nude, semi-nude, or sexual images to prove trust. Stop the conversation, keep the useful details if you safely can, and use blocking, reporting, or specialist support before replying again.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send nude, semi-nude, or sexual images to calm them down, prove trust, or keep the conversation going.
- Don’t pay money, send gift cards, or agree to one last photo if they escalate into threats.
- Don’t negotiate, apologise, or argue to win them over if you feel pressured.
- Don’t share extra personal details, such as your address, workplace, school, family details, or other social accounts.
- Don’t meet them in person to sort it out.
- Don’t forward or post any intimate image, especially if anyone in it may be under 18.
- Avoid deleting the chat or your account before getting help if you may want to report it. If you already deleted things, you can still get help with what you remember.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and name the pressure:** treat a request for nude photos “to build trust” as a red flag for coercion, grooming, or sextortion. You do not owe proof.
2. **Stop the flow:** send one short boundary message, or send nothing, then stop replying.
   - Example you can copy: “No. Don’t ask me for that again.”
3. **Keep useful details without spreading images:** save the username, profile link, platform name, messages, dates, times, payment details if any, and any threats. Screenshot messages if you can do that safely, but do not save, copy, forward, or share nude images of anyone who may be under 18.
4. **Block and report in the app:** use the platform’s report or safety tools for sexual pressure, harassment, blackmail, intimate image abuse, or child sexual exploitation if relevant. Then block the account.
5. **If they already have an intimate image of you, or claim they do:**
   - Do not pay or comply.
   - If you are 18 or over, consider StopNCII.org to help prevent sharing on participating platforms.
   - If you are 18 or over and in the UK, contact the Revenge Porn Helpline for support and takedown guidance.
6. **If you are under 18, or the person targeted is under 18:**
   - Tell a trusted adult now if you can.
   - You can report online sexual abuse or worrying online communication to the NCA CEOP Safety Centre.
   - If a sexual image or video of you has been shared, or you are worried it might be shared, use Report Remove from Childline and the Internet Watch Foundation.
7. **If you feel at immediate risk of harm:** call 999 now. If it is not immediate but you are scared, being blackmailed, or want police help, contact police on 101 or through your local police force.
8. **If money, gift cards, or financial demands are involved:** in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, you can report financially motivated sextortion through Report Fraud.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to confront them, warn others, make a public post, or explain yourself.
- You do not need to write a perfect report before asking for help.
- You do not need to prove whether they are real, whether they like you, or what their motives are.
- You do not need to make a long-term decision about the relationship or account right now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shocked, embarrassed, frozen, or unsure is a common response to sudden sexual pressure. Someone using trust language to push for nude photos is trying to move you past your own boundary. You are allowed to stop immediately without giving a reason.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and reduce harm. Later decisions about reporting, takedown requests, legal options, safeguarding, or ongoing contact may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information for a stressful moment, not legal, medical, therapeutic, safeguarding, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
