# What to do if… you suddenly feel unsteady on your feet or unusually clumsy without a clear reason (UK)

## Short answer
Sit or lie down somewhere safe now to reduce the chance of a fall. If this came on suddenly and is new or unexplained, especially if you are falling over, cannot walk normally, or have any stroke-like symptoms, call 999 and ask for an ambulance.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive, cycle, use machinery, or try to push through it to get somewhere.
- Do not use stairs, ladders, or carry a baby or heavy items while you feel unsteady.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you are worried this could be a stroke or another emergency; call 999.
- Do not take extra sleeping tablets, opioids, sedatives, or alcohol to try to steady yourself.
- Do not try to prove it is fine by doing balance tests, long walks, or exercise.
- Do not eat or drink if you are very drowsy, confused, or having trouble swallowing.

## What to do now
1. **Make falling much less likely immediately.**  
   Sit on the floor or in a firm chair with back support. Move nearby hazards away, such as bags, rugs, trailing cables, or sharp objects. If you feel faint, lie down.

2. **Check for stroke signs.**  
   Use FAST:
   - **Face:** one side drooping, or a new uneven smile.
   - **Arms:** weakness or numbness, especially on one side.
   - **Speech:** slurred speech, confusion, or trouble finding words.

   Also treat sudden trouble walking, falling over, sudden dizziness with loss of balance, sudden vision change, sudden severe headache, or new confusion as urgent. If any of these are present, call 999 now and say you are worried about a stroke. Call even if the symptoms improve.

3. **If you cannot walk safely, treat it as urgent.**  
   If you need support to stand or walk, keep stumbling or falling, or the unsteadiness feels markedly abnormal for you, call 999 or get urgent NHS advice now rather than waiting to see if it settles.

4. **If you are alone, make it easier for help to reach you.**  
   Keep your phone with you, sit where you can be heard, and unlock the front door if you can do this safely. If you are not calling 999, ask someone to come and stay with you while you get advice.

5. **Note the start time.**  
   Write down or text yourself when you were last definitely normal and when the unsteadiness or clumsiness started. This is useful if a stroke or another urgent cause needs to be considered.

6. **If it is mild or improving but still unusual, get same-day advice.**  
   Use NHS 111 online or call 111. They can tell you where to go next and how urgently. If symptoms get worse while waiting, call 999.

7. **Have a few key details ready without deep digging.**  
   Note any new or changed medicines, recent illness, alcohol or drug use if relevant, diabetes or other long-term conditions, and whether there was any fall or head injury. Bring your medicines or a list if you are told to attend a service.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to decide by yourself whether this is for a GP, urgent treatment centre, A&E, or ambulance; 999 or NHS 111 can direct you.
- You do not need full records before seeking help; start time, symptoms, and a medication list are enough for the first contact.
- You do not need to explain it perfectly before calling. Say what changed and whether you can walk safely.

## Important reassurance
Sudden unsteadiness or clumsiness can feel strange and frightening. Taking it seriously, sitting down, and getting urgent help when walking or stroke-like symptoms are involved is a practical safety step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate risk and get the right level of urgent care. Later testing, diagnosis, treatment, and follow-up decisions may need an in-person assessment and specialist medical help.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not a diagnosis and is not medical or professional advice. If symptoms are sudden, severe, worsening, or you are worried for any reason, seek urgent medical help.
