# What to do if… you receive an urgent message asking you to buy gift cards or make a payment for work (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all interaction with the message and do not purchase gift cards or initiate any payments. Verify the request through a known, official company channel and report the attempt to your IT or security department.

## Do not do these things
- Don't purchase gift cards, vouchers, or cryptocurrency for work purposes.
- Don't share images of card backs, serial numbers, or activation codes.
- Don't click links for invoices, shared files, or payment portals within the message.
- Don't reply to the message to ask for confirmation of identity.
- Don't keep the request secret, even if the sender claims the matter is confidential.
- Don't use contact details provided within the suspicious message for verification.

## What to do now
1. **Stop any transaction currently in progress immediately.**
   - Close banking applications or browser tabs used for the payment.
   - Step away from retail counters if you are at a point of sale.
2. **Verify the request using a trusted, independent contact method.**
   - Call the sender on their known internal extension or verified mobile number.
   - Speak to the person face-to-face if possible.
   - Assume the request is fraudulent until voice or in-person confirmation is received.
3. **Report the incident to your internal IT or security team.**
   - Forward suspicious emails as an attachment to your security desk.
   - Provide screenshots of text, WhatsApp, or Teams messages to your manager.
4. **Notify your finance or accounts department.**
   - Inform them of the impersonation so they can monitor for related payroll or vendor fraud.
5. **Forward suspicious emails to the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC).**
   - Use the address **report@phishing.gov.uk** for email reports.
6. **Forward suspicious text messages (SMS) to 7726.**
   - This service is free and alerts mobile providers to the scam.
7. **Contact your bank and the gift card issuer if money has been sent.**
   - Ask your bank to stop or reverse the transfer.
   - Contact the brand on the gift card to request a balance freeze.
   - Record the time of contact and any reference numbers provided.
8. **File an official report with the appropriate national authority.**
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland: Contact **Report Fraud** at `reportfraud.police.uk` or call **0300 123 2040**.
   - In Scotland: Report the incident to **Police Scotland** by calling **101**.
9. **Document the details of the communication.**
   - Save the sender's address, the timestamp, the specific request, and any account details provided.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine how the sender obtained your contact information right now.
- You do not need to respond to the scammer to tell them you know it is a scam.
- You do not need to perform a full technical audit of your personal devices immediately.

## Important reassurance
Impersonation scams are engineered to exploit professional diligence and a desire to be helpful. Receiving a convincing message is a common occurrence and does not indicate a personal or professional failing. Taking time to verify is the standard security procedure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying and reporting workplace impersonation scams. It does not cover internal disciplinary procedures, long-term technical remediation, or legal recovery of funds.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Reporting processes and the likelihood of fund recovery depend on individual bank policies, the timing of the report, and the specific payment methods used. Always follow your employer's specific data security and fraud reporting policies.
