# What to do if… a person who died left minor children and you need to arrange urgent care (UK)

## Short answer
Make sure the children are physically safe and with a safe, known adult. Do not make a permanent or disputed “custody” move until you have checked who has parental responsibility or equivalent legal authority, and whether a safe parent or existing order already covers what should happen now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not move the children to a new address unless it is needed for immediate safety, or someone with clear legal authority has agreed and you have written down who agreed.
- Do not rely on verbal claims such as “I’m the guardian” without seeing a will, court order, parental responsibility evidence, or advice from children’s social care or a family-law professional.
- Do not turn this into a confrontation at the home, school, hospital, or funeral setting; keep the focus on where the children are safe tonight.
- Do not sign anything you do not understand, especially anything that sounds permanent, like adoption, special guardianship, or giving up parental responsibility.
- Do not post the children’s details, location, school, family dispute, or documents in social media posts or group chats.
- Do not promise a child where they will live permanently while adults are still checking authority and safety.

## What to do now
1. **Check where the children are and whether they are safe right now.**  
   If a child is in immediate danger, call **999**. If there is no immediate danger, get clear facts: where each child is, who is with them, whether they have medication, food, clothes, keys, phone access, and who is due to collect them.

2. **Keep the children with the safest familiar adult for the shortest necessary pause.**  
   The first aim is not to decide permanent care. The first aim is a safe pause: a known adult, a known address, no unnecessary travel, and no handover to someone whose authority or safety is unclear.

3. **Work out who already has authority to make decisions today.**  
   Ask calmly for:
   - the name and contact details of any surviving parent;
   - whether that parent has parental responsibility or equivalent legal authority;
   - any Child Arrangements Order, residence order, special guardianship order, kinship care paperwork, or other court order;
   - any will or written appointment naming a guardian;
   - the child’s school, GP, nursery, or hospital contact if they are involved today.

4. **Treat a will appointment as important, but not as an instant handover.**  
   A guardian named in a will may be relevant, but the effect can depend on where in the UK the child is and whether someone else already has parental responsibility or equivalent authority. If anyone disputes it, pause the handover and get advice before moving the child.

5. **If there is a safe, reachable parent with clear authority, make a calm handover plan.**  
   Agree who is collecting the child, from where, at what time, and who the school, nursery, hospital, or temporary carer should contact. Write down the plan and keep it factual.

6. **If no safe parent is available, or authority is unclear, contact local council children’s social care urgently.**  
   Ask for the children’s social care or safeguarding team for the area where the child is. If it is evening, night, a weekend, or a bank holiday, ask for the out-of-hours emergency duty team. Say clearly that a parent or carer has died, minor children are affected, and an urgent safe-care decision is needed.

7. **Tell school, nursery, hospital, or childcare only what they need for today.**  
   Give one safe adult contact, say who is collecting the child today, and ask them not to release the child to anyone else unless the agreed contact or children’s social care confirms it.

8. **If adults are arguing and the child’s location cannot safely wait, use urgent official routes.**  
   Contact children’s social care, the police if there is immediate risk, or an urgent family-law adviser. The exact court route depends on where in the UK the child lives. In England and Wales, urgent child-arrangements cases may be able to proceed without a MIAM in limited circumstances, and urgent same-day or short-notice court help is usually for exceptional urgency or safety risks.

9. **Write a simple timeline and keep documents private.**  
   Note who told you about the death, when, where the children were, who collected them, any safety worries, and the names and phone numbers of adults involved. Keep copies or photos of documents only if necessary, and do not circulate them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide permanent custody today.
- You do not need to sort inheritance, trusts, benefits, possessions, or long-term schooling in the first hours.
- You do not need to settle old family arguments or decide who “deserves” to care for the children.
- You do not need to update every relative today; keep communication limited to people needed for the children’s immediate safety.
- You do not need to tell the children every adult detail; they need simple, truthful, age-appropriate information and calm care.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for adults to feel shocked, protective, and pressured after a sudden death involving children. A careful first response is usually to keep the children safe and stable, avoid rushed handovers, check legal authority, and use children’s social care or urgent legal routes if the situation is unclear or disputed.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours and days only. Later decisions about where the children live, guardianship, parental responsibility, special guardianship, kinship care, or court applications may need specialist family-law advice and support from children’s services.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice, medical advice, therapeutic advice, financial advice, or professional safeguarding advice. If a child is in immediate danger, call 999. If you are unsure who can lawfully make decisions or where a child should stay tonight, contact local council children’s social care, their out-of-hours emergency duty team, or an urgent family-law adviser before making a disputed move.
