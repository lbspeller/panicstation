# What to do if… a teacher, coach, or mentor asks you to communicate through secret or disappearing messages (UK)

## Short answer
Do not continue the secret or disappearing-message channel if you can avoid it safely. Tell a safe adult today, such as a parent, carer, trusted adult, or the safeguarding or child protection lead at your school, college, club, or organisation. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to keep the communication “just between us.”
- Do not move to a private app, vanishing mode, disappearing messages, or a hidden account because they asked.
- Do not meet them alone to “clear it up” or “talk privately.”
- Do not delete chats, photos, usernames, call logs, or message notifications because they ask you to.
- Do not confront them directly if you feel pressured, frightened, or unsure.
- Do not assume you need proof before telling someone.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.**  
   Stop replying for now if you can do that safely. If you feel safer doing so, mute or block the account, turn off notifications, or move away from the device. If stopping suddenly could make things worse, send no more than a neutral delaying message such as: “I can’t talk now.”

2. **Keep what is already there, if it is safe.**  
   Do not search for more messages or try to investigate. If the messages might disappear and your device is private, save a small amount showing the account name, date or time, and the request for secrecy, disappearing messages, deleting chats, or moving apps. If saving anything could put you at risk, skip this step.

3. **Tell the safeguarding or child protection lead.**  
   Contact the safeguarding or child protection lead at the school, college, club, tutoring service, faith group, sports club, or organisation connected to the adult. In England this role is often called the Designated Safeguarding Lead or DSL; elsewhere, ask reception, the office, or another staff member: “Who is the safeguarding or child protection lead?”  
   Say plainly: “A teacher, coach, or mentor asked me to use secret or disappearing messages and keep it private. I’m worried.”

4. **Use a different route if the person involved is the safeguarding lead.**  
   Tell the headteacher, principal, club welfare officer, organisation manager, chair of governors, proprietor, or another senior person. Ask them to follow their safeguarding process for concerns about an adult working with children or young people.

5. **Tell a safe adult outside the organisation as well.**  
   Tell a parent, carer, relative, friend’s parent, youth worker, or another adult you trust. You can start with: “I need you to listen first. I’m not in trouble. An adult asked me to use secret messages and I need help.”

6. **Get specialist help if you cannot tell someone locally.**  
   If you are under 19, contact Childline on 0800 1111. If you are an adult worried about a child, contact the NSPCC Helpline on 0808 800 5000 or use the contact options on their website. For worrying online communication, the CEOP Safety Centre is another specialist route. If a child may be at risk and local safeguarding routes are not enough, a trusted adult can also contact children’s social care through the local council.

7. **Act fast if contact escalates.**  
   If they threaten you, try to arrange a private meeting, turn up in person, pressure you to send images, or you feel unsafe right now, call 999. If it is not an emergency but you need police help, call 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to call it grooming, abuse, or misconduct.
- You do not need to make a formal complaint before asking for help.
- You do not need to write a full timeline now; the key facts are who it was, what they asked, where the messages happened, and roughly when.
- You do not need to confront the person, warn everyone, or investigate other people’s experiences.

## Important reassurance
Feeling confused, embarrassed, loyal, or worried about getting someone in trouble is common when the person is trusted or respected. Asking a child or young person to use secret or disappearing messages is a serious boundary concern, and it is enough to ask for help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: pause the private channel, keep yourself safer, and get the concern to people who can respond. Later decisions may need safeguarding, legal, police, medical, or specialist support, but you do not have to work that out alone right now.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, safeguarding, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If any step feels unsafe, prioritise getting to a safer place and contacting a trusted adult or specialist helpline.
