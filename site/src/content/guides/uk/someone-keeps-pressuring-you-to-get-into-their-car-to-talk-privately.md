# What to do if… someone keeps pressuring you to get into their car to talk privately (UK)

## Short answer

Do not get into the car. Move toward a busy, staffed place and get another person involved immediately. If you feel trapped, followed, blocked, grabbed, or at immediate risk, call 999.

## Do not do these things

- Do not agree to “just talk privately” in their car, in a car park, or anywhere less visible.
- Do not keep negotiating your sexual boundary to calm them down.
- Do not let them choose your route home, your pickup point, or your transport.
- Do not go somewhere empty to avoid “making a scene”.
- Do not go straight home if you think they may follow you there.
- Do not worry about being polite if politeness is making you less safe.

## What to do now

1. **Move toward other people now.** Go into a shop, venue, hotel lobby, petrol station, pharmacy, station concourse, or anywhere with staff, cameras, and other people.

2. **Say the problem out loud to staff or another safe-looking person.** Use a plain sentence such as: “I need help. This person keeps pressuring me to get into their car and I do not want to go with them.”

3. **Make the waiting place safer.** Stand near staff, security, a till, a queue, or a family group. If there is door staff or venue security, ask them not to let the person wait with you, walk you out alone, or follow you to your ride.

4. **Call or message someone while you are still around other people.** Ask them to stay on the phone, come to you, meet you inside, or track your live location. Arrange your own way home that does not involve the person, even if it changes your plans.

5. **If they block your path, grab you, keep following you, try to force the conversation, or wait by the exit, call 999.** If you are on a mobile and cannot speak safely, call 999 and try to make any response you can, such as whispering, coughing, tapping, or pressing 55 if prompted.

6. **Change the setup, not the argument.** Ask staff to call you a taxi, let you wait inside, move you to a staff-visible area, or help you reach a safer pickup point. If you drove there, ask staff, security, or someone you trust to walk with you to your car.

7. **If you think they might follow you, do not lead them to your home.** Stay in a staffed place first, then leave with someone you trust or by transport they cannot control.

8. **If anything threatening, sexual, or physical has already happened and you want support, contact specialist help when you are somewhere safer.** Sexual assault referral centres can provide medical, practical, and emotional support, and you can usually contact one without reporting to the police.

9. **If you may want support or to report later, keep only what is easy and safe to keep.** Save messages, note the time and place, and only if it is safely visible note the car make, colour, or registration. You do not need to decide anything else tonight.

## What can wait

You do not need to decide now whether to report this, confront them, explain yourself better, reply to their messages, apologise, or work out what the relationship means. The immediate job is only to get out of their control, get around other people, and get somewhere safer.

## Important reassurance

A person pushing for privacy right after you set a sexual boundary is giving you useful safety information. You are allowed to act on that without proving anything, debating it, or being “fair” to them in the moment.

## Scope note

These are first steps only. Later decisions about reporting, support, contact, complaints, or any relationship consequences can be made after you are somewhere safer and more settled, and may need specialist help.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If there is immediate danger, call 999. If something sexual happened without your consent, or you are not sure, specialist support is available even if you do not want to involve the police.
