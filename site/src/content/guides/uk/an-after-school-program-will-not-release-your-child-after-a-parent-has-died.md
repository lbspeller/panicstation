# What to do if… an after-school program will not release your child after a parent has died (UK)

## Short answer
Ask the after-school setting to keep your child safely supervised while the person with parental responsibility, or the adult who normally has care of the child, gives a temporary written pickup instruction for today and the collector brings photo ID.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign as the parent who died or use their phone, email, app, or account to approve pickup.
- Do not pressure staff to release your child without checks; they may be following safeguarding procedures.
- Do not send a friend or relative to collect without telling the setting first and asking what ID they need.
- Do not hand over your only original documents; offer a copy, a photo, or ask for any original to be copied and returned immediately.
- Do not argue in front of your child if you can avoid it; ask to speak to the manager or safeguarding lead quietly.

## What to do now
1. **Ask staff to keep your child safely supervised while this is sorted.**  
   Say: “Please keep my child indoors, supervised, and not released to anyone else while we confirm today’s collection.”

2. **Ask for the manager or safeguarding lead.**  
   Ask: “What do you need for a safe release today, and what can be updated after pickup?” Write down the answer.

3. **Get the right adult to send a temporary pickup instruction.**  
   Ask the person with parental responsibility, or the adult who normally has care of the child, to email or write a clear instruction for today. Ask the setting what proof, call-back, password, or ID check they need before they can rely on it. If there is a court order, care arrangement, or safeguarding restriction that limits who can collect, tell the setting that now.

4. **Keep the temporary instruction very simple.**  
   It should include:
   - the child’s full name
   - today’s date
   - the full name of the person collecting today
   - a phone number the setting can call back
   - the words: “Please release my child to [name] today.”

5. **Make sure the collector brings matching ID.**  
   Ask the setting whether they need photo ID, a password, a call-back to the usual contact number, or another check before release.

6. **If nobody with parental responsibility or usual care can be reached, ask for the uncollected-child procedure.**  
   Ask who the setting will contact, what time they escalate, and whether they will contact children’s social care if no safe authorised adult is available.

7. **If there is an immediate danger, call 999.**  
   If there is no immediate danger but you need police advice about a safe handover, call 101. If the concern is that there is no safe authorised adult for the child, contact local authority children’s services through the child’s local council.

8. **Send only the minimum documents needed for today.**  
   Useful documents may include the setting’s emergency contact form, a relevant court order, a care arrangement, guardianship paperwork, or written confirmation from the adult who normally has care of the child. Ask what they need for today and what can wait.

9. **Ask for the temporary arrangement in writing.**  
   Ask the setting to confirm by email who can collect today, what ID is needed, and what paperwork they want later for the permanent pickup list.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve permanent pickup permissions before your child is safely collected today.
- You do not need to deal with probate, benefits, funeral arrangements, school records, or wider family admin in this moment.
- You can update the permanent contact list after you have had time to gather documents and speak to the setting calmly.

## Important reassurance
A sudden request to update pickup permissions can feel harsh after a death. The setting’s checks are usually about preventing the wrong adult from collecting a child, and it is reasonable to ask for a safe temporary arrangement for today.

## Scope note
These are first steps for getting through today’s pickup safely and avoiding an accidental release to the wrong person. Later decisions about parental responsibility, guardianship, court orders, or disputes may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Policies and evidence requirements vary by setting and across the UK. If you are unsure who can authorise collection, or there is a dispute or safeguarding concern, ask the setting, local authority children’s services, or a qualified adviser for help.
