# What to do if… a dependent adult has been left without a caregiver due to a sudden death (UK)

## Short answer
Make sure the dependent adult is safe and not left alone if they cannot manage essential daily needs, then contact the local adult social care, social work, or Health and Social Care Trust route and ask for urgent support today.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave the person alone for the night if they cannot safely manage medication, mobility, toileting, food, drink, confusion, or wandering.
- Do not use hospital as a substitute for arranging care unless there is urgent medical need, immediate danger, or a life-threatening emergency.
- Do not agree to become the new main carer on the spot if you cannot realistically do it safely.
- Do not cancel existing paid care, meals, equipment, alarms, telecare, or key-safe arrangements until you have confirmed what is in place.
- Do not let arguments about wills, funerals, money, or family responsibility delay immediate safety and supervision.

## What to do now
1. **Check immediate safety right now.**  
   Check whether the person is alone, whether they can reach food, drink, heat, a toilet, and whether essential medication is due today. If there is immediate danger or a life-threatening medical emergency, call 999.

2. **Put one safe person physically with them if they cannot be left.**  
   Ask a trusted relative, friend, neighbour, or existing carer to stay with them until adult social care, social work, the trust, or another safe arrangement takes over. If nobody can stay safely, treat this as urgent and contact services now.

3. **Contact the correct local care route and ask for urgent help today.**  
   Use the fastest route you can reach:
   - **England or Wales:** contact the local council adult social care or social services team. Ask for the out-of-hours or emergency duty team if offices are closed.
   - **Scotland:** contact the local council social work service or Health and Social Care Partnership access point.
   - **Northern Ireland:** contact the local Health and Social Care Trust adult services or social services team.
   
   Say: **“Their main carer has died. They cannot be left safely. We need urgent interim support and a care needs assessment.”**

4. **Use safeguarding or adult protection wording if there is risk of harm.**  
   Say there is an **adult safeguarding concern** or **adult protection concern** if the person is at risk because care has stopped, they cannot protect themselves, they may self-neglect, or others may exploit the situation.

5. **Check whether any paid care or support already exists.**  
   Look for care agency rotas, invoices, medication records, alarm or telecare paperwork, care-plan folders, key-safe details, and names on the person’s phone. Call the provider and ask what visits are booked, who holds keys, and whether extra short-term calls are possible.

6. **Use urgent health routes for clinical worries.**  
   Contact the GP if open, or use NHS 111 or the urgent-care route for that part of the UK, if you are worried about missed medication, sudden confusion, falls, pressure sores, dehydration, pain, or whether same-day clinical help is needed. If medication has been missed, do not double up doses unless a prescriber, pharmacist, or urgent-care clinician tells you to.

7. **Write down the key facts before making calls.**  
   Keep this short:
   - the person’s name, address, and date of birth if known
   - what they cannot do safely alone
   - what changed, including that the carer died and when
   - current medication, allergies, diagnoses, and mobility needs if known
   - who has keys, key-safe codes, or permission to enter the home

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether the person will live at home, move in with family, or move to a care home.
- You do not need to sort inheritance, property, bills, or funeral decisions before immediate safety is covered.
- You do not need to prove eligibility in detail before asking for urgent help.
- You do not need to resolve family disagreements about who is responsible before contacting services.

## Important reassurance
A sudden care gap after a death can feel chaotic and frightening. The immediate job is not to solve the whole future; it is to keep the person safe today, get the local care route involved, and avoid taking on unsafe promises in panic.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Longer-term decisions about care packages, funding, legal authority, housing, benefits, or family responsibilities may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If anyone is in immediate danger, call 999. If you are unsure whether the person can be left safely, treat it as urgent and contact adult social care, social work, the local trust, NHS 111, or the local urgent-care route.
