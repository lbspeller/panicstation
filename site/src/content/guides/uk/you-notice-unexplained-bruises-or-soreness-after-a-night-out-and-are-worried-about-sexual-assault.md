# What to do if… you notice unexplained bruises or soreness after a night out and are worried about sexual assault (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer place if you can, and contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre or urgent medical care. You do not have to decide now whether to report to police.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront anyone you suspect while you are alone, unsafe, or in shock.
- Do not force yourself to work out exactly what happened before getting help.
- Do not blame yourself for drinking, freezing, having memory gaps, or not being sure.
- Do not assume you need proof before contacting a SARC or medical service.
- Do not delay urgent medical help if you have significant pain, bleeding, injury, confusion, faintness, vomiting, or possible head injury.
- Do not use a self-swab kit or try to investigate your own body for evidence.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer place.** Go to a trusted friend, staff member, family member, hotel reception, station staff, or another public place where you are not alone with anyone you fear.
2. **Call 999 now if there is immediate danger or urgent medical need.** This includes serious injury, heavy bleeding, breathing problems, loss of consciousness, severe confusion, possible head injury, or feeling at risk from someone nearby.
3. **Contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre.** A SARC can offer specialist medical care, emotional support, and options whether or not you want police involved.
4. **Use the UK route for where you are.**
   - **England:** use the NHS SARC finder or contact a nearby SARC directly.
   - **Wales:** use NHS Wales sexual assault referral centre information.
   - **Scotland:** you can self-refer to SARCS by phone 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.
   - **Northern Ireland:** contact The Rowan, the regional Sexual Assault Referral Centre.
5. **Get medical help today if your body feels wrong or you are worried about injury.** A&E is appropriate for significant pain, bleeding, head injury, faintness, vomiting, confusion, or injuries that need urgent care. If it is urgent but not life-threatening, use the urgent health advice route for where you are: NHS 111 in England or Wales, NHS 24 on 111 in Scotland, or GP out-of-hours in Northern Ireland.
6. **Say clearly if you think you may have been spiked or drugged.** Tell the SARC, A&E, urgent health advice service, or another clinician that you have memory gaps, unusual confusion, vomiting, injuries, or symptoms that do not fit what you remember.
7. **Ask someone trusted to stay nearby or help you make the call.** They do not need to ask for details; they can sit with you, help you travel safely, or stay on the phone while you contact support.
8. **Use specialist emotional support if speaking feels possible.** In England or Wales, the 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line can support you by phone or online chat; it is not an emergency service.
9. **If you may want the option of a forensic medical exam later, and only if it feels manageable, avoid washing or changing clothes for now; if you already have, you can still get help.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether it “counts” as assault.
- You do not need to decide now whether to report to police.
- You do not need to contact the venue, friends from the night, or anyone you suspect unless it helps your immediate safety.
- You do not need to write a full account, gather proof, or make long-term decisions today.
- You do not need to explain everything clearly before asking for help.

## Important reassurance
Memory gaps, numbness, freezing, confusion, and uncertainty can happen after trauma, intoxication, possible spiking, or shock. Bruises and soreness can have different causes, but being worried is enough reason to contact specialist support and medical care.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: get safer, get medical or SARC support, and keep your options open. Later decisions about medical follow-up, reporting, work, education, or ongoing support can be made with specialist help when you feel steadier.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical, legal, investigative, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or need urgent medical attention, call 999.
