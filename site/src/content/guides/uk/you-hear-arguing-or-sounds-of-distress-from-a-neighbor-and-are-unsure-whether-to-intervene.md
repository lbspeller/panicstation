# What to do if… you hear arguing or sounds of distress from a neighbor and are unsure whether to intervene (UK)

## Short answer
Do not intervene face-to-face. If you think someone may be in immediate danger, violence is happening, or a crime is in progress, call 999 from a safe place and report what you can hear.

## Do not do these things
- Do not knock on the door or step into the hallway to “break it up” if there is any risk to you.
- Do not confront anyone afterwards, warn them you are calling police, or argue through the wall.
- Do not film at the door, record through windows or letterboxes, or share recordings with neighbours or online.
- Do not dismiss screams, repeated banging, threats, or pleas for help as “just shouting”.
- Do not put yourself somewhere you could be trapped, such as outside your flat with your door closed behind you.
- Do not try to investigate by getting closer to the door or looking through gaps.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause and stay there.** Go inside your home, lock your door, keep your phone with you, and move away from the shared wall or front door if that feels safer. Keep a clear route out of your home in case you need to leave.

2. **Use what you can hear, not what you assume.** Treat it as an emergency if you hear screaming in fear, calls for help, repeated thuds or crashing, threats to kill or seriously hurt someone, mention of a weapon, or a child sounding distressed or unsafe.

3. **If it sounds like an emergency, call 999.** Say you are a neighbour. Give the exact address, flat number, floor, entrance, or building details you know. Describe what you can hear in plain words, such as “I heard someone shout ‘get off me’ and then three loud bangs.” If you are worried about repercussions, ask the call handler how your details are handled and ask them to use discretion.

4. **If you cannot safely speak on a 999 call, use the Silent Solution approach.** Call 999, stay on the line, and listen. If you can, answer by coughing, tapping, or responding to questions. If you are calling from a mobile and are prompted, press 55 to confirm it is a genuine emergency and be connected to police. Pressing 55 works on mobiles only and does not guarantee police can find your location, so give location information in any safe way you can. If you are calling from a landline, do not hang up if you can avoid it; background noise may lead to the call being connected to police, and landline calls usually provide location information.

5. **If it is not clearly an emergency but you are worried, call 101.** Say you are reporting a concern about a possible domestic incident or escalating argument next door. Give the time, what you heard, the address details you know, and whether this has happened before. Ask what information they need and what the safest next step is.

6. **Keep the area outside their door clear.** Do not gather in the corridor or encourage others to confront anyone. If you contact a trusted neighbour, keep it simple: ask them to stay inside and make their own report if they have first-hand concerns.

7. **Make a brief note while it is fresh.** Write the time, duration, address or flat details, and the exact things you heard. Keep it factual, such as “22:40, heard ‘get off me’, then loud banging for about two minutes.” This is only to help you report accurately if asked.

8. **If you later see the neighbour alone and it feels genuinely safe, keep it neutral.** You can say, “Hi, are you okay?” Do not mention the argument if anyone else can hear. Do not pressure them to explain, report, leave, or make any decision. If they ask for help, offer to sit with them while they contact someone they trust, police, or a specialist support service.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide what the relationship is, who is at fault, or whether it “counts” as abuse.
- You do not need to investigate, collect evidence, or confirm details by going closer.
- You do not need to contact a landlord or agent tonight unless you need building staff or security for immediate safety.
- You do not need to plan future involvement as a neighbour while the situation is still active.

## Important reassurance
Feeling unsure is normal. It is reasonable to prioritise safety, avoid direct confrontation, and let emergency services decide what response is needed when you are concerned someone may be at risk.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to help you stay safe and get appropriate help involved. If this becomes a pattern, later decisions about safer ongoing support may need specialist domestic abuse, housing, safeguarding, or legal guidance.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, safeguarding, or professional advice. If you believe someone is in immediate danger or a crime is happening, call 999. If you are not sure but are worried, calling 101 to report the concern is a reasonable next step.
