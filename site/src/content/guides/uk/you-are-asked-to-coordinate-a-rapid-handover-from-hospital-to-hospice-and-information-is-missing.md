# What to do if… you are asked to coordinate a rapid handover from hospital to hospice and information is missing (UK)

## Short answer
Request a pause in the transfer until a printed "minimum safe handover set" is provided: a current medication list with allergies, a physical ReSPECT or DNACPR form, and a documented symptom management plan for the next 48 hours.

## Do not do these things
- Do not allow the person to leave the ward if the hospice has explicitly stated they cannot accept them without specific missing documents.
- Do not accept verbal confirmation of medication doses or resuscitation status; these must be in writing to be legally and clinically actionable.
- Do not take responsibility for carrying original clinical records yourself unless instructed by the ward manager, as some must be handed directly to ambulance crews.
- Do not assume that "everything is on the computer"; systems between hospitals and hospices are often not linked.
- Do not feel pressured by "bed management" to proceed if you believe the lack of information will cause the person pain or distress tonight.

## What to do now
1. **Request a 10-minute pause and identify the Ward Manager.**
   - **Inform the nursing staff that the handover is currently "unsafe" due to missing information.** 
   - **Ask to speak with the Ward Manager or the Discharge Coordinator immediately.** 
   - **State: "I cannot facilitate this transfer until we have a safe clinical handover set in writing."**

2. **Secure the physical "Safe Handover Set" before the person is moved.**
   - **Ensure you have a printed Discharge Summary or Interim Letter.**
   - **Confirm you have a complete Medication Record (including "Medicines Reconciliation").** 
   - **Ask for the original physical ReSPECT or DNACPR form (often a red-bordered paper).**
   - **Verify that the "Just in Case" (anticipatory) medicines have been physically dispensed or a prescription has been sent ahead.**

3. **Verify the "Receipt" with the hospice clinical team.**
   - **Call the hospice admissions nurse directly and list what you have in your hand.**
   - **Ask specifically: "Is there anything you require that I do not have on this list?"**
   - **Confirm if they need the hospital pharmacist to speak with the hospice pharmacist before the patient arrives.**

4. **Confirm transport and equipment status.**
   - **Ask the ward staff for the "Transport Booking Reference" and the estimated time of arrival.**
   - **Verify that specific end-of-life equipment (such as an airflow mattress or oxygen) is already at the hospice or traveling with the patient.**
   - **If the person has a syringe driver (medicine pump), ask for a written "Syringe Driver Chart" to accompany it.**

5. **Clarify the "Tonight Plan" for out-of-hours care.**
   - **Obtain the direct 24/7 clinical number for the hospice.**
   - **Ask the ward if a "Special Patient Note" (SPN) has been added to the NHS 111 system to alert them to the person's palliative status.**
   - **Write down the name and phone number of the "Consultant of the Day" at the hospital in case the hospice needs to call back for clarification.**

6. **Involve PALS or the Site Manager if the transfer is being forced.**
   - **Contact the Patient Advice and Liaison Service (PALS) if you are being told the person "must leave now" despite missing safety information.**
   - **If it is out of hours, ask to speak with the "Site Manager" or "Hospital Bleep Manager."**

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle final funding (NHS Continuing Healthcare) paperwork during the hour of transfer.
- You do not need to update every family member on the minor details of the delay; focus only on the hospital and hospice staff.
- You do not need to worry about long-term care plans beyond the next 48 hours.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed or "difficult" when you are stopping a process that hospital staff seem to be rushing. You are acting as a vital safety check. Taking an extra 30 minutes now to get the paperwork right can prevent hours of distress or a failed admission once the person reaches the hospice.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization during a hospital-to-hospice transfer. It does not cover long-term palliative care planning or legal inheritance issues.

## Important note
This is for information only and not medical or legal advice. If the patient's condition changes rapidly or they appear in pain during the handover, call for the ward doctor or, if in transit, follow the ambulance crew's instructions.
