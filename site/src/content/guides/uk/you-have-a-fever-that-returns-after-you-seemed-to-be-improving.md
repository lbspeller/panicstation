# What to do if… you have a fever that returns after you seemed to be improving (UK)

## Short answer
Seek same-day medical advice from NHS 111 or your GP if a fever returns after you appeared to be recovering, as this may indicate a secondary infection or complication.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the recurrence is a normal part of the original illness.
- Do not ignore the return of symptoms even if they feel milder than before.
- Do not take more than the recommended dose of paracetamol or ibuprofen to "suppress" the new fever.
- Do not use forehead strips or infrared thermometers if a digital thermometer is available, as they are less accurate for diagnostic decisions.
- Do not wait until the morning if you develop difficulty breathing or new confusion.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency symptoms that require an immediate 999 call.**
   - **Difficulty breathing or struggling to speak in full sentences.**
   - **New confusion, slurred speech, or being difficult to wake.**
   - **Blue, grey, pale, or blotchy/mottled skin, lips, or tongue.**
   - **A rash that does not fade when a glass is pressed against it.**
   - **A seizure or a stiff neck combined with a severe headache.**
   - **Not passing urine for 12 hours or more.**

2. **Measure your temperature using a digital thermometer if available.**
   - **Place the thermometer under the tongue or in the armpit and wait for the beep.**
   - **Record the exact reading (a fever is 38°C / 100.4°F or higher).**
   - **Note the time of the reading and any fever-reducing medicine taken in the last 6 hours.**

3. **Contact NHS 111 or your GP practice for a same-day assessment.**
   - **Explain clearly that you were improving but have now deteriorated or the fever has returned.**
   - **Inform them if you are currently taking or have recently finished a course of antibiotics.**
   - **Identify if you are in a high-risk group (e.g., pregnant, over 65, immunocompromised, or have chronic lung / heart disease).**

4. **Monitor your fluid intake to prevent dehydration.**
   - **Sip water, diluted juice, or rehydration salts regularly.**
   - **Check the colour of your urine; it should ideally be pale yellow or clear.**

5. **Isolate from others until you have received medical advice.**
   - **Stay at home and avoid close contact to prevent spreading a potential secondary infection.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific bacteria or virus yourself.
- You do not need to decide on long-term childcare or work arrangements for the upcoming week.
- You do not need to perform household chores or strenuous physical activity.
- You do not need to worry about why the first recovery period ended.

## Important reassurance
It is common for viral illnesses to be followed by secondary issues, and noticing the change in your symptoms is a vital first step in managing your recovery. Seeking professional advice now is the standard procedure to ensure complications are identified and treated early.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute recurrence of fever and does not replace clinical diagnosis or long-term management plans provided by a doctor.

## Important note
All medical information provided here is for general guidance in the UK. If you feel your condition is rapidly worsening or you are concerned about your safety, seek urgent help through 111 or 999 regardless of your temperature reading.
