# What to do if… your circuit breaker keeps tripping when you use a normal appliance (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the appliance that caused the trip by unplugging it immediately and leave the circuit breaker off if it does not stay in the "on" position after a single reset attempt.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep resetting the breaker or RCD if it continues to trip.
- Don’t touch the appliance or plug if you see sparks, smoke, or water.
- Don’t use a different socket for the same appliance until the appliance is verified as safe.
- Don’t remove the cover of the consumer unit or attempt to bypass any safety devices.
- Don’t use extension leads to bypass a faulty socket or circuit.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate signs of fire or electrical damage.** Look for smoke, visible sparks, scorching on sockets, or the smell of burning plastic. 
   - If any of these are present, turn off the main power at the consumer unit (the large red switch) and exit the area.
2. **Unplug the appliance that was in use when the power cut out.** Physically remove the plug from the wall socket to ensure the device is fully isolated.
3. **Check for moisture or water ingress.** Look for water leaks near the appliance, dampness on the walls, or whether the appliance itself (like a kettle or iron) is leaking.
   - Do not touch any wet electrical items.
4. **Disconnect all other items on the same circuit.** Unplug everything in the affected room or area, including chargers, lamps, and small electronics.
5. **Reset the tripped switch once.** Locate the switch in the consumer unit that has moved to the "down" or "middle" position.
   - Push it fully down (off) first, then push it firmly up (on).
   - If it trips again immediately with everything unplugged, leave it off; there is a fault in the wiring or the breaker itself.
6. **Identify the trigger.** If the breaker stays on, plug your appliances back in one at a time. 
   - If the power cuts as soon as a specific item is connected, that item is faulty.
7. **Seek medical advice if you felt a shock.** Even a minor "tingle" or shock can cause internal rhythm issues that appear hours later. Contact NHS 111 or your GP for an assessment.
8. **Contact a professional for repairs.**
   - **If you rent:** Report the issue to your landlord or housing association immediately. In England, landlords can face fines of up to £40,000 for failing to maintain electrical safety.
   - **If you own:** Contact a registered electrician (NICEIC, NAPIT, or SELECT in Scotland) to test the installation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the fault is an "earth leakage" or an "overload" yourself.
- You do not need to replace the appliance or the socket immediately.
- You do not need to review your home insurance policy before stabilizing the electrical hazard.

## Important reassurance
Circuit breakers and RCDs are designed to trip to prevent fires and fatal electric shocks. If the system is cutting the power, it is performing its safety function correctly. Identifying a faulty appliance by unplugging items is a standard and safe procedure for a householder.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for domestic electrical tripping. It does not replace a professional Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR) or technical fault-finding by a qualified person.

## Important note
Electrical faults can be unpredictable. If you cannot safely isolate an appliance or if you hear persistent buzzing from your walls or fuse box, treat it as an emergency and contact a qualified electrician or the emergency services if a fire is suspected.
