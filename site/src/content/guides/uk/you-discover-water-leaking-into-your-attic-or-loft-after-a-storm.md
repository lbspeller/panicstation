# What to do if… you discover water leaking into your attic or loft after a storm (UK)

## Short answer
Treat water in a loft or attic as an electrical and ceiling-collapse risk first. Keep people away from wet ceilings and wet electrics, switch off power only if you can do it safely from a dry place, then document the leak and arrange urgent make-safe help.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t step into the loft if the ceiling below is bowing, sagging, cracking, or showing pooled water on plasterboard.
- Don’t touch wet light fittings, cables, junction boxes, switches, sockets, or the consumer unit.
- Don’t switch power back on to test it while any wiring, fittings, insulation, or ceiling area is damp.
- Don’t use mains-powered fans, heaters, or dehumidifiers near the affected area until the electrics have been checked or made safe.
- Don’t climb onto the roof during or just after a storm.
- Don’t pull down wet insulation in a rush, especially if there may be hidden wiring, old boards, dust, or unknown materials.
- Don’t throw damaged items away before photographing them and checking what your insurer wants, unless they are unsafe to keep.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger from a safe, dry place.** Keep everyone away if you see a bulging ceiling, water dripping through a light fitting, buzzing, crackling, scorch marks, smoke, a hot smell, or water close to electrical equipment. If there is fire, a serious electrical danger, or a ceiling-collapse risk affecting people, call 999.

2. **Make the electrics safe only if you can do it safely.** If you can reach the consumer unit without touching anything wet and without standing in water, switch off the affected circuit or the main switch. If the consumer unit is wet, close to water, overheating, arcing, or unsafe to reach, do not touch it; call a registered electrician. For power cuts, damaged external electricity equipment, or electricity network danger, contact your electricity network operator; in England, Wales and Scotland you can call 105, and in Northern Ireland you can call 03457 643643.

3. **If you smell gas or suspect carbon monoxide, leave the area and use the gas emergency route.** Do not use flames, smoke, or turn electrical switches on or off. Open doors and windows if you can do so safely, get everyone into fresh air, and call the gas emergency number for your area and fuel type. For natural gas in England, Wales and Scotland, the National Gas Emergency Service is 0800 111 999.

4. **Limit the water spread from below if it is safe.** Put a bucket, tray, or towel under active drips. Move stored items away from the wet area without stepping on unsafe loft boards or wet plasterboard. If a drip is coming from one clear point and you can reach it safely, use plastic sheeting or a tray to guide water into a container.

5. **Record what you can before disturbing the scene.** Take photos or video of the wet insulation, dripping point, roof timbers, ceiling stains, wet light fittings, damaged belongings, and any visible roof issue from ground level. Note the time you first noticed the leak and the rooms affected.

6. **Contact the right people for urgent make-safe help.** Contact your home insurer as soon as reasonably possible and ask what temporary repairs, photos, receipts, and damaged items they want kept. Arrange a roofer or suitable emergency contractor to make the roof weatherproof and identify likely entry points such as tiles, flashing, valleys, chimney areas, skylights, vents, or gutters. If you rent, tell your landlord or agent immediately and keep your own photos and messages.

7. **Ventilate only after electrical risk is under control.** Once the area is safe, open windows where weather and security allow, and keep doors to unaffected rooms closed. Do not remove soaked insulation or boards until a roofer, insurer, surveyor, or suitable specialist has advised what needs moving and how to do it safely.

8. **Stop if materials look old, dusty, unusual, or hard to identify.** Do not rip, sand, sweep, or bag unknown insulation, boards, or lagging as a first response. Keep the area undisturbed and ask for specialist advice.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether all insulation must be replaced.
- You do not need to choose permanent roof repairs until the leak source has been found and the damage has been documented.
- You do not need to repaint, scrub stains, or hide ceiling marks now.
- You do not need to compare upgrades, ventilation products, or insulation types while the leak is still active.
- You do not need to throw belongings away immediately unless keeping them creates a safety or hygiene risk.

## Important reassurance
A storm leak can look alarming because water travels along rafters, pipes, insulation, and plasterboard before it appears. The useful first move is not to solve the whole roof problem at once; it is to keep people away from wet electrics and weak ceilings, reduce further water spread, and get the damage recorded.

## Scope note
These are first steps to make the situation safer, limit damage, and buy time. Permanent roof repair, electrical testing, drying, mould treatment, and insulation removal may need a roofer, registered electrician, insurer, landlord, surveyor, or other suitable specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, insurance, surveying, electrical, gas, roofing, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe, cannot isolate electrics safely, suspect gas or carbon monoxide, or think the ceiling may collapse, treat it as urgent and get qualified help.
