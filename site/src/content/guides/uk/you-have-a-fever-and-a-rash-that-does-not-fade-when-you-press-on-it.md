# What to do if… you have a fever and a rash that does not fade when you press on it (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a life-threatening medical emergency. Call **999** or go to your nearest **A&E department immediately**, as a fever combined with a non-fading (non-blanching) rash is a primary indicator of meningitis or sepsis.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for more symptoms to appear or for the rash to spread.
- Do not assume the rash is a minor allergy, heat rash, or "just a virus."
- Do not use NHS 111 or wait for a GP callback if the rash is non-blanching.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel confused, dizzy, or drowsy.
- Do not keep re-testing the rash; if it fails the glass test once, seek help immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 immediately and request an ambulance.** **Tell the operator the person has a fever and a "non-blanching rash" (a rash that does not fade under a glass).**
   - State clearly if there are other "red flag" symptoms: confusion, severe headache, stiff neck, sensitivity to light, or cold hands and feet.
2. **Perform the "tumbler test" one time to confirm the non-blanching state.** **Press the side of a clear glass firmly against the spots or rash.** 
   - If you can still see the spots clearly through the glass while pressing, the test is "positive."
   - If you have dark skin, check for spots on paler areas: the palms of the hands, soles of the feet, the roof of the mouth, or the inside of the eyelids.
3. **Prepare for immediate transport while help is on the way.** **Unlock your front door and move to a visible, easy-to-access location near the entrance.** 
   - Keep your phone nearby and on loud/speaker.
   - If the patient is a child, keep them in your sight at all times.
4. **Gather essential items only if it does not delay your departure.** **Collect current medications and any known allergies to tell the paramedics.** 
   - Take a quick photo of the rash with a mobile phone to show doctors in case the appearance changes by the time you reach the hospital.
5. **Monitor for rapid deterioration while waiting for the ambulance.** **If the person becomes unresponsive, has a seizure, or struggles to breathe, update the 999 operator immediately.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to pack a bag or personal items.
- You do not need to determine the specific cause (meningitis vs. sepsis).
- You do not need to notify family or employers before seeking help.
- You do not need to wait for a fever to "break" before going to the hospital.

## Important reassurance
A non-blanching rash with a fever is a recognized clinical emergency. Acting immediately is the standard safety protocol and is the most effective way to ensure rapid treatment. Medical staff are trained to prioritize these symptoms; seeking help now is the correct and necessary action.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization instructions for the combination of fever and a non-blanching rash. It does not replace professional medical diagnosis or hospital-based treatment.

## Important note
This information is based on UK health protocols as of March 2026. A non-fading rash should always be assessed by an emergency clinician. Do not delay emergency care for any reason if these symptoms are present.
