# What to do if… you realise you sent money to the wrong person or wrong account details (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately to initiate a **Credit Payment Recovery (CPR)** request. Provide the specific transaction details and request a formal case reference number to track the recovery attempt.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send a second payment to the "correct" account until the first issue is logged.
- Don’t contact the recipient privately to negotiate - this can alert scammers or complicate the bank's recovery process.
- Don’t wait for the payment to "clear" before calling; banks can often intervene faster if the transaction is pending.
- Don’t pay any third-party "money recovery" firm or individual promising to get the funds back.
- Don’t delete the transaction receipt, confirmation screen, or related banking notifications.

## What to do now
1. **Gather the transaction evidence (2 minutes).** Capture the following details from your banking app or statement:
   - exact amount and currency
   - date and precise timestamp
   - the sort code and account number used
   - the payee name as it appeared during the transfer
   - the payment reference (if used)
2. **Identify the payment status.** Check if the payment is listed as "Pending," "Scheduled," or "Future-dated" in your banking app.
   - If it is a standing order or scheduled payment, cancel it immediately via the app interface.
3. **Call your bank's emergency line.** Use the number on the back of your card or the official app "Help" section.
   - **Tell the agent you are reporting a "misdirected payment" due to incorrect details.**
   - **Request a "Credit Payment Recovery" (CPR) process start.**
   - **Note down the case reference number and the name of the agent you spoke to.**
4. **Submit a written follow-up via secure message.** Log into your online banking and send a message restating the request for recovery.
   - Attach screenshots of the incorrect transaction.
   - Explicitly state that the funds were sent to the wrong account details by mistake.
5. **Verify the recovery timeline with the bank.** Ask the bank to confirm when they will contact the recipient's bank.
   - Under UK industry rules, your bank should notify the receiving bank within two working days of your report.
6. **Report as a crime if you were misled (Fraud).** If you sent the money because you were pressured or tricked (Authorised Push Payment/APP fraud), follow the fraud protocol.
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** Report to **Report Fraud** online at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.
   - **Scotland:** Report to **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
7. **Secure your account.** If the mistake happened because your login details were compromised or you clicked a suspicious link:
   - Change your banking password immediately.
   - Check your "Payee" or "Beneficiary" list for any other unfamiliar accounts and remove them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on legal action or Small Claims Court today; the bank must be given time to attempt recovery first.
- You do not need to contact the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) until you have received a "Final Response" letter from your bank or 8 weeks have passed since your initial complaint.
- You do not need to prove the recipient spent the money; the bank's investigation will determine the current status of the funds.

## Important reassurance
Inputting a wrong digit or selecting the incorrect payee from a list is a common occurrence. Banks in the UK use standardised industry procedures (CPR) specifically designed to handle these errors. Taking immediate action by calling your bank is the most effective way to protect your funds.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for accidental misdirected payments. It does not cover long-term debt recovery, international wire transfers (SWIFT), or commercial contract disputes.

## Important note
Recovery of funds is not guaranteed, especially if the recipient has already withdrawn or moved the money. This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Different banks may have slightly varying internal names for recovery processes.
