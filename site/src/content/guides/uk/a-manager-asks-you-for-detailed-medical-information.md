# What to do if… a manager asks you for detailed medical information (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before sending detailed medical records, test results, medication lists, or portal screenshots to your manager. Ask what work decision they need to make, then offer limited information about fitness for work, work impact, a fit note, or a proper confidential occupational health or doctor’s report route.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send your full diagnosis history, medication list, test results, or medical portal screenshots because you feel put on the spot.
- Do not agree immediately to “full medical records” or to your manager contacting your doctor without seeing what will be asked, who will see it, and what consent you are being asked to give.
- Do not discuss private health details in group chats, public messages, or open-plan conversations.
- Do not ignore the request if you can respond safely; send a brief holding reply and move it into writing.
- Do not fill silence by giving extra health details. A simple boundary is enough: “I can provide appropriate evidence about fitness for work and work impact, but I am not comfortable sharing detailed medical information directly with my manager.”

## What to do now
1. **Ask what they need and why, in writing.**  
   You can say: “Can you confirm what work decision you need to make, such as absence recording, return-to-work planning, safety, or adjustments, and what specific information you are requesting?”

2. **Offer work-impact information instead of medical detail.**  
   Share only what helps manage work safely, for example:
   - whether you are fit to work now
   - tasks you can or cannot do in practical terms
   - temporary limits such as hours, lifting, travel, screens, or workload
   - practical support that may help, such as adjusted duties, remote work, breaks, or a phased return

3. **Use the normal sickness evidence route.**  
   If you have been off sick for 7 calendar days or less, including non-working days, you usually self-certify using your employer’s process. If you have been off sick for more than 7 calendar days, a fit note is the usual evidence. A fit note is about fitness for work; it is not the same as giving your manager your full medical records.

4. **If they ask for evidence during the first 7 calendar days, keep it narrow.**  
   Ask what evidence they need, why it is needed, who will see it, and whether there may be any fee for obtaining it. Do not send wider medical documents unless there is a clear, necessary, and confidential reason.

5. **Redirect extra medical detail to a confidential route.**  
   You can say: “I am not comfortable sharing detailed medical information directly with my manager. I can provide appropriate documentation through HR, occupational health, or another designated confidential contact.”  
   If there is no HR or occupational health team, ask: “Who is the designated confidential contact for health and absence information?”

6. **If they want a doctor’s report, slow the process down.**  
   Ask for the request in writing. Check what questions will be asked, why the report is needed, and who will see it. Your employer must get your permission before asking for a doctor’s report about your health, and you have rights to refuse permission, ask to see the report before it is sent, ask the doctor to correct anything inaccurate or misleading, and refuse to let the report be shared.

7. **Act quickly if you want to see a doctor’s report before it is sent.**  
   If your employer applies for a doctor’s report and you want to see it first, you generally need to contact the doctor within 21 days of the application being made to arrange access.

8. **Ask how your health information will be handled.**  
   You can say: “Please confirm why this information is needed, who will have access, how it will be stored, how long it will be kept, and that only the minimum necessary information will be used or shared.”

9. **Keep a short record.**  
   Note the date, time, what was requested, and what you offered instead. Save messages. After any verbal conversation, email a short recap of what was asked and what you agreed to provide.

10. **If pressure continues, use the internal route.**  
   If your manager keeps pushing for detailed medical information after you have set a boundary, contact HR, the designated confidential contact, or the person named in your sickness or grievance process. Keep the message narrow: “I am willing to provide appropriate evidence through the correct confidential route, but not detailed medical information directly to my line manager.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to disclose a diagnosis label.
- You do not need to consent immediately to a doctor’s report request.
- You do not need to provide full medical records just because someone has asked quickly.
- You do not need to start a formal grievance unless the pressure continues after you have set a clear boundary and redirected the request.
- You do not need to prove you are unwell by sending extra private documents beyond what is actually needed for absence, fitness for work, safety, or adjustments.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel unsettled when a manager asks for detailed medical information. In many workplace situations, the useful information is limited: whether you are fit for work, what work tasks are affected, what evidence is needed for absence, and what practical support may help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to protect your privacy and keep the work process moving safely. Later decisions about capability, reasonable adjustments, sickness procedures, discrimination concerns, or disputes may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Workplace policies and the reason for the request can matter, so get appropriate advice if the situation becomes formal, disputed, or urgent.
