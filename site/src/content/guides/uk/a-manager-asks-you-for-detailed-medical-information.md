# What to do if… a manager asks you for detailed medical information (UK)

## Short answer
Do not approve any record that requires you to "certify" or "verify" accuracy if you lack the evidence to do so. Pause the approval, document your inability to verify in a neutral email, and request the organisation's established exception-handling or secondary-verification procedure.

## Do not do these things
- Do not "just sign it" to meet a payroll deadline if the wording confirms you have verified the hours.
- Do not make accusations of fraud or theft; stick to the statement: "I have no records to verify these entries."
- Do not edit or "correct" hours yourself unless you have a direct evidence trail and written authorisation to do so.
- Do not accept verbal-only instructions (phone calls or desk-side chats) to "just push it through."
- Do not ignore the prompt; unresolved pay errors can trigger Fair Work Agency inspections or HMRC penalties under Real Time Information (RTI) rules.

## What to do now
1. **Check the exact wording of the digital sign-off or declaration.** Determine if you are merely "moving the workflow" or "certifying accuracy." 
   - If the system says "I confirm these hours are correct," do not click until you have checked the source data.
2. **Send a brief, neutral email to the requester immediately.** State: "I am unable to verify the time entries for [Name/Date] as I do not have access to the corresponding [rota / logs / supervisor notes]. Please provide the verification source or advise on the correct exception route."
3. **Request objective evidence, not personal assurance.** Ask for the workplace's verified records, which may include:
   - System access logs or digital clock-in/out data.
   - Physical sign-in sheets or job tickets.
   - Rotas, schedules, or approved overtime forms.
4. **Identify a safe, non-verifying workaround if the deadline is urgent.** 
   - Suggest the employee provides their own written confirmation of accuracy if policy allows.
   - Ask payroll to mark the entries as "disputed" or "pending" rather than "approved."
   - Route the item to a site lead or manager who has direct sight of the work performed.
5. **Escalate to the owner of payroll controls if pressured.** Forward the request and your written concerns to HR, Finance, or the Internal Audit team. Ask for a written instruction on how to proceed when primary verification is impossible.
6. **Report to the Fair Work Agency if you suspect systemic falsification.** If you are being forced to approve records you know are false (e.g., to hide Minimum Wage breaches or hide excessive hours), you can contact the Fair Work Agency (launched April 2026) which enforces holiday pay and minimum wage compliance.
7. **Keep a private, dated log of the event.** Record what you were asked to do, why you could not verify it, the names of those involved, and a copy of your written response.
8. **Invoke the Whistleblowing Policy for serious concerns.** If you believe the pressure to sign is intended to conceal a criminal offence, a breach of a legal obligation, or a risk to health and safety (e.g., falsifying driving hours), follow your organisation’s Protected Disclosure route. Under the 2026 updates, you have day-one protection against detriment for such disclosures.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if an error was accidental or intentional right now.
- You do not need to perform a full forensic audit of past months today.
- You do not need to decide whether to raise a formal grievance immediately; pausing the sign-off is the priority.
- You do not need to resolve the employee's relationship issues regarding their pay until the records are verified.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel pressured when payroll deadlines loom, but asking for verification is a standard professional requirement. Taking a moment to document your lack of evidence protects both the organisation's funds and your own professional standing. 

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization to prevent false certification. It does not replace your specific employer’s payroll policy or formal legal advice regarding employment contracts.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and reflects UK employment standards as of 2026. Payroll accuracy is a legal requirement under HMRC RTI regulations. If you face threats or retaliation for refusing to sign unverified records, consult ACAS or a qualified employment solicitor immediately.
