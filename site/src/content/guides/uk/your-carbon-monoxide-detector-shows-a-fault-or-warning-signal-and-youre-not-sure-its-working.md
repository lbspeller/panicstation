# What to do if… your carbon monoxide detector shows a fault or warning signal and you’re not sure it’s working (UK)

## Short answer
Treat any ambiguous alarm signal as a potential emergency by immediately evacuating to fresh air. Only troubleshoot the device once everyone is outside and you have contacted the relevant emergency service to report a potential leak.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay inside to investigate the device or check the battery while the signal is active.
- Do not assume a "chirp" or "fault" light is a false alarm if you have fuel-burning appliances.
- Do not turn electrical switches on or off, use doorbells, or light matches if you suspect a gas-related issue.
- Do not use any boiler, gas fire, cooker, or wood-burning stove until the property is cleared.
- Do not sleep in the property until the device is replaced or the situation is professionally resolved.
- Do not attempt to open or repair a "sealed for life" alarm unit.

## What to do now
1. **Evacuate all people and pets to fresh air immediately.** Leave the property and move to a location where you are breathing outdoor air.
2. **Open doors and windows as you exit.** Only do this if it does not delay your evacuation; do not stay inside to ventilate.
3. **Turn off the gas supply at the meter if safe.** Locate the emergency control valve (ECV) and turn the handle 90 degrees so it is at a right angle to the pipe.
   - Do not enter a cellar or basement to do this if you smell gas or suspect a leak there.
4. **Call the gas emergency number from outside the property.**
   - England, Scotland, or Wales: **0800 111 999** (National Gas Emergency Service).
   - Northern Ireland: **0800 002 001** (NI Gas Emergency Service).
5. **Check for physical symptoms of exposure.** Look for headaches, dizziness, nausea, breathlessness, collapse, or confusion.
   - If anyone has collapsed or has severe breathing difficulty: **Call 999**.
   - If you have milder symptoms or are worried about exposure: **Call NHS 111**.
6. **Identify the device signal only when safe.** Once outside, if you can see the device through a window or have the manual/model details:
   - Check if the pattern matches "Low Battery" (often a single chirp every minute).
   - Check if the pattern matches "End of Life" (often a specific LED color or double chirp).
   - Check if the pattern matches "CO Detected" (usually a continuous, loud, repeating siren).
7. **Notify your landlord or a qualified engineer.** If you rent, your landlord is legally required to repair or replace faulty CO alarms.
   - For gas appliances, ensure any inspector is on the **Gas Safe Register**.
   - For solid fuel or oil, contact **HETAS** or **OFTEC** registered technicians.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine which specific appliance is faulty right now.
- You do not need to replace the alarm unit before reporting the potential leak.
- You do not need to decide on permanent repair costs or tenancy disputes today.
- You do not need to "prove" CO was present if you felt unwell or the alarm triggered.

## Important reassurance
Carbon monoxide (CO) is colorless and odorless, making it normal to feel uncertain or anxious when an alarm behaves unexpectedly. It is standard safety practice to treat a "fault" or "error" signal as a potential hazard until a professional confirms the device or environment is safe.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a suspected carbon monoxide alarm malfunction or emergency. It does not replace a professional safety inspection or a medical diagnosis.

## Important note
This information is for immediate safety stabilization. Carbon monoxide can be fatal within minutes at high concentrations. If the National Gas Emergency Service or a medical professional gives you instructions that differ from this guide, follow their professional advice immediately.
