# What to do if… you notice swelling in one leg that appears suddenly (UK)

## Short answer
If sudden one-sided leg swelling occurs with breathlessness, chest pain, or feeling faint, call 999 immediately. If these symptoms are absent, you must still seek same-day urgent medical assessment via NHS 111 or your GP to rule out a blood clot.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait until the next day to seek advice if the swelling is only in one leg and appeared rapidly.
- Do not massage, rub, or apply deep pressure to the swollen or painful area.
- Do not attempt to "walk it off" or perform strenuous exercise to reduce the swelling.
- Do not take any anticoagulant medication (blood thinners) that was not specifically prescribed for this instance.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel breathless, dizzy, or have chest discomfort.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate emergency signs and call 999 if you identify any life-threatening symptoms.**
   - new or worsening breathlessness or struggling to breathe
   - chest pain or a heavy / tight feeling in the chest (often worse when breathing in)
   - coughing up blood
   - feeling suddenly faint, lightheaded, confused, or very clammy / sick
   - palpitations or a noticeably fast / irregular heartbeat
2. **Contact the correct urgent-care service for your specific UK nation if you do not have the emergency signs listed above.**
   - **England:** Use 111 online (for those aged 5+) or call 111.
   - **Scotland:** Call NHS 24 on 111.
   - **Wales:** Call NHS 111 Wales or use the 111 Wales online symptom checkers.
   - **Northern Ireland:** Contact your GP immediately; if they are closed, call your local GP Out-of-Hours service.
   - When speaking to a call handler or clinician, state clearly: "I have sudden swelling in one leg" and mention any redness, warmth, or pain.
3. **Seek immediate maternity triage if you are currently pregnant or have given birth within the last six weeks.**
   - Use the 24-hour maternity emergency / triage number provided in your handheld notes.
   - If you cannot reach them immediately, use the national 111 routes specified in step 2.
4. **Prepare the necessary information to help the clinician assess the level of urgency.**
   - Note the exact time the swelling was first noticed and if it is getting worse.
   - Identify if the leg feels warm to the touch or appears red / darker than the other leg.
   - List any recent risk factors such as flights or car journeys over 3 hours, recent surgery, injury, or use of hormone-based medications.
5. **Keep the affected limb supported and comfortable while you wait for medical instructions.**
   - Remove tight footwear, socks, or restrictive clothing.
   - Keep the leg elevated to hip height if sitting, but do not cross your legs.
   - Avoid vigorous movement but perform very gentle ankle flexes if you are required to sit for a long period while waiting.
   - Gather your current medications and have your NHS number ready.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the cause of the swelling yourself or look up different medical conditions.
- You do not need to decide on whether you want specific tests like scans or blood work; the clinician will determine this.
- You do not need to contact your regular GP if you have already been directed to a hospital or urgent treatment centre by 111.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel anxious when a physical change happens suddenly. One-sided leg swelling requires a professional check because it is a physical sign that needs a clear explanation, and the NHS is set up to assess this specific issue quickly. Taking action now is the most effective way to manage the situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and navigation for the UK health system. It is designed to help you reach a professional assessment and does not replace clinical examination or diagnostic testing.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute medical advice or a diagnosis. If your condition changes rapidly or you develop chest pain or breathing difficulties while waiting, call 999 immediately.
