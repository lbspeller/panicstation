# What to do if… you are asked to hold a service in a home or venue and the owner objects at the last minute (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the service immediately and move all attendees to a safe distance while you confirm if permission has been formally withdrawn. If the owner or person in control refuses access, you must leave to avoid civil trespass and relocate to a pre-planned alternative.

## Do not do these things
- Don't attempt to start or continue the service once you have been told to leave (this constitutes civil trespass).
- Don't engage in an emotional or moral argument regarding the deceased or the family's grief with the owner.
- Don't block entrances, exits, or driveways with people or vehicles.
- Don't encourage mourners to "stand their ground" or ignore the owner’s instructions.
- Don't make verbal threats about legal action, lawsuits, or social media exposure on-site.

## What to do now
1. **Recognize the signs of a terminal venue dispute.**  
   Listen for phrases like "you have to leave," "I didn't agree to this," or "this isn't allowed," and watch for locked doors or the physical presence of staff blocking entry.

2. **Call a 60-second pause and move mourners to a neutral buffer zone.**  
   **Announce clearly that there is a brief delay and ask everyone to step back from the building or entrance.** Appoint one person to keep the group together and away from the owner or venue staff.

3. **Identify the person with the legal authority to withdraw permission.**  
   **Ask the person objecting: "Are you the legal owner, the tenant, or the authorised manager of this property?"** Permission can be withdrawn by whoever is in lawful control of the premises, even if a contract was previously signed.

4. **Confirm the exact nature of the objection quietly.**  
   **Ask if the refusal is for the entire gathering or a specific element.**
   - Is it the number of people?
   - Is it specific equipment (candles, music, incense)?
   - Is it a noise or parking issue?
   If a small change can resolve the issue immediately, offer it once. If they repeat the instruction to leave, stop negotiating.

5. **Establish a temporary "Plan B" marker for the mourners.**  
   **If you cannot enter, lead the group to a safe, public, or permitted nearby spot for a 2-minute "holding" ceremony.**
   - Offer a single reading or 60 seconds of silence.
   - This provides a sense of dignity and prevents a chaotic dispersal.

6. **Delegate the logistics of relocation to a professional or lead contact.**  
   **Direct the funeral director or a lead family member to immediately call alternative sites.**
   - Local community centres or church halls.
   - The funeral home’s own chapel.
   - A local hotel or restaurant with a private room.
   - A different family home where permission is certain.

7. **Deliver a single, clear instruction to all attendees.**  
   **Say: "The venue is no longer available for our use; we are moving to [Location] now. Please follow the lead car and do not engage with the venue staff on your way out."**

8. **Leave the premises promptly to prevent escalation.**  
   **Ensure all personal belongings, service sheets, and equipment are removed immediately.** Avoid leaving any items behind that would require a return visit today.

9. **Note details if you suspect discrimination under the Equality Act 2010.**  
   **If the refusal seems based on a protected characteristic (such as religion, race, disability, or sexual orientation), record exactly what was said and by whom.** Do not argue this on-site; keep notes for a later legal claim.

10. **Call 999 only if there is an immediate threat to life or safety.**  
    **If the owner becomes violent or if a breach of the peace is occurring, contact emergency services.** For non-emergency civil disputes, the police (101) typically will not intervene unless a crime is being committed.

## What can wait
- You do -not- need to calculate the cost of the lost venue or transport today.
- You do -not- need to check your contract terms or booking emails while mourners are waiting.
- You do -not- need to decide who is to blame for the lack of permission.
- You do -not- need to issue a formal complaint or start a legal claim in the next few hours.

## Important reassurance
Being turned away from a venue during a service is a high-stress, jarring experience. Your priority is to protect the dignity of the family and the safety of the guests. By moving the group and providing a brief, calm alternative, you are successfully managing a crisis that is not your fault.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization during a venue refusal. It does not cover contract law, refund recovery, or long-term legal disputes regarding property rights.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. In the UK, once permission to be on private property is withdrawn by the person in control, staying may lead to a civil claim for trespass. Use of force to remove people is governed by strict legal limits; always prioritize leaving peacefully.
