# What to do if… the same password works on one device but is rejected on another for the same account (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all repeated login attempts immediately to prevent a permanent lockout. Use the device that still works to change your password and sign out of all other sessions, then manually re-enter the new password on the failing device.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to guess the password more than three times in a row; this often triggers a 24-hour security lock.
- Do not use "password recovery" or "account unlocking" software found via search engines; these are frequently malware.
- Do not click "Reset Password" links in emails or texts that arrived unexpectedly while you were struggling to log in.
- Do not share your screen or provide one-time passcodes (OTP) to anyone claiming to be "technical support."
- Do not change your router or ISP settings until you have ruled out device-specific input errors.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and protect the account from lockout.**
   - **Stop entering the password** for at least 15 minutes if you have already seen an "Incorrect Password" or "Account Locked" message.
   - **Check the working device** to see if you are still logged in; if you are, do not log out yet as this may be your only way to manage the account.

2. **On the working device: update your security credentials.**
   - **Change the account password** to a new, unique string of three random words.
   - **Select the option to "Sign out of all other devices"** or "Remove all trusted sessions" in the security settings.
   - **Review "Recent Activity" or "Login History"** for any IP addresses or locations you do not recognise.
   - **Check for "Passkeys"** in your account settings; if a passkey was created on one device, the other device may be rejecting the "password" because it expects a biometric or hardware key instead.

3. **On the failing device: verify the input method.**
   - **Manually type the password** instead of using browser autofill or a password manager, which may be storing an outdated version.
   - **Verify the keyboard layout** (e.g., ensuring it is set to "UK English" and not "US English," which swaps the position of symbols like @ and ").
   - **Check for physical input errors** such as the Caps Lock light being on, "Num Lock" being active on a laptop, or "Sticky Keys" being enabled in accessibility settings.
   - **Type the password into a plain text note** first to visually confirm it is exactly correct, then copy and paste it into the login field.

4. **Clear cached data and bypass local interference.**
   - **Open a Private or Incognito window** in your browser and try logging in there; this bypasses corrupted cookies or extensions.
   - **Clear the browser cache and cookies** specifically for that website if the private window works.
   - **Update the device system time and date** to "Set automatically"; if the clock is off by even a few minutes, security certificates and 2FA codes will be rejected.

5. **Test the network connection.**
   - **Switch to a different network** (e.g., move from home Wi-Fi to mobile data) to see if your IP address has been temporarily "rate-limited" by the provider.
   - **Disable any VPN or Proxy** software temporarily, as many services block sign-ins from known VPN exit nodes for security reasons.

6. **Report suspicious activity to UK authorities.**
   - **Forward suspicious emails** to the Suspicious Email Reporting Service (SERS) at report@phishing.gov.uk.
   - **Forward suspicious text messages** to 7726 (free of charge on all UK mobile networks).
   - **Report confirmed fraud or hacking** to Report Fraud (formerly Action Fraud) via their website or by calling 0300 123 2040 if you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland.
   - **Contact Police Scotland via 101** if you are in Scotland and believe your account has been compromised or money has been stolen.

## What can wait
- You do not need to perform a factory reset of the failing device right now.
- You do not need to contact the police if no crime has been committed and you have regained control of the account.
- You do not need to update every other online account immediately unless you were reusing the same password across multiple sites.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for one device to reject a password that works elsewhere due to simple technical conflicts like cached data, regional keyboard settings, or temporary IP blocks. Regaining access is usually a matter of clearing these local errors or resetting the password from the "known good" device. Confusion, frustration, and a sense of panic are normal when technology fails, but taking a short break usually prevents a permanent lockout.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for account access issues and immediate harm prevention. It does not cover deep forensic recovery, legal action against service providers, or long-term cybersecurity auditing.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and does not constitute professional IT security, legal, or financial advice. Service providers' internal security protocols change frequently. If you believe your identity has been stolen or significant funds are at risk, contact your bank and the relevant authorities immediately. For emergencies, always call 999.
