# What to do if… you receive a short-notice request to attend a hearing for someone in custody (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the request by calling the relevant court's official office or the person's solicitor directly using verified contact details found on government websites.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t travel to a court building until you have confirmed the hearing is actually taking place and your presence is permitted.
- Don’t provide personal data, bank details, or payments to any individual claiming a "fee" is required for the hearing to proceed.
- Don’t forward unverified hearing details or custody locations on social media or group chats.
- Don’t bring prohibited items to the building, including liquids in glass bottles, sharp objects, or large quantities of luggage.
- Don’t attempt to contact witnesses or victims mentioned in the case to "verify" the hearing details.
- Don’t take photographs or recordings inside the court building or courtroom.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the source of the request.**
   - Determine if the message came from an official government domain, a known law firm, or a private mobile number.
   - If the request came via social media, WhatsApp, or an unknown caller, treat it as unverified.

2. **Retrieve official contact details for the court jurisdiction.**
   - **England and Wales:** Use the "Find a court or tribunal" service on GOV.UK to find the telephone number for the specific Magistrates' or Crown Court.
   - **Scotland:** Use the Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service (SCTS) "Find us" tool to locate the Sheriff Court or High Court contact details.
   - **Northern Ireland:** Contact the Northern Ireland Courts and Tribunals Service (NICTS) Customer Service Centre or the specific local court office.

3. **Call the court office to verify the hearing details.**
   - **Provide the name of the person in custody and the scheduled time if known.**
   - **Ask for the case reference number and the specific courtroom (court number).**
   - **Confirm if the hearing is open to the public or if there are reporting restrictions.**

4. **Contact the person’s solicitor if they have one.**
   - **Ask the solicitor to confirm your role, such as providing a character reference or acting as a bail surety.**
   - **If you are asked to be a bail surety (England/Wales/NI), confirm the financial implications and requirements before agreeing.**
   - **Ask for the name of the Duty Solicitor if a private firm is not yet instructed.**

5. **Prepare for arrival and security screening.**
   - **Bring a form of government-issued photo ID (passport or driving licence).**
   - **Arrive at least 30 to 45 minutes before the hearing time to clear security queues.**
   - **Ensure all liquids are in non-glass containers; be prepared to perform a "sip test" on drinks if requested by security.**

6. **Notify the court if you are a required witness and cannot attend.**
   - **Inform the court immediately if you have received a formal witness summons with insufficient notice.**
   - **Provide your contact details so the legal teams can be updated on your availability.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare a written statement or character reference immediately unless instructed by a solicitor.
- You do not need to arrange long-term childcare or leave for multiple days until the first hearing confirms if the person is remanded or released.
- You do not need to decide on a permanent legal representative for the person during this initial contact phase.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed by a sudden request to attend court. Taking 10 minutes to verify the hearing through official channels is the most effective way to ensure you are going to the right place for a genuine reason. Court staff are accustomed to helping the public navigate these initial steps.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for verifying and responding to a sudden request. It does not cover legal strategy, the specifics of giving evidence, or the outcomes of bail applications.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Court procedures and security rules may vary by specific building and jurisdiction within the UK. Always follow the direct instructions of court officials and qualified legal professionals.
