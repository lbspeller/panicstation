# What to do if… you are told a work complaint must be handled informally and you are uncomfortable with that (UK)

## Short answer
You can ask for the formal process and put that request in writing now. If informal handling would mean an off-the-record chat, direct confrontation, or a meeting you do not feel safe with, pause and ask for a written, trackable route.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to “off the record” handling if you want a record of what you raised and when.
- Do not let anyone rush you into a meeting you are not ready for, especially alone with someone involved in the complaint.
- Do not feel pressured to “have a quiet word” with someone if that would feel unsafe, intimidating, or likely to make things worse.
- Do not hand over your only copies of messages, notes, or documents you legitimately have access to.
- Do not resign, threaten resignation, or send an angry message in the heat of the moment.
- Do not assume a grievance pauses any possible legal time limit if you already think a deadline may be close.

## What to do now
1. **Create a pause and set one clear boundary.**  
   Send a short message: “I’m not comfortable with this being handled informally. Please confirm the formal process and who will handle it.”

2. **Ask for the written policy and the correct contact.**  
   Ask for the grievance policy and, if relevant, any bullying, harassment, discrimination, dignity-at-work, or whistleblowing policy. Also ask: “If my line manager is involved, who should I submit this to?”

3. **Choose the safest formal route for the issue.**  
   - If it is about how you have been treated, a workplace dispute, or a complaint about management, the grievance process is usually the route to ask for.
   - If it is about bullying, harassment, or discrimination, ask whether there is a specific formal policy for that type of complaint.
   - If it is about wrongdoing in the public interest, such as serious safety risk, fraud, or other reportable wrongdoing, ask for the whistleblowing or speak-up route and consider getting advice before making the disclosure.

4. **Put the complaint in writing.**  
   Send a dated email or letter to the person named in the policy, HR, a more senior manager, or another appropriate contact. Keep it factual and short:
   - what happened
   - dates, times, and locations, as best you can
   - who was involved or present
   - what evidence you have
   - what you need now, such as a formal meeting, a different manager handling it, written communication, or no direct contact with the person complained about while it is reviewed

5. **Say clearly if direct contact is the problem.**  
   You can write: “I do not feel comfortable approaching them directly. I am asking for this to be handled through the formal procedure.”

6. **Ask for practical protections while it is handled.**  
   If needed, ask for temporary steps such as a different reporting line, no 1:1 meetings with the person complained about, communication in writing, a neutral person present in discussions, or meetings at a time and place where you can participate safely.

7. **Ask for adjustments if health or disability is part of the risk.**  
   You do not need to overshare. You can say: “For health reasons, I need communication in writing and no direct contact while this is reviewed.” If you are disabled or may be disadvantaged by the process, ask for reasonable adjustments such as extra preparation time, written questions, breaks, remote attendance, or a support arrangement.

8. **Ask about being accompanied if a grievance meeting is arranged.**  
   If there is a formal grievance meeting, ask in writing to bring a companion, such as a colleague or trade union representative. Give their name and role when you can.

9. **Keep a clean record.**  
   Start a timeline with dates, what happened, who was present, what was said, and what happened next. Keep copies of relevant emails, messages, meeting invites, and notes. After calls or meetings, send a brief follow-up email: “My understanding of what we agreed is…”

10. **Get confidential outside guidance if the process is blocked.**  
   Speak to a trade union representative if you have one, or contact Acas for general guidance. If the complaint involves discrimination, harassment, whistleblowing, retaliation, or a possible claim deadline, consider getting independent advice early.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, appeal an outcome, start legal action, or go to a tribunal.
- You do not need a perfect timeline before raising the issue formally; you can submit what you know and add details later.
- You do not need to prove motive or intent right now; focus on specific events, the record you need, and what would make the process safer.
- If you already think a claim deadline may be close, do not rely on the grievance process alone to protect your position; get advice before waiting.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to feel uneasy when someone pushes for informal handling, especially where there is a power imbalance or you are worried about being exposed, minimised, or pressured. Asking for a formal route is a reasonable way to slow the situation down and make sure there is a clear record.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and move your concern into a safer, trackable process. Later decisions, such as appeals, mediation, settlement discussions, or formal claims, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Workplace policies, employment status, deadlines, and the facts of your situation can change what is appropriate.
