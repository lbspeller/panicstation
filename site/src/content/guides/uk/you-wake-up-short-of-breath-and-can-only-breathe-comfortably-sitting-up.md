# What to do if… you wake up short of breath and can only breathe comfortably sitting up (UK)

## Short answer
Stay sitting upright and treat this as urgent. Call 999 now if you are struggling to breathe, can only speak in short phrases, have chest pain or tightness, blue or grey lips or skin, confusion, severe drowsiness, faintness, a severe allergic reaction, or you are getting worse.

## Do not do these things
- Do not lie flat to “see if it passes” if sitting up is the only way you can breathe comfortably.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you feel faint, very breathless, or your symptoms are worsening.
- Do not take someone else’s inhaler, antibiotics, steroids, sleeping tablets, sedatives, or water tablets.
- Do not ignore new breathlessness at rest, especially if it woke you from sleep.
- Do not smoke or vape right now, and do not drink alcohol to calm down.

## What to do now
1. **Get into the safest breathing position.** Sit upright on the edge of the bed or in a chair, with your feet on the floor if you can. Support your arms on your thighs, pillows, or a table. Loosen tight clothing around your neck, chest, and waist.
2. **Check for 999 red flags now.** Call 999 if you have any of these:
   - severe difficulty breathing, gasping, choking, or not able to get words out
   - chest tightness, chest heaviness, or pain spreading to your arm, back, neck, or jaw
   - lips, tongue, face, or skin suddenly turning very pale, blue, or grey; on brown or black skin, this may be easier to see on the palms, soles, lips, gums, or inside the eyelids
   - sudden confusion, unusual drowsiness, severe dizziness, or feeling like you might pass out
   - sudden swelling of the lips, mouth, tongue, throat, or face, or another sign of a severe allergic reaction
3. **Use only your own prescribed breathing medicine.** If you have a prescribed reliever inhaler or an asthma or COPD action plan, follow the breathless or attack steps exactly as you were given them. If your inhaler is not helping, you feel worse, or you are worried, call 999.
4. **If there are no 999 red flags but you are still short of breath at rest, get urgent same-day medical advice.** Use the urgent NHS route for where you are:
   - in England, use NHS 111 online or call 111
   - in Scotland, contact your GP practice urgently during normal hours; phone NHS 24 on 111 if it is closed
   - in Wales, use NHS 111 Wales online or call 111
   - in Northern Ireland, contact your GP practice during opening hours; if it is closed, contact your GP out-of-hours service
5. **Say the key sentence clearly.** Use: “I woke up short of breath and I can only breathe comfortably sitting up. It is new or worse than usual.” If you have asthma, COPD, heart failure, heart disease, a recent infection, pregnancy, a recent operation, or a history of blood clots, say that early.
6. **Prepare while help is being arranged.** Stay upright. Keep your phone nearby. Gather your medicines, inhalers, spacer, allergies list, and any recent oxygen saturation, peak flow, blood pressure, or temperature readings if you already have them. Ask someone nearby to stay with you or check on you.
7. **Upgrade to 999 if anything worsens.** A change from uncomfortable breathlessness to struggling, faintness, confusion, chest pain, blue or grey colour, or not being able to speak normally matters.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause right now.
- You do not need to decide about tests, referrals, or long-term treatment now.
- You do not need to search online for reassurance while you are still breathless.
- You do not need to message lots of people; one nearby person and the right urgent service is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
Waking up breathless and needing to sit upright can feel frightening and panicky. Treating it as urgent is a reasonable safety step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stay safer and reach the right level of care. Later decisions about causes, tests, treatment, and prevention may need help from a clinician.

## Important note
This is general information, not a diagnosis and not a substitute for medical care. If you feel seriously unwell, symptoms are worsening, or you are unsure what to do, choose the safer option and seek urgent help.
