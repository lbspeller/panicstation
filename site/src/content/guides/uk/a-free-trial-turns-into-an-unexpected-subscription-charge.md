# What to do if… a free trial turns into an unexpected subscription charge (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the next payment first, using the place where the subscription is actually billed. Then ask for a refund in writing and keep screenshots.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume cancelling or replacing your card will definitely stop a subscription or recurring card payment.
- Don’t ignore it if another payment could be due soon.
- Don’t share one-time passcodes, card PINs, or banking passwords with anyone offering to “process a refund”.
- Don’t let anyone remote-access your phone or laptop to handle the refund.
- Don’t click links in unexpected texts or emails about the charge; go to the bank, app store, PayPal, or company website directly.
- Don’t start several disputes for the same payment unless your bank or card provider tells you to.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the payment details.**  
   In your bank, card, PayPal, Apple, or Google account, note the merchant name, amount, date, and payment route. Take screenshots of the transaction and any receipt email.

2. **Stop the next payment in the right place.**
   - **Apple subscription:** go to your Apple Account or device Subscriptions and cancel the subscription there.
   - **Google Play subscription:** go to Google Play subscriptions, select the subscription, and cancel it there.
   - **PayPal automatic payment:** go to PayPal automatic payments, subscriptions, or saved businesses, select the merchant, and cancel or stop paying with PayPal.
   - **Direct Debit:** contact your bank or building society and cancel the Direct Debit. The company may still say you owe money under the contract, so keep a record of what you cancelled.
   - **Card recurring payment or continuous payment authority:** tell your card provider you withdraw consent for further payments to that merchant and want the payment stopped. Card issuers should stop it when asked, but ask before the end of the business day before the next payment is due if you can.

3. **Check the subscription page, not just your memory.**  
   Log in through the official website or app. Screenshot the plan name, cancellation status, billing route, last charge, and next renewal date.

4. **Ask the company for a refund in writing.**  
   Use the company’s official support route. Say that you signed up for a free trial, did not expect paid billing to start, want the subscription cancelled, and want a refund of the charge taken on the date shown in your screenshot. Save the ticket number, chat transcript, or confirmation email.

5. **If it was a Direct Debit and you believe it was taken in error, contact your bank.**  
   Ask the bank to consider a refund under the Direct Debit Guarantee. If you receive a refund you were not entitled to, you may have to pay it back.

6. **If it was a card payment and the company will not refund, ask your card provider about a dispute or chargeback.**  
   Give the card provider your screenshots, the cancellation record, and the refund request you sent to the company. Ask what evidence they need and what deadline applies.

7. **If you paid by UK credit card, ask whether Section 75 might apply.**  
   This may be relevant where the cash price was more than £100 and up to £30,000, and the issue involves breach of contract or misrepresentation. It is not usually a simple fix for “I forgot to cancel”, but it may be worth asking if the trial or renewal terms were misleading.

8. **If the merchant looks suspicious, protect the account first.**  
   Use your bank’s official app or the number on your card to report the payment. Freeze the card in-app if available, change the account password for the service, and do not give anyone codes or remote access.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether you should have noticed the trial ending.
- Writing a long complaint about the wording of the offer.
- Cancelling your whole bank account or replacing cards unless your bank recommends it.
- Leaving reviews or reporting the business publicly.
- Deciding whether to keep using the service later.

## Important reassurance
Free trials often turn into paid subscriptions quietly, and missing a renewal date is common. The useful first step is not blame; it is stopping the next payment and creating a clear written record.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stop further charges and start the refund or dispute route. Later decisions may need help from your bank, card provider, PayPal, the app store, a consumer adviser, or the Financial Ombudsman Service.

## Important note
This guide is general information for urgent first steps, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Processes and eligibility can vary by payment method, card scheme, bank, provider, and the subscription terms.
