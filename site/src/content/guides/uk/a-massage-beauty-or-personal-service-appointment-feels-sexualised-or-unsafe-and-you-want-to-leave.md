# What to do if… a massage, beauty, or personal service appointment feels sexualised or unsafe and you want to leave (UK)

## Short answer
End the appointment as soon as you safely can and get yourself to a safer place, even if that feels awkward. If you feel in immediate danger or cannot leave safely, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t stay to be polite if you feel unsafe.
- Don’t argue, negotiate, or explain in detail in the room; keep it simple and leave.
- Don’t accept pressure to keep quiet, accept a discount, or continue the appointment.
- Don’t blame yourself for freezing, smiling, going quiet, or going along to get through the moment.
- Don’t force yourself to decide now whether to report, complain, confront anyone, or label what happened.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the service with a short phrase and move toward leaving.**  
   Use something minimal, such as “Stop”, “I need to end this now”, or “I’m not comfortable; please step back.” Sit up, stand, cover yourself as best you can, and move toward the door.

2. **Get out of the treatment room.**  
   Go to reception, a hallway, a public area, outside, or anywhere with other people. If you are partly undressed, wrap a towel, robe, coat, or clothing around you and prioritise leaving the room over being neat or polite.

3. **Ask for immediate help if you need it.**  
   If you are at a venue, say to reception, security, or another staff member: “I feel unsafe. I need someone to stay with me and help me leave now.” If the unsafe person is the only staff member, leave the building if you can and ask a nearby person or business for help.

4. **Use emergency help if you feel trapped or at risk.**  
   Call 999 if you are in immediate danger, someone is threatening you, you cannot leave safely, or you need urgent medical help. If you call 999 from a mobile and cannot speak, stay on the line and follow the operator’s prompts, such as pressing 55 or tapping when prompted. If it is not an emergency but you want police help, call 101.

5. **Bring a trusted person into the moment.**  
   Call or message someone you trust and ask them to stay on the phone, meet you nearby, or pick you up. A simple message is enough: “I’m at this appointment and I feel unsafe. Please call me now and stay with me.”

6. **Keep a minimal record once you are safe.**  
   In your phone, note the date and time, business name and address, practitioner name or description, what was said or done in plain words, and whether anyone else was nearby. Keep booking confirmations, texts, emails, receipts, call logs, and photos of the venue if you already have them.

7. **Get specialist support, even if you are unsure what to call it.**  
   If something sexual happened without your consent, or you are not sure, you can contact specialist sexual violence support. In England and Wales, the 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line supports people aged 16+ by phone on 0808 500 2222 and online chat. In Scotland, contact Rape Crisis Scotland. In Northern Ireland, contact the Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline through Nexus NI. If you are under 19 anywhere in the UK, Childline is available on 0800 1111.

8. **Consider sexual assault health support if any sexual contact happened or you are unsure.**  
   Sexual assault health services can usually offer medical care, emotional support, and options about forensic examination. You generally do not have to report to the police to ask for help. In England, you can use the NHS sexual assault referral centre finder; in Scotland, use NHS inform SARCS information; in Northern Ireland, use nidirect guidance.

9. **Preserve options only if you can do so without distress or danger.**  
   If you think you may want to report later, try not to wash clothing from the appointment before getting advice. If you already washed, changed, showered, or left, you can still seek support.

10. **Leave complaints and professional routes for later.**  
   If the practitioner works for a salon, clinic, chain, or hotel, you can later ask for the manager or written complaints process. If the practitioner claims to be registered with a professional body or healthcare regulator, you can later check that body’s complaints route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to report to police, complain, post a review, confront anyone, or return calls from the business.
- You do not need to write a perfect account; brief notes are enough for now.
- You do not need to know whether the right word is harassment, assault, misconduct, or a boundary violation before asking for support.
- You do not need to explain yourself to the practitioner, pay extra, accept an apology, or keep discussing what happened.

## Important reassurance
Feeling confused, frozen, numb, or uncertain can happen when a professional boundary is crossed. If the appointment felt sexualised, unsafe, or non-consensual, that is enough reason to stop, leave, and get support. You are allowed to end any massage, beauty, or personal service appointment at any time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: get safer, steady the moment, and keep your options open. Later decisions about health care, police, complaints, reviews, refunds, or legal action can be made with specialist support when you are ready.

## Important note
This guide provides general information for immediate safety and support in the UK. It is not legal, medical, investigative, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
