# What to do if… someone threatens to spread false claims about consent unless you stay silent (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause, stop engaging with the threat for now, and contact confidential specialist support. Save what they said somewhere they cannot access.

## Do not do these things
- Do not promise to stay silent because you feel pressured.
- Do not argue point by point, apologise, or try to prove anything to them in messages.
- Do not meet them in person to “clear it up” or go somewhere private with them.
- Do not delete messages, call logs, screenshots, or voicemails out of panic.
- Do not post about it on social media while you are overwhelmed.
- Do not let them isolate you by insisting that no one else can know.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If you are in immediate danger, someone is threatening violence, or you need urgent medical help, call **999**.
   - **If you need urgent police help but cannot speak:** dial **999**, listen to the operator, cough or tap if you can, and press **55** if prompted.
2. **Stop contact for now.** Do not reply to the threat. If you need to send one message for safety, keep it neutral, such as: “I am not discussing this now.”
3. **Save the threat simply.** Take screenshots of the threat, the person’s username or number, and the surrounding messages. Save them somewhere they cannot access, such as an email account, cloud storage you control, or with a trusted person.
4. **Tell one safe person.** Choose someone calm and practical. You can say: “Someone is threatening to say I consented unless I stay silent. I need support and I do not want to handle this alone.”
5. **Contact confidential sexual violence support.**
   - If you are **16 or over in England or Wales**, you can contact the **24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line** on **0808 500 2222** or by online chat.
   - If you are in **Scotland**, NHS Scotland’s SARCS route can help arrange sexual assault healthcare and support.
   - If you are in **Northern Ireland**, The Rowan is the regional Sexual Assault Referral Centre.
6. **Consider medical or SARC support if anything sexual happened that worries you.** A Sexual Assault Referral Centre can usually offer confidential healthcare, practical support, and a space to talk through options. Routes vary by nation and local service; use the official links below.
7. **If the threat involves intimate images or online posting:** if you are **18 or over and living in the UK**, the Revenge Porn Helpline can advise on intimate image abuse and takedown options. If you are under 18, speak to a trusted adult or age-appropriate child protection support as soon as you safely can.
8. **If you want police involvement:** you can report to police online or by phone. Use **101** for non-emergency contact, and **999** if it is urgent or unsafe.

## What can wait
- You do **not** have to decide right now whether to report to police.
- You do **not** have to write a full statement or timeline while you are distressed.
- You do **not** have to confront the person, correct rumours publicly, or reply today.
- You do **not** have to decide what label fits in order to get support.

## Important reassurance
Being pressured into silence about consent is not something you have to manage alone. Freezing, doubting yourself, or feeling ashamed can happen when someone intimidates you; it does not mean you caused the threat or have to obey it.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the moment and reduce harm. Later decisions, including reporting, complaints, longer-term support, or legal advice, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If any step would increase your risk, skip it and focus on immediate safety and confidential specialist support.
