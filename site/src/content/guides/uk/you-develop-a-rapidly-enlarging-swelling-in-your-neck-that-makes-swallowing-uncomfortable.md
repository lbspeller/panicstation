# What to do if… you develop a rapidly enlarging swelling in your neck that makes swallowing uncomfortable (UK)

## Short answer
Call **999** immediately if the swelling is progressing quickly, you are struggling to swallow saliva, or your breathing is affected. This is a potential airway emergency that requires immediate clinical intervention.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if the swelling stops growing if it has changed noticeably over minutes or hours.
- Do not attempt to eat or drink anything to test your swallowing ability.
- Do not take any over-the-counter antihistamines or new medications as a substitute for emergency help.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital; wait for an ambulance or have someone else drive you if an ambulance is not immediately available and you are stable.
- Do not lie flat if you are struggling to breathe.
- Do not ignore the symptoms even if the swelling is not painful.

## What to do now
1. **Assess for immediate airway obstruction and call 999 if any are present.**
   - **Noisy breathing (stridor)**, wheezing, or gasping for air.
   - **Inability to swallow saliva** or unexplained drooling.
   - **A muffled or "hot potato" voice** or sudden hoarseness.
   - **Significant tongue swelling** or feeling like the throat is closing.
2. **Administer prescribed rescue medication if you have a known severe allergy.**
   - **Use your adrenaline auto-injector (e.g., EpiPen, Jext, or Emerade)** immediately if you suspect anaphylaxis.
   - **Call 999 after using the injector**, stating "anaphylaxis" or "airway swelling."
   - **Note the time** the injection was given for the ambulance crew.
3. **Position yourself to maintain an open airway.**
   - **Sit upright** and lean slightly forward if you are having any difficulty breathing.
   - **Lie flat with legs raised** only if you feel faint or dizzy and your breathing is otherwise clear.
   - **Avoid any sudden movements** or standing up quickly.
4. **Contact urgent services if the swelling is fast-growing but your airway is currently clear.**
   - **Call 111** (England, Scotland, Wales) for an urgent clinical assessment and directions to the nearest A&E.
   - **Call the local GP Out-of-Hours service** if in Northern Ireland, or use the "Phone First" service where available.
   - **Prepare to leave immediately** for a hospital if advised by the operator.
5. **Collect essential information for the medical team.**
   - **Identify any new triggers** such as recent insect stings, new foods, or new medications (especially ACE inhibitors for blood pressure).
   - **Locate your current medications** or a recent prescription list to show clinicians.
   - **Identify the approximate time** the swelling first became noticeable.
6. **Ensure help can reach you if you are alone.**
   - **Unlock your front door** and keep your phone in your hand.
   - **Contact a neighbour or friend** to let them know you have called for help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific cause of the swelling (e.g., infection vs allergy) before seeking help.
- You do not need to wait for your regular GP surgery to open if the swelling is rapid.
- You do not need to pack a bag for a long stay; prioritize leaving for medical assessment immediately.

## Important reassurance
Rapid neck swelling is a high-priority symptom for NHS emergency services. By calling 999 or 111, you are ensuring that clinical experts can monitor your airway and provide the necessary treatment to stop the swelling. Feeling anxious during an airway-related event is a normal physiological response.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for acute, rapidly developing neck swelling and does not cover long-term management of chronic lumps or stable conditions.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only and is not a clinical diagnosis. Rapidly enlarging neck swelling can lead to sudden airway closure; always err on the side of caution by contacting emergency services.
