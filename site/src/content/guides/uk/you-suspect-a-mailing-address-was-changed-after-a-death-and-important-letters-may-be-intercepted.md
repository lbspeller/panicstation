# What to do if… you suspect a mailing address was changed after a death and important letters may be intercepted (UK)

## Short answer
Secure the physical mailbox immediately and report the suspected unauthorized redirection to Royal Mail’s security team, then apply for a formal "Special Circumstances" redirection to an address you control.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront a suspected person directly or make accusations as this may compromise evidence or personal safety.
- Do not share sensitive details such as death certificates, dates of birth, or account numbers over unencrypted email or social media.
- Do not assume missing post is a simple delay if multiple institutions confirm letters have been sent.
- Do not update online account credentials until you have confirmed a secure destination for verification codes and physical letters.
- Do not provide original documents like a Grant of Probate, Letters of Administration, or Confirmation to individuals without verified legal standing.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the physical letterbox at the deceased person's property immediately.**  
   If the property is accessible, empty the mail daily and ensure the box has a working lock; if the box is compromised or communal, arrange for a trusted person to collect mail as soon as it is delivered.

2. **Contact the police if you believe mail is being stolen or you feel physically unsafe at the property.**  
   Call 999 in an emergency where a crime is in progress or there is a threat to life; otherwise, use 101 to report the theft of post.

3. **Report the suspected unauthorized redirection or mail interference to Royal Mail’s security portal.**  
   Use the Royal Mail "Report a crime" web portal to log suspected theft or interference, and separately contact the Redirection support team to check if a redirection order is currently active for that name or address.

4. **Apply for a "Special Circumstances" mail redirection in person at a Post Office branch.**  
   **You must bring a certified copy of the death certificate, proof of your identity, and proof of your authority to act for the estate (such as a Grant of Probate, Letters of Administration, or Confirmation in Scotland).** This process allows you to override existing orders and ensures mail is sent to a secure address you specify.

5. **Instruct high-risk financial and government organizations to hold or secure sensitive correspondence.**  
   Call banks, pension providers, HMRC, and the DWP to notify them of the bereavement and the suspected mail interception; ask them to add a security note to the file preventing address changes without written executor verification.

6. **Report the suspected address fraud to the national reporting service, Report Fraud.**  
   If you believe the redirection was done to commit identity theft or financial fraud, submit a report at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040 (if in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland; call 101 for Police Scotland).

7. **Maintain a detailed log of all mail-related observations and communications.**  
   Record the dates and times you checked the mailbox, any items that were expected but missing, and the reference numbers for every report made to Royal Mail, the police, or financial institutions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the perpetrator or prove their intent before taking protective action.
- You do not need to resolve disputes between family members or beneficiaries before securing the mail flow.
- You do not need to update the address for low-priority subscriptions or marketing mail in the first 48 hours.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when estate administration is disrupted by suspected interference. Many organizations have established "bereavement teams" specifically trained to handle these risks; once you have secured the mail and made your reports, the risk of further unauthorized access is significantly reduced.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for securing estate correspondence and is not a substitute for legal advice or a full police investigation.

## Important note
This information is for harm prevention and does not establish legal authority over an estate. If you are unsure of your legal rights as an executor or next of kin, consult a qualified solicitor or the Citizens Advice service.
