# What to do if… a government agency contacts you demanding information and you are unsure what to provide (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before sharing anything, and verify the request using contact details you find independently on GOV.UK or the organisation’s official website. Ask for the request in writing, including exactly what they want, the deadline, and the legal power, notice, or reason they rely on.

## Do not do these things
- Do not give personal information, financial details, passwords, codes, documents, or account access under pressure during a call, text exchange, email exchange, or doorstep contact.
- Do not rely on caller ID, email display names, logos, letterhead, QR codes, or links in the message as proof that it is genuine.
- Do not click links, open attachments, scan QR codes, or call numbers from the message until you have checked them independently.
- Do not send original documents unless you have confirmed the request is genuine and you are following the organisation’s official instructions.
- Do not send extra documents, full bank statements, full device access, or wider personal information just to make the request go away.
- Do not ignore a formal-looking notice without checking it; some genuine requests may have deadlines or consequences.

## What to do now
1. **Break the live contact safely.** If this is a call, say: “I can’t deal with this right now. I’ll contact the organisation through its official details.” Then end the call. If it is an email or text, do not reply, click links, open attachments, or use the contact details in the message.

2. **Verify the organisation independently.**
   - Search GOV.UK or the organisation’s official website yourself.
   - Use the main switchboard, online account, official portal, or published contact route you find there.
   - Ask whether the reference number, team name, deadline, and request are genuine.

3. **If it claims to be HMRC, use HMRC’s official checking pages.**
   - Check whether the type of call, text, email, or letter is listed as genuine.
   - If the contact cannot be verified, stop engaging with it.
   - HMRC scam messages and calls can look convincing, so do not rely on the sender name or number alone.

4. **If it looks like phishing or impersonation, report it and stop engaging.**
   - Forward suspicious emails to report@phishing.gov.uk.
   - Forward suspicious text messages to 7726 if your mobile provider supports this service.
   - If you have already shared passwords, bank details, payment-card details, identity documents, or access codes, contact the relevant bank, account provider, or card provider using official contact details.

5. **Ask for the request in writing, with specifics.** Ask for:
   - exactly what information or documents they want;
   - why they want it;
   - the legal power, formal notice, or reason they rely on;
   - whether each item is compulsory or voluntary;
   - the deadline and what happens if you need more time;
   - the secure way to respond;
   - a reference number, team name, and official contact route.

6. **Sort the request into rough categories.**
   - **Routine or voluntary request:** you can ask questions, narrow the request, or decline items that are not required.
   - **Formal or compulsory notice:** it may cite a specific power, deadline, offence, penalty, or appeal route.
   - **Police or law-enforcement contact:** it may involve questions, a statement, an interview, or evidence.
   If you cannot tell which category it is, treat it as unclear and do not provide information until you have verified it and asked what makes it compulsory.

7. **Narrow the scope before sending anything.** Once verified, ask: “Please confirm the minimum information you require and the secure official route for providing it.” Send only what has been requested, unless you have had advice that more is needed.

8. **Use a safe delivery route.**
   - Use the organisation’s official portal, online account, postal address, or secure upload route found independently.
   - Avoid sending sensitive documents by ordinary email unless the organisation confirms that this is the correct route.
   - Keep copies of what you send.

9. **If police want an interview, statement, or informal chat, pause for legal advice.**
   - You can say: “I want legal advice before answering questions.”
   - If you are arrested or questioned at a police station, ask for free legal advice.
   - If you do not have your own solicitor at the police station, ask for the duty solicitor.

10. **Keep a simple record.** Note the date and time, who contacted you, what they asked for, the reference number, how you verified it, who you spoke to, and what you sent. Keep copies or screenshots of letters, emails, texts, envelopes, and web pages used to verify the contact.

11. **If the issue is about your personal information being mishandled, use the data-protection complaint route.** Usually start by complaining to the organisation and keeping a copy of that complaint. If it is not resolved, you can consider complaining to the Information Commissioner’s Office.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether you agree with the request; first confirm whether it is genuine and whether anything is compulsory.
- You do not need to gather every possible document now; wait until the request is clear and narrowed.
- You do not need to explain your whole situation on first contact; start with verification, written scope, and secure delivery.
- You do not need to use the fastest route if it is not secure; a verified route matters more than responding in panic.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to pause when someone claiming to be from the government demands information. Genuine agencies may contact people unexpectedly, and scammers also imitate them. Checking the request independently and asking for clear written details is a normal protective step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you avoid irreversible mistakes and buy time. Your exact obligations can depend on the agency, the type of notice, the information requested, and the deadline, so later decisions may need specialist legal, financial, data-protection, or other professional help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, data-protection, therapeutic, medical, or other professional advice. If you think you have received a formal legal notice, may face penalties, or are being asked for an interview, statement, or evidence, consider getting specialist advice before responding.
