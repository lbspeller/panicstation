# What to do if… you are asked to sign a document by an airline, hotel, or official and you do not understand it (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign any document unless you fully understand the content and the consequences of your signature. Request an English translation, a professional interpreter, or a copy to review privately before proceeding.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign "to get it over with" or because you feel embarrassed about the language barrier.
- Do not sign blank pages, partially completed forms, or documents where you have not seen every page.
- Do not accept verbal assurances that a document is "standard" or "just a formality" if the written text is unreadable to you.
- Do not hand over your passport as a form of security or "deposit" for a document or service.
- Do not sign a police statement or official record without a qualified interpreter present.

## What to do now
1. **State clearly that you cannot sign until you understand the text.** **Use a calm, firm tone to request a translation or an interpreter immediately.**
2. **Request a copy of the document to review in private.** **Take a high-quality photograph of every page, including the back of the document and any small print at the bottom.**
3. **Ask for a one-sentence summary of the document’s legal purpose.** **Write down exactly what the official or staff member claims the document is for, along with their name and job title.**
4. **Use a translation application as a secondary verification tool.** **Scan the text with a mobile translator to identify keywords such as "liability," "waiver," "fees," "penalty," or "consent."**
5. **Escalate to a supervisor or manager if you are being pressured by hotel or airline staff.** **Ask them to point to the specific policy or term of carriage that requires your signature in this moment.**
6. **Identify your local emergency and consular contact numbers.** **Check the FCDO travel advice for the country you are in to find the local emergency services number and the nearest British embassy details.**
7. **Contact the UK Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office (FCDO) if the situation involves authorities.** **Call the 24/7 consular help line at +44 (0)20 7008 5000 if you are being detained or pressured by police or immigration officials to sign a statement.**
8. **Request an independent interpreter if you are at a police station or government office.** **The FCDO can often provide a list of local English-speaking lawyers and interpreters, though they cannot pay for these services.**
9. **Log the details of the interaction while they are fresh.** **Record the time, location, names of individuals involved, and what happened when you requested a translation.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to file a formal complaint against the hotel or airline.
- You do not need to figure out your insurance claim details or refund procedures until you are in a safe, quiet environment.
- You do not need to worry about the long-term legal implications of the document until you have shared the photos of it with a professional.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel pressured or anxious when presented with official-looking paperwork in an unfamiliar language. Insisting on understanding what you sign is a standard and necessary precaution, not a sign of being difficult. Many travelers face this exact situation, and taking a pause to seek clarity is the safest course of action.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for document-related pressure abroad. It does not replace legal advice. If a document involves criminal charges, significant financial liability, or your right to remain in a country, you must seek professional legal counsel.

## Important note
The FCDO can provide lists of local lawyers and interpreters but cannot provide legal advice or interfere in the legal proceedings of another country. Always check the specific travel advice for your current jurisdiction on the GOV.UK website.
