# What to do if… your employer pressures you to choose a new role or sign documents immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Do not commit in the room. Ask for the role options, terms, process, and deadline in writing before you choose or sign anything.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign “to make it easier” or to avoid an awkward conversation.
- Do not sign anything you have not read fully, especially anything labelled “settlement agreement”, “compromise”, “waiver”, or “release”.
- Do not rely on verbal promises such as “we’ll fix pay later” or “it won’t affect your grade”; treat anything not written down as uncertain.
- Do not accept a role if key basics are unknown, including pay, grade, location, hours, reporting line, duties, notice, and benefits.
- Do not let “choose right now” push you into an irreversible step. Your first goal is clarity and time.

## What to do now
1. **Slow it down with one sentence.**  
   Say: “I can’t make a decision right now. Please send the role options, terms, process, and deadline in writing.” Then stop talking.

2. **Ask for the minimum written pack.**  
   Request, preferably by email: job titles, reporting line, job descriptions, pay or grade, hours, location or hybrid expectations, start date, selection method, and what happens if you do not choose.

3. **Clarify what this “choice” actually is.**  
   Ask: “Is this a preference exercise, a competitive selection, or an offer of suitable alternative employment? Are redundancies proposed, and am I being treated as at risk?”

4. **Create your own record immediately.**  
   Write down the date and time, who attended, the exact words used about urgency, any deadlines stated, and what documents were shown. Save it somewhere private.

5. **If redundancy is involved, ask what process applies.**  
   Ask when individual consultation starts, what the selection pool and criteria are, and who represents staff. If 20 or more redundancies are proposed within a 90-day period in one establishment, ask who the recognised union or elected employee representatives are for collective consultation.

6. **If redundancy is part of the process and you are offered an alternative role, ask about the trial period before treating the move as final.**  
   Ask whether the role is being treated as suitable alternative employment. If it is, ask for the 4-week trial period, the exact start and end dates, and how to raise concerns in writing if the role turns out to be unsuitable.

7. **If a settlement agreement is mentioned or handed to you, pause.**  
   Say you will not sign in the meeting. Ask for a copy to review and arrange independent advice. A settlement agreement normally needs independent legal advice before it can settle employment rights. Ask what contribution the employer will make towards advice costs.

8. **If outsourcing, a takeover, or a service change is mentioned, ask whether TUPE applies.**  
   Ask: “Is there a business transfer or service-provider change? If so, are roles transferring under TUPE, and what happens to terms and continuity?”

9. **Get quick independent guidance before you choose.**  
   If you are in a union, contact your rep and forward the written pack. If not, contact ACAS for free guidance on consultation, redundancy, and alternative roles.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to stay, leave, or change career direction.
- You do not need to negotiate pay or redesign the organisation chart right now; first get the basics in writing.
- You do not need to update your CV or LinkedIn tonight unless it calms you; your priority is avoiding a rushed, irreversible decision.
- You do not need to judge today whether everything is lawful. Gather facts, keep records, and get advice before agreeing or signing.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressured, shaky, or angry in a restructure meeting is a normal stress response. Your job right now is not to be decisive; it is to buy time, force clarity in writing, and avoid a signature or statement you cannot take back.

## Scope note
This is first steps only. What happens next may depend on your contract, length of service, the numbers affected, and whether this is redundancy, redeployment, or a transfer.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal or professional advice. Employment situations turn on details, including contract terms, numbers affected, timelines, and how decisions are communicated. Get independent advice before signing or agreeing to anything you are unsure about.
