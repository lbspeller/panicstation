# What to do if… you are asked to attend a confidential meeting at work and you are unsure why (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as potentially serious and slow the process down. Ask what the meeting is about in writing, and do not agree to, resign, or sign anything in the meeting.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume “confidential” means harmless or routine.
- Do not resign, accept an outcome, or agree to “an exit” on the spot.
- Do not sign anything, including a settlement agreement, confidentiality wording, or a “full and final” release, in the meeting.
- Do not assume you have a legal right to bring someone to a settlement discussion, but do ask to bring a companion anyway.
- Do not rely on verbal summaries such as “this will not affect your record”; ask for the wording in writing.
- Do not send emotional messages to colleagues or post about it while you are still unclear what this is.

## What to do now
1. **Reply and ask for the basics in writing before you attend.** Ask:
   - the purpose of the meeting, in one sentence
   - who will attend and their roles, such as HR, manager, or legal
   - whether this is about a proposed settlement agreement, protected conversation, or pre-termination negotiation
   - whether any documents will be discussed, and whether you can receive them in advance
2. **Ask to bring a companion.** There is no legal right to be accompanied to a settlement agreement meeting, but Acas says it is good practice to allow it. Ask to bring:
   - someone you work with
   - a workplace trade union representative certified by their union
   - an official employed by a trade union
3. **Ask for a short reschedule if you are refused a companion or documents.** You can say: “I want to understand the proposal properly. Please reschedule briefly so I can arrive prepared.”
4. **Check whether this is really a formal process meeting.** Ask them to confirm whether the meeting is:
   - a settlement discussion
   - a formal disciplinary hearing
   - a formal grievance meeting
   A legal right to be accompanied can apply at disciplinary hearings and can apply at grievance meetings about legal or contractual duties.
5. **Set a boundary before the meeting starts.** Write this in your email and repeat it at the start: “I will listen, take notes, and respond after I have seen anything in writing.”
6. **Clarify what “confidential” means in the first minute.** Ask:
   - “Is this intended to be a settlement discussion leading to a settlement agreement?”
   - “Are you treating this as without prejudice or as a section 111A pre-termination negotiation?”
   - “Are you saying I cannot discuss this with an adviser?”
7. **If they present terms, ask for the written offer and time to consider it.** You can say:
   - “Please send me the proposal in writing. I am not making decisions today.”
   - “What deadline are you proposing, and what happens if I need more time to take advice?”
8. **If they imply you must sign to get anything, pause the meeting politely.** You can say: “I am not in a position to respond without independent advice. I will come back to you after I have reviewed the written offer.”
9. **Immediately after the meeting, make a factual record.**
   - Write a dated note of what was said, who attended, and any deadlines or pressure.
   - Email HR or your manager a short factual summary beginning: “To confirm my understanding…”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to accept a settlement, leave, raise a grievance, or challenge anything.
- You do not need to explain yourself to colleagues right now.
- You do not need to negotiate in the meeting; your immediate job is to get clarity and a written proposal.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel blindsided by a confidential settlement discussion invite. Slowing the process down, asking for written clarity, and taking advice before signing are reasonable professional responses.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to prevent avoidable mistakes and buy time. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if the meeting involves performance, redundancy, discrimination, whistleblowing, a grievance, or a proposed exit.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, therapeutic, or other professional advice. In the UK, a settlement agreement usually needs to meet legal conditions before it can settle specific employment claims, including being in writing and the worker getting advice from a relevant independent adviser. Avoid signing until you have had that independent advice.
