# What to do if… police ask you to consent to access your cloud backups or online accounts (UK)

## Short answer
Do not provide passwords or consent in the moment. State clearly that you wish to speak to a solicitor before making a decision or signing any Digital Processing Notice (DPN).

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide passwords, PINs, or recovery patterns voluntarily.
- Do not approve multi-factor authentication (MFA) or "push" notifications on your device for the police.
- Do not unlock applications or "show them one thing" on a logged-in device.
- Do not sign a Digital Processing Notice (DPN) or consent form without legal advice.
- Do not delete data or "wipe" accounts remotely, as this may be treated as perverting the course of justice.
- Do not assume that refusing voluntary consent is a crime; however, do not ignore a formal Section 49 RIPA Notice.

## What to do now
1. **Clarify your current legal status immediately.** Ask the officer:
   - "Am I under arrest?"
   - "Am I free to leave?"
   - "Are you making a request for voluntary consent or exercising a statutory power?"
2. **State your request for legal advice clearly.** Use the following phrase: "I do not consent to the search of my cloud accounts or online data at this time and I require a solicitor before answering further questions."
   - If in a police station, you have a right to free independent legal advice.
   - If not in a station, you may still refuse voluntary consent until you have spoken to a solicitor.
3. **Ask to see the specific Digital Processing Notice (DPN).** If they provide a form (often a DPNa or DPNb), check for:
   - The specific accounts listed (e.g., Apple ID, Google, Instagram).
   - The date range of the data requested.
   - The specific categories of data (e.g., "all photos" vs "messages between X and Y").
4. **Distinguish between the device and the cloud.** If the police seize your physical phone, this does not automatically grant them the right to access your cloud backups.
   - State: "I understand you are seizing the device, but I do not consent to you logging into my online accounts or cloud storage."
5. **Check for a RIPA Section 49 Notice.** If the police claim you *must* hand over a password or face prison, ask if they are serving a formal Notice under Section 49 of the Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000.
   - A valid RIPA notice must be in writing and authorized by a senior officer or judge.
   - If served with this, you must seek immediate legal advice as refusal is a criminal offence.
6. **Note the details of the request.** Record the following as soon as possible:
   - Officer names and collar numbers.
   - The time and location of the request.
   - Exactly what was asked for and what your response was.
7. **Maintain a clear "no consent" boundary.** If pressured with statements like "innocent people have nothing to hide," repeat: "I will consider your request once I have received legal advice."

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain why you use specific apps or services.
- You do not need to decide the final scope of data sharing immediately.
- You do not need to "prove" that an account is empty or irrelevant.
- You do not need to provide passwords for old or secondary devices not in your immediate possession.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard legal procedure to refuse voluntary consent until a solicitor has reviewed the request. Police are trained to ask for broad consent, but you are entitled to insist on specific boundaries or professional legal review. Feeling pressured or anxious is a normal reaction to an official request for private data.

## Scope note
This guide covers the initial response to a request for digital consent. It does not replace the advice of a qualified solicitor once an investigation has begun or formal notices have been served.

## Important note
This information applies to the United Kingdom but police powers can differ under the Terrorism Act or when specific warrants are active. Providing false information or destroying evidence is a serious offence. Always request a solicitor when dealing with police digital evidence requests.
