# What to do if… an interviewer or recruiter asks sexual questions that feel unrelated to the role (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the interaction immediately, exit the environment, and prioritize your physical and digital safety. You are legally protected under the Equality Act 2010 from harassment during the recruitment process, and you do not need to answer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel obligated to justify your discomfort or explain why a question is inappropriate.
- Do not delete any evidence, including "disappearing" messages, call logs, or calendar invites.
- Do not agree to meet the person again in a private or "informal" setting to discuss what happened.
- Do not blame yourself for not "shutting it down" faster; boundary-crossing is the responsibility of the perpetrator.
- Do not assume you have lost the job opportunity solely by protecting your boundaries.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the boundary violation cues.** Recognise if the person is using specific "unwanted conduct" such as:
   - Questions about your sexual orientation, sex life, or relationship status.
   - Comments on your physical appearance, attire, or "attractiveness" for the role.
   - Questions about pregnancy, childcare, or future family plans.
   - Sexual jokes, suggestive remarks, or "testing" your reaction to inappropriate topics.
2. **Execute an immediate exit.** If you are in a physical office, stand up and state that the interview is over; if you are on a video or phone call, end the connection without waiting for a response.
   - You do not need to provide a detailed reason; "I am ending this interview now" is sufficient.
3. **Move to a safe public space.** Ensure you are in a location where you are not alone with the individual, especially if the interview took place in a private office or non-traditional workspace.
4. **Create a timestamped record of the event.** Write down exactly what was said as soon as possible, including the names of everyone present, the specific wording of the questions, and the time the interaction ended.
   - If the harassment occurred via email, LinkedIn, or text, **take screenshots immediately** and save them to a personal device.
5. **Isolate all future communication.** Move all contact to formal email only; do not answer phone calls or private social media messages from the recruiter or interviewer.
6. **Report the incident as a protected disclosure.** Since April 6, 2026, reporting sexual harassment is a "qualifying disclosure" under UK whistleblowing law, providing you with enhanced protection.
   - **If the person is from an agency:** Contact the agency’s Compliance or HR department in writing to report the recruiter.
   - **If the person is the employer:** Contact the company’s HR Director or Head of Recruitment.
7. **Contact the police if you feel in immediate danger.** If the individual follows you, makes threats, or if there was any unwanted physical contact, call 999 (emergency) or 101 (non-emergency).

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you want to file a claim with the Employment Tribunal (you generally have 3 months minus one day to do this).
- You do not have to decide if you still want the job; you can wait until you have processed the event.
- You do not need to respond to any "apology" or follow-up messages sent by the person who harassed you.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a "freeze" response or to feel "polite" when a professional in power behaves inappropriately. These are survival and social conditioning responses and do not mean you consented to the behavior. You have the right to a recruitment process that respects your dignity.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only to stabilize your immediate safety and preserve your legal rights. For long-term legal strategy or tribunal assistance, you must seek specialized advice.

## Important note
This guide provides general information regarding rights under the Equality Act 2010 (applicable in Great Britain) and the 2026 whistleblowing updates. It is not legal advice. If you are in Northern Ireland, different statutory rules may apply.
