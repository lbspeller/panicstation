# What to do if… you are asked for personal details to register a death and you do not have them (UK)

## Short answer
Inform the register office exactly what you do not know and do not guess; registrars can record details as "unknown" to allow the process to proceed.

## Do not do these things
- Don't guess dates, spellings, or addresses to "fill in the blanks" as correcting a death certificate later is a complex legal process.
- Don't wait until you have every document before contacting the registrar; the legal deadline (5 or 8 days) still applies.
- Don't assume you must provide the Medical Certificate of Cause of Death (MCCD) yourself; in 2026, these are sent electronically to the registrar by the doctor or Medical Examiner.
- Don't worry if you cannot find the National Insurance number or NHS number; while helpful, these are not mandatory for the registration itself.
- Don't allow a lack of information to stop you from acting as the informant if you are the closest relative available.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm that the registrar has received the electronic notification of death.**
   - Contact the Medical Examiner's office (England/Wales) or the certifying doctor (Scotland/NI) to ensure the paperwork has been sent to the register office.
   - You cannot complete the registration until the registrar has this electronic record.

2. **Create a "Known and Unknown" list of the following specific details required by the registrar.**
   - Full name and any previous names (e.g., maiden name).
   - Date and place of birth (town and county are usually sufficient if the exact address is unknown).
   - Last known occupation (or "retired").
   - Usual address at the time of death.
   - Full name and occupation of any surviving or deceased spouse or civil partner.

3. **Book the appointment at the register office for the area where the death occurred.**
   - **Explain clearly that you are missing some personal information.**
   - Ask them if they require you to bring any specific supporting documents you *do* have (e.g., the deceased's passport or birth certificate).

4. **Gather your own identification to bring to the appointment.**
   - You must bring proof of your own identity as the informant.
   - Recommended items include:
     - Your passport or driving licence.
     - A recent utility bill or bank statement showing your name and address.

5. **Search "safe spots" for 15 minutes only to find any missing data.**
   - Check the deceased's wallet, phone "Emergency Info," or the top drawer of their desk for letters from the DWP, local council, or NHS.
   - Contact the funeral director; they may have already gathered some of these details from the hospital or care home.

6. **State "Not known" clearly during the registrar's interview.**
   - **If the registrar asks a question you cannot answer, say: "I do not know this and I do not want to guess."**
   - The registrar will guide you on whether the field can be left blank or recorded as "Unknown."

7. **Request the "Tell Us Once" service at the end of the appointment (England, Wales, and Scotland only).**
   - **Ask for the Tell Us Once reference number even if you are missing some details like the National Insurance number.**
   - You can add more information to the Tell Us Once service online later (within 28 days) as you find it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find the deceased's Will before registering the death.
- You do not need to have a date for the funeral set before the registration.
- You do not need to resolve disagreements with other family members about the deceased's "usual address" or "occupation" before the appointment.
- You do not need to notify banks or private insurance companies today; this can wait until you have the physical death certificate.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for informants to not have a complete life history of the deceased, especially in cases of sudden death or family estrangement. Registrars are trained to handle "unknown" entries and will prioritize getting the death registered so that the funeral can proceed.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate first steps of death registration when information is missing. It does not cover long-term estate administration, probate, or the process for correcting a certificate once it has been signed.

## Important note
Registration laws differ across the UK; deaths must be registered within 5 days in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, and within 8 days in Scotland. Tell Us Once is not currently available in Northern Ireland. Always follow the specific instructions provided by your local registrar.
