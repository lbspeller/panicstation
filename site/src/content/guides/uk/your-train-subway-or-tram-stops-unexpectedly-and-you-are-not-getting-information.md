# What to do if… your train, subway, or tram stops unexpectedly and you are not getting information (UK)

## Short answer
Stay on board, keep away from the doors and tracks, and wait for staff instructions unless there is immediate danger. If there is smoke, fire, violence, serious illness, or someone is in danger, use the emergency alarm, intercom, or help point, whichever is available.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave the train, Tube, or tram, or step onto tracks, unless staff or emergency services tell you to, or there is immediate life-threatening danger where staying would be worse.
- Do not force doors open or use an emergency door release just to get off or get an update.
- Do not use the passenger alarm only for delay information; use it for safety, medical, security, or urgent welfare concerns.
- Do not walk along a track, tunnel, or trackside area because it looks quiet; trains, power rails, overhead wires, and hidden hazards may still be dangerous.
- Do not crowd around doors, alarms, or intercoms if others need space to breathe, sit, or speak to staff.
- Do not rely on rumours from other passengers as instructions to evacuate.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger.** Look and smell for smoke, fire, burning, or fumes. Notice violence, threats, someone collapsing, someone struggling to breathe, or anyone trapped in a door area. If none of this is happening, treat it as a disruption and stay put.

2. **Contact staff through the safest available route.** Use the emergency alarm, intercom, or help point if there is a safety concern, medical issue, violence, smoke, fire, or urgent welfare problem. Speak clearly: say what is happening, which carriage or vehicle you are in, and whether anyone needs urgent help.

3. **Call emergency services only for an emergency.** Call 999 or 112 if there is immediate threat to life, serious injury, fire, violence, or someone is in danger and you can safely make the call. Follow staff or emergency-service instructions over anything you read online.

4. **Make your location easier to identify.** Note the line or service, direction of travel, last station or stop, next expected station or stop, and any carriage, unit, or vehicle number you can see. If you have signal, check your operator’s official app or status page, but do not treat it as a substitute for staff instructions.

5. **Reduce heat, crowding, and panic pressure.** Step back from doors if you can. Leave space around alarms, intercoms, help points, wheelchair spaces, and anyone unwell. Sit or crouch if you feel faint, loosen tight clothing, sip water if you have it, and tell someone nearby if you may need help.

6. **If you feel unsafe, move towards safer contact, not isolation.** Move closer to other calm passengers, staff areas, or an intercom without blocking them. On the rail or Tube network, if you need police help and it is not an emergency, text British Transport Police on 61016 or call 0800 40 50 40 when safe. For tram systems, use staff, the operator’s emergency arrangements, or 999 in an emergency.

7. **If staff announce an evacuation, follow the instructions exactly.** Listen for which door or side to use, where to walk, whether power has been made safe, and what to leave behind. Take small essential items only if they will not delay you; leave anything staff tell you to leave.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your full reroute, refund, complaint, or onward plan while the vehicle is stopped.
- You do not need to keep refreshing apps or chasing rumours if it is draining your battery or attention.
- You do not need to message everyone in detail; one short message such as “stopped, unclear delay, safe for now, will update” is enough.
- You do not need to work out who is at fault right now.

## Important reassurance
Unexpected stops can happen during congestion, signal problems, safety checks, medical incidents, or other disruption. Lack of information is stressful, but it can mean staff are checking the safest next step; staying on board and waiting for clear instructions is usually the safest choice.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes to hour. Later decisions about onward travel, refunds, accessibility support, complaints, or formal reporting may need help from the operator, transport staff, police, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. In immediate danger, follow staff and emergency-service instructions and contact emergency services.
