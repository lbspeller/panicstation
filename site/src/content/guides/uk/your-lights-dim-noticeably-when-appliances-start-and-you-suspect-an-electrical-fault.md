# What to do if… your lights dim noticeably when appliances start and you suspect an electrical fault (UK)

## Short answer
Stop using the high-power appliances that trigger the dimming immediately and isolate the affected circuits if you see smoke, smell burning, or hear buzzing.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep turning the appliance on and off to "test" the fault.
- Don’t ignore a burning plastic smell, even if it is faint.
- Don’t touch any socket, switch, or appliance that feels hot or "tingles" to the touch.
- Don’t attempt to open the consumer unit (fuse box) or tighten internal wiring yourself.
- Don’t use block-style multiway adaptors to bypass a faulty socket.

## What to do now
1. **Unplug the triggering appliance immediately.** Turn off the specific kettle, shower, or heater that causes the lights to dip and leave it unplugged or isolated.
2. **Perform a 30-second sensory check for danger.** Look, listen, and smell for:
   - A smell of burning plastic or ozone.
   - Buzzing, crackling, or popping sounds from walls or the consumer unit.
   - Scorch marks or discolouration on sockets or plugs.
   - Lights that are staying very dim or becoming unusually bright.
3. **Isolate the power if danger signs are present.** Switch off the main red switch on your consumer unit if it is safe to reach.
   - If there is smoke or visible fire, evacuate everyone and call 999.
   - If you rent your home, contact your emergency out-of-hours repair line immediately after isolating the power.
4. **Report the issue to the correct authority.**
   - **In England, Scotland, or Wales:** Call **105** to reach your local Distribution Network Operator (DNO). They will check if the fault is on the external grid.
   - **In Northern Ireland:** Call **03457 643 643** to reach NIE Networks.
   - **For internal wiring:** If the DNO confirms the external supply is safe, contact a registered "Part P" qualified electrician immediately.
5. **Check with your neighbours.** Ask if their lights are also dimming; if they are, the fault is likely with the local network (DNO) rather than your home's wiring.
6. **Note the symptoms for the professional.** Document exactly which appliances trigger the dip and whether it affects the whole house or just one room.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific electrical theory (e.g., "lost neutral" vs "voltage drop") yourself.
- You do not need to replace your light bulbs or appliances until the wiring has been verified as safe.
- You do not need to decide on a full house rewire tonight.

## Important reassurance
Noticing these signs early is a positive step toward preventing a more serious electrical failure. Most voltage or wiring issues are manageable once identified by the correct professional, and following isolation protocols significantly reduces immediate risk.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for suspected electrical faults and voltage fluctuations. It does not replace the need for a mandatory inspection by a qualified electrician or network operator.

## Important note
Electrical faults carry a risk of fire or electric shock. If you observe sparks, smoke, or smell burning, treat the situation as an emergency. All repairs must be conducted by a registered, competent person in accordance with UK building regulations.
