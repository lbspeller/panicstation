# What to do if… your smart device is acting strangely and you suspect your account has been accessed (UK)

## Short answer
If it is safe, pause remote control by taking the affected device, hub, or bridge offline, then secure the account that controls it from a different trusted device.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep testing the device while it is still online; it may expose more information or delay securing the account.
- Don’t factory reset first unless the provider’s recovery steps require it because you are locked out.
- Don’t reuse an old password, a similar password, or a password you use anywhere else.
- Don’t click security-alert links from emails or texts about this incident; go to the provider’s app or website directly.
- Don’t give one-time codes, SMS codes, or authenticator codes to anyone claiming to be support.
- Don’t assume it is just a glitch if you see new users, new routines, new devices, login alerts, or changed settings.

## What to do now
1. **Pause remote control if it is safe.**
   - Unplug the device, remove its batteries, or power off the hub or bridge it uses.
   - If the device affects locks, alarms, heating, cameras, or care equipment, keep essential safety functions working and use a manual backup if you have one, such as a physical key or manual control.

2. **Use a different trusted device to secure the controlling account.**
   - Use a phone or computer you trust, preferably on mobile data or a Wi-Fi network you trust.
   - Change the smart-home account password to a long, unique password.
   - Sign out of all other sessions or devices; look for settings such as active sessions, logged-in devices, or log out everywhere.
   - Turn on two-step verification or two-factor authentication for that account.

3. **Secure the email account that can reset the smart-home account.**
   - Change the email password if you suspect it may also be at risk.
   - Sign out of other sessions.
   - Turn on two-step verification.
   - Check forwarding rules, filters, recovery email addresses, and recovery phone numbers, and remove anything you did not set up.

4. **Remove unknown access inside the smart-home app.**
   - Look for household members, home members, shared users, guests, linked services, integrations, and old phones or tablets.
   - Remove anything you do not recognise or no longer use.
   - Check whether any new device, bridge, voice assistant, camera, lock, or automation service has been added.

5. **Check for changed behaviour that could keep access open.**
   - Review automations, routines, scenes, schedules, notification settings, camera privacy settings, microphone settings, and lock codes.
   - Remove unfamiliar routines, such as cameras turning off at certain times, microphones enabling, lights changing at night, doors unlocking, or notifications being silenced.
   - Change smart-lock PINs, keypad codes, and guest codes if the device uses them.

6. **Lock down your home Wi-Fi basics.**
   - Log in to your router app or router admin page and change the router admin password, not just the Wi-Fi password.
   - Update the router through the router app, admin page, or your broadband provider’s instructions.
   - Disable WPS if the setting is available.
   - Change the Wi-Fi password and reconnect only devices you still use.
   - Remove unknown devices from the router’s connected-device list if your router shows one.

7. **Only after the account is secure, reset and re-pair the smart device.**
   - Factory reset the affected device, hub, or bridge using the provider’s instructions.
   - Set it up again using the newly secured account.
   - Apply device updates during setup.
   - Do not reuse old device PINs, old guest codes, default passwords, or old shared access.

8. **Write down what you saw.**
   - Note the date and time, what changed, and which device or account was affected.
   - Save screenshots of login alerts, new users, new routines, unknown devices, changed lock codes, or changed camera settings.
   - Keep this lightweight; the aim is to help support, your bank, or a reporting service understand what happened.

9. **Get outside help if money, identity, stalking, harassment, or physical safety is involved.**
   - If you lost money or see unusual transactions, contact your bank or card provider immediately using the number in your banking app, on your card, or on the official website.
   - If you live in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland and want to report fraud or cyber crime, use Report Fraud.
   - If you live in Scotland, or the crime happened in Scotland, report cyber crime to Police Scotland by 101 or through another current Police Scotland non-emergency route.
   - If the device affects physical access, such as a smart lock, doorbell, alarm, or garage door, treat this as a home security issue as well and change physical access codes, spare-key access, and manual locks where needed.
   - If this may be linked to stalking, harassment, domestic abuse, or immediate danger, prioritise personal safety and contact emergency or specialist support in the safest way available to you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to redesign your whole smart home setup now.
- You do not need to buy replacement devices before you have secured the account, email, router, and access list.
- You do not need to decide now whether to close accounts you rely on every day.
- You do not need a deep technical investigation before taking the basic lockout steps above.
- You can decide later whether to make a formal report, unless money is missing, the incident is ongoing, or there is a safety risk.

## Important reassurance
Odd smart-device behaviour can come from updates, outages, settings changes, or a simple fault. Securing the account first is still a sensible first step because it reduces the chance of ongoing access, privacy loss, or being locked out.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and regain control. Later decisions, such as replacing devices, making a formal complaint, investigating a wider compromise, or getting help with stalking or harassment, may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, technical, financial, security, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe, or the situation involves stalking, harassment, coercive control, or immediate home security risk, prioritise personal safety and seek appropriate help.
