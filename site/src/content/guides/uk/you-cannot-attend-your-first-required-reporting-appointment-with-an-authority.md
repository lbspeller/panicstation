# What to do if… you cannot attend your first required reporting appointment with an authority (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the authority before the appointment time, or as soon as you realise there is a problem, using the contact details on your official paperwork. Say briefly why you cannot attend, ask what you should do next, and keep proof that you tried to contact them.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the appointment because you have a good reason; non-attendance may still be treated as non-compliance unless you explain it quickly.
- Do not assume a new time is agreed until the authority confirms it.
- Do not rely on a friend, family member, or support worker to pass the message on unless your paperwork says that is allowed.
- Do not send a long explanation with unnecessary details; keep it short, factual, and easy to match to your case.
- Do not invent a reason, alter documents, or give false information.
- Do not delete call logs, emails, texts, letters, travel alerts, appointment letters, or other evidence that may show what happened.

## What to do now
1. Check the paperwork and write down:
   - the exact date and time of the first appointment
   - where or how you were told to report
   - who set the requirement, such as police, probation, licence supervision, or Home Office reporting
   - any reference number, case number, custody number, bail reference, probation contact, licence contact, or Home Office reference.

2. Contact the authority using the route on the paperwork. Call the number given, and also email or message if the paperwork gives that option. If you cannot find the paperwork, use only an official website, a solicitor, or a known office number to find the correct contact route.

3. Use a short message like this: “I am required to report on [date and time]. I cannot attend because [brief reason]. Please tell me what I should do next and whether there is an alternative time I must attend.”

4. Ask for the safest confirmed next step. This may be a different time, a later appointment, or another instruction, but do not treat it as changed unless they confirm it.

5. If the requirement is police bail, pre-charge bail, or court bail, contact your solicitor immediately if you have one. Ask them to help notify the relevant custody officer, officer in the case, court contact, or other contact shown on your paperwork before the appointment time if possible.

6. If the requirement is probation, licence supervision, or post-sentence supervision, contact the named probation officer, offender manager, responsible officer, or probation office on the paperwork. Say clearly that this is your first appointment and that you need urgent instructions.

7. If the requirement is a Home Office immigration reporting appointment, contact the reporting centre by telephone or email. In any email, include your name, date of birth, Home Office reference, mobile phone number, appointment date, and a brief reason you cannot attend.

8. If you cannot get through, keep trying in a structured way. Make a simple log with the time, number or email used, what happened, and any voicemail or automated response.

9. If the appointment time passes before you manage to speak to anyone, contact them again immediately. Say that you missed the appointment, explain briefly why, mention the attempts you made before the appointment, and ask for the earliest confirmed instruction.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a full legal explanation today.
- You do not need to decide whether the reporting condition is fair right now.
- You do not need to gather every possible document before making contact; make contact first, then send evidence if asked.
- You do not need to solve the underlying work, travel, childcare, health, or money problem before telling the authority there is a problem.

## Important reassurance
Practical problems with first appointments do happen, including late letters, illness, transport disruption, and confusion about where to go. The safest first move is usually quick direct contact, a clear request for instructions, and a saved proof trail.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce immediate risk from a missed reporting requirement. Later decisions about changing conditions, challenging a requirement, making a complaint, or dealing with a breach may need specialist legal advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, criminal defence, probation, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Reporting requirements can be strict and can vary by paperwork, authority, and case, so follow the official instructions you are given and ask for clarification in writing where possible.
