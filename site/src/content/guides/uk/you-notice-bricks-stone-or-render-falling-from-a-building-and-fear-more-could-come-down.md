# What to do if… you notice bricks, stone, or render falling from a building and fear more could come down (UK)

## Short answer
Move well away from the building, keep other people out of the drop zone, and treat it as a life-safety hazard. If debris is still falling, someone is hurt, or anyone could be struck, call 999 immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t stand under the area that is shedding material, including doorways, shopfronts, bay windows, balconies, parapets, or signs.
- Don’t go into the fall zone to clear debris, check above, or look up from directly underneath.
- Don’t go closer to see how bad it is or try to catch, hold, pull, or prop anything.
- Don’t try quick DIY fixes such as tape, screws, sealant, propping, or removing loose pieces.
- Don’t route people, children, mobility aids, pets, or deliveries through the danger zone if there is any safer route.
- Don’t assume it is safe because it has stopped for now; more material may fall without warning.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance immediately.** Move to the opposite side of the street if you can, or at least well beyond where pieces are landing. Avoid standing below overhangs or directly under the façade.
2. **Keep others back from a safe position.** Warn nearby people calmly: “Debris is falling; please move back.” If you can do it without going closer, ask a nearby shop, resident, or building reception to keep people away from the affected doorway or pavement.
3. **Call 999 if there is immediate danger.** Call 999 if debris is actively falling, someone is injured, the public are still passing underneath, traffic is affected, or you think part of the building could collapse.
4. **If it is not an immediate 999 situation, report it to the relevant local authority route.**
   - In England and Wales, report a dangerous building or structure to the local council, often through Building Control or a dangerous structures service.
   - In Scotland, contact the local authority Building Standards service about a dangerous building.
   - In Northern Ireland, contact the local council Building Control service about a dangerous or derelict property.
5. **Give clear location details.** Say the exact address or nearest shop name or building number, which side of the building is affected, what is falling, whether it is still falling, and whether the pavement, doorway, road, or neighbouring property is affected.
6. **If you live, work, or have access there, reduce exposure without going near the façade.**
   - Keep people away from windows, doors, balconies, and rooms directly below the affected area.
   - Do not use entrances or exits beneath it; use another route if one is safely available.
   - If you can do so safely from inside, keep others out of rooms beside or below the fall zone.
7. **Tell the person responsible for the building after emergency calls or reports.** Contact the building owner, landlord, managing agent, shop manager, or building management and say there may be a dangerous structure risk affecting people outside.
8. **Document only from a safe distance.** If it is safe, take a photo or video from well back to show the location and debris on the ground, and note the time. Do not move closer for a better picture.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now who is at fault.
- You do not need to argue with the owner, landlord, contractor, or neighbour.
- You do not need quotes, repair planning, insurance paperwork, or a full building history right now.
- You do not need to clean up debris from the fall zone; leave it until the area has been made safe.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to treat falling masonry, stone, or render as serious even if the pieces look small. Your first job is only to get people away from where more could fall and alert the right responders.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce the risk of injury and trigger an official safety response. Later decisions about inspection, making-safe work, liability, repairs, rent, access, or insurance may need help from building professionals, the building owner, the local authority, or a specialist adviser.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, engineering, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If someone could be struck or the structure may collapse, treat it as an emergency and call 999.
