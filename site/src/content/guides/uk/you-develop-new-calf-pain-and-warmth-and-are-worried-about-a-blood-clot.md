# What to do if… you develop new calf pain and warmth and are worried about a blood clot (UK)

## Short answer
If you think you might have a DVT, get urgent same-day medical advice: call 111 where available, use 111 online in England, or contact your GP practice or local out-of-hours service. If you also have breathlessness, chest pain, coughing blood, fainting, collapse, or you feel severely unwell, call 999 or go to A&E.

## Do not do these things
- Do not massage, deep-rub, or use a foam roller on the painful calf to try to “break it up”.
- Do not do strenuous exercise or long walks to “test it out”.
- Do not take leftover or borrowed blood thinners, or double up aspirin, to self-treat.
- Do not ignore new symptoms because you can still walk on the leg.
- Do not start a long car, train, or plane journey to “wait it out” if you can avoid it; prioritise getting assessed.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you feel unwell, faint, breathless, or have chest pain.

## What to do now
1. Check right now for emergency symptoms. Call 999 or go to A&E if you have leg symptoms and any of: new breathlessness, chest pain, coughing blood, fainting, collapse, or feeling severely unwell. Do not drive yourself; ask someone to take you or call an ambulance.
2. If there are no emergency symptoms, get urgent same-day advice. Call 111 where available, use 111 online in England, or ask your GP practice for an urgent same-day appointment. In Northern Ireland, contact your GP practice, or GP out-of-hours if the practice is closed. Say clearly: “New calf pain and warmth in one leg, worried about DVT or a blood clot.”
3. Follow the advice you are given and go promptly if you are told to attend A&E, an urgent assessment service, or another same-day service. If you need to travel and you feel at all unwell, ask someone else to take you or use a taxi rather than driving.
4. While you are waiting to be assessed, keep the leg calm.
   - Do not rub or massage it.
   - Avoid strenuous activity.
   - Do not “test it” with a long walk.
   - Gentle, normal movement around the house is usually OK, but keep things low-effort and prioritise being seen.
5. Gather key details to tell the clinician.
   - When the symptoms started.
   - Whether it is one leg or both legs.
   - Any swelling, redness, tenderness, warmth, or visible vein changes.
   - Any recent long travel, immobility, surgery, injury, pregnancy or recent birth, cancer treatment, previous clots, or close family history of clots.
   - Medicines you take, especially combined hormonal contraception, HRT, and any anticoagulants you already take.
6. If you are given tests or treatment today, follow the instructions exactly. You may be sent for a leg ultrasound, blood tests, or both, and you may be started on treatment if the clinician thinks it is needed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether it is definitely a clot or a strain; the priority is being checked.
- You do not need to research risk percentages, buy supplements, or change diet or exercise habits right now.
- You do not need to measure your calf repeatedly or take photos every few minutes. Note changes once, then focus on being seen.
- You do not need to stop prescribed medicines unless a clinician tells you to.

## Important reassurance
Many things can cause calf pain and warmth, and not every painful warm calf is a clot. New one-sided calf symptoms are still worth urgent assessment, because getting checked is safer than trying to guess at home.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: they are meant to help you get safely assessed and avoid common panic mistakes. Later decisions about tests, treatment, travel, work, exercise, and follow-up may need specialist medical advice.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. It is not a diagnosis. If symptoms worsen or you develop chest pain, breathlessness, coughing blood, fainting, collapse, or you feel severely unwell, treat that as an emergency and call 999 or go to A&E.
