# What to do if… you receive a message containing your location that you did not share (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as a safety issue first: get to a safer setting, do not reply, and preserve the message. If you feel in immediate danger, or the sender says they can see you, are nearby, or may be coming to you, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply to test them, argue, or ask how they found you.
- Do not click links, open attachments, scan QR codes, or install anything they send.
- Do not confirm the location is right or send photos, routines, plans, or live location.
- Do not agree to meet, move to a private chat app, or prove anything because they demand it.
- Do not send money, gift cards, crypto, or bank details if they threaten you.
- Do not post about it publicly while you may still be exposed.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause point.** If you are alone, go somewhere with other people if you can, such as a shop, café, reception, neighbour, or trusted person’s place. If you are at home and staying there is safest, lock doors and avoid giving the sender any more information.
2. **Decide whether this is an emergency.**
   - If you think you are at risk right now, call 999.
   - If it is not immediate danger but you feel threatened, harassed, stalked, or scared, call 101 or report to your local police online.
3. **Preserve the message before blocking.** Take screenshots showing the sender name, number or handle, timestamps, the location text, links, usernames, and any threats or payment demands. Keep the original thread if you can do so safely.
4. **Stop obvious location sharing.** Check messaging apps, map apps, family sharing, “Find My” style services, social media location tags, and recent posts. Turn off live location sharing you did not mean to leave on.
5. **Check app location permissions.** On your phone, review which apps can use location and set them to “Never” or “While using” where that is safer. Remove location access from apps you do not recognise or do not need.
6. **Lock down the account and email route.** Check your email account for forwarding rules or filters you did not create. Then change the password on your email account, change the password on the account the message arrived on, sign out of other sessions or devices where possible, and turn on two-step verification where it is offered.
7. **Report it in the right place.** Report the message inside the app or platform, then block the sender. If there is cyber crime, fraud, account takeover, extortion, or a scam angle, use Report Fraud if you are in England, Wales or Northern Ireland. If you are in Scotland, contact Police Scotland on 101 for non-emergency reporting, or 999 if you are in immediate danger.
8. **Tell one trusted person what is happening.** Send them where you are, what the message said, and what you are doing next. If you need to move, ask them to stay on the phone or meet you somewhere public.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly how they got the location right now.
- You do not need to confront anyone you suspect.
- You do not need to message mutual contacts immediately.
- You do not need to delete every account or scrub your whole online presence right now.
- Deeper device checks, changing numbers, changing accounts, and longer-term privacy cleanup can wait until you are safer and less overwhelmed.

## Important reassurance
A message like this is often designed to make you panic and react. The location may have come from something ordinary, such as an old post, a photo tag, broad IP-based location, a data leak, or location sharing that was left on. Even if it turns out to be a bluff, the calm safety steps above are still reasonable.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: they are meant to reduce immediate risk, preserve useful information, and stop obvious location or account exposure. If the messages continue, later decisions may need specialist help, police support, platform support, or personal safety planning.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe, use emergency services.
