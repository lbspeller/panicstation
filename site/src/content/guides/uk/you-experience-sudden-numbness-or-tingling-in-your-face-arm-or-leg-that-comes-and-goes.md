# What to do if… you experience sudden numbness or tingling in your face, arm, or leg that comes and goes (UK)

## Short answer
Treat sudden, one-sided numbness or tingling as a medical emergency and call **999** immediately, even if the symptoms have already disappeared or are currently resolving.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if the symptoms improve further or stop completely before seeking help.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital; wait for an ambulance or follow 999 dispatcher instructions.
- Do not take aspirin or any other medication until a paramedic or doctor tells you to.
- Do not eat or drink anything until you have been assessed by a clinician.
- Do not ignore the episode just because it was brief or felt like a "pinched nerve."

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 immediately and request an ambulance for a suspected stroke or TIA.** 
2. **Tell the operator the exact time the numbness or tingling started and if it is still happening.**
3. **Perform the FAST test to check for other hidden symptoms.**
   - Face: Can you smile evenly, or does one side of your mouth or eye droop?
   - Arms: Can you raise both arms and keep them there, or does one drift down?
   - Speech: Can you speak clearly, or is your voice slurred or garbled?
4. **Unlock your front door and clear a path for paramedics to enter.**
5. **Sit or lie down on the floor to prevent injury in case of a fall or sudden weakness.**
6. **Gather your current medications or a list of your prescriptions to show the ambulance crew.**
7. **If the symptoms happened more than 24 hours ago and have fully resolved, call your GP for a same-day urgent appointment or call 111.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the cause of the numbness (such as a migraine or nerve issue) yourself.
- You do not need to contact your workplace or cancel upcoming appointments until you are at the hospital.
- You do not need to research "mini-stroke" treatments online; hospital assessment is the only reliable next step.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel hesitant about calling 999 when symptoms are "coming and goes" or seem minor. Paramedics and doctors prefer to assess you and rule out a stroke rather than have you wait at home. Feeling confused or worried during these episodes is a common response to sudden neurological changes.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for suspected TIA or stroke in the UK. It does not cover long-term recovery, diagnostic testing, or chronic neurological conditions.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only and does not constitute medical advice or a formal diagnosis. Sudden neurological symptoms require immediate professional assessment by the NHS to prevent a major stroke.
