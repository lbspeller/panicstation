# What to do if… you are asked to document your work processes and you suspect your job is being outsourced (UK)

## Short answer
Pause, get the request clearly defined in writing, and respond professionally while quietly creating a dated record of what is being asked and what you provide.

## Do not do these things
- Do not refuse, threaten, or accuse anyone of outsourcing in writing based on suspicion.
- Do not delete, alter, or “clean up” work systems, logs, tickets, emails, or files to protect yourself.
- Do not take confidential documents or customer data home or to personal accounts “just in case”.
- Do not add speculation, such as “this is to replace me”, into the notes or into chats or emails.
- Do not agree to unrealistic deadlines or scope in the moment if you are panicking. Slow it down and clarify.
- Do not sign new confidentiality, settlement, or exit paperwork on the spot if it appears. Ask for time to read it and get advice.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the request in writing, or summarise it back in writing.**  
   Ask what exactly they want, such as SOPs, runbooks, screenshots, workflow maps, or checklists. Ask how detailed it should be, which processes are included, who the audience is, and when it is needed. If it was verbal, send a short recap message: “To confirm, you are asking for X, covering Y, by Z date.”

2. **Clarify boundaries before you start writing.**  
   Ask whether there is a preferred template, where it should live, such as a company wiki or shared drive, and what must be excluded, such as credentials, security details, client personal data, or confidential vendor terms. If they want “everything”, ask them to prioritise the top 3 to 5 processes first.

3. **Create a private, factual timeline for yourself, keeping it non-confidential.**  
   On your own device or paper, keep dated notes of who asked, what was asked, deadlines, meetings scheduled, and what you delivered, such as file names and locations. Do not copy company documents, screenshots, customer data, or internal files into your personal notes, and do not forward work emails or files to personal accounts.

4. **Deliver transferable documentation, not personal extras.**  
   Write clear steps and checks, but avoid adding personal commentary, blame, or anything that reads like an argument. Use neutral language such as “If X happens, escalate to [team or role]” rather than naming individuals unless required.

5. **Ask one direct process question that forces clarity without confrontation.**  
   Use calm wording, such as:
   - “Is this part of business continuity planning or a wider restructure?”
   - “Will there be any consultation or changes to the team structure connected to this work?”

6. **If you believe your role may be affected, start using the right channels early.**  
   - If you have a union, contact your rep and share the facts: what was requested, when, by whom, and any meetings scheduled.
   - If redundancy is mentioned or you are told you are “at risk”, ask what consultation process will be followed and when.
   - If outsourcing or a contract change is openly discussed, ask whether a TUPE transfer or service-provider change is being considered and how staff will be informed or consulted.

7. **If pressure escalates or you are singled out, get support before it becomes disciplinary.**  
   If you are being threatened for asking for clarity, consider getting early advice from Acas and, if relevant, checking your workplace grievance process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, “fight it”, or confront leadership.
- You do not need to decide a full job-search plan in this moment. Stabilise the request and the paper trail first.
- You do not need to negotiate redundancy or severance terms unless and until something concrete is put to you.

## Important reassurance
Employers ask for better documentation for many reasons, including audit, business continuity, quality control, training, and scaling. It is also understandable to feel alarmed when the request looks like knowledge transfer. You can protect yourself by slowing the process down, keeping everything factual, and making expectations and processes clear without doing anything dramatic or risky.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance to help you avoid panic mistakes, create time, and protect your position. If the situation becomes a formal restructure, redundancy process, TUPE situation, disciplinary issue, or settlement discussion, you may need specialist advice based on your contract and facts.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Employment situations turn on specific facts, including your contract, employment status, sector, workplace policies, length of service, and what your employer actually communicates. If you feel under immediate pressure, get personalised advice from a qualified employment adviser or union.
