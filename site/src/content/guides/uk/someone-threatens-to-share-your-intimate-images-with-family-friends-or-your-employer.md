# What to do if… someone threatens to share your intimate images with family, friends, or your employer (UK)

## Short answer
Stop communicating with them. Do not pay, send more images, or negotiate; focus on basic proof, account security, and UK support routes.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay money, send gift cards, send crypto, or agree to “one last thing”.
- Don’t send more images or give them access to any account.
- Don’t negotiate, argue, threaten them back, or try to prove whether they really have the images.
- Don’t wipe chats, delete accounts, or close payment records in a panic before saving basic details.
- Don’t forward intimate images to anyone “for proof” or ask friends to view them.
- Don’t screenshot, record, or share an intimate image if anyone in it may be under 18.
- Don’t hire random “takedown” or “hack back” services that contact the blackmailer for you.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Put the device down for a minute and move somewhere you are not being watched or pressured. If someone is in immediate danger, someone is using violence, or someone is threatening violence, call **999**.
2. **Stop contact.** Do not reply again. Block the account, number, email address, and any new accounts they use.
3. **Report the account on the platform.** Use the in-app report route for blackmail, harassment, sextortion, intimate image abuse, or non-consensual intimate images. Save any report confirmation if it appears.
4. **Save basic proof, not everything.** Keep the threat message, username or handle, profile link, payment demand, bank or wallet details if given, platform name, date, and time. Screenshots of threats and usernames can help. Only screenshot an intimate image if you are sure everyone shown is over 18.
5. **Secure the accounts they contacted or mentioned.** Change passwords for email and the affected social accounts. Turn on two-factor authentication. Set accounts to private where you can, and limit who can see your contacts, followers, friends list, phone number, or workplace.
6. **If you paid anything, stop now.** Do not send more. Contact your bank, card provider, or payment platform and say you are being blackmailed and need help stopping or tracing the payment.
7. **Use the right UK reporting route.** You can use your local police website or call **101** for non-emergency help. If you are under 18, you can report online sexual abuse to **CEOP**, and you can ask a trusted adult or Childline to help you.
8. **Use a removal-prevention tool if it fits.** If you are currently over 18, were 18 or older when the image was taken, are the person in the image, and still have the image or video, **StopNCII.org** may help by creating a digital fingerprint for participating platforms. If you are under 18, **Childline Report Remove** is the more appropriate route.
9. **Get specialist support.** The Revenge Porn Helpline supports adults in the UK affected by intimate image abuse, sextortion, and threats to share images.
10. **If the threat targets your workplace, keep it brief.** You can tell HR or a trusted manager: “I’m being harassed and blackmailed online. Someone may try to contact the company with fake or private material. Please do not engage with them and route anything to HR.” You do not need to give details.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide now whether to tell family, friends, or your employer in advance.
- You don’t need to write a public post or defend yourself online.
- You don’t need to collect perfect evidence before asking for help.
- You don’t need to know the exact offence name before reporting or getting support.
- You don’t need to decide any legal, work, relationship, or long-term steps tonight.

## Important reassurance
This threat is designed to make you panic and act quickly. Feeling scared, ashamed, frozen, or sick is a common reaction, and it does not mean you did anything wrong. Slowing down is a protective step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on stabilising the situation, reducing exposure, and getting you to specialist help. Later decisions about police, work, relationships, legal options, or platform escalation may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If a child or young person is involved, prioritise child-protection reporting routes and specialist support.
