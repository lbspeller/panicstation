# What to do if… you hear water running inside walls when no taps or appliances are on (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible hidden leak: turn off your main stopcock, also called a stop tap, and check whether the sound changes. If you cannot safely turn the water off, or the sound continues, contact an emergency plumber, or your landlord or agent if you rent.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore a new running-water sound; hidden leaks can damage walls, ceilings, floors, and electrics.
- Don’t start opening walls or floors in a panic.
- Don’t touch sockets, switches, lights, or electrical equipment if there is any sign of water nearby.
- Don’t force a stuck stopcock with excessive force.
- Don’t run taps for long to test the noise; it may feed an active leak.
- Don’t climb into unsafe spaces or lift heavy meter covers.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause and scan for immediate risk.** Look for new damp patches, dripping, a bulging or sagging ceiling, pooling water, or water near lights, sockets, or the consumer unit. Keep people and pets away from any wet area.
2. **Turn off the water at the main stopcock.** Common locations include under the kitchen sink, under the stairs, near the front door, in a hallway cupboard or utility cupboard, or where the water pipe enters the home. Turn it clockwise. You should not need to force it.
3. **Open a cold tap briefly to confirm the supply is off.** The water may take a short time to slow or stop because water remains in the pipes. If the tap keeps running normally, the stopcock may not be fully closed or may not control the part of the system where the leak is.
4. **Listen again after the stopcock is off.** If the wall noise stops or reduces, tell the plumber or landlord that the sound changed after isolating the supply. If the noise continues, do not assume one cause; it may involve a shared supply, a supply pipe, heating pipework, a tank, a toilet cistern, or another source.
5. **If you can check your water meter safely, use it only as a quick clue.** Turn off appliances that use water, do not lift a heavy cover, and do not check a meter in an unsafe place. If the meter still moves after the internal stopcock is closed, it may suggest a supply pipe leak or another supply arrangement. Report this to your water company and the plumber.
6. **If water is anywhere near electrics, avoid contact.** Do not touch wet switches, sockets, appliances, lights, or exposed wiring. If the consumer unit is clearly dry and safe to reach, switch off the electricity there. If the consumer unit area is wet, damaged, sparking, overheating, or unsafe to approach, leave it and get urgent professional help.
7. **Contact the right help for your situation.**
   - Owner-occupier: contact an emergency plumber and say you suspect a hidden leak in or behind a wall and have shut off the stopcock, or could not do so.
   - Tenant: contact your landlord or agent repairs line straight away and say there is a suspected active leak; explain whether you have shut off the stopcock.
   - Flat or maisonette: alert neighbours below or next door and the building manager, because leaks can travel.
8. **Limit damage while you wait, without risky DIY.** Put towels or buckets under visible drips, move valuables away from the wet area, and keep away from sagging ceilings.
9. **Record the basics.** Take quick photos or videos of damp patches, drips, the meter reading if safe, and the stopcock position. Note the time you first heard the sound and what changed after turning the stopcock off.
10. **If the leak may be on the supply pipe outside your home, contact your water company.** Ask what they are responsible for, what you may be responsible for, and whether they offer any help with finding or repairing supply pipe leaks.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide who is to blame right now.
- You do not need to open walls, lift floors, or strip rooms before a professional has found the leak.
- You do not need to complete the whole insurance process before stopping the water. Once the immediate leak is controlled, check your policy or contact your insurer.
- You do not need to work out final repairs, drying, redecorating, or mould checks in the first few minutes.

## Important reassurance
A running-water sound inside a wall can feel alarming, but the first useful actions are simple: stop the water if you safely can, avoid wet electrics, and get the right help involved. You are trying to reduce damage and buy time, not solve the whole repair immediately.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance to stabilise a suspected hidden leak. Later decisions about plumbing repairs, electrical checks, drying, damp, mould, insurance, tenancy responsibilities, or supply pipe responsibility may need specialist help.
