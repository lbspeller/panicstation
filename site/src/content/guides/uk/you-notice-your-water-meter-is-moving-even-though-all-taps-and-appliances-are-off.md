# What to do if… you notice your water meter is moving even though all taps and appliances are off (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate your property by turning off the internal stop tap (stopcock) and check the meter; if it continues moving, the leak is likely on your external supply pipe, whereas if it stops, the leak is inside your home.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore a moving meter even if there are no visible puddles; underground leaks can erode foundations.
- Don’t attempt to dig up the external supply pipe yourself without a professional survey.
- Don’t force a seized stopcock; use a lubricant or contact a plumber if it will not turn by hand.
- Don’t assume the water company is responsible for the entire pipe; homeowners are typically responsible for the pipe from the property boundary to the house.
- Don’t leave hoses or irrigation systems connected while performing a meter test.

## What to do now
1. **Identify and locate any obvious internal leaks immediately.** Check behind toilets, under sinks, and around the boiler for dampness or hissing. 
   - Do not touch any wet electrical outlets or appliances.
   - If water is near electrics, turn off the power at the consumer unit (fuse box) if safe to do so.
2. **Turn off the internal stop tap (stopcock) completely.** This is usually found under the kitchen sink, in a hallway cupboard, or under the stairs. Turn the handle clockwise until it stops.
3. **Confirm the water is fully isolated.** Open the cold tap in the kitchen; the water should run for a few seconds and then stop completely. If water continues to flow, your stop tap is faulty and the test cannot be completed accurately.
4. **Check the water meter for movement with the stop tap off.** Observe the small dial or digits on the meter (often located in a chamber in the pavement or near the property boundary).
   - If the meter **continues to move**: the leak is between the meter and your house (the external supply pipe). 
   - If the meter **stops moving**: the leak is inside your home plumbing system (after the stop tap).
5. **Take a timed meter reading to measure the rate of loss.** Write down all digits (including red numbers) and take a photo. Wait exactly 30 minutes with the water still isolated and take a second reading.
6. **Check your water provider's smart dashboard if applicable.** If you have a 2026-standard smart meter, log in to your provider’s app (e.g., Thames Water, Anglian Water, or Yorkshire Water) to view "continuous flow" alerts or hourly usage data.
7. **Contact the correct professional for repair.** 
   - For internal leaks: Use the **WaterSafe** website to find an approved plumber.
   - For external supply pipe leaks: Contact your water provider to report the leak, but be prepared to hire a private contractor for the section on your land.
   - For tenants: Contact your landlord or letting agent immediately with the results of your stop tap test.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact point of an underground leak yourself.
- You do not need to pay any "Estimated" bills immediately if you have confirmed an active leak; contact the provider for a "Leak Allowance" form once the repair is done.
- You do not need to replace the entire plumbing system; most internal leaks are caused by a single valve or joint.

## Important reassurance
Finding a leak via the meter is the most effective way to prevent catastrophic property damage. Most water companies in the UK provide a "leakage allowance" which credits your account for the cost of the wasted water once you provide proof that the leak has been repaired by a qualified professional within a specific timeframe (usually 30 days).

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only to identify the location and responsibility of a water leak. It does not cover technical plumbing repairs or legal disputes regarding shared supply pipes.

## Important note
This guide is for information purposes and does not constitute professional engineering or legal advice. If you cannot locate your stop tap or the meter is in a dangerous location (such as a busy road), contact your water provider’s emergency line for assistance.
