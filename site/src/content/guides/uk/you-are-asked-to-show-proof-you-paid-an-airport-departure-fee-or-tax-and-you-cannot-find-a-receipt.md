# What to do if… you are asked to show proof you paid an airport departure fee or tax and you cannot find a receipt (UK)

## Short answer
Request a list of acceptable alternative proof from the official at the desk, such as a digital e-ticket, card transaction record, or system verification. If forced to pay again to board, do so only at a fixed cashier desk and obtain a fresh physical receipt immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hand over your passport or mobile device to anyone claiming to be a "helper" who is not behind a fixed official counter.
- Don’t pay any individual who approaches you in a queue or transit area away from a designated airline or airport authority desk.
- Don’t engage in lengthy policy debates or arguments about the tax rules; focus solely on the evidence required to proceed.
- Don’t delete any email confirmations, app notifications, or bank alerts while searching for documents.
- Don’t pay a second time without first asking the official to check the airline’s internal system for a "tax paid" status.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact evidence requested by the official.** **Ask for a specific list of documents that will satisfy the requirement right now.**
   - Request to know if an e-ticket, itemised invoice, or mobile banking transaction is sufficient.
2. **Move to a fixed, official service location.** **Ensure you are speaking to staff at a clearly marked airline check-in desk, gate desk, or airport finance counter.**
   - Look for staff wearing high-visibility ID badges and uniforms.
   - Avoid interacting with mobile "agents" in the terminal.
3. **Open your airline’s mobile application or "Manage Booking" page.** **Locate the "Receipt," "Tax Breakdown," or "Invoice" section of your active itinerary.**
   - If travel was booked through an agent, check the agent’s app for an itemised bill showing "Taxes and Fees" as paid.
4. **Perform a high-speed search of your email inbox.** **Use keywords including the airline name, "receipt," "invoice," "tax," "fee," and your "booking reference" or "PNR" number.**
   - Check the "Spam" or "Promotions" folders if the confirmation is not in the primary inbox.
5. **Access your mobile banking or digital wallet app.** **Find the transaction matching the date and amount of your initial ticket purchase or fee payment.**
   - Show the merchant name, date, and "Cleared" status to the official.
6. **Request a system verification from the airline agent.** **Ask the staff to check if your booking status confirms all mandatory airport taxes have been settled.**
   - If they cannot see it, ask if a "duplicate receipt" or "proof of payment" can be printed from their terminal.
7. **Complete a secondary payment only at an official kiosk if no proof is found.** **Use a credit or debit card for the transaction to ensure an independent bank record of the double-payment exists.**
   - Insist on a physical receipt that includes the date, time, airport name, and the specific name of the fee.
8. **Capture a digital photograph of all new documents immediately.** **Take a clear photo of any new receipt or written instruction provided by the airport staff before moving to the next checkpoint.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to contact your bank to dispute the original charge while still at the airport.
- You do not need to file a formal complaint or refund request until after you have completed your journey.
- You do not need to contact the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office (FCDO) or the British embassy unless you are being detained, threatened with arrest, or denied access to your passport.

## Important reassurance
Administrative errors regarding airport fees are common in jurisdictions where taxes are not always bundled into the ticket price. Most officials will accept a digital record or a system check once you have identified yourself at a fixed desk. Panic or frustration is a normal reaction to potential delays, but staying at the counter usually leads to a resolution.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for document disputes at an airport. It is intended to prevent scams and help you proceed through a checkpoint. It does not cover legal claims for refunds or long-term travel insurance disputes.

## Important note
The Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office (FCDO) and British consular staff cannot pay airport fees, taxes, or fines on your behalf. They cannot intervene in private disputes between a passenger and an airline. Local airport regulations and staff discretion always apply.
