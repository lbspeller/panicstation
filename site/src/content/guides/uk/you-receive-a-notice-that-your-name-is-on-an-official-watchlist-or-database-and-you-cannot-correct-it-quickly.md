# What to do if… you receive a notice that your name is on an official watchlist or database and you cannot correct it quickly (UK)

## Short answer
Pause, check the notice through an official route, and keep everything in writing. Then ask the organisation that appears to hold the record for access, correction, or at least a note that you dispute the accuracy.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the notice is genuine, and don’t pay anyone who claims they can remove you from a watchlist for a fee.
- Don’t use contact details in the notice until you have checked them against an official website.
- Don’t turn up unannounced at a police station, airport office, or government building unless you have been asked to attend.
- Don’t send original identity documents unless an official process clearly requires originals.
- Don’t post the notice or your personal details online, or share it widely.
- Don’t try to work around the issue by changing spellings, using a different name, or using someone else’s booking, account, or identity.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether the notice is real.**
   - Use an official website you already trust, for example GOV.UK, a police force website, or the organisation’s known website.
   - Use the main switchboard, webform, or published contact route from that website, not the contact details in the notice.
   - Ask: “Can you confirm whether you sent this notice, and what reference number should I use in writing?”

2. **Save the evidence and make a short timeline.**
   - Keep the original notice, envelope, email headers, screenshots, booking references, and any refusal or delay messages.
   - Write down the date, time, organisation, person or team, reference number, and exact effect, such as travel delay, job check paused, entry refused, account blocked, or extra screening.

3. **Ask for the minimum clarity you need in writing.**
   - Ask which organisation controls the record, if they can tell you.
   - Ask which system or database the notice relates to, if they can say.
   - Ask whether the issue appears to be a name match, identity mismatch, wrong record, or another type of flag.
   - Ask which route you should use to challenge accuracy or request a review.

4. **Use the closest subject access route.**
   - If it may be a police record from England, Wales, Northern Ireland, Jersey, the Isle of Man, or British Transport Police, use the GOV.UK police records route, which points to ACRO.
   - If it may be a Police Scotland record, use Police Scotland’s subject access route.
   - If it looks like a local police record rather than a Police National Computer record, use that police force’s subject access or data protection contact route.
   - If it looks immigration, border, visa, or citizenship related, use the Home Office route for personal information held in the borders, immigration and citizenship system.
   - If you are not sure who holds the record, write to the organisation named in the notice and ask them to identify the correct data protection contact or controller if they can.

5. **Ask for correction or a dispute note at the same time.**
   - Say clearly what you believe is wrong, for example: “This appears to relate to another person with a similar name.”
   - Include only the evidence needed to identify you and explain the error, such as a copy of ID, the notice reference, a booking reference, or previous correspondence.
   - Ask them to correct any inaccurate personal data, complete any incomplete personal data, or record that you dispute the accuracy while they check it.
   - Ask them to tell you if they need more proof of identity or more detail from you.

6. **If the impact is urgent, ask for a practical note or reference.**
   - If travel, work clearance, access, or an appointment is affected today or this week, ask whether they can add a note, give a reference number, or tell you what to quote during checks while the review is pending.
   - Keep the request factual: “I am trying to avoid repeated false matches while this is being checked.”

7. **If you are blocked or ignored, complain in order.**
   - First, complain to the organisation that is handling the record or request.
   - Include your timeline, copies of your request, proof you sent it, and the practical harm caused by the delay or error.
   - If you believe the organisation is mishandling your data rights, you can complain to the Information Commissioner’s Office. You will usually need a copy of the complaint you made to the organisation.

8. **Get legal advice before any risky meeting or questioning.**
   - Consider independent legal advice if you are repeatedly stopped, detained, prevented from travelling, facing job loss, asked to attend an interview under caution, or told the issue is national-security related.
   - A solicitor can help you keep communications clear and avoid saying more than is needed while formal checks are happening.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out today why your name appears on a watchlist or database.
- You do not need to write a long life history or explain everything at once.
- You do not need to threaten legal action in your first message.
- You do not need to contact every possible organisation at the same time; start with the organisation named in the notice or the closest official route.

## Important reassurance
A flag can be caused by a similar name, a data entry mistake, an identity mismatch, or an old record being read incorrectly. Your immediate task is not to solve the whole system; it is to verify the notice, keep a record, and start the right written route.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A longer dispute may need help from a solicitor, a regulated immigration adviser, or a data protection specialist, depending on which organisation and system are involved.

## Important note
This is general practical information, not legal, immigration, data protection, or professional advice. Some law-enforcement, border, immigration, and national-security records may have limited disclosure, exemptions, or different handling rules, and an organisation may not be able to confirm every detail.
