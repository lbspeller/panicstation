# What to do if… your credit card is suddenly cancelled or reduced without warning and bills are due (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your card issuer immediately using the number on the back of the card to confirm the reason for the restriction, then move any essential bill payments due within 48 hours to a debit card or bank transfer.

## Do not do these things
- Don't use contact details from a text or email notification; only use the number on your physical card or official banking app.
- Don't attempt multiple small transactions to "test" the card, as this can trigger automated fraud blocks and merchant blacklists.
- Don't ignore upcoming bill deadlines; late fees often apply automatically even if the failure was due to the card issuer.
- Don't apply for high-interest emergency loans or "no credit check" apps while in a state of panic.
- Don't stop communication with billers; silence is usually interpreted as a refusal to pay rather than a technical issue.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact status of the account (5 minutes).** Log in to your banking app or online portal to see if the account is marked as "closed," "suspended," or if the "available credit" has changed.
   - Look for "pending" or "authorised" transactions that may be consuming a newly reduced limit.
2. **Call the issuer’s security or customer service line.** Use the number on the back of the card. Ask specifically:
   - If this is a temporary security block or a permanent account closure.
   - If it is a limit reduction, what the new "available to spend" figure is right now.
   - How long any "investigation" or "review" is expected to take.
   - For payment services complaints (like blocked transfers), the issuer must typically provide a final response within 15 business days.
3. **List every bill due in the next 7 days.** Check your calendar or statement for any Recurring Card Payments (also known as Continuous Payment Authorities or CPAs).
   - Prioritize "priority debts" such as rent, mortgage, council tax, and utilities.
4. **Switch payment methods for immediate essentials.** If the card is unavailable, contact the company you owe and provide an alternative:
   - Use a debit card from a different bank account.
   - Set up a one-off Bank Transfer (Faster Payments) to ensure the funds arrive today.
   - Update your payment details on merchant websites for any "recurring card payments" to avoid service disconnection.
5. **Request a temporary payment extension from billers.** If you cannot pay an essential bill because of the card issue, tell the provider your card has been unexpectedly restricted.
   - Ask for a "payment holiday" or a 14-day extension to resolve the technical issue.
   - Keep a log of who you spoke to and the date/time of the call.
6. **Report suspected fraud to the correct regional body.** If the card was cancelled due to suspicious activity, follow the issuer's internal fraud process.
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** Report the incident to **Report Fraud** (the successor to Action Fraud) at reportfraud.police.uk or 0300 123 2040.
   - **Scotland:** Report to **Police Scotland** by calling **101**.
7. **Initiate a formal complaint if the issuer’s action was incorrect.** If the issuer cannot justify the cancellation or failed to follow notice rules (typically 2 months for permanent changes, though security blocks are exempt), submit a written complaint.
   - If you do not receive a final response within 8 weeks (or 15 days for certain payment services), you can refer the matter to the **Financial Ombudsman Service**.
   - You must refer the case to the Ombudsman within 6 months of the issuer's final response.

## What can wait
- You do not need to check your credit score today; the immediate priority is preventing late payment markers.
- You do not need to decide on a new permanent credit card provider while the current situation is unresolved.
- You do not need to argue the "fairness" of a limit reduction until your immediate bills are covered.

## Important reassurance
Sudden card blocks or limit cuts are often triggered by automated algorithms and do not necessarily reflect your financial "worth" or creditworthiness. These systems frequently flag legitimate activity as suspicious; your immediate goal is simply to reroute essential payments while the bank completes its review.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a sudden loss of credit access. It does not cover long-term debt management or legal challenges to banking terms and conditions.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute financial or legal advice. If you are facing a total loss of access to funds (including your main current account), contact the Citizens Advice consumer helpline or a debt charity like StepChange immediately for emergency assistance.
