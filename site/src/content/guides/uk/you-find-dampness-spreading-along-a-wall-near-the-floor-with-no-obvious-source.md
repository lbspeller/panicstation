# What to do if… you find dampness spreading along a wall near the floor with no obvious source (UK)

## Short answer
Treat spreading damp at floor level like a possible active leak until you have ruled that out: keep electrics safe, stop water flow if needed, and do a quick stop tap and water meter check if you can.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore spreading damp because it looks small.
- Don’t paint over it, seal it, or push furniture or rugs against it to hide it.
- Don’t use sockets, extension leads, heaters, or electrical appliances on or near the damp area.
- Don’t touch wet switches, wet sockets, exposed wiring, or a wet consumer unit.
- Don’t pull off skirting, lift flooring, or cut into plaster in a hurry if you do not know what is behind it.
- Don’t keep turning the heating up and hoping it dries out if the patch is still being fed by water.
- Don’t mix cleaning chemicals.

## What to do now
1. Make the area safer first.
   - Keep children and pets away from the damp patch.
   - Move portable electrical items away if you can do this without touching wet plugs, sockets, switches, or cables.
   - If the damp is near sockets, wiring, a boiler, or the consumer unit, stop using that area electrically. Switch off the affected circuit only if you can do it safely and without touching anything wet. If there is sparking, a burning smell, visible water in the consumer unit, or you would have to stand in water, move away and get urgent professional help.

2. Check whether water is still moving.
   - Stop using water in the home for the check: taps, toilets, washing machine, dishwasher, bath, and shower.
   - If you have a water meter and can reach it safely, take a reading or watch the small dial. Do not lift a heavy external meter cover.
   - Wait 30 minutes to 1 hour with no water use, then check again. A higher reading or moving dial may mean an ongoing leak.

3. Turn off the internal stop tap if the damp is spreading or the meter suggests a leak.
   - Look under the kitchen sink first, then check cupboards, a downstairs toilet, a utility room, garage, cellar, or under the stairs.
   - Turn the stop tap clockwise to shut it.
   - Open a cold tap briefly to check the supply has stopped and to relieve pressure, then close it.

4. Look for nearby likely sources without dismantling anything.
   - Check the room above, behind, and next to that wall section.
   - Look for water around a toilet base, bath or shower edge, sink trap, washing machine hose, dishwasher hose, radiator valve, or visible pipework.
   - If it is an outside wall and it has rained recently, look outside for overflowing guttering, a leaking downpipe, cracked render, blocked air bricks, raised ground, or water pooling against the wall.

5. Limit spread while help is arranged.
   - Put towels or absorbent pads at the edge of the damp area and replace them when wet.
   - Ventilate the room in short bursts if it is safe to do so.
   - Use a dehumidifier only from a dry socket away from the damp area, with no cable trailing across damp flooring.

6. Contact the right person today.
   - If you rent, report it to your landlord or letting agent in writing straight away. Include photos, the date, where the damp is, whether it is spreading, and what happened when water use stopped or the stop tap was turned off.
   - If you own the home, contact a plumber promptly if the damp is spreading, the meter suggests a leak, you cannot find the source, or you suspect pipework under the floor or inside the wall.
   - If you rent and the landlord or agent does not respond while conditions worsen, check your local council’s housing or environmental health route for damp and repair concerns.

7. Keep a simple record.
   - Take photos or video with a ruler, coin, or other object for scale.
   - Note the time, date, weather, whether anyone used water, meter readings if available, and whether the patch changed after the stop tap was turned off.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether it is rising damp, penetrating damp, condensation, or a hidden leak.
- You do not need to book a full damp survey before basic leak checks and, if needed, a plumber.
- You do not need to strip plaster, remove skirting, lift floors, choose repairs, or argue responsibility in the first hour.
- You do not need to clean or treat mould until the water source is understood and the immediate electrical and leak risks are controlled.

## Important reassurance
It is common for the visible damp patch to appear away from the actual source because water can travel behind plaster, along joists, or under flooring. Acting early, isolating water if needed, and recording what you see can limit further damage.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: make the area safer, reduce water damage, and create a clear record. Later decisions about repairs, drying, mould treatment, insurance, surveys, or tenancy disputes may need a qualified plumber, electrician, surveyor, insurer, landlord, council, or adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional, legal, electrical, plumbing, surveying, medical, financial, or insurance advice. If you are unsure about electrical safety or hidden water damage, treat the area as potentially hazardous and use qualified tradespeople.
