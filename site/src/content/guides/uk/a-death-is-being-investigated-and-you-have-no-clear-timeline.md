# What to do if… a death is being investigated and you have no clear timeline (UK)

## Short answer
Ask for one named death-investigation contact, the reference number, what is needed from you now, and when the next update is expected. Do not set fixed funeral dates, clear belongings, or sign anything you do not understand while the investigation route is still unclear.

## Do not do these things
- Do not rely on rumours, social media, or second-hand messages for what is happening; keep to the named official contact and your written notes.
- Do not agree to on-the-spot decisions you do not understand; ask for a written explanation and time to read it.
- Do not clean, sort, donate, or throw away belongings if there is any chance they may be relevant to the death investigation.
- Do not set a firm funeral date until the funeral director and death-investigation contact confirm that release and paperwork are in place.
- Do not make repeated parallel calls to several offices if you already have a named contact; ask that person for the right route and a clear callback plan.
- Do not assume the process is the same everywhere in the UK; Scotland, Northern Ireland, and England and Wales use different death-investigation routes.

## What to do now
1. **Ask who is handling family updates.**  
   Ask for one named contact and write down their full name, role, phone number, email address, office, and reference number. Depending on where the death is being investigated, this may be a coroner’s office, the Coroners Service, COPFS or Procurator Fiscal contact, police family liaison contact, or the investigating officer handling family updates.

2. **Ask which process applies in this case.**  
   Use plain questions:
   - “Is this being handled by a coroner, the Coroners Service, COPFS or Procurator Fiscal, the police, or more than one team?”
   - “What is the reference number I should use?”
   - “Who should I contact if I receive conflicting information?”
   - “Who will tell the family when the next step has happened?”

3. **Ask what is known about the next investigation step.**  
   Ask whether a post-mortem has been ordered, whether identification is needed, whether any medical history or documents are needed from the family, and whether there is any expected trigger for the next update. If the answer is uncertain, ask when you should check back if you have heard nothing.

4. **Ask about release for the funeral before making fixed plans.**  
   If you have chosen a funeral director, ask the death-investigation contact whether the person can be released yet and what is still needed. Ask the funeral director to confirm what paperwork they are waiting for before you choose fixed dates, cremation arrangements, travel, or venue bookings.

5. **Ask what proof of death is available while the investigation continues.**  
   If registration is delayed in England or Wales because an inquest is needed, ask the coroner about an interim death certificate. In Scotland or Northern Ireland, ask the relevant death-investigation contact what document, confirmation, or temporary proof of death may be available for urgent practical matters.

6. **Start a one-page case log.**  
   Write down the date and time of every call, who you spoke to, their role, what they said, what they asked you to do, and the next update point. Keep emails, letters, screenshots, reference numbers, and funeral-director messages in one folder.

7. **Make only the immediate practical holds.**  
   If you have access and it is safe, secure the home, keys, pets, urgent medicines needed by others, and obvious valuables without sorting or disposing of belongings. Do not unlock, search, alter, delete from, or move devices, papers, medication, clothing, vehicles, or other items that may be relevant to the investigation unless there is an immediate safety reason. Ask the named contact what they want you to leave untouched.

## What can wait
- You do not need to understand the full investigation today.
- You do not need to finalise funeral details until release and paperwork are confirmed.
- You do not need to start probate, estate decisions, or possessions sorting now.
- You do not need to give detailed updates to everyone; one short message through one trusted person is enough.
- You do not need to decide whether to complain, seek legal advice, speak to the media, or challenge any decision in the first shocked hours unless an urgent deadline has been clearly given to you in writing.

## Important reassurance
An unclear timeline after an investigated death is common and does not automatically mean that someone is hiding information or that the family has done something wrong. A named contact, a reference number, and a simple written log can reduce repeated explanations and help you keep track while the official process catches up.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, for the first hours and days while the situation is unclear. Later decisions about inquests, Fatal Accident Inquiries, criminal investigations, complaints, legal advice, estates, family disputes, or media contact may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information for the UK and is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Local practice and the correct death-investigation route can vary, so use the named official contact for instructions about the specific case.
