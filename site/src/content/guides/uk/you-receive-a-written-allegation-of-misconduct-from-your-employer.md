# What to do if… you receive a written allegation of misconduct from your employer (UK)

## Short answer
Pause, keep everything, and send one brief written acknowledgement asking for the specific allegations, evidence, process steps, and deadlines before you give a detailed response.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign “to get ahead of it” or send an angry, ashamed, or emotional reply.
- Do not delete messages, files, access logs, calendar entries, or “tidy up” work devices or accounts.
- Do not sign a statement you have not read carefully or that you do not fully agree with.
- Do not discuss the allegation widely at work; keep it to HR, your manager if appropriate, your union rep, your companion, or a trusted adviser.
- Do not assume the first meeting is your only chance to respond; you can ask for time to prepare.
- Do not secretly record a workplace meeting. Recording should generally be agreed by everyone and allowed by policy.

## What to do now
1. **Save the allegation letter and attachments.**  
   Keep the email or letter, any attachments, and the envelope or email headers if they matter. Save a copy of the allegation letter and your own notes somewhere you control. Do not copy restricted or confidential employer material off-system; if you need documents, ask HR for the evidence pack or the material they intend to rely on.

2. **Write down what the letter actually says.**  
   Make a private note of:
   - what you are alleged to have done
   - the dates, times, locations, systems, clients, customers, or colleagues mentioned
   - whether it says “investigation meeting”, “disciplinary hearing”, “gross misconduct”, “suspension”, or “possible dismissal”
   - who is running the process
   - the deadline to respond and any meeting date

3. **Reply once, briefly, to acknowledge receipt and ask for clarity.**  
   Send a calm note saying you have received the letter, you want to cooperate, and you are asking for:
   - the specific allegation or allegations
   - the policy, rule, or standard said to have been breached
   - the evidence being relied on, where it can be shared
   - whether the next meeting is an investigation meeting or a disciplinary hearing
   - the timetable and how you should submit your response

4. **If the meeting is a disciplinary hearing, ask to be accompanied.**  
   For a disciplinary hearing, you can make a reasonable request to bring a companion. This can be a workplace colleague, a workplace trade union representative who is certified or trained to act as a companion, or an official employed by a trade union. Tell your employer the person’s name and role as soon as you can. If the meeting is an investigation meeting, there is usually no legal right to be accompanied, but it is good practice for employers to allow it, so ask in writing.

5. **Start a clean timeline while your memory is fresh.**  
   In a private document, note what happened in date order. Include who was present, what was said, what you did, and what records may exist, such as emails, Teams or Slack messages, tickets, rotas, swipe access, call logs, CCTV locations, customer records, or expense records. Keep it factual.

6. **Preserve supporting records without breaching workplace rules.**  
   Keep your own notes, calendar entries, and copies of messages you are allowed to retain. If key information is in a work system, make a list of where it is, such as a folder name, ticket number, message thread, time window, CCTV location, or system name, so you can request it properly.

7. **Use a support channel before you draft a detailed response.**  
   If you are in a union, contact your rep and share the allegation letter. If you are not in a union, consider contacting Acas for process guidance. Choose one calm person outside work to help you read letters, check dates, and avoid rushed replies.

8. **Ask for reasonable adjustments if a disability, neurodivergence, or health condition affects the process.**  
   You can ask for practical changes such as extra time to respond, breaks, written questions, a quieter room, remote attendance, accessible documents, or questions in advance. Put the request in writing and explain briefly what would help you take part fairly.

9. **If you are suspended, get the terms in writing.**  
   Ask what the suspension means for pay and benefits, expected length, review points, contact rules, access to systems, and who your point of contact is. Suspension should not be treated as a decision that you did something wrong, and it should usually be kept as brief as possible and reviewed. Follow any written terms, and ask HR to clarify anything vague.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write your full response today unless there is an immediate deadline.
- You do not need to decide now whether to resign, raise a grievance, negotiate, appeal, or bring a legal claim.
- You do not need to explain your motives or feelings before you have the allegation details and evidence.
- You do not need to answer every point until you understand the process and what material the employer is relying on.

## Important reassurance
A written misconduct allegation can feel shocking and personal, even when it is part of a workplace process. The safest early move is to slow the situation down, keep records, ask for the exact allegations and evidence, and respond in a calm, factual way.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation, protect your position, and avoid irreversible mistakes. Later decisions, including grievances, settlement discussions, appeals, or legal claims, depend on the details and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information for the UK and is not legal advice. Workplace processes vary by employer, contract, policy, sector, and facts, so get appropriate support for your specific situation.
