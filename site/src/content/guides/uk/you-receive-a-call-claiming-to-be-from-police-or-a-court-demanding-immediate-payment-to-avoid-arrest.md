# What to do if… you receive a call claiming to be from police or a court demanding immediate payment to avoid arrest (UK)

## Short answer
End the call and do not pay. Verify the claim only through an official police, court, or bank contact route you find yourself, not through a number, link, or transfer offered by the caller.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay on the line to be transferred to a supervisor, court clerk, payment team, or officer in charge.
- Do not pay by bank transfer, card, cash voucher, gift cards, cryptocurrency, or a link sent during the call.
- Do not share your address, date of birth, bank name, account details, one-time codes, passwords, PINs, or memorable words.
- Do not trust caller ID, a reference number, a badge number, or a convincing script as proof.
- Do not follow instructions to keep the call secret, stay on the phone while you pay, or lie to your bank.
- Do not call back a number the caller gives you or click a link or attachment they send.

## What to do now
1. **End the call.** Say, “I’m going to verify this through official channels.” Then hang up.
2. **Clear the line before making any calls.** If you were called on a landline, wait at least five minutes and make sure you hear a normal dial tone, or use a different phone.
3. **If money, bank details, cards, or payment codes were involved, contact your bank safely.** Use the number on the back of your bank card, your bank’s official app, or dial **159** to be connected to your bank where the service covers your bank.
4. **If the caller claimed to be the police, verify independently.** Contact your local police force through its official website or call **101** for non-emergency help. If you are in immediate danger, call **999** now.
5. **If the caller claimed to be a court, fine office, warrant office, bailiff, or enforcement officer, verify independently.** Use GOV.UK or another official court contact route you find yourself. Do not use a number, email address, text link, or payment route from the caller.
6. **Save what you already have without re-contacting the caller.** Note the claimed organisation or court, caller name, displayed number, reference number, payment demand, payment method, date, and time. Save voicemails, texts, emails, screenshots, and payment details.
7. **If you paid or shared details, treat it as urgent.** Ask your bank whether it can stop or recall a payment, cancel affected cards, add extra checks, and protect your account. If you shared passwords or one-time codes, change the affected passwords, starting with email and banking.
8. **Report it through the right route.** In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report fraud to **Report Fraud** online or by phone. If you live in Scotland, report it to **Police Scotland on 101**. You can also ask a trusted advice service for scam-reporting guidance.

## What can wait
- You do not need to argue with the caller, prove it is a scam, or clear your name during the call.
- You do not need to decide today whether to change your phone number.
- You do not need to contact every organisation today if you have ended the call, secured any affected bank or online accounts, and made one official report.
- You do not need to respond to further calls, texts, voicemails, or emails from the same people.

## Important reassurance
These calls are designed to create panic and urgency, including threats of arrest or official action. Hanging up and checking through official routes is a reasonable response, and genuine organisations should allow you to verify who you are speaking to.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stop immediate harm and buy time. If you later find there is a genuine fine, warrant, court matter, or debt enforcement issue, deal with it through verified official contact details and consider specialist legal or debt advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Processes can vary by location and situation. If you think you or someone else is in immediate danger, contact emergency services.
