# What to do if… you are told to continue working after a safety incident while it is being assessed (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and move away from the affected area, equipment, or task if you can do so safely. Tell your manager you cannot continue there until the immediate controls are clear, and ask for the instruction and current control measures in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go back into the affected area or restart the same task “just this once” if you reasonably believe there is still a serious risk.
- Do not accept an on-the-spot ultimatum without asking for a short pause to clarify the safety controls.
- Do not argue about blame at the scene; keep it calm, factual, and about preventing repeat harm.
- Do not sign a statement that the area or task is safe unless you have read it and agree with it.
- Do not post photos, names, or details on social media or informal group chats in the moment.
- Do not assume “it is being assessed” means “it is safe”; ask what has actually changed since the incident.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Step away from the immediate area, equipment, or process involved. If there is immediate danger, such as fire, gas, structural risk, violence, or an uncontrolled hazard, follow site emergency procedures and call **999** if needed.
2. **State the hazard plainly.** Say: “There has been a safety incident at [location]. I cannot continue [task] there until the controls are confirmed and I understand them.”
3. **Ask what has changed.** Ask: “What controls are in place right now to stop this happening again?” and “Who checked the area or task before asking people to continue?”
4. **Ask for a safer temporary task.** Request reassignment away from the affected area or task while it is being assessed, such as work in another area, admin, packing, training, or housekeeping outside the affected zone.
5. **Ask for the instruction in writing.** If someone still tells you to continue, ask them to confirm by message, email, or log: “Please confirm you are instructing me to continue [task] in [area] after the incident, and what controls are in place.” Keep your own reply short and factual.
6. **Report it through your workplace route.** Use the incident, near-miss, accident, or hazard reporting system. Include the time, exact location, what happened, what you were told to do, and what controls you could or could not see. Ask for a reference number, copy, or receipt if the system provides one.
7. **Bring in safety support quickly.** Contact your supervisor or manager, the site health and safety lead, and your union safety rep or another trained safety representative if you have one. Ask them to check the area or controls with you before you return to the task.
8. **Use clear serious-risk wording if needed.** If you reasonably believe there is serious and imminent danger, say so plainly: “I believe there is serious and imminent danger, and I am not refusing work generally; I am asking for safe alternative work until the risk is controlled.” Stay available, propose safer work, and keep notes.
9. **If you are agency staff or a contractor, tell your own route too.** Message your agency, contract manager, or site contact briefly in writing that you have raised an unresolved safety concern and are asking for safe alternative work until controls are confirmed.
10. **Escalate if the concern is not acted on.** If you have raised the issue and nothing changes, check whether HSE, your local council, or another enforcing authority covers the workplace. Use the correct route rather than guessing.
11. **If anyone was hurt or exposed, prioritise health.** Use workplace first aid or medical help. Write down symptoms, exposure details, substances, equipment, and location while it is fresh. If you are worried after being told to “wait and see”, ask for occupational health, NHS 111, your GP, or urgent medical help depending on how serious it is.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to raise a grievance, resign, start a claim, or accuse anyone of legal fault.
- You do not need to prove everything in the moment; focus on avoiding repeat harm and getting the controls confirmed.
- You do not need a perfect statement immediately; a short factual note with times, places, names, and what was said is enough for now.
- You do not usually need to decide personally whether a formal RIDDOR report is required unless you are the responsible person for reporting; ask the manager, safety lead, or employer to confirm who is handling that.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to want a pause and clear controls before continuing after a safety incident. Staying calm, stepping away from the immediate risk, and asking for specific safety information is a practical response, not overreacting.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate period after the incident. Later decisions about investigations, formal complaints, regulator involvement, employment rights, or claims may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you think you or someone else is in immediate danger, use site emergency procedures and emergency services.
