# What to do if… you are being pressured to sign documents quickly after a death (UK)

## Short answer
Pause the signing. Take a full copy, step away from the pressure, and get independent advice before you put your name on any legal, financial, estate, or funeral document.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign just to stop the pressure or get it over with.
- Don’t rely on a verbal summary such as “it’s standard” or “it doesn’t change anything”.
- Don’t hand over originals, such as a passport, driving licence, will, or death certificate, to someone you do not fully trust.
- Don’t share bank logins, PINs, one-time passcodes, or full card details with anyone handling things.
- Don’t send money to new bank details given by email, text, or messaging app until you have checked them through a route you already trust.
- Don’t use phone numbers or links from the same message that is asking you to sign or pay.
- Don’t let the person pressuring you isolate you with lines such as “don’t tell the family” or “don’t speak to another solicitor”.

## What to do now
1. **Create a hard pause out loud.** Say: “I’m not signing anything today. I need the full paperwork and independent advice first.” Repeat it once; you do not need to argue.

2. **Get the whole document before discussing it further.** Ask for:
   - every page of the document
   - any attachments, terms, schedules, or small print
   - the name, role, firm, and contact details of the person asking you to sign
   - a written explanation of what they say happens if you do not sign today

3. **Name the document before you engage with it.** Common pressure points after a death include:
   - funeral home contracts or authorisation forms
   - probate or estate administration papers
   - executor or administrator forms
   - documents that may change who receives inheritance, such as variations or disclaimers
   - releases or indemnities saying you will not claim later
   - bank, pension, insurance, or property forms

4. **Treat unclear documents as high-risk.** If you cannot clearly say what the document does, who benefits from it, and what changes after you sign, do not sign it yet.

5. **Move the conversation to a safer channel you control.** If the pressure came by call, text, social media, or messaging app, say: “Email me the full paperwork from your official address.” Do not open links they send. Type official websites yourself or use contact details you already have.

6. **Verify any professional or organisation independently.** If they say they are a solicitor, funeral provider, bank, insurer, or estate administrator, contact the organisation using a number or website you find independently, not the details in the pressure message.

7. **Bring in an independent person now.** Ask a trusted friend or relative to sit with you and read the paperwork calmly. For estate, inheritance, property, liability, executor, or administrator documents, speak to your own independent solicitor, not someone chosen by the person pressuring you. For consumer pressure around a paid service, Citizens Advice may also help.

8. **If money or bank details are involved, contact your bank now.** Use the number on your card, statement, banking app, or official website. Tell them you are bereaved and being pressured. Ask whether:
   - any pending payment can be stopped or delayed
   - any payment already made can be recalled or reviewed
   - the account can be noted for extra caution
   - a scam refund process may apply

9. **If the pressure looks like fraud or impersonation, report it through the right route.**
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud.
   - In Scotland, contact Police Scotland on 101 for non-emergency fraud reporting, or use Police Scotland’s official contact route.
   - Call 999 if someone is threatening you, at the door, or you feel in immediate danger.

10. **Be cautious about claims based on power of attorney after death.** A lasting power of attorney usually ends when the person who made it dies. If someone says they can still act under it after the death, pause and get independent advice before giving them documents, money, or authority.

11. **Write a short record while it is fresh.** Note the date and time, names, phone numbers, email addresses, what they said, what they wanted you to sign, and any deadlines they claimed. Screenshot messages and keep copies of emails.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who gets what, who is right, or whether you will challenge anything.
- You do not need to answer repeated calls or messages after you have set one clear boundary.
- You do not need to keep the peace by signing something you have not understood.
- Complaints, formal letters, probate steps, and longer legal decisions can wait until you have stabilised and got independent advice.

## Important reassurance
Pressure to sign after a death can happen in scams, family disputes, and misunderstandings. Pausing, getting the full paperwork, and bringing in an independent person is a reasonable response, even if the document later turns out to be routine.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to slow things down and prevent irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about inheritance, property, liability, probate, contracts, or complaints may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. If you feel threatened, unsafe, or coerced, prioritise immediate safety and seek urgent help.
