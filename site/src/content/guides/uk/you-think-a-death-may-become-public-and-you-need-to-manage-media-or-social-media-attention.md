# What to do if… you think a death may become public and you need to manage media or social media attention (UK)

## Short answer
Choose one family spokesperson now and agree one short holding statement for everyone else to use. If police or another organisation is involved, ask the named contact or press office how media enquiries should be routed.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t let several relatives reply separately to journalists, neighbours, colleagues, or social media contacts.
- Don’t share unconfirmed details about cause of death, timelines, names, locations, or blame.
- Don’t argue now about who has the most right to speak; choose the person who can stay calm and repeat the agreed words.
- Don’t post in anger or try to correct every rumour in real time.
- Don’t forward private family messages, screenshots, photos, or voice notes outside the smallest necessary group.
- Don’t feel pressured to answer a reporter at the door, on the phone, or by message.

## What to do now
1. **Take a brief pause and choose one spokesperson.**  
   Pick the closest agreed person, or the calmest person who can answer consistently and follow a script. Choose one backup only for missed calls.

2. **Send one rule to close family and key friends.**  
   Use this wording: “If anyone contacts you about this, please reply: ‘Please contact [Name]. We’re not speaking individually.’ Then do not add anything else.”

3. **Write one holding statement and reuse it word-for-word.**  
   Keep it plain, short, and non-specific: “We’re grieving and trying to support each other privately. We’re not ready to say more at the moment. Please respect our privacy. If there are updates we want to share, we will do so through our family spokesperson.”

4. **Make a short no-comment list.**  
   Put the topics on paper so the spokesperson does not have to decide under pressure. This might include cause of death, exact circumstances, medical details, family disagreements, blame, rumours, and anything police or official death investigators may be considering.

5. **If police are involved, ask for the safest communication route.**  
   Ask whether there is a Family Liaison Officer or another named police contact. Ask what the family can safely say, what should not be discussed publicly, and whether media enquiries should be directed to the force press office.

6. **If the death happened in a hospital, care setting, workplace, school, or public organisation, ask who handles media enquiries.**  
   Say: “If journalists contact you or ask about this death, who handles that, and what should our family say if we are contacted?” Write down the name, role, phone number, and email if they give one.

7. **Create one intake route for enquiries.**  
   Use one phone number or one email address controlled by the spokesperson. The spokesperson can reply with the holding statement and does not need to answer every call live.

8. **Reduce accidental public disclosure for the next 24 hours.**  
   Ask close family not to post new public tributes, photographs, locations, or details yet. Check visible social media posts, tags, and profile photos that reporters or strangers could copy.

9. **If journalists are at the door or repeatedly contacting relatives, keep the response short.**  
   Do not debate. Say: “Please contact [Name] at [email]. We are not speaking at the door. Please leave.” Close the door or end the call.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on an obituary, funeral details, fundraising, interviews, or a full public statement now.
- You do not need to correct every rumour today.
- You do not need to choose the perfect spokesperson; you can change the arrangement later.
- You do not need to reply to every message, comment, tag, or missed call immediately.
- You do not need to decide whether the family will ever speak publicly.

## Important reassurance
Choosing one spokesperson is not the same as making the death public. It is a way to protect the family from pressure, reduce confusion, and give everyone a simple answer while you are grieving.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours or day. Later decisions about statements, complaints, legal issues, inquests, interviews, funeral privacy, or online content may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, media, or other professional advice. If the death is connected to a police investigation, a coroner, a procurator fiscal, an inquest, a fatal accident inquiry, a workplace incident, or a public body, be cautious about public details and ask the relevant official contact what should be handled through official channels.
