# What to do if… you get an alert that a garage door, gate, or secure entry opened unexpectedly and you did not do it (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible intruder until you can safely rule it out. Stay out, move away from the entry point, and call 999 if someone may be there now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go outside, go back inside, or approach the garage, gate, or entry point just to check.
- Don’t confront, shout warnings at, follow, or try to detain anyone.
- Don’t search rooms, the garage, garden, driveway, or shared entry areas yourself.
- Don’t repeatedly operate the door or gate from the app to test it.
- Don’t touch, tidy, or repair areas that may have been accessed, unless you must for immediate safety.
- Don’t post live details such as your address, that you are away, or camera locations.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer position first.**
   - **If you’re inside:** move yourself and anyone with you away from the opened entry point. Go to a room that locks if you can, keep your phone with you, and stay quiet.
   - **If you’re outside or returning home:** don’t enter. Go to a neighbour, a staffed place nearby, or stay in your locked car at a safe distance.

2. **Check only what you can see without approaching.**
   - Look at any doorbell camera, CCTV, motion alert, entry log, or alarm panel message from where you are.
   - If you can do it remotely, or from your current safe spot, turn on inside lights. Don’t move towards the entry point to do this.

3. **Call 999 if someone may be there now.**
   - Call 999 if a crime may be in progress, someone may be on the property, or anyone is at immediate risk.
   - If you cannot speak safely, call 999 and stay on the line. If the operator prompts you and you are calling from a mobile, pressing 55 can indicate that it is a genuine emergency.

4. **If you are away from home, don’t rush back.**
   - Stay where you are and call 999 if you believe someone may be there now.
   - If you live in a building with a concierge, site staff, or building security, ask them to observe from a safe distance. Do not ask them to enter, search, or confront anyone.

5. **If you have a monitored alarm, contact the alarm company once you are safe.**
   - Tell them you received an entry-opened alert that you did not authorise.
   - Ask what zones, timestamps, and response actions they can see.

6. **Only if it is clearly safe, reduce immediate exposure.**
   - If you have a live camera view showing the doorway, gate, or drive is clear, and closing it will not trap or endanger anyone, you may choose to close it once from the app.
   - If you cannot see the area clearly, do not operate it. Stay safe and wait for help.

7. **Preserve what you noticed without re-entering.**
   - Note the time of the alert and any observations, such as voices, footsteps, vehicles, clothing, lights, or movement.
   - Save, export, or screenshot relevant camera clips, app notifications, and access logs if your system allows it.

8. **When police or support arrive, let them lead.**
   - Stay visible, keep your hands free, and follow instructions.
   - Don’t enter the property, garage, or shared entry area until you are told it is safe.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide right now whether this was a device fault, a neighbour mistake, a shared-user issue, or criminal activity.
- You don’t need to review every camera angle, check every room, or make a full inventory while you are still shaken.
- You don’t need to reset app access, remove shared users, change codes, replace fobs, arrange repairs, or change locks until the immediate risk is over.

## Important reassurance
This alert is meant to get your attention, and it is sensible to treat it seriously at first. False alerts do happen, but staying back and getting help is a calm safety step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps to keep you safe and prevent a rushed decision that could put you in danger. Later decisions about access logs, shared users, device faults, repairs, insurance, or reporting may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, technical, insurance, or professional advice. If you believe someone may be on the property or anyone is at risk, call 999.
