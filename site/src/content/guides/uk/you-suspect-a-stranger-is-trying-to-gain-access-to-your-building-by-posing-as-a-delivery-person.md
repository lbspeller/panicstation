# What to do if… you suspect a stranger is trying to gain access to your building by posing as a delivery person (UK)

## Short answer
Do not buzz them in, open the door, share an entry code, or hold the communal door for them. Keep a barrier between you and them while you verify from a distance, and call 999 if they are trying to force entry, threatening anyone, or you feel in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t let them in because they look like a courier, seem rushed, or say another resident is waiting.
- Don’t share an entry code, fob, swipe card, or key-safe information.
- Don’t go downstairs alone to sort it out if you feel unsure or pressured.
- Don’t argue at the door or let the situation pull you into a face-to-face confrontation.
- Don’t click unexpected missed-delivery links or pay redelivery fees while you are stressed.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Lock your own door and step back from it. If you use a chain or door bar, use it only while speaking, then remove it afterwards so it does not delay you leaving in an emergency. If you are at the building entry system, do not buzz anyone in.
2. **Speak only from behind a barrier.** Use the closed door, intercom, or video entry system. Ask who the delivery is for, which flat it is for, and which company they are with. Then say: “I’m not letting anyone in. Please ring the intended flat or contact the recipient.”
3. **Keep your refusal short.** Repeat one line if needed: “I can’t help. Please use the correct buzzer.” If your building has a reception, concierge, parcel room, or caretaker, tell them to use that route.
4. **Verify without using information they give you.** If you are expecting a parcel, check the tracking in the official app or website you normally use. Do not use a link, number, or QR code given by the person at the door or in an unexpected message. If you cannot verify quickly, treat it as unverified and do not grant entry.
5. **Alert someone responsible for the building.** Contact the concierge, caretaker, security desk, landlord, managing agent, or building management. Say: “Possible fake delivery attempt at [door/lobby] at [time]. Please check the communal entrance is secure and follow your normal process.”
6. **Use the right police route for the risk.** Call 999 if someone is trying to force entry, threatening you or someone else, a serious offence is happening, or anyone is in immediate danger. If they have left and there is no immediate danger, call 101 or report to your local police force to share the suspicious behaviour.
7. **Note only what you can safely notice.** If you can do it without getting closer, write down the time, what was said, a basic description, and any vehicle details. Do not chase them or try to take photos if that would draw you into contact.
8. **If this began with a delivery message, stop using that message.** Do not enter passwords, payment details, or banking information. If you have already shared card or bank details, contact your bank using the number in your banking app or on your card. You can forward scam texts to 7726.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it was definitely a scam.
- You do not need to confront them, follow them, or gather proof.
- You do not need to change locks or overhaul building security in the moment, unless a lock or entry system is visibly failing and needs urgent repair.
- If money or personal details were taken, reporting fraud can wait until you are safe. Report Fraud is generally the route for England, Wales and Northern Ireland; in Scotland, report fraud to Police Scotland on 101.

## Important reassurance
It can feel awkward to refuse entry, especially when someone sounds impatient or official. In a secure building, not letting an unknown person in is a sensible default, even if the delivery later turns out to be real.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and prevent unsafe access. Later decisions about building security, formal reports, complaints, or repairs may need help from building management, police, your landlord, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, security, financial, therapeutic, medical, or other professional advice. If you believe someone is trying to enter unlawfully or anyone is in immediate danger, prioritise getting to safety and contacting emergency services.
