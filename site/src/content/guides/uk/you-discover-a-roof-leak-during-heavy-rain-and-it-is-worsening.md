# What to do if… you discover a roof leak during heavy rain and it is worsening (UK)

## Short answer
Make it safe first: keep people away from the wet area and any bulging ceiling, switch off affected electrics only if you can do so safely, contain the water, and report the leak through the right urgent route for your home.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t climb onto the roof during heavy rain, darkness, lightning, or high winds.
- Don’t go into the loft if water is near electrics, the ceiling is sagging, or access is unsafe.
- Don’t touch light fittings, sockets, extension leads, appliances, or wet cables.
- Don’t touch the consumer unit if it is wet, if there is water nearby, or if you would have to stand in water to reach it.
- Don’t stay in a room where the ceiling is bulging, sagging, cracking, or dropping plaster.
- Don’t start pulling down ceilings or cutting into walls during the storm.
- Don’t throw damaged items away before taking quick photos, unless they are creating an immediate safety risk.

## What to do now
1. **Move people and pets away from the leak.** Keep everyone out of the affected room if the ceiling is bulging, sagging, cracking, or dropping plaster.

2. **Deal with electrical risk before the water spread.** If water is near a ceiling light, smoke alarm, socket, appliance, extension lead, or cable, switch off the affected circuit at the consumer unit if you can do that safely. If you are not sure which circuit is affected, use the main switch only if the consumer unit is dry and safe to reach.

3. **Do not touch unsafe electrics.** If the consumer unit, meter area, floor, or wall around it is wet, do not touch it. Keep people away from the area and get urgent professional help. If there is sparking, smoke, a burning smell, or fire, call 999.

4. **Contain the water without standing under danger.** Put a bucket, bowl, or large plastic storage box under the main drip point if the ceiling above is not bulging. Put towels around the container to slow spreading water.

5. **Move vulnerable items out of the splash zone.** Move phones, laptops, chargers, paperwork, rugs, soft furnishings, and anything plugged in away from the leak. Do not unplug anything wet while standing on a wet floor.

6. **Limit spread where it is safe.** If water is running down a wall, move furniture away from that wall. Use a plastic sheet, bin bags, or a tarpaulin to protect furniture or funnel drips into a container only if you can do it without reaching under a bulging ceiling.

7. **Report it through the right urgent route.**
   - If you rent, contact your landlord, letting agent, council landlord, or housing association using their emergency repairs route.
   - If you own a house, contact your home insurer if you may need to claim, and contact an emergency roofer for temporary make-safe work.
   - If you own or rent a flat, also contact the building manager, managing agent, freeholder, or emergency building contact if the leak may involve the roof, another flat, or a communal area.

8. **Record the situation quickly.** Take a few photos or a short video of the active leak, any bulging or sagging ceiling, wet electrics from a safe distance, and damaged items. Do not delay safety steps to collect evidence.

9. **Keep it in make-safe mode until the rain eases.** Keep affected electrics off, keep unsafe rooms closed off, empty buckets before they overflow, and wait for qualified help rather than trying exterior repairs in dangerous weather.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact roof fault now.
- You do not need to choose a permanent roofer or compare quotes during the storm.
- You do not need to start drying, dehumidifying, or deep cleaning until the leak is contained and electrics have been made safe.
- You do not need to decide today whether damaged items can be repaired or replaced.
- You can leave non-urgent messages, paperwork, and insurance details until people are safe and the water is under control.

## Important reassurance
A worsening roof leak in heavy rain is frightening, but the first job is limited: avoid electric shock, avoid a possible ceiling collapse, slow the water spread, and get the right urgent help involved. You do not have to solve the whole roof problem tonight.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the first safe pause during ongoing heavy rain. Later decisions may need a qualified electrician, roofer, insurer, landlord, managing agent, surveyor, or housing adviser.
