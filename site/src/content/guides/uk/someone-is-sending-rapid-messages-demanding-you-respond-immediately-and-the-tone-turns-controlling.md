# What to do if… someone is sending rapid messages demanding you respond immediately and the tone turns controlling (UK)

## Short answer
Stop replying immediately and mute the conversation to create a safe pause. If you are in immediate danger or fear the person is nearby and escalating, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t get pulled into a rapid back-and-forth exchange to try and calm the other person down.
- Don’t provide personal details such as your current location, who you are with, or your planned arrival time.
- Don’t delete the messages, as they may be required as evidence of harassment or coercive control later.
- Don’t agree to meet the person in person to "settle the matter" while they are in a controlling or aggressive state.
- Don’t assume the situation is minor simply because it is happening digitally; digital control often mirrors physical risk.

## What to do now
1. **Interrupt the pressure cycle by putting your phone down immediately.** **Place the device face down in another room or out of sight for at least 60 seconds to break the psychological loop of needing to respond.**
2. **Mute the specific conversation or contact without blocking them yet.** **This stops the constant pings and notifications from escalating your stress while still allowing messages to be received for evidence purposes.**
   - Use the "Mute" or "Hide Alerts" function in the app.
   - Disable "Read Receipts" and "Last Seen" status if it is safe to do so.
3. **Assess your physical safety in your current location.** **Determine if the person sending the messages has physical access to you, knows your location, or has made threats against you or others.**
   - If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
   - If you cannot speak safely on a mobile, listen to the operator, and when prompted by the automated system, press 55 (the Silent Solution).
   - If calling from a landline and you cannot speak, stay on the line; the operator will listen for background noise and may connect you to the police.
4. **Notify a trusted person about the situation immediately.** **Send a brief message to a friend or family member stating that you are being harassed and may need them to stay by their phone.**
5. **Secure digital evidence of the controlling behavior.** **Take screenshots that clearly show the sender’s name or number, the timestamps of the rapid messages, and the specific controlling language used.**
6. **Apply a firm and neutral boundary if you choose to respond once.** **If you feel a single response will de-escalate the person, send one line: "I am not available to message now and will not be replying further today."**
   - Do not engage in any follow-up arguments or justifications.
7. **Contact a specialist support service for guidance on coercive control.** **Call the National Domestic Abuse Helpline at 0808 2000 247 for confidential advice on managing digital harassment and safety planning.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the future of the relationship right now.
- You do not need to file a formal police report in this exact moment unless you are in immediate danger.
- You do not need to explain your silence to the person sending the messages.
- You can wait to change your phone number or social media privacy settings until you are in a calmer environment.

## Important reassurance
Feeling panicked or "trapped" by a flurry of messages is a common reaction to digital coercion. Taking space is a valid safety measure, not a sign of weakness or "ignoring" a problem. You are not responsible for the other person’s inability to regulate their tone or volume of contact.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for acute digital harassment and controlling behavior. Long-term safety planning should be done in consultation with specialist services or the police.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal or professional safety advice. If you believe a crime is being committed or you are at risk of harm, contact the police via 999 (emergency) or 101 (non-emergency).
