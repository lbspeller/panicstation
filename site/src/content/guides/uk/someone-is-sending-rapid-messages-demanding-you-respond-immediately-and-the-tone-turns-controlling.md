# What to do if… someone is sending rapid messages demanding you respond immediately and the tone turns controlling (UK)

## Short answer
Create breathing space first: pause the rapid back-and-forth, mute the conversation if it is safe, and move yourself into a calmer, safer pause. If you think you or someone else is in immediate danger, call 999 now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t get pulled into a rapid back-and-forth if it is making you feel unsafe, watched, or pressured.
- Don’t send your location, who you are with, when you will be home, passwords, private photos, or other details to “prove” anything.
- Don’t meet up “just to settle this” if the tone is controlling, threatening, or unpredictable.
- Don’t delete the messages in a panic if you may want help later, unless keeping them puts you at risk.
- Don’t block, confront, or announce that you are collecting evidence if you think that could make them escalate.
- Don’t assume it is “not serious” just because it is happening by message.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the pressure cycle for a moment.**  
   Put the phone face down, step away from the screen, and remind yourself: “I do not have to answer at their speed.”

2. **Reduce the incoming pressure without creating extra risk.**
   - Mute the chat or contact.
   - Turn on Do Not Disturb, allowing calls from trusted people if you need to.
   - If it is safe, turn off read receipts, last-seen status, online status, or location sharing for that app.

3. **Check immediate safety.**
   Consider whether they are nearby, likely to come to your home, workplace, or school, have keys or access, are demanding your location, or have threatened you, themselves, your property, your job, your family, or your pets. If you believe there is immediate danger, call 999.

4. **Use Silent Solution only if you need emergency police help and cannot speak.**  
   Dial 999, listen to the operator, cough or tap if you can, and press 55 if prompted so the call is treated as a genuine emergency.

5. **Tell one trusted person what is happening.**  
   Send a short message such as: “Can you stay available? Someone is bombarding me with controlling messages and I feel pressured.” If you are at work, school, university, or a staffed building, consider telling reception, security, a manager, or another safe person that you may receive unwanted contact.

6. **Preserve the messages only if it is safe.**  
   Take screenshots that show the number or account name, timestamps, and the controlling demands or threats. If the app allows it and this will not alert them, export or download the conversation. Make a short note for yourself with the date, time window, what they demanded, and any threat.

7. **Use one neutral boundary only if replying feels safer than silence.**  
   Keep it short and do not argue the details. For example: “I’m not available to message right now. I’ll respond later.” If any reply feels unsafe or likely to invite more pressure, send nothing.

8. **Block or report in the app when it is safe to do so.**  
   Use the app’s block and report tools if that reduces risk. If blocking often makes them escalate, prioritise safety, support, and police or specialist advice before blocking.

9. **If this may be domestic abuse, coercive control, stalking, or harassment, use a safe device to get support.**  
   GOV.UK lists domestic abuse helplines for England, Northern Ireland, Scotland, and Wales. In England, you can contact the National Domestic Abuse Helpline on 0808 2000 247. For stalking support, you can contact the National Stalking Helpline on 0808 802 0300. If you think your phone or browsing is being monitored, use a trusted person’s phone, a work device you are allowed to use, or another safer device.

10. **Report non-emergency harassment or threats if you choose to.**  
   If it is not an immediate emergency, you can contact police on 101 or use your local police force’s online reporting route. You do not have to know whether the behaviour is legally stalking, harassment, domestic abuse, or something else before asking for help.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide right now whether to end the relationship, make a police report, start legal action, or change every account.
- You do not have to write the perfect explanation or prove that your need for space is reasonable.
- You can decide later whether to change your number, review privacy settings across all apps, make a longer timeline, or get specialist advice.
- Right now, the priority is slowing the pressure, checking immediate safety, and not being alone with it.

## Important reassurance
Feeling frozen, guilty, or panicky is a common reaction when someone demands instant access to you. Wanting space is reasonable. Rapid messages, pressure, monitoring, and threats can be warning signs, and taking time to get support is a protective step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to reduce immediate pressure and help you reach a safer pause. Later decisions about reporting, relationships, housing, workplace safety, legal options, or longer-term safety planning may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you or someone else is in immediate danger, call 999.
