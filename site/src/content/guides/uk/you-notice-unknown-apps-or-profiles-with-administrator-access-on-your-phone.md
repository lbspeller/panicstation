# What to do if… you notice unknown apps or profiles with administrator access on your phone (UK)

## Short answer
Treat unknown administrator or device-management access as a possible compromise. Pause sensitive activity on that phone, disconnect it from networks if you can, and remove any unknown management access before using the phone for banking, email, passwords, or identity checks.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using the phone for sensitive logins, banking, email, password managers, or verification codes until you have dealt with the unknown administrator access.
- Don’t open the unknown app to “see what it is”, accept prompts, or grant more permissions.
- Don’t disable built-in protections, such as Google Play Protect, to make warnings go away.
- Don’t wipe the phone before quickly noting the app or profile name if it is safe to do so.
- Don’t confront anyone or make accusations based only on seeing a profile, app, or setting.
- If this is a work, school, or organisation-issued phone, don’t remove management controls until you have checked whether they are legitimate.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and contain the phone.**
   - Turn on **Airplane mode**.
   - If Wi-Fi or Bluetooth stays on, turn those off too.
   - Keep the phone powered on for now unless it is overheating, damaged, or unsafe to keep nearby.

2. **Capture quick details, if safe.**
   - Screenshot the administrator, device-management, VPN, or profile screen.
   - Write down the app or profile name, icon, and the time you noticed it.
   - Note whether the phone is personal, work-issued, school-issued, or shared.

3. **If it’s an iPhone, check for unknown management profiles.**
   - Go to **Settings → General → VPN & Device Management**.
   - If you see a profile, management entry, or VPN you do not recognise, review its name.
   - If it is clearly not from your employer, school, mobile provider, or an app you knowingly installed, remove it if the phone allows this, then restart the iPhone.
   - If removal is blocked and the phone may be managed by work, school, or another organisation, pause and verify with that organisation from a separate contact route.

4. **If it’s Android, remove unknown device administrator access first.**
   - Open **Settings** and look for **Security**, **Security & privacy**, **Device admin apps**, or **Device administrators**.
   - Turn off administrator access for any app you do not recognise.
   - Then uninstall the app from **Settings → Apps** or through Google Play.
   - If the app name looks like a system component but you are not sure, pause and check the phone maker’s support guidance before removing it.

5. **Use Safe Mode on Android if the app blocks removal.**
   - Boot the phone into **Safe Mode** using the steps for your phone model.
   - In Safe Mode, try again to turn off the unknown device administrator access and uninstall the app.
   - Restart normally after removal.

6. **Run the built-in Android malware check where available.**
   - Open the **Google Play Store**.
   - Tap your profile icon, then **Play Protect**.
   - Run a scan and follow any removal prompts.
   - If Play Protect is turned off, turn it back on unless a legitimate managed-device policy prevents this.

7. **Check other phone controls that can expose messages or activity.**
   - Check **Accessibility** for services you did not enable.
   - Check **Notification access** for apps that can read notifications or codes.
   - Check **VPN** settings for unknown VPNs.
   - Check work profiles, device management, and accounts for entries you do not recognise.

8. **Use a different known-clean device for account security.**
   - Start with your email account, then your Apple Account or Google account, then banking and payment accounts.
   - Change passwords for accounts you used on the affected phone.
   - Turn on 2-step verification where offered.
   - Check email forwarding rules, filters, recovery email addresses, recovery phone numbers, and signed-in devices.

9. **If you still cannot trust the phone, consider a factory reset.**
   - A factory reset is usually the cleanest reset option for a phone that may still be infected or managed without your consent.
   - It will erase apps, settings, messages, photos, and other local data.
   - Only restore from a backup you are confident was made before the unknown administrator app, profile, VPN, or management entry appeared.
   - If you cannot be sure, reset and rebuild without restoring apps or device settings at first.

10. **If fraud, money loss, or identity misuse may be involved, use UK reporting routes.**
   - Contact your bank or payment provider using an official app, official website, or the number on your card.
   - If you have lost money or suspect fraud or cyber crime, report it to Report Fraud. In Scotland, contact Police Scotland by calling 101.
   - Keep the screenshots and notes you took in case your bank, mobile provider, employer, school, or a reporting service asks for details.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly who did this right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether the phone can be trusted long term.
- You do not need to install new security apps before dealing with the unknown administrator access.
- You do not need a full forensic investigation for these first steps.
- You do not need to confront anyone while you are still securing the phone and accounts.

## Important reassurance
Finding unknown administrator access is unsettling, and taking it seriously is reasonable. The immediate goal is simple: stop sensitive use on that phone, remove unknown control where possible, secure key accounts from a clean device, and avoid rushed decisions.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the app or profile comes back, removal is blocked, money is missing, or you suspect targeted harassment or coercive control, later decisions may need specialist cyber security, banking, workplace, safeguarding, or legal support.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional security, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Phone menus vary by model, Android version, iOS version, mobile provider, and management setup. Some administrator apps and management profiles are legitimate on work, school, or organisation-managed phones, so verify before removing required management.
