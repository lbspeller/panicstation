# What to do if… your motorcycle chain looks loose or damaged and you are deciding whether it’s safe to ride (UK)

## Short answer
Do not ride the motorcycle until you have verified that the chain and sprockets meet manufacturer specifications and show no signs of structural failure. A loose or damaged chain can derail, lock the rear wheel, or snap, leading to an immediate loss of control.

## Do not do these things
- Don't attempt to "limp home" or ride slowly to test the stability of the chain.
- Don't put your fingers, tools, or rags near the chain or sprockets while the engine is running, even in neutral.
- Don't apply WD-40 or harsh degreasers to an O-ring / X-ring chain as this can destroy internal seals.
- Don't tighten the chain without a torque wrench to secure the rear axle to the manufacturer's specified setting.
- Don't ignore "red dust" (fretting corrosion) around pins, which indicates internal failure.
- Don't attempt to ride if you see any "hooked" or missing sprocket teeth.

## What to do now
1. **Choose the safer default and do not ride.** If you are questioning the safety of the drivetrain, treat the vehicle as non-roadworthy until a formal inspection is completed.
2. **Move the bike to a safe, level surface.** Ensure the engine is off and the bike is stable on its side stand or a center stand / paddock stand if available.
3. **Perform a visual "stop-ride" inspection.** Look for any of the following critical failures:
   - Cracked, bent, or missing link plates.
   - A master link clip that is missing, backwards, or loose.
   - Multiple "kinked" links that remain in a V-shape even when the chain is moved.
   - Sprocket teeth that are sharp, pointed (hooked), or chipped.
   - Shiny wear marks on the swingarm or chain guard indicating the chain is striking the bike.
4. **Measure the chain slack against the manufacturer's specification.**
   - **Locate the slack requirement** on the swingarm sticker or in the owner’s handbook.
   - **Find the tightest spot** by rotating the rear wheel (in neutral, engine off) and checking slack at multiple points along the bottom run.
   - **Check for excessive wear** by attempting to pull the chain away from the very rear of the sprocket; if you can see more than half a tooth, the chain is likely stretched beyond safe limits.
5. **Identify sensory warning signs.**
   - **Check for "clunking" or "clicking"** when pushing the bike manually.
   - **Look for excessive vibration** that was previously felt through the footpegs or handlebars.
6. **Arrange for professional adjustment or recovery.** 
   - **If the chain is out of spec but undamaged:** Only adjust it if you have the correct tools, including a torque wrench and alignment markers.
   - **If the chain is damaged or you lack tools:** Contact a mobile mechanic or a breakdown recovery service. Inform them it is a "drivetrain / chain failure" so they bring appropriate transport equipment.
7. **Document the condition.** Take clear photographs of the chain slack, the adjuster positions, and the sprocket teeth to show a mechanic or for your own maintenance records.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide between a budget or premium replacement set right now.
- You do not need to perform a deep-clean of the drivetrain if you have already determined it is unsafe to ride.
- You do not need to diagnose the cause (e.g., poor lubrication vs. high mileage) until the bike is in a repair environment.

## Important reassurance
It is common for chains to develop "tight spots" or surface rust, especially after wet weather or storage. Choosing to pause and verify the specifications is the standard safety protocol for motorcyclists; most catastrophic chain failures are preceded by visible or audible warning signs that were observed but not acted upon.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety screening only. It does not replace the specific maintenance procedures found in your vehicle's service manual. Different motorcycles (e.g., dirt bikes vs. street bikes) have significantly different slack requirements.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute mechanical or legal advice. If you are not confident in your ability to assess or adjust the drive system, do not ride. In the UK, riding a vehicle in a dangerous condition can lead to prosecution and invalidate your insurance. (MOT standards for Great Britain are used as the safety baseline).
