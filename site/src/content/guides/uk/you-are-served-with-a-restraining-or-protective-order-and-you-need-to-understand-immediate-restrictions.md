# What to do if… you are served with a restraining or protective order and you need to understand immediate restrictions (UK)

## Short answer
Assume all restrictions apply **immediately** from the moment you are served or otherwise become aware of the order. Stop all direct and indirect contact with the Applicant and follow all exclusion rules until you have read the full text and obtained legal advice.

## Do not do these things
- Don't contact the Applicant (the person who applied for the order) via any method, including "likes," tags, shared gaming chats, or third-party apps.
- Don't ask friends, family members, or colleagues to pass messages or "check in" on the Applicant for you.
- Don't enter any property, street, or zone named as an exclusion area, even if you believe the Applicant is not currently there.
- Don't return to a home you have been ordered to vacate, even for essentials, without explicit written permission from the court or a police-supervised arrangement.
- Don't rely on the Applicant telling you "it's fine" or "I won't call the police" — the order is a court instruction and only the court can change or cancel it.
- Don't delete message histories or call logs; these are evidence of your compliance or the context of the application.
- Don't miss the "return date" or "hearing date" listed on the papers.

## What to do now
1. **Read the entire order twice and create a "Prohibited Actions" list.**  
   **Identify the exact wording of every restriction, specifically looking for phrases like "must not contact directly or indirectly" or "must not go within [distance] of."**  
   - Use a pen to list every "must not" on a single sheet of paper.  
   - Note if the order allows for specific exceptions, such as contact through solicitors or regarding child arrangements.
2. **Isolate all digital points of contact immediately.**  
   **Mute, block, or archive conversations with the Applicant to prevent a reflex reply or accidental notification.**  
   - Disable "Quick Reply" features on messaging apps.  
   - If you share digital accounts (Netflix, iCloud, bank apps), do not log in to view the Applicant's activity, as this may be interpreted as "indirect contact" or monitoring.
3. **Establish a clear geographic boundary for your movements.**  
   **Locate every address listed in the order (home, workplace, school) on a map and plan routes that avoid these areas entirely.**  
   - If your commute or grocery run passes an excluded address, change your route now.  
   - If the order specifies a "zone" (e.g., 100 meters), treat that as a hard boundary.
4. **Follow instructions for immediate vacation of a property if an "Occupation Order" or "Exclusion Requirement" is included.**  
   **Leave the property calmly and immediately if ordered to do so, taking only your essentials (ID, phone, charger, bank cards, medication).**  
   - Do not argue or attempt to negotiate a "later move-out" with the Applicant.  
   - For any belongings left behind, wait to arrange a "safe collection" through your solicitor or by following the specific police/court instructions provided with the papers.
5. **Identify the specific type of order and its enforcement rules.**  
   **Look for the header to see if it is a Non-Molestation Order (Family Court), a Restraining Order (Criminal Court), or a DAPN / DAPO (Police / Cross-jurisdictional).**  
   - Check for a "Penal Notice" which warns that breach can lead to imprisonment.  
   - Understand that for most 2026 protective orders, breach is a criminal offence that allows for immediate arrest.
6. **Locate the "Annex 2" (Form FL435) or Response Form.**  
   **Identify the form served with the papers that asks if you consent to the order, do not oppose it (without admissions), or wish to contest it.**  
   - Note the deadline for returning this form, which is often 5 working days before the hearing.
7. **Contact the court office for administrative logistics only.**  
   **Call the court number listed on the order to confirm the time, date, and method (in-person or remote) of the next hearing.**  
   - Ask for the email address where you must send your response form.  
   - Do not attempt to explain your case to court staff; they cannot provide legal advice.
8. **Secure a solicitor specializing in the relevant court (Family or Criminal).**  
   **Tell the solicitor you have been served with an immediate order and need a "fixed-fee consultation" to understand your restrictions before the hearing.**  
   - If you cannot afford a solicitor, check the papers for info on Legal Aid or contact the Citizens Advice Bureau immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove the Applicant is "lying" or "wrong" in the next few hours; your only priority is avoiding an arrest for breach.
- You do not need to notify your employer or mutual friends immediately unless the order specifically restricts you from your place of work.
- You do not need to finalize your witness statement today; brief factual notes are sufficient for now.

## Important reassurance
Being served with a court order is a high-stress event that often triggers confusion or a desire to explain the situation. This reaction is normal. By following the "no contact" and "exclusion" rules strictly for the next few days, you are stabilizing the legal situation and preventing it from escalating into a criminal breach charge.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization to prevent accidental breach and ensure the user understands immediate restrictions. It does not cover long-term legal defense, evidence gathering, or the process for Varying or Discharging an order.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Protective orders are strictly enforced. Under 2026 guidance, you have the right to apply to the court to "set aside or vary" an order if it contains errors or creates impossible restrictions (e.g., preventing you from attending your own workplace). You should consult a solicitor immediately to exercise these rights.
