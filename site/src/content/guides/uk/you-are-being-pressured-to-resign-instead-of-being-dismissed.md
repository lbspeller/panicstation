# What to do if… you are being pressured to resign instead of being dismissed (UK)

## Short answer
Do not resign or sign anything in the moment. Slow it down: ask for the concerns and the process in writing, and get advice before you make any final decision.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hand in a resignation just to get the meeting over with if you feel cornered.
- Do not sign a settlement agreement, mutual exit, or voluntary resignation paperwork on the spot.
- Do not rely on verbal promises, such as a good reference, as the reason for resigning.
- Do not send an emotional resignation message or admit misconduct you dispute.
- Do not export, forward, or copy confidential business, client, or staff information.
- Do not assume you must decide by the end of today if you can safely pause.

## What to do now
1. **Buy time in writing.** Send a short message such as: “I am not resigning today. Please confirm in writing what the concerns are and what formal process, if any, is being started.”
2. **Ask them to name the route.** Ask whether this is performance capability, disciplinary, redundancy, a protected conversation, a settlement proposal, or something else.
3. **Get the meeting details.** If you are invited to a meeting, ask for the agenda, who will attend, whether it is a formal hearing, the possible outcomes, and any documents they plan to rely on.
4. **Start a clean timeline.** Write down dates, names, who said what, and exact wording such as “resign or we will dismiss you”. Keep this on a personal device or account.
5. **Secure your own employment documents lawfully.** Keep copies of your contract, job description, handbook policies, payslips, objectives, performance reviews, warnings, performance improvement plans, restructure messages, and emails or letters about concerns. Do not take confidential business, client, or staff information.
6. **Ask for the policy being used.** Request the disciplinary, capability, redundancy, grievance, or settlement process they say applies, and ask them to put any deadline in writing.
7. **Ask about a companion.** For a disciplinary hearing, an employee or worker can usually bring a companion such as someone they work with, a certified workplace trade union representative, or an official employed by a trade union. For a grievance meeting, ask to be accompanied; this right can apply where the grievance is about a legal or contractual duty, and employers should usually allow it if unsure.
8. **If you think the pressure is unfair or retaliatory, say so calmly in writing.** Use plain wording such as: “I feel I am being pressured to resign rather than being taken through a fair process. I want the concerns and process confirmed before I respond.”
9. **If a settlement agreement is mentioned, move to paper only.** Ask for the written offer and draft agreement. A settlement agreement generally needs to be in writing and you usually need advice from a relevant independent adviser for it to be legally valid.
10. **Protect time limits early.** Employment tribunal deadlines can be short. In many cases the deadline is 3 months minus 1 day from the problem at work or the end of employment, and Acas may need to be notified within the time limit. Check the correct route for where you work, especially if your workplace is in Northern Ireland.
11. **Pause before resigning for any reason.** Constructive dismissal and ordinary unfair dismissal claims often depend on employment status, length of service, and what happened. Some claims, such as discrimination, whistleblowing, or certain health and safety claims, can work differently, so get advice before making resignation final.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, accept an exit deal, or fight it.
- You do not need to write a long defence before you have the allegations, documents, and process in writing.
- You do not need to negotiate references, job titles, confidentiality terms, or leaving dates right now.
- You do not need to decide whether you have a tribunal claim before you have taken initial advice.

## Important reassurance
Pressure to resign is often about speed and control. Asking for written clarity, taking notes, and not signing in the meeting are normal, steady steps that keep your options open.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, aimed at preventing irreversible mistakes and preserving your position. If this moves toward dismissal, resignation, a settlement agreement, or a possible claim, specialist employment advice may be needed.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Employment rights and deadlines depend on your employment status, where you work, your length of service, and the facts of what happened.
