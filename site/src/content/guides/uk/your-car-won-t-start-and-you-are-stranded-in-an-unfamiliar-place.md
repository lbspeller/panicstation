# What to do if… your car won’t start and you are stranded in an unfamiliar place (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself and any passengers away from moving traffic first if you can do that safely. Then call your breakdown provider, or call 999 or 112 if you are in a live traffic lane, feel in immediate danger, or cannot get to a safer place.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep trying the ignition if you smell fuel, see smoke, or think there may be a fire risk.
- Don’t stand between your car and passing traffic, or lean into the road while checking anything.
- Don’t try repairs on a motorway or other fast road.
- Don’t put a warning triangle on a motorway.
- Don’t accept towing or recovery from someone who just turns up unless you can verify who sent them and where they are taking you.
- Don’t leave children, disabled passengers, older passengers, or pets in a clearly unsafe place while you walk away to find help.

## What to do now
1. Decide whether this is a traffic danger.
   - If you are in a live lane, on a blind bend, partly blocking fast traffic, or somewhere vehicles could hit you easily, keep your seat belt on, put hazard lights on, call 999 or 112, and ask for the police.
   - If you are already off the road, in a car park, at a petrol station, in a lay-by, or on a quiet street, stay visible and move to the next steps.

2. Make the car visible without putting yourself in the road.
   - Switch on hazard warning lights.
   - If it is dark or visibility is poor, use sidelights if you can.
   - Put on a high-vis or reflective layer if you have one and it is within easy reach.
   - If the car can be moved safely without pushing into traffic, move it to a safer place such as a marked bay, lay-by, service area, or well-lit area away from bends.

3. If you are on a motorway or slip road, follow motorway breakdown safety.
   - If the car is still moving and you can leave at the next exit, reach a service area, or reach an emergency area, do that rather than stopping in a live lane.
   - If you must stop, go left if you can, use the hard shoulder or an emergency area where available, and stop as far left as possible.
   - If it is safe and possible to get out, leave by the side furthest from traffic and wait well away from the vehicle, behind a safety barrier if there is one.
   - If you cannot get left or cannot get out safely, stay in the vehicle with seat belts and hazard lights on, call 999 or 112, and ask for the police.

4. Call the right help.
   - Call your breakdown provider, or your insurer if roadside assistance is included. Say clearly if you are alone, travelling with children, disabled, older, unwell, or feeling unsafe.
   - If you are on a motorway, major road, trunk road, or need traffic-management help as well as recovery, use the relevant road operator contact if it is safe to do so:
     - England: National Highways, 0300 123 5000.
     - Scotland: Traffic Scotland, 0800 028 1414.
     - Wales: Traffic Wales, 0300 123 1213.
     - Northern Ireland: for an immediate emergency call 999 or 112; for a serious threat to life or safety involving the road, DfI Roads lists 0300 200 7899 for emergency out-of-hours contact.
   - These road operators do not replace your recovery service; they can help with road safety, traffic information, or urgent road hazards.

5. Give a location that help can find.
   - Look for the road number, direction of travel, junction number, last exit, motorway marker post, driver location sign, nearby business name, car park name, petrol station name, or landmark.
   - Send a map pin to your breakdown provider or to someone you trust if you can.
   - If you are using an emergency phone on a motorway, face traffic while you speak and stay alert to moving vehicles or debris.

6. Choose a safer waiting place.
   - On a motorway or fast road, wait well away from the vehicle and moving traffic if it is safe to get out.
   - If you are not near fast traffic and feel safer inside, stay in the locked car with your seat belt on.
   - Keep children close and away from the carriageway.
   - Keep your phone usable: reduce screen brightness, use low power mode, and send one clear message to someone you trust with your location and who you have called.

7. Deal with offers of help carefully.
   - It is usually safer not to get into a stranger’s vehicle.
   - If someone offers to wait nearby while you call recovery, keep your phone, keys, and wallet with you.
   - If you accept a simple jump-start in a public, well-lit place, stop if anything feels wrong and do not hand over your keys unless you are sure who is helping you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether it is the battery, starter motor, fuel, immobiliser, or something else right now.
- You do not need to choose a garage, approve repairs, or decide what the fault will cost while you are still stranded.
- You do not need to argue about exact arrival times; get a reference number, confirm your location, and make a safer waiting plan.
- You do not need to move belongings out of the car unless you can do it without stepping into traffic or making yourself less safe.

## Important reassurance
Feeling exposed or panicky is understandable when your car will not start somewhere unfamiliar, especially if it is dark, cold, or you are alone. The first useful goal is not to solve the car problem; it is to become visible, reduce traffic risk, and make one clear call for help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and avoid common immediate safety mistakes. Later decisions about repairs, recovery costs, insurance, complaints, or onward travel may need help from your breakdown provider, insurer, garage, road operator, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, safety, mechanical, insurance, or professional advice. If you believe you or someone else is in immediate danger, call 999 or 112 and follow instructions from emergency services or the road operator.
