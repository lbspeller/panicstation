# What to do if… you feel throat tightness or hoarseness after exposure to a potential allergen (UK)

## Short answer
Treat throat tightness, trouble swallowing, or new hoarseness after allergen exposure as possible anaphylaxis: use your prescribed adrenaline auto-injector now if you have one, then call 999 and say “anaphylaxis”.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if throat or voice symptoms pass after a suspected allergen exposure.
- Do not let antihistamines or an inhaler delay adrenaline and calling 999.
- Do not stand up, walk around, or go outside “for air” if you feel faint, dizzy, weak, or unwell.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E.
- Do not eat or drink to test whether your throat is swelling.
- Do not go somewhere private and lock the door.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the exposure if you can do this immediately.**
   - Stop eating or drinking the suspected trigger; spit out anything still in your mouth.
   - If it was food, rinse and spit once if you can do that safely. Do not swallow.
   - If it was a medicine, do not take any more.
   - If it was an insect sting and the sting is visible in the skin, scrape it sideways with a bank card or similar object. Do not pinch or squeeze the sting.

2. **Use adrenaline now if you have a prescribed auto-injector available for you.**
   - Use it immediately for throat tightness, trouble swallowing, or hoarseness after exposure.
   - Inject into the outer middle thigh, through clothing if needed, following your device instructions.
   - Note the time you gave it.

3. **Call 999 for an ambulance and say “anaphylaxis”.**
   - If you are alone, use the auto-injector first if it is with you, then call 999.
   - Tell the call handler what you were exposed to, what symptoms started, and when adrenaline was given.
   - Keep the phone on speaker if you can.

4. **Get into the safest position while you wait.**
   - Lie flat and raise your legs if you can.
   - If breathing is difficult, raise your shoulders or sit up slowly with support. Avoid standing.
   - If you are pregnant, lie on your left side.
   - Stay in position even if you start to feel better, unless the 999 call handler tells you otherwise.

5. **Use a second auto-injector after 5 minutes if needed.**
   - Use a second dose if symptoms are not improving, are getting worse, or improve and then return.
   - Use the second auto-injector in the other thigh if you can.
   - Tell the 999 call handler the time of the second dose.

6. **Make it easy for help to reach you.**
   - Unlock the front door if you can do that without walking around much.
   - Put a light on and keep your phone close.
   - Ask someone nearby to bring your spare injector, stay with you, watch your breathing and alertness, and meet the ambulance if possible.

7. **If you do not have adrenaline, still treat this as urgent.**
   - Call 999 now and follow their instructions.
   - Stay with someone if possible.
   - If you have a prescribed reliever inhaler for asthma symptoms, use it as prescribed while waiting, but do not let it delay 999 or adrenaline if adrenaline becomes available for you.

## What can wait
- Working out the exact trigger with certainty.
- Deciding whether you “really needed” adrenaline.
- Non-urgent calls or messages to work, school, friends, or family.
- Sorting repeat prescriptions, replacement injectors, allergy testing, or future avoidance plans.

## Important reassurance
This is a frightening set of symptoms, and acting quickly is the safer choice. You are not overreacting by treating throat tightness, trouble swallowing, or new hoarseness after allergen exposure as urgent.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first minutes: stop the exposure if you can, use prescribed emergency treatment, get into a safer position, and get urgent medical help. Later decisions about testing, avoidance, replacement injectors, and follow-up may need specialist medical help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice or a diagnosis. Throat tightness, trouble swallowing, or hoarseness after allergen exposure can be a sign of a severe allergic reaction and needs urgent assessment, even if symptoms start to improve after adrenaline.
