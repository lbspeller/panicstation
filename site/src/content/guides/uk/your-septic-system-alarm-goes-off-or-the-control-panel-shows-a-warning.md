# What to do if… your septic system alarm goes off or the control panel shows a warning (UK)

## Short answer
Silence the alarm if there is a silence button, then reduce or stop water use in the property while you arrange help. Treat the alarm as a warning that the system may not be pumping or draining properly.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep running water to test it, including toilets, showers, baths, washing machines, or dishwashers.
- Don’t open lids, chambers, or covers on the tank or pump chamber.
- Don’t touch wiring, pumps, floats, or anything inside the control panel if you are not trained.
- Don’t repeatedly reset the system and walk away if the warning comes back.
- Don’t try to handle, divert, hose away, or contain escaping sewage or effluent yourself.
- Don’t let children or pets near wet ground, open covers, standing water, or any suspected sewage.

## What to do now
1. **Silence the noise without trying to fix the system.** If your panel has a silence, mute, or acknowledge button, press it. Leave the alarm system powered unless you can smell burning, see smoke, or there is an immediate electrical danger.
2. **Cut water use right down.** Ask everyone in the property to stop showers, baths, laundry, dishwasher use, and repeated toilet flushing. Use one toilet only if essential.
3. **Do only a safe outside check.** From a safe distance, look for standing water, wet ground, sewage smell, overflowing inspection chambers, or water near the tank, pump chamber, treatment plant, or drainage field. Do not lift covers.
4. **Check for a simple power issue only if it is safe, dry, and accessible.** Look for a tripped breaker or RCBO labelled pump, sewage, treatment plant, effluent, or drainage. Reset it once only if it has clearly tripped. If it trips again, stop and wait for a professional.
5. **Treat a recent power cut or heavy rain as important information.** If there has been a power cut, assume the pump may not have caught up yet. If there has been heavy rain, assume the system may be overwhelmed or taking in extra water until checked.
6. **Call the right help for your system.** If you have a service or maintenance company, call them first and say: “The septic or sewage treatment alarm is sounding, we have reduced water use, and we need urgent advice.” If you do not have one, contact the installer, a septic or sewage treatment plant engineer, or a septic tank emptying contractor if the tank may need emptying.
7. **Act faster if there are signs of backup or overflow.** If sewage smells are indoors, drains are gurgling throughout the property, toilets are slow or rising, water is backing up, or sewage is visible outside, keep water use near zero and ask for urgent attendance.
8. **Report pollution if effluent may be escaping outdoors.** If sewage or effluent is reaching land, a drain, a ditch, a stream, a river, a pond, or surface water, report it to the right regulator:
   - England: Environment Agency incident hotline 0800 80 70 60.
   - Wales: Natural Resources Wales 0300 065 3000.
   - Scotland: SEPA pollution hotline 0800 80 70 60; live operators are generally available 8am to 6pm, Monday to Friday, and the automated system triages urgent reports outside those hours.
   - Northern Ireland: Northern Ireland Environment Agency water pollution hotline 0800 80 70 60.
9. **Write down what you can see.** Note the time, alarm wording, error code, light pattern, recent power cut, heavy rain, heavy water use, and any smells, slow drains, backup, or wet ground. Give this to the engineer or contractor.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact fault now.
- You do not need to decide today whether the pump, float switch, control panel, drainage field, or treatment plant needs replacing.
- You do not need to start digging, draining, lifting covers, or trying repairs.
- You do not need to work out the legal position now, unless there is active pollution to report.

## Important reassurance
A septic or sewage treatment alarm is there to warn you before the situation gets worse. Reducing water use quickly and getting the right help gives you the best chance of avoiding a backup or limiting damage.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation, reduce water going into the system, and avoid unsafe contact with sewage or electrical equipment. Later decisions may need a qualified engineer, sewage contractor, environmental regulator, insurer, landlord, or property specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, environmental, engineering, medical, financial, or other professional advice. If you smell burning, see smoke, have sewage backing up indoors, or think anyone has been exposed to sewage, prioritise immediate safety and get urgent professional help.
