# What to do if… you are asked to identify personal items for return but are unsure what belongs to whom (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and do not guess. Ask the organisation to keep the items secured, give you an itemised inventory, and explain their written property release process before you sign anything confirming identity or ownership.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pick what seems right under pressure.
- Do not sign a form saying you confirm the items, ownership, or receipt if you are not sure.
- Do not take items away to sort out later if there may be more than one possible owner or claimant.
- Do not argue at the ward, counter, reception desk, or property office; ask for the bereavement, patient affairs, property, or senior contact and slow the handover down.
- Do not post photos of items publicly to identify them.

## What to do now
1. **Ask them to pause the handover and keep everything secured.** Say: “I’m not sure what belongs to whom. Please keep the items logged and secured until they can be identified properly.”

2. **Ask for an itemised inventory.** Ask for:
   - a written list of every item,
   - any property, valuables, or safekeeping forms,
   - any sealed bag, property bag, safe, cashier, lost property, or property office reference,
   - photos if their policy allows this.

3. **Ask what they need from you today, and what you should not sign yet.** You may be able to provide your name, contact details, relationship to the person, and proof of identity without confirming that the items are yours or belong to a particular person.

4. **Ask who the organisation can release the property to under its policy.** Use their wording. Depending on the organisation, the items, and the documents available, this may involve next of kin, an executor, an administrator, a solicitor, or another authorised representative.

5. **Make a short “known items” note using only reliable clues.** Write down only what you are genuinely confident about, such as:
   - distinctive jewellery or engraved items,
   - a recognisable phone case, glasses, keys, or wallet,
   - serial numbers, receipts, warranties, or recent photos,
   - descriptions from someone who saw the items recently.

6. **If there is doubt or more than one possible claimant, ask for the formal route.** Ask for the relevant contact, such as:
   - the bereavement office, patient affairs team, ward manager, or property office for a hospital,
   - the manager or administrator for a care home,
   - the property office reference if police hold the items,
   - the coroner’s officer or mortuary contact route if a coroner or mortuary process is involved.

7. **Ask who currently holds the property.** Write down whether the items are with a ward, mortuary, cashier, patient affairs team, property office, police, funeral director, care home, or another body.

8. **Protect higher-risk items without guessing ownership.** If wallets, identity documents, bank cards, phones, jewellery, or keys are present and you are unsure, ask them to:
   - keep the items sealed and logged,
   - record that you have not confirmed ownership,
   - explain what proof is needed before release.

9. **Write down what happened before you leave or end the call.** Record:
   - the date and time,
   - who you spoke to,
   - where the items are being kept,
   - any reference numbers,
   - what paperwork you were asked to sign,
   - what was agreed next.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who should receive disputed items.
- You do not need to sort, donate, return, or distribute anything until there is a clear inventory and release route.
- You do not need to resolve family disagreement right now.
- You do not need to rush before you have the organisation’s written process, contact details, and timescale.

## Important reassurance
Pausing is reasonable. In hospitals, care homes, transfers, and shared storage, belongings can be logged, moved, bagged, or handed over by different people, and careful checking can prevent avoidable loss or dispute.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on slowing the handover, getting a clear inventory, and keeping items secure. Later decisions about estate administration, ownership, disputes, or distribution may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes vary by organisation and circumstances, especially if a hospital, police force, coroner, mortuary, care home, funeral director, or estate process is involved.
