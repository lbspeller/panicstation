# What to do if… your credit file is suddenly frozen or locked and you did not request it (UK)

## Short answer
Treat a sudden credit-file lock, freeze, restriction, or blocked credit check as a possible identity-fraud or account-security issue until you know otherwise. First, get your statutory credit reports from Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion, then contact the credit reference agency or lender that shows the restriction and ask exactly what has been applied.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click “unlock” or “fix your credit file” links in unexpected emails, texts, adverts, or pop-ups.
- Don’t keep reapplying for credit to test whether it works; this can add searches and make the situation harder to read.
- Don’t pay a third party who promises to remove fraud markers or repair your file quickly.
- Don’t send ID documents to an address, number, or upload link from an unsolicited call, message, or advert.
- Don’t assume the problem is definitely fraud; a name, address, date-of-birth, or account mismatch can also block a check.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm where the locked, frozen, restricted, or blocked message came from.**  
   If a lender, landlord, agent, mobile provider, or finance company told you, ask which credit reference agency they checked: Experian, Equifax, or TransUnion. Ask whether they can pause the application and use manual or alternative ID checks instead of repeatedly running the same check.

2. **Get your statutory credit reports from all three UK credit reference agencies.**  
   Check Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion, because different organisations can use different agencies and the files may not match exactly. Look for:
   - accounts you do not recognise
   - recent hard searches you did not authorise
   - addresses, name variations, or financial associates you do not recognise
   - fraud warnings, protective registrations, or other restriction notes
   - missing or wrong date-of-birth, address, or electoral register details

3. **Check for simple mismatch problems before assuming the worst.**  
   Compare your name spelling, date of birth, current address, previous addresses, and address dates across all three reports. If an address or account record is wrong, contact the organisation that supplied that record and ask how to correct it; for electoral register information, check with your local council.

4. **Contact the credit reference agency or company that shows the restriction.**  
   Use contact details from the agency’s official website, not from a search advert or unexpected message. Say: “My credit file appears to be locked, frozen, restricted, or blocked and I did not request this. Please tell me what type of restriction this is, when it was applied, who requested it, and the safe process to regain access or challenge it.”

5. **If an online account may have set the lock, treat it as possible account takeover.**  
   Change the password for the email account used for credit, banking, and ID checks. Turn on two-factor authentication where available, then change passwords for banking, credit-card, loan, phone, and finance apps. Check your email forwarding rules and filters for anything you did not set.

6. **If you see a fraud marker or fraud-prevention flag, get the source before trying to remove it.**  
   If the report points to Cifas or another fraud-prevention database, ask which organisation filed the marker and use the relevant subject access route to see what is held about you. Contact the organisation that filed it and ask for its fraud-team process to challenge anything incorrect.

7. **If there are signs of identity fraud, report it through the right UK route and keep the reference.**  
   In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud. In Scotland, report non-emergency fraud to Police Scotland through its online, phone, or in-person routes. Keep the report reference, because lenders and credit reference agencies may ask for it.

8. **Contact any lender or company you do not recognise on your reports.**  
   Ask for the fraud team. Say you did not apply for or open the account, ask them to stop processing any application, and ask what they need from you to investigate. Keep a simple log of the date, time, company, person spoken to, and next step agreed.

9. **Consider extra friction only if your details may be repeatedly misused.**  
   Cifas Protective Registration can ask many member organisations to carry out extra checks when your details are used. Cifas says it costs £30 for two years. It can slow genuine applications, so use it only if it fits what you are seeing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to buy credit monitoring today to deal with a sudden lock or blocked check.
- You do not need to fix your credit score before checking whether your file contains mistakes or fraud signs.
- You do not need to decide today whether to apply somewhere else.
- You do not need to send documents to anyone until you have confirmed the official route.

## Important reassurance
A credit file that appears locked, frozen, restricted, or blocked does not automatically mean you have done anything wrong. It can come from security settings, fraud-prevention checks, identity mismatches, address problems, or an online-account issue. Checking all three reports and using official contact routes gives you a clear next step without rushing.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and prevent immediate harm. Later decisions, including disputes, complaints, fraud-marker challenges, or financial applications, may need help from the relevant credit reference agency, lender, regulator, adviser, or specialist service.

## Important note
This guide is general information for urgent first steps. It is not legal, financial, fraud-investigation, or professional advice.
