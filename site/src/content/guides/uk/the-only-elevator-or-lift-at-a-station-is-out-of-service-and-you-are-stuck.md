# What to do if… the only elevator or lift at a station is out of service and you are stuck (UK)

## Short answer
Move to a safe, visible place away from platform edges and moving escalators, then contact station staff straight away. Use a Help Point, ticket office, barrier line, or uniformed staff member and ask for step-free help to the platform, out of the station, or to an accessible alternative.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go onto tracks, trackside areas, staff-only routes, or alarmed routes unless station staff or emergency responders tell you to.
- Do not attempt stairs or escalators if you are not confident you can use them safely with your wheelchair, mobility aid, pram, luggage, pain, fatigue, balance, or breathing.
- Do not force lift doors, hold barriers open, or try to reset equipment yourself.
- Do not follow an app route that may include stairs-only bridges, steep passages, or non-step-free exits unless staff confirm it is step-free.
- Do not let a queue, crowd, or missed train pressure you into a risky move.

## What to do now
1. **Get to the safest nearby pause point.** Stand well back from platform edges, escalators, and moving crowds. Choose a well-lit, visible place where staff can find you.
2. **Contact staff using the closest safe option.** Use a Help Point, ticket office, barrier line, information desk, or uniformed staff member.
3. **Say one clear sentence:** “The lift I need is out of service. I cannot reach the platform or exit step-free. I need staff assistance now.”
4. **Ask for the exact outcome you need.**
   - To reach a platform: “Is there another step-free route or entrance you can take me through?”
   - To leave the station: “Please help me to the step-free exit.”
   - If this station is not accessible to you now: “What alternative accessible transport can you arrange so I can continue my journey without extra charge?”
5. **If you booked Passenger Assist, say so immediately.** Give your name, train time, destination, and direction of travel so staff can update the plan and contact the next station or train crew.
6. **Ask staff to stay with you or tell you exactly where to wait.** This matters if you are behind ticket barriers, in a large interchange, on a platform, or somewhere staff may not easily find you again.
7. **If you are on the Tube, Elizabeth line, or London Overground, ask for TfL’s lift-outage help.** Ask staff to plan an alternative step-free route. If there is no reasonable step-free alternative, ask them to book an accessible taxi at TfL’s cost.
8. **Make a simple record only if it is safe and does not slow down help.** Ask staff to log the lift outage and your assistance request. Note the station, time, lift location, and the name or role of the staff member you spoke to.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, claim costs, or work out who is responsible.
- You do not need to prove everything while you are still stuck.
- You do not need to keep your original timetable. Safe step-free access comes first.
- You do not need to explain your whole medical history or disability. Say what access you need right now.

## Important reassurance
A failed lift can leave you suddenly trapped or unable to continue, and it is reasonable to feel shaken or embarrassed. Asking staff for step-free help is appropriate; Help Points, station staff, and assisted travel processes exist for situations like this.

## Scope note
These are first steps to get you safely to the platform, out of the station, or into an accessible next option. Later decisions about refunds, complaints, missed trains, or future assistance may need the train operator, TfL, Network Rail, Passenger Assist, or another specialist body.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. What staff can arrange may vary by operator, station layout, staffing, and time of day. If you are in immediate danger or having a medical emergency, call 999.
