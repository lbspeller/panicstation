# What to do if… your car fuel gauge is unreliable and you are unsure if you can reach help (UK)

## Short answer
Assume you may have less fuel than the gauge claims. Do not set off until you have either confirmed a very short, safe route to fuel or arranged fuel help, roadside assistance, or recovery.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t test the gauge by driving further when you are already unsure you can reach help.
- Don’t keep restarting the engine to see whether the gauge fixes itself if you may be low on fuel.
- Don’t walk along a motorway or fast road to look for fuel or a phone signal.
- Don’t get into a stranger’s vehicle or leave a safe public place to accept fuel.
- Don’t attempt roadside work around fuel lines, the tank, or fuel components.
- Don’t use a warning triangle on a motorway.

## What to do now
1. **If you are on or near a motorway or other fast road, treat safety as the first problem.**
   - If you can, stay in or move to a place of relative safety, such as a service area, a proper lay-by, or an emergency area.
   - Put your hazard lights on.
   - If it is safe and possible, leave the vehicle by the side away from traffic and wait well away from traffic, behind a barrier where there is one.
   - If you cannot leave the vehicle safely, keep your seat belt on, keep your hazard lights on, and call 999.
   - If you are in an emergency area, use the SOS phone and follow the operator’s advice before rejoining.

2. **Make it easier for help to find you.**
   - Plug in your phone or preserve battery.
   - Share your location with someone you trust.
   - Note details you can read out: road name, junction number, services name, car park name, marker post, driver location sign, or nearby landmark.

3. **Do a two-minute fuel reality check without moving the car.**
   - Think back to where and when you last refuelled.
   - Check the trip mileage since refuelling, if it is available.
   - Take any low-fuel warning light or message seriously, even if the needle disagrees.
   - If the display shows estimated range or miles to empty, treat it as only an estimate and not a promise that you can reach the next station.

4. **Check for signs that driving would be unsafe.**
   - If it is safe, look for fresh wet patches under or around the car.
   - Notice any strong petrol or diesel smell.
   - Check that the fuel filler cap and fuel flap are properly closed.
   - If you suspect a fuel leak, do not drive; arrange recovery.

5. **Choose between a very short, low-risk drive and staying put.**
   - Only consider driving if a filling station is very close, the route is on slow roads, there are no complex detours, and you are genuinely confident you can reach it.
   - If the nearest fuel is on a motorway, dual carriageway, unfamiliar route, or fast road, do not rely on a doubtful gauge.
   - If you are not confident, stay put and arrange roadside assistance or recovery.

6. **Arrange help using the most reliable option you have.**
   - Contact your breakdown provider, insurer, bank packaged account, vehicle assistance service, or a trusted person who can help from a safe public place.
   - Ask specifically whether fuel assistance, roadside attendance, or recovery to a filling station is available.
   - If your location becomes unsafe, you are stuck in a live lane, or you cannot get to a safe place, call 999.

## What can wait
- Diagnosing why the gauge is wrong, such as a sender unit, wiring, sensor, or instrument fault.
- Choosing a permanent repair.
- Comparing garages or prices.
- Writing a full account of what happened beyond a quick note of your location and the fuel information you have.

## Important reassurance
A doubtful fuel gauge makes a normal journey feel uncertain very quickly. Acting as if the car has less fuel than shown is a cautious way to avoid turning a small vehicle fault into a dangerous roadside stop.

## Scope note
These are first steps for staying safe and getting help while you are already stopped somewhere safe. Later decisions about diagnosis, repair, recovery costs, or insurance may need a mechanic, breakdown provider, insurer, or other specialist help.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not mechanical, legal, financial, or professional advice. If you are on or near a high-speed road and feel in immediate danger, call 999.
