# What to do if… you develop severe cramps and weakness after vomiting or diarrhea and cannot rehydrate well (UK)

## Short answer
Seek urgent medical assessment by calling NHS 111 or your GP immediately. If you have severe red flags such as confusion, green vomit, or inability to stay awake, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to drive yourself to a hospital or GP if you feel faint, dizzy, or severely weak.
- Do not drink undiluted fruit juice, fizzy drinks, or alcohol, as these can worsen diarrhoea and dehydration.
- Do not take anti-inflammatory painkillers like ibuprofen or naproxen, as these can damage the kidneys when you are dehydrated.
- Do not take anti-diarrhoeal medication (like loperamide) if you have a high fever or blood in your stools.
- Do not swallow large amounts of fluid at once, as this often triggers further vomiting.
- Do not ignore a total lack of urination for more than 6 to 12 hours.

## What to do now
1. **Check for 2026 NHS emergency red flags and call 999 immediately if any are present.**
   - You have vomited blood or material that looks like coffee grounds.
   - Your vomit is bright green.
   - You have a sudden, severe headache or a stiff neck with pain when looking at bright lights.
   - You are confused, extremely drowsy, or difficult to wake.
   - You have severe, constant abdominal pain that is worsening.
2. **Contact NHS 111 or your GP practice for an urgent assessment.**
   - State clearly that you have severe cramps and weakness and cannot keep fluids down.
   - Mention if you are in a high-risk group, such as being over 65, pregnant, or having a pre-existing condition like diabetes or kidney disease.
3. **Begin "micro-sipping" rehydration salts if you are not currently vomiting.**
   - Mix oral rehydration solution (ORS) sachets with water exactly as directed on the packaging.
   - Take one teaspoon or a tiny sip every 1 to 5 minutes rather than drinking from a glass.
   - If you do not have ORS, use small sips of water or very well-diluted squash until you can obtain some.
4. **Identify and pause "Sick Day Rules" medications if applicable.**
   - Check if you take blood pressure tablets (ACE inhibitors/ARBs ending in 'pril' or 'sartan'), diuretics (water tablets), or metformin.
   - Contact a pharmacist or NHS 111 to confirm if you should temporarily stop these to protect your kidneys until you can rehydrate.
5. **Monitor your physical stability when moving.**
   - Stay lying down or seated; if you must stand, do so very slowly.
   - If your vision "greys out" or you feel you will collapse when standing, sit back down immediately and escalate your call to 111 or 999.
6. **Note the time of your last urination and the colour.**
   - Write down when you last passed urine to tell the clinician.
   - Note if the urine is very dark brown or orange.

## What can wait
- You do not need to eat solid food right now; hydration and salt replacement are the only immediate priorities.
- You do not need to determine the specific cause of the illness (such as food poisoning vs. virus) before seeking help for the dehydration.
- You do not need to worry about returning to work or school; you must remain isolated until 48 hours after your last episode of vomiting or diarrhoea.

## Important reassurance
Feeling very weak and experiencing muscle cramps is a common physical response to losing salts and fluids. While it feels overwhelming, these symptoms are well-understood by medical professionals. Calling for help now is the correct way to stop the situation from escalating.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for suspected acute dehydration. It does not replace a clinical assessment, which is necessary to determine if intravenous fluids or specific treatments are required.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional medical advice. Always follow the specific instructions provided by NHS 111, 999, or your GP.
