# What to do if… you suspect someone is accessing the money or property of a person who has died (UK)

## Short answer
Act fast to pause access: tell the relevant bank, building society, credit card provider, or card issuer that the account holder has died and that you suspect unauthorised access. If theft, violence, a break-in, or a confrontation is happening now, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront the suspected person alone or in a heated moment.
- Don’t log in to the person’s online banking, use their card, use their PIN, or try to “test” whether access still works.
- Don’t post details about the death, accounts, property, valuables, or family dispute on social media.
- Don’t throw away envelopes, statements, receipts, devices, cards, cheque books, or notes that might show what happened.
- Don’t change locks, remove items, redirect post, or take control of the home unless you are clearly entitled to do so or have advice.
- Don’t wait until you can prove who did it before asking banks or reporting channels to help stop further access.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause and check immediate risk.**  
   If someone is inside the home removing property, there is an active break-in, you are being threatened, or a crime is happening now, call 999. If it is not an emergency but you believe a crime has happened, call 101 or use the appropriate fraud reporting route below.

2. **Contact each bank, building society, credit card provider, or card issuer urgently.**  
   Ask for the bereavement team or fraud team and say: “The account holder has died and we suspect unauthorised access.” Ask what they can do now to:
   - freeze or block sole accounts from withdrawals and transfers
   - cancel cards and cheque books
   - lock online and telephone banking access
   - place a clear fraud or bereavement warning note on the account
   - tell you what proof they need next, such as a death certificate or interim certificate

   If you do not yet have formal authority, still report the concern and ask what immediate protective steps they can take. If the situation is time-sensitive, contact the bank directly rather than relying only on a multi-bank notification service.

3. **Tell the executor or likely administrator, if you know who that is.**  
   The executor is named in a will. If there is no will, an administrator may later deal with the estate. Send one calm message with:
   - what you noticed
   - dates, times, amounts, account names, items, or keys involved
   - which bank, police, fraud, or property routes you have contacted
   - a request that they take over formal notifications and record-keeping if they have authority

4. **Make a simple record before memories blur.**  
   Write down what happened in date order. Keep screenshots, bank alerts, statements, texts, emails, receipts, photographs, doorbell footage, and names of anyone who may have seen the access. Do not alter documents or devices to try to investigate.

5. **Reduce risk at the home without taking over unlawfully.**  
   If you are a lawful occupant, legitimate keyholder, executor, administrator, or have clear permission, secure loose keys and note who has copies. Photograph rooms and obvious valuables as they are, using wide shots first. Do not start removing possessions or changing locks unless you are clearly entitled to control access or have advice.

6. **Treat missing post or new financial letters as a warning sign.**  
   If you can lawfully see that bank letters, cards, PIN reminders, cheque books, loan letters, title documents, or replacement cards are arriving or missing, make a note and tell the bank, executor, administrator, or police reporting route. Do not open, redirect, or remove post unless you have authority.

7. **Report suspected fraud through the right UK route.**  
   For England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report suspected fraud or cyber crime through Report Fraud. For Scotland, report fraud to Police Scotland by calling 101. Keep the report factual: who died, what access or loss you noticed, what you have already done to stop further access, and what records exist.

8. **If a property in England or Wales may be targeted, set up Property Alert.**  
   If the person who died owned a registered property in England or Wales, HM Land Registry Property Alert can email alerts about certain activity on the title. It does not automatically block changes, but it can help someone spot suspicious activity quickly. If you already have authority or advice, ask a solicitor about whether any further title protection is appropriate.

9. **Use wider notification services only for non-immediate administration.**  
   Tell Us Once can notify many government organisations after a death in England, Scotland, or Wales when the death has been registered or an interim certificate is available. The Death Notification Service can help notify participating financial institutions, but for urgent suspected access, contact the bank or provider directly first.

## What can wait
- You do not need to sort out probate, inheritance tax, or the whole estate today.
- You do not need to prove who did it before asking banks or reporting channels to help stop access.
- You do not need to decide who should inherit anything right now.
- You do not need to negotiate family disputes today; focus on stopping further access and preserving a clear record.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel shocked, suspicious, or guilty when money or property concerns appear just after a death. Asking institutions to pause access and keeping a factual record is a protective step, not a final accusation.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce loss, lower conflict, and preserve options. Later decisions about probate, estate disputes, recovery of funds, property title issues, or allegations against another person may need help from a solicitor, the bank’s bereavement or fraud team, or the police process.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, professional, or police advice. If you feel unsafe or threatened, prioritise immediate safety and emergency services. If you are unsure whether you have the right to enter, secure, or control the property, focus on notifying banks, providers, insurers, police reporting routes, or the person with estate authority first.
