# What to do if… you are served legal papers and you do not recognise the case or the parties (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the documents as genuine until independently verified and act immediately to protect legal deadlines; contact the court directly using official Government Gateway or GOV.UK contact details to confirm the case exists.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the documents because you believe it is a mistake — a court can still issue a default judgment against you if you do not respond.
- Do not use any phone numbers, email addresses, or QR codes printed on the received papers for verification.
- Do not pay any "enforcement fee" or "settlement" to stop the case until you have confirmed it is real via official court channels.
- Do not hand over sensitive personal data, such as your date of birth or bank details, to anyone calling about the papers.
- Do not sign or return an admission form sent by the claimant or their solicitor until the case is verified.
- Do not post images of the claim form or case number on social media.

## What to do now
1. **Record the exact time and method of delivery.**
   - Write down the date and time the papers were received.
   - Save the envelope, as the postmark or delivery method (e.g., recorded delivery or hand-delivered by a process server) is critical for calculating deadlines.
2. **Photograph or scan every page of the document immediately.**
   - Ensure the claim number (usually in the top right) and the court seal are clearly visible in the images.
   - Capture the "Particulars of Claim" section, which outlines why the money is being claimed.
3. **Calculate your "deemed served" deadline.**
   - In England and Wales, you generally have 14 days to respond, starting from the day the papers are "served."
   - For documents sent by first-class post, they are usually "deemed served" on the second business day after the postmark. 
4. **Verify the case using the official HMCTS "Find a Court" tool.**
   - Navigate to the official GOV.UK website to find the contact details for the specific court listed on the form.
   - Call or email the court office directly to ask: "Is claim number [your number] a live and valid case in this court?"
   - Confirm if your name and address match the defendant details on the court's system.
5. **Protect the deadline by filing an "Acknowledgment of Service" (AoS) if the case is real.**
   - If the court confirms the case exists but you do not recognize the debt or parties, filing an AoS gives you a total of 28 days from service to investigate and file a defense.
   - You can do this via the official "Money Claim Online" (MCOL) or "Online Civil Money Claims" (OCMC) portals if the papers provide a login/password.
6. **Identify "Wrong Person" admin errors.**
   - Check for spelling mistakes in the name, old addresses where you no longer live, or middle names that do not belong to you.
   - Note down these discrepancies as they will form the basis of your response to the court.
7. **Consult free legal resources immediately for "eyes-on" help.**
   - Contact Citizens Advice or a local Law Centre and state that you have been served papers you do not recognize and need help protecting the deadline.
   - Do not wait for an appointment if the 14-day deadline is approaching.
8. **Report the documents as a scam if the court confirms they are fake.**
   - If the court has no record of the case, use the national "Report Fraud" portal (reportfraud.police.uk) to submit the details.
   - Contact your bank if you have shared any financial details with the sender.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove the debt is not yours today; you only need to acknowledge the claim to stop a default judgment.
- You do not need to contact the claimant or their solicitor to "explain the mistake" yet.
- You do not need to gather evidence of your identity until the initial court response is filed.
- You do not need to pay for a private solicitor immediately if you are using free advice services to verify the claim.

## Important reassurance
Receiving court papers for a case you do not recognize is a common result of administrative errors, identity theft, or "trace" searches hitting the wrong person with a similar name. It does not mean you have committed a crime or that you are currently in debt. Following the verification steps allows you to resolve the error or the fraud without the situation escalating.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for UK civil court claims (England, Wales, and Scotland). It does not cover criminal summonses or long-term legal defense strategies.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for legal advice. Deadlines and procedures vary significantly between England/Wales and Scotland. If the 14-day window is close to expiring, you must prioritize filing a response with the court directly.
