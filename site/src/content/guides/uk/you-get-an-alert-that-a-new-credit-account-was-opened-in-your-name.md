# What to do if… you get an alert that a new credit account was opened in your name (UK)

## Short answer
Act as if the alert may be identity fraud until you have checked it safely. Contact the lender using contact details you find independently, not links or numbers in the alert, and ask their fraud team to stop or freeze the account while they investigate.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, call numbers, or reply to emails or texts from the alert until you’ve checked they are genuine.
- Don’t give your date of birth, address history, passwords, one-time codes, or bank details to anyone who contacted you first.
- Don’t pay a debt you don’t recognise just to make the pressure stop.
- Don’t ignore letters from the lender or a debt collector; respond using independently verified contact details.
- Don’t send scans of your ID by ordinary email unless the lender or credit reference agency gives you a secure official upload method.
- Don’t apply for several new credit products while this is being checked unless it is unavoidable.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the alert safely.**  
   Go to the lender’s official website by typing the address yourself, or use a phone number from a genuine card, statement, or app. Ask for the fraud team and say you received an alert about a credit account you did not open.

2. **Ask the lender to stop or freeze the account.**  
   Ask them to:
   - mark the application or account as suspected identity fraud
   - stop the application, freeze the account, or block any further borrowing where possible
   - confirm in writing that the account is disputed as fraud and that they will not pursue you for payment while they investigate
   - tell you what details were used to apply, such as address, phone number, email address, or previous address
   - explain how they will remove or correct any credit search or account entry caused by the fraud

3. **Check your credit files with the three main UK credit reference agencies.**  
   Check Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion. They can hold different information. If you want the fullest check, MoneyHelper also lists Crediva as a UK credit reference agency. Look for:
   - the new account, loan, card, store finance, or catalogue account
   - hard searches you do not recognise
   - addresses, phone numbers, email addresses, or name versions that are not yours
   - financial links to people you do not know  
   Save screenshots or PDFs showing the name of the agency, the date, the lender, and any reference numbers.

4. **Dispute anything fraudulent or wrong on the credit file.**  
   Raise a dispute with the credit reference agency that shows the account, search, or wrong personal detail. Also keep pressing the lender to correct the data at source. Keep every dispute reference number.

5. **Add extra checks before more credit can be opened.**  
   Consider one or both of these:
   - **Cifas Protective Registration:** this places a fraud-warning flag so Cifas member organisations carry out extra checks. It costs £30 for two years, lasts 24 months unless you remove it, and can make genuine applications take longer.
   - **Notice of Correction:** you can add a short statement to your credit file. Where the credit reference agency offers it, you can use a password-style Notice of Correction asking lenders to request the password before granting credit. This can interrupt automated decisions and slow genuine applications.

6. **Report the fraud through the right official route.**  
   - If you live in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report it to Report Fraud online or by phone on 0300 123 2040.
   - If you live in Scotland, report it to Police Scotland online, by phone, or in person.  
   Save the report reference number.

7. **Lock down accounts that could be used to apply again.**  
   Change the password on your email account first, because email is often used to reset other accounts. Turn on two-step verification where you can. Then check your banking, credit, shopping, and mobile phone accounts for unknown devices, new contact details, or password-reset messages.

8. **Protect your phone number if it may have been used.**  
   If you have lost signal, received SIM-swap messages, or noticed mobile account changes you did not make, contact your mobile provider using its official app, website, or phone number. Ask them to check for a SIM swap, add an account PIN or password, and block unauthorised changes.

9. **Keep a simple evidence log.**  
   In one note, write the date and time, who you contacted, the phone number or website used, what they said, and any reference number. This helps if the lender, credit reference agency, police, or complaints team asks for a timeline.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to pay for credit monitoring.
- You do not need to make a formal complaint unless the lender or credit reference agency will not act.
- You do not need to replace every document unless you see evidence that a passport, driving licence, bank card, or other specific document has been misused.
- You do not need to explain the whole situation perfectly before making the first call; it is enough to say the account was not opened by you.

## Important reassurance
A new credit account alert is upsetting, but there are clear first steps. The priority is to stop the account being used, get it marked as suspected fraud, check whether anything else has appeared, and create a record that you acted quickly.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on what the lender finds, what appears on your credit files, and whether you need help from a complaints body, debt adviser, fraud-support service, or legal adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Lender and credit reference agency processes can vary, so use official contact routes and keep written records of what each organisation tells you.
