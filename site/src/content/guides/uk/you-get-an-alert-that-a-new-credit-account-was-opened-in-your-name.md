# What to do if… you get an alert that a new credit account was opened in your name (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the lender’s fraud department immediately using an independently verified phone number to report the account as identity fraud. Then, report the crime to Report Fraud (England, Wales, and NI) or Police Scotland and place a protective marker on your credit files.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click any links or call any numbers provided in the initial alert, email, or text.
- Don’t provide your full PIN, passwords, or any one-time passcodes (OTPs) to anyone claiming to be from a bank or "fraud department" who called you.
- Don’t pay any requested "activation fees" or "debt settlements" to an unknown lender in an attempt to close the account.
- Don’t assume the alert is a mistake or a glitch; treat it as an active impersonation attempt until confirmed otherwise.
- Don’t delete the original alert, as it may contain timestamps or reference numbers needed for your report.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the lender’s identity and contact their fraud team directly.**  
   Find the official contact details for the bank or lender by typing their name into a search engine or checking their official website. Call them and state clearly that you have received an alert for an account you did not authorize and wish to speak to the **Fraud or Financial Crime department**.

2. **Instruct the lender to freeze the account and provide a fraud reference.**  
   Tell the lender that you are a victim of identity impersonation and demand that they:
   - **Freeze or close** the account immediately to prevent further drawdowns
   - Confirm in writing that you are not liable for the balance
   - Issue a letter or email confirming the account is being investigated as fraud
   - Remove the "hard search" or account entry from your credit records

3. **Check your statutory credit reports from all three major UK agencies.**  
   Access your data from **Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion** to see if other accounts have been opened.
   - Use the "statutory report" option for each, which is a free, comprehensive legal right.
   - Look for addresses you have never lived at or "searches" by lenders you don't recognize.

4. **Apply for Cifas Protective Registration to block further applications.**  
   Go to the **Cifas website** and pay for Protective Registration (currently £30 for 2 years). This places a flag on your credit file that forces lenders to perform manual, enhanced identity checks before approving any new credit in your name.

5. **Report the incident to the national fraud reporting service.**  
   **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** Use the **Report Fraud** online portal (reportfraud.police.uk) or call **0300 123 2040**.  
   **Scotland:** Call **Police Scotland on 101** or report via their online form.  
   Keep the crime reference number provided, as lenders and credit agencies will require it to finalize their investigations.

6. **Add a Notice of Correction (NOC) with a password to your credit files.**  
   Contact each of the three credit agencies to add a short statement (under 200 words) stating you are a victim of fraud. Include a specific **password** in the note and state that no credit should be granted unless the applicant provides this password; this forces applications out of automated systems and into manual review.

7. **Secure your primary email account and banking apps.**  
   Change your email password immediately and enable **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** if it is not already active. Check your bank accounts for any "linked" accounts or new payees you did not set up.

## What can wait
- Disputing the specific credit score number (focus on removing the fraudulent entries; the score will correct itself once they are gone).
- Hiring a private identity restoration service (the steps above are the core of what these services do).
- Replacing your physical passport or driving license, unless you have confirmed that the physical documents themselves were stolen.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a fraud alert is startling, but the alert itself means the system has functioned as intended. By following these steps within the next hour, you are effectively "locking the door" against further misuse. Most UK lenders have established protocols for identity impersonation and will not hold you liable for debts incurred by a fraudster once the report is filed.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and harm reduction for identity fraud. It does not cover long-term legal disputes, recovery of funds already lost from existing accounts, or tax-related identity theft.

## Important note
Processes for credit reference agencies and police reporting can change. Always use the official websites (.gov.uk or .police.uk) for reporting. This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice.
