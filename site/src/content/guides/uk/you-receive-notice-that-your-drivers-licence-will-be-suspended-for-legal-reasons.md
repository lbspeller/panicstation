# What to do if… you receive notice that your driver’s licence will be suspended for legal reasons (UK)

## Short answer
Stop driving immediately from the date and time specified on the notice or from the moment a police officer issues a roadside revocation. Do not get behind the wheel until you have verified your entitlement through the official GOV.UK or DVA online services.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive "just one last time" to get home or to work; driving while disqualified or revoked is a criminal offence that can lead to imprisonment and vehicle seizure.
- Do not assume that Section 88 (driving while a licence application is pending) applies to you if you have received a formal notice of revocation or refusal.
- Do not ignore the letter; deadlines for appeals are strict and missing them can permanently end your right to challenge the decision.
- Do not use third-party "licence check" websites that charge a fee; only use official government portals.
- Do not attempt to "test" your entitlement by driving past a camera or police car to see if you are stopped.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact time your driving entitlement ends by reading the notice carefully.** If the letter states "with immediate effect" or follows a failed roadside eyesight test, you must not drive another metre; park the vehicle safely and leave the driver's seat.
2. **Verify your current status using the official 'View or share your driving licence information' service on GOV.UK.** 
   - You will need your National Insurance number, driving licence number, and the postcode on your licence.
   - Check the "Status" and "Endorsements" tabs to see if the suspension or disqualification is already active on the digital record.
3. **Determine if the notice came from a Court, the DVLA (GB), or the DVA (Northern Ireland).**
   - Court orders for disqualification (e.g., for drink driving or "totting up" 12 points) usually take effect the second they are spoken in court.
   - DVLA/DVA administrative revocations (e.g., medical or identity issues) have specific effective dates written in the text.
4. **Note the specific appeal deadline based on your location and the type of notice.** 
   - In England and Wales, you generally have 6 months to appeal a medical revocation to a Magistrates’ Court.
   - In Scotland, you must lodge an appeal with the Sheriff Court within 21 days.
   - In Northern Ireland, contact the DVA immediately to confirm the specific statutory review period for your notice.
5. **Secure your vehicle and ensure no one else drives it unless they are specifically insured.** Your own insurance policy is likely void the moment your entitlement ends; if you have "any driver" cover, verify with the provider that the policy remains active for other named drivers.
6. **Report the change in entitlement to your employer if your contract requires it or if driving is a core part of your role.** Do not attempt to hide a ban from an employer, as most professional driving insurance policies now use automated 2026 real-time DVLA check systems.
7. **Arrange immediate alternative transport for essential medical or care needs.** 
   - Use public transport, taxis, or ask a licensed friend or family member.
   - If you are in a remote area and have no transport, contact local community transport schemes or your local council for emergency transport options.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to sell your vehicle or cancel your insurance policy today; wait until you have confirmed if the suspension is temporary or permanent.
- You do not need to submit your full "defence" or medical history in the first hour; simply acknowledge the notice and stop driving.
- You do not need to notify your car finance company immediately unless the car is being seized by authorities.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a notice that ends your independence is a high-stress event, and feeling a sense of panic or disbelief is a normal response. The digital systems used by the DVLA and police in 2026 are highly accurate, but they are not infallible; following the rules today preserves your right to correct any errors through the proper legal channels later.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction for the immediate hours following a licence notice. It does not provide legal strategies for court or medical evidence preparation for DVLA reviews.

## Important note
This information is for guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Driving laws and appeal procedures differ significantly between England/Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. Always refer to the specific contact details and instructions provided on your official notice.
