# What to do if… you find an unfamiliar item, note, or suspicious object on your car (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself to a safer place before dealing with the item. If it appears attached, hidden, wired, box-like, leaking, under the car, or not quickly identifiable, move away and call 999 once you are clear of it.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t touch, open, move, peel off, or photograph from close range anything that looks attached, hidden, wired, boxed, leaking, or placed underneath the car.
- Don’t use your mobile phone within 15 metres of a suspicious item.
- Don’t get into the car if you feel watched, followed, boxed in, or unsafe; step back and move towards people, staff, security, or a public building.
- Don’t confront someone you think may have placed it.
- Don’t reply to a note with your phone number, address, workplace, social accounts, or routine.
- Don’t drive the car away if an unknown or suspicious object appears attached to it, especially underneath, near the wheels, near the fuel flap, or near the engine area.
- Don’t post photos online that show your number plate, home street, regular workplace, or other identifying details.

## What to do now
1. **Make yourself safer first.**  
   Go back inside a shop, workplace, station, venue, or other staffed place. If you are outside, stand in a well-lit, busier area and ask staff, security, or a nearby trusted person to stay with you.

2. **Briefly look from a safe position without touching.**  
   Decide whether it seems to be:
   - **A loose paper note, flyer, tag, or item** sitting on the car or tucked under a wiper, with no sign it is connected to the vehicle.
   - **Something attached, hidden, installed, or possibly hazardous**, such as tape, magnets, wires, a box, batteries, liquid, putty-like material, or anything underneath the vehicle.

3. **If it is attached, hidden, installed, or possibly hazardous, treat it as unsafe.**  
   - Do not touch the item or the vehicle.
   - Move away and, where you can do so safely, ask others nearby to keep clear.
   - Do not use your mobile phone within 15 metres of the item.
   - If you believe there may be a risk to life, move at least 100 metres away if you can do so safely.
   - Report it to staff or security if they are immediately available from a safe place; otherwise call 999 and say there is an unknown item on your vehicle, then describe what you can see.
   - Follow any directions given by police, staff, or security.

4. **If it is only a loose note, flyer, tag, or harmless-looking loose object, preserve options without making a big decision.**  
   - Photograph it in place from a safe distance, including one wider photo showing the car and surroundings.
   - Write down the time, exact location, and anything you noticed, such as a person waiting nearby, a vehicle lingering, or a repeated pattern.
   - If you are in a car park, shop, workplace, station, or managed site, ask staff or security whether they can preserve CCTV for the relevant time window, and note who you spoke to.
   - If you may want to report it later, remove it with minimal handling if it is safe to do so, then put it in a bag or envelope.

5. **Before getting in, do one careful outside check.**  
   Walk around the car once and look for anything new on the wipers, door handles, mirrors, wheel arches, number plates, fuel flap, rear bumper, or underneath visible edges of the car. If anything appears attached, hidden, wired, box-like, leaking, or placed underneath, step back and follow Step 3.

6. **Check for unwanted tracker alerts on your phone.**  
   If your phone shows an unknown tracker alert, take screenshots of the alert, map, time, and any device information shown on your phone. If your phone offers a manual unknown tracker scan, you can run one from a safe place; this may detect some Bluetooth item trackers, but it will not detect every possible tracking device. Do not handle a physical tracker if doing so feels unsafe.

7. **If this feels targeted or part of a pattern, report or get support from a safe place.**  
   - If you are in immediate danger, being followed, or someone is waiting nearby, call 999.
   - If there is no immediate danger but you are concerned, call 101 or use a police online reporting route and ask for an incident or crime reference if a report is made.
   - If the item, message, or behaviour may be stalking or harassment, contact the National Stalking Helpline for specialist support.
   - If you genuinely suspect terrorism-related suspicious activity, you can report it through Action Counters Terrorism.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out who placed it or why right now.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether it is “serious enough” to report.
- You do not need to confront anyone, investigate the item closely, or search online for explanations while you are still at the car.
- You do not need to change your routine, move home, replace your phone, or overhaul accounts in this moment unless there is clear ongoing risk and you have support.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to feel shaken or unsure. Many unfamiliar items on cars turn out to be flyers, mistakes, lost property, or harmless notes, but it is reasonable to treat an attached, hidden, suspicious, or targeted item as a safety issue until you are away from it and have help.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes only. If notes, objects, unwanted contact, tracking alerts, or being followed become a pattern, later decisions may need specialist stalking, harassment, legal, security, or police support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, investigative, or professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 999.
