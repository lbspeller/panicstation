# What to do if… someone is filming you in public and refuses to stop when asked (UK)

## Short answer
Stop engaging, move to a safer staffed place, and involve staff, security, or police rather than trying to physically stop the recording.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t grab their phone or camera, block it with your hands, or try to delete footage yourself; this can escalate and could lead to accusations of assault or damage.
- Don’t follow them, corner them, or keep arguing about whether it is legal.
- Don’t threaten them, insult them, or say personal details you would not want recorded.
- Don’t post their face online to name and shame them in the moment.
- Don’t go somewhere isolated to get away if it makes you less safe.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance first.** Step away, change direction, and put a physical barrier between you, such as a pillar, shop doorway, counter, or other people. Aim for a staffed area: a shop counter, station staff point, reception desk, or security desk.
2. **Say one clear line, once, then stop talking.** For example: “Stop recording me. I’m leaving now.” Repeating yourself can turn into a confrontation they can continue to film.
3. **Bring in someone with authority where you are.**
   - **In a shop, venue, workplace, school, or reception area:** Tell staff: “A person is filming me and won’t stop. I feel unsafe. Can you help me move somewhere safe and handle this?”
   - **On trains or at stations:** Move toward other passengers and staff. For non-emergencies on the rail network, you can text British Transport Police on 61016 with your location, direction of travel, carriage or platform if known, and a description. If you are in immediate danger, call 999 instead.
4. **If you feel threatened, followed, trapped, or blocked in, treat it as a safety incident.**
   - If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 999.
   - If it is not an emergency but feels like harassment, intimidation, or threatening behaviour, call 101 or report online once you are in a safe place.
5. **Ask staff or security to log it.** If you are in a venue, workplace, shop, station, or transport hub with cameras, ask staff or security to record an incident entry with the time, location, description, and what happened. Ask whether they can retain any relevant CCTV and give you an incident or reference number.
6. **Make a quick, private note for later.** Write down the time, place, what they said or did, what you said, what made you feel unsafe, and a description such as clothing, height, bag, vehicle, or distinguishing features. Note any staff names, roles, and reference numbers.
7. **Reduce what they can capture about you.** Turn away or cover name badges, lanyards, school logos, work passes, parcel labels, documents, and screens. Avoid leading them toward your car, home, workplace entrance, school entrance, or a quiet side street.
8. **If you think the recording is being used to target you online, do not search or engage while you are still nearby.** Get safe first, then save any links, usernames, screenshots, or messages you later come across without contacting the person.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether the recording was allowed.
- Deciding whether to make a formal complaint or police report.
- Searching social media for the video.
- Trying to identify the person online.
- Writing a detailed statement; a quick note now is enough.
- Contacting them later to demand an apology or make them delete it.

## Important reassurance
Feeling exposed, angry, or shaken by unwanted recording is understandable. Creating distance, reducing what they can capture, and bringing in witnesses or staff is a sensible way to lower the risk of escalation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: immediate safety, de-escalation, and preserving your options. If the behaviour repeats, becomes targeted, involves threats, involves sexualised recording, or is connected to abuse or stalking, you may need specialist help or police advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional safety advice. In the UK, filming or photographing in public is often lawful, but behaviour connected to filming may still be harassment, intimidation, threatening behaviour, or another concern depending on what is happening. If you feel unsafe, prioritise safety and get help promptly.
