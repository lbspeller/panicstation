# What to do if… an unfamiliar animal bites you and the skin is broken (UK)

## Short answer
Move away from the animal, wash the bite with soap and warm water, cover it with a clean dressing, and get same-day medical advice because bites that break the skin can become infected.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore a bite that has broken the skin, especially on the hand, foot, face, head, or near a joint.
- Do not try to close the wound yourself with glue, closure strips, or tape.
- Do not put thick creams, butter, oils, or powders into the wound.
- Do not scrub hard into puncture holes or dig around in the wound.
- Do not try to catch, corner, or follow the animal if that could put you at risk of another bite.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safe pause first.** Move away from the animal and get somewhere you can wash the wound without rushing.
2. **Wash the bite.**
   - Rinse it under running water.
   - Wash the wound and the skin around it with soap and warm water.
   - If it is a puncture bite, wash around it carefully; do not push anything deep into the hole.
3. **Let it bleed slightly if it is not already bleeding.** Gently squeeze the wound so it bleeds a little, then stop any ongoing bleeding with firm pressure using a clean cloth or dressing.
4. **Cover it.** Pat the area dry and cover it with a clean, non-stick dressing or a clean cloth.
5. **Go to A&E or call 999 now if any of these apply:**
   - the wound is large or deep
   - you cannot stop the bleeding
   - part of a finger, ear, or other body part has been torn off
   - you feel faint, very unwell, confused, or rapidly worse
   - you cannot safely get to A&E without an ambulance
6. **Get urgent medical advice today if any of these apply:**
   - the bite is on your hand, foot, face, or head
   - the bite is near a joint, tendon, or fingernail
   - you think teeth, hair, dirt, or another object may be in the wound
   - the bite is becoming hot, swollen, red, darker than nearby skin, more painful, leaking fluid, or smells unpleasant
   - you feel hot, cold, shivery, feverish, numb, or unable to move the bitten area normally
   - you are not sure whether your tetanus protection is up to date, especially if the wound is dirty
   - the animal was wild, stray, unwell, unusually aggressive, or could not be identified
7. **Use the right urgent route.** Use NHS 111 where available, or your local urgent medical route if 111 is not available. Use 999 for emergency help.
8. **If the bite happened abroad, or involved a bat, say this clearly.** Animal bites or scratches abroad, and bat bites or scratches in the UK, need prompt rabies risk assessment.
9. **Write down the details without taking risks.**
   - the time, date, and place it happened
   - what animal it was, as best you can tell
   - whether the animal seemed unwell or unusually aggressive
   - whether the bite happened in the UK or abroad
   - any owner details and vaccination information, only if it is safe to ask
10. **Keep the bitten area still and raised if it is swelling.** This is especially important for hand or finger bites until you have been assessed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to formally report the incident.
- You do not need to prove exactly what animal it was; give the best description you can.
- You do not need to repeatedly change the dressing unless it becomes wet, dirty, or loose.
- You do not need to decide about antibiotics, tetanus, or rabies treatment yourself; a clinician can assess that.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel shaken, disgusted, or jumpy after a bite. The useful first steps are simple: get safe, wash the wound well, cover it, and get medical advice the same day.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the hours after an unfamiliar animal bite. A clinician may need to assess infection risk, wound depth, tendon or nerve injury, tetanus protection, and rabies risk.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical diagnosis or a substitute for professional care. Use emergency services if the situation feels immediately dangerous or you are rapidly getting worse.
