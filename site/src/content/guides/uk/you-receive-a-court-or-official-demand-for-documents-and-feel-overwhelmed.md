# What to do if… you receive a court or official demand for documents and feel overwhelmed (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before sending anything. Put the deadline somewhere visible, preserve anything that may be relevant, and verify through an official contact route who issued the summons, order, or notice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not bin it, ignore it, or assume it is informal because you are not sure what it means.
- Do not delete, shred, edit, rename, backdate, or tidy up emails, messages, call logs, photos, files, or paper records that may be relevant.
- Do not send original documents unless the summons, order, or notice clearly requires originals or inspection.
- Do not send a large bundle of unrelated personal data just in case.
- Do not send anything you think may be legally privileged, confidential, or highly sensitive without getting legal advice if you can.
- Do not use only the phone number or email address printed on the document if you have any doubt it is genuine.

## What to do now
1. **Make one calm summary page.**  
   Write down: who issued it, the case or reference number, what documents or records it asks for, the date range, the deadline and time, and how it says documents must be provided.

2. **Verify it through an official route you find yourself.**  
   If it says it is from a court or tribunal, use an official court or tribunal contact route and quote the reference number. If it is from an authority, regulator, HMRC, Ofcom, or another official body, use that body’s official website or switchboard rather than relying only on contact details printed on the notice.

3. **Start a “do not delete” hold now.**  
   Stop routine deletion, shredding, mailbox clean-up, auto-delete, or file reorganisation for anything that could fall within the request. Keep originals as they are.

4. **Put everything in one safe place.**  
   Make one paper folder and one digital folder. Save a scan or photo of the summons, order, or notice, plus copies of every email, letter, proof of posting, upload receipt, or call note about it.

5. **Work out what type of document it is, without guessing.**  
   Look for words such as “witness summons”, “court order”, “statutory notice”, “information notice”, “notice to produce”, or the name of a regulator or authority. If you cannot tell, treat it as formal until you have checked.

6. **Separate the requested material into three groups.**  
   Use:
   - clearly requested and easy to copy
   - unclear or outside the date range
   - sensitive, confidential, private, or possibly legally privileged

7. **Ask for advice before dealing with the difficult group.**  
   If the request is broad, unclear, impossible in the time, linked to an investigation, or asks for sensitive material, contact a solicitor if you can. If cost is a barrier, check whether legal aid or free initial advice may be available.

8. **If the deadline is too close, ask about the proper way to request more time.**  
   Contact the issuing court, tribunal, authority, or your legal adviser. Say you are trying to comply, give the reference number, and ask what process they use for an extension, variation, narrowing, or clarification. Do this before the deadline if possible.

9. **If it is a civil witness summons to produce documents, ask about variation or setting aside.**  
   There may be a formal way to ask for a summons to be varied, set aside, narrowed, or clarified. This is time-sensitive, so get advice quickly and do not assume you can simply refuse.

10. **Provide documents in a controlled, trackable way.**  
   Send copies unless originals or inspection are clearly required. Make a short index of what you provide, keep your own full copy, and use a delivery or upload method that gives confirmation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to organise every record you own today.
- You do not need to write a long explanation of the whole background unless asked.
- You do not need to decide the whole case or investigation now.
- You do not need to answer unclear, sensitive, or possibly privileged parts without first trying to get advice.

## Important reassurance
Official paperwork can feel much bigger than the first step actually is. The safest first moves are simple: verify the source, preserve records, identify the deadline, and avoid sending sensitive material in a panic.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The correct next move depends on the exact wording of the summons, order, or notice, who issued it, and which UK court, tribunal, regulator, or authority is involved. Later decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, professional, therapeutic, or other specialist advice. If the request may expose you or your organisation to penalties, investigation, prosecution, contempt, confidentiality problems, or loss of privilege, get legal advice before responding in detail.
