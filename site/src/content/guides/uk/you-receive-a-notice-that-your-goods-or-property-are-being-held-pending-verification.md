# What to do if… you receive a notice that your goods or property are being held pending verification (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the notice using official contact details you find yourself, not links or numbers in the notice. Then respond in writing through the verified route with the reference number and copies of ownership evidence; do not pay fees or send original documents until you know the notice is genuine.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay “release”, “verification”, “storage”, or “courier” fees from a link or number in the notice until you have independently verified the organisation.
- Do not share one-time passcodes, passwords, full card details, or banking security information to “verify identity”.
- Do not send original documents such as a passport, V5C, deeds, certificates, or original receipts unless a verified official route clearly requires it and you understand why.
- Do not ignore a genuine customs seizure notice, because some options have strict time limits.
- Do not go to a police station, property office, depot, or port address without confirming the address, opening hours, ID needed, and whether an appointment is required.
- Do not rely on a phone number, QR code, payment page, or email address printed in a notice that you have not checked independently.

## What to do now
1. **Save the notice and the delivery details.** Keep the envelope, screenshots, PDFs, attachments, email headers if available, and any tracking information. Write down the date you received it, every reference number, and exactly what the notice says is being held.

2. **Check whether anyone is in immediate danger.** If the notice is connected to a situation where someone is in immediate danger or a crime is happening now, call 999. Otherwise, treat this as a verification and paperwork problem, not something you need to solve in a panic.

3. **Verify the sender without using the notice’s contact details.**
   - If it claims to be from customs, start from GOV.UK and use the seizure or reference number only after you are on an official route.
   - If it claims to be from the police, use Police.uk or your local police force’s official website, or call 101 for a non-emergency enquiry.
   - If it came by email or text and asks you to click, pay, or upload ID, treat it as suspicious until confirmed through an official route.

4. **Ask for the basic facts in writing.** Ask the verified organisation to confirm who holds the goods or property, the reference number, the legal or practical reason it is being held, a description or inventory of the items, what verification they need, and the next decision point.

5. **Prepare a short verification pack using copies only.** Include your name, contact details, the reference number, and a one-sentence request such as: “I believe I am the lawful owner and request return or release once verification is complete.” Add copies of relevant proof, such as a receipt, order confirmation, serial-number photo, insurance listing, repair record, tracking record, or a bank statement line showing the merchant and amount. Send ID copies only if the verified organisation asks for them, and mask irrelevant details where you reasonably can.

6. **If it is a customs seizure, choose the route carefully.**
   - If you accept the seizure may have happened but want the items returned, this is usually a restoration request. Send it as soon as possible and aim for it to be received within 45 days; if more time has passed, still ask the verified office what route is available. Non-perishable goods may usually begin to be disposed of within 45 days, and perishable goods may be disposed of straight away.
   - If you think the seizure was unlawful and want a court hearing, this is a notice of claim. GOV.UK says the notice of claim must be received within one month of the things being seized.
   - If personal property was left in a seized vehicle, write to the address on the seizure notice or seizure information notice. GOV.UK says the letter must arrive within 45 days of the date of seizure and collection should be arranged within a reasonable period, described as 2 months from the date of seizure.

7. **If it is police-held property, ask what can be released and what is still needed.** Ask for the property reference or receipt, the exact proof of ownership or ID needed, whether the item is being held as evidence, whether any part can be released now, and how collection works.

8. **Keep a simple record.** Keep copies of what you send and receive. Log dates, names, reference numbers, phone numbers you independently found, and what was agreed.

9. **If return is refused or keeps being delayed, ask for the formal route.** Ask the verified organisation what review, complaint, application, or court route applies to your situation. If the item is valuable, essential, or linked to an investigation, get legal advice before threatening court action or making detailed admissions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, go to court, or challenge the whole decision.
- You do not need to write a long explanation at the start; verification, ownership evidence, and a clear written request are enough for the first step.
- You do not need to replace the item immediately unless it is essential for health, safety, housing, work, or caring responsibilities.
- You do not need to answer pressure calls, payment demands, or urgent-sounding messages while the notice is still unverified.

## Important reassurance
A notice saying goods or property are held pending verification can be routine, including identity checks, ownership checks, evidence handling, import checks, or shipping flags. Slowing down, checking the sender, and using written records helps protect you from scams and from missing a real deadline.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: verify the notice, preserve your position, and avoid irreversible mistakes. Later decisions may need specialist legal advice, especially if the goods were seized, are evidence, are high value, or are connected to an investigation.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, professional, or specialist advice. Processes and deadlines depend on who is holding the goods or property and the legal basis for holding them. If you feel pressured, stop using the contact route in the notice and insist on written confirmation through official channels.
