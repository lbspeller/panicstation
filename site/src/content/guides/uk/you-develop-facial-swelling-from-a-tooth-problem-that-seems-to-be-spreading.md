# What to do if… you develop facial swelling from a tooth problem that seems to be spreading (UK)

## Short answer
Call 999 or attend A&E immediately if you have difficulty breathing, trouble swallowing, or swelling that prevents you from opening your mouth. For all other spreading facial swelling, call your dentist or NHS 111 for a same-day urgent appointment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to a GP surgery; they cannot provide the required dental treatment or drainage.
- Do not try to pop, squeeze, or needle the swelling yourself.
- Do not apply a hot water bottle or heat pack to the swollen area.
- Do not lie completely flat; stay propped up with pillows to reduce pressure.
- Do not wait until the next morning if the swelling is moving towards your eye or down your neck.
- Do not take more than the recommended dose of painkillers or mix different products containing paracetamol.

## What to do now
1. **Identify if you need an ambulance or A&E immediately:**
   - **Call 999 or go to A&E now** if you have any of the following:
     - Difficulty breathing or a feeling of throat closure.
     - Difficulty swallowing your own saliva or uncontrolled drooling.
     - Swelling that prevents you from opening your mouth more than two fingers' width (trismus).
     - Swelling that is closing your eye or affecting your vision.
     - Signs of serious illness such as a high fever (over 39.5°C), shivering, or confusion.
2. **Access urgent dental care for non-respiratory swelling:**
   - **Call your own dental practice** first and state that you have "rapidly spreading facial swelling."
   - **Use NHS 111 online** (111.nhs.uk) or call 111 if you do not have a dentist or it is out of hours (evenings, weekends, or bank holidays).
   - **Request an "urgent dental hub" or "urgent dental care" appointment** for same-day assessment.
3. **Manage the area while waiting for your appointment:**
   - **Keep your head elevated** by sitting upright or using extra pillows if resting.
   - **Apply a cold compress** (like a bag of frozen peas wrapped in a towel) to the outside of the face for 10 minutes on and 10 minutes off.
   - **Take paracetamol or ibuprofen** as directed on the packaging for pain, ensuring you do not exceed the 24-hour limit.
4. **Monitor for sensory changes that indicate escalation:**
   - Look for redness that is moving further across the skin.
   - Feel for skin that is becoming hot or tight to the touch.
   - Watch for any new difficulty in speaking or moving your tongue.
5. **Prepare information for the clinician:**
   - Note exactly when the swelling started and if it has moved in the last few hours.
   - List any allergies to antibiotics (especially penicillin).
   - Mention if you have a weakened immune system or diabetes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on permanent treatments like root canals or extractions today; the priority is stabilizing the infection.
- You do not need to wait for a routine check-up slot; swelling is categorized as an urgent need.
- You do not need to travel to a distant dentist if NHS 111 can locate a closer urgent care hub.

## Important reassurance
Facial swelling is a common sign of a dental abscess that has moved beyond the tooth. While it requires immediate professional attention, dental teams and hospitals are equipped to manage these infections quickly with drainage or antibiotics once you are assessed.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only to ensure you reach the correct level of medical or dental care. Treatment plans must be determined by a qualified clinician.

## Important note
General information only. This guide is not a substitute for professional medical or dental advice. If symptoms such as breathing or swallowing difficulties occur, seek emergency medical help immediately.
