# What to do if… your online banking login stops working and you suspect a lockout or account takeover (UK)

## Short answer
Do not keep trying passwords or codes. Contact your bank now using a trusted route, such as the number on your bank card or statement, the official banking app if it still opens, or 159 if you received a suspicious bank call and your bank is supported.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use links, phone numbers, QR codes, or chat buttons from texts, emails, pop-ups, or search adverts about the problem.
- Don’t share one-time passcodes, card reader codes, PINs, passwords, or banking app approval prompts with anyone.
- Don’t approve a payment, new payee, device registration, or login request because someone says it will “secure” your account.
- Don’t move money to a “safe account”, “holding account”, or “security account”.
- Don’t install remote-access, screen-sharing, or “support” apps because someone claims they are from the bank.
- Don’t delete suspicious messages or alerts yet; keep them available for your bank unless doing so puts you at risk.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and note the facts you can see.** Write down the exact error message, the time it happened, and any new-device, login, passcode, payment, or payee alerts. Screenshot alerts if you can do that without clicking links.

2. **Contact your bank through a trusted route.** Use the phone number on the back of your bank card, a recent statement, the official contact option inside your banking app if it still opens, or your bank’s website address typed in yourself. If this started with a suspicious call claiming to be your bank, hang up and, where supported, dial 159; 159 will not call you first.

3. **Use clear wording so the bank treats it as urgent.** Say: “I can’t log in and I’m worried about a lockout or account takeover. Please secure my online banking and check for unauthorised activity.”

4. **Ask the bank to check and restrict the risky parts now.** Ask whether they can temporarily block or restrict online banking, app banking, payments, transfers, new payees, and device registrations until identity checks are complete.

5. **Ask the bank to review specific account changes.** Ask them to check recent transactions, pending payments, new payees, standing orders, direct debits, contact detail changes, password resets, device registrations, and card activity. Ask whether suspicious payments can be stopped or recalled.

6. **Reset access only through the bank’s trusted process.** Follow the bank’s instructions for new credentials, app re-registration, device removal, card replacement, or extra verification. Ask for a reference number and how to confirm the report in writing or inside the app.

7. **Secure the email and phone number linked to the bank.** From a trusted device, change the password for the email account linked to your bank and turn on two-factor authentication if available. If your phone suddenly has no signal, shows SIM-change messages, or stops receiving expected texts, contact your mobile network using its official route and ask them to secure your mobile account.

8. **Reduce device risk without getting stuck.** If you recently installed a remote-access, screen-sharing, “support”, or “security” app, do not use that device for banking until you have removed the app or had the device checked. Use a different trusted device for bank and email password changes if you are unsure.

9. **Report scam messages after the bank is secured.** Forward suspicious emails to report@phishing.gov.uk. Forward suspicious texts to 7726 if your mobile provider supports it; if 7726 does not work, ask your provider how to report the text. You can also report a suspicious website to the National Cyber Security Centre.

10. **If money has left your account or you were tricked into approving something, report the fraud.** Tell your bank first. If you live in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report it to Report Fraud. If you live in Scotland, report it to Police Scotland by calling 101.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to close the account or switch banks.
- Arguing about refunds or liability before the account is secured and the incident is logged.
- Changing every password you have ever used; prioritise your bank, linked email, and mobile account first.
- Deep-cleaning every device before you have spoken to the bank.
- Reporting to every possible organisation unless money has gone, you have shared personal details, or the bank asks you to.

## Important reassurance
A locked banking login can happen for ordinary reasons, including security checks, app problems, maintenance, or too many attempts. Treating it as a possible takeover is still sensible: use a trusted contact route, get the bank to secure the account, then deal with the connected email, phone, and device risks.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce further harm and buy time. If money has gone, identity details were exposed, or the bank’s response is disputed, later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. Banks use different security systems and processes; follow your bank’s instructions once you have reached them through a trusted route.
