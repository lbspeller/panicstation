# What to do if… you receive repeated unwanted messages that suddenly become threatening (UK)

## Short answer
If you think the person may act soon, knows where you are, or you feel in immediate danger, call 999 now. If there is no immediate danger, stop engaging, keep the messages if you can, and report it to police on 101 or online.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t reply to argue, explain, threaten back, or try to calm them down.
- Don’t agree to meet them, talk it out in person, or prove anything face to face.
- Don’t delete the messages, voicemail, account, or thread in panic.
- Don’t post screenshots publicly, because this can escalate the situation and expose your details.
- Don’t click links, open attachments, or download files they send.
- Don’t rush to change your number or wipe accounts before saving what you can, unless you need to for immediate safety.
- Don’t assume it is only online if the messages mention your home, work, school, routine, family, or plans to come to you.

## What to do now
1. Get to a safer pause.

   If you are alone, move somewhere you feel safer, such as near other people, behind a locked door, or with a trusted person. Tell one trusted person what is happening and ask them to stay reachable. If you are at work, school, college, or a venue with reception or security, tell staff you are being threatened and ask them not to confirm where you are.

2. Decide whether this is an emergency.

   Call 999 if someone is in immediate danger, the person says they are coming now, they are nearby, they are threatening violence, they know where you are and you are scared they will show up, or you feel unsafe staying where you are.

   If it is not an immediate emergency, call 101 or report stalking or harassment online through police.uk or your local police force.

3. Use silent or accessible 999 routes only if they fit your situation.

   If you are in danger but cannot speak, call 999 and try to cough, tap, whisper, or respond to the operator if you can. From a mobile, if you do not speak or answer questions, press 55 or tap when prompted so the call can be transferred to police.

   If you are deaf, hard of hearing, or speech-impaired, Relay UK can support emergency contact through 18000 or emergencySMS. EmergencySMS requires your mobile number to be registered before use, so do not rely on texting 999 if you have not already registered.

4. Preserve what you already have without delaying help.

   You do not need perfect evidence before reporting. If it is safe and quick:
   - keep the full message threads, emails, voicemails, voice notes, call logs, usernames, profile links, and phone numbers;
   - take screenshots that show the sender, date, time, and threatening words;
   - write a short log with when it started, how it escalated, key threats, and whether they know your home, workplace, school, or routine.

5. Reduce ways they can locate you right now.

   Turn off location sharing in the apps involved. Check who can see your posts, stories, online status, workplace, address, school, regular places, or tagged locations. Ask people you live or work with not to engage with the sender and not to confirm details about you.

6. Report the account or messages without wiping the trail.

   Use the platform’s report tools for threats, harassment, impersonation, or stalking. If the threats are by SMS or calls, check whether your mobile network has a nuisance-call or abusive-message reporting route. If you plan to block the person, save screenshots and key details first where you can.

7. Use specialist support if the pattern fits.

   If the contact feels persistent, fixated, intrusive, or stalking-like, you can contact the National Stalking Helpline through the Suzy Lamplugh Trust on 0808 802 0300 or use its enquiry route. If the sender is a current or former partner or a family member, you can contact the National Domestic Abuse Helpline on 0808 2000 247.

8. When you report, say clearly what changed.

   Use plain words: “This started as unwanted messages and has now become threatening. I am worried they will do X.” Tell police whether the person knows your location, has said they are coming to you, has contacted your family, workplace, school, or friends, or has made threats before. Ask for the safest way for police to contact you if a call or letter could put you at risk.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether you want charges, a formal statement, or court action.
- You do not need to confront them, warn them, or explain your boundaries again.
- You do not need to immediately move home, delete accounts, or change your number unless there is a safety reason.
- You can sort out longer-term digital security, passwords, privacy settings, and support once the immediate risk is lower.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaky, embarrassed, frozen, or unsure is common when unwanted messages suddenly become threatening. Taking the escalation seriously and asking for help early is a protective step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: immediate safety, preserving key information, and getting the right support in motion. Later decisions may need specialist help from police, an advocacy service, a domestic abuse service, a stalking service, or a legal adviser.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
