# What to do if… you receive repeated unwanted messages that suddenly become threatening (UK)

## Short answer
If you believe the sender is nearby, knows your current location, or intends to act immediately, call **999** now. Otherwise, cease all engagement, preserve the messages as they are, and report the escalation to the police via **101** or the **Report Fraud** service for cyber-related threats.

## Do not do these things
- Don't reply to the sender to explain, argue, or attempt to de-escalate the situation.
- Don't delete the messages, the thread, or the sending account until you have captured records.
- Don't agree to meet the person to "settle things" or "talk in person."
- Don't post screenshots of the threats on public social media profiles.
- Don't click links, download attachments, or open files sent by the person.
- Don't wipe your phone or factory reset your devices before reporting.
- Don't ignore the escalation if the messages move from annoying to descriptive threats.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate danger triggers.**  
   **Call 999 immediately if the messages contain specific details about your current physical location, threats of immediate violence, or if you believe the person is outside your home or workplace.** 
   - Use the "Silent Solution" if you cannot speak: dial 999, stay on the line, and **press 55** when prompted to be transferred to the police.
   - If in Scotland, call **999** for emergencies or **101** for non-emergencies; do not use the Report Fraud service.

2. **Move to a safer pause.**  
   **If you are not in immediate danger but feel unsafe, move to a secure location such as a room with a lock, a friend's house, or a public space with staff.** Tell a trusted person exactly what is happening and ask them to remain contactable. If at work, notify security or management that you are receiving threats.

3. **Preserve the digital evidence without engaging.**  
   **Take screenshots that clearly show the sender’s handle, the date and time of the messages, and the specific threatening content.** 
   - Save voicemails or voice notes if the platform allows.
   - Do not worry about "perfect" evidence; a basic visual record is sufficient for an initial report.
   - Keep a brief log of the timeline, noting when the tone changed from unwanted to threatening.

4. **Restrict the sender's access to your information.**  
   **Disable location sharing on all social media apps and check your privacy settings to ensure your home address or workplace is not visible.** 
   - Review your "tagged" photos and "check-ins" to remove recent location data.
   - Instruct family members or housemates not to provide any information about your whereabouts to callers or visitors.

5. **Report the incident to the appropriate authorities.**  
   **For non-emergencies in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report cyber-enabled threats to the Report Fraud service at 0300 123 2040 or online at reportfraud.police.uk.**
   - For offline threats (letters, in-person messages) or if you prefer local police contact, call **101**.
   - Report the account to the hosting platform (e.g., Meta, X, WhatsApp) using their internal "Report Harassment/Threat" tools.

6. **Contact specialist support services.**  
   **If the messages involve a current or former partner, call the Freephone 24-hour National Domestic Abuse Helpline at 0808 2000 247.** 
   - If the behavior feels like stalking (fixated, obsessive, unwanted, and repeated), call the **National Stalking Helpline** at **0808 802 0300**.
     - Mon/Wed: 09:30–20:00
     - Tue/Thu/Fri: 09:30–16:00

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to press charges or provide a full witness statement today.
- You do not need to permanently delete your social media accounts or change your phone number immediately.
- You do not need to understand "why" the person is doing this before you seek safety.
- Longer-term digital security overhauls can be managed after the immediate threat is reported.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed, hyper-vigilant, or hesitant to "make a fuss" when digital messages turn threatening. Taking these steps is not an overreaction; it is a standard response to an escalation in behavior. You are not responsible for the sender's choices or their aggression.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute escalation of digital threats. It is intended to help you reach a safer state and begin the reporting process. It does not cover long-term legal protection orders or forensic digital investigations.

## Important note
This information is for guidance purposes and does not constitute legal advice. In any situation where you feel your life or safety is at risk, contact emergency services at **999** immediately.
