# What to do if… a therapist, counsellor, or support worker crosses sexual boundaries in messages (UK)

## Short answer
Stop replying immediately and contact a specialist support service or a trusted person outside the situation. You are not responsible for managing the professional's behavior or "fixing" the situation through further contact.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete the messages immediately if you can avoid it, as they may be needed for future evidence.
- Do not agree to meet in person to "discuss" or "resolve" the inappropriate contact.
- Do not apologize for setting a boundary or for stopping the conversation.
- Do not assume you are "overreacting" or that you must have encouraged the behavior.
- Do not feel pressured to report to the police or a regulator until you feel ready.

## What to do now
1. **End the contact immediately without further explanation.** **If you feel you must send a final message to create a record, keep it strictly professional and brief.**
   - Example: "Do not contact me via personal messages again."
   - Example: "Our professional relationship is over; do not message me."

2. **Save a copy of all communication to a safe location.** **Take screenshots of texts, direct messages, or emails, ensuring the sender's name or number and the date/time are visible.** If possible, export the entire chat history and email it to yourself or a trusted friend, then move the messages out of your immediate view.

3. **Contact a specialist sexual violence support service.** **Call the Rape Crisis England & Wales 24 / 7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line on 0808 500 2222 for confidential guidance.** They can help you process what has happened without any pressure to take formal action. If you are in Scotland, call Rape Crisis Scotland on 08088 01 03 02; in Northern Ireland, contact Nexus on 028 9032 6803.

4. **Identify the professional's oversight body for future reference.** **Check if the individual is registered with a regulatory body like the HCPC, or a membership body like BACP or UKCP.** You do not need to contact them now, but knowing who they answer to can help you feel more in control.

5. **Tell one person you trust that you no longer feel safe with this professional.** **Ask this person to help you manage logistics, such as finding a new service or being present if you need to pick up belongings.** You do not need to handle the emotional or practical fallout alone.

6. **Prioritize your immediate physical safety if you are at home or alone.** **Call 999 if the person knows where you live and you believe they may show up or if their messages are threatening.** If you are not in immediate danger but want police advice, call 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a formal complaint or police report.
- You do not need to find a new therapist or support worker immediately.
- You do not need to answer any follow-up messages or "justification" attempts from the professional.
- You do not need to decide if what happened "counts" as a crime before seeking help.

## Important reassurance
It is completely normal to feel a mixture of shock, confusion, guilt, or even "frozen" when a professional in a position of trust crosses a boundary. The responsibility for maintaining professional and sexual boundaries lies entirely with the practitioner, regardless of what was discussed in your sessions or messages.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling inappropriate sexual contact from a professional. It does not cover the long-term process of formal complaints, legal action, or therapeutic recovery.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or clinical advice. Regulatory processes vary between organizations; always prioritize your immediate safety by contacting emergency services if you feel threatened.
