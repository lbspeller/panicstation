# What to do if… you suspect a hidden camera was placed in a bathroom, bedroom, or changing area (UK)

## Short answer
Leave the area immediately and go to a safe, public location before calling the police on 999 or 101. Do not touch or move any suspicious objects, as this may damage forensic evidence or alert the person who placed it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to dismantle, unplug, or "test" the suspected device (this can alert the owner and may destroy forensic evidence).
- Do not confront a landlord, host, or suspect while you are still alone or in the property.
- Do not search the rest of the property for more cameras (prioritize leaving rather than investigating).
- Do not post images of the device or location on social media before speaking to the police.
- Do not assume you are "imagining it" or wait for 100% proof before getting to a safer location.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer location immediately.** Leave the room or property and go to a public space, such as a hotel lobby, a nearby cafe, or a friend’s house, before taking further action.
2. **If you feel you are in immediate danger or are being followed, call 999.** If the situation is not an immediate emergency, call **101** or use the "Report a crime" tool on the **Police.uk** website (or the Police Scotland / PSNI websites) once you are in a safe place.
3. **Leave all objects exactly as you found them.** Do not touch, move, or cover the suspected camera or any surrounding items, as the police may need to check them for fingerprints or DNA.
4. **Take a single photo or video of the suspicious object from a distance if it is safe to do so.** Only do this if you can do it quickly without touching the object or lingering in the space; if you have already left, do not go back inside to take a photo.
5. **Check for visible Wi-Fi network names on your phone once you are outside.** Take a screenshot of the available Wi-Fi networks, as "spy" cameras often broadcast their own unique network IDs (e.g., long strings of numbers or letters), but **do not** attempt to connect to them.
6. **Note down the exact time, date, and location where you saw the object.** Write this down while the memory is fresh, including a description of why it looked suspicious (e.g., "a lens inside a smoke detector" or "a USB charger facing the bed").
7. **Contact a specialist support service for guidance.** If you are in England or Wales, call the **24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line** on **0808 500 2222**; if you are in Scotland, call the **Rape Crisis Scotland** helpline on **08088 01 03 02**.
8. **Request medical or forensic support through a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) if you believe a physical assault may have also occurred.** You can find your nearest SARC by searching "NHS SARC" or by calling **111** (England/Wales) or the NHS Scotland self-referral line on **0800 148 8888**.
9. **Secure your communication and logs if you are in a rental.** Keep copies of check-in instructions, keypad codes, and any messages between you and the host or management, but do not tell them you have found a camera until the police advise you to do so.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific model or type of camera.
- You do not need to decide right now if you want to press charges or go to court.
- You do not need to prove the camera was active or recording for your concerns to be valid.
- You do not need to notify the booking platform (like Airbnb or Booking.com) until you have reached safety and spoken to the police.

## Important reassurance
The feeling of being watched or having your privacy violated is a physical shock to the system, and it is normal to feel lightheaded, panicked, or "on edge." Taking immediate action to leave the space is a protective response, not an overreaction. Support services are available to help you process this, regardless of whether a device is ultimately found.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety advice only. It does not cover technical camera detection, long-term legal claims, or property dispute resolution.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Voyeurism is a serious criminal offence under the Sexual Offences Act 2003. If you believe you are in danger, always call **999**.
