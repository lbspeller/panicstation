# What to do if… you are contacted about workplace benefits after someone has died and a deadline is approaching (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and verify the contact before sending documents or bank details. Use a trusted employer, HR, pension scheme, or insurer route, ask what the deadline is for, and request written confirmation of any extension or minimum step needed to log the claim.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t reply using phone numbers, email links, payment links, or attachments from an unexpected message until you have verified the contact independently.
- Don’t send ID, bank details, death certificates, or a signature just because the message says the claim is urgent.
- Don’t pay a processing fee, release fee, tax fee, or transfer fee to receive workplace death benefits.
- Don’t assume you are the right person to claim just because you were contacted; ask who the scheme or insurer can deal with.
- Don’t guess dates, National Insurance numbers, addresses, relationship details, or bank information; say you are checking.
- Don’t make promises about sharing, spending, investing, or distributing any money while you are still verifying the claim.

## What to do now
1. **Make a simple deadline map.** Write down who contacted you, the benefit mentioned, the stated deadline, what they say is needed, and what they say will happen if the date is missed.

2. **Verify the contact through a trusted route.** Use the employer’s official website, a number already held in the person’s papers, a payslip, a benefits booklet, or a pension scheme document. Do not rely on the details in the unexpected message.

3. **Ask exactly what the deadline is.** Ask them to confirm in writing whether the date comes from law, scheme rules, an insurer process, tax treatment, or an internal target. Ask what can still be done if you cannot provide everything by that date.

4. **Ask for the minimum safe step to log the claim.** Use plain wording: “I am verifying this and gathering documents. Please confirm what minimum information you need now to log the claim and protect the deadline.”

5. **Request an extension in writing if documents are missing.** Say what you are waiting for, such as a death certificate, proof of relationship, probate document, letters of administration, or executor details. Ask them to reply with the new date or the next safest step.

6. **Check who has authority to deal with the benefit.** Ask whether the scheme can deal with a named beneficiary, a nominee, a dependant, an executor, an administrator, or the next of kin. If you are not the personal representative, ask what they can tell you now and what authority they need.

7. **Ask what documents they will accept before sending anything.** Ask whether they need an original death certificate, an official certified copy, a scan, an interim death certificate, proof of relationship, proof of address, bank evidence, probate, or letters of administration. Send only what is needed, using the safest route they confirm.

8. **Order or locate official certified copies if needed.** Some organisations may need to see an official copy, but others may accept a scan or may not need one yet. Keep a note of where each copy is sent and ask for originals to be returned.

9. **Use Tell Us Once if it applies.** If the person who died lived in England, Scotland, or Wales, Tell Us Once can notify many government organisations and some public sector pension schemes. If the person lived in Northern Ireland, use the NI Direct guidance instead.

10. **Keep one benefits admin folder.** Save the message, claim pack, envelopes, screenshots, forms, and proof of posting or uploading. Keep a call log with dates, times, names, departments, and what was agreed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today how any money will be used, invested, shared, or distributed.
- You do not need to finish every estate task before replying to the employer, pension scheme, or insurer.
- You do not need to work out tax treatment now; ask the scheme or insurer what they will deduct, report, or explain later.
- You do not need to resolve family disagreements before asking the organisation to protect the deadline and explain who they can deal with.

## Important reassurance
This can feel urgent and threatening when you are grieving, especially if the message mentions a deadline. The safest first move is not to rush; it is to verify the contact, get the deadline explained in writing, and ask what minimum step keeps the claim open while documents are gathered.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce the risk of missed deadlines, scams, and avoidable admin mistakes. Later decisions about probate, tax, disputes, complaints, or distribution of money may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, pension, insurance, employment, or professional advice. Workplace benefits and pensions vary by employer, scheme, insurer, and the person’s circumstances.
