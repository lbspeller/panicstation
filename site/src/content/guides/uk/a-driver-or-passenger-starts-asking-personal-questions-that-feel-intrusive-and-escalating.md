# What to do if… a driver or passenger starts asking personal questions that feel intrusive and escalating (UK)

## Short answer
Stop providing personal information, share your live location with a contact immediately, and request to end the trip at a well-lit, busy public location if your discomfort increases.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t share your exact home address, workplace, daily routine, or whether you live alone.
- Don’t hand your phone to the other person or unlock it to show them social media profiles.
- Don’t agree to detours, "scenic routes," or stops that were not part of your original booking.
- Don’t attempt to "prove a point" or escalate an argument if you sense the other person is becoming aggressive.
- Don’t exit the vehicle in a dark or isolated area just to end the conversation; wait for a public spot.

## What to do now
1. **Identify escalating cues and set a single, neutral boundary.**
   Observe if the person is repeatedly looking in the mirror, slowing the vehicle down, or asking follow-up questions after you have been quiet. Use a flat, polite statement such as: "I don't discuss my personal life during rides, thank you."

2. **Establish immediate external accountability by making a call.**
   **Call a friend or family member and speak loudly enough to be heard.**
   If no one answers, speak as if you are leaving a voicemail:
   - "I am in a vehicle near [current landmark/street]."
   - "I will be home in [number] minutes; please wait for me at the door."
   - "I am sending you the vehicle registration number now."

3. **Use the in-app safety tools if using a rideshare service.**
   **Open your app and use the "Share Trip Status" or "Trusted Contacts" feature.**
   This allows a contact to track your journey in real-time. If the situation feels dangerous, locate the "Emergency Assistance" (often a shield icon) to access the in-app emergency button which shares your GPS and vehicle details with dispatchers.

4. **Change your destination to a busy, well-lit public location.**
   **Instruct the driver to drop you off at a petrol station, supermarket, or hotel lobby.**
   You do not have to go to your front door. If you feel unsafe, say: "Actually, please drop me at this [petrol station/shop] instead, I need to pick something up."

5. **Discreetly check your exit path.**
   **Ensure the door is not locked by child-safety locks by testing the handle slightly.**
   If you are on a train or at a station and feel harassed, you can text the British Transport Police on 61016 to report the incident discreetly.

6. **Utilize the "Silent Solution" if you cannot speak to emergency services.**
   **Dial 999 and, if you are prompted by the automated system because you cannot speak, press 55.**
   This informs the operator that your call is a genuine emergency. If you are on a landline (e.g., at a station), the operator will transfer you to the police if they hear background noise even if you stay silent.

7. **Enter a staffed building immediately after exiting the vehicle.**
   **Go into a shop, cafe, or hotel and stay there until you are certain the vehicle has driven away.**
   Wait several minutes before arranging your final walk home or calling a different transport provider.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to file a formal complaint or a police report.
- You do not need to justify your "rudeness" or worry about the driver’s rating.
- You do not need to remember every detail of the conversation; capturing the vehicle plate and time is sufficient for now.

## Important reassurance
It is a normal and valid response to feel alarmed when a stranger in a confined space asks intrusive questions. Your priority is your physical exit and safety; you are not required to be social or provide information to someone who makes you uncomfortable.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an escalating interaction in a vehicle or on transport. It does not cover long-term reporting or legal actions against transport providers.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. In any situation involving an immediate threat to life or safety, contact 999 immediately.
