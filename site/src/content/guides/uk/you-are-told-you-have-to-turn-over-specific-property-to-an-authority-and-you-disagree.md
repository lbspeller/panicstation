# What to do if… you are told you have to turn over specific property to an authority and you disagree (UK)

## Short answer
State clearly that you do not consent to a voluntary handover, ask for the specific legal power or written warrant being used, and ensure you receive a formal itemised receipt before the property is removed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not physically resist, attempt to snatch items back, or obstruct officers, as this can lead to arrest for "obstruction of justice" or "assault."
- Do not "just hand it over" without a written record; once property enters the "chain of custody" without a receipt, it is significantly harder to track or recover.
- Do not sign any document that describes the handover as "voluntary" or "consensual" if you actually disagree with the seizure.
- Do not delete, wipe, or hide digital or physical evidence once a demand has been made, as this may be treated as a separate criminal offence.
- Do not provide passwords or PINs immediately just because an officer asks; wait for a formal written "Section 49" notice which typically provides a window for legal advice.
- Do not get into a verbal argument about the "rightness" of the seizure; focus exclusively on the paperwork and the receipt.

## What to do now
1. **Identify exactly which authority is making the demand and who the lead officer is.**  
   Ask for the officer’s name, warrant number, and the station or office they are based at (e.g., Police, HMRC, Border Force, or Prison Service).

2. **Ask if the handover is a voluntary request or a legal requirement.**  
   If the officer states it is voluntary, you may say: "I do not consent to handing this over voluntarily. If you are using a legal power to seize it, please specify which power you are relying on."

3. **Request to see and read the warrant, production order, or written "Notice of Powers and Rights."**  
   If they have a warrant, check that the address, date, and description of items match what is being taken; under PACE Code B, you are entitled to a copy of this notice or warrant.

4. **Insist on a comprehensive, itemised receipt (such as a Seizure Information Notice or Property Receipt) before the items leave your sight.**  
   The receipt must list specific identifiers for every item, such as brand names, serial numbers, IMEI numbers for phones, or the exact number of documents in a folder.

5. **State your disagreement clearly but calmly for the official record.**  
   Say: "I do not consent to this seizure and I wish it to be recorded that I am handing this over under protest because I believe I am legally required to do so, not because I agree with it."

6. **Check for a "Notice 12A" if the seizure is being conducted by HMRC or Border Force.**  
   This specific form is the legal trigger for customs seizures; keep it safe, as you only have one calendar month from the date on this notice to file a "Notice of Claim" to challenge the legality of the seizure.

7. **Verify if the items are being "seized" or "detained for sifting" elsewhere.**  
   Under the Criminal Justice and Police Act 2001, authorities may take large volumes of material to "sift" through later; if this happens, ask for a written explanation of why the sifting cannot be done on-site.

8. **Demand a formal "Section 49 RIPA Notice" if you are being ordered to provide a password or PIN.**  
   A verbal request is not a legal compulsion; a valid notice must be in writing, authorised by a judge (or senior officer in some cases), and specify a deadline for compliance (it is rarely "immediate").

9. **Request an update to your "Property Card" or inventory immediately if this occurs within a prison.**  
   Under Prison Rule 43, staff must maintain an accurate inventory; ensure the item is listed as "disputed" or "taken by staff" on your records and do not sign the inventory until the description of the item’s condition is accurate.

10. **Contact a solicitor immediately to provide the reference numbers from your seizure paperwork.**  
    If you are in custody, request the duty solicitor; if you are not in custody, find a specialist solicitor who handles "condemnation proceedings" (for HMRC) or "PACE challenges" (for police).

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove the items are "innocent" or "legal" in the first few minutes.
- You do not need to file a formal complaint against the officer on the spot.
- You do not need to decide which court route to take (such as a Section 59 application for the return of property) until you have spoken with a solicitor.
- You do not need to provide passwords or encryption keys until a formal notice with a future deadline is served.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a loss of control when personal property is taken by the state. Authorities are bound by strict procedural codes (like PACE Code B); by insisting on a receipt and a legal basis now, you are creating the necessary evidence for a solicitor to challenge the seizure later.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the moments during and immediately after a property demand. It does not cover the long-term legal strategy for "restoration" or "condemnation" proceedings in court.

## Important note
This information is for guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Procedures vary significantly between the Police (PACE), HMRC/Border Force (CEMA), and the Prison Service. If you feel that refusing to hand over an item will lead to immediate physical harm or arrest, prioritize your safety and focus on obtaining an accurate written receipt for later challenge.
