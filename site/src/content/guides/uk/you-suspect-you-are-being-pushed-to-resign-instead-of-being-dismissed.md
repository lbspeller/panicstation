# What to do if… you suspect you are being pushed to resign instead of being dismissed (UK)

## Short answer
Request a written summary of your status immediately and do not sign any documents or agree to "voluntary" terms during the initial meeting. Your priority is to establish if you are "at risk" (consultation) or already dismissed (notice), and to secure your personal employment records before system access is revoked.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign or offer to leave "on your own terms" — this usually waives your right to statutory redundancy pay and may block you from claiming unfair dismissal.
- Do not sign a settlement agreement or redundancy letter in the room — you are entitled to take these away to review them calmly or seek legal advice.
- Do not take or copy company-confidential data or client lists — this can be grounds for gross misconduct and may invalidate your redundancy package.
- Do not use company email to send angry or emotional messages — all internal communications can be used as evidence in a future tribunal.
- Do not assume a verbal promise of a "good package" is binding — if it is not in a signed document, it may not be honored.

## What to do now
1. **Request a written statement confirming your current employment status and the reasons for the announcement.** This document must clarify if you are "at risk of redundancy" (the start of a consultation) or if a final decision has already been made to issue you "notice of dismissal."
2. **Download or screenshot your essential personal employment records immediately.** Prioritize your contract of employment, your last three months of payslips, your P60, and any record of your remaining holiday balance.
   - Save these to a personal device or personal cloud storage, not a company-owned drive.
3. **Ask for the specific timetable for the consultation period and how selection was determined.** If the employer is proposing 20 or more redundancies at one site within 90 days, collective consultation rules apply, requiring a minimum 30-day (or 45-day for 100+ staff) period before dismissals.
4. **Identify the "selection pool" and the scoring criteria used to include you.** Ask in writing for the objective criteria (such as skills, attendance, or performance) and request your individual scores if you have already been selected.
5. **Establish exactly how your final payments will be structured and when they will be paid.** Ask for a breakdown including:
   - statutory redundancy pay (if you have 2+ years of service)
   - notice pay (statutory or contractual, whichever is longer)
   - payment for unused holiday days
   - any "payment in lieu of notice" (PILON) or garden leave arrangements.
6. **Check your right to "reasonable time off" to look for new work.** If you have at least 2 years of continuous service and are under notice of redundancy, you have a statutory right to paid time off for interviews or training.
7. **Create a private, dated timeline of all events and conversations related to the announcement.** Record who was present, what was said, and any deadlines you were given, as memories fade quickly and these notes are vital for appeals or tribunal claims.
8. **Contact your trade union representative or Acas if you suspect the process is unfair or rushed.** Be aware that the time limit for bringing most employment tribunal claims (including unfair dismissal) is now 6 months from the date your employment ends.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to appeal the redundancy or accept an enhanced package in the first 24 hours.
- You do not need to update your professional profiles or notify your network immediately.
- You do not need to calculate your long-term financial position before you have the final written breakdown of your package.

## Important reassurance
The physical and mental shock of an unexpected redundancy announcement — such as shaking, difficulty focusing, or sudden exhaustion — is a normal physiological response to a crisis. These reactions do not mean you are unable to handle the situation; they are signs that you need to slow down and avoid making immediate, binding commitments until the initial shock has passed.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate stabilization of your employment rights in the hours and days following a surprise redundancy announcement. It does not provide long-term career advice or detailed legal strategies for complex tribunal litigation.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment law is subject to specific eligibility criteria, including length of service and employment status. From April 2026, penalties for failing to follow collective consultation rules have increased significantly; if you believe your employer has skipped consultation, seek professional advice from a union or solicitor immediately.
