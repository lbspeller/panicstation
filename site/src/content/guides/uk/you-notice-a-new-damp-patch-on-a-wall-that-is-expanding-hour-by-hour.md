# What to do if… you notice a new damp patch on a wall that is expanding hour by hour (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as an active leak until proven otherwise: keep people away from nearby electrics, reduce or shut off the water if safe, and get urgent repair help arranged.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore it because it is “only damp”; hour-by-hour growth can mean water is still entering the wall.
- Do not touch sockets, switches, chargers, extension leads, or wet surfaces near the patch.
- Do not walk through standing water to reach a switch, plug, appliance, or consumer unit.
- Do not drill, cut, prise open, or “make a hole to drain it”; you could hit wiring or a pressurised pipe.
- Do not paint over it, seal it, or spray anti-mould products while it is actively spreading.
- Do not place a portable heater tight against the wet wall while the source is unknown.

## What to do now
1. **Make a safer pause and check for immediate danger.**  
   Keep people and pets back from the damp area. If you see sparking, smell burning, hear buzzing or crackling from a socket or switch, see smoke, or someone may be at risk of electric shock, call **999**.

2. **If it is safe, isolate electricity affecting the damp area.**  
   If the damp patch is near sockets, switches, lights, appliances, or cables, switch off the affected circuit at the consumer unit. If you cannot identify the circuit confidently, use the main switch only if you can reach it safely. If the consumer unit is wet, damaged, buzzing, sparking, or would require crossing water, do not touch it; keep clear and call a qualified electrician.

3. **Reduce water use, then shut off the supply if the patch keeps growing.**  
   Stop using nearby taps, showers, toilets, radiators, boilers, washing machines, and dishwashers if they might be connected to the area. If the patch continues to expand, turn off the inside stop valve or stopcock. It is often under the kitchen sink, under stairs, in a garage, utility room, bathroom, or near where the water supply enters the home. Turn it clockwise to close if it is a screw-type tap.

4. **Do a quick non-invasive source check.**  
   Look only where you can do so safely. If the patch is under or near a bathroom, kitchen, boiler, radiator, washing machine, dishwasher, or airing cupboard, assume a plumbing or heating leak may be involved. If it is on an external wall during heavy rain or wind, rainwater may be getting in through roofing, guttering, flashing, pointing, render, or a window frame. If it appears after using a shower, bath, sink, toilet, or appliance, a waste pipe, seal, or appliance connection may be involved.

5. **Get the right help moving early.**  
   If you rent, report it urgently to your landlord, letting agent, housing association, or council repairs service, whichever applies. Say that the damp patch is expanding hour by hour and whether it is near electrics. If you own, contact an emergency plumber, or a heating engineer if the signs point strongly to a heating system leak. If electrics may have been affected, contact a qualified electrician. If you cannot isolate the incoming supply or think the problem is with a shared or external supply pipe, contact your water company’s emergency route.

6. **Limit damage without putting yourself at risk.**  
   Move furniture, valuables, rugs, electronics, and paperwork away from the wall. Lift items off the floor. Catch drips with a bucket or towels if you can do it without going near electrics. Keep wet towels, buckets, and water away from leads, plugs, appliances, and low sockets.

7. **Document the change.**  
   Take clear photos or video from a safe distance, including one wide shot showing the room and one close-up of the damp patch. Note the time, size, nearby rooms or fittings, recent water use, heavy rain, and whether the patch is still growing.

8. **Ventilate gently, but do not make drying the priority yet.**  
   Open a window if safe and practical. Do not focus on drying, decorating, or damp treatment until the water source is stopped and any affected electrics have been checked.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether it is “damp”, “condensation”, “penetrating damp”, or “a leak” right now; the rapid change is enough reason to act cautiously.
- You do not need to remove plaster, lift flooring, open walls, or start redecorating today.
- You do not need to choose a long-term damp treatment now.
- You do not need to settle insurance, liability, or blame immediately; first make the area safer, document it, and get urgent repair help moving.

## Important reassurance
A fast-spreading damp patch is unsettling, but the first steps are limited: stay away from electrical risk, reduce or stop possible water flow, record what is happening, and contact the right repair help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and prevent avoidable damage. Finding the exact cause and choosing the proper repair may need a plumber, heating engineer, roofer, builder, electrician, landlord repair team, insurer, or other specialist once the situation is safer.

## Important note
This is general information, not a professional inspection, electrical advice, plumbing advice, legal advice, or insurance advice. If you are unsure whether electrics or the structure are affected, treat the area as unsafe and get qualified help.
