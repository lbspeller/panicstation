# What to do if… a flexible supply line under a sink starts spraying or dripping rapidly (UK)

## Short answer
Shut off the water immediately at the local isolation valves under the sink or the main internal stopcock. If water is touching electrical outlets or appliances, isolate the electricity at the consumer unit before touching anything in the wet area.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t touch any metal taps, pipes, or pooled water if you see water dripping into or near electrical sockets or appliances.
- Don’t attempt to tighten the connection while it is under full pressure; this can cause a weakened flexible hose to snap completely.
- Don’t use temporary fixes like duct tape or towels to "plug" a high-pressure spray; the water will find a new path and may cause more hidden damage.
- Don’t leave the property until the water flow has completely stopped and the leak is contained.
- Don’t use a communal or external stopcock without first attempting to notify neighbors, as this will cut off their supply as well.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritise electrical safety if water is near sockets or appliances.** **If you can safely reach your consumer unit (fuse box) without standing in water or touching wet surfaces, turn off the "Main Switch" (usually a large red switch) or the specific circuit for the kitchen.** If water is actively dripping onto the consumer unit itself, do not touch it; stay away and call an emergency electrician.
2. **Close the local isolation valves located on the pipes under the sink.** **Identify the small metal valves on the copper pipes leading to the flexible hoses and turn the slotted screw or lever 90 degrees (a quarter turn) so it sits across the pipe.** If the valve has a handle, turn it clockwise until it stops; if you are unsure which pipe is leaking, close both the hot and cold valves.
3. **Shut off the main internal stopcock if the local valves fail or are missing.** **Locate your internal stopcock—usually found under the kitchen sink, in a hallway, or under the stairs—and turn it firmly clockwise until it will no longer move.** Do not use excessive force or tools to turn it, as a snapped valve will worsen the emergency.
4. **Power down your boiler or water heater if you have shut off the main water supply.** **Switch off the boiler at its fused spur (the wall switch) or at the consumer unit to prevent the system from running "dry" while the water is isolated.** This protects the heat exchanger from damage due to lack of pressure.
5. **Open the lowest cold tap in the property to drain the system.** **Turn on the kitchen or bathroom taps at the lowest level to relieve the remaining pressure in the pipes and stop the spraying at the leak site faster.** Keep a bucket or bowl under the flexible hose to catch the residual water that drains out.
6. **Contact your landlord or building manager immediately if you are in a rented property or flat.** **Under UK regulations (including Awaab’s Law for social housing), emergency hazards that could cause serious harm or structural damage must be addressed urgently, often within 24 hours.** If you live in a flat and the leak is coming from a communal area or a neighbor, notify the building management or concierge immediately.
7. **Contain the remaining water to prevent floor and cabinetry damage.** **Use towels to create barriers around the base of the units and move any items stored in the sink cupboard to a dry area.** If water has traveled under floorboards or into the ceiling below, clear the area underneath to prevent further property loss.
8. **Document the damage once the situation is stable.** **Take clear photographs of the failed flexible hose and the extent of the water spread for insurance or landlord repair records.** Do not attempt to repair the braided hose yourself; these components usually require complete replacement once they have failed or "kinked."

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause of the hose failure (e.g., corrosion, over-tightening, or manufacturing defect) right now.
- You do not need to mop the area perfectly dry until the primary water source is fully isolated and the boiler is safe.
- You do not need to decide on a permanent plumbing repair or replacement tonight; the goal is simply to keep the water off.

## Important reassurance
Flexible hose failures are common in UK plumbing and the spraying often looks more catastrophic than it is due to mains pressure. Once the isolation valve or stopcock is closed, the immediate danger of flooding is over. You have successfully stabilised the emergency.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Replacing high-pressure flexible hoses or repairing seized stopcocks requires a qualified plumber (WaterSafe approved) to ensure the installation meets UK Water Regulations.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization. If water has entered electrical fittings, do not restore power until a qualified electrician has inspected the circuit. If you cannot find or operate your stopcock, contact an emergency plumber or your local water supplier (e.g., Thames Water, United Utilities) for assistance with the external valve.
