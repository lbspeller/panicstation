# What to do if… you are asked to attend an interview as a witness but you worry you could be blamed (UK)

## Short answer
Do not attend the interview or provide a written statement until you have consulted a criminal defense solicitor. If the police read a "caution" to you, you are being treated as a suspect, and you must request immediate legal representation before answering any questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume that being labeled a "witness" provides legal protection; your status can be changed to "suspect" at any point during a conversation.
- Do not provide an immediate written statement, timeline, or digital device access under pressure or out of a desire to appear helpful.
- Do not attempt to guess details or fill gaps in your memory; verbalizing uncertainties can be interpreted as inconsistency or deception.
- Do not engage in "informal" or "off-the-record" conversations in police vehicles, corridors, or via telephone; these comments can be documented and used as evidence.
- Do not attend any police meeting alone if you feel overwhelmed, as stress may lead to accidental self-incrimination.

## What to do now
1. **Request clear clarification of your legal status in writing.** Ask the officer specifically if you are being treated as a witness or a suspect and whether the interview will be conducted "under caution." If they state it is a "Voluntary Interview" or "Caution Plus 3," you are being interviewed as a suspect and have the right to free legal advice.
2. **Decline to attend or provide a statement until you have secured legal advice.** You may state: "I am willing to cooperate with the investigation, but I will not attend an interview or provide a statement until I have spoken with a solicitor." Repeat this phrase if pressured to have a "quick chat."
3. **Contact a criminal defense solicitor immediately to arrange representation.** If the interview is scheduled at a police station, you are entitled to free, independent legal advice from a duty solicitor if you are being interviewed as a suspect. In Scotland, under the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Act 2016, you have a statutory right to a solicitor if you are a suspect attending voluntarily.
4. **Provide only your basic identifying details if required by law.** In Scotland, you are legally required to provide your name, address, date of birth, place of birth, and nationality to the police when asked; refusing to do so can lead to arrest. In England and Wales, you generally only need to provide these details if you are being summonsed or arrested.
5. **Instruct your solicitor to request "Disclosure" from the investigating officer.** This allows your solicitor to understand what evidence the police hold and why you are being questioned, which helps you avoid being blindsided by accusations during the interview.
6. **Set a formal boundary if you decide to attend as a witness.** State clearly at the start of any recording: "I am attending today as a witness; if at any point I am suspected of an offense, I require the interview to be paused immediately so I can consult a solicitor."
7. **Document the interaction in your own private records immediately afterward.** Write down the date, time, location, the names of the officers involved, whether a caution was read, and exactly what items or information they requested from you. Keep this note private for your solicitor.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you will eventually testify or provide a full statement.
- You do not need to hand over your phone, laptop, or private documents today; wait for a formal written request or a warrant.
- You do not need to "prove your innocence" or explain away someone else's accusations during the initial contact.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard and protective response to feel anxious when the police contact you, especially if you believe you are being unfairly implicated. Requesting a solicitor is a procedural right, not an admission of guilt, and it is the most effective way to ensure your account is recorded accurately and safely.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate stabilization steps for the initial invitation to a police interview. It does not cover trial procedures, court appearances, or specific defense strategies, which must be managed by a qualified legal professional.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Police procedures and legal rights differ between England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. If you are concerned about criminal liability, you must consult a qualified criminal solicitor in your specific jurisdiction.
