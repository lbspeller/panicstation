# What to do if… you get a “chargeback” notification and you think someone is trying to reverse a legitimate payment (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the notice inside your payment portal (not via email links), record the response deadline, and submit a "reason-matched" evidence pack that proves the transaction was authorized or the goods were delivered.

## Do not do these things
- Don't click links or download attachments in the notification email until you have confirmed the sender is legitimate.
- Don't issue a manual refund to the customer once a chargeback is active; doing so often results in losing the money twice (the refund plus the forced reversal).
- Don't ignore the deadline; card schemes like Visa and Mastercard treat a non-response as an automatic admission of liability.
- Don't contact the customer with aggressive language or threats, as this correspondence can be used as evidence against you in the dispute.
- Don't submit a bulk file of every document you own; payment issuers often reject "messy" evidence packs that are not mapped to the specific dispute reason code.

## What to do now
1. **Log in to your payment processor or merchant bank portal directly to verify the claim.**
   - Do not use email links; go to the official website and check the "Disputes" or "Reporting" section.
   - **Identify the specific Reason Code (e.g., Visa 10.4 or Mastercard 4837) and the deadline for "Representment."**
2. **Review the transaction for sensory "red flags" that indicate a phishing attempt or a genuine error.**
   - **Look for cues**: Check if the notification email uses a generic greeting (e.g., "Dear Merchant"), contains spelling errors, or comes from a non-official domain.
   - **Check for "Friendly Fraud"**: Look if the customer has made successful, undisputed purchases before or if the shipping address matches the billing address (AVS match).
3. **Decide whether to "Accept" or "Challenge" based on your available evidence.**
   - **Accept the dispute** if you lack proof of delivery or if the cost of the chargeback fee and time exceeds the transaction value.
   - **Challenge the dispute** if you have concrete data that directly contradicts the customer's claim.
4. **Gather evidence that is strictly relevant to the dispute reason.**
   - **For "Unauthorised" claims**: Provide the IP address used at checkout, the device ID, AVS (Address Verification) and CVV match results, and any prior history of undisputed transactions from this customer.
   - **For "Item Not Received" claims**: Provide a carrier tracking number that shows "Delivered" status to the customer’s specific postcode, or a proof-of-delivery signature.
   - **For "Not as Described" claims**: Provide a copy of the product description shown at the time of sale and any communication where the customer previously expressed satisfaction.
5. **Format your rebuttal as a concise, single-page summary.**
   - **Bold the most important facts**, such as the date of delivery or the specific IP address used.
   - Include a clear timeline of events: Order Date → Verification → Dispatch Date → Delivery Date → Dispute Date.
6. **Submit the evidence pack through the official portal and save the confirmation.**
   - Ensure all images are high-resolution and text is legible; issuers often ignore evidence they cannot read easily.
7. **Check for wider security compromises if the dispute seems suspicious.**
   - **Review your recent account logins** to ensure no unauthorized staff or external actors have accessed your payment settings.
8. **Report suspected criminal fraud to the relevant UK authorities.**
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland**: Report via the **Action Fraud (Report Fraud)** portal online or call **0300 123 2040** (Mon–Fri, 8am–8pm).
   - **Scotland**: Report by calling **Police Scotland** on **101**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to seek legal advice or a solicitor today; the immediate priority is meeting the card scheme deadline.
- You do not need to change your entire checkout process or fraud filters until this specific case is resolved.
- You do not need to initiate a debt collection process yet; wait for the final "Arbitration" or issuer decision.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a chargeback is a standard part of running a business and does not necessarily mean you have done something wrong. The system is designed to favor the cardholder initially, but a well-organized evidence pack can and does reverse legitimate payments that were disputed in error or via "friendly fraud."

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. It focuses on preventing immediate financial loss and meeting merchant deadlines. High-value disputes or high dispute ratios may require a formal audit of your merchant services agreement.

## Important note
Chargeback rights are governed by private card scheme rules (Visa, Mastercard, Amex), not by UK statutory law. This differs from Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act, which is a legal right for credit card users. Always follow the specific requirements of your payment processor.
