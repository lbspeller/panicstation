# What to do if… your employer says there is a system error with your recorded hours or attendance (UK)

## Short answer
Do not confirm exact hours until you have seen which dates and times are affected. Reply in writing that you are willing to help reconcile the record, but you need the details first.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess, estimate as fact, or sign off a blanket confirmation if you are unsure about any dates or times.
- Do not ignore the message; silence can create confusion or be treated as non-cooperation.
- Do not accuse anyone of fraud or misconduct while it may be a genuine system problem.
- Do not edit or overwrite records informally; if you submit a correction, make sure it is clearly labelled as a correction with dates, times, and a short note.
- Do not hand over more personal data than is needed, such as full location history, if a simpler work record will do.
- Do not rely on informal chats only; keep a written trail.

## What to do now
1. **Get clarity in writing before you confirm anything.** Reply asking for:
   - the exact date range affected
   - what system failed, such as the clock-in app, swipe access, rota, or payroll system
   - what they mean by “confirm attendance”: whether they mean you were scheduled, present, working specific hours, or took specific breaks
   - the deadline for replying
   - who is handling corrections, such as your manager, HR, or payroll

2. **Ask what they currently have on file.** Ask to see or be sent the relevant entries, screenshot, export, or written summary they are relying on, including:
   - the hours currently recorded for you
   - any missing clock-ins, missing clock-outs, exceptions, or manual changes they think apply

3. **Rebuild your own timeline using records you already have.** For the affected days, check:
   - rota or schedule screenshots
   - shift confirmations
   - calendar entries
   - work emails or messages sent during the shift
   - Teams, Slack, ticket, call, or job-sheet timestamps
   - travel, parking, or access records, only if they are helpful and proportionate

4. **Write a simple reconciliation list.** For each affected day, note:
   - the start and finish time you believe you worked
   - any break you remember
   - what evidence supports it
   - anything you are not sure about, marked clearly as “uncertain”

5. **Confirm only what you can stand behind.** Use wording like: “I can confirm I attended on [dates]. For [date], I believe I worked approximately [times] with [break]. I cannot confirm exact times for [date] without checking the underlying records. Please cross-check this against rota, access logs, and manager sign-off.”

6. **If pay might be affected, say that clearly and ask for the correction route.** Ask payroll or HR:
   - whether the error affects this pay period
   - when corrections will be made
   - whether any correction will be paid in the current pay run or as an adjustment
   - what written confirmation you will receive once the time record is corrected

7. **Check the payslip or pay breakdown if your pay changes.** If your pay varies by hours worked, ask for the payslip or written pay breakdown to show the hours being paid so you can compare it with your own reconciliation.

8. **If you are pressured to sign something you do not agree with, slow it down.**
   - Ask for the request in writing and time to review it.
   - Write “I cannot confirm this as accurate yet” if you need to respond but the record is still unclear.
   - If you are invited to a formal disciplinary hearing, you usually have the right to ask to bring a companion, such as a workplace colleague or trade union representative.
   - If you are invited to a formal grievance meeting about an employer’s legal or contractual duty, you may also have a right to be accompanied; ask in writing and check the workplace procedure.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to raise a formal grievance.
- You do not need to prove every minute today; start with your best factual reconstruction and ask them to show the records they are relying on.
- You do not need to make big job decisions, such as resigning, while the facts are still unclear.
- You do not need to accuse anyone or explain why the system failed.

## Important reassurance
System, rota, clocking, and payroll errors can happen, and employers may ask staff to help reconcile records. Staying factual, written, and careful about what you confirm helps protect your pay record while showing that you are cooperating.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation, protect your record, and buy time. If this turns into a pay dispute, grievance, disciplinary issue, or repeated underpayment, later decisions may need specialist help based on your contract, workplace policy, and exact facts.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, employment, or professional advice. Workplace policies, contracts, pay arrangements, and the reason for the attendance confirmation request can affect what is appropriate.
