# What to do if… you learn essential services are in the name of a person who died and shutoff notices may be coming (UK)

## Short answer
Contact each utility provider immediately to report the death and request an "account hold" while the responsibility is transferred to the occupier or executor. Use the word "bereavement" to reach specialized teams who can pause automated disconnection and debt collection processes.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore physical mail or "red letters" even if they are addressed to the person who died.
- Don't cancel existing Direct Debits immediately if there are funds in the account, as this can trigger automated "failed payment" disconnection protocols.
- Don't assume that government services like "Tell Us Once" will automatically notify private utility companies or heat networks.
- Don't provide a personal guarantee to pay the deceased person’s debts over the phone unless you were a joint account holder or have legal advice to do so.
- Don't attempt to "test" if the supply is still live by turning on high-load appliances if the property has been empty for some time.

## What to do now
1. **Identify every essential energy and water provider for the property.**
   - Locate the most recent physical or digital bills for gas, electricity, and water.
   - Determine if the property is on a heat network (common in new builds / flats), which as of 2026 is regulated by Ofgem in the same way as gas and electricity.
   - Check the meter cupboard for the supplier's name or a serial number if no bills are available.

2. **Capture the current state of all meters immediately.**
   - **Take clear photographs of the gas, electricity, and water meters** showing the current reading and the serial number.
   - If the property has a prepayment meter, photograph any "Emergency Credit" or "Debt" screens.

3. **Call the dedicated bereavement team for each supplier.**
   - **Tell the agent: "The account holder has died and I am reporting this to prevent any automated disconnection of service."**
   - Provide the date of death and the name of the person now living at the property, or state clearly if the property is currently unoccupied.
   - Ask for a "breavement pause" or "suspension of recovery action" on any outstanding balances while the estate is settled.
   - Request that the account be moved into the name of the "Executors of [Deceased Name]" if the property is empty, or the name of the new occupier.

4. **Register the household for the Priority Services Register (PSR).**
   - **Ask the supplier: "Please add this property to the Priority Services Register due to temporary vulnerability following a bereavement."**
   - This provides extra protection against disconnection and ensures priority support if there is a power or water outage.
   - Mention if anyone living in the house is over 65, has a disability, or relies on medical equipment.

5. **Contact Citizens Advice if you face immediate disconnection threats.**
   - **Call the Citizens Advice consumer helpline on 0808 223 1133 if a supplier refuses to pause a shutoff.**
   - Inform them that the account holder has died and you are being threatened with disconnection, which may qualify for a referral to the Extra Help Unit.

6. **Notify the water company separately as they operate under different regional rules.**
   - **Ask the water supplier for their specific "bereavement grace period,"** which often provides up to 12 weeks of paused billing or protection while names are changed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a Grant of Probate or Letters of Administration during the initial "stop shutoff" call.
- You do not need to settle the final bill of the deceased person from your own funds today.
- You do not need to choose a new energy tariff or provider until the account is stabilized in a living person's name.
- You do not need to clear previous arrears before the supplier agrees to keep the supply running for a new occupier.

## Important reassurance
Utility companies in the UK have mandatory internal policies for handling bereavement. Disconnection for a reported death is extremely rare and usually only occurs when the supplier is unaware the account holder has died. Once you make contact, the automated system is typically overridden by a human agent in a specialist team.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization of essential utility supplies (gas, electricity, water, and heat networks) following a death. It does not cover the long-term distribution of estate assets, tax implications of inherited debt, or the cancellation of non-essential contracts like streaming services.

## Important note
This information is based on UK utility regulations as of early 2026. Rights regarding disconnection can vary if the supply is commercial rather than domestic. If you are a tenant and the bills were in the landlord's name, different "change of tenancy" rules may apply.
