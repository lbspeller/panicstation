# What to do if… you are locked out of your home in urgent weather or at night (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself and anyone with you into warmth, light, and safety first, then call the quickest legitimate keyholder or a reputable locksmith. If someone may have hypothermia, someone is trapped in immediate danger, or you feel under immediate threat, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay outside in cold, wet, dark, or isolated conditions just to watch the door.
- Do not force a door or window, climb, or break glass unless emergency services tell you to; it can injure you, damage the property, or be mistaken for a break-in.
- Do not keep trying a stuck key hard; forcing it can snap it or worsen the lock problem.
- Do not call a locksmith from a random top advert without checking who they are and asking the likely total cost.
- Do not agree to drilling or replacing the whole lock as the first assumption; ask whether non-destructive entry will be tried first.
- Do not hand over cash or approve card payment until the price has been explained, including call-out, labour, parts, and VAT if VAT applies.
- Do not argue alone with an intimidating trader at your door; move away if you can and call for help.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause first.**
   - Knock on a trusted neighbour, go to a nearby open shop, petrol station, hotel lobby, transport hub, or other staffed place.
   - If you are with children, an older person, a disabled person, or anyone soaked, shivering, confused, very tired, or slurring words, prioritise shelter over watching the door.
   - If you think you or someone with you may have hypothermia, call 999 or go to A&E; do not drive yourself to A&E.

2. **If someone or an animal is locked inside, judge immediate danger.**
   - If a child, vulnerable person, pan on the hob, fire risk, medical risk, or another urgent hazard means someone could be harmed soon, call 999.
   - If a pet is locked inside and may be in distress, tell the keyholder, landlord, managing agent, or locksmith that there is an animal inside so they understand the urgency.
   - Stay nearby but safe, keep your phone available, and follow emergency service instructions if emergency services are involved.
   - Do not break in yourself unless there is no safer option and emergency services direct you.

3. **Do one quick non-damaging check.**
   - Confirm you are at the right entrance.
   - Check whether there is another authorised way in, such as a back door, communal entrance, concierge, or building manager.
   - If you have a key, try it gently once. If it will not turn, stop.

4. **Call the fastest legitimate keyholder.**
   - Call someone nearby who has a spare key.
   - If you rent, call your landlord or letting agent emergency or out-of-hours number.
   - If you live in a managed block, call the managing agent, concierge, caretaker, or building emergency contact.
   - Keep messages short: say you are locked out, where you are sheltered, whether anyone is inside, and whether cold or darkness makes it urgent.

5. **Protect your phone and ability to pay.**
   - Turn on battery saver if your phone is low.
   - Send your location to one trusted person.
   - Keep enough battery for calls from the landlord, managing agent, locksmith, or emergency services.

6. **If you need a locksmith, slow the choice down by one minute.**
   - Prefer a locksmith from a checked directory such as the Master Locksmiths Association, a known local business with a real local address, or a recommendation from someone you trust.
   - Ask before they attend: “What is the likely total price range tonight, including call-out, labour, parts, and VAT if VAT applies?”
   - Ask: “Will you try non-destructive entry first?”
   - Ask: “What would make the price increase?”
   - If they will not give a clear range, use pressure, or say drilling is definitely needed before seeing the lock, end the call and try another locksmith.

7. **Be ready to show you are allowed in.**
   - Have photo ID ready if you have it.
   - If your ID is inside, be ready to show another sign you live there after entry, such as a tenancy email, council tax or utility account on your phone, banking app address details, or confirmation from a neighbour.
   - Do not send unnecessary personal documents to a stranger; show only what is needed.

8. **If the trader at the door feels wrong, pause the job.**
   - Ask for the price again before work starts.
   - If the price changes sharply, drilling is pushed immediately, or you feel intimidated, say you are not going ahead and move back to a staffed or safe place.
   - If there is an immediate threat to your safety, call 999. If it is not an emergency but you need police help, call 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to upgrade locks, fit a key safe, change your whole security setup, or make a spare-key plan.
- You do not need to make an insurance claim while you are still cold, outside, or dealing with entry.
- You do not need to argue about refunds or complaints at the doorstep; that can wait until you are safe and inside.
- You do not need to solve tomorrow’s inconvenience now. The immediate task is warmth, safety, and authorised entry.

## Important reassurance
Being locked out at night or in bad weather can make a simple problem feel frightening. Getting warm and making a few careful calls usually gives you more options and lowers the chance of injury, damage, or being pressured into an expensive decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the urgent lockout moment. Later decisions about lock repairs, landlord responsibilities, spare keys, complaints, insurance, or security changes may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If anyone may have hypothermia, call 999 or go to A&E and do not drive yourself; while waiting, move indoors or somewhere sheltered, remove wet clothing if possible, keep warm with dry layers, avoid alcohol, and do not use a hot bath to warm up.
