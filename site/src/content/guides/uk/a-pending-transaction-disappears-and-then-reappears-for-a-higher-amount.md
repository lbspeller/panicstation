# What to do if… a pending transaction disappears and then reappears for a higher amount (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the payment as unfinished until you know whether it is pending or completed. Save proof, compare it with what you agreed to pay, then contact the merchant or your card provider if the higher amount is wrong or unrecognised.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume you have been charged twice just because a pending item disappeared and returned.
- Don’t spend money that seemed to come back after a pending card payment vanished.
- Don’t ignore a higher amount if it is more than you agreed to pay.
- Don’t cancel the card before speaking to your provider unless you think the card is being misused or your app tells you to.
- Don’t delete receipts, booking confirmations, emails, screenshots, or messages about the purchase.
- Don’t wait for the merchant to fix it silently if the completed amount is wrong.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether the payment is pending or completed.**  
   In your banking app or online banking, look for words such as pending, authorised, completed, or posted. Screenshot the merchant name, amount, date, time, and any reference or transaction details.

2. **Compare the higher amount with what you agreed to pay.**  
   Check the receipt, booking confirmation, order page, bill, email, or message from the merchant. Look especially for service charges, tips, deposits, incidentals, delivery charges, currency conversion, or a corrected final amount.

3. **Look for a normal card-hold explanation, but do not excuse a wrong amount.**  
   A different final amount can happen with hotel or car-hire deposits, pay-at-pump petrol, tabs in bars or restaurants, foreign currency payments, transport, parking, tolls, market stalls, and some in-flight purchases. If the amount still does not match what you authorised, treat it as a billing problem.

4. **Ask the merchant what amount they captured or settled.**  
   If you recognise the merchant, contact them and ask for an itemised receipt. Use clear wording such as: “My pending card payment disappeared and later reappeared for a higher amount. What amount have you captured or settled?” Ask them to void, correct, or refund the difference if it is wrong.

5. **Write down the merchant’s answer.**  
   Save any chat transcript, email, receipt, refund confirmation, or name of the person you spoke to. Note the date and time. This helps if you need your card provider to step in.

6. **Contact your card provider if the amount is wrong, unrecognised, or not fixed.**  
   Use your bank or card provider’s in-app dispute route, secure message, or the number on the back of the card. Say the transaction disappeared, then reappeared for a higher amount, and the completed or pending amount does not match what you authorised.

7. **Ask which card route fits the payment.**  
   For debit cards and many credit-card disputes, chargeback may let your card provider ask for a refund under card-scheme rules. It is not a guaranteed refund and time limits vary, so start promptly. For credit-card purchases, Section 75 may apply where the item or set of items cost more than £100 and up to £30,000 and there is a breach of contract or misrepresentation. Ask your provider: “Should this be handled as chargeback, Section 75, or a disputed transaction?”

8. **Reduce knock-on damage while it is unresolved.**  
   If the higher amount could push you into overdraft or make bills bounce, avoid tight-margin payments for the moment if you can. Keep enough money aside for essential bills and keep checking for a refund, correction, duplicate charge, or extra charge.

9. **Use fraud steps if the transaction is not yours.**  
   Freeze the card in your banking app if available, contact your card provider, and check for other unfamiliar activity. Follow your bank’s fraud reporting process rather than treating it as a normal merchant dispute.

10. **Escalate if your provider does not resolve it.**  
   Make a formal complaint to the bank or card provider. You can usually take it to the Financial Ombudsman Service after a final response you disagree with, or after 8 weeks for most financial complaints.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to close the account or switch banks.
- You do not need to write a long complaint unless the merchant and card-provider route stalls.
- You do not need to report it to the police unless you believe it is fraud, identity misuse, or another crime.
- You do not need to understand every card-scheme rule before asking your provider to dispute the payment.

## Important reassurance
Pending card payments can disappear, change, or reappear when the merchant settles the final amount. That does not mean you have done anything wrong, and saving proof early gives you a clearer route to challenge a higher amount.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on the merchant’s evidence, the card provider’s rules, whether the payment was debit or credit, and whether fraud is suspected.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Card-provider processes, chargeback rules, and complaint outcomes can vary, so check with your own bank or card provider.
