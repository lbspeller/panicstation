# What to do if… you receive a legal hold or preservation notice to keep records for a case (UK)

## Short answer
Stop deleting, editing, resetting, or tidying anything that may be connected to the notice. Preserve the material as it is, note what you have done to stop deletion, and send the notice to the named contact, your organisation’s legal/compliance team, or a solicitor as soon as you can.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t delete, edit, “tidy up”, rename, crop, selectively remove, or rewrite messages, photos, emails, files, call logs, notes, documents, or social media DMs that may be connected to the matter.
- Don’t factory-reset, wipe, “clean” storage, replace your phone, or move to a new device in a way that could remove or overwrite data.
- Don’t turn on disappearing messages, auto-delete, or bulk clean-up tools for anything that may be relevant.
- Don’t keep only screenshots while deleting the originals; screenshots can lose context and technical details.
- Don’t create a new version and treat it as the original.
- Don’t ask anyone else to delete, edit, clean up, or “make it look better” for you.
- Don’t send material to the other side, the police, an employer, or anyone else unless the notice, a named official contact, or your legal adviser tells you how to do that.

## What to do now
1. **Freeze deletion now.**
   - Stop deleting anything that may be connected to the people, dates, events, places, accounts, devices, or documents mentioned in the notice.
   - Include messages, photos, videos, emails, DMs, call logs, notes, cloud files, shared drives, downloads, backups, and work-system records.

2. **Stop automatic loss.**
   - Turn off disappearing messages, auto-delete, bulk clean-up apps, and automatic “free up space” tools where they may affect relevant material.
   - Make a short note of what setting you changed and when.
   - If changing a setting could itself delete or sync changes across devices, stop and ask the named contact, your organisation’s legal/compliance team, or a solicitor before changing it.

3. **Do not reset, upgrade, migrate, or reorganise devices yet.**
   - Do not wipe a phone, factory-reset a laptop, replace a device, clear a cloud account, or run a large file clean-up.
   - Do not move everything into new folders or export only selected items.
   - Keep the device and account in ordinary use as far as you safely can, but avoid actions that delete or overwrite relevant material.

4. **Read only enough of the notice to identify the preservation scope.**
   - Look for the date range, names, organisations, case reference, subject matter, and types of records mentioned.
   - If the scope is unclear, preserve anything that may reasonably fall within it until you have clarification.
   - Do not spend hours searching, sorting, or deciding what is “good” or “bad” evidence.

5. **Make a short preservation note.**
   - Write down the date and time you received the notice.
   - Write down the steps you took to stop deletion.
   - List the devices and accounts that may contain relevant material, such as your phone, email, WhatsApp, Signal, Telegram, Instagram, Facebook Messenger, cloud photos, work email, Teams, Slack, shared drives, or backups.
   - Keep this factual. Do not add arguments, guesses, or explanations beyond what happened.

6. **Send the notice to the right person.**
   - If this came through work or an organisation, send it to the named contact or the legal, compliance, HR, data protection, or records team and ask what to do about work systems and retention settings.
   - If this is personal, contact a solicitor or use a legal-advice route for your part of the UK and ask what must be preserved and how.
   - If the notice names a sender or reference number, keep that information with your preservation note.

7. **If police or criminal proceedings are involved, keep your response preservation-only.**
   - Ask for the named officer or contact route, the case or reference number, and any written preservation instructions.
   - Tell them you have stopped deletion.
   - Do not investigate, curate, edit, or try to explain the material yourself before getting advice.

8. **Warn other people who could accidentally delete material.**
   - If you share a phone, tablet, computer, family account, work account, cloud storage, or login, send a short neutral message such as: “I have received a notice to preserve records for a case. Please do not delete, edit, or clean up anything that may be related.”
   - Do not discuss the facts of the case in that message unless you have been told to.

9. **If something may already have been deleted, do not try to cover the gap.**
   - Do not recreate, backdate, alter, or hide anything.
   - Write down what you think happened, when it happened, and whether it may have been automatic deletion, a sync change, a device change, or a manual deletion.
   - Ask the named contact, your organisation’s legal/compliance team, or a solicitor what to do next.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, settle, plead, resign, report someone, or give a detailed account.
- You do not need to organise everything into folders, make a timeline, or search every device immediately.
- You do not need to decide what is helpful or harmful.
- You do not need to contact the opposing side directly unless the notice clearly requires it or you have been advised to do so.
- You do not need to hand anything over until you understand the request, the deadline, and the proper route.

## Important reassurance
A notice like this can feel frightening or accusatory, but the first safe response is usually simple: stop deletion, keep things intact, and get clear instructions. Preserving material is not the same as admitting fault or agreeing to hand everything over immediately.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to prevent loss, alteration, or accidental deletion of records. Later decisions about collection, review, disclosure, privilege, privacy, employment systems, criminal procedure, or handing material over may need specialist help and may depend on the type of case and where in the UK it is being handled.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, criminal defence, employment, data protection, technical, forensic, or professional advice. If the notice has a deadline, if police or court proceedings are involved, if the material is on work systems, or if you think something has already been deleted, get legal or specialist advice promptly.
