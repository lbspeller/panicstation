# What to do if… your account security questions or recovery details were changed without your permission (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible account takeover. Secure your email first, then use the affected account provider’s official recovery route to regain control and undo security-setting changes.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep trying to log in from lots of devices; repeated attempts can make recovery harder.
- Don’t click password-reset links or security-alert links in unexpected messages; go directly to the provider’s official website or app.
- Don’t reuse an old password just to get back in quickly.
- Don’t share one-time codes, backup codes, passwords, or security-question answers with anyone.
- Don’t assume it is only one account if your email account may also be affected.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer setup before you recover anything.** Use a device you trust, on a connection you trust, and type the account provider’s address yourself or open its official app.

2. **Secure your email account first.** Your email can often be used to reset other accounts.
   - Sign in through the official email website or app.
   - Change the email password to a strong, unique password.
   - Turn on 2-step verification, also called 2FA or MFA, if available.
   - Check for forwarding, filters, rules, auto-delete, or archiving settings you did not create, and remove them.
   - Check for delegated mailbox access, connected apps, app passwords, or trusted devices you do not recognise, and revoke them where possible.
   - Review recent sign-ins or security activity, and sign out of other sessions if the provider offers that option.

3. **Use the affected account’s official recovery process.**
   - Go to the provider’s help or recovery page from its official website.
   - Follow the recovery checks.
   - Look specifically for changes to security questions, recovery email, recovery phone, trusted devices, or 2-step verification.

4. **When you get back in, lock the affected account down.**
   - Change the password to a long, unique one.
   - Sign out of other devices or sessions if that option is available.
   - Re-enable 2-step verification if it was turned off.
   - Correct the recovery email and phone number.
   - Replace security-question answers with answers that are hard to guess, where the provider allows this, and store the exact answers safely.

5. **Check whether your phone number may be involved.**
   - If your phone suddenly lost signal, you received a SIM-change message, or text-message codes stopped arriving, contact your mobile provider using its official website, app, or customer-service number.
   - Ask the provider to check for unauthorised SIM changes and to secure the mobile account.
   - After the urgent recovery is done, use an authenticator app or security key instead of text-message codes where the account provider supports it.

6. **Triage the accounts most likely to be affected next.**
   - Check banking and payment accounts first.
   - Check your main email, Apple, Google, Microsoft, social media, and shopping accounts.
   - Change passwords anywhere you reused the same password.
   - Turn on 2-step verification on important accounts that do not already have it.

7. **If money, purchases, or fraud is involved, contact the right place quickly.**
   - Contact your bank or card provider using the number on your card, its official app, or its official website.
   - If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report cyber crime or fraud through Report Fraud.
   - If you live in Scotland or the crime happened there, contact Police Scotland on 101 for non-emergencies.
   - If a crime is happening now, use 999 for emergencies or 101 for non-emergencies.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly how it happened right now.
- You do not need to delete all your accounts.
- You do not need to wipe devices as a first move unless a trusted support provider tells you to.
- You do not need to confront anyone you suspect.
- Longer-term upgrades, such as changing password managers or creating a new email address, can wait until access is secure.

## Important reassurance
This is a common account-takeover pattern, and it does not mean you have failed. Work from the account that can reset the others, usually your email, then close the obvious routes back in.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise access and reduce further damage. Later decisions, such as device checks, identity-fraud protection, or specialist technical help, may need support from the account provider, your bank, your mobile provider, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, technical-forensics, or professional advice. If there is immediate danger, a crime happening now, or significant fraud in progress, contact the relevant emergency service, bank, account provider, or reporting service.
