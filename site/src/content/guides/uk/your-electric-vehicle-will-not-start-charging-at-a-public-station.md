# What to do if… your electric vehicle will not start charging at a public station (UK)

## Short answer
Make the situation safe first, then do one clean restart of the charging session: stop the session, unplug, wait a short moment, plug in firmly, and start again. If it still will not start, call the charge point operator’s helpline shown on the unit and ask them to check, remote-start, reset, or direct you to another working bay.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t force the connector, slam it, twist it hard, or put anything into the vehicle charge port.
- Don’t keep retrying rapidly; repeated failed starts can confuse the session, the app, or the charger.
- Don’t touch exposed metal, use visibly damaged equipment, or continue if you see sparking, smoke, melted plastic, unusual heat, or smell burning.
- Don’t stay in a traffic lane, isolated corner, or other unsafe place just to keep trying.
- Don’t assume the vehicle is broken until you have tried one controlled change, such as another bay, socket, connector type, or nearby charger.
- Don’t start a second paid session until you have checked that the first attempt has stopped, failed, or been released.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If the bay is exposed to moving traffic, poorly lit, or making you feel unsafe, move to the safest nearby parking space before troubleshooting.
2. **Check for immediate electrical danger.** If there is smoke, sparking, crackling, strong heat, melted plastic, fire, or immediate danger, step back, keep others away, and call **999**. If there is damage to electricity network equipment, overhead lines, substations, or street electrical equipment, call **105** in England, Wales or Scotland; in Northern Ireland, call **03457 643 643** for NIE Networks.
3. **Do one clean charging restart.**
   - Stop or end the session in the app, on the charger screen, or with the stop button if there is one.
   - Unplug from the vehicle if it is safe to do so.
   - Wait a short moment.
   - Plug in again firmly until the connector seats properly.
   - Start the session again only once the charger or app shows it is ready.
4. **Check the vehicle-side blockers.**
   - Put the vehicle in Park and switch off any drive-ready mode if your vehicle requires that.
   - Unlock the vehicle and try again, because some vehicles lock or release the charge port through the central locking.
   - Look at the vehicle screen or app for a charge schedule, departure-time setting, charge limit already reached, battery temperature warning, port lock message, or charging error code.
   - If the vehicle has a “restart charging”, “release cable”, or charge-port reset option, use it once.
5. **Check the charger and payment-side blockers.**
   - Confirm that the charger has selected the connector you are actually using.
   - Check that the screen or app shows the session has started, not just that the charger is available.
   - If the app, RFID card, contactless payment, or QR code appears to have failed, wait until the previous attempt clearly stops or fails before trying one alternative start method.
   - Check whether the charger is asking for payment details, app login, cable release, or confirmation on the vehicle screen.
6. **Try one controlled swap to isolate the fault.**
   - Move to a different bay or socket at the same site if one is available.
   - If your vehicle supports it and you have enough range, try a different charger type nearby, such as AC instead of rapid DC, or rapid DC instead of AC.
   - If another driver is charging successfully on the same unit, ask briefly whether their connector started normally; do not block them or rely on them to fix it.
7. **Call the charge point operator helpline shown on the charger.** Most public charge points run by larger operators should display a free, staffed helpline. Tell them the site name, charger ID, bay number, connector type, vehicle model, payment method used, screen message, and any error code. Ask whether they can remote-start, remote-reset, release the connector, confirm a fault, or direct you to the nearest working unit on the same network.
8. **Protect range while you decide.**
   - Turn off heated screens, seat heaters, and other heavy loads you do not need.
   - Keep cabin heating or cooling modest if it is safe and comfortable to do so.
   - Avoid moving between chargers unless you have enough range for the journey and a fallback plan.
9. **If you may be stranded, arrange help.** Call your breakdown provider, lease provider, vehicle roadside assistance, or insurer’s breakdown line and say: “My electric vehicle will not start a charge at a public charger, I have tried another bay or start method, and my remaining range is low.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the vehicle, buy a cable, book a service, or decide whether the car has a fault right now.
- You do not need to argue with other drivers or prove whether the charger is broken.
- You can check receipts, pending card payments, app logs, refund requests, and formal fault reports later, once you have charge or recovery arranged.
- You can decide later whether the vehicle needs inspection if the same problem happens across more than one charging network or connector type.

## Important reassurance
A failed public charging start is often a session, connector, payment, software, bay, or network issue rather than a serious vehicle fault. A calm restart, one controlled swap, and an early call to the operator’s helpline often gives you the quickest route to either a working charge or a safe recovery plan.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about refunds, repeated charger faults, vehicle repair, battery warnings, or complaints may need help from the charge point operator, vehicle manufacturer, breakdown provider, insurer, or a qualified technician.

## Important note
This guide is general information for immediate first steps, not professional electrical, mechanical, legal, financial, or safety advice. If there are signs of electrical danger, fire, smoke, sparking, burning smell, unusual heat, or damaged equipment, prioritise distance and emergency help over troubleshooting.
