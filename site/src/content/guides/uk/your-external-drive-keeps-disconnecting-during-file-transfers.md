# What to do if… your external drive keeps disconnecting during file transfers (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the transfer and stop writing to the drive until the connection is steadier. Repeated disconnects during writes may leave files incomplete and can sometimes damage the drive’s file system.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep retrying the same large transfer again and again while the drive is still dropping out.
- Don’t pull the cable straight away if the copy freezes; cancel the transfer first if the computer still responds.
- Don’t reformat, erase, or “initialise” the drive to fix it unless you are sure you do not need anything on it.
- Don’t run several transfers at once while the drive is unstable.
- Don’t use an unpowered USB hub or loose adapter if you can connect the drive directly.
- Don’t run repair tools repeatedly on a drive that is clicking, overheating, or vanishing from the computer.

## What to do now
1. **Stop new writes.**  
   Cancel or pause the transfer if you can. If the computer is frozen, wait a minute before touching the cable. The immediate aim is to avoid adding more half-written files.

2. **Make the connection as simple as possible.**  
   Plug the drive directly into the computer, not through a hub, dock, monitor, or adapter. Try another USB port. If the drive has its own power supply, plug it in. If it is powered only by USB and keeps dropping out, use a powered hub only if direct connection is not stable.

3. **Change the cable.**  
   Use a short, good-quality cable that fits firmly at both ends. A slightly loose or damaged cable can cause repeated disconnects during copying.

4. **Stop sleep or power saving interrupting the copy.**  
   On Mac, temporarily prevent automatic sleep while the copy is running; the exact setting name can vary by Mac. On Windows, keep the laptop plugged in and check the power settings for USB power saving if the drive drops out when the computer idles. You can put these settings back later.

5. **Check whether the drive shows warning signs.**  
   If it is clicking, grinding, getting unusually hot, or disappearing within seconds of reconnecting, stop using it as much as possible and focus on getting specialist data-recovery help if the files matter.

6. **Copy the most important files first.**  
   If the drive stays connected, copy one small test folder first. Then copy the most important folders in smaller batches, such as one project, month, or folder at a time, instead of one huge transfer.

7. **Only run checks after the drive is stable.**  
   If it remains connected and there are no physical warning signs, you can use the built-in disk check: Disk Utility First Aid on Mac, or Windows drive error checking. If a tool reports serious errors, stop repeated repair attempts and prioritise copying what matters.

8. **Keep simple notes if the drive is new or recently bought.**  
   Save the receipt or order confirmation. Note when it disconnects, which cable and port you used, and any exact error message. This helps if you need to contact the retailer or manufacturer.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether the drive is permanently dead.
- You do not need to reorganise, rename, or deduplicate files during the first safe copy.
- You do not need to buy a replacement immediately if a different cable, port, power supply, or sleep setting makes the transfer stable.
- You do not need advanced diagnostics before you have tried the basic cable, port, power, and small-batch copy steps.

## Important reassurance
This pattern is common, and it is often caused by a cable, port, hub, power, or sleep setting rather than by total drive failure. Slowing down and avoiding repeated write attempts gives you a better chance of getting a clean copy.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the connection and reduce the risk of incomplete or corrupted copies. Later decisions, such as repair, replacement, warranty routes, or professional data recovery, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not professional IT, legal, financial, or data-recovery advice. If the data is irreplaceable and the drive shows physical warning signs, minimise use and consider specialist help.
