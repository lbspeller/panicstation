# What to do if… you suddenly feel unusually impaired and suspect something is wrong (UK)

## Short answer
Stop what you are doing, stop consuming anything more, and get to a safer place with a trusted person or staff. Call 999 now if you are getting worse, very drowsy, confused, having chest pain, having a seizure, vomiting repeatedly, or struggling to breathe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive, cycle, use an e-scooter, or try to “push through” the journey home.
- Do not leave alone with someone you do not know or do not fully trust.
- Do not keep drinking alcohol, taking drugs, or taking extra medication to “level out”.
- Do not continue a drink or food you suspect may be involved.
- Do not try to make yourself vomit.
- Do not try to flush anything out by drinking lots of water.
- Do not assume it is “just anxiety” if the impairment is sudden, unusual for you, or getting worse.
- If you suspect carbon monoxide, fumes, or a gas emergency, do not stay inside to investigate.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer place immediately.**  
   Move to a staffed, public, well-lit place such as bar staff, venue security, reception, a shop, or a transport staff point. Sit down if you feel unsteady.

2. **Tell one safe person exactly what is happening.**  
   Say: “I suddenly feel unusually impaired and I need you to stay with me.” Ask them not to leave you alone until you are medically checked or safely with someone you trust.

3. **Stop intake and move away from the possible source.**  
   Stop drinking or eating anything you are currently consuming. If you still have the drink, food, cup, bottle, or packaging, put it aside and do not keep using it.

4. **Call 999 now if there are emergency symptoms.**  
   Call 999 if you may pass out, cannot stay awake, are suddenly confused, are severely unsteady, have chest pain, have a seizure, are struggling to breathe, cannot stop vomiting, or symptoms are rapidly worsening. Say: “I suddenly feel unusually impaired and I am worried I may have been spiked, poisoned, or exposed to something harmful.”

5. **If it is not a 999 emergency, still get urgent medical advice.**  
   Use NHS 111 if available where you are, or local urgent healthcare advice. Go to A&E or call 999 if symptoms become severe, you are alone and unsafe, or you cannot get urgent advice quickly.

6. **If symptoms started indoors or others feel unwell, treat carbon monoxide as possible.**  
   Carbon monoxide cannot be seen or smelt. If symptoms started in a room, vehicle, tent, garage, or building, or more than one person feels unwell, go outside into fresh air and stay out. Do not go back in until you have had advice. Call 999 if anyone is confused, weak, has chest pain, has trouble breathing, or loses consciousness.

7. **If you smell gas or suspect a gas or carbon monoxide emergency, leave and call the gas emergency number.**  
   Do not smoke, light matches, or operate electrical switches. Leave first, then call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999. Do not re-enter until you are told it is safe.

8. **If you are in a pub, club, event, or venue, ask staff or security for help.**  
   Ask for a safe place to sit, help contacting a trusted person, and medical help if needed. If you feel able, tell them if you suspect spiking or noticed suspicious behaviour.

9. **Ask a trusted person to handle simple logistics.**  
   Ask them to arrange safe transport, keep your phone, keys, and cards together, and stay with you until help arrives or you are safely with someone you trust.

10. **Make a short note only if you can do it easily.**  
   Write down where you are, when symptoms started, what you consumed, and any medication or substances you took today. This can help medical staff and may help later if you choose to report.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report to the police.
- You do not need to prove what happened before getting medical help.
- You do not need to work out whether it was spiking, carbon monoxide, medication, alcohol, illness, or anxiety before asking for help.
- You can deal with messages, work, payments, lost items, or replacing cards after you are safe and medically checked.

## Important reassurance
Feeling suddenly impaired can be frightening, and it is reasonable to treat it as a safety issue even if you are not sure what caused it. Getting help early is not overreacting; it protects you while your judgement, balance, or memory may be affected.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the moment and reduce immediate risk. Later decisions, including medical follow-up, reporting, testing, or practical support, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel very unwell, symptoms are getting worse, or you are not safe where you are, call 999. If you think someone may have intentionally drugged you, you can report it to the police by calling 999 in an emergency or 101 when it is not an emergency, but your immediate priority is safety and medical help.
