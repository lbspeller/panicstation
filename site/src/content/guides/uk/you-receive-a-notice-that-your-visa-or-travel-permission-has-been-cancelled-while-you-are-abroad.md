# What to do if… you receive a notice that your visa or travel permission has been cancelled while you are abroad (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the notice as urgent, verify it through the issuing country’s official immigration channel, and get your current status and any deadline in writing. Until you have written clarification, act cautiously as if the notice may be real.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore the notice or assume it only affects your next trip; it may affect your current stay.
- Don’t assume it is a scam just because it arrived suddenly; verify it before dismissing it.
- Don’t click payment links, reinstatement links, or upload passport scans through an unverified message.
- Don’t argue at an airport or border checkpoint; calmly ask for the reason and next steps in writing.
- Don’t hand your passport to an unofficial person. If an authority says it must hold your passport, ask for a receipt or written record where it is safe to do so.
- Don’t pay an agent, fixer, or online service before checking whether they are legitimate and whether the issuing authority recognises them.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a steady moment and read the notice carefully.** Note the issuing authority, date and time, your name, passport number, reference number, and any instruction to report, leave, attend an appointment, or stop travelling.
2. **Verify it through official channels, not through the message link.** Type the issuing authority’s website into your browser yourself, use an official visa or immigration portal, or call a number listed on that government’s own website.
3. **Check whether it affects your current stay, future travel, or boarding.** A visa, eVisa, ETA, entry permission, or travel authorisation can mean different things in different countries. Ask the issuing authority to confirm your current lawful status and any deadline.
4. **Ask the local immigration authority for written answers to two questions:**
   - “What is my lawful status right now, today?”
   - “What do I have to do next, and by when?”
   Ask for a reference number or case ID and keep a note of who you spoke to.
5. **If you are due to travel soon, contact your airline before going to the airport.** Ask what their system shows and what they need for boarding, such as updated authorisation in the carrier system or written confirmation from the immigration authority.
6. **Make a small evidence pack you can show quickly.** Save offline copies or photos of the notice, your passport photo page, entry stamp or permit card, any official portal status page, booking details, and a short call log.
7. **If you are detained, held, or taken for questioning, ask to contact British consular services.** Ask to contact the nearest British embassy, high commission or consulate, and ask the officer what the local process is. If you cannot call yourself, ask a trusted person to contact consular services for you.
8. **Contact British consular services if you are stuck, detained, or at risk of being unable to leave safely.** They may be able to provide information about local processes, lists of local lawyers or interpreters, help contact family or friends, and support you if you are detained. They cannot give legal advice, change another country’s immigration decision, help you enter a country, or stop deportation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to challenge the decision long-term; first confirm your current status and any deadline.
- You do not need a perfect file of documents now; start with the notice, passport details, immigration status evidence, travel booking, and written instructions.
- You do not need to contact multiple agents or paid services immediately; prioritise the issuing authority, then your airline if you are travelling, then consular support if you are stuck or at risk.

## Important reassurance
A sudden cancellation notice can make everything feel urgent at once. The safest first move is to verify the notice, get the deadline in writing, and take only the next step that prevents an avoidable overstay, missed flight, detention, or airport crisis.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Formal reviews, appeals, reapplications, overstaying issues, and detention processes are country-specific and may need a qualified local immigration lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, immigration, or other professional advice. Immigration rules and local procedures vary widely, so confirm through official government channels and follow written instructions from the relevant authority.
