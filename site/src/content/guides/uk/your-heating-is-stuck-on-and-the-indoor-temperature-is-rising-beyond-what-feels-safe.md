# What to do if… your heating is stuck on and the indoor temperature is rising beyond what feels safe (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the power to the heating system immediately using the **fused spur or isolation switch** (usually located near the boiler) and move all occupants to a cool room. If a carbon monoxide alarm sounds or anyone shows signs of confusion or heatstroke, evacuate to fresh air and call emergency services.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to dismantle the boiler, remove covers, or adjust internal valves if you are not a qualified engineer.
- Do not touch radiators, boiler casings, or exposed pipes directly, as they may reach temperatures capable of causing burns.
- Do not leave children, elderly persons, or pets in a room that is actively overheating while you troubleshoot.
- Do not rely solely on open windows if the heating system is still active; you must shut down the heat source.
- Do not ignore symptoms of heatstroke (confusion, red/dry skin, seizures) or carbon monoxide poisoning.

## What to do now
1. **Move all people and pets to the coolest part of the property immediately.**
   - Close curtains or blinds in the "cool room" to block sunlight.
   - Use fans or cross-ventilation (opening windows on opposite sides of the building) in this specific room only.
2. **Attempt to shut down the heating via standard controls.**
   - Turn the main thermostat to the lowest possible setting or "OFF" mode.
   - Use the programmer or mobile app to set the system to "OFF" rather than "Auto" or "Timed."
   - Turn radiator valves (TRVs) to the lowest setting (usually "0" or "❄️") in the cool room to protect it.
3. **Isolate the power supply to the heating system safely.**
   - **For gas/oil boilers:** Locate the "fused spur" or isolation switch (a wall switch, often with a red neon light, near the boiler) and switch it to **OFF**.
   - **For electric/storage heaters:** Turn off the individual wall switches or isolators for each unit.
   - **For communal/district heating:** If you cannot find a local isolation valve, focus on turning all radiator TRVs to "OFF" and contact the building manager or emergency concierge.
   - If you cannot find a dedicated switch, do not experiment with the main consumer unit (fuse box) unless the circuits are clearly and professionally labelled.
4. **Identify and respond to medical or gas emergencies.**
   - If a Carbon Monoxide (CO) alarm sounds: **Evacuate the building immediately.** Call the National Gas Emergency Service (GB) at **0800 111 999** or (NI) at **0800 002 001**.
   - If someone has symptoms of heat exhaustion (dizziness, nausea, heavy sweating): Move them to a cool place, give water, and cool their skin. Call **111** if they do not improve within 30 minutes.
   - If someone has symptoms of heatstroke (confusion, loss of consciousness, seizure): Call **999** immediately. If they are unconscious, place them in the **recovery position**.
5. **Report the emergency repair.**
   - **Tenants:** Contact your landlord or letting agent's emergency 24-hour line. Under 2026 standards, a heating system that cannot be turned off and creates a health hazard is an emergency repair.
   - **Homeowners:** Contact a Gas Safe registered engineer (for gas) or a qualified electrician (for electric systems). State clearly that the "heating is stuck on and cannot be controlled."

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the mechanical cause of the failure (e.g., a stuck 3-way valve or faulty relay) right now.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of the repair or future energy bills until the immediate temperature is stabilised.
- You do not need to bleed radiators or check boiler pressure, as these will not stop a "stuck on" system.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when a primary utility system fails to shut down. By isolating the power at the fused spur, you have physically stopped the system from firing. Once the power is off, the property will begin to cool down, and the immediate danger to health will subside.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only to prevent overheating and ensure occupant safety. Permanent repairs must be conducted by a Gas Safe registered engineer or a qualified heating professional.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not replace professional medical or technical advice. Always prioritise evacuation if you suspect a gas leak or carbon monoxide, and seek emergency medical care for severe heat-related symptoms.
