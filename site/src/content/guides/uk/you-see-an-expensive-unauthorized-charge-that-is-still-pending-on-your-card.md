# What to do if… you see an expensive unauthorized charge that is still pending on your card (UK)

## Short answer
Freeze or lock the card now, then contact your bank or card provider using the official app, statement, or number on the back of your card. Say clearly: “This is an unauthorised pending card transaction.”

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume a pending transaction will definitely disappear.
- Don’t call a phone number shown only in the transaction description.
- Don’t click refund, security, or cancellation links sent by text or email about the charge.
- Don’t unfreeze the card to test whether it still works.
- Don’t delete app alerts, emails, texts, or order confirmations connected to the transaction.
- Don’t post screenshots publicly, because they may show account, card, merchant, or reference details.
- Don’t make a false fraud report; only say what you know and what you do not recognise.

## What to do now
1. **Check what type of payment it is.**  
   In your banking app, look for whether it is a pending card transaction, a Direct Debit, a standing order, a bank transfer, or a recurring card payment. If it is a Direct Debit you do not recognise, ask your bank whether it can be refunded under the Direct Debit Guarantee and cancel it if it is not yours.

2. **Freeze or lock the card immediately.**  
   Use your banking app if you can. If the app allows it, also turn off online card payments, contactless use, cash withdrawals, and overseas use until your bank tells you what to do next.

3. **Contact your bank or card provider using an official route.**  
   Use the official app, your statement, or the number printed on the back of the card. Tell them:
   - the transaction is pending,
   - the purchase is expensive,
   - you did not make or authorise it,
   - you have frozen the card,
   - you want the card blocked and replaced if they think the details are compromised.

4. **Ask what can be done before it settles.**  
   Ask whether the bank can decline, void, reverse, or stop the authorisation before it becomes a posted transaction. This is not always possible, but asking early is still useful.

5. **Ask about the money being held.**  
   If the pending amount is reducing your available balance or affecting essential payments, say that plainly. Your bank may say the merchant has to release the authorisation hold, but they can still tell you what they can do and what to watch for.

6. **Save the transaction details while they are visible.**  
   Take a screenshot or write down the amount, date, time, merchant descriptor, reference number, and whether it says pending. Keep any emails, texts, app notifications, or order confirmations connected to the charge.

7. **Secure the account routes that could have been used.**  
   Change the password for the email account linked to your bank and shopping accounts. Turn on two-step verification if available. Then check recent shopping accounts for unknown orders, saved-card use, new delivery addresses, and new-device login alerts.

8. **Contact the merchant only if you can verify them safely.**  
   Do not use contact details from a suspicious transaction description, text, or email. If you can identify the real merchant through a website or app you navigate to yourself, ask them to cancel or void the order and release the authorisation hold.

9. **Watch for another attempt.**  
   Keep the card frozen until the bank confirms it is blocked or replaced. Contact the bank again immediately if another pending transaction appears.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a fraud report; first secure the card and accounts.
- You do not need to complete long dispute forms unless your bank asks for them; some providers can only start certain steps once the transaction posts.
- You do not need to chase the merchant if you cannot confidently verify who they are.
- You do not need to close your bank account unless your provider advises it.
- You do not need to work out how the card details were obtained before calling the bank.

## Important reassurance
A pending card payment is often only an authorisation stage, and merchant names can look unfamiliar. A large unknown pending transaction is still worth treating as urgent, and banks deal with this kind of report routinely.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stop further use, reduce harm from a pending hold, and get the right case opened with your provider. Later steps may involve your bank’s fraud process, a formal complaint, the Financial Ombudsman Service, or a fraud report if you choose to make one.

## Important note
This is general information, not financial, legal, professional, or fraud-investigation advice. Whether a pending authorisation can be stopped varies by provider, card network, merchant, and payment type. Contact your bank as soon as you notice an unauthorised payment, because delay can affect what help is available.
