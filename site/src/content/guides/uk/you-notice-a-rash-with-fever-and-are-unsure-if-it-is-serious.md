# What to do if… you notice a rash with fever and are unsure if it is serious (UK)

## Short answer
If someone has a rash with fever and seems very unwell, treat it as time-sensitive. If the rash looks like bruising or bleeding under the skin, does not fade when pressed, or there are severe symptoms, call **999** now; otherwise call **111 if available where you are** or use your local urgent medical advice service for same-day advice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait overnight if they look very unwell, are hard to wake, confused, not responding normally, or getting worse.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if they might deteriorate, such as if they are drowsy, faint, confused, or struggling to breathe; call **999**.
- Do not go to a GP surgery, urgent treatment centre, or A&E without calling first if measles could be a possibility, unless it is an emergency and you are following **999** advice.
- Do not cover the rash with new creams, ointments, or makeup right now, because clinicians may need to see it clearly.
- Do not assume it is only a viral rash if the fever is high, the rash is changing quickly, or your instinct says something is seriously wrong.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency red flags. Call 999 now if any apply.**
   - The rash looks like small bruises or bleeding under the skin, or it does not fade when pressed.
   - They are very sleepy, hard to wake, confused, agitated, floppy, or not responding like they usually do.
   - They have difficulty breathing, very fast breathing, grunting, or blue, grey, pale, or blotchy skin, lips, or tongue.
   - They have a stiff neck, severe headache, or are very bothered by light.
   - They have a seizure, collapse, sudden swelling of the lips, mouth, throat, or tongue, or a rapidly worsening condition.

2. **Do a quick pressure check on the rash, but do not let it delay help.**
   - Press the side of a clear glass firmly against the rash for a few seconds.
   - If the spots do not fade, call **999** now.
   - If the spots fade, it can still be serious. Continue with the steps below and get help again if they worsen.

3. **Use the right service for how unwell they seem.**
   - Call **999** if any red flags apply, or if you think they are seriously unwell.
   - If there are no immediate red flags but you are unsure, seek same-day urgent medical advice.
   - In England, call **111**; use **111 online** for someone aged 5 or over, or call **111** for a child under 5.
   - In Scotland, call **111** for NHS 24.
   - In Wales, call **111** where the service is available.
   - In Northern Ireland, use your local GP out-of-hours or Phone First route if it is not an emergency.

4. **If a baby or young child has a rash and fever, act earlier.**
   - If a baby is under 3 months old and has a temperature of **38°C**, ask for urgent medical advice the same day.
   - If a child has a rash you are worried about, feels hotter than usual, is shivering, is not feeding normally, or has fewer wet nappies or is peeing less than usual, ask for urgent medical advice the same day.
   - Escalate to **999** if any emergency red flags appear.

5. **Reduce infection risk while you seek help.**
   - Keep them away from others where possible until you have advice.
   - If measles could be possible, call the GP surgery, **111 if available where you are**, or the local urgent advice service before going anywhere in person so they can advise how to attend safely.
   - If chickenpox could be possible, avoid close contact with newborn babies, anyone who is pregnant, and anyone with a weakened immune system while you seek advice.

6. **Write down the details you may be asked for.**
   - When the fever started, the highest temperature, and how it was measured.
   - When the rash started, where it began, whether it is spreading, and whether it fades with pressure.
   - Any new medicines or recent doses, recent vaccines, recent travel, known contact with measles or chickenpox, pregnancy, immune problems, and any breathing, feeding, peeing, alertness, or neck symptoms.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause of the rash right now.
- You do not need to decide about school, work, childcare, or notifications until you have medical advice.
- You do not need to track every number or symptom in detail; a short timeline and a clear description or photo, if safe, is enough.

## Important reassurance
Rash plus fever can look frightening, and it is normal to feel panicky. Acting quickly when someone looks very unwell, the rash does not fade, or your instinct says something is wrong is a sensible safety choice, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps to help you choose the safest next action and reach the right NHS or urgent medical service. Diagnosis and treatment decisions should be made by clinicians.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical diagnosis or personalised medical advice. If you think someone is seriously unwell, trust that instinct and call **999**.
