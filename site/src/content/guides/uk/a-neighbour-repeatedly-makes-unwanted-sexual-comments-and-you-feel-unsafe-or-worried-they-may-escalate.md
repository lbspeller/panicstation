# What to do if… a neighbour repeatedly makes unwanted sexual comments and you feel unsafe or worried they may escalate (UK)

## Short answer
Prioritise getting space from them and telling one safe person what is happening. If you or someone else is in immediate danger, or the situation is happening now and you cannot safely leave it, call 999; otherwise you can contact police on 101 or online for advice.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t meet them privately to talk it through, explain yourself, or negotiate.
- Don’t argue, joke back, or try to keep them sweet if that keeps you in the interaction.
- Don’t threaten, retaliate, or warn them that you will “make them pay”.
- Don’t assume you need perfect evidence before asking for help.
- Don’t put yourself at risk to record, photograph, or follow them.
- Don’t post about it publicly in building groups or on social media while you still share space with them.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance at the first safe pause.**  
   Avoid being alone with them in lifts, stairwells, corridors, car parks, bins areas, or shared entrances where you can. If you see them, it is okay to wait, turn around, go back inside, or leave by another safe route.

2. **Use a short exit line if you need one.**  
   If you feel safe enough to speak, keep it brief: “Do not speak to me about sexual things. Stop.” Then end the interaction. If saying this may provoke them, skip it and focus on getting away.

3. **Tell one trusted person today.**  
   Share the neighbour’s name, flat number if you know it, what has been happening, and when you usually cross paths. Agree a simple check-in, such as texting when you are home or having someone available by phone when you enter or leave if that helps you feel safer.

4. **Make simple notes without turning it into an investigation.**  
   Write down the date, time, place, what happened, any witnesses, any messages or notes, and any obvious CCTV locations. Save messages or screenshots you already have, but do not put yourself in danger to collect more.

5. **Reduce door and shared-space contact.**  
   Keep your phone charged when you go in or out. Use a door viewer, chain, intercom, or peephole if you have one. Do not open the door unless you are comfortable doing so; speak through the door or do not respond.

6. **Ask your housing contact to create a written record.**  
   If you rent, contact your landlord or letting agent. If you live in a block, contact the building manager, housing association, council housing officer, or managing agent. Ask them to log your concern and consider practical safety steps, such as repairs to entry doors, lighting, or communal-area security. If there may be CCTV, ask whether it exists and whether relevant footage can be preserved.

7. **Contact police for advice if it is repeated, threatening, or you feel unsafe.**  
   If there is immediate danger, call 999. If it is not an emergency, call 101 or use your local police force’s online reporting route. Describe what has happened in plain language: repeated unwanted sexual comments or conversations, where it happens, whether they block your way or wait for you, and why you worry it may escalate.

8. **Use specialist sexual-violence support if it would help to talk it through.**  
   If you are aged 16 or over in England or Wales, Rape Crisis England & Wales’ 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line can support people affected by sexual harassment as well as other forms of sexual violence. You do not have to decide whether to report before contacting support.

9. **If the pattern feels like stalking or harassment, use stalking-specific support.**  
   GOV.UK’s stalking page signposts the National Stalking Helpline. This may help if the neighbour is repeatedly waiting for you, following you, watching you, contacting you, turning up uninvited, or making you feel scared, distressed, or threatened.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a formal statement, go to court, or move.
- You do not need to confront them again or write the perfect message.
- You do not need to tell the whole building or explain yourself to neighbours.
- You do not need to work out whether the exact label is harassment, stalking, antisocial behaviour, or something else before asking for help.
- You do not need a long-term plan tonight; focus on distance, one trusted person, and a written record.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, minimising, or doubting yourself is common when someone pushes sexual boundaries, especially when they live nearby. Feeling unsafe or pressured is enough reason to take protective steps and ask for support.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and reduce risk in the next hours or days. Later decisions about housing action, police reporting, civil protections, or specialist advocacy may need help from a qualified support service or legal adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or professional advice. If you or someone else is in immediate danger, call 999.
