# What to do if… lights flicker in multiple rooms and you are unsure if it’s an electrical fault (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the property by reducing electrical load and checking for signs of burning, heat, or buzzing. Contact the electricity network operator on 105 to rule out a local grid fault before arranging a private electrical inspection.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore flickering that affects multiple circuits or rooms, as this often indicates a problem with the main supply or the consumer unit.
- Don't continue using high-power appliances such as electric showers, kettles, or immersion heaters until the cause is identified.
- Don't attempt to tighten internal wiring, open the consumer unit cover, or dismantle sockets.
- Don't touch any electrical equipment that feels hot, smells of fish or burning plastic, or is making a visible arc/spark.
- Don't reset a tripped breaker more than once if it immediately clicks back to the "off" position.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate fire or safety hazards.**  
   **Look and listen for smoke, sparks, a persistent "fishy" or burning plastic smell, or loud buzzing/crackling from the consumer unit (fuse box) or electricity meter.**  
   - If these are present, turn off the main switch on the consumer unit if it is safe to reach.  
   - If there is fire or if you cannot safely isolate the power, evacuate and call **999**.

2. **Reduce the electrical demand on the property.**  
   **Turn off and unplug non-essential appliances, particularly those that generate heat.**  
   - Switch off heaters, dishwashers, washing machines, and kettles.  
   - This reduces the load on potentially loose connections and may prevent an electrical fire.

3. **Determine the scope of the flickering.**  
   **Observe if the flickering happens only when a specific high-load appliance starts up or if it is constant regardless of use.**  
   - Check if your neighbours have similar issues or if street lights are flickering.  
   - If the issue is widespread in the street, it is likely a network fault.

4. **Contact the electricity network operator.**  
   **Call the national fault line to report the issue and check for local network problems.**  
   - **England, Scotland, and Wales:** Call **105** (free from landlines and mobiles).  
   - **Northern Ireland:** Call **03457 643 643** (NIE Networks 24-hour helpline).  
   - They can tell you if there is a known fault with the substation or the service cable entering your property.

5. **Verify the status of your consumer unit.**  
   **Check the switches on your consumer unit without removing any covers.**  
   - If an RCD or circuit breaker has tripped, try to reset it once.  
   - If it trips again immediately, leave it off; this indicates a persistent fault that requires a professional.

6. **Coordinate repairs based on your housing status.**  
   **Inform the relevant party of the specific symptoms (e.g., "flickering in all rooms" or "burning smell near meter").**  
   - **Renters:** Contact your landlord or letting agent immediately. Under UK law, landlords must ensure electrical installations are safe and must investigate reported faults promptly.  
   - **Homeowners:** Contact a registered electrician (check the NICEIC or Napit registers) to perform an urgent inspection of the consumer unit and main incoming connections.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific loose wire or faulty component yourself.
- You do not need to replace light bulbs or fixtures until the main supply is confirmed as stable.
- You do not need to decide on a full rewire or major upgrades during the initial stabilization phase.

## Important reassurance
Flickering across multiple rooms is often a sign of a loose neutral connection or a grid-side voltage fluctuation. While it requires urgent professional attention, identifying it early and reducing the electrical load significantly lowers the risk of equipment damage or fire.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and hazard identification only. It does not replace a formal Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR) or professional diagnosis by a qualified electrician or network technician.

## Important note
Electrical faults can be unpredictable. If you feel unsafe or observe signs of overheating, isolate the power and seek professional help. Ensure your smoke alarms are functional while waiting for an electrician.
