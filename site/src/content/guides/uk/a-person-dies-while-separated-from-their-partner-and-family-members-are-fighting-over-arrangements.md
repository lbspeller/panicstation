# What to do if… a person dies while separated from their partner and family members are fighting over arrangements (UK)

## Short answer
Slow the situation down, choose one calm point of contact for updates, and ask the hospital, care home, or funeral director to record that there is a family dispute. Do not agree to any irreversible funeral, cremation, burial, ashes, or property decisions until authority and paperwork have been checked.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t let several relatives give separate instructions to the funeral director, hospital, care home, registrar, crematorium, burial authority, or faith leader.
- Don’t agree to cremation, burial, moving the body, a funeral date, or ashes arrangements just to stop the argument.
- Don’t remove valuables, documents, keepsakes, phones, bank cards, or keys from the home unless access is lawful or agreed and the item is photographed, listed, and witnessed.
- Don’t use the person’s bank cards, online accounts, phone, email, or social media unless you already have clear authority.
- Don’t privately search the home for a will in a low-trust situation if it could look like tampering.
- Don’t change locks, block access, or exclude someone who may have a lawful right to enter unless there is an immediate safety or property risk and you have taken urgent advice.
- Don’t post accusations, screenshots, or blame on social media or community groups.
- Don’t assume “next of kin,” separation, or not being divorced automatically settles who has authority.

## What to do now
1. Choose one point of contact for the next 24 hours.
   - Pick one calm person, and one backup, to speak to the hospital, care home, funeral director, registrar, or other professional.
   - Send both sides one short message: “For now, please send practical updates to [Name]. We are keeping a written record and not making irreversible decisions until authority is clear.”

2. Ask professionals to record that there is a dispute.
   - Contact the funeral director, hospital bereavement office, care home manager, or mortuary office.
   - Say: “There is a family dispute. Please record that no funeral date, transfer, cremation, burial, ashes decision, or change of instructions should happen without written authority being checked.”
   - Ask what proof they need before they act, such as ID, the will, executor details, administrator details, written agreement, or local forms.

3. Check whether there is a will, executor, funeral plan, or written funeral instruction.
   - Start with low-conflict sources: emails, letters, a solicitor’s name, a will-writing service, funeral plan documents, insurance papers, or a named funeral director.
   - If a solicitor may hold the will, ask only whether they hold a will and who the named executors are.
   - If the home must be checked, do it with a neutral witness or one person from each side, photograph relevant folders as found, list what is opened, and stop once will or funeral-plan information is found.

4. Clarify who appears to have authority before arranging anything irreversible.
   - In England and Wales, a named executor or appointed administrator is usually important for funeral arrangements; if there is no executor or administrator, relationship priority may be relevant.
   - In Scotland, a nominated person or the statutory nearest-relative hierarchy may decide who can apply for burial or cremation; there are specific rules where a spouse or civil partner was permanently separated.
   - In Northern Ireland, registration, coroner involvement, funeral arrangements, and estate authority can follow different processes.
   - If two people both claim authority, keep the message simple: “We are verifying authority. No irreversible steps until this is clear.”

5. Register the death or confirm who is doing it.
   - Ask the hospital, GP, medical examiner office, registrar, coroner, procurator fiscal, or other official process what must happen before registration, as relevant.
   - In England and Wales, you generally wait for medical examiner confirmation before registering; Scotland and Northern Ireland have different routes and timings.
   - Ask who will receive or hold the certificate needed for burial or cremation, and keep a written note of where each document is.

6. Secure the home without turning it into a possession dispute.
   - If the home is empty, check doors and windows are locked and urgent risks are dealt with, such as pets, heating, leaks, visible valuables, or open windows.
   - Take quick photos of each room and any obvious high-value items as they are.
   - If important documents, keys, passports, cards, or devices must be gathered, put them in one labelled envelope or box, list the contents, record who was present, and do not use them.
   - Tell both sides what was secured and where it is being held, unless doing so would create a safety risk.

7. Put all family logistics in writing.
   - Use one email thread or one messaging group for practical updates only.
   - Send short scheduled updates, such as morning and evening, rather than answering every call or accusation.
   - Use neutral wording: “This is what has been confirmed,” “This is still being checked,” and “No decision has been made yet.”

8. Pause possessions, ashes, and ceremony details.
   - Say clearly that belongings, ashes, readings, music, speeches, seating, flowers, and wake arrangements are not being decided today.
   - If someone is pushing for a quick decision, repeat: “We are not refusing; we are waiting until authority and paperwork are clear.”

9. Treat threats, forced entry, or intimidation as a safety issue.
   - Leave the immediate area if people are threatening, following, blocking, or trying to force entry.
   - Call 999 if there is immediate danger to a person or property.
   - For non-emergency threats, harassment, or attempted forced entry, use 101 or the police website, and keep screenshots, voicemails, and a simple date-and-time timeline.

10. Use the right death-notification route for the part of the UK.
   - If the person was living in England, Scotland, or Wales, ask the registrar about Tell Us Once after registration or when an interim death certificate is available.
   - If the person was living in Northern Ireland, use nidirect information about the Bereavement Service and who to tell.
   - Keep non-government contacts separate, such as banks, landlord, mortgage provider, utilities, employer, insurer, and pension provider, and only contact them urgently where there is a practical risk.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the funeral style, readings, music, speakers, flowers, wake, ashes scattering, memorial, or headstone today.
- You do not need to clear the home, divide belongings, sell property, close every account, or start full estate administration today.
- You do not need to answer every message, allegation, or demand. A short written update once or twice a day is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
Conflict after a death during separation is common because people can feel afraid of being excluded, blamed, or ignored. Slowing down, recording actions, and pausing irreversible decisions is not taking sides; it is a way to reduce harm and keep later options open.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the first days, reduce conflict, and prevent irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about funeral authority, estate administration, belongings, children, housing, money, or disputed access may need specialist legal advice, mediation, or support from the relevant professionals.

## Important note
This is general information for urgent first steps, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Authority and process can depend on the will, the estate, the relationship, the home situation, safety concerns, and whether the death involves a coroner, procurator fiscal, or other official process.
