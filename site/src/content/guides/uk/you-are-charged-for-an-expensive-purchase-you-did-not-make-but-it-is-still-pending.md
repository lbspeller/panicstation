# What to do if… you are charged for an expensive purchase you did not make but it is still “pending” (UK)

## Short answer
Freeze your card immediately through your banking app to stop further charges, then call your bank's official fraud line to report the unauthorised authorisation hold.

## Do not do these things
- Don't wait for the transaction to "settle" or disappear on its own before taking action.
- Don't use any phone numbers found in the transaction description or in an SMS/email about the charge.
- Don't follow links in "refund" or "verification" messages that claim to be from your bank.
- Don't unfreeze the card to test if it still works or to make other purchases.
- Don't ignore the charge even if the merchant name looks vaguely familiar but the amount is wrong.

## What to do now
1. **Verify you are using the official banking application or website.**  
   - Close any windows opened via links and navigate to your bank's website by typing the address manually.  
   - Check that the "pending" status refers to a card authorisation and not a scheduled Direct Debit.
2. **Use the "Freeze Card" or "Block Card" feature in your app.**  
   - This prevents new transactions from being authorised while you investigate.  
   - If your app allows, specifically disable "Online" or "International" payments.
3. **Call your bank using the number on the back of your physical card.**  
   - If you do not have the card, use the official number listed on the bank's verified website.  
   - Tell the agent: "I am reporting an unauthorised card transaction currently showing as pending."
4. **Instruct the bank to stop the card and issue a new number.**  
   - Confirm that the current card is permanently blocked so no further "settlements" can occur from that specific card detail.  
   - Ask if they can "void" the authorisation hold, though be aware that most banks must wait for the merchant to release it or for the hold to expire (usually 1 to 7 days).
5. **Request a review of your "digital tokens" or mobile wallets.**  
   - Ask the bank to check if your card has been added to Apple Pay, Google Pay, or Samsung Pay on a device you do not own.  
   - Request that all active tokens for that card be deleted immediately.
6. **Note down the exact transaction details for your records.**  
   - Record the merchant name (descriptor), date, time, and the exact amount.  
   - Take a screenshot of the "pending" screen if your banking app allows it.
7. **Change the password and enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) on your primary email account.**  
   - Fraudsters often gain access to banking apps via compromised email addresses.  
   - Use a strong, unique password that you have never used for any other service.
8. **Contact the merchant only if it is a reputable, well-known company you have used before.**  
   - If the merchant is a major retailer (e.g., Amazon, Apple), log into your account on their official site to see if an order exists.  
   - Do not contact the merchant if the name is unrecognised or suspicious, as this may be part of the scam.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a formal "Chargeback" claim yet; these usually cannot be processed until the transaction has fully "settled" (posted).
- You do not need to report the incident to Action Fraud immediately, though you should do so once your bank account is secured.
- You do not need to close your entire bank account unless the bank confirms your login credentials (not just your card) were compromised.

## Important reassurance
Seeing a large, unrecognised charge can be alarming, but "pending" status means the money has not technically left your account yet; it is merely being held. UK banks have strict obligations under the Payment Services Regulations 2017 and the FCA's Consumer Duty to protect you and, in most cases of clear fraud, they must refund unauthorised payments by the end of the next business day.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization for unauthorised card charges in a pending state. It does not cover long-term credit score repair or legal action against merchants.

## Important note
The ability to "stop" a pending charge is limited by the rules of the card network (Visa/Mastercard). Your bank is your primary point of contact for securing your funds. This guide does not constitute financial or legal advice.
