# What to do if… a social media platform asks for proof of death to memorialise an account and time feels urgent (UK)

## Short answer
Submit the platform's official deceased-user or memorialisation form using an obituary link or digital memorial notice if you do not yet have the death certificate. Meta platforms (Facebook and Instagram) often accept these for memorialisation, allowing you to secure the profile while waiting for official documents.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to log in using the deceased person’s password or guess security answers, as this can trigger a permanent security lockout.
- Do not send original physical death certificates in the post; platforms only require digital scans or clear photographs.
- Do not assume every platform offers memorialisation; X (Twitter) and TikTok currently only offer account deactivation or removal.
- Do not keep opening multiple support tickets for the same account, as this resets your place in the review queue.
- Do not delete the account immediately if you might need to retrieve photos, videos, or messages later, as deletion is usually irreversible.
- Do not provide more personal data (like your own National Insurance number or passport) than the specific form requires.

## What to do now
1. **Identify whether you want memorialisation or full account removal.**
   - Memorialisation (Meta, LinkedIn) adds a "Remembering" badge, freezes the profile, and prevents future logins while keeping posts visible to the original audience.
   - Removal (Google, X, TikTok, Meta) deletes the account entirely.
2. **Check for a pre-designated "Legacy Contact" or "Inactive Account Manager".**
   - If the person named you as a Legacy Contact on Facebook, you may already have limited permissions to manage the profile once you notify the platform.
   - Check the deceased person's email for any "Inactive Account Manager" notifications from Google if they previously set this up.
3. **Gather the specific evidence required for your chosen path.**
   - **For Memorialisation (Fastest):** Most platforms accept a link to an online obituary, a scan of a newspaper death notice, or a funeral memorial card.
   - **For Deletion (Stricter):** Platforms usually require a digital scan of the death certificate and proof of your authority (e.g., being an executor or immediate next-of-kin).
4. **Register the death with the local authorities if you have not already done so.**
   - In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, you must register within 5 days.
   - In Scotland, you must register within 8 days.
5. **Order official death certificate copies from the correct regional service.**
   - England and Wales: General Register Office (GRO).
   - Scotland: National Records of Scotland.
   - Northern Ireland: General Register Office Northern Ireland (GRONI).
6. **Submit the report using the platform’s dedicated "Deceased User" form.**
   - **Boldly** enter the exact username, full legal name, and date of death.
   - Attach the obituary link or certificate scan as requested.
7. **Flag active safety risks if the account is being misused.**
   - If the account is currently being hacked, impersonated, or posting spam, use the platform's "Report Compromised Account" or "Urgent Safety" route in addition to the bereavement report.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the long-term fate of every minor digital account today.
- You do not need to settle questions of "digital inheritance" or data downloads before securing the profile.
- You do not need to have the Grant of Probate or Letters of Administration to submit a basic memorialisation request.

## Important reassurance
The request for proof is a standard security measure to prevent unauthorised account takeovers, not a challenge to your relationship with the deceased. It is normal to feel overwhelmed by this bureaucracy while grieving. If you only have an obituary link right now, submitting that to Meta platforms is a valid first step that secures the account immediately.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilisation and securing of social media profiles. It does not cover legal claims to digital assets, complex probate issues, or the retrieval of private encrypted messages.

## Important note
This is general information and does not constitute legal advice. Platform policies regarding deceased users are subject to change without notice. Always refer to the specific Help Centre of the service you are contacting for the most current requirements in the UK.
