# What to do if… you develop new calf pain and warmth and are worried about a clot (UK)

## Short answer
Seek medical assessment **today** via **NHS 111** or an **urgent GP appointment** if you have new pain and warmth in one leg. Call **999** immediately if you have leg symptoms combined with **breathlessness, chest pain, or fainting**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not **massage**, rub, or use a foam roller on the painful area.
- Do not perform **strenuous exercise** or long walks to test the leg.
- Do not take **aspirin or blood thinners** that were not prescribed for this specific episode.
- Do not ignore the symptoms because the pain is mild or you can still walk.
- Do not **drive yourself** to the hospital if you feel unwell, faint, or short of breath.
- Do not wait for a routine appointment if you have swelling and warmth in one leg.

## What to do now
1. **Check immediately for emergency signs.** Call **999** or go to **A&E** if you have calf symptoms along with any of the following:
   - Sudden breathlessness or difficulty breathing.
   - Sharp chest pain that feels worse when you breathe in.
   - Fainting, feeling very dizzy, or collapsing.
   - Coughing up blood.
2. **Contact NHS 111 or your GP for an urgent same-day assessment.** If you do not have emergency signs but have new calf pain, warmth, or swelling, you must be seen today.
   - Use **111.nhs.uk** or call **111**.
   - State clearly: "I have new pain, warmth, and swelling in one leg and I am worried about a blood clot."
3. **Prepare for your assessment.** Gather information that will help the clinician make a decision.
   - Note when the pain started and if it is only in one leg.
   - Check for skin changes; redness or darkening may be harder to see on brown or black skin but the area may look deeper in colour or purple.
   - List any recent surgery, injury, or long travel (over 3 hours) in the last few weeks.
   - List current medicines, specifically hormone replacement therapy (HRT) or the contraceptive pill.
4. **Isolate the limb and limit movement.** While waiting to be seen, stay off your feet where possible.
   - Keep the leg elevated if it is swollen.
   - Limit movement to essential trips (e.g., to the bathroom) only.
   - Do not apply heat or ice to the area until a clinician has assessed it.
5. **Bring your medications with you.** If told to attend a clinic or hospital, take your current medicines or a printed prescription list with you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause (such as a muscle strain vs. a clot) yourself; let the clinical tests decide.
- You do not need to research long-term treatment or recovery steps right now.
- You do not need to measure the circumference of your calf repeatedly.
- You do not need to stop taking your regular medications unless specifically instructed by a doctor today.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel anxious when a symptom is linked to a serious condition like a blood clot. Many different issues can cause calf pain and warmth, but because a clot requires a specific scan to rule out, seeking urgent medical advice is the standard and safest response.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and advice on how to access the UK healthcare system for a suspected DVT. It does not replace clinical diagnosis or provide long-term medical management.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only. Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE) are serious conditions. If you experience a sudden worsening of breathing or chest pain, treat it as a life-threatening emergency and call 999.
