# What to do if… someone posts online about a death in your family before the immediate family has been told (UK)

## Short answer
Prioritise direct, verbal notification of the closest living relatives immediately. Request the post's removal once and then use platform reporting tools to flag the content as a privacy violation or malicious communication.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t reply publicly to the post, even to correct details or express anger (engagement often increases the post's visibility in algorithms).
- Don’t confirm the death in public comments while any immediate family members remain uninformed.
- Don’t attempt to coordinate a mass response from friends before the closest relatives have been told.
- Don’t engage in a back-and-forth argument with the poster; one request for removal is sufficient.
- Don’t reshare or screenshot the post into large group chats, as this further distributes the information you are trying to contain.

## What to do now
1. **Designate one person to handle all immediate notifications.** If you are not the next-of-kin, identify who is most capable of making phone calls to ensure the inner circle is told before they see it online.
2. **Contact the closest relatives by phone or in person immediately.** Use a direct approach to explain that news is circulating online and you wanted them to hear the facts from family first.
3. **Capture evidence of the post without staying on the page.** Take a single screenshot that includes the username, the content, and the timestamp, then close the application.
4. **Send a single, neutral request for removal to the poster.** Use a templated message:
   - "Please delete this post immediately. The closest family have not all been informed yet. Do not share this information further."
5. **Report the content to the social media platform.** Use the specific reporting flow for "Privacy," "Sensitive Personal Information," or "Harassment."
   - Under the Online Safety Act and UK GDPR (Right to Erasure), platforms have a higher duty to act on sensitive personal data involving deceased individuals.
6. **Report the incident as a Malicious Communication if the content is grossly offensive.** If the post is intentionally distressing or threatening, use the online reporting tool at **police.uk** or call **101**. 
7. **Adjust your own privacy and tagging settings.** 
   - Set "Tag Review" to 'On' so no further posts appear on your timeline without approval.
   - Mute or block the account responsible for the leak to prevent further exposure.
8. **Contact the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO) if a platform fails to remove the data.** If the platform does not respond within 72 hours to a privacy report involving sensitive personal data, you can flag the failure via the ICO's 2026 digital reporting portal.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to determine how the "leak" happened or who told the poster right now.
- You don’t need to draft an official family statement or obituary today.
- You don’t need to decide on long-term legal action or formal complaints against the poster.
- You don’t need to answer messages from distant acquaintances or the "worried well" until the immediate crisis is managed.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of violation or panic when private family news is made public prematurely. While you cannot "undo" the initial post, taking direct action to inform the family and using formal reporting channels is the most effective way to limit the duration and reach of the distress.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a digital privacy breach during a bereavement. It does not cover long-term legal claims, estate management, or formal complaints against institutions.

## Important note
This guide is for general information and does not constitute legal or professional advice. If the online activity involves specific threats of violence or immediate danger, call **999** immediately.
