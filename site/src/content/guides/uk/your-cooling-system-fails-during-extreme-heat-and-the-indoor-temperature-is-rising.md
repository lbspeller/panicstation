# What to do if… your cooling system fails during extreme heat and the indoor temperature is rising (UK)

## Short answer
Move people to the coolest available space, block heat from coming in, and start cooling bodies with water, shade, and fluids. If anyone may have heatstroke, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep windows wide open for air if it feels hotter outside than inside.
- Don’t rely on a fan as the main cooling method if the indoor temperature is 35°C or higher.
- Don’t aim a fan directly at someone’s body for long periods.
- Don’t use the oven or hob unless it is essential.
- Don’t ignore heat-illness warning signs such as dizziness, headache, nausea, cramps, unusual weakness, confusion, collapse, or symptoms that are getting worse.
- Don’t attempt DIY electrical repairs on the cooling system.
- Don’t leave children, vulnerable adults, or pets in parked cars, conservatories, or other small closed spaces.

## What to do now
1. **Check who needs priority help.**  
   Move higher-risk people first: babies and young children, older adults, pregnant people, people with heart, breathing, kidney, diabetes, serious mental health or mobility problems, and anyone taking medicines that affect hydration or temperature regulation.

2. **Choose one cool room.**  
   Pick the coolest shaded room, often a north-facing room or the lowest level. Move water, medicines, chargers, a phone, light bedding, and pet water there so people do not need to keep moving around.

3. **Stop more heat coming in.**  
   Close curtains, blinds, shutters, or temporary coverings on windows in direct sun. Turn off unused lights, heated appliances, games consoles, computers, and other equipment giving off heat.

4. **Use windows only when they help.**  
   If the air outside feels cooler than inside, usually late evening, overnight, or early morning, open windows if it is safe and try to create a through-draft. If it feels hotter outside, keep sunny-side windows and coverings closed.

5. **Use fans carefully.**  
   If the indoor temperature is below 35°C, a fan may help comfort and air movement. If the room is 35°C or higher, use water-based cooling instead and prioritise getting people to a cooler place.

6. **Cool people directly.**  
   Sip cool drinks regularly unless a clinician has told you to restrict fluids. Use cool showers, a damp cloth, a spray bottle, or cool packs wrapped in cloth on the neck, armpits, or wrists. Remove unnecessary layers.

7. **Move pets to shade and water.**  
   Put pets in the coolest shaded area, give them fresh water, and avoid exercise or travel in the heat. If a pet seems distressed, weak, heavily panting, confused, or unable to settle, contact a vet.

8. **Set a simple leaving threshold.**  
   If the room keeps getting hotter, someone is vulnerable, or symptoms are starting, go to a cooler place if you can: a friend or family member’s home, a library, a supermarket, a place of worship, or another public building.

9. **Report the cooling failure without getting stuck in an argument.**  
   If you rent, tell your landlord or agent in writing that the cooling system has failed during extreme heat, the indoor temperature is rising, and whether anyone vulnerable is affected. Ask what temporary measures or repair arrangements are available, and check your tenancy agreement if the cooling system was supplied with the home.

10. **Arrange repair help if it is your responsibility.**  
   If you own the system or are responsible for it, contact home emergency cover, your insurer if relevant, or a qualified air-conditioning or cooling repair service. Ask about temporary safe cooling options while waiting.

11. **Get medical help when needed.**  
   Call 999 if someone may have heatstroke, with signs such as confusion, collapse, seizure, hot skin that is not sweating, fast breathing, or not improving after about 30 minutes of cooling. Use NHS 111 online or call 111 if symptoms are worrying, worsening, or you are unsure and it is not an emergency.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to replace the whole cooling system.
- You do not need to cool every room; focus on one cooler space and people’s bodies.
- You do not need to settle responsibility, compensation, or a landlord dispute in the heat of the moment.
- You do not need to plan insulation, new blinds, or long-term home improvements today.

## Important reassurance
A rising indoor temperature can make it much harder to think clearly. The useful target is small and immediate: reduce heat exposure, keep vulnerable people cooler, and get help if symptoms appear.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours. Later decisions about repairs, housing rights, temporary accommodation, veterinary care, or medical follow-up may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, engineering, veterinary, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If someone is deteriorating, confused, collapsed, or not improving with cooling, seek urgent medical help.
