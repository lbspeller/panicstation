# What to do if… you are told you must report regularly to an authority and you cannot make the first appointment (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the specific office or officer named on your paperwork immediately **before the appointment time** to request a rearrangement and obtain a clear, alternative instruction.

## Do not do these things
- Do not "just miss it" without prior contact — **unexplained non-attendance is usually treated as a breach of conditions** or a failure to surrender.
- Do not rely on informal verbal messages given to a third party or a general switchboard without getting a reference or name.
- Do not assume a voicemail is sufficient if an email or written message can also be sent as proof.
- Do not provide false information or invented excuses; **if evidence is required (e.g., a medical note), it must be authentic**.
- Do not wait for the authority to contact you after the time has passed.

## What to do now
1. **Identify exactly which type of reporting you are required to do.** Look at your official paperwork (e.g., a Police Bail Sheet, a Probation Induction letter, or an Immigration Bail 201 form) to find:
   - the **name of the authority** (Police, Probation, or Home Office)
   - the **specific location** or office address
   - the **named officer or team** (e.g., "Officer in the Case" or "Offender Manager")
   - your **case or reference number**.
2. **Contact them using the phone number or email provided on that paperwork.** If you are calling, ask to speak directly to the person or team named on the notice.
3. **State clearly that you cannot attend and provide a factual reason.** Keep the communication brief and focus on the immediate hurdle (e.g., "I have a medical emergency" or "The notice was received after the appointment time").
4. **Request an immediate alternative instruction.** 
   - Ask if you can report later the same day, the next morning, or via a different method if permitted.
   - **Obtain a new date and time** and write it down immediately.
5. **Create a record of your attempt to contact them.** 
   - Note the **date and time** of the call.
   - Write down the **name of the person** you spoke to and what they said.
   - Save a **screenshot of your outgoing call log** and keep a copy of any sent emails or texts.
6. **If this is an Immigration Reporting Centre appointment:** send an email to the specific centre address provided on the GOV.UK "Immigration reporting centres" list.
   - Include your **Full Name, Date of Birth, Home Office Reference**, and a **mobile phone number** where you can be reached.
7. **If this is a Probation appointment:** ensure you provide any requested evidence (such as a doctor's fit note) within **5 working days** of the missed appointment.
8. **Contact your solicitor if the reporting is a condition of bail or a court order.** A solicitor can help notify the authority and advise you if an application to the court is needed to formally vary the condition.
9. **Go to the reporting location as soon as you are physically able if you cannot get through by phone.** If a police station is closed, use the external "yellow hatch" phone or call 101 to have your presence and the situation logged.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a long-term legal strategy or contest the fairness of the conditions today.
- You do not need to gather a lifetime of background documents; only focus on the specific reason for missing this one appointment.
- You do not need to worry about future appointments until this first missed one is resolved and rearranged.

## Important reassurance
It is common for first appointments to be missed due to administrative errors, travel disruptions, or short notice. Authorities generally prioritize **re-establishing contact** over immediate enforcement action, provided you reach out proactively and provide a factual explanation.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only to help stabilize your status and prevent immediate arrest or breach proceedings. Later decisions regarding changing your conditions permanently may require formal legal applications or specialist advice.

## Important note
This guide is for general information only and does not constitute legal advice. Reporting requirements are legally binding; failing to "answer bail" (the return date) is a criminal offence under the Bail Act 1976. Breaching a "reporting condition" (signing) can lead to immediate arrest and a return to custody or court.
