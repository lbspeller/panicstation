# What to do if… you suspect your car has been sabotaged or tampered with before you drive away (UK)

## Short answer
Do not attempt to start or move the vehicle. Move to a safe, public location and contact the police (999 if the suspect is present or you are in danger; 101 or online for non-emergencies) and your breakdown provider.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to drive the car to a garage or "test" the brakes or steering.
- Do not touch or remove any suspicious objects attached to or near the vehicle.
- Do not confront anyone nearby or attempt to investigate the source of the tampering yourself.
- Do not put your hands into the wheel arches, engine bay, or under the chassis.
- Do not attempt to clean or repair the damage, as this may destroy forensic evidence.
- Do not share your exact location or photos of the scene on social media while still at the site.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritise your personal safety and create distance.**  
   **Move away from the vehicle and find a well-lit, populated area** such as a shop, staffed reception, or busy public walkway. If you are already inside the car and feel threatened, **lock all doors**, keep windows closed, and do not exit until help arrives.

2. **Assess the need for an emergency police response.**  
   **Call 999 immediately if you believe a crime is in progress**, if you feel you are being watched or followed, or if you find a suspicious device attached to the car.
   - For situations where there is no immediate threat, report the incident via **101** or the **online reporting tool** of your local police force.
   - Note that vehicle tampering is a specific offence under Section 25 of the Road Traffic Act 1988.

3. **Perform a visual "no-touch" check from a safe distance.**  
   **Look for obvious signs of mechanical interference or hazards** around the car.
   - Check for fresh fluid leaks (oil, brake fluid, or coolant) on the ground.
   - Observe the tyres for sudden deflations, deep sidewall cuts, or missing wheel nuts.
   - Look for "ghost plates" or signs that your number plates have been swapped or altered.
   - Identify any wires, tape, or unfamiliar objects hanging from the underside.

4. **Document the scene thoroughly without touching the car.**  
   **Use your phone to take wide-angle and close-up photos of the vehicle and surroundings.**
   - Capture images of all four tyres, the ground beneath the engine, and any visible damage.
   - Record the location, bay number, and any visible CCTV cameras in the vicinity.
   - Note the exact time you discovered the issue and when the vehicle was last left secure.

5. **Contact site security or property management.**  
   **Notify the manager of the car park, workplace, or residential block immediately.**
   - Formally request that they **preserve CCTV footage** for the time window the car was parked.
   - Do not attempt to view the footage yourself; the police will request this as part of an investigation.

6. **Notify your insurance provider and breakdown service.**  
   **Inform your insurer that you suspect malicious damage or tampering.**
   - Request **flatbed recovery** to a certified garage for a safety inspection rather than a "tow" that requires the wheels to turn.
   - Emphasize that the car may be **unroadworthy** and requires a professional check of the braking and steering systems.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the perpetrator or determine their motive right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to make a formal insurance claim until you have a mechanic's report.
- You do not need to perform a mechanical diagnostic or "rule out" accidental wear and tear yourself.

## Important reassurance
Choosing not to drive a vehicle you suspect is unsafe is the correct technical and safety procedure. Vehicle tampering is a serious matter, and involving professional recovery and the police ensures that you are protected both physically and legally.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and harm prevention. Long-term mechanical repairs, legal proceedings, or insurance disputes will require specialist professional advice.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or mechanical advice. In the UK, driving a vehicle in a dangerous condition is an offence; always rely on a qualified mechanic's assessment before returning the car to the road.
