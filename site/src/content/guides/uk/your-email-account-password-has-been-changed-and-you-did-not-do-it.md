# What to do if… your email account password has been changed and you did not do it (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible account takeover. Start recovery through your email provider’s official app, saved bookmark, or website, then secure recovery details, sessions, forwarding rules, and two-step verification before using the account normally again.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click password reset links in emails or texts you were not expecting; use the provider’s official app or website instead.
- Don’t keep guessing passwords repeatedly; it may trigger delays, extra checks, or temporary lockouts.
- Don’t reuse the same password, or a similar one, just to get back in.
- Don’t ignore forwarding, filters, rules, recovery details, or delegated access settings.
- Don’t warn contacts from the compromised account until you are confident the attacker has been signed out.

## What to do now
1. **Use a safer device if you can.** If the device you normally use has strange pop-ups, unknown apps, unknown browser extensions, or recent suspicious downloads, use another trusted device for recovery. If you cannot, update the device and browser before entering new passwords.
2. **Start the official account recovery process.** Open the email provider’s official app, use a saved bookmark, or type the provider’s address carefully. Use its account recovery or help flow and follow the identity checks.
3. **Once you regain access, sign out other sessions.** In account security settings, sign out of all other devices and sessions. Remove unfamiliar connected apps, app passwords, mail clients, or delegated access.
4. **Change the password again.** Use a long, unique password that you have not used anywhere else. A password manager can help create and save it.
5. **Check recovery details and two-step verification.**
   - Make sure the recovery email address and phone number are yours.
   - Turn on two-step verification if it is available.
   - Remove any backup method you do not control.
   - Save backup codes somewhere safe and separate from the compromised mailbox.
6. **Check for mailbox persistence.** Remove anything you did not set up, including:
   - forwarding addresses
   - mail rules or filters
   - auto-replies and signatures
   - “send mail as” settings
   - delegated mailbox access
   - unfamiliar folders, sent mail, deleted mail, or archived messages
7. **Protect the most sensitive accounts linked to this email.** From a safe device, check banking, payment, shopping, cloud storage, and main social accounts. Change reused passwords, enable two-step verification, and review recent sign-ins, orders, transfers, or password resets.
8. **Warn close contacts safely.** Use a different channel, such as phone, text, or another trusted email account. Tell them to ignore unexpected messages, payment requests, invoice changes, gift card requests, or urgent links from your compromised email.
9. **Report suspicious phishing messages.** If you have a suspicious email, forward it to report@phishing.gov.uk. If you are not sure which message caused the takeover, report any likely login alert, invoice, delivery message, or security warning that looks suspicious.
10. **Report fraud or money loss through the right UK route.** If money was lost, payment details were misused, or the takeover was part of a scam, contact your bank or payment provider first. If you are in England, Wales or Northern Ireland, report cyber crime or fraud through Report Fraud. If you are in Scotland, report fraud to Police Scotland by calling 101.
11. **Escalate work or organisation-related risk.** If this email is used for work, school, volunteering, client contact, or shared systems, tell the relevant IT or security contact now. If a separate organisation may have exposed or mishandled your personal data, use the ICO guidance to decide what to ask that organisation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly how it happened right now.
- You do not need to delete the whole mailbox or close the account immediately.
- You do not need to contact everyone you have ever emailed; start with people who might act on urgent payment, gift card, invoice, or login messages.
- You do not need to make every report at once; first regain access, cut off sessions, and protect linked financial accounts.
- You do not need to buy new software or pay anyone claiming they can instantly recover the account.

## Important reassurance
This can feel personal, but many email takeovers are automated and opportunistic. Regaining access, removing hidden access, securing recovery options, and checking linked accounts are the steps that reduce immediate harm.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on regaining control and preventing immediate damage. Later decisions, such as detailed device checks, identity protection, workplace incident handling, or legal complaints, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, IT, cyber security, or other professional advice. Follow official instructions from your email provider, bank, employer, school, police reporting service, or regulator where they apply.
