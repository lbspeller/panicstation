# What to do if… you have sudden loss of vision in one eye or a curtain-like shadow (UK)

## Short answer
If you suddenly cannot see from one eye, call 999 or go to A&E now; do not drive yourself. If you can still see but have a new dark curtain or shadow, new flashes or floaters, or sudden blurred vision, get urgent same-day help through NHS 111, an urgent opticians appointment, or an eye emergency department.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if it settles, even if there is no pain.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E or urgent eye care.
- Do not rub, press, or massage the eye.
- Do not put contact lenses back in.
- Do not use leftover prescription eye drops or medication unless a clinician has told you to.
- Do not assume it is “just a migraine” if this is new, one-sided, or includes a curtain-like shadow.

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 now if you suddenly cannot see from one eye, or if you have any stroke signs.**  
   Say: “I have sudden loss of vision in one eye.”  
   Mention any face weakness, arm weakness, speech problems, confusion, sudden weakness or numbness, dizziness or falling, severe headache, or sickness.

2. **If you can still see but have a new curtain-like shadow, new flashes or floaters, or sudden blurred vision, get urgent same-day eye assessment.**  
   Use NHS 111, an urgent opticians appointment, or an eye emergency department. If symptoms are getting worse, you cannot get urgent direction quickly, or you cannot travel safely, call 999.

3. **Do not go alone if you can avoid it.**  
   Ask someone to drive you, stay with you, or meet you at A&E or urgent eye care. If no one is available and you cannot travel safely, call 999.

4. **Make the space safer while help is being arranged.**  
   Sit down away from stairs, cooking, tools, or traffic. Keep your phone with you. If you are alone, unlock the door and message or call someone nearby.

5. **Note the timing.**  
   Write down when the vision change started, when you were last seeing normally, and whether it is still there or came and went.

6. **Write a short symptom note to show the clinician.**  
   Include:
   - which eye is affected
   - whether it is total vision loss, a curtain, a shadow, missing side vision, flashes, floaters, distortion, or blurred patches
   - whether there is pain, redness, recent eye injury, or recent eye surgery
   - any headache, jaw pain when chewing, scalp tenderness, weakness, numbness, speech problems, confusion, dizziness, or sickness

7. **Bring essentials if they are easy to grab.**  
   Take your glasses, contact lens details if available, a list or photos of medicines, allergies, medical conditions, and a contact person’s details.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause before getting help.
- You do not need to book a routine GP appointment first.
- You do not need to keep testing your vision.
- You do not need to search symptoms online or try home treatments.
- You do not need to decide about treatment now; the immediate job is to get urgent assessment.

## Important reassurance
Feeling frightened is understandable. Sudden one-eye vision changes can be time-sensitive, and getting checked quickly is the safest step, even if the symptoms partly improve.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about tests, treatment, follow-up, work, driving, or recovery may need help from urgent care, an eye specialist, your GP, or another qualified clinician.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not a diagnosis, medical advice, or a substitute for urgent professional assessment. Sudden vision loss or a curtain-like shadow can be a medical emergency even without pain.
