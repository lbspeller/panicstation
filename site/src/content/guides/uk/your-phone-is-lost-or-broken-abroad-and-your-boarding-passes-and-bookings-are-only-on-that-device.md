# What to do if… your phone is lost or broken abroad and your boarding passes and bookings are only on that device (UK)

## Short answer
Find your booking reference via an internet-connected device (hotel or airport computer), go to your airline’s check-in or customer service desk at least three hours early with your passport, and immediately contact your UK mobile provider to bar the SIM.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume boarding is impossible; airlines can re-verify your identity and print documents if you have a valid passport.
- Do not repeatedly attempt passwords on a public computer if you are unsure, as this may trigger a permanent account lockout.
- Do not leave your mobile wallet or banking apps unsecured if the phone is missing.
- Do not allow strangers to assist with typing passwords or account recovery on their personal devices.
- Do not walk away from a shared computer without fully logging out and closing the browser.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a secure location with stable internet access.**
   - Seek out a hotel reception, airport business centre, or a staffed internet cafe where you can sit and focus without distractions.
2. **Use a browser to remotely lock or wipe your device.**
   - For Apple devices, log into iCloud.com/find to enable 'Lost Mode' or wipe the device.
   - For Android devices, log into google.com/android/find to lock or erase the phone.
3. **Retrieve your booking reference (PNR) and flight details.**
   - Log into your email or travel agent account to find your confirmation number.
   - Write these details down on physical paper along with the flight number and departure time.
4. **Contact your UK mobile network provider within 24 hours.**
   - Request that they bar the SIM card and block the IMEI (handset).
   - Reporting within 24 hours is required by most UK networks to limit your liability for unauthorised charges to a maximum of £100.
5. **Freeze your bank cards and mobile wallets.**
   - Log into your banking portal via a browser or call your bank's emergency number (found on their official website) to disable Apple Pay, Google Pay, and physical cards.
6. **Go to the airport at least three hours before your flight.**
   - Present your **passport** at the airline’s check-in desk or customer service counter.
   - Inform the staff that your device is lost or broken; even digital-only airlines like Ryanair provide free re-prints at the airport for passengers who have already checked in online but lost their device.
7. **Secure your primary email account first.**
   - Change your email password as the priority, as this is the "key" to all other travel accounts.
   - Use account recovery codes or secondary email options if two-factor authentication (2FA) was linked to the lost phone.
8. **Obtain a local police report if the phone was stolen.**
   - Most UK travel insurance policies require a police report or a "crime reference number" filed within 24 hours of the incident to process a claim.

## What can wait
- You do not need to buy a replacement handset immediately if you can access your flight and hotel through a computer.
- You do not need to notify all social media or non-essential apps right now; focus only on security for your SIM, email, money, and bookings.
- You do not need to initiate the full insurance claim until you have returned to the UK or reached a stable destination.

## Important reassurance
Airlines and airport staff encounter lost or broken phones daily. Provided you have your passport and can provide basic booking information, they have established protocols to verify your identity and get you on the aircraft. Your safety and the security of your accounts are the immediate priorities; the hardware can be replaced later.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for accessing travel documents and securing accounts immediately after a loss. It does not cover long-term device replacement or detailed insurance claim procedures.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Airline policies and airport security requirements vary significantly by country and carrier. If you are in a situation where you feel physically unsafe or have lost all forms of identification, contact the nearest British Embassy or Consulate for emergency assistance.
