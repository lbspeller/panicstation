# What to do if… you are asked for proof of onward travel and cannot access your booking confirmation (UK)

## Short answer
Ask check-in staff exactly what proof they will accept, then try to retrieve or re-send a verifiable itinerary while you are still at the desk. If you cannot access it quickly, ask the airline or agent to re-send or print it, and ask about moving to a later flight rather than arguing at the gate.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t rely only on a verbal explanation that you will book onward travel later; staff may need something they can record or verify.
- Don’t buy a random, non-changeable ticket in panic unless you genuinely intend to use it.
- Don’t delete emails, apps, screenshots, downloads, or payment records while trying to “fix” your phone.
- Don’t get into a confrontation at the desk or gate; ask calmly for a supervisor if the first answer is not clear.
- Don’t assume the airline app is the only way to prove the booking.

## What to do now
1. **Ask what exact proof they need to see.**  
   Say: “What will you accept: a booking reference, PNR, e-ticket number, confirmed itinerary email, or printed itinerary?”

2. **Search the fastest places for your booking details.**
   - Search your email for the airline name, “itinerary”, “e-ticket”, “receipt”, “confirmation”, “PNR”, and “booking reference”.
   - Check screenshots, downloads, files, wallet apps, and saved PDFs.
   - Check the account you booked through, including “Manage booking”, “Trips”, or “Bookings”.
   - Check your bank or credit card app for the merchant name, payment date, and amount if you need to call the airline or agent.

3. **Try recovery routes that do not need the original email link.**
   - Use the airline’s “Manage booking” page with your surname and booking reference if you have them.
   - Look for “Find my booking” or “Resend confirmation” on the airline or travel agent site.
   - If you have a frequent-flyer account, check your trips while logged in.
   - If your phone is the problem, ask airport staff, the airline desk, or a travel companion whether you can use a charging point, airport Wi-Fi, or another device just to retrieve the itinerary.

4. **Ask the airline desk to re-issue the proof.**
   - Ask them to search using your passport name, flight number, travel date, and payment details if needed.
   - Ask for a printed itinerary or a re-sent confirmation email while you are standing there.
   - If the staff member cannot help, ask: “Could a supervisor check what can be accepted or reissued?”

5. **If the onward journey is on a separate booking, make it easy to read.**
   - Show the separate onward ticket confirmation, including passenger name, destination, date, and booking reference.
   - If onward travel is by train, bus, or ferry, show the confirmed reservation and date if you have one.
   - If you genuinely do not have onward travel arranged, you may need to arrange acceptable onward or return travel before the airline will let you board.

6. **If nothing can be retrieved quickly, switch to buying time.**
   - Ask whether you can be moved to a later flight while you recover the proof.
   - If you booked through a travel agent or online travel agent, call or message them and ask for an immediate re-send of the itinerary to email or SMS.
   - If you must buy onward travel, check the destination requirement first where possible and avoid a non-refundable purchase unless you are willing to use it.

7. **If you are refused boarding, record what happened without escalating.**
   - Ask for the reason in writing if staff can provide it.
   - Write down the time, desk or gate number, airline, flight number, and the wording used, such as “proof of onward travel” or “inadequate documentation”.
   - Keep screenshots, emails, booking records, payment evidence, and receipts for extra costs.

8. **Check the requirement, but keep the focus on producing proof.**
   - Airlines often use travel-document checking systems such as Timatic, and the public IATA Travel Centre can help you check passport, visa, and entry-document requirements for your route.
   - GOV.UK foreign travel advice can also show destination entry requirements for UK travellers.
   - If those sources suggest onward or return travel proof may be needed, focus on getting a verifiable itinerary rather than debating the rule at the counter.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, claim compensation, or pursue a refund.
- You do not need to settle whether the rule is fair while you are at check-in.
- You do not need to sort out your whole trip; the immediate task is to produce the minimum proof the airline will accept or create time to retrieve it.

## Important reassurance
This is a common airport problem, especially when apps log out, emails are buried, Wi-Fi is poor, or bookings are on separate tickets. The fastest solution is usually practical: recover, re-send, or print proof that already exists.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation at check-in or boarding and avoid panic decisions. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if you want to challenge a refusal to carry; being bumped because a flight is oversold is different from being refused because staff believe your travel documents or entry evidence are not adequate.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional travel advice. Entry rules and airline boarding checks depend on your nationality, route, visa status, destination rules, and airline policy, and staff may apply them strictly.
