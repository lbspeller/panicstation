# What to do if… you miss a dose of important medicine and symptoms are starting (UK)

## Short answer
If symptoms are starting, do not double up, guess, or try to “make up” the dose. Check the missed-dose instructions for that exact medicine and get same-day advice from a pharmacist, your usual clinical team, or urgent medical advice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take an extra dose or double dose unless the medicine leaflet, label, pharmacist, prescriber, or specialist team specifically tells you to.
- Do not take a dose “just in case” if you are not sure whether you already took it.
- Do not stop or change other related medicines to try to balance things out.
- Do not drive, use machinery, cook, swim, climb, or do risky tasks if you feel dizzy, drowsy, confused, shaky, faint, or otherwise impaired.
- Do not use someone else’s medicine, old medicine, or a different strength to improvise a replacement dose.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency symptoms first.** If you have trouble breathing, chest pain, fainting or collapse, a seizure, new severe confusion, signs of a severe allergic reaction, or you are rapidly getting worse, call **999** now.

2. **Get the exact medicine details in front of you.** Put the box, bottle, blister pack, label, or repeat prescription where you can see it. Note the medicine name, strength, usual dose, when it was due, and what symptoms have started.

3. **Check the missed-dose instructions for that exact medicine.** Look at the patient information leaflet, the pharmacy label, or the medicine’s official online leaflet. Follow the medicine-specific instruction only if it clearly matches your situation and your symptoms are not severe.

4. **Because symptoms are starting, ask for same-day advice.** Contact a community pharmacist, your GP surgery, your specialist nurse or clinic, or the team that normally manages the condition. If this is insulin, antiseizure medicine, Parkinson’s medicine, an anticoagulant, transplant medicine, cancer medicine, steroid medicine, heart medicine, or any medicine with a personal action plan, contact the usual condition team as early as you can.

5. **Use urgent medical advice if you cannot quickly reach your usual help.** In England, Scotland, or Wales, phone **111** if you need advice now and it is not a 999 emergency. In Northern Ireland, or if 111 is not available where you are, use your local GP out-of-hours, pharmacy, or urgent-care route.

6. **If you might have taken too much, say that first.** If you are unsure whether you took the missed dose, or you may have taken an extra dose, tell the pharmacist, 111 adviser, GP, or specialist team before taking anything else. If you become very unwell, call **999**.

7. **If you have completely run out, say whether symptoms are present.** If you need medicine and symptoms have started, call for urgent advice rather than only using an online emergency prescription form. If you have run out but do not have symptoms, an emergency supply route, pharmacy, GP surgery, or urgent treatment service may be able to help, but some medicines cannot be supplied this way.

8. **Reduce risk while you wait for advice.** Sit somewhere safe, keep the medicine packaging nearby, and ask another adult to stay with you or check on you if symptoms could worsen. If fluids are normally safe for you and you are not choking, vomiting, or being told not to drink, small sips of water are usually reasonable.

9. **Write a short timeline.** Note the missed dose time, any doses taken since, symptoms, when they started, and any readings from a monitor you normally use, such as blood glucose, peak flow, blood pressure, or pulse. Share this with the pharmacist, clinician, or urgent adviser.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out why this happened right now.
- You do not need to change your treatment plan, routine, dose schedule, or long-term reminders until you have dealt with the immediate missed dose.
- You do not need to finish tasks, attend non-urgent plans, or push through symptoms before getting advice.
- Pill organisers, alarms, prescription timing, and follow-up reviews can wait until you are stable.

## Important reassurance
Missing a dose can happen, even with important medicines. The safest next step is not to blame yourself or guess, but to get medicine-specific advice quickly and avoid taking doses too close together.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the next few hours. The right action depends on the medicine, dose, timing, symptoms, other conditions, and any personal action plan you have, so later decisions may need help from a pharmacist, GP, specialist nurse, clinic, or urgent-care service.

## Important note
This guide is general information only and is not a diagnosis, prescription, or substitute for personal medical advice. If symptoms are severe, rapidly worsening, or you are unsure what you took, seek urgent help immediately.
