# What to do if… your device clock resets and secure websites stop loading correctly (UK)

## Short answer
Do not call the provided number or click any buttons within the pop-up. Close the browser tab or force-quit the application, then run a scan using your device's built-in or trusted security software.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t call the number, engage with a chat window, or provide any personal details to the "technician."
- Don’t allow anyone to remotely access your device or install software like AnyDesk or TeamViewer based on their instructions.
- Don’t enter passwords, banking login details, or verification codes into any prompts linked to the pop-up.
- Don’t pay for "repairs" or "subscriptions" using gift cards, cryptocurrency, or bank transfers.
- Don’t assume your computer is actually infected because the pop-up looks professional or uses official logos.

## What to do now
1. **Exit the pop-up or browser immediately.**
   - On Windows: press **Ctrl + Shift + Esc** to open Task Manager, select your browser, and click **End Task**.
   - On Mac: press **Option + Command + Esc**, select your browser, and click **Force Quit**.
   - On iOS / Android: swipe up or use the app switcher to close the browser app entirely.
2. **Temporarily disconnect from the internet.**
   - Turn off Wi-Fi or unplug your Ethernet cable for a few minutes.
   - This prevents the malicious script from attempting to reload or communicate with external servers while you clean up.
3. **Remove any unauthorized browser permissions or notifications.**
   - Go into your browser settings and check the "Notifications" and "Pop-ups and redirects" sections.
   - Remove any websites you do not recognize that have "Allow" permissions.
4. **Run a full system scan with trusted security software.**
   - Use Windows Security / Microsoft Defender, Apple's built-in tools, or a reputable third-party anti-malware program.
   - Follow the software's instructions to remove any identified threats and restart your device if prompted.
5. **Contact your bank if you shared financial information or paid money.**
   - Use a different, known-safe device to find your bank's official contact number (e.g., from the back of your card).
   - Inform them that you have been targeted by a tech support scam and follow their instructions to secure your accounts.
6. **Report the incident to the appropriate authorities.**
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** report to **Report Fraud** (the successor to Action Fraud) online at reportfraud.police.uk or call **0300 123 2040**.
   - **Scotland:** report to **Police Scotland** by calling **101**.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide immediately whether to wipe your device or "factory reset" it.
- You don’t need to purchase a new computer or phone.
- You don’t need to change every password you own right now, unless you entered them into the scam pop-up or granted remote access.

## Important reassurance
These pop-ups are a common form of "scareware" designed to exploit fear and urgency. The appearance of the alert on your screen does not necessarily mean your files have been accessed or that a virus is currently active on your system. By closing the window and not interacting, you have likely prevented the actual attack from succeeding.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling deceptive pop-ups and initial actions to take if financial data was compromised. It does not cover deep-level forensic analysis or long-term cybersecurity auditing.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional IT security advice or legal counsel. If you granted remote access to your device, you should assume the system is compromised until it has been inspected by a qualified professional. Always use official contact methods for banks and government agencies.
