# What to do if… someone threatens to end the relationship unless you have sex (UK)

## Short answer
You do not owe anyone sexual contact to maintain a relationship, and consent given under the threat of a breakup is not free consent. Get to a safer physical distance, stop the interaction, and contact a specialist service for confidential guidance on your safety.

## Do not do these things
- Do not "just go along with it" to make the verbal threats or pressure stop if you do not want to participate.
- Do not attempt to negotiate or offer "alternatives" (e.g., "maybe tomorrow") if you feel afraid or coerced, as this can prolong the dangerous situation.
- Do not isolate yourself in a room where the exit is blocked or your phone is out of reach.
- Do not delete text messages, voice notes, or call logs that contain threats or ultimatums.
- Do not use a shared digital device or a monitored account to search for help if you believe your partner is tracking your activity.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause immediately by moving to a different space.** 
   - If you are in a room with the person, move to a bathroom with a lock, go outside, or move toward a public area or a neighbor.
   - If you can leave the premises safely, do so and go to a trusted friend’s house or a 24-hour public space like a cafe or supermarket.
2. **Call 999 if you are in immediate danger or if the person is becoming physically aggressive.** 
   - If you cannot speak, use the "Silent Solution" by pressing 55 when prompted by the operator.
   - If speaking would escalate the situation, keep the line open so the operator can hear what is happening.
3. **State one clear boundary and stop engaging in the conversation.** 
   - Use a simple phrase: "I am not having sex and I am stopping this conversation now."
   - You are not required to justify your decision or "argue" your way out of the ultimatum.
4. **Reach out to a trusted person to let them know you are unsafe or under pressure.** 
   - Ask them to stay on the phone with you while you leave or while you are in a separate room.
   - If you are trapped, ask them to call the police on your behalf.
5. **Contact specialist support services for confidential advice.** 
   - **Rape Crisis England & Wales 24/7 Support Line (16+): 0808 500 2222.** They provide support for anyone affected by sexual violence or coercion.
   - **National Domestic Abuse Helpline (Refuge): 0808 2000 247.** This is a 24-hour service for women experiencing any form of domestic abuse, including sexual coercion.
   - **Respect Men’s Advice Line: 0808 8010 327.** Support for men experiencing domestic abuse (Monday–Friday).
6. **Visit a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) if any non-consensual contact has occurred.** 
   - SARCs provide medical care, emergency contraception, and emotional support.
   - You can access a SARC without involving the police; they can collect and store evidence if you think you might want to report later.
7. **Use NHS 111 for urgent but non-emergency medical or sexual health advice.** 
   - They can direct you to the nearest clinic or SARC and advise on time-sensitive care like PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis) if relevant.
8. **Preserve digital records of any threats or ultimatums.** 
   - Screenshot messages or save voice notes and send them to a secure email address that the other person cannot access.
   - Do this only if it is safe to do so and does not involve interacting with the person.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you are ending the relationship permanently right this second.
- You do not need to decide if you are going to report the situation to the police.
- You do not need to provide a "better" or "more valid" reason for saying no to sex.
- You do not need to explain yourself to the other person or respond to their follow-up messages.

## Important reassurance
Threatening to leave someone to force them into sex is a form of coercion, and it is natural to feel panicked, confused, or "frozen" in response. Your reaction—whether you went quiet, tried to be "nice" to stay safe, or felt numb—is a common survival response and is not your fault. You are entitled to bodily autonomy regardless of your relationship status.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm reduction for the immediate crisis. Decisions regarding long-term safety planning, legal options, or relationship status should be made with the support of the specialist services listed above.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for legal advice or professional medical care. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
