# What to do if… you smell burning plastic or see scorching around an outlet or switch (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible electrical fire risk. Stop using that outlet or switch, turn off power to that circuit at the consumer unit if it is safe, and leave and call 999 if there is smoke, flame, active sparking, or you cannot safely isolate it.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep testing it by switching it on and off, plugging things back in, or repeatedly resetting a breaker.
- Don’t touch a hot faceplate, melted plastic, damaged plug, or exposed metal.
- Don’t remove the faceplate, loosen screws, or poke anything into or behind the outlet or switch.
- Don’t spray water on it or use wet cloths.
- Don’t attempt a DIY repair, including “just tightening” something.
- Don’t ignore a burning smell or scorch mark because it stopped briefly.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger.** If you see flames, sustained sparks, smoke, or the smell is getting stronger, get everyone out, close doors behind you if you can, and call 999.
2. **If it is safe, stop using the affected point.** Do not use the outlet, switch, plug, charger, appliance, or extension lead connected to it.
3. **Turn off the electricity to that circuit if you can do this safely.** Go to the consumer unit, sometimes called the fuse box, and switch off the relevant circuit, often labelled “sockets” or “lights”.
4. **If you are not sure which circuit it is, switch off the main switch if it is safe.** Do not touch the consumer unit if it smells burnt, feels hot, is smoking, or looks damaged.
5. **If a breaker or RCD trips again, leave it off.** Do not keep resetting it to see what happens.
6. **Unplug only if it is clearly safe.** If the plug, socket, switch, or cable is hot, melted, stuck, scorched, sparking, or crackling, leave it alone and keep the power off.
7. **If one plug-in item seems involved, keep it out of use.** If it is cool and safe to move, place it on a dry, non-combustible surface such as tile or a metal tray, away from paper, curtains, bedding, and other things that can burn.
8. **Clear the area without touching the damaged fitting.** Move paper, fabrics, chargers, and other loose items away from the outlet or switch if you can do this safely.
9. **Take a quick photo only after the immediate risk is controlled.** Photograph scorch marks, the damaged plug, and the consumer-unit switch you turned off; this can help an electrician or landlord.
10. **Arrange a registered or properly qualified electrician urgently.** Say there has been a burning plastic smell, heat, scorching, buzzing, crackling, tripping, or melting at an outlet or switch, and say whether the circuit is off.
11. **If you rent, report it as an urgent repair.** Use your landlord, agent, council, or housing association emergency repairs route if you have one, and say there are signs of electrical overheating and the circuit has been switched off.
12. **If the smell or heat seems to come from the consumer unit, meter cupboard, service head, or incoming supply equipment, do not touch it.** If there is smoke, heat, flame, or active sparking, leave and call 999. For a possible electricity network or supply-side problem with no immediate fire, call 105 in Great Britain; in Northern Ireland, contact NIE Networks on 03457 643 643.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause now.
- You do not need to decide about insurance, redecorating, replacement fittings, or long-term repairs now.
- You do not need to restore power to check whether it is fine. Keep the affected circuit off until it has been checked.

## Important reassurance
A burning plastic smell, scorch marks, buzzing, crackling, repeated tripping, or a hot socket or switch are sensible reasons to stop and make the area safe. Turning the power off and getting the fault checked is a protective first step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce fire and electric shock risk. Later decisions about repairs, replacement fittings, wiring checks, landlord responsibilities, insurance, or further inspection may need help from a qualified electrician, emergency services, your landlord, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, electrical, fire-safety, insurance, or professional advice. If you think there is a fire risk, visible smoke or flames, active sparking, or you feel unsafe, leave and call 999.
