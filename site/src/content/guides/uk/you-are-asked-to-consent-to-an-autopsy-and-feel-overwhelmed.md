# What to do if… you are asked to consent to an autopsy and feel overwhelmed (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before signing and ask whether this is a hospital post-mortem that needs consent, or a post-mortem ordered as part of an official death investigation. Ask who has authority to decide, what is being asked of you, and whether any urgent religious, cultural, family or funeral concerns can be recorded now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign a consent form just to stop a rushed conversation.
- Don’t assume “next of kin” automatically means you are the right person to consent to a hospital post-mortem.
- Don’t assume you can stop a coroner or Procurator Fiscal post-mortem by refusing consent.
- Don’t agree to optional tissue, organ, teaching or research permissions unless you understand them and want to agree.
- Don’t let several relatives give separate instructions if one family contact can safely handle the calls; it can cause confusion.
- Don’t finalise funeral timing until the body has been released or the relevant office has told you what can be arranged.

## What to do now

1. **Ask what kind of post-mortem this is.**  
   Say: “Is this a hospital post-mortem that needs consent, or is it being ordered as part of a coroner, Procurator Fiscal or official death investigation?”

2. **Ask exactly what decision is needed from you.**  
   Say: “What decision are you asking me to make, who is allowed to make it, and what happens if I need time to understand the forms?”

3. **Get the caller’s details and one contact route.**  
   Write down the person’s name, role, organisation, phone number, email if available, and any reference number. Ask: “Who is the single best contact for questions from the family?”

4. **If this is a hospital post-mortem, ask for the consent form and options before deciding.**  
   Say: “I am overwhelmed. Please send or read through the consent form and explain what is required, what is optional, and how much time I have.”  
   Ask whether the post-mortem can be limited to certain parts of the body, and whether any tissue or organs could be retained.

5. **If this is an official death investigation, record urgent concerns now.**  
   Say: “Please record that we have religious, cultural or family concerns about an invasive post-mortem and want to know whether a less invasive option, such as scanning or a limited examination, is possible in this case.”  
   Ask who decides this and how quickly they need the information.

6. **Ask about funeral and viewing timing without making final arrangements yet.**  
   Ask:
   - “Can we view them before the post-mortem, or only afterwards?”
   - “When is the post-mortem likely to happen?”
   - “When might the body be released to the funeral director?”
   - “Who will tell us when release has been authorised?”

7. **Ask about tissue, organs and samples in plain words.**  
   Say: “Could any tissue, organs or samples be kept after the post-mortem? If yes, what might be kept, why, who decides what happens next, and who will explain our choices?”

8. **If the death is not being investigated by a coroner or Procurator Fiscal in England or Wales, ask about the medical examiner.**  
   Say: “Can the medical examiner office speak to me about the death certification process and any concerns I have before registration?”

9. **Use a holding line if you are being pressed.**  
   Say: “I cannot make a safe decision while I am this overwhelmed. Please tell me the exact decision, the deadline, and who can explain the options. I will respond after that.”

## What can wait

- You do not need to decide now about teaching or research use unless that specific consent is being clearly explained and you want to decide.
- You do not need to understand the full medical detail today.
- You do not need to resolve every family disagreement immediately; choosing one temporary contact person is enough for now.
- You do not need to chase the final post-mortem report today; ask when and how results are usually shared.

## Important reassurance

It is normal to feel unable to think clearly when someone has died and you are suddenly handed a sensitive decision. You are allowed to slow the conversation down, ask what power you do or do not have, and have urgent concerns written down.

## Scope note

These are first steps only: they are to help you clarify what is being asked, avoid signing under pressure, and create one clear contact route. Later decisions may need help from the hospital bereavement team, the relevant death investigation office, a faith or cultural adviser, a funeral director, or specialist legal or medical advice.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic or other professional advice. Post-mortem processes vary across England and Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland, and by the circumstances of the death.
