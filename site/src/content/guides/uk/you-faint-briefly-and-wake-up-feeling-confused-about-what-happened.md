# What to do if… you faint briefly and wake up feeling confused about what happened (UK)

## Short answer
Lie down flat with your legs raised and stay still for several minutes to restore blood flow to the brain. Call **999** immediately if you have chest pain, a racing heart, a seizure, a head injury, or if you fainted while lying down or exercising.

## Do not do these things
- Don't stand up or walk around until you have been fully steady for at least 10 minutes.
- Don't drive a car, ride a bike, or operate machinery until you have been medically cleared.
- Don't ignore the episode even if you feel better now; all first-time or unexplained faints require a clinical assessment.
- Don't consume alcohol or caffeine immediately following the episode.
- Don't attempt to "test" your balance or strength by performing physical activities.

## What to do now
1. **Remain lying flat on your back and raise your legs above the level of your heart.** You can prop your feet up on a chair, sofa, or cushions to help blood return to your brain.
2. **Check yourself for immediate physical injuries or "red flag" symptoms.** 
   - Feel the back of your head for bumps or bleeding.
   - Check if you can move all four limbs and speak without slurring.
   - Note if you have any chest tightness or an irregular heartbeat.
3. **Call 999 immediately if you meet any emergency criteria.**
   - You fainted while exercising or while already lying down.
   - You have chest pain or heart palpitations (pounding / fluttering).
   - You experienced a seizure or jerking movements while unconscious.
   - You cannot be woken within 60 seconds of the faint.
   - You have new weakness on one side of your body or difficulty speaking.
4. **Contact NHS 111 or your GP for a same-day assessment if the faint was unexplained.** 
   - Even if you feel normal now, a medical professional must rule out underlying heart or neurological causes.
   - If you are alone, tell the 111 operator so they can advise on safe monitoring.
5. **Create a safe environment for your recovery over the next hour.**
   - Open a window for fresh air and loosen any restrictive clothing around your neck or waist.
   - If you are alone, unlock your front door and call a friend or neighbor to tell them what happened.
6. **Note down the specific circumstances of the faint for your doctor.**
   - Record what you were doing (e.g., standing for a long time, in a hot room, or experiencing pain).
   - List any "prodromal" symptoms you felt before passing out, such as nausea, sweating, or blurred vision.
   - Note how long you were unconscious and how long the post-faint confusion lasted.
7. **Verify your blood sugar levels if you have a known condition like diabetes.** Follow your prescribed protocol for "hypos" if your reading is low, but do not eat or drink until you are fully alert and sitting up safely.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the medical cause of the faint yourself.
- You do not need to notify the DVLA in the first few minutes, though you must stop driving until a doctor advises otherwise.
- You do not need to return to your normal schedule or chores for the remainder of the day.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel disoriented, tired, or slightly anxious after a brief loss of consciousness. Most simple faints resolve quickly once you are lying down with your legs raised, and the confusion usually fades as blood flow stabilizes.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a brief fainting episode. It does not replace a clinical diagnosis or the need for professional cardiovascular and neurological screening.

## Important note
Fainting (syncope) can sometimes indicate a serious underlying condition. If you are pregnant, over 65, or have a history of heart disease, you should seek medical attention urgently regardless of how minor the faint seemed.
