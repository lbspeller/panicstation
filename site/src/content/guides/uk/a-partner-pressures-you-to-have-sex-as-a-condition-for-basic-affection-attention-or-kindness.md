# What to do if… a partner pressures you to have sex as a condition for basic affection, attention, or kindness (UK)

## Short answer
You do not owe sexual activity in exchange for basic kindness, affection, or attention. Move to a safe pause away from the partner and contact a specialist support service to discuss your options in confidence.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel you must "give in" to stop the partner from being mean, silent, or angry.
- Do not engage in a debate about your reasons for saying no; you are allowed to say no without justification.
- Do not confront the partner if you feel they may become physically aggressive or block your exit.
- Do not assume this behavior is normal or acceptable just because you are in a long-term relationship or marriage.
- Do not delete evidence of digital pressure (texts or messages) until you have spoken with support, if it is safe to keep them.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause by moving to a different physical space immediately.** 
   - Move to a room with a lock or a clear exit route.
   - Pick up your phone, keys, and a coat if you are able to do so quickly.
   - If the situation feels physically threatening, leave the building and go to a public place.
2. **Observe the situation for safety cues that indicate you should leave.**
   - Listen for aggressive tones, shouting, or "the silent treatment" meant to punish you.
   - Notice if they are physically standing between you and the door.
   - If you feel a "knot" in your stomach or a sudden urge to flee, trust that instinct.
3. **Use a brief, non-negotiable statement to set a boundary.** 
   - State: "I am not having sex right now." 
   - Do not offer excuses (e.g., "I'm tired") as this may encourage them to try to solve the "problem" to get sex.
4. **Make contact with a trusted person or a support service right now.** 
   - Text a friend to ask for a "check-in" call.
   - Use a "safe word" or code message if you have one pre-arranged.
5. **Call emergency services if you are in immediate danger or fear for your safety.** 
   - Dial **999** from any phone. 
   - If you cannot speak, dial 999 and then press **55** when prompted (Silent Solution).
6. **Connect with the relevant specialist helpline for your area.**
   - **England & Wales (16+):** Call the 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line free on **0808 500 2222**.
   - **Scotland:** Call the Rape Crisis Scotland helpline on **08088 01 03 02** (5pm–midnight daily) or the NHS SARC self-referral line on **0800 148 88 88** (24/7).
   - **Northern Ireland:** Call the 24-hour Domestic & Sexual Violence Helpline on **0808 802 1414** or contact The Rowan (SARC) on **0800 389 4424**.
7. **Consider visiting a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) for medical or forensic support.**
   - You can usually self-refer to a SARC without involving the police. 
   - They provide healthcare, emergency contraception, and can store evidence if you are unsure about reporting.
8. **Record what happened in a secure, private way if it is safe to do so.** 
   - Write down the date, time, and what was specifically said or done. 
   - Keep this note in a "cloud" account or a hidden folder that the partner cannot access.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to end the relationship tonight.
- You do not need to decide if you want to report the coercion to the police.
- You do not need to label the experience as "abuse" or "assault" to be eligible for support.
- You do not need to explain your situation to friends or family if you are not ready.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel guilty, confused, or like you are "overreacting" when a partner uses affection as a bargaining chip. Feeling like you have to "fawn" (act overly nice or give in) to keep the peace is a standard survival response. You are not at fault for your partner's choice to use pressure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety information. It does not cover long-term safety planning, legal advice regarding domestic abuse orders, or therapeutic recovery.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or medical advice. If you are in immediate danger, always call 999. Specialists at the helplines listed can provide tailored advice for your specific situation.
