# What to do if… someone you blocked keeps creating new accounts to contact you (UK)

## Short answer
Stop engaging, save enough of what is happening to show the pattern, and tighten your privacy settings. If you or someone else is in immediate danger, call 999; if it is not an emergency but you feel unsafe or threatened, contact police on 101 or through your local police force’s online reporting.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply to explain, argue, plead, or prove a point.
- Do not agree to meet them or move the conversation to a more private channel.
- Do not publicly call them out while you are frightened or unsure what they can see.
- Do not delete messages or account details before saving the key information, unless keeping them puts you at more risk.
- Do not wait until your evidence is perfect before asking for help or reporting.
- Do not share a new phone number, handle, email address, or location with anyone who might pass it on.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause first.** If you think they are nearby, know where you are, are threatening to come to you, or someone is in immediate danger, call 999. If it is not an emergency but you feel unsafe, contact police on 101 or use your local police force’s online reporting route.

2. **Save the pattern, then stop reading.**
   - Save the messages and the account profile showing the username, handle, date, time, and any threats.
   - Save profile links, message links, call logs, texts, emails, and report confirmation emails where available.
   - Some apps may notify the other person if you screenshot certain content. If you are unsure, consider photographing the screen with another device or using the platform’s download or export tools.
   - Keep a simple note with the date, time, platform, account name, what happened, and whether it made you feel afraid or watched.
   - Keep the copies somewhere they cannot access, such as a separate cloud folder, a safer email account, or with a trusted person.

3. **Tighten the accounts they are using to reach you.**
   - Set social profiles to private.
   - Remove unknown followers, friends, and recent accounts you do not trust.
   - Restrict who can message you, tag you, comment on posts, or send message requests.
   - Turn off “find me by phone number or email” and contact syncing where the platform allows it.
   - Hide or limit follower lists, friends lists, story views, and activity status where possible.
   - Turn off location sharing and remove recent location tags if they could reveal where you are.

4. **Protect your login access.**
   - Turn on two-step verification or two-factor authentication for email and social media accounts.
   - Change passwords if there is any chance they know them.
   - Use strong, separate passwords, especially for your email account.
   - If you think they may have access to your device or accounts, make password changes from a safer device first.

5. **Report each new account on the platform.**
   - Use the in-app report route for harassment, stalking, impersonation, threats, or block-evasion where those options exist.
   - In the report, say you blocked the person and they are returning through new accounts.
   - Save report confirmations so the repeated pattern is easier to show later.

6. **Tell one trusted person today.**
   - Send them a brief summary and ask them not to contact the person or post about it.
   - Share the evidence folder or your incident note if that feels safe.
   - Agree one simple check-in phrase, such as “please call me now”, so you do not have to explain while panicked.

7. **Report the pattern if it is ongoing, escalating, threatening, or making you afraid.**
   - Tell police it is repeated unwanted contact after blocking, from new accounts, and that it is making you feel unsafe.
   - Say whether there are threats, impersonation, sexual content, doxxing, location references, or attempts to contact your friends, family, workplace, or school.
   - Ask for an incident or crime reference number and how to add further messages later.
   - Ask whether the pattern may be treated as harassment or stalking and what immediate protective options may be available.

8. **Use specialist support without making a big decision.**
   - Contact the National Stalking Helpline if you want help thinking through what to save, what to report, and how to reduce contact routes.
   - You do not have to decide now whether to change your number, delete accounts, or take formal legal steps.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to delete your accounts, change your phone number, or change every username.
- Writing a perfect statement or organising every screenshot.
- Deciding whether to pursue a court process or formal order.
- Telling lots of people, posting publicly, or trying to prove the whole history at once.
- Reading every message in detail once the key evidence is saved.

## Important reassurance
You are not overreacting because blocking did not stop it. Repeated unwanted contact from new accounts can be frightening, and it is reasonable to focus on safety, privacy, and a clear record rather than politeness.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the moment, reduce contact routes, preserve the basic pattern, and get appropriate help. Later decisions may need specialist stalking support, police guidance, legal advice, workplace or school safeguarding support, or cyber-security help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, cyber-security, or professional advice. If you or someone else is in immediate danger, call 999. If it is not an emergency but you feel unsafe or threatened, contact police on 101 or through your local police force’s online reporting.
