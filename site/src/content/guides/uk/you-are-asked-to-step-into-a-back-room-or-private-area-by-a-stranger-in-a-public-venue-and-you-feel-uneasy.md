# What to do if… you are asked to step into a back room or private area by a stranger in a public venue and you feel uneasy (UK)

## Short answer
Stop moving toward the private area immediately and move to a crowded space or a staff member. Tell a worker you feel unsafe and need help staying in public.

## Do not do these things
- Don't follow the person into any corridor, office, store cupboard, stairwell, or "quiet spot" outside.
- Don't prioritize politeness over your physical safety or worry about "making a scene."
- Don't hand over your personal belongings, such as your phone, ID, or drink, to the stranger.
- Don't allow the stranger to stand between you and the nearest public exit.
- Don't accept any drink or item that was not prepared or opened in your direct line of sight.
- Don't leave the venue alone if you suspect the person may be waiting outside.

## What to do now
1. **Immediately stop walking toward the restricted or private area.** Use a clear, firm statement such as "I am staying here" or "I am not going into that room."
2. **Move directly to a staff member or a highly visible public area.** Locate a bartender, security guard, or reception desk and state clearly: "I need assistance; a stranger is trying to isolate me and I do not feel safe."
3. **Use the "Ask for Angela" code phrase if the venue displays the official posters.** **Ask a staff member for "Angela" to signal that you are in a vulnerable situation and require a discreet intervention.**
   - Note: If staff do not appear to recognize the phrase, immediately switch to plain language to explain your safety concern.
4. **Identify a "witness group" and stand near them.** **If you cannot find staff immediately, stand near a large group of people and tell someone: "I am uncomfortable with that person; can I stand with you for a moment?"**
5. **Request specific safety actions from the venue staff.** 
   - Ask them to monitor the individual and keep them away from you.
   - Ask for a safe escort to a taxi or a pre-arranged pickup point.
   - Ask them to call the police if the person refuses to leave you alone.
6. **Call 999 if you believe you are in immediate danger or the person is escalating.** 
   - **If you are on a mobile and cannot speak, listen to the automated prompt and press 55 to signal an emergency.**
   - If you are on a landline, the operator will automatically see your location; stay on the line even if you cannot talk.
7. **Request that the venue preserves any CCTV footage of the interaction.** **Inform the manager as soon as it is safe that an incident occurred and that the footage may be required for a police report.**
   - Be aware that many digital recording systems overwrite data within 24 to 48 hours.
8. **Stay inside the venue until your safe transport has arrived.** **Ask a member of staff or security to watch you enter your vehicle to ensure you are not followed.**

## What can wait
- Deciding whether you want to file a formal criminal complaint.
- Contacting the venue's head office or management for a follow-up.
- Reviewing the incident to "check" if you overreacted; safety is the priority regardless of intent.
- Providing a detailed written statement if you are still feeling overwhelmed.

## Important reassurance
Internal feelings of unease or "gut instincts" are valid safety cues in social environments. Legitimate venue staff or safe individuals will never pressure you to enter a private area against your will or react with anger to a refusal.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for avoiding physical isolation in a public venue. It does not cover long-term legal reporting or the management of ongoing harassment.

## Important note
This information is for immediate safety stabilization and does not constitute legal advice. If a crime has been committed or is in progress, contact the police immediately via 999.
