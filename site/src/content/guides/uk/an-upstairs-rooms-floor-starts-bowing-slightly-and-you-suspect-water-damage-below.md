# What to do if… an upstairs room’s floor starts bowing slightly and you suspect water damage below (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all foot traffic in the room and clear the area immediately below the bowing floorboards. Isolate the water supply if an active leak is suspected and seek a professional structural assessment.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t walk on the affected area to "test" its strength or check if it still feels spongy.
- Don’t move heavy furniture across the bowed section, as the increased load could trigger a collapse.
- Don’t drill holes or cut into the floorboards or ceiling while the structural integrity is unverified.
- Don’t use electrical switches, sockets, or light fittings in the room or the area directly beneath it.
- Don’t attempt to prop the floor or ceiling with makeshift supports or "acrow" props without professional guidance.

## What to do now
1. **Clear the area and restrict access immediately.** Keep everyone, including pets, out of the room and off the bowed section of the floor. 
2. **Remove people from the room directly underneath the bowed area.** A bowing floor above often indicates that the ceiling below is at risk of partial or total collapse.
3. **Isolate the water supply if you suspect an active leak.**
   - Turn off the **stopcock** (main water valve) to stop the flow of pressurized supply pipes.
   - Note that turning off the stopcock will not stop leaks coming from "waste" pipes (bath, shower, or toilet drainage); for these, simply stop using all plumbing fixtures in the house.
4. **Isolate electrical circuits for the affected area at the consumer unit.** **Switch off the individual circuit breakers (MCBs) labelled for the "Upstairs Lights" or "Upstairs Sockets" if the consumer unit is dry and safe to touch.**
   - If you cannot identify the correct circuit, use the **Main Switch** (usually a large red switch) to turn off all power to the property.
5. **Look for sensory signs of escalating danger from a safe distance.** Watch for visible water dripping, a distinct "musty" or "earthy" smell (indicating rot), or audible "popping" or "creaking" sounds from the timber. 
6. **Contact a qualified professional for an urgent inspection.** 
   - Call an **emergency plumber** if there is active water ingress.
   - Call a **RICS-regulated structural engineer** or a reputable builder if the floor remains bowed after the water is off.
7. **Notify your landlord or building manager immediately if you do not own the property.** **Provide a written record of the time you noticed the bowing and any actions you have taken to mitigate the risk.**
8. **Contact your local council's Building Control department if the structure appears imminently dangerous.** Most UK councils have a 24-hour "Dangerous Structures" team that can assess if a building is a risk to its occupants or the public.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the exact cause of the damage (e.g., wet rot vs. a pipe burst) before professional help arrives.
- You do not need to begin drying out the property or lifting floorboards yourself.
- You do not need to contact your insurance company in the first few minutes; stabilization of the structure and water isolation are the priorities.

## Important reassurance
Bowing floors are often caused by long-term, slow leaks that saturate timber joists, causing them to "creep" or bend. While this requires professional repair, the risk of sudden collapse is significantly reduced once you remove the weight (people and furniture) and stop the source of the water.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. It does not replace a professional structural survey or electrical inspection. Long-term repairs will require adherence to UK Building Regulations (Part A for structure).

## Important note
This guide provides general safety information and is not professional engineering, plumbing, or legal advice. If you hear loud cracking or see the floor actively sinking, evacuate the building immediately and call the emergency services.
