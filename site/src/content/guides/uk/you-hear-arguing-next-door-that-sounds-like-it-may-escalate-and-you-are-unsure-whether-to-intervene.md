# What to do if… you hear arguing next door that sounds like it may escalate and you are unsure whether to intervene (UK)

## Short answer
Do not intervene physically or confront the individuals. If you believe someone is in immediate danger or a crime is occurring, call 999 from a safe, quiet location and report exactly what you hear.

## Do not do these things
- Don't knock on the door, shout through the wall, or enter the hallway to intervene yourself.
- Don't threaten to call the police or announce your intention to report the incident.
- Don't attempt to record the argument by placing devices against shared walls or looking through letterboxes.
- Don't wait to see if the situation "calms down" if you hear sounds of physical assault, breaking glass, or calls for help.
- Don't share details of the incident or your concerns on local social media groups or neighborhood chats.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a location where you cannot be heard and secure your door.** Place physical distance between yourself and the shared wall or hallway to ensure you can speak to emergency services without being overheard.
2. **Identify specific emergency triggers to report.** Listen for indicators of immediate risk that require a priority response:
   - Screams for help or sounds of a struggle.
   - Thuds, crashing, or the sound of objects being thrown.
   - Threats of serious harm or mention of weapons.
   - Sounds of a child or vulnerable person in distress.
3. **Call 999 if the situation is active or dangerous.** State clearly that you are a neighbor and provide the specific flat number or address where the noise is originating.
   - Provide your location and clarify if you wish to remain anonymous to the neighbors.
   - Describe the noises (e.g., "screaming," "repeated banging") rather than making assumptions about the relationship.
4. **Use silent procedures if you cannot speak safely.** If you are in a position where speaking aloud would alert the neighbors to your call, use these protocols:
   - **On a mobile phone:** Listen to the operator. If prompted, cough or tap the handset. If you still cannot speak, press 55 when the automated system asks you to. This "Silent Solution" confirms the emergency is genuine and routes the call to police.
   - **On a landline:** Stay on the line. Landlines provide automatic location data. If the operator hears suspicious background noise and you do not speak, they will transfer the call to a police handler.
   - **Emergency SMS:** If you have pre-registered with the emergencySMS service, you can text 999.
5. **Call 101 if the argument has ended but you have ongoing concerns.** Use the non-emergency line to report the incident for a welfare check.
   - Explain that you are concerned for a neighbor's safety following an earlier disturbance.
   - Provide a timeline of how long the argument lasted and if it is a recurring issue.
6. **Note down the details while they are fresh.** Write a brief, factual account of what you heard and at what time.
   - Use direct observations (e.g., "Heard female voice shouting 'No' at 21:15, followed by heavy thud").
   - Keep this note for your own records in case you are asked for a statement later.
7. **Maintain a neutral distance if you see the neighbors later.** If you encounter a neighbor you are concerned about in a safe, public space, a simple, non-pressured check-in is sufficient.
   - Do not demand an explanation or insist they report the incident if they are not ready.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause of the argument or who started it.
- You do not need to decide if the situation qualifies as domestic abuse; the police will make that assessment.
- You do not need to gather evidence for the police by filming or investigating.
- You do not need to contact the landlord or housing association during the crisis unless there is a physical building emergency (e.g., fire or flood).

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel hesitant or worry about "interfering" in private matters. Reporting a concern for a neighbor's safety is a responsible action, and emergency operators are trained to handle calls where the caller is unsure of the severity.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for neighbors witnessing a potential crisis. It does not cover long-term legal options, housing disputes, or witness protection procedures.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. In any situation involving a threat to life or property, always contact the emergency services immediately.
