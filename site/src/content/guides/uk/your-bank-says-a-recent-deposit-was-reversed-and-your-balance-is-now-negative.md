# What to do if… your bank says a recent deposit was reversed and your balance is now negative (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate your account from further fees by stopping scheduled payments and call your bank immediately via the 159 hotline or the number on your card to establish the specific reason for the reversal.

## Do not do these things
- Don't send any "refund" or "transfer" to the original payer or any third party until the bank has completed a full fraud investigation.
- Don't assume available funds are cleared funds; UK banking systems can reverse certain deposits (like cheques) several days after they appear in your balance.
- Don't attempt to "test" the account by making small purchases or withdrawals to see if they work.
- Don't ignore the situation; unarranged overdrafts in the UK can trigger high daily interest and negatively impact your credit file within days.
- Don't use contact details provided in a text or email notification about the reversal.

## What to do now
1. **Secure a verified line to your bank's fraud or customer service department.**
   - **Dial 159 from your phone to be connected securely to most UK retail banks.**
   - Alternatively, use the specific number printed on the back of your physical debit card or the official "Contact Us" section within your banking app.

2. **Obtain the specific reversal "Reason Code" and transaction details.**
   - **Ask the representative for the exact reason the funds were removed.**
      - Examples: "Cheque returned unpaid," "Duplicate payment corrected," "Payer bank recall," or "Instruction from Public Authority."
   - **Request the date the original deposit was made and the date the reversal was processed.**
   - **Ask if your account is under a "protective block" or if you have full access to remaining funds.**

3. **Isolate your account to prevent a "fee cascade."**
   - **Review your pending Direct Debits and Standing Orders for the next 48 hours.**
   - **Cancel non-essential subscriptions or scheduled transfers immediately if you do not have an agreed overdraft to cover them.**
   - **Ask the bank to "pause" unarranged overdraft fees for 24 hours while you investigate the source of the reversal.**

4. **Document the current state of your digital banking records.**
   - **Take screenshots of the transaction history showing the deposit and the subsequent reversal.**
   - **Save copies of any digital deposit receipts or ATM "deposit accepted" slips.**
   - **Log the time, date, and name of the bank staff member you spoke with.**

5. **Report suspected scams to the national reporting centre.**
   - **If the reversal involved a cheque from a stranger or an "overpayment," report it to Report Fraud (formerly Action Fraud).**
   - **Use the online portal at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.**
   - **Obtain a crime reference number if a report is filed.**

6. **Initiate a formal complaint if the reversal appears to be a bank error.**
   - **Submit a formal complaint through the bank's internal process and state that it involves a "payment service" issue.**
   - **Note that for payment service complaints, the bank must provide a final response within 15 business days.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle the full negative balance today if you are disputing the reversal.
- You do not need to contact the Financial Ombudsman Service yet; you must allow the bank's internal complaint window to close first.
- You do not need to close your account or switch banks immediately, as this may complicate the investigation.

## Important reassurance
A negative balance due to a reversal is a high-pressure situation, but it is a procedural event that banks handle daily. Reversals can occur due to simple processing errors, bank-side duplicate corrections, or standard security protocols. Following the steps to isolate the account will stop the immediate accumulation of fees while the underlying cause is identified.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a financial emergency in the UK. It focuses on harm reduction and fee prevention. It does not provide legal advice regarding debt liability or long-term credit repair.

## Important note
Banking regulations and clearing cycles are subject to change. This information is based on UK protocols as of 2026. If you are unable to afford essentials like food or heating due to a negative balance, contact your local Citizens Advice or a debt charity like StepChange for emergency assistance.
