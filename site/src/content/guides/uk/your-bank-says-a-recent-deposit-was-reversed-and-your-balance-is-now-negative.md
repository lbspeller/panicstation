# What to do if… your bank says a recent deposit was reversed and your balance is now negative (UK)

## Short answer
Stop non-essential spending, do not send money back to anyone pressuring you, and contact your bank using a trusted route from your card, app, or the bank’s official website. Ask exactly which deposit was reversed and what you can do now to stop further harm.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the money was safely yours just because it once showed as available.
- Don’t send money back to the payer, a buyer, an employer, or anyone else until your bank explains what happened.
- Don’t use a phone number or link from a text, email, social message, or advert about the reversal.
- Don’t keep spending as normal while the account is negative.
- Don’t ignore direct debits, standing orders, rent, council tax, utilities, or subscriptions that may now fail.
- Don’t delete messages, receipts, bank notifications, or call notes.

## What to do now

1. **Freeze the damage for the next day or two.**
   - Check your balance, available balance, overdraft position, and pending transactions in your banking app.
   - Check direct debits, standing orders, card subscriptions, and scheduled payments due soon.
   - Pause or cancel only non-essential payments where it is safe to do so.
   - For essentials such as rent, council tax, utilities, insurance, or phone service, contact the payee before the payment fails if you can.

2. **Contact your bank through a trusted route.**
   Ask the bank to tell you:
   - which deposit was reversed, including the date, amount, reference, and deposit method;
   - the reason or description the bank can share, such as unpaid cheque, returned payment, duplicate entry, fraud check, or account restriction;
   - whether your account, card, app, or outgoing payments are restricted;
   - what action they need from you now;
   - whether they can send a secure message or written note confirming what they have told you.

3. **Ask the bank to reduce immediate knock-on harm.**
   - Ask what interest, charges, declined-payment effects, or account restrictions may apply while the account is negative.
   - Ask whether any pending outgoing payments can still be stopped safely.
   - Ask whether they can pause, waive, or refund any charges while the reversal is being checked.
   - Ask what you should do if you cannot bring the account back above zero immediately.

4. **Save the evidence before it disappears.**
   - Screenshot or download the balance before and after the reversal if visible.
   - Save the reversal entry, deposit entry, receipt, cheque image or paying-in confirmation, and any bank messages.
   - Save messages from the payer, buyer, employer, marketplace, or anyone asking you to move or return money.
   - Write down call dates, times, names, and what was agreed.

5. **Check the payer without using details from a suspicious message.**
   - If the deposit was wages, benefits, a refund, sale proceeds, or money from someone you know, contact them using a number, portal, or email address you already trust.
   - Ask whether they know why the payment was returned, cancelled, duplicated, or disputed.
   - Do not accept pressure to send money elsewhere while the bank is still explaining the reversal.

6. **Treat it as a possible scam if the story involves overpayment or forwarding money.**
   Red flags include a stranger’s cheque, a buyer paying too much, a request to refund part of the deposit, pressure to act fast, or someone telling you to ignore the bank.
   - Tell your bank you are worried about fraud or a scam.
   - Follow the bank’s security steps before moving any money.
   - If your card, cheque book, banking app, or account details may have been misused, report that to your bank immediately.
   - You can also report fraud or attempted fraud through Report Fraud, the UK police reporting route for fraud and cyber crime.

7. **Make a complaint if the bank will not explain or you think it has made a mistake.**
   - Use the bank’s formal complaints process and ask for a written response.
   - Include the deposit date, amount, reversal date, what the bank told you, and what harm the negative balance has caused.
   - For many payment-service complaints the bank usually has 15 days to reply; for most other financial complaints it usually has up to 8 weeks.
   - If you receive a final response you disagree with, or the bank does not reply within the relevant time, you can usually ask the Financial Ombudsman Service to look at it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to close the account, switch banks, start legal action, or agree to a long repayment plan.
- You do not need to argue with the payer before you have the bank’s explanation.
- You do not need to decide who is at fault until you know whether this was an unpaid cheque, returned payment, duplicate entry, fraud concern, bank error, or account restriction.

## Important reassurance
A reversed deposit and sudden negative balance can feel alarming, but the first job is not to solve everything at once. Slow the situation down, stop extra damage, get the bank’s explanation in writing, and keep evidence of what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and reduce avoidable harm. Later decisions may need help from your bank, the payer, a debt adviser, the Financial Ombudsman Service, or another specialist service depending on what caused the reversal.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, fraud-investigation, or professional advice. Bank processes, account terms, and payment types vary, so use your bank’s secure channels and official UK reporting or complaint routes for your specific case.
