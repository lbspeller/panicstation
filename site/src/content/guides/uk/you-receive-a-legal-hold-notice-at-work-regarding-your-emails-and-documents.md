# What to do if… you receive a legal hold notice at work regarding your emails and documents (UK)

## Short answer
Stop deleting, editing, moving, or “tidying” anything that could relate to the matter. Follow the hold notice, and ask the named contact whether IT or records staff can apply any available technical preservation to the relevant accounts, folders, systems, or devices.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete, tidy up, rename to hide, edit, or overwrite anything related to the matter.
- Do not empty Deleted Items, Junk, chat histories, downloads, old versions, or local folders if they could contain relevant material.
- Do not move items into personal email, USB sticks, screenshots, private cloud storage, or another account for “safekeeping”.
- Do not selectively preserve only what seems important, helpful, or unhelpful.
- Do not forward the hold notice widely or discuss the matter in group chats; keep questions to the named contact.
- Do not create new recap documents, timelines, commentary, or explanations unless the named contact asks for them.
- Do not assume backups mean deletion is safe; routine retention, auto-delete settings, or device changes may still remove material.

## What to do now
1. **Pause deletion and clean-up immediately.**  
   Stop deleting emails, clearing chat history, emptying folders, shredding paper notes, overwriting drafts, or removing old versions that could relate to the topic.

2. **Stop any auto-delete behaviour you control.**  
   If you can safely do so, pause mailbox clean-up rules, auto-archive rules, chat auto-delete settings, scripts that purge folders, and routines that clear downloads or temporary folders. If you are not sure how to do this, do not experiment; tell IT or the named contact.

3. **Read the hold notice once and identify the essentials.**  
   Note only what you need to comply or ask questions: the matter name or reference, date range, topics, people, projects, systems mentioned, and the named contact. Keep this factual and do not add opinions or a narrative.

4. **Acknowledge receipt if the notice asks you to.**  
   Reply as soon as you reasonably can. Keep it short: confirm you have received the notice, say you will preserve potentially relevant material, and ask what to do if any instruction is unclear.

5. **Ask about technical preservation.**  
   Ask whether IT, legal, HR, records, or the data protection officer will place a hold on your mailbox, Teams or Slack account, SharePoint or OneDrive areas, shared drives, work phone, laptop, or other relevant systems.

6. **Flag anything at risk of routine deletion or change.**  
   Tell the named contact about mailbox quota pressure, chat retention settings, planned laptop or phone replacement, offboarding steps, shared folders you manage, scheduled deletion jobs, paper files due for disposal, or project tools with short retention periods.

7. **List where relevant material could realistically be located.**  
   Make a simple inventory for the named contact: Outlook folders, Teams or Slack messages, shared drives, SharePoint, OneDrive, local laptop folders, work phone messages, work WhatsApp messages if used, notes apps used for work, calendar items, attachments, and paper notebooks. This is not a prompt to export or copy the material yourself.

8. **If personal devices or accounts were used for work on this topic, disclose that before moving anything.**  
   Say plainly what exists and where, for example that some project messages are on a personal phone. Do not self-export, screenshot, forward, back up, or delete that content unless the named contact gives you an approved method.

9. **Keep new communications factual and necessary.**  
   Use the designated contact and normal work channels. Avoid speculation, jokes, side commentary, or fresh summaries unless you are asked for them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide what is legally relevant right now; preserve first and let the official process sort scope later.
- You do not need to collect, export, print, or send documents unless the named contact tells you how.
- You do not need to write a detailed account of events unless the appropriate internal contact asks for one.
- You do not need to contact clients, regulators, external lawyers, or other outside parties yourself unless your employer instructs you to.
- You do not need to solve retention, privacy, or personal-device questions alone; raise them before changing where data lives.

## Important reassurance
A legal hold can feel serious or personal, but it is often a routine preservation step when a dispute, claim, inquiry, investigation, or possible litigation may involve documents. The safest first response is simple: stop deletion, avoid moving data off-system, and use the named contact so preservation is coordinated.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to prevent accidental deletion, alteration, or confusion. Later decisions about disclosure, relevance, privilege, data protection, employment issues, or personal devices may need help from your employer’s legal, HR, IT, records, or data protection teams, or from specialist advice.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, employment, data protection, IT, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are unsure about scope, personal-device involvement, retention conflicts, or anything that would change where data is stored, ask the named contact before acting.
