# What to do if… you are pressured to agree to sexual acts you do not want as a “compromise” to avoid conflict (UK)

## Short answer
You do not need to “compromise” on sexual acts to prevent an argument or stop someone from being angry. Create physical distance immediately and contact a specialist support line to speak with someone trained in sexual coercion.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to sexual acts to stop someone from being sulky, persistent, or aggressive.
- Do not try to negotiate or "meet in the middle" regarding your physical boundaries while you feel pressured or scared.
- Do not accept the idea that you "owe" sex as a way to fix a relationship problem or end a conflict.
- Do not let anyone prevent you from leaving a room, using your phone, or contacting help.
- Do not judge yourself for "going along with it" or freezing—these are common survival responses to pressure.

## What to do now
1. **Create an immediate physical pause.** Use a direct statement such as "I am not doing this," or "I need to stop now," and move to a separate room, a locked bathroom, or outside the building.
2. **Contact emergency services if you are in immediate danger.** Dial **999** if you feel unsafe or are being prevented from leaving. 
   - If you are on a mobile and cannot speak, listen for the automated prompt to **press 55** (the Silent Solution) to let the operator know it is a genuine emergency.
3. **Get a trusted person "into the room" remotely.** Call or message a friend or family member and ask them to stay on the phone with you while you establish a safe distance or prepare to leave.
4. **Access the 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line for England and Wales.** Call **0808 500 2222** or use the online chat at **247sexualabusesupport.org.uk** to speak with a specialist.
   - This service is for anyone aged 16+ who has experienced sexual pressure, coercion, or assault, regardless of when it happened.
   - In Scotland, call Rape Crisis Scotland on **08088 01 03 02**.
   - In Northern Ireland, contact Nexus NI on **028 9032 6803**.
5. **Contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) for medical or forensic options.** You can find your nearest centre by searching "SARC" on the NHS website; these centres provide specialist care whether or not you choose to involve the police.
6. **Keep a record of the pressure if it is safe to do so.** Avoid deleting messages, call logs, or recordings that demonstrate the persistence or threats used against you, as these may be helpful if you decide to seek professional or legal help later.
7. **Prioritize your immediate physical comfort and safety over explanations.** You do not need to provide a "good enough" reason for stopping or leaving; your withdrawal of interest is sufficient.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to report this to the police right now.
- You do not need to determine the future of your relationship today.
- You do not need to find the "perfect" way to explain your boundaries to the other person.
- You do not need to gather evidence or build a case before seeking emotional support.

## Important reassurance
Pressure and persistence are not consent. If you said "yes" because you felt worn down, scared of the consequences, or just wanted the situation to end, that is not a failure of your character. It is a common response to being placed in an impossible situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only for individuals facing sexual pressure or coercion. Later decisions regarding safety planning, legal rights, or relationship status should be made with the guidance of specialist sexual violence advocates.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or medical advice. If you are experiencing a mental health crisis or feelings of despair, you can contact **Samaritans on 116 123** at any time.
