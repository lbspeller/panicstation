# What to do if… you are asked to pay a funeral home deposit immediately and you are unsure what is normal (UK)

## Short answer
Request the funeral director’s written, itemised estimate and their written terms of business before authorizing any payment. It is normal for a deposit to cover third-party costs (disbursements) like crematorium or cemetery fees, but you are entitled to see a full price breakdown first.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay a deposit immediately based only on a verbal quote or a "package" price.
- Do not feel obligated to pay in cash or via personal bank transfer if you feel pressured.
- Do not sign a contract or "terms of business" document until you have read the sections on deposits and cancellations.
- Do not agree to optional extras like floral tributes, limousines, or high-end coffins while feeling rushed.
- Do not assume that the first figure quoted is the final total; always check if it includes third-party fees.

## What to do now
1. **Request the funeral director's written estimate and payment terms immediately.** You should state: “I require a written estimate and a copy of your terms of business regarding deposits before I can proceed with payment.”
2. **Identify exactly what the requested deposit is intended to cover.** Ask specifically if the amount requested is for the funeral director's professional services or strictly for third-party disbursements such as doctor's fees, officiant fees, or crematorium costs.
3. **Verify the firm’s Standardised Price List against the Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) requirements.** Legally, all UK funeral directors must display this list in their window, inside their premises, and on their website within one click of the homepage.
4. **Compare the estimate provided against the firm’s Additional Options Price List.** Ensure that every item on your estimate matches the prices listed on their mandatory public price lists to check for unauthorized mark-ups.
5. **Contact one other local funeral director to ask for their deposit terms as a benchmark.** Ask: "What is your required deposit for a standard funeral, and when is it due?" to determine if the first request is within the local average.
6. **Disclose any financial concerns or applications for government support to the funeral director early.** If you are applying for a Funeral Expenses Payment or a Funeral Support Payment (Scotland), inform them now, as many firms have specific procedures for these cases.
7. **Use a traceable payment method such as a debit or credit card for any authorized deposit.** Paying via a business account provides a clear record of the transaction and may offer additional consumer protection compared to cash.
8. **Stop all interactions if you encounter high-pressure sales tactics or a refusal to provide documentation.** If you suspect fraudulent activity or if the firm is not complying with CMA price transparency rules, you can report them to the CMA or Action Fraud (0300 123 2040).
   - In Scotland, report suspicious financial requests to Police Scotland via 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on optional extras such as stationery, flowers, or catering right now.
- You do not need to finalize the choice of a headstone or permanent memorial today.
- You do not need to commit to the first funeral director you speak with if their terms are unclear.
- You do not need to resolve the entire distribution of the deceased's estate before addressing the immediate deposit request.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when asked for large sums of money shortly after a death. High-quality funeral directors expect clients to ask for written estimates and will not interpret your caution as a lack of respect for the deceased. Taking a few hours to review documents is a standard part of the process.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling immediate financial requests from funeral homes. It does not cover long-term probate, legal claims against an estate, or detailed eligibility for state benefits.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Funeral industry regulations and codes of practice may differ between England, Wales, Northern Ireland, and Scotland. Always insist on written documentation for any financial commitment.
