# What to do if… your personal contact details have been shared internally at work without your consent (UK)

## Short answer
Ask your employer, in writing, to stop further sharing, remove or restrict access where possible, and tell you who could see your personal contact details.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send an angry all-staff message or confront people in a heated way; it can escalate the situation and create more copies of your details.
- Do not delete emails, chats, screenshots, or system notices that show what happened.
- Do not assume it was allowed just because it was internal; internal sharing can still be inappropriate or excessive.
- Do not assume it was automatically unlawful only because you did not consent; ask what policy, reason, and access controls were used.
- Do not share extra personal details while explaining the problem; keep messages short and factual.
- If you feel unsafe, do not keep following normal routines without telling a manager, HR, security, or another trusted workplace contact.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause and record the basics.** Note what was shared, such as your mobile number, home address, or personal email; where it appeared; when you found out; and who could see it if you know. Save the evidence, such as an email, chat post, staff list, directory page, or HR system screenshot.

2. **Send a stop-and-contain message today.** Email your line manager and HR. If your workplace has a data protection officer or data protection contact, include them. Ask them to:
   - confirm exactly what personal contact details were shared,
   - confirm who could access them,
   - stop further distribution,
   - remove, retract, or limit access where possible,
   - delete unnecessary copies where possible,
   - restrict future access to people who genuinely need it for work, and
   - tell you what immediate safeguards are being put in place.

3. **Ask them to log and assess it.** Say plainly that you want it logged as a personal data incident and assessed as a possible personal data breach. Ask whether it is reportable to the ICO, whether you should receive a breach explanation, and what action they are taking to reduce risk to you.

4. **Tell them immediately if there is any safety impact.** If you are receiving unwanted calls, texts, visits, threats, or contact from someone you were trying to avoid, say this clearly. Ask for practical steps such as removing your address from staff lists, replacing your personal number with a work contact route, limiting rota or location visibility, and briefing reception or security if needed.

5. **Ask for a written answer to a short list.** Keep it limited to what you need now:
   - who had access,
   - how long the details were visible,
   - whether the details have been removed or restricted,
   - what copies may still exist,
   - who is responsible for fixing it, and
   - what to do if it happens again.

6. **Use a subject access request if you need a clearer picture.** You can ask for a copy of your personal information and information about how it is used, where it came from, and who it has been shared with. Keep the request focused on your contact details and the disclosure. Some information may be withheld or redacted where other people’s data is involved.

7. **If your employer does not act, raise it through the workplace problem or grievance route in writing.** Keep it factual: what happened, what evidence you have, the impact on you, what you asked for, and what response you received. Ask for practical remedies, such as restriction, deletion where possible, process changes, and reassurance that raising the issue will not be held against you.

8. **If there is a grievance meeting, ask to be accompanied if you want support.** In many formal grievance meetings, employees and workers have a right to bring a companion, usually a colleague or trade union representative. Ask in advance and name the person you want to bring.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, threaten legal action, or go public.
- You do not need to prove whether the sharing was malicious or accidental before asking for containment.
- You do not need to collect opinions from lots of colleagues; keep your circle small and your communications controlled.
- A complaint to the ICO can usually wait until you have raised the issue with your employer and gathered the key facts, unless there is urgent harm or safety risk and you need advice sooner.

## Important reassurance
Feeling exposed, angry, or unsafe after your personal contact details spread at work is understandable. You are not overreacting by asking for containment, access limits, and a clear written explanation.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce harm, stop further spread, and get the facts in writing. Later decisions may need specialist help, such as a trade union representative, Acas, the ICO, or legal advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, employment, data protection, safety, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Workplace policies and the best next step can vary by role, sector, contract, and risk level. If you feel at immediate risk of harm, prioritise personal safety and contact emergency services.
