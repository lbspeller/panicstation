# What to do if… you get a new severe neck pain and stiffness without an obvious injury (UK)

## Short answer
If the neck pain and stiffness are new and severe, call **999** or go to A&E immediately if you also have a fever, headache, light sensitivity, or feel generally unwell. Otherwise, contact **NHS 111** for an urgent same-day assessment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force your neck to move, "crack" it, or perform aggressive stretches to "loosen" the stiffness.
- Do not wear a neck collar unless a medical professional has specifically told you to do so.
- Do not wait for a rash to appear before seeking help if you feel seriously ill.
- Do not drive yourself to a clinic or hospital if you have a severe headache, dizziness, or blurred vision.
- Do not take more than the recommended dose of painkillers while waiting for advice.

## What to do now
1. **Find a neutral, supported position.** Lie on a firm surface with a single low pillow or sit in a chair that supports your head; avoid twisting or tilting your head.
2. **Check for immediate "Call 999" red flags.** Call **999** or go to A&E right now if you have a new severe stiff neck along with any of the following:
   - A high temperature (fever) or feeling shivery and very unwell.
   - A severe headache or a "worst ever" sudden headache.
   - Dislike of bright lights (photophobia).
   - Confusion, drowsiness, or difficulty being woken up.
   - A rash that does not fade when a glass is pressed against it.
   - Very cold hands and feet despite a fever, or mottled/pale skin.
   - Seizures or fits.
3. **Check for "Call 999" neurological signs.** Seek emergency help immediately if the neck pain is accompanied by:
   - Sudden weakness in your arms or legs or difficulty walking.
   - New facial droop or slurred speech.
   - Sudden loss of bladder or bowel control (new leaking or inability to go).
4. **Contact NHS 111 for urgent same-day advice if you have "nerve" cues.** If you do not have the emergency signs above but have these, seek an assessment **today**:
   - Shooting pains in your arms or legs that feel like electric shocks.
   - New pins and needles, numbness, or a "heavy" feeling in your arms or hands.
   - New clumsiness, such as dropping things or struggling with buttons.
5. **Prepare a summary for the clinician.** Note down exactly when the pain started, if you have had a recent infection (like a sore throat or flu), and any medications you have taken in the last 24 hours.
6. **Alert someone to check on you.** If you are alone, tell a neighbour or family member that you are seeking medical advice for a stiff neck and ask them to check back with you in 30 minutes.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to identify the exact cause (such as a muscle strain versus a disc issue) before seeking help.
- You do **not** need to book a physical therapy or massage appointment today.
- You do **not** need to apply high-intensity heat or ice until a clinician has ruled out serious infection.

## Important reassurance
Sudden neck stiffness is frequently caused by acute muscle spasms (torticollis) or minor nerve irritation, which usually resolve with basic care. However, because new severe stiffness can occasionally indicate a time-sensitive infection or neurological issue, getting an urgent professional assessment is the standard safety protocol.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions only. It does not replace a clinical examination or provide a diagnosis for the cause of the neck pain.

## Important note
This information is based on 2026 UK health guidelines. If your symptoms worsen rapidly or you feel "wrong" or significantly unwell, do not wait; contact emergency services immediately.
