# What to do if… someone is repeatedly sliding notes or objects under your door and you feel targeted (UK)

## Short answer
Treat repeated unwanted items as a safety risk: remain behind your locked door, document every incident, and report the pattern to the police. If you are in immediate danger or hear an active attempt to enter, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door to confront the individual or attempt to "catch" them.
- Do not follow anyone into communal corridors or stairwells to investigate.
- Do not engage in any communication, including writing back or sliding notes in return.
- Do not touch, move, or dispose of the items until you have recorded their position.
- Do not post images or details on public building forums or social groups.
- Do not assume the behaviour is harmless if it causes you distress; repetition is a key indicator of stalking.

## What to do now
1. **Secure your immediate space and move away from the door.** **Ensure your front door is locked and move to a room where you cannot be seen from the hallway or door aperture.** 
2. **Call 999 if there is an immediate threat to your safety.** **If you hear someone actively trying to open your door, if you are being threatened through the door, or if you feel unsafe to stay in the property, contact emergency services immediately.** 
   - If you are in danger but cannot speak, call 999 and press 55 when prompted (the Silent Solution).
   - Provide your exact address and flat number clearly.
3. **Document the incident using your phone.** **Take clear photographs of the note or object exactly where it lies on the floor before touching it.** 
   - Capture any marks on the door or carpet.
   - Record the exact date and time.
   - Note any sounds you heard, such as footsteps, whispering, or the sound of the letterbox.
4. **Collect and preserve the items as evidence.** **Using a clean bag or envelope, pick up the item and seal it without excessive handling to preserve potential forensic evidence.** 
   - Label the bag with the date and time found.
   - Store all collected items together in a safe, dry place.
5. **Report the pattern of behaviour to the police via 101.** **Contact the police non-emergency line or use their online reporting tool to log the incident as "repeated harassment" or "stalking".** 
   - Explicitly state that this is a "course of conduct" (two or more incidents).
   - Request an incident or crime reference number and record it.
6. **Notify your landlord or building management in writing.** **Inform the person responsible for the building that you are being targeted and request that they check communal entry systems.** 
   - Formally ask them to preserve any CCTV footage from the relevant timeframes.
   - Under UK GDPR, you have the right to submit a Subject Access Request (SAR) for footage of yourself if the management is reluctant to assist.
7. **Contact the National Stalking Helpline for specialist advice.** **Call 0808 802 0300 (run by the Suzy Lamplugh Trust) to speak with a trained advisor about safety planning and your rights.** 
   - They can help you identify if the behaviour fits a wider stalking typology.
   - They provide guidance on how to communicate effectively with the police.
8. **Establish a check-in system with a trusted contact.** **Inform a friend, family member, or neighbour about what is happening and agree on a time to message them each day to confirm you are safe.** 
9. **Review your physical door security without making unauthorised modifications.** **Check that your door closes fully and that any communal lights are functioning, but do not install cameras or door strips without written permission if you live in a flat with fire-rated doors.** 

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the person or their motivation right now.
- You do not need to gather a specific amount of "proof" before making your first police report.
- You do not need to decide on long-term actions, such as moving house, tonight.
- You do not need to respond to any questions or claims made in the notes.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant anxiety or "hyper-vigilance" when your home environment is compromised by repeated, unwanted attention. Taking these steps is a proactive way to regain control and ensure that a formal record exists of the behaviour, which is the most effective way to trigger a professional intervention.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for UK residents facing door-based harassment. It does not replace professional legal advice or a full police investigation.

## Important note
This information is based on UK stalking and harassment legislation current to 2026. Always trust your instincts; if you feel an immediate threat to your life or property, contact 999.
