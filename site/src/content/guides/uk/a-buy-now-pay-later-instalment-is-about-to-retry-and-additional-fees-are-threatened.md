# What to do if… a buy-now-pay-later instalment is about to retry and additional fees are threatened (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the BNPL provider immediately to request a payment holiday or reschedule the due date before the retry occurs. If the payment risks your ability to pay for essentials, instruct your bank to withdraw consent for the Continuous Payment Authority (CPA) no later than the day before the retry.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the payment will fail without consequence; repeated retries often trigger automatic late fees from the provider and potentially "unpaid item" fees from your bank.
- Do not rely on cancelling your debit or credit card as a solution; BNPL providers use recurring payment authorities that may still be honoured by banks on replacement cards.
- Do not allow a BNPL retry to take priority over "priority debts" such as rent, council tax, energy bills, or food.
- Do not attempt to pay using multiple different cards without checking your balance first, as this can trigger fraud alerts or multiple accidental authorizations.
- Do not agree to a high-repayment "catch-up" plan that you cannot realistically afford just to stop the immediate notifications.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the exact timing and amount of the scheduled retry within the BNPL app.**
   - Identify the **specific date and time** the provider intends to attempt the collection.
   - Record the **exact fee amount** threatened for a failed attempt.
   - Save **screenshots** of the current payment schedule and any "failed payment" notifications.

2. **Pay the instalment manually now if funds are available and essentials are covered.**
   - Clearing the balance proactively is the only certain way to stop the automated retry and the associated fees.
   - Ensure the manual payment is confirmed in the app; some systems take several hours to update and may still attempt a retry if paid too close to the deadline.

3. **Request a "Payment Holiday" or date change through the BNPL app or web portal.**
   - Look for buttons labeled **Reschedule**, **Snooze**, or **Change due date**.
   - If the app does not allow a change, use the **live chat** feature to request a hold.
   - State clearly: "I cannot afford this payment today. I am requesting a hold on this instalment and a waiver of the late fee as I am contacting you before the retry."

4. **Contact your bank to cancel the Continuous Payment Authority (CPA) if the payment will cause hardship.**
   - Tell your bank or card issuer: "I am withdrawing my consent for any further payments to [Provider Name] under the Continuous Payment Authority."
   - You must do this **no later than the end of the business day before** the payment is due.
   - Note that while this stops the bank from paying the money, it does not cancel the debt itself; you will still owe the BNPL provider.

5. **Initiate a return or dispute pause if the payment relates to an item you no longer have.**
   - Provide the BNPL firm with your **return tracking number** or **cancellation confirmation email**.
   - Ask them specifically to **pause the account** while the retailer processes the refund.
   - For agreements made after July 15, 2026, you have enhanced rights under FCA regulation to expect fair treatment during disputes.

6. **Seek immediate free debt advice if you are choosing between BNPL and essential bills.**
   - Contact **StepChange**, **National Debtline**, or **Citizens Advice**.
   - If you live in England or Wales, ask about the **Statutory Debt Respite Scheme (Breathing Space)**; once an adviser starts this, it provides 60 days of legal protection from fees and retries.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to close your BNPL account permanently today.
- You do not need to worry about your credit score in the next hour; stopping the immediate fee is the priority.
- You do not need to provide a detailed life history or budget to the BNPL provider just to request a one-time 14-day extension.

## Important reassurance
The countdown timers and "urgent" notifications in BNPL apps are designed to create pressure, but they do not remove your legal rights. Banks in the UK are legally required to stop a payment if you withdraw consent, and regulated BNPL firms (from July 2026) must show forbearance to those in financial difficulty. Taking one clear step to communicate with the provider or your bank will stop the immediate "retry" cycle.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate payment crisis. It does not cover long-term debt management, credit score repair, or formal legal challenges to BNPL contracts.

## Important note
This guide contains general information and does not constitute financial or legal advice. Fees and terms vary significantly between providers. For agreements entered into on or after July 15, 2026, you may have the right to escalate unresolved complaints to the Financial Ombudsman Service.
