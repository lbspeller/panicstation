# What to do if… you suspect someone is tampering with your drink or watching it closely (UK)

## Short answer
Stop drinking from it, move away from the person, and go straight to venue staff, security, or trusted people.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep sipping to avoid a scene or to test whether anything is wrong.
- Don’t leave your drink unattended again, including to dance, vape, or go to the toilet.
- Don’t confront the person alone or let them steer you somewhere quieter.
- Don’t accept a replacement drink from the same person, or from anyone you do not trust.
- Don’t go outside or home alone right now if you feel uneasy, pressured, or unwell.

## What to do now
1. **Stop drinking immediately.**  
   If the drink has been out of your control, treat it as not safe to consume. You can leave it behind if carrying it keeps you near the person.

2. **Move to a safer, busier place.**  
   Go to the bar, security, a staffed area, a well-lit busy spot, or directly to people you trust. If you are with friends, say clearly: “I don’t feel safe. Come with me now.”

3. **Tell venue staff or security what is happening.**  
   Say: “Someone keeps watching my drink and hovering when I set it down. I’m worried about spiking. Can you help me move somewhere safe and help me leave safely?”  
   Ask them to stay with you while you find friends, call a trusted person, book transport, or wait somewhere safer.

4. **Use a discreet request if that feels easier.**  
   If the venue participates, you can ask staff for “Angela”. If you are not sure, say: “I need help. I feel unsafe,” and ask to be taken to a safe space.

5. **Create separation without negotiating.**  
   If the person approaches, use one short line if you need to: “No. I’m staying with staff or friends.” Then turn back to staff or trusted people.

6. **Treat feeling unwell as urgent.**  
   If you have symptoms such as sudden dizziness, confusion, extreme sleepiness, vomiting, trouble breathing, collapse, being unable to stay awake, or anything feels seriously wrong, call 999 or ask staff to call. If you are worried but it is not an emergency, call NHS 111 where available, or use local urgent medical help.

7. **Preserve options only if it does not increase risk.**  
   If it is safe, you can keep the cup or bottle and note the time, venue, and what happened on your phone. If that keeps you near the person or makes you feel less safe, leave it and move away.

8. **Decide about reporting later if you need to.**  
   You do not have to decide in the venue. If you later want to report a non-emergency spiking concern, you can contact police on 101 or through the relevant police website.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report to police.
- You do not need to prove what happened before asking for help.
- You do not need to work out what substance it could be.
- You do not need to confront the person, get their name, or get an admission.
- You do not need to search symptoms online while you are still in the venue.

## Important reassurance
Noticing repeated hovering, watching your drink, and timing movements around your drink is enough reason to act. Moving away, asking staff for help, or making the situation awkward is a protective choice, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the moment you notice a credible risk. If anything escalates, or if you later think you were drugged or assaulted, specialist support may help you decide what you want next.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel in danger or seriously unwell, call 999. For urgent medical advice that is not an emergency, use NHS 111 where available or local urgent medical help.
