# What to do if… a partner dismisses your boundaries as “rules” and keeps testing them in sexual conversations (UK)

## Short answer
End the conversation immediately and create physical or digital distance without further explanation. Contact a confidential specialist service to discuss the situation; you can access support without needing a label for what is happening or committing to a police report.

## Do not do these things
- Do not engage in a debate to justify why your boundary is reasonable or "fair."
- Do not send sexual messages, images, or "compromise" talk to stop the other person from sulking or escalating.
- Do not agree to meet in person to "explain yourself" if you feel any pressure or hesitation.
- Do not delete messages or call logs unless keeping them on your device poses an immediate physical safety risk (e.g., phone monitoring).
- Do not assume that because you have been sexual with this person before, your current "no" is less valid.
- Do not wait for a physical incident to occur before seeking confidential specialist advice.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the interaction immediately and without providing a long explanation.** Use a single, neutral statement such as "I am ending this conversation now" or "Do not send further sexual messages," then mute the chat, block the number, or leave the room.
2. **Physically move to a different location if you are in the same building.** If you are at their home or a private location, leave and go to a public space or a trusted person's house; if you are at your own home, ask them to leave or go to a room with a lock until you can arrange for them to go.
3. **Contact a trusted person to tell them you are not currently safe or comfortable.** Ask them to stay on the line with you while you travel home or to sit with you while you process the immediate situation.
4. **Identify the correct specialist service for your specific nation.** 
   - **England and Wales:** Contact the Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line at 0808 500 2222 (24/7) or the National Domestic Abuse Helpline at 0808 2000 247 (24/7).
   - **Scotland:** Contact the Rape Crisis Scotland Helpline at 08088 01 03 02 (daily, 5pm–midnight) or the Domestic Abuse and Forced Marriage Helpline at 0800 027 1234 (24/7).
   - **Northern Ireland:** Contact the Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline at 0808 802 1414 (24/7).
5. **Use the "Silent Solution" if you need the police but cannot speak safely.** Dial 999 from a mobile and, when prompted, press 55 to let the operator know you are in danger but cannot talk.
6. **Capture a record of the boundary-testing behavior if it is safe to do so.** Take screenshots of messages where you said "no" or "stop" and their subsequent sexual responses, then email them to a secondary account or a trusted friend for safekeeping.
7. **Consult Victim Support for practical help and information on your rights.** 
   - **England and Wales:** 0808 16 89 111 (24/7).
   - **Scotland:** 0800 160 1985 (Mon-Fri 8am-8pm, Sat-Sun 10am-4pm).
   - **Northern Ireland:** 028 9024 3133 (Mon-Fri 9am-5pm).

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to end the relationship permanently right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to report the behavior to the police.
- You do not need to find the "perfect" words to explain your feelings to the partner.
- You do not need to review your entire relationship history to see if there were earlier signs.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel confused, guilty, or like you are "overreacting" when someone you know or care about ignores your boundaries. These feelings are common responses to coercion and do not mean you have done anything wrong. A boundary is not a "rule" for the other person to follow; it is a statement of what you will and will not participate in.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for sexual boundary-pushing and coercion. It does not replace legal, therapeutic, or long-term safety planning advice from a qualified specialist.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or medical advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. Specialist services can provide confidential support regardless of whether you choose to report a crime.
