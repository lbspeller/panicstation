# What to do if… you are worried a friend is in danger and they have suddenly stopped responding (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a safety concern, not a mystery to solve. If you think they may be in immediate danger, call 999 now; otherwise, make one calm safety check, write down the basic facts you already know, and contact police on 101 if you remain concerned.

## Do not do these things
- Do not message or confront the person you are worried about.
- Do not flood your friend’s phone with long, emotional, or alarming messages; their phone may be seen by someone else.
- Do not post publicly about where they might be or who they might be with.
- Do not enter a private place, force contact, or put yourself in danger to check.
- Do not try to hack, track, impersonate, or access anyone’s accounts.
- Do not pressure your friend to report anything if they later make contact.

## What to do now
1. Write down the basic facts you already know:
   - their full name, age if known, phone number, and any immediate medical or vulnerability concerns
   - their last known location, time, plans, and who they said they were with
   - the last message or call that made you worried
   - key messages or location information already visible to you, without searching accounts or investigating

2. Send one short, neutral message that is easy to answer:
   - “Thinking of you. Are you safe to reply yes or no?”
   - “Can you send any emoji so I know you are okay?”
   - If you already have a code word with them, use it. If not, keep the message simple and calm.

3. Call once. If they do not answer, leave a neutral voicemail such as: “Call me when you can.” Avoid saying anything that could increase risk if someone else hears it.

4. Try one safe alternate contact route:
   - Ask one trusted mutual friend or family member whether they know your friend’s current plans.
   - If they were expected at a venue, hotel, workplace, or reception desk, you can ask staff to pass on a neutral message. They may not be able to confirm anything.
   - Do not contact the person you are worried may be coercive.

5. Choose the police route based on immediate danger:
   - If you think harm may be happening now, or you are unsure but the risk feels urgent, call 999. Say you are concerned for your friend’s immediate safety, give their last known location, and explain what changed.
   - If you are worried but it does not seem immediate, call 101 or use your local police force’s online contact option. Explain that your friend has stopped responding and you are concerned for their safety.
   - You do not have to wait 24 hours to report someone missing or contact police if you are concerned for their safety.

6. If you know a likely location, do not go there alone or enter a private place. Stay somewhere public and safe. If anything feels wrong, leave and call police.

7. If you need support while you decide what to do, contact a relevant specialist support service for sexual violence, abuse, or missing-person concerns. They can help you stay calm, think about safer wording, and understand support options without pressuring your friend to report.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide right now whether this is definitely abuse, exploitation, assault, or a misunderstanding.
- You do not have to collect proof, interview people, or investigate.
- You do not have to decide whether your friend should report anything to police.
- You do not have to write a perfect message; short, calm, and safe is enough.

## Important reassurance
Feeling panicked is understandable when someone suddenly goes silent and you are worried about coercion. Taking a calm safety-check approach is a way to act without escalating the situation or taking control away from your friend.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, careful communication, and getting appropriate help involved. Later decisions may need specialist sexual violence, domestic abuse, medical, or legal support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you think someone is in immediate danger, call 999. If you are unsure, you can still contact police on 101 for guidance and contact specialist support services for confidential help.
