# What to do if… you are told a loved one has died and you need to verify the details (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before telling anyone else. Verify the caller and get one official point of contact through a police, hospital, coroner, or Procurator Fiscal route before you pass the news on.

## Do not do these things
- Do not post anything on social media or message a wide group to help confirm it.
- Do not treat a second-hand message as confirmation.
- Do not stay on a call that feels pressured, suspicious, or rushed; end it and call back through an official route.
- Do not give banking details, passport details, passwords, or remote access to an unverified caller.
- Do not start calling multiple relatives at once before the death and identity have been officially confirmed.
- Do not drive straight away if you feel shaky, numb, confused, or unsafe to drive.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a steadier pause.** Sit down, keep your phone charged, and take two or three slow breaths. If you are alone, ask one calm person to sit with you or stay on the phone while you verify details, without spreading the news.

2. **Ask the caller for basic identifying details.** Write down their full name, role, organisation, phone number or extension, and any incident or reference number. Keep the conversation factual.

3. **Call back through an official route.** For police, use 101, police.uk, or the relevant police force’s official website. For a hospital, use the hospital’s official website or main switchboard. If the call felt suspicious, wait a few minutes before calling back so the previous call has clearly ended.

4. **Ask only for the minimum facts needed before informing others.** Use plain questions:
   - “Has the death been confirmed?”
   - “Has the person been formally identified, or is identification still in progress?”
   - “Who is being treated as next of kin or the main family contact right now?”
   - “Which organisation is handling this now?”
   - “Who should I speak to next, and what is their direct line or extension?”

5. **Write down what is confirmed and what is not.** Note the date and time of each call, names, roles, reference numbers, and exact wording such as “confirmed”, “not yet confirmed”, “formally identified”, or “identification still in progress”.

6. **If a coroner or Procurator Fiscal is involved, ask only for the next contact step.** Ask which office is handling the death, whether you should wait for them to contact you, and whether there is anything you must not do before they update you.

7. **Delay wider family messages until you have official confirmation.** Once confirmed, tell one trusted person first so they can support you. Then tell the closest people privately and ask them not to post or forward the news until immediate family have been told.

8. **Use a short script if you need one.** Say: “I have had official confirmation from the official contact. I do not have full details yet. Please do not post or message others until immediate family have been told.”

9. **If you are asked to attend an identification or meeting, slow it down.** Ask what the process will involve, where you need to go, whether you can bring someone with you, and what alternatives exist if you cannot cope with attending.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide about funerals, belongings, probate, benefits, banks, or public announcements in this first verified moment.
- You do not need to answer every message or call straight away.
- You do not need to gather documents unless the official contact asks for something specific now.
- You do not need to explain details you do not yet have.

## Important reassurance
Feeling numb, shaky, confused, or as if this is not real can happen in shock. Taking a short pause to verify facts is not cold or disloyal; it helps prevent avoidable harm, including telling people something that has not yet been confirmed.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours only. Later decisions about registration, funeral arrangements, finances, belongings, and family coordination can wait until you have official confirmation and, where needed, specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information for crisis first steps, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Processes vary depending on where in the UK the death happened and whether police, a hospital, a coroner, or the Procurator Fiscal is involved. If anything is unclear or contradictory, keep communication limited and re-verify through an official main number.
