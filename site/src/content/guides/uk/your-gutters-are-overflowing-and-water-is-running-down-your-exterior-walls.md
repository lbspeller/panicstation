# What to do if… your gutters are overflowing and water is running down your exterior walls (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safe pause, then prioritise electrical safety and stopping water from getting further inside: check indoors for water near electrics, contain drips, and only use ground-level diversion outside.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use ladders or go onto the roof while it is wet, windy, dark, or you are rushing.
- Do not touch switches, sockets, light fittings, appliances, cables, or the consumer unit if they are wet or you are standing in water.
- Do not poke tools into gutters or downpipes near external lights, cables, damaged equipment, or anything you cannot clearly see.
- Do not force water into a blocked downpipe with a hose if you cannot see where it will discharge.
- Do not remove gutter sections, reseal joints, or start proper repairs while water is still actively cascading.
- Do not walk or drive through flood water if the problem has spread beyond the property.

## What to do now
1. **Check inside the affected wall area first.** Look for active dripping, spreading damp patches, water near sockets, water near light fittings, or wet extension leads. Keep people and pets away from any wet electrical area.
2. **Make the electrics safe only if you can do it safely.** If you can reach the consumer unit or fuse box without stepping in water and without touching anything wet, switch off the electricity. If the consumer unit, sockets, switches, or wiring look wet or damaged, do not touch them; keep away and get urgent help from a qualified electrician or the electricity network emergency route.
3. **Contain water that is already inside.** Put buckets, trays, towels, or a washing-up bowl under drips. Move rugs, soft furnishings, documents, electronics, and valuables away from the affected wall and off the floor.
4. **Check outside only from ground level.** Look for a downpipe outlet packed with leaves, a blocked shoe at the bottom of the downpipe, a gully grate covered with debris, or water splashing straight onto airbricks, thresholds, or window surrounds. Clear only loose debris you can reach safely from the ground.
5. **Reduce splashback onto the wall.** If the water is spilling from one obvious point, place a large tub, bin, or container under the main spill if it is safe to do so. Empty it away from the house before it overflows.
6. **Divert water away from openings and foundations.** If you have a flexible downpipe extension, spare pipe, or plastic sheeting, use it as a temporary ground-level chute to guide water away from the wall, airbricks, thresholds, and basement or cellar openings. Do not send the water towards a neighbour’s property, public footpath, or place where people may slip.
7. **Use the right reporting route if the problem is bigger than your gutter.** For a blocked public drain or flooded road, contact your local council. For sewer flooding or a burst water main, contact your water company. For flooding from main rivers, the sea, or reservoir concerns in England, use the Environment Agency route shown on GOV.UK. For immediate danger to life, call 999.
8. **Keep away from damaged electrical equipment outside.** If you see sparking, fallen lines, damaged service equipment, or dangerous electrical damage, stay well clear. Call 105 in England, Scotland and Wales, or 03457 643643 in Northern Ireland. Call 999 as well if there is an immediate serious risk.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact cause while it is still raining heavily.
- You do not need to decide today whether it is a blocked downpipe, poor gutter fall, a leaking joint, undersized guttering, or a roof issue.
- You do not need to climb a ladder, remove guttering, or use sealant while conditions are unsafe.
- You do not need to start drying plaster, lifting flooring, or running dehumidifiers until water entry has stopped and electrics are safe.
- You do not need to decide about insurance or contractors until the immediate spread of water is contained.

## Important reassurance
Overflowing gutters can look alarming because a lot of water is concentrated in one place. Focusing on electrical safety, keeping water away from openings, and using a safe temporary diversion is enough for the first stage.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance to reduce harm and buy time. Once conditions are safe, a competent tradesperson may need to check for blocked gutters, blocked downpipes, loose joints, poor fall, damaged fascia or soffit, and drainage problems at ground level.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, electrical, building, insurance, or other professional advice. If you suspect electrical danger, structural damage, sewage flooding, or significant flooding, prioritise safety and seek urgent specialist help.
