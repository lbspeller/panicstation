# What to do if… part of your ceiling starts sagging after a leak (UK)

## Short answer
Get everyone out of the affected room and keep away from the sagging area. Stop the water source only if you can do it safely, and do not use electrics in or near the wet area until they have been made safe.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t stand under the sagging or bulging area to check it or take photos.
- Don’t poke, cut, drill, or try to release the bulge yourself.
- Don’t switch lights on or off, use sockets, or touch fittings in the wet area if water may have reached electrics.
- Don’t touch the consumer unit, meter cupboard, or electrical equipment if it looks wet, is in a damp cupboard, or you would be reaching across water.
- Don’t put buckets, towels, ladders, or furniture directly under the sagging part.
- Don’t assume it is only cosmetic if the sag is growing, cracking, noisy, or near a light fitting.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Move people and pets out of the room. Close the door if you can. Keep the room empty if the sagging area is large, getting worse, dripping near electrics, or making cracking noises.
2. **Look only from a safe edge or doorway.** Check whether the sag is near a light fitting, smoke alarm, socket, switch, ceiling fan, or other electrical item. Notice whether it is growing, cracking, spreading, or dripping steadily.
3. **Stop the water source if you can do that without going under the sag.**
   - If the leak is from plumbing, turn off the stopcock, or a local isolation valve to the toilet, sink, bath, shower, appliance, or tank if you can identify it safely.
   - If the leak is from a flat above, contact the neighbour, building manager, landlord, or managing agent and say there is an active leak with a sagging ceiling below.
   - If the leak is from the roof during rain, do not climb into the roof space or onto the roof. Keep away from the sagging ceiling and contact an emergency roofer or building manager.
4. **Treat wet electrics as a priority hazard.** Do not use lights, switches, sockets, extractor fans, ceiling fans, or appliances in the affected room. Keep phones, chargers, extension leads, and portable heaters away from the wet area.
5. **Isolate electricity only if it is clearly safe.** If the consumer unit is dry, you are not standing in water, and you can reach it safely, switch off the affected circuit or the main switch. If anything around the consumer unit or meter area is wet, or you are unsure, do not touch it; contact a qualified electrician urgently. For electricity network or power emergency help, call 105 in England, Scotland, or Wales, or 03457 643 643 in Northern Ireland.
6. **Catch drips only from a safe position.** Put towels or a bucket at the edge of the dripping area if you can do this without standing under the sag. Keep the route out of the room clear.
7. **Report it urgently if you rent or the building is managed.** Contact your landlord, letting agent, housing association, council repairs line, freeholder, or managing agent. Use plain words: “active leak, ceiling sagging, possible collapse risk, possible electrical hazard.”
8. **Call the most relevant urgent help.** Use an emergency plumber for a plumbing leak, an emergency roofer for a roof leak, and a qualified electrician if water may have reached electrics. If the ceiling is changing quickly, cracking loudly, or a large area is sagging, ask for urgent building assessment through your landlord, managing agent, insurer, builder, or surveyor.
9. **Record only what you can from safety.** Take a few photos or a short video from the doorway or another safe position. Note when you noticed the leak, when the ceiling started sagging, and what you turned off.
10. **Leave and call 999 if there is immediate danger.** Call 999 if the ceiling is starting to collapse, you see sparks or arcing, smell burning, see smoke, suspect an electrical fire, or cannot safely keep people away from the hazard.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today how the ceiling will be repaired, plastered, redecorated, or dried.
- You do not need to move everything from the room if that means going under the sagging area.
- You do not need to use fans, dehumidifiers, heaters, or extension leads in the affected room until the leak is stopped, the room is safe to enter, and electrics have been confirmed safe.
- You do not need to settle blame, liability, insurance details, or neighbour disputes while the leak and safety risk are still active.

## Important reassurance
A sagging ceiling after a leak is alarming, but the first useful steps are simple: keep people out, avoid wet electrics, stop the water if safe, and get urgent practical help. You do not have to solve the repair plan while the room may still be unsafe.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and buy time. Later decisions about drying, electrical testing, ceiling repair, insurance, tenancy rights, or building defects may need qualified trades, your insurer, your landlord, a managing agent, or specialist advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, electrical, structural, insurance, or professional building advice. Follow emergency services, electricity network, landlord, insurer, and qualified trades advice for your specific situation.
