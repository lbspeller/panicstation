# What to do if… your commission or bonus is withheld at the last minute (UK)

## Short answer
Do not make a lump-sum bank transfer immediately. Under Section 14 of the Employment Rights Act 1996, UK employers have a legal right to recover overpayments via payroll deductions without your consent, so you must quickly negotiate a sustainable repayment schedule in writing to prevent a total loss of your next paycheck.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send a bank transfer or cash payment until you have a written breakdown of the gross and net figures.
- Do not ignore the request; unlike most deductions, overpayment recovery does not require your prior written agreement.
- Do not assume your pay is protected by National Minimum Wage (NMW) limits; overpayment deductions are one of the few legal exceptions where pay can fall below the NMW.
- Do not agree to a repayment schedule that leaves you unable to pay essential bills (rent, mortgage, food).
- Do not sign a new contract or "deduction authorization" under pressure until you have checked your existing terms.

## What to do now
1. **Request a full written breakdown of the overpayment immediately.** **You must ask for the specific pay periods affected, the reason for the error, and the calculation of the gross versus net amounts.**
   - Ensure the employer is only asking for the "Net" amount (the extra cash that actually reached your bank account) if you are paying them back directly.
   - If they are deducting from future pay, they usually adjust the "Gross" figure through payroll to ensure tax and National Insurance are corrected.

2. **Verify the claim against your own records within the next hour.** **Gather your payslips, bank statements, and any correspondence regarding pay rises, bonuses, or changes in hours.**
   - Determine if the overpayment is a genuine error or a misunderstanding of a contractual entitlement.
   - Note the date you first received the extra money and whether you could have reasonably known it was a mistake.

3. **Respond in writing to propose a "reasonable" repayment plan.** **State clearly that you acknowledge the error (if verified) but require a structured repayment plan to avoid financial hardship.**
   - Propose a specific amount to be deducted each month (e.g., "I propose a deduction of £50 per month over 10 months").
   - Explicitly mention if a full or fast deduction would cause "significant financial hardship" or prevent you from meeting essential living costs.

4. **Check your employment contract for specific overpayment clauses.** **While the law allows deductions without a clause, your contract or staff handbook may outline a specific process the employer has promised to follow.**
   - Look for terms regarding "notice of deduction" or "repayment period" that might give you more time to negotiate.

5. **Request a pause on deductions if the amount is in dispute.** **If you believe the calculation is wrong or that no overpayment occurred, formally request in writing that all recovery actions be suspended until a grievance meeting is held.**
   - Use the phrase "I dispute the validity and amount of the alleged overpayment" to trigger formal HR procedures.

6. **Contact your trade union or Acas if the employer refuses to negotiate.** **If the employer threatens to take your entire next paycheck or refuses a reasonable installment plan, seek external mediation immediately.**
   - Acas can provide a template for "Hardship" letters and advice on whether the employer's behavior breaches the "implied term of mutual trust and confidence."

7. **Determine if you have a "change of position" defense.** **If you received the money in good faith, did not notice the error, and have already spent it on something you otherwise would not have bought, you may have a legal defense against repayment.**
   - This is complex and usually requires legal advice or a Citizens Advice consultation before being raised with an employer.

## What can wait
- You do not need to agree to the first repayment amount suggested by the employer.
- You do not need to pay back the "Gross" amount out of your own pocket (the employer should reclaim the tax/NI from HMRC).
- You do not need to accept a verbal explanation; you can wait for the written breakdown before committing to a plan.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel defensive or anxious when a payroll error is discovered. Employers generally prefer a cooperative employee who pays back the money over a long period rather than a legal dispute. Proposing a plan shows you are acting in good faith while protecting your immediate cash flow.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for overpayment demands under UK employment law. It does not cover recovery of overpaid benefits (DWP) or complex tax-year-end adjustments involving HMRC directly.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. While Section 14 of the Employment Rights Act 1996 grants employers broad powers to recover overpayments, they are still expected to act reasonably. If you are facing immediate financial crisis due to a deduction, contact Citizens Advice or a qualified employment solicitor.
