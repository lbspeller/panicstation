# What to do if… a childcare provider or school threatens to end your place over an unpaid balance (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the demand using trusted contact details, then get the exact balance and the exact consequence in writing. Ask for a same-day hold while you pay, dispute, or agree a short payment plan.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay through a new link, changed bank details, gift cards, crypto, or a personal account unless you have independently verified it with the school or provider.
- Don’t agree to withdraw your child, cancel a place, or sign new terms while you are panicking.
- Don’t ignore the message; even a short written reply can help buy time and show you are trying to sort it out.
- Don’t assume “place will be lost” means the same thing everywhere; it may mean wraparound care, meals, nursery sessions, funded hours, or an independent-school contract.
- Don’t send an angry message. Keep it short, factual, and in writing.

## What to do now
1. **Make a quick record.** Screenshot the message, invoice, portal note, or payment page. If it was a call, write down the time, the name of the person who spoke to you, and the exact words used, such as “lost place”, “removed”, “terminated”, or “sessions stopped”.

2. **Verify the demand before paying.** Contact the school, nursery, club, childminder, or provider using a number or email from their official website, app, contract, or previous paperwork. Do not rely only on contact details inside a surprising payment message.

3. **Ask for the key facts in writing today.** Ask them to confirm:
   - the exact balance and what it covers, such as sessions, meals, late fees, deposits, clubs, trips, or wraparound care
   - the deadline they are applying
   - what “place will be lost” means in practice, including what stops and from when
   - the policy, contract clause, or charging rule they are relying on

4. **Ask for a same-day hold.** Send a short message such as: “Please hold the place today while we confirm the balance and agree a payment plan. I am trying to prevent disruption for my child.”

5. **Separate a school place from a paid service.** If this is a state-funded school, ask them to confirm whether they mean the school roll itself or an optional paid service such as breakfast club, after-school club, meals, trips, or nursery sessions. In England, schools generally have specific rules about charging for school activities and specific rules about when a pupil’s name can be removed from the admission register. If they are implying removal from the school roll, ask them to put the rule, policy, or reason in writing. In Scotland, Wales or Northern Ireland, ask for the equivalent local school attendance, charging, or admissions policy.

6. **If it is childcare, wraparound care, or an independent-school fee issue, ask what action they are actually taking.** Ask whether they are pausing sessions, ending a contract, reallocating a place, charging late fees, or refusing future booked sessions. Ask for the notice wording and any complaints process if you think the decision is wrong.

7. **If you can pay something today, offer a part-payment and a dated plan.** Keep it simple: “I can pay £___ today and £___ on [date]. Please confirm whether this keeps the place or sessions open while the balance is cleared.”

8. **If the amount looks wrong, dispute only the disputed part.** Ask for an itemised statement showing dates, sessions, meals, clubs, deposits, late fees, and payments already made. If you can, pay the amount you agree is due and write: “I dispute £___ because ___. I can pay £___ today while this is checked.”

9. **Ask for practical short-term options.** Ask whether they can pause late fees, accept a smaller holding payment, move the deadline to the next working day, use any deposit or credit on the account, or let you speak to the finance or bursar team.

10. **If you think it is a scam, stop before paying.** Verify directly with the provider. If you have already paid through suspicious details, contact your bank immediately using trusted details from your card or banking app, then report the suspected fraud or cyber crime through the appropriate reporting route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to change childcare provider or school.
- You do not need to write a full complaint today; first get the balance, deadline, and consequence in writing.
- You do not need to solve the whole debt today if a short hold, part-payment, or dated plan will keep the place stable.
- You do not need to check every benefit or childcare-cost option today, unless it helps you make an immediate payment safely.

## Important reassurance
End-of-day deadlines can create panic. Slowing the situation down, verifying the demand, and getting the consequence in writing can turn a cliff-edge message into a short-term plan.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if the issue involves a state-school roll, a childcare contract, an independent-school contract, funded childcare hours, or a disputed debt.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, educational, debt, or professional advice. Rules and complaints routes can differ across the UK, so check the provider’s written policy and the relevant local route before relying on any deadline or threat.
