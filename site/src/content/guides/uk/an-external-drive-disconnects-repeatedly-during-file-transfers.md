# What to do if… an external drive disconnects repeatedly during file transfers (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all active file transfers immediately and disconnect the drive safely to prevent file system corruption. Stabilize the hardware connection by removing hubs and disabling software-based power-saving features before attempting to resume in small batches.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep restarting the same large file transfer; repeated interruptions during write operations can lead to permanent data corruption.
- Do not leave the drive connected if it is making clicking, grinding, or loud buzzing noises.
- Do not attempt to "fix" the drive by reformatting it if you still need to recover the data.
- Do not use unpowered USB hubs or extension cables during the recovery process.
- Do not touch the drive or cable if you notice a burning smell or excessive heat.

## What to do now
1. **Force a stop to all write operations.**
   **Cancel the current transfer immediately using the on-screen progress window.**
   - If the system is unresponsive, wait 60 seconds before unplugging the drive.
   - Use the "Safely Remove Hardware" (Windows) or "Eject" (Mac) feature if the drive is still visible.

2. **Isolate the drive from power-sharing hardware.**
   **Connect the drive directly to a main USB port on the computer chassis.**
   - Do not use monitors, keyboards, or unpowered hubs as a bridge.
   - If the drive has a dedicated power adapter, plug it directly into a wall socket rather than a power strip.

3. **Disable software-driven USB suspension (Windows).**
   **Navigate to Settings > Bluetooth & devices > USB and toggle off "USB battery saver."**
   - If the option is missing, open Device Manager, expand "Universal Serial Bus controllers," right-click each "USB Root Hub," and under "Power Management," uncheck "Allow the computer to turn off this device to save power."

4. **Disable disk sleep settings (Mac).**
   **Open System Settings and adjust the hardware-specific energy tab.**
   - For laptops: Go to "Battery" > "Options" and toggle on "Prevent automatic sleeping on power adapter when the display is off."
   - For desktops: Go to "Energy" and toggle on "Prevent automatic sleeping when the display is off."
   - Toggle off "Put hard disks to sleep when possible" in either menu.

5. **Perform a sensory and stability check.**
   **Listen for rhythmic clicking and feel the casing for high temperatures.**
   - If the drive clicks or is too hot to hold, stop all troubleshooting and seek professional data recovery.
   - If the drive is silent and cool, wait five minutes to see if it stays connected without a transfer running.

6. **Resume copying using the "Small Batch" method.**
   **Copy one folder at a time, starting with the most critical files first.**
   - Do not attempt to move the entire library at once.
   - Verify each batch opens correctly on the destination drive before starting the next.

7. **Invoke Consumer Rights Act protections for faulty hardware.**
   **Contact the retailer (not the manufacturer) if the drive was purchased within the last six months.**
   - Under the Consumer Rights Act 2015, you have a short-term right to a refund within 30 days if the goods are of unsatisfactory quality.
   - Between 30 days and six months, the retailer must provide a repair or replacement.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the fault is in the drive's internal controller or the USB bridge today.
- You do not need to buy replacement cables until you have tested the drive on a different computer to confirm the source of the issue.
- You do not need to worry about reorganizing the files; focus only on moving them to a stable location.

## Important reassurance
Physical connection drops are frequently caused by power management or worn cables rather than total drive failure. By stopping the cycle of "fail and retry," you are significantly reducing the risk of a "head crash" or file system corruption. Most data remains accessible once a stable power supply is established.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. It is intended for external mechanical hard drives and SSDs experiencing intermittent connectivity. It does not cover recovery for drives that are physically smashed or completely unrecognized by all computers.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional IT or legal advice. Technical steps involving the operating system should be performed with caution. If the data on the drive is of high financial or personal value, avoid DIY fixes and contact a professional data recovery service immediately.
