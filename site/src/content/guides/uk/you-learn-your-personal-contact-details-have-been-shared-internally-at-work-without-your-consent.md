# What to do if… you learn your personal contact details have been shared internally at work without your consent (UK)

## Short answer
Send a written request to your employer's Data Protection Lead and HR to restrict access to the data immediately and confirm in writing how the spread has been contained.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send a mass email or "all-staff" message to complain, as this often draws more attention to the leaked details.
- Do not delete evidence of the leak, such as screenshots of the directory, Teams messages, or emails, even if you find them distressing.
- Do not confront the person who shared the details in an aggressive manner, as this can complicate a formal grievance later.
- Do not assume that "internal" sharing is always legal; employers must still have a lawful basis and follow the principle of data minimisation.
- Do not ignore your safety; if the leak puts you at risk from a specific person, do not wait for the employer to offer help—ask for it explicitly.

## What to do now
1. **Secure copies of the leak and create a factual log.** Save screenshots or PDFs that show exactly what was shared (e.g., mobile number, home address), who shared it, and where it was posted (e.g., a shared spreadsheet, an email chain, or a public channel).
2. **Submit a formal "Request for Restriction of Processing" in writing.** Email your line manager, HR, and the Data Protection Officer (or the person responsible for data protection) stating that you are exercising your right under Article 18 of the UK GDPR.
   - Ask them to immediately remove the data from public view or restrict it to "need-to-know" staff only.
   - Request that they recall any emails or delete any posts containing the details.
3. **Ask for an immediate risk assessment regarding your physical safety.** If you have specific reasons to fear your details being public (e.g., a history of stalking or domestic abuse), tell HR clearly that this is a "high risk" breach to your safety.
   - Request temporary adjustments such as a change in work location, updated security briefings for reception, or a temporary work phone.
4. **Identify the "lawful basis" the employer claims to have used.** Ask the employer to specify which lawful basis under Article 6 of the UK GDPR they relied upon to share your personal contact details with that specific group of people.
   - Remind them that even if they claim "legitimate interests," those interests must not override your fundamental right to privacy.
5. **Request a formal update on the "containment" status within 24 hours.** Ask for written confirmation that the data has been removed and that all recipients have been instructed to delete any copies they made.
6. **Lodge a formal grievance if the employer refuses to act or downplays the event.** Follow your company’s written grievance policy. State that the unauthorised disclosure has caused you "distress" or "safety concerns," which are recognised forms of damage under UK data protection law.
7. **Use a Subject Access Request (SAR) to uncover the extent of the breach.** If the employer is being vague, submit a SAR specifically asking for "all records of internal disclosures of my personal contact details" and "the categories of recipients who have accessed my data in the last 30 days."
8. **Request to be accompanied to any formal meetings.** If you are called to a grievance meeting to discuss the leak, you have a statutory right to be accompanied by a colleague or a trade union representative.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to report the matter to the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO) today; you generally must exhaust the employer's internal complaint process first.
- You do not need to calculate financial "damages" or seek a lawyer immediately while the primary goal is still containment.
- You do not need to explain the "why" of your privacy to colleagues; you are entitled to privacy regardless of your reasons.
- You do not need to decide if this constitutes a "constructive dismissal" situation right now; focus on getting the data secured first.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a loss of control or a sense of "exposure" when private information is handled carelessly at work. Your contact details belong to you, and wanting them kept private is a standard professional boundary, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an internal data leak in a UK workplace. It focuses on immediate containment and exercising statutory rights under the UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018. It does not cover long-term litigation or complex data breach compensation claims.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Data protection laws in the UK, including the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, involve specific thresholds for "high risk" reporting. If you believe your physical safety is in immediate danger due to the disclosure of your address or location, contact the police on 999.
