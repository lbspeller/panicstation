# What to do if… a funeral home or service provider is pressuring you into fast decisions (UK)

## Short answer
Pause the conversation and ask for the price information, terms, and estimate in writing before you sign or pay. You do not have to settle every funeral choice in the same conversation.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign documents or pay a deposit just to stop the pressure if you do not understand what you are agreeing to.
- Don’t agree to upgrades such as extra cars, a higher-cost coffin, a more expensive time slot, flowers, printing, catering, or other extras in the moment unless you truly want them.
- Don’t assume something is required because it is presented that way; ask what is required for the funeral to go ahead and what is optional.
- Don’t hand over card details while distressed if you have not seen a written, itemised estimate.
- Don’t let anyone isolate you from a calmer relative or friend who can help you slow the conversation down.
- Don’t argue on the spot if you feel overwhelmed; ending the call or leaving the branch is enough for now.

## What to do now
1. **Use a short delaying script and pause the conversation.**  
   Say: “I can’t decide today. Please email me the full written breakdown and your terms. I’ll come back to you.” If they keep pushing, repeat it once and end the call or leave.

2. **Ask for the required price and business information before deciding.**  
   Ask for:
   - the Standardised Price List
   - the Additional Options Price List
   - any local crematorium price information they are required to show you
   - the terms of business, payment terms, and any disclosure of interests  
   Say: “Please show me these now and send me a copy or link so I can review them away from the branch.”

3. **Ask for one written estimate for the plan you are actually considering.**  
   Ask for a single document that separates:
   - the funeral director’s own charges
   - third-party costs, such as crematorium, cemetery, minister, celebrant, venue, or notice fees
   - what is included
   - what is optional
   - any deposit, cancellation term, deadline, hold, or condition

4. **Ask what genuinely has to happen today.**  
   Say: “Which decisions are genuinely time-critical today, and which can wait until tomorrow?” Ask them to put any claimed deadline or risk in writing.

5. **Choose only the minimum needed for the immediate next step.**  
   If something genuinely needs action now, keep it basic: confirm only immediate care or collection, and ask them to hold all other choices until you have reviewed the written estimate.

6. **Get a second view before agreeing to extras.**  
   Send the written estimate to a calm relative, friend, or trusted person and ask them to help spot optional extras, unclear fees, or pressure phrases. You can also ask another funeral director for a comparable written estimate.

7. **Write down what happened while it is fresh.**  
   Note the date and time, who you spoke to, what you were told was required, what costs were mentioned, and any phrases such as “must decide today” or “this price will not hold.” Keep brochures, emails, leaflets, screenshots, and receipts.

8. **Use a complaint or advice route if the pressure continues or you think you were misled.**  
   Start by asking the funeral director, in writing, to stop the pressure and clarify the charges. If the provider is an NAFD member, check NAFD Resolve. If the provider is a SAIF member, check SAIF’s complaints information. For consumer advice and possible Trading Standards reporting, use Citizens Advice in Great Britain or Consumerline in Northern Ireland.

## What can wait
- You do not need to pick a coffin upgrade, cars, flowers, printed materials, catering, keepsakes, or a package right now.
- You do not need to decide every detail of the service today, such as music, readings, wording, photos, or the order of service.
- You do not need to commit to one provider just because you made an enquiry, had an appointment, or allowed an initial collection.
- You do not need to make a complaint perfectly today; getting things in writing and stepping away is enough for now.
- You do not need to decide how to handle every family disagreement before you have a clear written estimate.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressured while grieving can make ordinary choices feel urgent and irreversible. Slowing the process down is a protective step, not a failure.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to buy time and reduce the risk of rushed or expensive decisions. Later decisions about contracts, payments, complaints, or disputes may need specialist consumer advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Processes and options can depend on what has already been agreed, what has already been provided, and which organisation the provider belongs to.
