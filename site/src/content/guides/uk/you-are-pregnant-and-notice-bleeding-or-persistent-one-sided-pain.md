# What to do if… you are pregnant and notice bleeding or persistent one-sided pain (UK)

## Short answer
Seek a same-day medical assessment through an Early Pregnancy Assessment Unit (EPAU) or your midwife. Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if you have bleeding combined with severe one-sided pain, shoulder-tip pain, or if you feel faint.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait until the next morning if you have persistent one-sided pain, heavy bleeding, or feel dizzy.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel lightheaded, weak, or are in severe pain.
- Do not use tampons or menstrual cups; use a sanitary pad to monitor blood loss.
- Do not insert anything into the vagina to check the source of the bleeding.
- Do not take ibuprofen or aspirin unless specifically prescribed by your obstetrician; use paracetamol if needed.
- Do not assume the situation is "fine" or "over" based on a home pregnancy test or internet research alone.

## What to do now
1. **Identify if you need emergency care (999/A&E).**  
   **Call 999 or attend the nearest Emergency Department immediately if you experience any of the following:**
   - Severe or worsening abdominal or pelvic pain, especially on one side.
   - Pain at the very tip of your shoulder (where the arm joins).
   - Heavy bleeding that soaks through a sanitary pad in an hour or less.
   - Fainting, collapse, or feeling severely dizzy/lightheaded.
   - Diarrhoea or significant pain when opening your bowels/passing urine.
2. **Contact the correct service for your gestation.**  
   **If you are under 20 weeks pregnant, contact your local Early Pregnancy Assessment Unit (EPAU) or Early Pregnancy Unit (EPU).**
   - Most units take referrals from your GP or NHS 111, though some allow self-referral (check your local Trust website).
   - If you are 20 weeks pregnant or over, call your Maternity Triage or Pregnancy Assessment Unit number found in your handheld notes.
   - If you cannot reach these services, call NHS 111 for urgent triage.
3. **Monitor and record sensory cues for the clinician.**  
   **Note the specific characteristics of the bleeding and pain to report during your assessment.**
   - Colour: Bright red (fresh), pink, or brown (old blood/discharge).
   - Flow: Spotting, light flow, or heavy flow with clots.
   - Pain location: Is it central, one-sided, or radiating to the shoulder?
4. **Prepare your medical history for the intake call.**  
   **Have your pregnancy details and risk factors ready to share with the nurse or doctor.**
   - Your estimated weeks of pregnancy (or date of your last period).
   - Whether this pregnancy resulted from IVF or if you have a Coil (IUD) in place.
   - Any history of previous ectopic pregnancy or surgery on your fallopian tubes.
5. **Sit or lie down while waiting for transport or advice.**  
   **Rest in a safe position to prevent injury if you become lightheaded.**
   - Ask someone to stay with you until you have been assessed by a professional.
   - Keep your phone and a charger nearby.
6. **Disclose your blood group if you are RhD negative.**  
   **Tell the clinician your blood group so they can determine if anti-D immunoglobulin is required.**
   - Under 2026 guidelines, anti-D is generally not given for bleeding before 12 weeks.
   - It is considered between 12+0 and 12+6 weeks for heavy or recurrent bleeding.
   - It is routinely recommended for sensitising events after 13 weeks.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause of the bleeding yourself; this requires professional clinical tools.
- You do not need to repeat home pregnancy tests today, as they cannot confirm the location or viability of the pregnancy.
- You do not need to notify your employer or arrange long-term childcare until you have received an initial assessment and plan.

## Important reassurance
Vaginal bleeding occurs in approximately 20% of pregnancies during the first trimester, and many of these continue to a healthy birth. However, because symptoms like one-sided pain can indicate time-sensitive complications like an ectopic pregnancy, medical staff expect and prefer you to seek assessment immediately for these symptoms.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and navigation for UK-based users only. It does not replace clinical diagnosis or ultrasound scanning.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute medical advice. If you feel your condition is deteriorating rapidly or you feel you may lose consciousness, call 999.
