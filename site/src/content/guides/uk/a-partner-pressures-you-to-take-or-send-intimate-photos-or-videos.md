# What to do if… a partner pressures you to take or send intimate photos or videos (UK)

## Short answer
You do not have to create or send intimate images to keep someone calm, prove love, or stop an argument. Get to a safer pause, stop the pressure if you safely can, and contact someone you trust or a specialist support service from a device your partner cannot access.

## Do not do these things
- Do not create, send, or allow filming “just to stop the pressure” if you do not want to.
- Do not hand over your phone, passcode, fingerprint, Face ID, email password, or cloud password.
- Do not argue about whether it “counts” as abuse; focus on getting support and reducing risk.
- Do not confront them if you feel they may escalate, threaten you, monitor you, or stop you leaving.
- Do not rely on private browsing if they may monitor your device, accounts, or internet use.
- Do not delete messages or threats if you may want help later and it is safe to keep them.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** End the conversation if you can and move somewhere you can breathe and think, such as another room, outside, a public place, or near someone you trust. If you are in immediate danger, call **999**.
2. **Use the shortest safe response.** If it feels safe, say or write: “I’m not doing that. Don’t ask again.” If replying may escalate things, you do not have to reply.
3. **Contact support from a safer device.** Use a friend’s phone, a work device, a library computer, or an account your partner cannot access. If they may be monitoring you, avoid making sudden account changes until you have advice.
4. **Tell one safe person what is happening.** A short message is enough: “I’m being pressured to make sexual images and I need support today.” Ask them to stay with you, call you, or help you use a safer device.
5. **If threats were made or images already exist, contact specialist image-abuse support.** If you are **18 or over**, the Revenge Porn Helpline can support adult victims of intimate image abuse in the UK, including threats and takedown options. If you are **under 18**, use Childline Report Remove or CEOP, or ask a trusted adult to help you do this.
6. **Reduce immediate digital risk only if it is safe.** If you can do so without alerting them, secure your phone unlock and your email account first, because email can reset other passwords. If changing passwords may alert them, do this later with support.
7. **Check shared access.** If it is safe, check whether photos or videos are backing up to a shared cloud account, shared album, family device, or device your partner can access.
8. **Keep only a minimal record if safe and wanted.** You can save messages or threats somewhere they cannot access, such as with a trusted person or a safer account.
9. **If images exist and you are 18 or over, consider StopNCII.** It may help prevent intimate images being shared across participating platforms if you meet its criteria.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to report to police.
- You do not need to gather every message, write a full account, or prove anything before asking for help.
- You do not need to decide the future of the relationship right now.
- You do not need to fix all device and account security in one go; start with the safest small step.

## Important reassurance
Feeling frozen, guilty, confused, or worried about making things worse is common when someone close to you pressures you sexually. You are allowed to say no to photos, videos, filming, and sharing. The pressure is not your fault, and support is available even if you are unsure what you want to do next.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce immediate risk and help you get support. Ongoing coercion, threats, monitoring, or image abuse may need specialist domestic abuse support, image-abuse support, legal advice, or police help later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice, medical advice, therapeutic advice, or a substitute for professional support. If you feel unsafe or at immediate risk, call **999**. If you are not in immediate danger but want police help, you can use non-emergency routes such as **101**, online reporting, or a police station when it is safe. If you are under 18, prioritise specialist child-safety support.
