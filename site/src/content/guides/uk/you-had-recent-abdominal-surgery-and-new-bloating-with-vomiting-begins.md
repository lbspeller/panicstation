# What to do if… you had recent abdominal surgery and new bloating with vomiting begins (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your **Surgical Assessment Unit (SAU)** or the **surgical ward** listed on your discharge paperwork immediately for an urgent clinical review.

## Do not do these things
- Do **not** wait for your next scheduled follow-up appointment if you are actively vomiting or cannot pass wind.
- Do **not** take laxatives, enemas, or "natural" remedies to move your bowels without direct surgical advice.
- Do **not** force yourself to drink large volumes of water to stay hydrated as this may trigger further vomiting and pain.
- Do **not** take extra doses of opioid painkillers (such as codeine or tramadol) as these can further slow bowel motility.
- Do **not** drive yourself to the hospital; have someone else drive you or call for transport if you feel dizzy or weak.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all intake of food and drink immediately.** If your mouth is extremely dry, you may use a damp sponge or take a single tiny sip of water, but do not swallow significant amounts.
2. **Locate your surgical discharge letter or "Going Home" pack right now.** Look specifically for the 24-hour contact number for the **Surgical Assessment Unit (SAU)**, **Acute Surgical Unit (ASAU)**, or the specific **Surgical Ward** where you were treated.
3. **Call the surgical contact number provided on your paperwork.** Tell the staff: "I am a recent post-op abdominal surgery patient with new, persistent vomiting and a bloated, distended abdomen."
   - State the date and type of your operation.
   - Mention if you have stopped passing wind or having bowel movements.
4. **Call 999 immediately if you experience life-threatening symptoms.** Request an ambulance if you have:
   - Vomit that is bright green (bile), dark brown, or smells like faeces.
   - Severe, "doubling-over" abdominal pain that is constant or worsening.
   - A high fever (above 38°C) accompanied by shivering or "rigors."
   - Difficulty breathing or a very rapid heartbeat.
   - Confusion, fainting, or cold, clammy skin.
5. **Call NHS 111 if you cannot reach your surgical team.** Explain that you have recent surgical complications; they can often arrange a direct referral to the hospital’s on-call surgical registrar.
6. **Pack your essential medical information for immediate departure.** Ensure you have:
   - Your physical discharge letter and any operation notes.
   - A complete list of all medications you are currently taking.
   - A record of when you last passed wind or a bowel movement.
   - If you have a stoma, bring spare supplies and note if the output has stopped or changed significantly.
7. **Monitor your symptoms without physical investigation.** Do not press hard on your abdomen or try to "feel" for blocks; simply note if the swelling is visibly increasing or if the pain changes from cramping to constant.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to determine if this is a "normal" part of recovery; let the surgical team make that distinction.
- You do **not** need to worry about your nutritional intake for the next few hours.
- You do **not** need to investigate the specific medical cause, such as an ileus or a mechanical obstruction, before seeking help.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant anxiety when new symptoms appear after surgery. Post-operative bloating and vomiting are recognized complications that surgical teams are specifically trained to manage. Seeking an assessment is the standard, expected step for these symptoms and does not mean you have failed in your recovery.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and escalation advice only. It is not a substitute for the clinical assessment required to diagnose post-operative complications.

## Important note
Abdominal surgery carries risks of bowel motility issues (ileus) or obstructions that require professional intervention. Always prioritize the instructions on your specific hospital discharge paperwork. If you feel your condition is deteriorating rapidly, seek emergency care without delay.
