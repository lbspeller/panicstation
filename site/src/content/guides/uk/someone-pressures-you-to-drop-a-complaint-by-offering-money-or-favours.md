# What to do if… someone pressures you to drop a complaint by offering money or favours (UK)

## Short answer

Do not agree privately or decide on the spot. Move the contact into a written, formal, or specialist support route as soon as you safely can.

## Do not do these things

- Do not accept money, gifts, favours, “help”, references, accommodation, or career support in return for dropping, softening, delaying, or changing your complaint.
- Do not negotiate alone in person if you feel shaken, cornered, watched, or unsafe.
- Do not delete messages, voicemails, emails, call logs, payment records, gift offers, calendar entries, or screenshots connected to the approach.
- Do not let anyone rush you into saying it was “sorted”, “misunderstood”, or “not worth pursuing”.
- Do not hand over your phone, account access, or original records so someone else can “deal with it”.
- Do not confront the person again just to get them to admit what they meant.
- Do not blame yourself for freezing, replying politely, staying quiet, or needing time to think.

## What to do now

1. **End the conversation without agreeing.**  
   A short reply is enough: “I’m not discussing this privately. Please put anything through the formal process.” You do not need to explain, argue, or decide anything there and then.

2. **Write down exactly what happened while it is fresh.**  
   Note the date, time, place, who was there, how they contacted you, the exact words you remember, and whether money, gifts, career help, references, accommodation, introductions, protection, or other favours were mentioned.

3. **Keep the contact in its original form.**  
   Save texts, emails, direct messages, voicemail, call logs, payment details, gift offers, screenshots, or calendar entries in one place. Keep originals where possible, and make a backup to a safe account or device you control.

4. **Tell a safe formal contact that this happened.**  
   If this is through work, university, college, a club, a regulator, or another organisation, send a short written update to the named complaints contact, HR, safeguarding lead, trade union representative, caseworker, student support team, or another safe formal contact. Ask for the contact to be logged separately from the original complaint.

5. **Ask for contact boundaries in writing.**  
   You can ask the organisation handling the complaint to confirm that any future contact about the complaint must go through them, not directly to you. If the person is contacting you through friends, colleagues, classmates, managers, family members, or social media, say that too.

6. **Use specialist support before making any bigger decision.**  
   A UK-wide directory or a specialist sexual violence support service for the nation you are in can help you think through options without pressure to report to police. If you are thinking about reporting, an Independent Sexual Violence Advocate or other specialist advocate may be able to explain the process and support you. If you want medical, practical, or emotional support after sexual assault, you can contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre, a local sexual assault healthcare service, a GP, a sexual health service, or another local health service.

7. **If the pressure includes threats, repeated intimidation, stalking, or you feel unsafe, contact the police.**  
   Use 999 if there is immediate danger or a crime is happening now. If it is not an emergency, you can call 101 or check your local police force website for reporting options.

## What can wait

You do not need to decide today whether to continue, pause, escalate, report to police, accept any formal settlement process, or tell everyone involved the full story again. You also do not need to work out the legal meaning of the offer before you take the simpler step of recording it, keeping the evidence, and moving it away from private pressure.

## Important reassurance

Freezing, doubting yourself, replying politely, or feeling pulled between relief and alarm is a common reaction to pressure. An offer of money or favours can make things feel blurred very quickly; that does not mean you have done anything wrong.

## Scope note

This is first steps only. Later decisions about complaints, employment, education, police, safeguarding, settlement, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. In the UK, pressure linked to a work-related harassment or discrimination complaint may raise victimisation or safeguarding concerns depending on the setting and facts. Threatening, repeated, or coercive contact may also need police or safeguarding involvement. If you are unsure, keep your steps small: do not agree privately, keep the record, and use a formal or specialist support route.
