# What to do if… a memory card is unreadable on multiple devices and you need the photos (UK)

## Short answer
Stop using the card immediately and do not choose “format”, “erase”, “initialise”, “repair”, “scan and fix”, or “First Aid”. If the photos matter, the safest first move is usually to recover from a sector-by-sector image or clone saved to another drive, or to stop DIY and use a reputable data-recovery service.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t press **Format**, **Erase**, **Initialize**, **Initialise**, or any prompt that says it will make the card usable.
- Don’t run file-system repair tools such as **Disk Utility First Aid**, Windows **Scan and fix**, or **CHKDSK** as your first move if you need the photos.
- Don’t keep retrying the card in lots of devices; repeated attempts may add stress or trigger prompts that write to the card.
- Don’t install random “one-click” recovery or repair apps that want to “fix” the file system first.
- Don’t recover files back onto the same card.
- Don’t bend, scratch, wash, heat, freeze, open, tape, or force the card or adapter.

## What to do now
1. **Remove and protect the card.**  
   Take it out of the camera, phone, drone, or reader. Put it in a small bag or envelope so it cannot be damaged. If it is a full-size SD card, you can slide the lock tab to the locked position, but treat this only as an extra precaution because not every device treats it as absolute protection.

2. **Check for an existing copy before touching the card again.**  
   Look for the photos in places that may already have them:
   - Your phone or computer photo library, if you imported them earlier.
   - Cloud photo sync or backup, such as iCloud Photos, Google Photos, OneDrive, or Dropbox.
   - Messages, email threads, shared albums, or social apps where you sent the photos.
   - The camera or phone’s internal storage, if that device can save to both internal storage and a card.

3. **Do one careful detection check only.**  
   Use a known-good card reader and cable. The only question is whether the computer can see a device at all.
   - On **Mac**: open **Disk Utility** and look for the card or device in the sidebar. Do not click **Erase** and do not run **First Aid** at this stage.
   - On **Windows**: open **Disk Management** and check whether the card appears. Do not format it, assign a new volume, run **Scan and fix**, or run **CHKDSK** at this stage.

4. **If the card is detected, prioritise an image or clone before recovery.**  
   The safer DIY pattern is generally to make a sector-by-sector image or clone of the card onto a separate drive with enough free space, then attempt photo recovery from that image or clone only. Do not save recovered files back to the card.

5. **If the photos are irreplaceable, stop DIY before making things worse.**  
   Use a data-recovery service rather than trying more repair tools. Look for a service that gives a written quote or authorisation step before paid work, says it will not write to the original card during diagnosis, and returns the original card. Tell them: “SD or microSD card unreadable on multiple devices; please attempt photo recovery; do not format or repair the original card.”

6. **Keep refund or fault evidence separate from recovery.**  
   Keep the card, packaging, proof of purchase, and a photo or screenshot of any error messages. In the UK, help with faulty goods is usually pursued through the retailer or payment route, but that can wait until the photos are protected or recovery has been attempted.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide today whether to replace your camera, phone, drone, card reader, or card brand.
- You don’t need to “fix the card” so you can keep using it. Treat it as unreliable until recovery is finished.
- You don’t need to argue with a retailer or manufacturer right now if the urgent priority is saving photos.
- You don’t need to redesign your whole backup system in this moment.

## Important reassurance
An unreadable memory card is frightening, but the pause matters. Many avoidable losses happen after a format, repair, or recovery attempt writes new data to the card, so stopping now can preserve options.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent further loss and improve the chance of photo recovery. Imaging, recovery software, warranty issues, payment disputes, or chip-level recovery may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional data-recovery advice, and it is not a guarantee that photos can be recovered.
