# What to do if… police ask you to meet them to sign paperwork but will not explain what it is (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the request as potentially serious. Do not attend voluntarily or sign anything until you know what the paperwork is and have had legal advice.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go in “just to get it over with” or attend alone because it sounds informal.
- Don’t sign anything you have not read in full and understood, especially anything described as a statement, interview record, admission, caution, community resolution, penalty notice, bail, undertaking, or agreement.
- Don’t give your side of the story by phone, text, email, or social media message before getting advice.
- Don’t let anyone rush you by saying it is routine or quicker without a solicitor.
- Don’t voluntarily unlock devices, hand over passcodes, or agree to a search unless a solicitor has confirmed what you have to do.

## What to do now
1. Slow the situation down. Use calm, clear words: “I am willing to cooperate, but I need to know what this is about and I want legal advice before I meet or sign anything.”

2. Ask whether this is voluntary or required. Ask:
   - “Am I being asked to attend voluntarily, or am I required to attend?”
   - “Am I suspected of an offence?”
   - “Is this an interview? If so, is it under caution?”
   - “What is the document called, exactly?”
   - “What would I be signing to confirm?”

3. Ask for enough detail to pass to a solicitor. Ask for:
   - the officer’s name, collar number if available, station, and unit
   - the proposed date, time, and place
   - whether you are being treated as a witness, suspect, or something else
   - the alleged offence or incident, if there is one
   - whether you are free not to attend or to rearrange

4. Ask for legal advice before any meeting or signing. Say: “I want to speak to a solicitor before attending or signing anything.” If the contact is at a police station or involves questioning, ask how legal advice will be arranged, including duty solicitor arrangements where available.

5. Ask for the basics in writing. A text or email is enough. If they will not confirm what the meeting is for, pause and get legal advice before agreeing to attend voluntarily.

6. Check it is really the police. If the call or message feels odd, end the contact politely and call back using the police force’s published main number or station switchboard. Ask to be put through to the officer by name.

7. If they say you may be arrested if you do not attend, do not argue about the facts. Ask: “Are you saying I am required to attend now, or that you intend to arrest me if I do not attend?” Then contact a solicitor promptly and tell them exactly what was said.

8. If you are already at the station or with the officer, say: “I do not want to sign or answer questions until I have spoken to a solicitor.” If you feel unwell, overwhelmed, need medication, need an interpreter, or need support because of age, disability, mental health, injury, or communication needs, say so clearly.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a statement or fully explain what happened.
- You do not need to answer detailed questions before getting advice.
- You do not need to agree to the first suggested appointment time if it is voluntary and you need time to get legal advice.
- You do not need to sort every message, document, or recording now. Keep things unchanged and do not delete, edit, or send material around unless a solicitor asks you to.
- You do not need to decide whether the officer is being fair, unfair, friendly, or threatening. The immediate task is to avoid signing or saying something without advice.

## Important reassurance
Asking for legal advice is normal. It does not mean you are being difficult, and it does not mean you have done anything wrong.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to prevent irreversible mistakes such as unintended admissions, signing the wrong document, or being questioned without support. Later decisions may depend on whether you are a witness, a suspect, or being asked to sign a statement, interview record, caution, bail form, undertaking, or another document, and may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Police processes and wording vary across the UK and by circumstances, so get advice from a solicitor before attending, answering questions, or signing paperwork.
