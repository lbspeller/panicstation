# What to do if… someone keeps trying to isolate you from your group (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a safety issue. Stay with people you trust and move towards staff, security, or a busier public area rather than explaining yourself to the person.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go somewhere quieter with them to avoid awkwardness.
- Don’t leave for the toilet, exit, cloakroom, smoking area, taxi rank, or outside air on your own if they are hovering nearby.
- Don’t accept a lift, walk home, shortcut, drink, or private conversation from them.
- Don’t negotiate a short amount of alone time if you do not want to be alone.
- Don’t worry about seeming rude; getting help early is allowed.

## What to do now
1. **Move into the middle of your group.** Stand or sit where friends are around you, not at the edge nearest a door, corridor, toilet route, smoking area, or exit.
2. **Say one clear sentence to someone in your group.** For example: “I do not want to be alone with them. Please stay with me.” Say it plainly enough that at least one trusted person understands.
3. **Choose one buddy and stay attached to them.** If the group is scattered, pick one person now. Go together for water, the toilet, a coat, fresh air, transport, or payment.
4. **Move closer to staff or security.** Go to the bar, door staff, cloakroom, reception, shop counter, transport staff, or another staffed place. Say: “I feel unsafe. That person keeps trying to get me alone. Can you help me stay away from them?”
5. **Use Ask for Angela if the venue offers it.** At a participating venue, you can ask staff for “Angela” as a discreet way to say you need help. If you do not see a poster, you can still ask staff directly for help.
6. **Change the plan with your group, not with the person pressuring you.** Move to another area, leave together, or wait somewhere busy and well lit. Do not linger outside alone.
7. **Keep your phone, keys, bag, and drink with you if you safely can.** If you have to choose, prioritise staying with safe people over collecting belongings alone.
8. **Call 999 if you feel in immediate danger.** If you cannot speak safely, call 999 and try to respond by whispering, coughing, tapping, or pressing 55 on a mobile when prompted. For non-emergency police help, call 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to report anything.
- You do not need to confront the person or explain your reasons.
- You do not need to decide whether their behaviour “counts” as harassment, stalking, or sexual violence.
- You do not need to write a perfect account of what happened right now.
- You do not need to finish the night out, stay at the venue, or protect the person’s feelings.

## Important reassurance
Wanting to stay with the group is enough. You do not have to be certain about their intentions before asking friends, staff, security, or police for help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on getting through the immediate situation safely. Later decisions about support, reporting, complaints, travel home, or what to do next may need specialist help at your pace.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, safeguarding, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or need urgent help, call 999.
