# What to do if… you are told you must attend a formal work meeting alone (UK)

## Short answer
Before you agree to go into a formal meeting alone, ask what type of meeting it is and what outcomes are possible. If it is a disciplinary hearing, or a grievance meeting where the issue is about a legal or contractual duty, make a written request to be accompanied and ask to rearrange the meeting if your companion cannot attend.

## Do not do these things
- Do not treat it as “just an informal chat” if it could lead to a warning, dismissal, demotion, loss of pay, or another formal sanction.
- Do not resign on the spot, threaten resignation, or agree to leave while you are trying to steady yourself.
- Do not sign an outcome letter, agreement, or meeting notes unless you have read them and are clear what you are agreeing to.
- Do not send long, emotional messages while you are upset; keep requests short and factual.
- Do not secretly record the meeting unless you clearly understand your workplace rules and the possible consequences. If you want a recording, ask in advance.

## What to do now
1. **Ask in writing what type of meeting it is and what could happen.**  
   Send: “Please confirm whether this is an investigation meeting, disciplinary hearing, grievance meeting, or another type of meeting. Please also confirm whether any formal outcome, such as a warning, dismissal, demotion, loss of pay, or other disciplinary action, is possible.”

2. **Ask for the basic information before you attend.**  
   Ask for the concern or allegation in writing, the evidence they plan to rely on, the relevant workplace policy, who will be in the meeting, whether notes will be taken, and whether you will receive a copy.

3. **If it is a disciplinary hearing, make a clear written request to be accompanied.**  
   Name the person you want to bring and say whether they are a fellow worker, a workplace trade union representative certified by the union, or an official employed by a trade union.

4. **If it is a grievance meeting, make the same request and explain why the right applies.**  
   Say that your grievance is about a legal or contractual duty if that is true. If you are not sure whether the legal right applies, ask the employer to allow a companion as good practice.

5. **If your companion cannot attend, propose a reasonable new time.**  
   Give a specific date and time that is practical and not more than 5 working days after the original meeting date.

6. **If they call it an investigation meeting and say you must attend alone, ask them to confirm the limits of the meeting.**  
   Send: “Please confirm that this investigation meeting will not by itself result in disciplinary action, and that any decision would only be made later at a disciplinary hearing.” Then ask whether they will still allow a companion as good practice.

7. **If you are disabled or need communication support, ask for the adjustment you need.**  
   This might be permission to bring a support worker, interpreter, carer, or another person who can help you take part as a reasonable adjustment or communication support. Keep the request specific: who you want to bring, why they are needed, and what they will do.

8. **If they still refuse and the meeting goes ahead, protect your position calmly at the start.**  
   Say: “I asked to be accompanied and that was refused. Please record that in the notes. I will do my best to answer, but I may need breaks and may need to follow up in writing after I have reviewed the documents.”

9. **If you do not have the allegation, evidence, or policy, ask for a pause before answering detailed questions.**  
   Say: “I have not had the information I need to answer fully. Please pause or rearrange after I have had a reasonable chance to prepare.”

10. **Make your own short record.**  
   Note the date and time, who attended, what type of meeting they said it was, what outcomes were mentioned, what you asked for, what they refused, and any deadlines they gave you.

11. **Get support quickly if the stakes are high.**  
   Contact your union if you have one. If dismissal, discrimination, victimisation, or detriment is a realistic risk, consider contacting Acas and getting specialist employment advice.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to raise a formal grievance, appeal an outcome, or start a legal claim.
- You do not need to give a full written response until you know what the meeting is about and what evidence or policy they are relying on.
- You do not need to agree in the room that the notes are accurate; you can ask to review them and correct anything important in writing.
- You do not need to choose a long-term strategy while you are still trying to understand the immediate meeting.

## Important reassurance
Being told you must attend alone can make the situation feel closed down. You can still slow the process down by asking what kind of meeting it is, asking what outcomes are possible, making a clear companion request, and keeping a written record.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you avoid immediate mistakes and buy time. Later decisions about grievances, appeals, settlement, tribunal deadlines, discrimination, dismissal, victimisation, or detriment may need specialist employment help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Your position can depend on your contract, workplace policies, union arrangements, disability or communication needs, and the exact type of meeting.
