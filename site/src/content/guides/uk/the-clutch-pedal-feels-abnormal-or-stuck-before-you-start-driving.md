# What to do if… the clutch pedal feels abnormal or stuck before you start driving (UK)

## Short answer
Do not set off. Keep the vehicle stationary and arrange help unless you are in a safe place, the pedal feel is clearly normal again, and you can select gears while fully stationary without difficulty, noise, or movement.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “just try it” on a public road, in traffic, on a hill, or around pedestrians.
- Do not rely on pumping the clutch as a plan to drive; if pumping changes the feel, treat that as a warning sign and pause for breakdown help or a garage check.
- Do not force the gear lever.
- Do not crawl under the vehicle in an unsafe place or near moving traffic.
- Do not let time pressure override your judgement about whether you can control the vehicle safely.

## What to do now
1. Secure the vehicle where it is. Put the handbrake on, keep your foot on the brake if needed, and do not try to move off. If the vehicle is causing an obstruction, switch on the hazard warning lights while you arrange help.
2. If there is immediate danger, a collision, or the vehicle is stopped where traffic cannot safely get around you, keep the hazard warning lights on and call 999 for the police. Do not step into moving traffic.
3. Check for a simple obstruction at the pedal. Look for a trapped floor mat, loose bottle, bag, cable, or other object that could stop the clutch pedal moving freely. Remove anything in the way and check the pedal once.
4. Test the pedal without committing to movement. With the vehicle stationary, press and release the clutch a few times. Stop and do not drive if it sticks down, does not return, suddenly feels much softer or heavier than normal, or has much more or much less travel than usual.
5. Only if you are on flat ground with clear space around the vehicle, do a stationary gear check. If you are not comfortable doing this, skip it and arrange help. Keep the handbrake on, cover the foot brake, press the clutch fully down, start the engine, and gently try selecting 1st gear and reverse. If selection is difficult, crunchy, inconsistent, or the vehicle tries to move, switch off and stop.
6. Look only for obvious warning signs you can check safely. Check for dashboard warning lights that stay on, fresh fluid under the vehicle, or unusual dampness around the clutch pedal area. Do not dismantle anything.
7. If the vehicle has a hydraulic clutch, low fluid may indicate a leak. On many cars the clutch may share fluid with the brake reservoir, but designs vary, so only check the handbook or reservoir markings if it is safe and easy.
8. Arrange assistance rather than “driving carefully.” Call your breakdown provider for recovery to a garage. If it is a work or fleet vehicle, tell the responsible contact that you cannot operate it safely and need recovery or inspection.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the fault is the clutch, hydraulics, cable, linkage, gearbox, or driver technique right now.
- You do not need to decide repair costs, parts, or which garage while you are still dealing with the immediate safety decision.
- You do not need to prove that the vehicle is unsafe. Uncertainty about control is enough to stop and get help.
- You can check for an outstanding safety recall once the vehicle is secure and help is being arranged.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to second-guess yourself when a car seems almost driveable. If a primary control does not feel right, choosing not to drive is a sensible safety decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to help you avoid loss of control and reach the next safe step. A qualified mechanic or appropriate fleet support may need to assess the vehicle before it goes back on the road.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, mechanical, or other professional advice. If you cannot confidently control the vehicle, the safest option is not to drive and to arrange professional assistance.
