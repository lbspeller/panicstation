# What to do if… you notice someone repeatedly watching your drink and hovering when you set it down (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a credible safety risk: stop consuming the drink immediately, physically move to a crowded or staff-monitored area, and alert venue security or trusted companions.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t continue drinking to "test" if the beverage has been tampered with or to avoid appearing rude.
- Don’t leave the drink unattended again to go to the toilet, dance, or vape.
- Don’t attempt to confront or "catch" the person yourself.
- Don’t accept any replacement drinks from the suspect or people you do not know.
- Don’t leave the venue alone or agree to go to a quieter area with the person who was hovering.

## What to do now
1. **Stop drinking from the container immediately.**  
   Treat any drink that has been out of your direct sight or control as unsafe. Abandon it or take it directly to the bar to alert staff.
2. **Move to a safer, high-traffic location at once.**  
   **Walk to the bar, a security station, or a group of people you trust.** If you are with friends, use a clear directive such as "I feel unsafe, we need to move to the bar right now."
3. **Alert venue staff or security using the Ask for Angela scheme or direct language.**  
   **Check for the "Ask for Angela Official Pledge" signage, which indicates staff are trained in 2026 safety protocols.** If you cannot see signage, tell staff directly: "I am concerned someone is attempting to spike my drink; I need to stay in a safe space while I arrange a way home."
4. **Identify and record the person’s appearance if safe to do so.**  
   **Note their clothing, physical features, and the time you noticed the behaviour on your phone.** Do not take photos or videos if it risks a direct confrontation or escalation.
5. **Request assistance with safe transport away from the venue.**  
   **Ask staff to stay with you while you wait for a verified taxi or a trusted person to collect you.** Do not wait outside the venue alone.
6. **Seek medical help immediately if you start to feel unexpected symptoms.**  
   **Call 999 if you experience sudden confusion, nausea, loss of coordination, or extreme drowsiness.** If you are worried but stable, call NHS 111 for clinical advice.
7. **Contact the police to report the attempted spiking.**  
   **Report via 999 in an emergency or use the police.uk online reporting tool for non-emergencies.** As of April 2026, spiking is a standalone criminal offence; the police can facilitate forensic testing, which is most effective if a sample is taken within 12 hours of the incident.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether you want to press charges or provide a full witness statement tonight.
- You do not need to identify exactly what substance might have been used.
- You do not need to prove that the person successfully tampered with the drink to report the suspicious behaviour.

## Important reassurance
Trusting your instinct when you notice someone monitoring your movements or your drink is a protective action. Acting on these observations is not an overreaction; it is a necessary step to maintain your physical safety. Panic or hesitation in these moments is a normal response to a perceived threat.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active safety concern. It does not replace medical diagnosis or legal advice. Decisions regarding long-term reporting or forensic evidence should be made in consultation with specialists.

## Important note
Spiking is a serious crime. If you feel unwell or believe you have already consumed a tampered drink, seek emergency medical attention (999). You have the right to feel safe in public spaces, and venue staff have a duty of care to assist you when you report a threat.
