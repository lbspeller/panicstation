# What to do if… you discover someone has saved intimate photos you sent them (UK)

## Short answer
Pause contact for now, do not send anything else, and make a minimal record of what happened without re-sharing the images. If there is any immediate danger, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send more images, “proof,” passwords, money, or personal details to try to make it stop.
- Don’t agree to demands for money, more images, a meeting, or silence.
- Don’t threaten them publicly or start a public argument; it can increase the risk of wider sharing.
- Don’t keep rereading upsetting messages after you have saved the key details.
- Don’t meet them alone to “sort it out” if there is any intimidation, coercion, stalking, blackmail, or safety risk.
- Don’t forward the intimate images to friends or helpers; share links, usernames, dates, and screenshots of messages instead.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If this person is nearby, leave the conversation, move to a place where you feel safer, and contact someone you trust to stay with you or be available.
2. **Stop the flow of new material.** Assume anything you send could be saved or copied. Do not send more intimate content, explanations, passwords, account codes, or “one last message” while you feel panicked.
3. **Make a minimal record without re-sharing the images.**
   - Screenshot messages, threats, usernames, profile links, phone numbers, dates, and times.
   - Write a short timeline of what you discovered and when.
   - If anything has already been posted online, copy the URL and note the account name.
4. **Secure accounts that could give them more access.**
   - Change the password for your email account first, then key social media and cloud accounts.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication.
   - Check active sessions or logged-in devices and sign out of anything you do not recognise.
5. **Use specialist adult support if you are 18 or over.** Contact the Revenge Porn Helpline for confidential UK support with intimate image abuse, including practical help with reporting and removal where possible.
6. **Use prevention and takedown tools where relevant.** StopNCII can create a digital fingerprint, called a hash, of an intimate image or video and share that hash with participating companies to help detect and remove matching uploads on their services.
7. **If there are threats, coercion, stalking, blackmail, or sharing without consent, report it.**
   - If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
   - If it is not an emergency, report it to your local police force or use the Police.uk reporting route.
   - For sextortion or blackmail, the National Crime Agency information page can help you understand the immediate reporting route.
8. **If you are under 18, or anyone in the images is under 18, use child-safety routes.**
   - Report concerns about online sexual abuse or exploitation to CEOP.
   - If a nude or sexual image or video of you is online, Childline’s Report Remove service can help young people under 18 in the UK report it for removal.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to confront them, forgive them, end the relationship, tell everyone, or make a legal decision.
- You do not need to gather every possible detail before asking for help; a few key screenshots, URLs if there are any, and a short timeline are enough to start.
- You do not need to contact the person now. Get support first, especially if there are threats, pressure, stalking, blackmail, or a history of abuse.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shocked, violated, or panicky is an understandable response. The priority is not to fix everything at once; it is to stop any further exposure, keep a basic record, and bring in specialist support.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation, reduce immediate risk, and connect you with specialist support. Later decisions about police, legal steps, school, work, relationships, or longer-term safety planning may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide provides general first-step information and is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
