# What to do if… interior doors suddenly start sticking and you suspect structural movement or swelling (UK)

## Short answer
Stop forcing the door and conduct a quick check for major diagonal cracks (wider than a £1 coin), sagging ceilings, or the smell of gas. If you suspect immediate structural failure, evacuate and call **999**; otherwise, document the changes and contact your buildings insurer or landlord immediately to report potential subsidence or moisture damage.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t plane, sand, or cut the doors to fit (this destroys evidence for surveyors and may hide a progressive structural failure).
- Don’t keep slamming or forcing a door that is binding (this can cause further damage to the architrave or wall finishes).
- Don’t ignore it if several doors and windows are sticking simultaneously or if movement is paired with new, wide cracks.
- Don’t enter lofts, basements, or crawl spaces if you see bowed walls or falling debris.
- Don’t attempt to "dry out" the area with high heat if a leak is suspected, as rapid drying can cause timber to warp or split further.

## What to do now
1. **Ensure you have at least one clear, easy-to-open exit route from the property.** If the sticking door is an essential fire or escape door, find an alternative route and keep keys for that exit immediately accessible.
2. **Perform a 60-second "safety scan" of the immediate area.** Look specifically for:
   - diagonal cracks over 3mm wide (roughly the thickness of a £1 coin) above door or window frames,
   - the smell of gas (which may indicate a ruptured pipe from movement),
   - fresh water stains, bulging plaster, or the sound of dripping water,
   - gaps opening between the floor and the skirting board.
3. **If you believe the structure is actively failing, evacuate and escalate.**
   - If there is a risk of collapse or public danger, **call 999**.
   - If you smell gas, **call 0800 111999** (National Gas Emergency Service).
   - If the situation is urgent but not a life-safety emergency, report it to your local council's **Building Control** department (England/Wales) or **Building Standards** (Scotland).
4. **Isolate potential moisture sources if you suspect swelling over movement.**
   - If you find a leak, turn off the internal stop tap (usually under the kitchen sink).
   - If the sticking is following heavy rain, check that external gutters are not overflowing near the affected wall.
5. **Take dated photographic evidence before anything is moved or repaired.**
   - Photograph the specific point where the door is rubbing or binding.
   - Capture a wide-angle shot of the door frame to show any "racking" (the frame becoming a parallelogram).
   - Take a photo of any cracks with a £1 coin held next to them for scale.
6. **Report the issue to the party responsible for the building structure.**
   - **Homeowners:** Call your **buildings insurance provider**’s emergency or claims line; they will often appoint a loss adjuster or RICS surveyor to assess for subsidence.
   - **Renters:** Notify your landlord or letting agent **in writing** immediately, stating that the sudden sticking may indicate a structural or damp emergency.
   - **Leaseholders:** Contact the block manager or freeholder, as they are typically responsible for the external and load-bearing walls.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause (subsidence, heave, or humidity) right now.
- You do not need to hire your own private surveyor until you have spoken to your insurer or landlord.
- You do not need to worry about cosmetic repairs to plaster or paint until the structural movement has been stabilized.

## Important reassurance
Many doors stick due to simple seasonal humidity changes or minor "settlement" in newer properties. Identifying the issue early and taking photos ensures that if there is a larger problem, you have the evidence needed for a successful insurance claim or landlord repair.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. It is intended to help you assess immediate safety and preserve evidence for professional inspection.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace the advice of a structural engineer, chartered surveyor, or emergency services. If you suspect an immediate risk of collapse, evacuate the building.
