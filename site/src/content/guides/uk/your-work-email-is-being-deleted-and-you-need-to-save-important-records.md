# What to do if… your work email is being deleted and you need to save important records (UK)

## Short answer
Act today: get the cutoff date in writing and ask HR, IT, or your manager to retain and move the specific records you need into an approved company-owned place, such as a shared mailbox, SharePoint, Teams, or a team drive, before your access ends.

## Do not do these things
- Do not forward large volumes of work emails or attachments to your personal email or personal cloud storage just in case.
- Do not mass-download confidential files, client data, or sensitive HR material to a personal device.
- Do not delete emails, folders, Teams chats, or files to tidy up while access is changing.
- Do not assume there will be an archive you can personally access later.
- Do not wait until your final day, because account access may end earlier than expected.

## What to do now
1. **Get the cutoff and retention position in writing.** Email HR, IT, or the person who told you, and ask:
   - the exact date and time your mailbox access ends;
   - whether the mailbox will be deleted, disabled, archived, or retained;
   - who will be able to access it after your access ends;
   - whether your OneDrive, Teams, SharePoint, HR portal, or other work systems are also changing.

2. **Make a short must-keep list.** Spend 10 minutes listing the exact records you need, using subject lines, dates, senders, file names, and locations where you can. Include only clearly important items, such as:
   - your contract, offer letter, job description, or agreed changes to your role;
   - pay, pension, holiday, or benefits emails that are not already in an HR portal;
   - performance reviews, objectives, promotion, bonus, or pay change confirmations;
   - disciplinary, grievance, warning, sickness, adjustment, or capability records;
   - project or client threads that someone else needs for handover.

3. **Ask for an approved transfer route.** Ask HR, IT, or your manager to move the specific records into a company-owned place rather than making unauthorised personal copies. Suitable options may include:
   - moving key folders to a role or project shared mailbox;
   - saving essential threads or files into a team-owned SharePoint or Teams channel;
   - creating a restricted handover folder with a named owner;
   - confirming where HR records, payslips, reviews, or case documents can be accessed after your login ends.

4. **Ask IT to preserve the mailbox while the transfer is sorted.** Use simple wording such as: “Please retain my mailbox under the organisation’s retention policy until the agreed handover or retrieval of the listed records is complete.” Ask for the name or role of the person who will be responsible for the mailbox after your access ends.

5. **If you need information about you, make a narrow Subject Access Request.** A Subject Access Request is for your personal data, not every email in the mailbox. Put the request in writing if you can, and make it specific by giving date ranges, topics, senders, and the type of record you need. Some information may be withheld or redacted, for example where it includes other people’s data or legally privileged material.

6. **Create a retrieval map before you are locked out.** In a company-owned document or email, list:
   - where key records are stored;
   - the folders, systems, dates, and subject lines needed to find them;
   - who owns each record after you leave or lose access;
   - any urgent handover items that must be available to your team.

7. **Get workplace help if this is connected to a dispute.** If you are in a union, contact your union rep. You can also contact Acas for workplace guidance, or Citizens Advice for help with a work problem. Keep your requests factual and specific while you are getting advice.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to raise a formal grievance, bring a claim, or negotiate anything.
- You do not need to organise every old email.
- You do not need to explain the whole history of the problem.
- You do not need to prove everything now; the immediate job is to stop important records becoming hard to find.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to feel panicked when access to records may disappear. The safest first move is a clear written request for retention and approved transfer, not a rushed download or a large personal forwarding exercise.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise access and reduce the risk of avoidable loss. Later decisions about complaints, settlement, employment rights, data protection concerns, or legal action may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, employment, data protection, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Workplace systems, confidentiality duties, retention policies, and what you may copy can vary, so use approved channels and keep requests specific and in writing.
