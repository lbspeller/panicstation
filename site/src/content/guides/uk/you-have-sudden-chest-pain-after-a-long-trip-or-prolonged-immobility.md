# What to do if… you have sudden chest pain after a long trip or prolonged immobility (UK)

## Short answer
Call 999 immediately for an ambulance. Sudden chest pain following travel or immobility requires urgent professional assessment to rule out a blood clot in the lung or a heart emergency.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if the pain subsides or attempt to sleep.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital or an Urgent Care Centre.
- Do not walk, stretch, or exercise to "test" the pain or "loosen" a stiff leg.
- Do not massage or rub a painful or swollen leg.
- Do not take indigestion medication, aspirin, or painkillers as a reason to delay calling 999.
- Do not assume the pain is simple muscle strain or travel-related fatigue.

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 immediately and state that you have sudden chest pain after a period of immobility.**
   - Mention specifically if you have any of the following:
   - Sharp pain that worsens when you breathe in or cough.
   - Unexplained shortness of breath or gasping.
   - A painful, red, warm, or swollen calf or thigh.
   - Coughing up even small amounts of blood.
   - A heart rate that feels significantly faster than usual.
2. **Stop all physical activity and sit in a comfortable, upright position.**
   - Stay still to reduce the oxygen demand on your heart and lungs. 
   - Choose a chair or the floor rather than lying flat if breathing is difficult.
3. **If you are alone, unlock your front door and return to your seat.**
   - This ensures paramedics can enter without you needing to get up again.
   - Keep your phone within reach on speakerphone.
4. **Gather your essential items only if they are within immediate reach.**
   - This includes:
   - Your current medications or a printed prescription list.
   - Your phone charger and identification.
   - Any regular inhalers or GTN (glyceryl trinitrate) spray.
5. **Aspirin: only if explicitly directed by the 999 call handler.**
   - Do not take aspirin unless the operator tells you to do so. 
   - If directed, chew one 300mg adult aspirin. 
   - **Do not take aspirin** if you have a known allergy, a bleeding disorder, are on existing blood thinners (like warfarin, apixaban, or rivaroxaban), or are under age 16.
6. **Use prescribed heart medication only if it is part of your existing care plan.**
   - If you have GTN for diagnosed angina, use it as prescribed. 
   - If the pain is new, different, or does not ease immediately with GTN, do not wait for a second dose before calling 999.
7. **If the person collapses and is not breathing normally, start CPR.**
   - The 999 operator will provide instructions for chest compressions.
   - If a Public Access Defibrillator (AED) is nearby and a second person is available, ask them to retrieve it.

## What can wait
- Determining whether the pain is a pulmonary embolism, a heart attack, or a muscle strain.
- Contacting your GP or calling NHS 111 (these services are not for sudden emergency chest pain).
- Cancelling travel plans, notifying your employer, or sorting luggage and tickets.
- Researching symptoms online or waiting for a family member to arrive.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel hesitant about calling emergency services, especially if you feel "mostly fine" otherwise. However, symptoms of a post-travel blood clot can be subtle or sharp, and hospital-grade tests are the only way to ensure your safety. Paramedics prefer to find a minor issue than to arrive after a situation has escalated.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency contact instructions only. It does not replace professional medical diagnosis or clinical treatment.

## Important note
Chest pain following immobility is a high-priority medical symptom in the UK. Emergency protocols are designed to assess these risks quickly. Always follow the direct instructions of the 999 call handler over any generalized advice.
