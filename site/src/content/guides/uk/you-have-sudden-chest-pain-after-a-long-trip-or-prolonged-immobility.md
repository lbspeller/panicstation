# What to do if… you have sudden chest pain after a long trip or prolonged immobility (UK)

## Short answer
Call 999 now for an ambulance if chest pain is sudden, severe, does not go away, or comes with shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, light-headedness, faintness, a very fast heartbeat, coughing blood, or one painful swollen leg. Do not drive yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if serious chest pain settles on its own.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E or ask someone to speed you there.
- Do not walk around to test it or try to stretch it out.
- Do not take indigestion remedies or painkillers to delay getting help.
- Do not eat, drink alcohol, smoke, or use calming substances if doing that delays calling 999.
- Do not take aspirin instead of calling 999.

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 immediately** and say: “I have sudden chest pain after a long trip or a long period of sitting still.”
   - Tell them if you have difficulty breathing, pain worse on breathing or coughing, coughing blood, a very fast heartbeat, fainting or near-fainting, or one leg that is swollen or painful.
2. **Stop what you are doing and rest.**
   - Sit upright, or in the position that makes breathing easiest. Keep movement minimal.
3. **If someone is with you, ask them to stay.**
   - Ask them to bring your phone, charger, front door key, a list of medicines, and any inhaler or GTN spray you normally use.
4. **If you are alone, make access easier without exerting yourself.**
   - Unlock the door if you can do that safely, turn on a light, and keep your phone on speaker near you.
5. **Aspirin: only after calling 999.**
   - Follow the 999 call handler’s instructions.
   - If the pain feels like a heart attack, such as heavy, tight, squeezing, or pressure-like central chest pain, and a 300mg aspirin is already within reach, you may be told to chew and swallow it while waiting.
   - Do not take aspirin if you are allergic to it, under 16, or have been told not to take it. If you are unsure, ask the 999 call handler.
6. **If you have prescribed GTN for angina, use it only as you have been told to.**
   - If this pain is new, severe, different from your usual angina, or not easing quickly, treat it as an emergency and do not wait to call 999.
7. **If someone collapses or stops breathing normally, call 999 and start CPR.**
   - Use an AED if one is available and follow the voice instructions.

## What can wait
- Working out whether it is a clot, heart problem, muscle strain, reflux, anxiety, or something else.
- Phoning your GP or NHS 111 instead of 999 for sudden serious chest symptoms.
- Searching online, finishing your journey, or getting home first.
- Sorting tickets, insurance, work messages, or family updates until emergency help is on the way.

## Important reassurance
Chest pain after prolonged immobility can have several causes, and some need urgent treatment even if the symptoms do not feel dramatic at first. Calling 999 is a safe and reasonable first step when these symptoms are happening now.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to keep you safe in the moment. Clinicians may need tests to rule out serious causes, and later decisions may need medical specialist help.

## Important note
This guide provides general information for immediate safety and is not medical or professional advice. If symptoms are severe, new, worsening, or you feel something is seriously wrong, treat it as an emergency and call 999.
