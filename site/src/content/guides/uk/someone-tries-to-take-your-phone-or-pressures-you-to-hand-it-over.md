# What to do if… someone tries to take your phone or pressures you to hand it over (UK)

## Short answer
Keep hold of your phone, create space, and move towards other people, staff, or a lit busy place. If you feel in immediate danger, someone is using or threatening violence, or the grabbing is happening now, call 999 as soon as you safely can.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hand your phone over “just for a second”, even if the person seems upset or urgent.
- Don’t unlock your phone to prove anything, let them type a number, or let them see your passcode.
- Don’t move with them to a quieter spot, doorway, side street, car, lift, stairwell, or place behind you.
- Don’t get into a tug-of-war if they are pulling hard; your safety matters more than the device.
- Don’t follow them if they run off with it.
- Don’t stay in place trying to reason with them while they crowd your space.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the phone and create space.**  
   Put the phone away in a deep pocket or bag if you can do that without fumbling. Keep one hand free, step back, and turn your body so the phone side is away from them.

2. **Use one clear boundary phrase.**  
   Say, “No. I can’t help you,” or “Step back.” Repeat it once if needed, then stop explaining.

3. **Leave the interaction and move towards safer people.**  
   Walk into the nearest shop, café, reception, ticket barrier area, staffed desk, or visible group of people. If you are near transport, move towards staff, barriers, help points, or a well-lit, busy platform area.

4. **Make it public if they keep coming closer.**  
   Say clearly, “I don’t know you. Please step back.” If there is a specific person nearby, say, “Can you help me? This person is trying to take my phone.”

5. **Use the right reporting route for the situation.**
   - If you are in immediate danger, someone is using or threatening violence, or a theft is happening now, call 999 or ask someone nearby to call 999.
   - If you are now safe and want to report a non-emergency crime or attempted theft, report online through the police force for the area or call 101.
   - If it happened on the railway or at a station and it is not an emergency, contact British Transport Police by texting 61016 or calling 0800 40 50 40.

6. **Once safe, protect the phone and accounts.**  
   If the phone was unlocked, they saw your screen or passcode, or they got close enough for that to be possible, treat it as a possible account-risk moment:
   - Change your phone unlock code when you can do it safely.
   - Use another trusted device to lock the phone, mark it as lost, or check that it is still under your control.
   - If the phone was taken, contact your mobile network provider as soon as possible so they can tell you how to block the SIM or device.
   - Check banking, payment, email, and password-reset alerts for anything unexpected.
   - Contact your bank promptly if payment apps may be exposed or you see suspicious activity.

7. **If the phone was taken or nearly taken, note the key facts.**  
   Write down the location, time, direction they went, description, and any bike, scooter, vehicle, or station details while they are fresh. Do not chase them to get more details.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether you “should have helped” or replaying the conversation.
- Insurance claims, replacing the handset, or switching numbers.
- A full written statement beyond the key facts needed for a report.
- Longer account-security checks once the phone, bank, email, and payment basics are safe.

## Important reassurance
Urgency, guilt, or “I only need one quick call” can be used to pressure you into handing over your phone. You are allowed to refuse without explaining. The priority is getting yourself away from pressure, force, or isolation.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment someone is becoming pushy, threatening, or physical about your phone. Later decisions about police reports, insurance, replacement devices, or wider account checks may need help from the police, your bank, your mobile network, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cyber-security, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or the incident is in progress, call 999. For non-emergency reporting, use 101 or the relevant online police reporting route; use British Transport Police contact options for non-emergency incidents on the rail network.
