# What to do if… you are asked to provide fingerprints, a DNA sample, or other identification for a case (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and ask the officer to state clearly if the request is **mandatory** or **voluntary**—then request a free solicitor before signing any consent forms or answering questions.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree to provide a "voluntary" sample or sign a "consent to retain" form just to speed up the process.
- Don’t physically resist if you are in custody—state your objection clearly and verbally, and allow your solicitor to challenge the legality later.
- Don’t assume a sample is "just for elimination"—voluntary samples can still be loaded onto databases if you sign the wrong form.
- Don’t provide context or explanations (e.g., "I was there but I didn't see it") while discussing the sample request.
- Don’t let yourself be pressured into a "voluntary" identification procedure on the spot if you are not under arrest; you have the right to leave and seek advice.

## What to do now
1. **Request the specific legal grounds and the officer's details immediately.** **The entire first sentence of each step must be bolded.** Ask for the officer’s name, collar number, and the station they are based at.
2. **Determine if you are being treated as a suspect, a witness, or a volunteer.** **Ask clearly: "Am I a suspect or a witness in this case?"**
   - If you are a witness or volunteer, providing a sample is almost always voluntary and requires your written consent.
   - If you are a suspect under arrest for a recordable offence, police generally have the power to take non-intimate samples (fingerprints and mouth swabs) without your consent.
3. **Ask the officer: "Am I legally required to provide this sample or attend this procedure right now?"** **Listen for the specific power cited (such as PACE Section 61 or 63).**
   - If they say you are required to attend at a later date, ask for this request in writing.
   - Under the 2022 Act, police can specify a date and time for attendance for biometrics if you were not previously sampled.
4. **Identify the type of sample being requested to understand your rights.** **Ask: "Is this an intimate or non-intimate sample?"**
   - Non-intimate: Fingerprints, mouth swabs, skin swabs, or hair (not pubic). These can often be taken without consent from arrested suspects.
   - Intimate: Blood, urine, dental impressions, or swabs from other orifices. These require your written consent and authorization from an officer of at least Inspector rank.
5. **Request a free duty solicitor if you are at a police station or under arrest.** **Say: "I want to speak to the duty solicitor before I agree to or provide anything."**
   - This is your right under PACE Code C and it is free of charge.
   - Do not sign any "consent to retain" or "voluntary disclosure" forms until your solicitor has reviewed them.
6. **If you are a juvenile or a vulnerable adult, demand an appropriate adult.** **Do not permit any samples to be taken or procedures to be started until an appropriate adult is present.**
   - This applies to anyone under 18 or anyone who is "mentally disordered or otherwise mentally vulnerable."
7. **Inquire about the retention and destruction timeline for the sample.** **Ask: "When will the biological sample itself be destroyed?"**
   - Under the Protection of Freedoms Act 2012, biological samples (the actual swab or hair) must generally be destroyed within 6 months once a digital profile is created.
   - Digital profiles and fingerprints may be retained longer depending on whether you are charged or convicted.
8. **Clarify the nature of any identification procedure (ID parade or video ID).** **Ask: "Is this a witness-based identification procedure under PACE Code D?"**
   - You have the right to legal advice before participating in video identification or a live parade.
   - Ask for a copy of the "Notice to Suspect" which outlines your rights regarding these procedures.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to apply for "Early Deletion" of your records if you are not charged.
- You do not need to provide a statement explaining why your DNA might be at a scene.
- You do not need to decide whether to make a formal complaint about the officer's conduct.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel intimidated or "guilty" for asking for a solicitor when "you have nothing to hide." Asking for legal advice is a standard procedural step that helps ensure the police follow the correct PACE codes and that your data is not retained longer than the law allows.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only for handling the initial request for biometrics or identification. Later decisions regarding the retention of your data or its use in court require consultation with a criminal defense specialist.

## Important note
Police powers to take and retain biometrics vary significantly based on whether you are arrested, charged, or a voluntary attender, and whether the offence is a "qualifying" (serious) offence. If you are told you have no choice, ask for the legal authority and wait for a solicitor.
