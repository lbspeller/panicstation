# What to do if… a benefits payment is suspended for review and you have essential bills due (UK)

## Short answer
Contact whoever pays the benefit today to find the exact reason it is suspended and what single action or document may restart it, then send that and keep proof. At the same time, contact your landlord, energy supplier, and council tax team to ask for a short hold or affordable temporary plan while the review is dealt with.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore letters, online account messages, journal messages, texts, or requests for evidence.
- Do not assume the payment will restart without you checking whether an identity check, evidence upload, form, appointment, or interview is needed.
- Do not take high-cost credit, payday loans, or money from illegal lenders if there are safer support routes to try first.
- Do not cancel rent, energy, or council tax payments without first asking for a hold or payment plan.
- Do not share benefit login details, one-time passcodes, bank details, or documents with unexpected callers, texts, or emails.
- Do not spend the whole day on complaints before you know what is needed to get the payment moving.

## What to do now
1. **Find the payer and use the fastest contact route today.**  
   Check the latest letter, online account, journal message, text, or email for the payer and reference number:
   - **Universal Credit and most DWP benefits:** use your online account, journal, or the contact details on the letter.
   - **Housing Benefit or Council Tax Reduction:** contact your local council benefits team.
   - **Northern Ireland:** use the contact route given by the Northern Ireland benefits service or nidirect.
   
   Ask: “My payment is suspended for review. What exactly is missing, how do I send it today, and how will you confirm it has been received?”

2. **Send the missing item and keep proof.**  
   Common review blockers can include identity checks, proof of address, bank details, fit notes, payslips, childcare cost evidence, rent or tenancy proof, bank statements, or a missed appointment. Send only what they ask for, using the route they give you, and keep screenshots, upload receipts, postal proof, or a dated note of the call.

3. **Ask what short-term benefit support is available while you wait.**  
   If you are on Universal Credit, ask whether an advance or other financial support is available in your circumstances. If rent is at risk, ask whether an Alternative Payment Arrangement or managed payment to landlord may be possible. If your Universal Credit has stopped or reduced because of a sanction or fraud penalty, ask whether a recoverable hardship payment applies.

4. **Protect rent first.**  
   Contact your landlord, housing officer, letting agent, or mortgage adviser if relevant. Say the benefit is suspended for review, explain that you are sending the requested evidence, and ask for a short breathing period or temporary written arrangement while the benefit office responds.

5. **Contact your energy supplier before the bill or top-up problem gets worse.**  
   Ask for more time to pay, an affordable payment plan, hardship support, or help if you cannot top up a prepayment meter. If you are disabled, older, pregnant, have young children, have a long-term health condition, have recently lost work, or rely on powered medical equipment, ask your supplier or network operator about the Priority Services Register.

6. **Contact council tax separately.**  
   Council tax is handled by your local council, even if another benefit is suspended. Tell the council tax team your benefit payment is suspended for review and ask for a short hold, a temporary payment plan, Council Tax Reduction help if relevant, and any local hardship support.

7. **Use emergency help that matches where you live.**  
   - **England:** ask your local council about the Crisis and Resilience Fund, local welfare help, or cost-of-living crisis support.
   - **Scotland:** apply to your local council for a Scottish Welfare Fund Crisis Grant.
   - **Wales:** apply to the Discretionary Assistance Fund for an Emergency Assistance Payment.
   - **Northern Ireland:** apply for Discretionary Support if you are in crisis or an extreme or exceptional situation.
   
   If rent is at risk and you receive Housing Benefit or the housing element of Universal Credit, ask your council what extra housing-cost help applies where you live.

8. **If a formal decision arrives and you disagree, protect the challenge route.**  
   If you receive a decision notice or letter and you think it is wrong, you can usually ask for a mandatory reconsideration within 1 month of the decision date, unless the notice says otherwise. Keep the decision, copies of what you sent, and notes of calls.

9. **If debt enforcement is close, get free debt advice quickly.**  
   If you have court letters, bailiff or enforcement notices, eviction papers, council tax enforcement, or several missed priority bills, contact a free debt adviser. In England and Wales, ask about Breathing Space. In Scotland or Northern Ireland, ask what local debt protections and urgent options apply.

## What can wait
- You do not need to solve every debt today; focus on rent, energy, council tax, food, heat, and anything linked to health or safety.
- You do not need a perfect long-term repayment plan today; ask for short holds and temporary arrangements first.
- You do not need to decide about a complaint until you have asked what is missing and tried the quickest route to restart or unblock the payment.
- You do not need to send extra documents that were not requested unless the benefit office or a specialist adviser tells you they are needed.

## Important reassurance
A suspension for review can be caused by paperwork, verification, missing evidence, or a missed contact; it does not automatically mean you have done anything wrong. The most useful first move is to get a precise list of what is needed, send it with proof, and reduce the immediate pressure from rent, energy, and council tax while the review continues.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the next few days. Later decisions about challenges, complaints, debt plans, housing options, or benefit entitlement may need help from a benefits adviser, debt adviser, housing adviser, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, debt, housing, or professional advice. Benefit rules and crisis support can vary by benefit type and by where you live in the UK. If you are at immediate risk of homelessness, have no food or heat, cannot top up energy, or rely on electricity for medical equipment, say this clearly when you contact the benefit office, council, energy supplier, and any adviser.
