# What to do if… you discover hail or wind damage to windows or roof that could worsen in the next storm (UK)

## Short answer
Ensure the area is safe, then stop wind or water ingress using low-risk temporary covers from inside the building and contact your insurer or landlord immediately to report the damage.

## Do not do these things
- Do not climb onto a roof or use a ladder during high winds, rain, or in darkness.
- Do not touch or approach downed power lines, sparking wires, or any cables tangled in trees.
- Do not use candles or naked flames to inspect damage in lofts or enclosed spaces; use a battery-powered torch instead.
- Do not attempt to remove glass from a window frame with your bare hands.
- Do not throw away pieces of damaged tiles, glass, or fittings until your insurer confirms they are no longer needed for evidence.
- Do not ignore small leaks or cracks, as these can fail catastrophically during the next period of high pressure or heavy rain.

## What to do now
1. **Move people and pets away from the immediate hazard zone.**  
   **Keep everyone out of rooms with shattered glass, sagging ceilings, or visible structural movement.**  
   - Close the doors to affected rooms to contain draughts and debris.
   - If a ceiling is bulging with water, do not stand under it; move all furniture and valuables to a dry area.
2. **Check for secondary utility hazards immediately.**  
   **If you smell gas, suspect a leak, or see damaged gas pipes, evacuate to fresh air immediately.**  
   - Call the National Gas Emergency Service on **0800 111 999** (Great Britain) or **0800 002 001** (Northern Ireland).
   - Do not operate any electrical switches, including lights or entry phones, if gas is suspected.
   - If you see downed power lines or sparking cables, call **105** (England, Scotland, Wales) or **03457 643 643** (Northern Ireland). Call **999** if there is an immediate risk to life on a public highway.
3. **Isolate electrical circuits if water is near fittings.**  
   **If water is dripping from a light fitting or running down a wall near a socket, do not touch the switches.**  
   - If you can safely access your consumer unit (fuse box) and it is in a dry area, switch off the specific circuit for that room.
   - If the consumer unit itself is wet or sparking, do not touch it; evacuate and wait for a professional.
4. **Apply temporary "inside-only" weather protection.**  
   **Only attempt temporary fixes if you can do so without climbing onto the roof or leaning out of windows.**  
   - **For windows:** Wear heavy gloves and sturdy shoes. Clear away loose glass that might fall, then tape heavy-duty plastic sheeting (such as bin bags) or cardboard over the opening from the inside.
   - **For roof leaks:** Place buckets or plastic containers under drips in the loft. If you can see the entry point from the loft, do not step off the ceiling joists; stay on the solid timber beams.
5. **Document all damage from a safe distance.**  
   **Take photographs or video of the damage as it is now, before you make any changes.**  
   - Photograph external damage from ground level using a zoom lens if available.
   - Document internal water damage, broken glass, and the temporary covers you have installed.
6. **Notify the responsible party to log an emergency repair.**  
   **Contact your insurer or landlord immediately to inform them the property is no longer weather-tight.**  
   - **Tenants:** Report the damage to your landlord or letting agent. Follow up with an email containing your photos to create a timestamped record.
   - **Homeowners:** Call your buildings insurance 24-hour emergency helpline. Ask for permission to instruct an emergency roofer or glazier if they cannot send one immediately.
7. **Secure the surrounding area for the next storm.**  
   **Check the garden or yard for loose items that could be picked up by the wind.**  
   - Bring garden furniture, bins, and plant pots into a shed or garage.
   - Ensure all other windows and doors are fully latched.

## What can wait
- You do not need to clean up water-damaged carpets or debris perfectly until the leak is stopped.
- You do not need to obtain multiple repair quotes today if your insurer is sending their own contractor.
- You do not need to decide on permanent replacement materials (e.g., specific tile types or window frames) in the first few hours.
- You do not need to settle the final insurance claim immediately; the priority is stabilisation.

## Important reassurance
Storm damage is a common emergency, and insurers have established protocols to handle these events. By moving to a safe area, stopping the immediate ingress of water, and reporting the incident, you have completed the most critical steps to protect the property. You are not required to put your physical safety at risk to save building materials.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilisation and hazard mitigation only. It does not cover permanent structural repairs, professional working-at-height procedures, or the long-term management of insurance disputes.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional building, legal, or electrical advice. Always prioritise life safety over property. If a structure appears unstable or utilities are compromised, evacuate and contact the appropriate emergency services or qualified professionals.
