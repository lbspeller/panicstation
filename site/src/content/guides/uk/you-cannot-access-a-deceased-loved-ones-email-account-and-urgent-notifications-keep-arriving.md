# What to do if… you cannot access a deceased loved one’s email account and urgent notifications keep arriving (UK)

## Short answer
Do not try to break into the account or bypass security. Use the notification previews to identify urgent senders, contact those organisations through verified routes, and start the email provider’s deceased-user process with realistic expectations.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t repeatedly guess passwords or keep pressing “forgot password”; it may trigger security locks and make the situation harder.
- Don’t try to bypass two-factor authentication or use tools to get around account security.
- Don’t click links in email previews unless you can verify the sender independently.
- Don’t send messages from the deceased person’s account if you do gain access.
- Don’t erase phones, reset devices, throw away paperwork, or delete accounts until the urgent notifications have been identified.
- Don’t assume the email provider will give you the inbox just because you are family or an executor.

## What to do now
1. **Triage the notifications without opening the inbox.**
   - Write down the sender, subject line, date and time from each notification preview.
   - Prioritise anything that appears to involve fraud, security alerts, missed payments, account closure, benefits, pensions, insurance, utilities, rent, mortgage, tax, or employment.
   - If it is safe and respectful to do so, take screenshots or photos of the notification previews so you can quote the details later.

2. **Contact urgent senders through independent details.**
   - Use a phone number or web address from a statement, card, bill, official website, or previous paper letter, not a link from the email preview.
   - Ask for the bereavement, deceased customer, estates, fraud, or account security team.
   - Ask them to note the death, pause avoidable automated action where possible, stop sensitive email-only contact where possible, and tell you what proof they need.

3. **Prepare the documents you may be asked for.**
   - Death certificate, or interim death certificate if that is what you have.
   - Your photo ID.
   - The will naming the executor, if there is one.
   - Grant of Probate or Letters of Administration, if already available.
   - If probate is not available yet, ask what the organisation can do now with a death certificate.

4. **Start the email provider’s deceased-user route.**
   - Identify the provider, such as Google, Apple iCloud Mail, Microsoft Outlook, Hotmail, Yahoo or another provider.
   - Use only the provider’s official support page or account recovery route for deceased users.
   - Aim first to reduce harm, preserve important information and close or secure the account where appropriate, not to force immediate inbox access.

5. **Set expectations for the main providers.**
   - **Google:** Google can work with immediate family members and representatives to close an account where appropriate, and may provide content in certain circumstances, but it will not provide passwords or login details. Check whether the person had set up Inactive Account Manager.
   - **Apple:** Legacy Contact is usually the easiest route to Apple Account data. Without a Legacy Contact access key, Apple may require legal documentation, and access is not guaranteed.
   - **Microsoft:** If you do not know the account credentials, Microsoft says Outlook.com and OneDrive may be frozen after one year and Microsoft accounts expire after two years of inactivity. Any request for account contents may require formal legal process and may still be refused.
   - **Yahoo:** Yahoo UK and Ireland can usually process account closure requests with documents. Content disclosure is treated separately and may require an Irish Court Order addressed to Yahoo International Limited.

6. **Create replacement contact routes for urgent admin.**
   - Ask each urgent sender to add the executor, administrator or estate contact as the correspondence contact where they can.
   - Ask for paper copies or secure messages to be sent to the person dealing with the estate.
   - Keep a simple log with the date, organisation, person spoken to, reference number and agreed next step.
   - If the deceased lived alone or important letters may be missed, consider redirecting or stopping post through the appropriate postal route.

7. **Use government death-notification routes where relevant.**
   - If the person lived in England, Scotland or Wales, ask the registrar about Tell Us Once and use the reference number within 28 days of getting it.
   - Tell Us Once does not cover every organisation, so you may still need to contact banks, utilities, insurers, employers, landlords, mortgage providers, pension providers and subscription services directly.
   - If the person lived in Northern Ireland, use the official Northern Ireland guidance instead of Tell Us Once.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether every account should be memorialised, deleted or left inactive.
- You do not need to read the whole inbox to act responsibly.
- You do not need to finish probate before making urgent bereavement notifications.
- You do not need to solve every subscription or online account now; stabilise the urgent senders first.

## Important reassurance
This problem is common because bills, fraud alerts and official messages are often routed through email, while providers still have privacy and security duties after someone dies. Working through verified senders and official deceased-user processes is slower, but it reduces the risk of fraud, disputes and accidental loss of important data.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, designed to buy time and reduce immediate harm. Later decisions about probate, digital accounts, devices, data access and estate administration may need specialist legal or technical help.

## Important note
This is general information only, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic or other professional advice. If a notification suggests immediate fraud or financial loss, contact the organisation’s verified fraud or bereavement team and keep a clear record of what they say.
