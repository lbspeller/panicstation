# What to do if… you are asked to meet with police and are unsure if you are a witness or a suspect (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the meeting as suspect-risk until your status and the meeting type are clear. Do not attend, answer questions, give a statement, or hand over material until you have spoken to a criminal defence solicitor, unless a solicitor tells you there is a legal requirement to do something sooner.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume “pre-charge” means you are only a witness.
- Don’t assume “informal chat” means it cannot affect you.
- Don’t attend alone “just to clear things up” while your status is unclear.
- Don’t give a written statement, timeline, message screenshots, or a “quick explanation” before legal advice.
- Don’t guess, fill gaps, or agree with words you are not sure about.
- Don’t discuss the matter with other witnesses, colleagues, friends involved in the events, or on social media.
- Don’t volunteer phone access, passwords, messages, photos, documents, or searches because you feel pressured, unless a solicitor tells you there is a legal requirement.
- Don’t ignore the request; pause, ask for details in writing, and get advice.

## What to do now
1. **Ask which legal system is handling it.** Say: “Is this being dealt with in England and Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland?” The words and interview rules can differ, so use a solicitor who deals with criminal law in the part of the UK handling the case.

2. **Ask for the request in writing.** Ask for:
   - whether you are being treated as a witness, a suspect, or neither has been decided;
   - whether this is a voluntary interview, an interview under caution, pre-charge engagement, a witness statement, or something else;
   - what alleged offence or incident the meeting is about;
   - the date, place, reference number, and name of the officer or investigator;
   - whether any prosecutor, investigator, or other decision-maker will be involved.

3. **Use a safe holding reply.** You can say: “I am willing to cooperate, but I need legal advice before I attend or answer questions. Please put the request, my status, and the purpose of the meeting in writing.”

4. **Ask whether attendance is voluntary or legally required.** If they say you must attend, ask: “What is the legal basis for requiring me to attend?” Do not argue the facts; contact a criminal defence solicitor immediately with the exact wording you were given.

5. **Arrange legal advice before any meeting.** Ask for a solicitor who handles police interviews or criminal investigations in the relevant part of the UK. In England and Wales, free legal advice is available for a voluntary police interview, and you can ask for the duty solicitor if you do not have your own.

6. **Do not start with “a quick chat”.** If anyone calls or messages you before you have advice, repeat: “I want legal advice before answering questions. Please send the request and topic in writing for my solicitor.”

7. **Clarify what “pre-charge meeting” means in your case.** It may mean a voluntary interview, an interview under caution, a witness statement request, or pre-charge engagement. Pre-charge engagement is usually about communication before a charging decision, and it may happen face to face or in writing; your solicitor can tell you whether you should attend personally.

8. **If you are told you are a witness, still check risk before giving a statement.** Ask what they want from you, whether it will be a written statement, an audio recorded interview, a video recorded interview, or specific information. If any topic could involve your own conduct, messages, devices, work records, immigration position, driving, drugs, money, or another offence, pause and get legal advice first.

9. **If you are under 18 or may be vulnerable, ask for safeguards.** Say that you need an appropriate adult or the equivalent safeguard for the legal system handling the case, and that you do not want to be interviewed or asked to sign anything until the right support and legal advice are in place.

10. **Make a private note for your solicitor.** Write down names, ranks, phone numbers, dates, times, reference numbers, and the exact words used about your status and the meeting. Keep the note factual and do not send it to anyone except your solicitor.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to give your side, make a statement, hand over your phone, share documents, agree search terms, or attend an informal meeting. You can wait until your status, the meeting type, and the legal system handling the case are clear and you have had legal advice.

## Important reassurance
Being asked to attend a pre-charge meeting does not automatically mean you will be charged. Slowing down to confirm your status and get legal advice is a normal protective step, not an admission of guilt.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about interviews, statements, devices, documents, pre-charge engagement, bail, release under investigation, or any complaint about police conduct may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Police processes and rights vary within the UK and depend on the exact type of meeting being proposed.
