# What to do if… IT asks to collect your work laptop immediately and you are not told why (UK)

## Short answer
Hand the laptop over calmly, but slow the moment down enough to get a written handover record: what was collected, when, by whom, and who authorised it. Ask HR or your manager to confirm your work status, access restrictions, and next steps in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not refuse to return the laptop or argue on the spot.
- Do not wipe, reset, tidy, delete files, clear histories, or change settings.
- Do not email yourself work files, screenshots, client data, code, documents, or chat logs.
- Do not plug in storage devices or copy anything from the laptop.
- Do not send long messages guessing what this is about.
- Do not contact colleagues about it if you have been told not to, or if you suspect an investigation is starting.
- Do not sign anything you have not read calmly; say you will review it and respond in writing.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the request is real and authorised.** Ask, briefly: “Who authorised this collection, and can you note that in the ticket or email?” If the person is not from your usual IT team, contact HR, your manager, or the normal IT service route before handing anything over.

2. **Create a clean handover record.** Before the equipment leaves, write down:
   - the date and time;
   - the collector’s name, role, and team;
   - where the handover happened;
   - the laptop make, model, serial number, or asset tag;
   - any charger, dock, phone, security key, badge, bag, or other item taken;
   - any visible damage or condition issues.

3. **Ask for a receipt or confirmation.** Ask IT to email your personal email address, HR, or your manager a simple note saying what was collected and when. If they will not do that, write your own factual note immediately after the handover and keep it off work systems.

4. **Photograph only the outside condition and accessories if you can do it without delay or conflict.** A quick photo of the closed laptop and accessories laid out can help avoid later confusion. Do not photograph screens, files, messages, client data, or confidential material.

5. **Do not alter the device.** If personal accounts, browser tabs, files, or USB devices are visible, ask IT or HR to record that and tell you what they want you to do. Do not sign out, delete, remove, sync, copy, or disconnect anything unless IT specifically instructs you and records it.

6. **Ask one status question in writing.** Send HR or your manager a short message: “Please confirm whether I am suspended, on paid leave, expected to keep working, or only subject to temporary access restrictions. Please also confirm my point of contact and what I should or should not do today.”

7. **Ask for any boundaries to be written down.** Ask whether you should avoid the workplace, stop using work systems, avoid contacting colleagues, stay available during working hours, or wait for an investigation meeting invitation. Follow written boundaries unless you get them changed in writing.

8. **If you are told you are suspended, ask for written confirmation.** Ask the confirmation to include when it started, who your contact is, what the next step is, what restrictions apply, and what happens to pay and contractual benefits. Suspension is not a disciplinary outcome in itself, and pay and contractual benefits should generally continue during suspension.

9. **Start a private factual timeline.** On your own phone or paper, write:
   - exactly what was said, by whom, and when;
   - what equipment was taken;
   - what access changes you noticed, such as email disabled, badge stopped, or chat access removed;
   - who witnessed the handover;
   - any messages or meeting invitations you received.

10. **Get support lined up before any formal meeting.** If you are in a union, contact your representative. If you are invited to a disciplinary or grievance hearing, you can make a reasonable request to be accompanied by a workplace colleague, a certified workplace trade union representative, or an official employed by a trade union. There is usually no legal right to be accompanied at an initial fact-finding investigation meeting, but you can still ask.

11. **Ask for a controlled route for urgent practical needs.** If you need payslips, rota information, personal belongings, medical information, or contact details stored at work, ask HR how to get them safely. Do not try to regain access to systems after being told not to use them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, raise a grievance, start legal action, or write a full defence.
- You do not need to guess the reason or explain everything before you know what process is happening.
- You do not need to contact colleagues for information if you have been told not to.
- You do not need to solve access to every work file now; ask HR for a controlled route for anything genuinely urgent.

## Important reassurance
A sudden laptop collection can happen for several reasons, including asset control, security precautions, role changes, access reviews, or an investigation. The safest first step is to avoid anything that could look like tampering or data copying, and to create a calm written record of what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours or day only. If this becomes a formal investigation, suspension, disciplinary process, grievance, or dismissal risk, later decisions may need specialist help based on your contract, workplace policy, and the exact facts.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, employment, financial, technical, therapeutic, or professional advice. Workplace policies, contracts, facts, and the law that applies to your role can matter. If a conversation becomes pressured, pause and ask HR to continue in writing.
