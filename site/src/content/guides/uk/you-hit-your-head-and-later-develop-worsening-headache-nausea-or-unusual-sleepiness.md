# What to do if… you hit your head and later develop worsening headache, nausea, or unusual sleepiness (UK)

## Short answer
Seek immediate medical assessment at the nearest A&E or call 999 if red flags like repeated vomiting, extreme drowsiness, or confusion appear. Do not stay alone or drive yourself while symptoms are actively worsening.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay alone for the next 48 hours; you need someone to monitor your level of responsiveness.
- Do not drive any vehicle, including a bicycle, until you have been medically cleared by a clinician.
- Do not drink alcohol or use recreational drugs as these can mask worsening symptoms.
- Do not take aspirin for pain relief after a head injury unless specifically prescribed, and do not stop prescribed blood thinners without urgent medical advice.
- Do not take sedating medications, such as sleeping tablets or strong antihistamines, as they interfere with neurological monitoring.
- Do not eat or drink anything until you have been assessed by a medical professional in case emergency surgery is required.
- Do not ignore symptoms because the initial impact seemed minor; internal bleeding can develop hours or days later.

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 immediately if you observe any high-risk red flags.** These indicators suggest a potential life-threatening emergency:
   - **Loss of consciousness** (being "knocked out") even for a few seconds.
   - **Repeated vomiting** (more than once).
   - **Extreme drowsiness** or difficulty being woken up.
   - **Confusion**, irritability, or behaving "out of character."
   - **A seizure** or fit (shaking or stiffening).
   - **Problems with speech**, vision, hearing, or balance.
   - **Clear fluid or blood** leaking from the ears or nose.
   - **Bruising behind the ears** or a black eye without a direct hit to the eye.
   - **A dent in the skull** or an open wound.
   - **Weakness or numbness** in any part of the body.
2. **Call 999 if the injury involved a high-energy impact or dangerous mechanism.** This includes:
   - **A fall from a height** of more than 1 metre or 5 stairs (or for babies, a fall higher than their own height).
   - **A high-speed collision** such as a car crash, being hit by a vehicle, or a cycling accident.
   - **A blow from a high-speed object** like a golf club or bat.
3. **Go to A&E immediately if symptoms are worsening but no 999 flags are present.** If you are unable to travel safely or are unsure, call **NHS 111** for urgent direction.
4. **Identify yourself as high-risk if you take certain medications or have specific history.** Inform medical staff immediately if:
   - **You take blood thinners** (anticoagulants like warfarin, apixaban, or rivaroxaban) or antiplatelet drugs (like clopidogrel).
   - **You have a bleeding disorder** (like haemophilia).
   - **You have had brain surgery** in the past.
   - **You are over 65 years old.**
5. **Ensure a responsible adult remains with you at all times.** They must be instructed to check your responsiveness regularly and seek help if you become difficult to rouse or more confused.
6. **Gather essential information for the medical team.** While waiting or travelling, prepare the following:
   - **The exact time** of the injury and the cause.
   - **A list of all medications** currently being taken.
   - **Specific changes** noticed (e.g., "The headache started as a 2/10 and is now an 8/10").

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose yourself with "concussion" or "haemorrhage" now; professional imaging or assessment is required for this.
- You do not need to determine when you can return to work, school, or sport today; focus entirely on the first 24–48 hours of stabilization.
- You do not need to worry about long-term memory or cognitive impacts until the acute phase is cleared by a doctor.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel frightened when symptoms change after a head injury. Choosing to get assessed is the correct and safest action. Most people with head injuries recover fully, but prompt medical checks ensure that any rare complications are caught and managed early.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency identification only. It does not replace the neurological examination or imaging required in a clinical setting.

## Important note
Head injuries can be deceptive; symptoms of internal bleeding can be delayed (the "talk and die" syndrome). If you are in doubt about whether a symptom is "bad enough," treat it as an emergency and seek professional help immediately.
