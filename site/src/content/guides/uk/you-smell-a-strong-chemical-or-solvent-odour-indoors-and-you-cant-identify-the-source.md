# What to do if… you smell a strong chemical or solvent odour indoors and you can’t identify the source (UK)

## Short answer
Evacuate all people and pets to fresh air immediately. If the odour is overwhelming or anyone has difficulty breathing, call **999** from outside.

## Do not do these things
- Do not search for the source of the smell while remaining inside the building.
- Do not operate any electrical switches, including lights, appliances, doorbells, or entry buzzers.
- Do not use a mobile phone or landline while inside the affected area.
- Do not light matches, lighters, or any naked flames.
- Do not attempt to "neutralise" the smell by spraying air fresheners or mixing cleaning chemicals.
- Do not remain inside to open every window; only open those immediately on your exit path.

## What to do now
1. **Evacuate the building immediately.** Move everyone outside and at least 30 metres away from the property. Ensure you stay upwind of the building so any escaping fumes are blown away from you.
2. **Open windows only as you pass them.** If a window or door is in your immediate path to the exit, open it to begin ventilation, but do not move deeper into the property to reach other windows.
3. **Call 999 from a safe distance outside.** Use a mobile phone once you are clear of the building. Tell the operator you have an "unidentified strong chemical odour indoors" and report if anyone is experiencing:
   - Severe difficulty breathing or gasping.
   - Chest pain or tightness.
   - Seizures, fainting, or loss of consciousness.
   - Severe confusion or agitation.
4. **Report a suspected gas leak if the odour is sulphurous.** If the smell resembles rotten eggs or you are unsure if it is gas, call the **National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999** immediately after calling 999.
5. **Turn off the gas at the meter if safe.** Only do this if the meter is located outside or in an easily accessible external cupboard. Do not enter a cellar, basement, or the affected rooms to reach the valve.
6. **Decontaminate if you have touched a substance.** If liquid or residue is on your skin or clothes:
   - Remove affected outer clothing without pulling it over your head.
   - Place clothing in a sealed plastic bag outside.
   - Rinse affected skin with lukewarm water for at least 15 minutes.
7. **Contact NHS 111 for non-emergency symptoms.** If you have a mild headache, slight nausea, or stinging eyes but can breathe normally, call 111 for medical advice once you are in fresh air.
8. **Warn neighbours without using electrics.** If you are in a flat or shared building, knock on doors to alert others as you leave. Do not use internal phones, buzzers, or lifts.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify what the chemical is before calling for help.
- You do not need to retrieve personal belongings or "pack a bag" if the smell is strong.
- You do not need to contact your landlord or building manager until the emergency services have secured the site.
- You do not need to investigate if the smell is coming from a neighbouring property.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel lightheaded or panicked when encountering strong, unknown smells. These odours are designed to be noticed. Moving to fresh air is the most effective way to stop the immediate physical effects of inhalation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for unidentified indoor odours. It does not cover specific industrial chemical spills or long-term environmental health investigations.

## Important note
This information is for immediate safety and does not replace professional emergency response. Always follow the instructions provided by the 999 operator or the National Gas Emergency Service.
