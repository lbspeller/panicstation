# What to do if… your employer says your sick leave paperwork is missing or insufficient (UK)

## Short answer
Ask for the decision and the exact missing paperwork in writing, then send the minimum evidence you can provide while keeping copies. If the issue is Statutory Sick Pay, ask for written reasons and use the HMRC dispute route if it cannot be fixed with your employer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave this as a verbal conversation; get the missing paperwork list, the deadline, and the pay decision in writing.
- Do not hand over your original fit note or other documents without keeping a clear copy.
- Do not send full medical records unless you have taken advice and are sure they are needed.
- Do not resign, stop reporting sickness absence, or agree to unpaid leave on the spot just to end the conversation.
- Do not assume “insufficient paperwork” means you have no entitlement; it may be an admin problem that can be corrected.

## What to do now
1. **Ask HR or payroll to put the decision in writing.** Ask them to confirm whether they mean Statutory Sick Pay, company sick pay, contractual sick pay, occupational sick pay, or a mix of these.
2. **Ask for the exact missing item.** Ask for the document name, the absence dates it must cover, where to send it, the deadline, and whether pay is being paused while they wait or stopped because they say you are not entitled.
3. **Check the timing of your sickness absence.**
   - If you have been off sick for 7 calendar days or less, you usually do not need a fit note or other medical evidence for Statutory Sick Pay; your employer can ask you to self-certify.
   - If you have been off sick for more than 7 calendar days, including weekends and non-working days, you usually need a fit note from an authorised healthcare professional.
4. **Check your contract or sickness policy.** Company, contractual, occupational, or discretionary sick pay can have extra paperwork rules, but your employer should be able to point to the rule they are relying on.
5. **If your fit note is delayed, tell them in writing now.** Say why it is delayed, when you expect to send it, and ask the healthcare professional whether the fit note can cover the relevant absence dates.
6. **If they are asking for clinician evidence for the first 7 days, ask why.** Say you understand self-certification is usually enough for Statutory Sick Pay for 7 calendar days or less. If they still ask for clinician evidence and a fee is charged, ask HR in writing whether they will cover that fee.
7. **Send the evidence in a trackable way.** Email is usually enough. Include your name, employee number if you have one, the sickness absence dates, the attached document, and a request for written confirmation that pay will be corrected and any arrears paid.
8. **If they say you are not entitled to Statutory Sick Pay or that it is ending, ask for form SSP1 or written reasons.** Keep the form or email because it may matter for a dispute or a benefit claim.
9. **Escalate if it is not fixed after you send the evidence.** Use your employer’s HR, payroll, manager, or internal escalation route first. If the unresolved dispute is about Statutory Sick Pay, contact HMRC’s Statutory Payment Dispute Team.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to raise a formal grievance, start a legal claim, or get legal representation.
- You do not need to write a long explanation of your medical condition; focus on the document they say is missing and the pay decision.
- You do not need to challenge the whole sickness policy right now; stabilise the paperwork and pay position first.

## Important reassurance
This can be a paperwork mismatch, such as the wrong inbox, missing dates, an unclear policy rule, or payroll not seeing a document already sent. A written checklist, the minimum needed evidence, and a clear record of what you sent often help get the pay issue corrected.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise your pay and reduce the chance of a rushed mistake. If your employer keeps withholding pay after you have provided the requested evidence, later decisions may need specialist employment advice based on your contract, policy, dates, and pay records.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Sick pay rules can differ depending on your contract, your employment status, and whether the pay is statutory or contractual.
