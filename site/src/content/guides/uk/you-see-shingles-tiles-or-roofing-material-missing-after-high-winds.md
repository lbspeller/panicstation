# What to do if… you see shingles, tiles, or roofing material missing after high winds (UK)

## Short answer
Stay clear of the area below the roof and do not climb up to inspect the damage. Contact your insurer or landlord immediately to arrange professional temporary weatherproofing and prevent internal water damage.

## Do not do these things
- Do not climb onto the roof or use a ladder to "check" the damage (wind-weakened structures are unstable).
- Do not attempt to fix tarpaulins or "emergency" covers yourself if it involves working at height.
- Do not pull at hanging materials or loose tiles from the ground.
- Do not dispose of fallen tiles or shingles until your insurer has confirmed they do not need them as evidence.
- Do not pay for on-the-spot repairs from "door-to-door" contractors who arrive immediately after a storm.
- Do not switch on lights if water is dripping through the ceiling near electrical fittings.

## What to do now
1. **Clear the drop zone immediately.** Ensure no one walks directly beneath the damaged section of the roof, as remaining tiles or debris may still be loose and could fall without warning.
2. **Observe the damage from a safe distance.** Look for visible cues of further risk from ground level or through windows.
   - Signs of immediate danger include a sagging roofline, large sections of felt exposed, or cracking sounds.
   - If the structure appears to be failing or a chimney looks unstable, **evacuate the immediate area and call 999**.
3. **Isolate electrical hazards if water is entering the building.** If water is dripping near sockets, switches, or light fittings, **turn off the electricity at the consumer unit / fuse box**.
   - Only do this if the area around the fuse box is dry and you can reach it safely.
4. **Contain internal leaks to prevent secondary damage.** Place buckets under drips and move furniture or electronics away from affected ceilings.
   - If a ceiling is bulging or "bowing" due to water, do not stand under it; it may collapse.
5. **Document the damage for insurance or landlord records.** Take photos from the ground and interior photos of any leaks or fallen debris.
   - Note the time the damage was noticed and the approximate time the high winds occurred.
6. **Report the fault to the responsible party.**
   - **Homeowners:** Call your home insurance emergency 24-hour line to report "storm damage" and ask for an approved roofer.
   - **Tenants:** Contact your landlord or letting agent. Under 2026 regulations (Awaab's Law expansion), structural hazards in social housing must be investigated within 24 hours.
   - **Block Managers:** If you live in a flat, contact the managing agent as the roof is typically a communal responsibility.
7. **Contact utility providers if external infrastructure is damaged.**
   - If a power line has been brought down by falling tiles or a tree: **stay at least 10 metres away and call 105**.
   - If you smell gas: **leave the property and call 0800 111 999 (Great Britain) or 0800 002 001 (Northern Ireland)**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a permanent repair method (e.g., full re-roofing vs. patch repair) today.
- You do not need to clean up all debris in the garden until the wind has fully subsided and the roof is confirmed stable.
- You do not need to settle an insurance claim immediately; the priority is making the building "wind and watertight."

## Important reassurance
Missing tiles are a common result of UK storms, and modern roofing systems are designed with layers (like sarking felt) that provide some short-term protection even when tiles are gone. As long as you remain on the ground and keep the area clear, the immediate physical risk to you is low. Professionals have the specific equipment (scaffolding or reach-platforms) to handle this safely.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for residential roof damage caused by wind. It does not cover long-term structural surveys, legal disputes over boundaries, or commercial property regulations.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and does not constitute professional engineering or legal advice. Always prioritize advice from emergency services (999) if there is a risk of building collapse.
