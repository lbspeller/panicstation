# What to do if… you suspect someone is trying to lure a child or vulnerable person nearby (UK)

## Short answer
Stay at a safe distance and contact police now: call 999 if anyone seems in immediate danger, a crime is happening now, or you think the person may leave with them soon. If it is concerning but not an emergency, call 101 or report it online.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the person or try to test them; it can escalate and may increase risk to the child or vulnerable person.
- Do not shout accusations or create a scene that could panic the person at risk or block exits.
- Do not follow closely, chase, or physically intervene if that would put you or anyone else in danger.
- Do not delay reporting because you are not completely sure; report what you observed.
- Do not post photos, video, names, or identifying details on social media.

## What to do now
1. Move to a safer viewing point.
   Stay where you can still see what is happening, with other people, staff, security, a doorway, or a clear exit nearby. If you are in a shop, station, leisure centre, school site, or other staffed place, go to staff or security rather than moving closer yourself.

2. Contact police with the right urgency.
   Call 999 if the person at risk appears distressed, confused, blocked, pulled, guided away, or likely to be taken away soon. Call 101 or report online if the behaviour is concerning but there is no immediate danger and no crime appears to be happening now.

3. Start with the key sentence.
   Say: “I am at a safe distance. I am worried someone is trying to lure a child or vulnerable person.” Then give the exact location, nearest landmark, entrance, road, platform, shop, or direction of travel.

4. Describe observations, not conclusions.
   Tell police what you saw or heard: for example, offering a lift or gifts, asking the person to come with them, guiding them away from others, blocking their path, touching or pulling them, or trying to get them into a vehicle.

5. Note details without moving closer.
   Look for clothing, height, build, distinctive features, bags, the time, and any vehicle colour, make, model, registration, parking position, or direction of travel. A partial registration or clear direction is still useful.

6. Quietly involve nearby adults who have a role there.
   If you can do it safely, tell staff, security, a teacher, a transport worker, or another responsible adult nearby: “I have contacted police because I am concerned someone may be trying to lure a child or vulnerable person. Please keep an eye without confronting them.”

7. If they move away, do not pursue.
   Stay safe and update police with the direction of travel, route, vehicle movement, or last place you saw them.

8. After the immediate moment, use safeguarding routes if risk may remain.
   If you are worried a child may still be at risk, you can contact the children’s social care team at the child’s local council. If you are worried an adult at risk may still be unsafe, contact the local council’s adult safeguarding team. If anyone seems in immediate danger at any point, call 999.

## What can wait
- Writing a perfect timeline; give the essentials now and add detail later if asked.
- Deciding the person’s motive; your role is to report the concern, not investigate.
- Calling several organisations while the situation is live; police first if there may be immediate danger.
- Seeking helpline advice before dealing with immediate safety.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to prove what was happening before you ask for help. Reporting what you genuinely observed gives police, staff, or safeguarding teams a chance to assess the risk.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first few minutes only. Later decisions, statements, safeguarding reports, or support needs may require specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information for quick, harm-reducing first actions. It is not legal, medical, therapeutic, safeguarding, or professional advice. If you believe anyone is in immediate danger, call 999.
