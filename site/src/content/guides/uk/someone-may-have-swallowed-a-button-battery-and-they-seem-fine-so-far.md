# What to do if… someone may have swallowed a button battery and they seem fine so far (UK)

## Short answer
Go to the nearest A&E immediately, even if the person appears completely well. Call 999 if they are choking, struggling to breathe, or vomiting blood.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for symptoms to appear before seeking medical help.
- Do not induce vomiting or give laxatives.
- Do not give any food or drink, except for the specific honey protocol for children over 12 months.
- Do not delay leaving for the hospital to look for the battery or search the house.
- Do not poke the throat, nose, or ears with fingers or tools to check for the item.

## What to do now
1. **Go to the nearest Accident and Emergency (A&E) department immediately.** Inform the reception desk staff immediately that you suspect a button battery has been swallowed.
2. **Prioritise travel to the hospital over any other action.** If the person is a child aged 12 months or older, and you suspect the ingestion happened within the last 12 hours, you may give honey while on the way to the hospital.
   - Only do this if honey is immediately available and does not delay your departure.
   - Give 10 mL (approximately 2 teaspoons) of honey every 10 minutes, up to a maximum of 6 doses.
   - Stop immediately if the child begins to vomit, choke, or becomes too drowsy to swallow safely.
   - Do not give honey to infants under 12 months of age due to the risk of botulism.
3. **Keep the person "nil by mouth" otherwise.** Do not provide water, snacks, or other liquids unless specifically instructed by a medical professional at the hospital.
4. **Gather evidence of the battery type without delaying your departure.** If possible, bring:
   - The device the battery came from (e.g., remote, toy, car fob).
   - An identical spare battery or the original packaging.
5. **Call 999 for an ambulance immediately if any red-flag symptoms develop.** These include:
   - Difficulty breathing or noisy, whistling breaths.
   - Persistent coughing or gagging.
   - Inability to swallow or uncontrollable drooling.
   - Vomiting blood or material that looks like coffee grounds.
   - A sudden, unexplained change in the sound of their voice.
   - Collapse or loss of consciousness.
6. **Alert staff if the battery might be in the nose or ear.** In young children, button batteries are sometimes inserted into other orifices. If you suspect this, do not attempt to flush or remove it; tell the triage nurse.

## What can wait
- You do not need to confirm the battery is definitely missing before leaving.
- You do not need to wait to see if the battery passes in the stool.
- You do not need to search for the specific model number if it takes more than a few seconds to find.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for button battery ingestion to show no symptoms for several hours. Internal chemical burns can occur even when the person feels fine. Seeking help now is a standard precautionary measure and is the most effective way to prevent serious injury.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency actions only. Hospital assessment, including X-rays and potential specialist removal, is required for all suspected ingestions.

## Important note
This information is for emergency guidance and does not replace professional medical diagnosis. Button battery ingestion is a time-critical medical emergency.
