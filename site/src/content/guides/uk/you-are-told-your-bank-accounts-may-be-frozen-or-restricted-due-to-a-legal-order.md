# What to do if… you are told your bank accounts may be frozen or restricted due to a legal order (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before moving money. Verify the claim through your bank’s official channels, get the exact order name and case details in writing if possible, and contact a solicitor in your part of the UK quickly.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t rush transfers or withdrawals to “save” the money after a warning.
- Don’t move money to get around a freeze or suspected court order.
- Don’t share one-time passcodes, PINs, passwords, or remote access to your phone or computer.
- Don’t use phone numbers, links, or attachments from a surprising text, call, or email.
- Don’t ignore written notices from a bank, court, solicitor, police force, or other official body.

## What to do now
1. **Contact the bank only through a known route.**  
   Use the number on your card, the official app, or the bank’s official website. Say you have been told there may be a legal order affecting your accounts and ask for the legal, fraud, or compliance team.

2. **Ask exactly what the bank can confirm.**  
   Write down:
   - whether any account is already frozen or only at risk,
   - which accounts are affected,
   - whether incoming payments can still arrive,
   - whether direct debits, standing orders, card payments, and cash withdrawals are blocked,
   - any reference number the bank can give you.

3. **Get the order details.**  
   Ask for the exact document name, the court, the case reference, the date the bank received it, and whether the bank can send confirmation in writing. The wording matters: it may be an Account Freezing Order, a restraint order, or another court process.

4. **If the contact came out of the blue, treat it as unverified until checked.**  
   End the call or stop replying. Contact the bank, court, police force, or other body through a verified route. Do not click links, download files, or open “order” attachments from unexpected messages.

5. **Protect today’s essentials without touching frozen funds.**  
   If you cannot buy food, medicine, travel to work, or keep a critical bill from failing, ask the bank what access, emergency support, or court-authorised payment route may exist. Keep notes of who you spoke to and what they said.

6. **Do not reroute income to avoid the order.**  
   If salary, benefits, or business income is due soon, first ask the bank what the order covers. Then ask a solicitor before changing where money is paid.

7. **Contact a solicitor quickly.**  
   Ask for someone experienced with Proceeds of Crime Act matters, Account Freezing Orders, restraint orders, or financial crime. Have ready:
   - the order name, court, case reference, and date received,
   - which accounts are affected,
   - what payments are blocked,
   - proof of income,
   - urgent bills such as rent, mortgage, utilities, food, travel, and medication.

8. **Ask about essentials and legal costs in practical terms.**  
   Some Account Freezing Orders may include or later allow exclusions for withdrawals or payments. Ask the solicitor whether an application can be made for essential living costs, and for legal expenses if available in your part of the UK. Ask the bank what it would need before processing any court-authorised payment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain your whole situation to several people right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to challenge the order fully.
- You do not need to gather every document immediately.
- You do not need to close accounts, open new accounts, or change all payment details in a panic.

## Important reassurance
This is frightening, but the safest first move is usually to slow the situation down. Verifying the contact, avoiding rushed money movements, and getting the exact order details gives you a clearer route to protect essentials.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Different orders work in different ways, and later decisions may need help from a solicitor or another specialist adviser.
