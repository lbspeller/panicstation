# What to do if… you are pressured to falsify records, hours, or performance figures at work (UK)

## Short answer
Do not knowingly change, sign, approve, or submit anything you believe is false. Pause the task, ask for the instruction in writing, and move the concern into a safer internal route or an appropriate external route if needed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “just do it this once” to get through the day; it can create disciplinary, legal, or professional risk for you.
- Do not argue in the moment or accuse people of fraud without evidence; keep it calm and factual.
- Do not sign, approve, or submit figures you believe are false while hoping they will be fixed later.
- Do not delete messages, drafts, screenshots, calendar entries, or notes related to the request.
- Do not secretly export bulk company, client, customer, patient, or confidential data to yourself.
- Do not post publicly or contact the media as a first step; external disclosures can be risky and may need advice first.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safe pause before anything is submitted.**  
   If you are being pressed live, use a short sentence such as: “I can’t submit figures I believe are inaccurate. I need to check this before anything goes in.”

2. **Ask for the instruction in writing.**  
   Ask: “Can you confirm exactly what you want changed, why it is being changed, and who has approved it?” If they will not put it in writing, make a note that they refused.

3. **Make a contemporaneous note for yourself.**  
   Write down the date, time, who asked, what record, hours, or figure they wanted changed, what words they used, who else was present, and what deadline or pressure was applied. Keep the note factual.

4. **Check the ground truth you are allowed to use.**  
   Compare the requested change with records you can legitimately access, such as your rota, your submitted timesheet, approvals, work allocation, task record, or payroll entry. Keep only documents you are entitled to keep, such as your own payslips or your own submitted timesheets. If the proof is in a restricted system or involves confidential data, note where it is instead of exporting it.

5. **Use a safer internal route, not only the person pressuring you.**  
   Look for the whistleblowing policy, speak-up route, ethics or compliance contact, internal audit contact, HR route for pay or hours records, or another named reporting route. Put the concern in writing and include what you were asked to falsify, the dates, whether you raised it already, the response, and what you need now, such as clear instruction not to alter the record.

6. **Contact your union or staff representative if you have one.**  
   Tell them you are being asked to change records or figures you believe are false, and ask for help putting the concern in a safe written form.

7. **If it may be whistleblowing, keep the public-interest point in mind.**  
   UK whistleblowing protection generally concerns wrongdoing that affects others, not only a personal workplace dispute. Pressure to falsify hours, payroll, safety, compliance, audit, customer, investor, or performance records may affect others. If it is only about your own employment issue, the grievance route may be more appropriate.

8. **If internal reporting feels unsafe or is ignored, consider an appropriate prescribed person or body.**  
   The UK has an official list of prescribed people and bodies. Choose the one that matches the issue, such as the regulator for your sector or the type of record involved. Keep the disclosure factual and limited to what you know.

9. **Get confidential guidance before escalating further.**  
   Acas can explain whistleblowing at work, workplace options, and what to do if you are treated badly. Citizens Advice can help with work problems. Protect may be a specialist whistleblowing advice route; use the Charity Commission register contact page rather than relying on the direct Protect website while it is not loading as expected.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign.
- You do not need to threaten legal action in your first message.
- You do not need to prove the whole wrongdoing before you ask for the instruction to be checked.
- You do not need to make a public accusation.
- You do not need to choose an external regulator unless internal routes are unsafe, conflicted, or failing.

## Important reassurance
Feeling trapped or panicked in this situation is understandable. Slowing the process down, refusing to knowingly submit false information, and keeping a factual record are reasonable first steps. In the UK, workers can be protected if they make a qualifying whistleblowing disclosure in the right way, and protection can apply from the start of employment.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help based on your role, employment status, sector, contract, and the type of records involved.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, regulatory, therapeutic, or professional advice. The safest route depends on the facts. If retaliation has already started, such as threats, suspension, disciplinary action, dismissal, reduced hours, or bullying, get prompt confidential advice and keep a clear timeline.
