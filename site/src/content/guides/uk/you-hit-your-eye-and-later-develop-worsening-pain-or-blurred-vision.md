# What to do if… you hit your eye and later develop worsening pain or blurred vision (UK)

## Short answer
Seek immediate medical assessment at an Accident and Emergency (A&E) department if pain worsens or vision changes after an eye injury. Call 999 if the injury was caused by a high-speed impact or if you cannot safely travel.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive yourself if your vision is blurred or double.
- Do not blow your nose, as this can cause air to enter the tissues around the eye.
- Do not rub, press, or apply pressure to the eye or eyelid.
- Do not attempt to remove any objects that appear stuck or embedded in the eye.
- Do not wear contact lenses until a clinician has confirmed it is safe to do so.
- Do not use eye drops or ointments unless they have been prescribed for this specific injury.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate 999 or A&E triggers.** Go to A&E immediately or call 999 if:
   - The injury involved a high-speed impact (e.g., from power tools, lawnmowers, or a fast-moving ball).
   - There is a visible cut to the eyeball or blood inside the eye.
   - The pupil looks oddly shaped or is a different size than the other.
   - You have a sudden loss of vision, double vision, or significant blurring.
   - You have a severe headache, are feeling sick, or are vomiting.
2. **Contact NHS 111 if you are unsure which service to attend.** Call 111 or use 111 online to be directed to the nearest urgent eye service or A&E. State clearly that you sustained an injury earlier and symptoms are now deteriorating.
3. **Protect the eye without applying pressure.** If the eye is painful to keep open, keep it closed gently. You may loosely tape a clean paper cup over the bone surrounding the eye to act as a shield if there is a risk of further accidental contact.
4. **Use standard over-the-counter pain relief if required.** Paracetamol or ibuprofen can be taken to manage discomfort, provided you have no medical reasons to avoid them. Follow the dosage instructions on the packaging and inform the clinician what you have taken.
5. **Remove contact lenses only if it is painless.** If a lens feels stuck or if removing it causes any increase in pain, leave it in place and inform the medical staff.
6. **Gather details about the injury for the clinician.** Prepare to describe:
   - Exactly what hit the eye and the speed of impact.
   - The time the injury occurred and when the symptoms worsened.
   - Any specific visual changes, such as flashes of light, new floaters, or shadows.
7. **Arrange safe transport.** Ask someone to drive you or use a taxi. Do not attempt to use public transport or walk alone if your vision is compromised.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the specific cause of the pain or vision change before seeking help.
- You do not need to wait for a GP appointment if symptoms are worsening.
- You do not need to apply ice or compresses if they require pressing against the eye.

## Important reassurance
It is common for symptoms of an eye injury to appear or worsen several hours after the initial impact. Vision changes and increasing pain are physiological signals that require a professional assessment. Seeking help promptly is the standard protective action for eye health.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for worsening symptoms following a blunt eye injury. It does not replace professional medical diagnosis or long-term clinical care.

## Important note
Eye injuries can involve internal damage that is not visible on the surface. If you experience worsening pain, light sensitivity, or any change in your sight, you must be assessed by a healthcare professional today.
