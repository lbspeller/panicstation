# What to do if… you receive a notice that your vehicle has been impounded or seized (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as time-critical. Verify who has the vehicle and contact the named police pound, recovery operator, police force, or ordering authority today so you know the deadline, documents, charges, and release route.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore the notice until the weekend; storage fees can increase and some vehicles can be disposed of if they are not reclaimed in time.
- Don’t pay anyone who contacted you by text, social media, or an unexpected payment link unless you have checked the route through an official source.
- Don’t travel to a pound or police station without checking what documents they will accept.
- Don’t assume the vehicle must be released just because it is yours; release can depend on identity, keeper or owner status, insurance, licence position, and the reason it was taken.
- Don’t argue at the counter if you are upset. Ask what is needed for release, then ask separately how to dispute the seizure or charges.

## What to do now
1. **Check the notice and identify who has the vehicle.**
   - Find the vehicle registration, reference number, date, stated reason, location, and deadline.
   - If the notice came by text, social media, or looks suspicious, check the police force or authority through its official website or call 101 before using any payment route.

2. **Ask exactly what deadline applies.**
   - For many police seizures under section 165A of the Road Traffic Act 1988, force guidance says the owner or registered keeper must attend the required pound or police station within seven working days of the seizure notice or notice letter.
   - Some force guidance says the vehicle may be disposed of if it is not reclaimed, or if the legal requirements are not met, after a further period such as 14 days. Follow the dates on your notice and the instructions from the force or authority holding the vehicle.

3. **Find out which process you are in.**
   - **Police seizure for insurance or licence issues:** ask what proof of identity, ownership, driving licence, insurance, MOT, and tax position they need before release.
   - **Parking, obstruction, abandoned-vehicle, or local-authority removal:** ask which authority ordered the removal, where the vehicle is held, what must be paid now, and how to make any representations later.
   - **Held for investigation or evidence:** ask for the crime or case reference and which officer, unit, or authority must authorise release.

4. **Ask whether you must go to a police station before the pound.**
   - Some forces require you to take documents to a specified police station, or to have a seizure form stamped or a release notice issued, before the pound can release the vehicle.
   - Ask: “Do I need police-station authorisation before the pound will release it?”

5. **Gather a release pack before you travel.**
   - Ask the force, pound, or authority what they will accept. Commonly requested items include:
     - photo ID
     - the seizure, impound, or removal notice
     - proof that you are the registered keeper or owner, such as the V5C, new keeper supplement, bill of sale, or other accepted evidence
     - valid driving licence, if you or a nominated driver will drive it away
     - valid insurance evidence that covers release of an impounded vehicle, if required
     - MOT evidence or recovery arrangements if the vehicle cannot legally or safely be driven away
   - If you are not the registered keeper or owner, ask what written authority, identity documents, and attendance rules apply.

6. **If a document is missing, ask about substitutes immediately.**
   - Tell them what you do have, such as a purchase receipt, DVLA keeper evidence, digital or emailed insurance evidence, or proof of address.
   - Ask what exact wording they need on any insurance certificate, especially if insurance was arranged after the seizure or if a nominated driver will collect the vehicle.

7. **Call ahead before travelling.**
   - Confirm the address, opening hours, whether an appointment is needed, payment methods, who must attend in person, and whether property can be collected if the vehicle cannot yet be released.
   - Ask whether repairs are allowed at the pound; many police pounds do not allow repairs there, so you may need an insured recovery operator.

8. **Get the costs in writing or on a receipt.**
   - Ask for the current removal, storage, and any disposal-related charges.
   - Ask what keeps increasing daily and from when. Keep the itemised receipt.

9. **If the notice is wrong, split release from dispute.**
   - Say clearly if it is not your vehicle, the plate may be cloned, you sold it before the date on the notice, you were not the driver, or the keeper details are wrong.
   - Ask two separate questions: “What is needed to stop fees and disposal risk now?” and “What is the route to challenge the seizure, removal, or charges?”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a formal complaint, start a legal claim, or write a long challenge letter.
- You do not need to settle every argument about fault at the pound counter.
- You do not need to know the whole law right now. First confirm where the vehicle is, the deadline, what documents are needed, and what action stops fees or disposal risk getting worse.

## Important reassurance
A notice like this can feel shocking and urgent, especially if you were not expecting it. The useful first step is not to solve the whole dispute; it is to verify the notice, protect the vehicle from avoidable fees or disposal, and keep your options open.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The exact release route, documents, charges, and dispute options can vary by police force, local authority, pound, vehicle condition, and reason for seizure or removal. Later decisions may need specialist legal, motoring, insurance, or debt advice.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, insurance, or professional advice. If anything on your notice conflicts with this guide, follow the notice and confirm the details directly with the named police force, pound, recovery operator, or ordering authority through an official contact route.
