# What to do if… you smell exhaust fumes inside the cabin while stopped or parked (UK)

## Short answer
Switch off the engine immediately and move all occupants into fresh air away from the vehicle. Do not stay inside the car to monitor the smell or wait for it to dissipate.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep the engine running while attempting to locate the source of the smell.
- Do not remain in the vehicle with windows open as a "solution" while the engine is on.
- Do not use the "recirculate" setting on your ventilation system to try and block the smell.
- Do not drive yourself to a hospital or GP if you feel lightheaded, confused, or physically weak.
- Do not assume the situation is safe just because the smell is faint; carbon monoxide is odourless and may be present alongside other exhaust gases.

## What to do now
1. **Turn off the engine and remove the key or de-activate the ignition immediately.** This stops the production of further fumes at the source.
2. **Evacuate all passengers and pets to a location with clear, moving air.** Move at least 10 metres away from the vehicle.
   - Prioritise children, elderly passengers, and pets, as they are often affected by fumes more quickly.
3. **Perform an immediate check for symptoms of carbon monoxide (CO) exposure.** Look for signs in yourself and others:
   - Headache, dizziness, or nausea.
   - Feeling unusually tired, weak, or sleepy.
   - Confusion or difficulty thinking clearly.
   - Shortness of breath or chest pain.
   - Flushed, hot, or red skin.
4. **Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if high-risk symptoms are present.** Seek emergency help if anyone:
   - Is struggling to breathe or has chest pain.
   - Is suddenly confused or behaving strangely.
   - Has collapsed or lost consciousness.
   - Cannot be easily woken up.
5. **Contact NHS 111 for medical advice if symptoms are mild but persistent.** Tell the operator you have been exposed to vehicle exhaust fumes and describe your symptoms.
6. **Arrange for a recovery service or mobile mechanic rather than driving the car further.** Operating the vehicle while an exhaust leak is suspected allows toxic gases to continue entering the cabin.
7. **Keep the vehicle doors or windows open if it is in an enclosed space like a garage.** Only do this if you can do so quickly without re-entering the vehicle for more than a few seconds.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the mechanical cause of the leak (such as a manifold crack or tailpipe blockage) right now.
- You do not need to decide on repair options or costs while you are still in the immediate aftermath of exposure.
- You do not need to "air out" the car today; professional inspection is required before the vehicle is safe to use again.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of panic or physical "fogginess" when smelling exhaust fumes. Moving to fresh air is the single most effective action to stop further exposure, and most mild symptoms begin to improve once you are away from the source.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and harm reduction only. It does not replace a professional mechanical inspection or a formal medical assessment.

## Important note
Exhaust fumes contain carbon monoxide, which can be fatal. If you suspect exposure has occurred, do not rely on your own judgement of "feeling fine" if you have experienced any dizziness or confusion. Always seek a professional medical opinion following suspected gas inhalation.
