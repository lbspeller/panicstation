# What to do if… you notice a new irregular heartbeat or skipped beats and feel unsettled (UK)

## Short answer
If you have an irregular or skipped heartbeat accompanied by **chest pain, shortness of breath, severe dizziness, or fainting**, call **999** or go to A&E immediately. If you do not have these emergency symptoms but the sensation is **new**, worsening, or you feel generally unwell, contact **NHS 111** or your GP surgery for an urgent assessment.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not drive yourself** to any medical facility if you feel faint, lightheaded, or unsteady.
- **Do not engage in vigorous activity** or exercise to "test" your heart while the rhythm feels irregular.
- **Do not take extra doses** of your own medication or use anyone else's medicine to try and regulate your heart.
- **Do not consume stimulants** such as caffeine, energy drinks, nicotine, or alcohol to "calm down" or cope with the sensation.
- **Do not repeatedly check your pulse** or monitor your heart rate for long periods, as this can increase anxiety and physical symptoms.

## What to do now
1. **Sit down and remain still for 5 minutes.** Find a chair where you can sit upright with your feet flat on the floor. Allow your breathing to settle without trying to force it.
2. **Perform an immediate emergency check.** **Call 999 now** if your heart palpitations occur alongside any of the following:
   - **Chest pain, tightness, or a heavy pressure sensation.**
   - **Sudden or severe shortness of breath.**
   - **Feeling like you are about to faint, or actually losing consciousness.**
   - **Severe, persistent dizziness or lightheadedness.**
3. **Contact NHS 111 if emergency symptoms have stopped.** If you experienced the red flags above but they have now passed, you must still seek **urgent medical advice** via 111 (phone or online) or by calling your GP surgery for a same-day appointment.
4. **Provide specific details to the 111 operator or clinician.** When you speak to them, be ready to confirm:
   - If the irregular rhythm is **happening right now.**
   - Exactly what it feels like (e.g., fluttering, thumping, or a sudden pause).
   - Any associated symptoms like cold sweats, nausea, or mild breathlessness.
   - Any history of heart conditions or thyroid problems in yourself or your close family.
5. **Create a brief 60-second log of the episode.** Write down in your phone:
   - The time it started and how long it lasted.
   - What you were doing at the time (e.g., resting, eating, or standing up).
   - Any potential triggers in the last few hours, such as high stress, poor sleep, or a large amount of caffeine.
6. **Limit pulse checks to one brief observation.** If you need to check your pulse to report it to 111, touch your wrist gently. **Do not press hard on your neck** or check both sides of your neck at once, as this can cause further dizziness.
7. **Isolate yourself from further triggers.** 
   - Ask someone to stay with you until the sensation passes or you receive medical advice.
   - Avoid all nicotine and caffeine for the remainder of the day.
   - Drink a small glass of water if you have not had enough fluids today.

## What can wait
- **You do not need to diagnose the cause** of the rhythm change yourself; heart-related sensations are often benign but require professional verification.
- **You do not need to research "arrhythmia" or "ectopic beats" online** right now, as this often leads to unnecessary alarm.
- **You do not need to make long-term lifestyle changes** today, such as starting a new diet or supplement regime.
- **You do not need to decide about driving for the long term**, but you should avoid driving until a clinician confirms it is safe to do so.

## Important reassurance
Palpitations and skipped beats are very common and, in many cases, are not caused by a serious heart condition. Feeling unsettled is a normal response to noticing your heartbeat. Taking the step to get it checked by a professional is the standard, safest way to handle a new symptom.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions for the UK. It is not a substitute for clinical diagnosis, which typically requires an ECG or professional monitoring.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only. If your symptoms worsen or you are worried that you are having a medical emergency, always call 999. Under UK law, if you are diagnosed with certain heart conditions that cause "incapacity," you may be required to stop driving and notify the DVLA.
