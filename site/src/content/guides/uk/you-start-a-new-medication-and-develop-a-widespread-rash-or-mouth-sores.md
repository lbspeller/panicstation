# What to do if… you start a new medication and develop a widespread rash or mouth sores (UK)

## Short answer
Seek urgent medical assessment immediately by calling 999 if you have any "red flag" symptoms (breathing trouble, skin peeling, or sores in the mouth, eyes, or genitals). If you are not in immediate danger, do not take any more doses and contact NHS 111 or your GP for a same-day appointment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take another dose of the medication to "see if it gets better" or to "test" the reaction.
- Do not apply steroid creams, take leftover antibiotics, or use antihistamines to mask the symptoms before being seen by a clinician.
- Do not pick, rub, or attempt to burst any blisters or peel away loose skin.
- Do not wait until the next morning if the rash is spreading, painful, or accompanied by a fever.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel dizzy, faint, or have any swelling of the face, tongue, or throat.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency red flags and call 999 immediately if they appear.** 
   - Symptoms requiring an emergency ambulance include:
   - Difficulty breathing, wheezing, or feeling like your throat is closing.
   - Swelling of the lips, face, tongue, or throat.
   - A rash that is blistering, peeling, or feels like it is burning or extremely painful.
   - Sores or ulcers inside the mouth, on the genitals, or involving the eyes (redness, pain, or light sensitivity).
   - Feeling faint, confused, or collapsing.
2. **Stop taking the suspected medication immediately unless a doctor has told you it is dangerous to stop it suddenly.**
   - If you are unsure if the medicine can be stopped (such as certain psychiatric or heart medications), call NHS 111 or your GP for an immediate "safe stop" instruction.
3. **Contact NHS 111 or your GP surgery for a same-day urgent assessment if you do not have 999 red flags.**
   - Tell the operator or receptionist: "I have started a new medication and I have developed a widespread rash and mouth sores."
   - Provide the specific name of the medication and when you started it.
4. **Gather the physical evidence of the reaction to show the clinician.**
   - Keep the medication packaging and the paper Patient Information Leaflet (PIL).
   - Note down the exact time you took the first dose and the exact time the first symptoms appeared.
   - Take clear photos of the rash in natural light, including "wide shots" showing the spread and "close-ups" of any specific spots or blisters.
5. **Monitor for systemic symptoms while you wait for medical contact.**
   - Check your temperature with a thermometer if possible.
   - Look for "target" spots (red circles that are darker in the middle, resembling a bullseye).
   - Watch for any new soreness when swallowing or peeing.

## What can wait
- Deciding on a long-term alternative medication; your immediate safety is the priority.
- Formally reporting the side effect via the MHRA Yellow Card scheme (this is done after you are stable).
- Determining if you have a lifelong allergy or just a one-off reaction; this requires a later specialist review.
- Telling your employer or school details beyond "I am seeking urgent medical care."

## Important reassurance
Developing a reaction to a new medication is not your fault and it is not an "overreaction" to seek urgent help. Medical professionals take widespread rashes and mouth sores very seriously because early intervention is the most effective way to manage potential drug-induced skin conditions like SJS or DRESS syndrome.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency identification only. It does not replace clinical diagnosis or specialist allergy testing.

## Important note
This information is for guidance and does not constitute medical advice. Medication reactions can escalate rapidly; if you feel you are worsening or cannot reach a GP, go to your nearest Accident & Emergency (A&E) department immediately.
