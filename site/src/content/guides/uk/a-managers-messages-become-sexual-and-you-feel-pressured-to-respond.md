# What to do if… a manager’s messages become sexual and you feel pressured to respond (UK)

## Short answer
Cease interaction with the sexual content immediately, preserve copies of all communication including your responses, and move the matter into your employer's formal reporting system or HR to activate legal protections.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete any messages, even if you feel ashamed or panicked by their content.
- Do not feel obligated to respond to sexual content to maintain your employment status or safety.
- Do not meet the manager in a private or isolated location to "clear things up."
- Do not assume you must handle this alone or that your responses (or lack thereof) have made the situation your fault.
- Do not resign immediately without seeking advice, as this may impact your legal rights regarding constructive dismissal.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe pause and stop non-work communication.** Physically step away from the device or the person if they are present.
   - You are not required to reply to sexual or inappropriate content.
   - If a response is necessary for work functions, keep it strictly professional and do not acknowledge the sexual elements.
2. **Preserve the evidence across all platforms.** 
   - Screenshot every message, ensuring the sender’s name, phone number or handle, and the timestamp are visible.
   - Capture the context of the conversation, including what was said before and after.
   - If the messages are on a work-issued device or account, take photos of the screen with a personal phone or write a detailed log to ensure you have a record if your access is revoked.
3. **Check your employer's Harassment or Dignity at Work policy.**
   - Identify the specific person or department (often HR or a designated "Safeguarding Lead") responsible for receiving harassment complaints.
   - Confirm if there is a confidential reporting hotline or an anonymous reporting tool available.
4. **Initiate a formal report via email to create a paper trail.**
   - State clearly that you are receiving unwanted sexual messages from your manager and that it is creating a hostile environment.
   - Note that as of 6 April 2026, this report constitutes a "qualifying disclosure" under UK whistleblowing law, providing you with automatic legal protection from detriment or dismissal.
5. **Request interim safety measures from HR.** 
   - Ask for a change in reporting line so you no longer report to that manager during the investigation.
   - Request that all future communication with that manager be conducted through a third party or strictly via monitored work channels.
6. **Contact your Trade Union representative if you are a member.**
   - They can provide advice and have a legal right to accompany you to any grievance or investigation meetings.
7. **Contact ACAS or a specialist support service.**
   - Call the ACAS helpline (0300 123 1100) for impartial advice on your employment rights.
   - Contact the Rape Crisis England & Wales 24/7 Support Line (0808 500 2222) for confidential emotional support regarding sexual harassment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you want to take the case to an Employment Tribunal.
- You do not need to have a "perfect" or polished statement ready; the raw messages and a simple timeline are sufficient for the initial report.
- You do not need to notify the police unless you feel there is an immediate threat of physical harm or if you choose to report a crime.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel frozen, confused, or worried about your job when a manager crosses a boundary. These reactions are typical in situations involving power imbalances. Taking a step back and moving the situation into a formal channel is a standard procedure designed to protect your employment and your well-being.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for workplace sexual harassment via digital messaging. It does not cover the full grievance process, legal strategy for tribunals, or long-term career planning.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or medical advice. Workplace laws, including the Employment Rights Act 2025 and the Worker Protection Act, provide specific protections, but individual cases vary. If you are in immediate physical danger, call **999**.
