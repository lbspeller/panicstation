# What to do if… you are contacted by a detention facility saying someone listed you as their emergency contact (UK)

## Short answer
End the call immediately and verify the request by calling the official switchboard of the facility using a number you have sourced yourself from a trusted government website. Do not provide personal information, confirm your identity, or transfer any funds during an unsolicited incoming call.

## Do not do these things
- Do not trust caller ID or "spoofed" numbers that appear to be from a local police station or prison.
- Do not send money for bail, "release fees," phone credit, transport, or legal vouchers, as official UK facilities do not request these via telephone from emergency contacts.
- Do not give out your full address, date of birth, or National Insurance number to "verify" your status on an incoming call.
- Do not agree to collect property or visit a facility until you have independently confirmed the person's identity and location.
- Do not feel pressured to stay on the line if the caller sounds aggressive, urgent, or insists on secrecy.

## What to do now
1. **End the incoming call immediately to take control of the situation.**  
   **Inform the caller that you will call back through the official switchboard once you have verified the number.** Hang up even if they claim the person is in immediate need or that the "line is recorded."

2. **Source the official contact number for the facility yourself.**  
   **Use GOV.UK to find the "Prisons in England and Wales" directory or search for your local police force's official website.**
   - For police custody: Call **101** and ask to be put through to the custody suite of the station mentioned.
   - For prisons: Call the main switchboard number listed on the official GOV.UK prison finder page.

3. **Verify the individual's presence in that facility with the official staff.**  
   **Ask the staff member to confirm if a person by that name is currently detained and if your number is listed as their emergency contact.** Note that staff may be limited by data protection but can usually confirm if you are on the approved contact list for an individual in their care.

4. **Request to be removed from the contact list if you do not know the person.**  
   **State clearly: "I do not recognize this person and I do not consent to being their emergency contact; please remove my details from their record immediately."** If the calls persist, you may need to escalate to the Unwanted Prisoner Contact Service.

5. **Report the contact attempt if it involved a request for money or seemed fraudulent.**  
   **Contact Report Fraud (the national service replacing Action Fraud) on 0300 123 2040 or via their official website.** If you received a suspicious text or want to report a scam call, forward the details to **7726** (the free national reporting service).

6. **Contact the Safer Custody team if you have genuine concerns about a known person's welfare.**  
   **Search for the specific prison's "Safer Custody hotline" number on GOV.UK if you believe the individual is at risk of self-harm or is being bullied.** This is a dedicated line for families and emergency contacts to report urgent safety concerns.

7. **Use the Unwanted Prisoner Contact Service if the contact feels harassing or unsafe.**  
   **Complete the "Stop prisoner contact" online form on GOV.UK if you are receiving unwanted letters, calls, or messages from someone in prison.** This service can legally block a prisoner from contacting your number or address.

8. **Check with your bank if you have already shared financial details or made a payment.**  
   **Call 159 immediately to be connected to your bank's fraud department if you suspect you have been targeted by a scam.** They can freeze your account and help secure your funds.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to be the primary support person for the detainee right now.
- You do not need to notify other family members or employers until the identity of the detainee is 100% confirmed.
- You do not need to arrange legal representation; detainees are entitled to free, independent legal advice at the point of detention.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a call from a detention facility is naturally distressing, and scammers rely on this "fight or flight" response to bypass your better judgment. Taking five minutes to hang up and call back through an official 101 or prison switchboard is the safest and most responsible action you can take. It will not result in the detainee losing their rights or being treated poorly.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for verifying the legitimacy of a contact claim from a UK detention facility. It does not cover long-term legal strategy, prison visiting procedures, or bail applications.

## Important note
This information is for harm prevention and does not constitute legal advice. Procedures in Scotland and Northern Ireland may vary (call 101 for local police guidance). Always call 999 if you believe a life is in immediate danger or a crime is currently occurring.
