# What to do if… you are asked to decide about donating a loved one’s body to medical science with little time (UK)

## Short answer
First, confirm whether this is whole-body donation to a medical school or anatomy department, not organ or tissue donation. Across the UK, family cannot newly agree whole-body donation after death if the person did not complete the required donation or bequest paperwork while alive.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t say “yes” just to stop the pressure; ask for time to check the paperwork and get clear written details.
- Don’t sign anything you have not read, or anything that does not clearly say who is taking the body, what will happen next, and what costs may fall to the estate.
- Don’t assume organ donation, tissue donation, post-mortem examination, research donation, and whole-body donation are the same process.
- Don’t pay anyone quickly “to secure the donation” without confirming who they are and what the payment is for.
- Avoid embalming or major preparation until the medical school, anatomy office, or Scottish university bequest co-ordinator confirms what they require.

## What to do now
1. **Ask the person contacting you to slow down and name the exact process.** Say: “Is this whole-body donation to a medical school or anatomy department, organ or tissue donation, or a post-mortem examination?” Write down the answer, their name, role, organisation, phone number, and the time they called.
2. **Look for the person’s own donation paperwork.** Check for a signed body donation or anatomy bequest form, a card or letter from a medical school, paperwork kept with the will, or documents held by the executor or solicitor.
3. **If you cannot find paperwork completed while they were alive, say so clearly.** You can say: “We cannot find valid body donation paperwork completed by them. What are the immediate alternatives?” Ask the hospital bereavement team or funeral director what needs to happen next.
4. **If you do find paperwork, contact the named medical school, anatomy office, or Scottish university bequest co-ordinator directly.** Ask: “Can you accept the donation today, and what do you need from us before anything is moved?” Acceptance is not guaranteed, even when paperwork exists.
5. **Ask the practical questions before agreeing to any next step.** Write down the answers to: “Are we within your usual area?”, “Who pays for transport if we are outside it?”, “What happens if the donation is declined?”, and “What happens afterwards, including ashes or any memorial service?”
6. **Check whether official release timing may affect donation.** Ask the hospital bereavement office whether the death is being referred to the coroner, or in Scotland the Procurator Fiscal, and whether that is likely to affect acceptance by the donation programme.
7. **Ask someone to hold the situation steady while you verify.** Ask the bereavement team or funeral director what the real deadline is, what will happen if the medical school declines, and what they will do while you check consent and acceptance.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide now what kind of memorial, funeral, or service there will be.
- You do not need to justify the decision to relatives, hospital staff, or anyone else while you are checking the facts.
- You can leave detailed financial planning until you know whether the donation can be accepted and what costs, if any, may apply.
- You can decide later how to talk about the decision with wider family or friends.

## Important reassurance
This is a lot to be asked during shock and grief. A careful pause is reasonable: the immediate job is to check whether your loved one made this choice while alive, avoid irreversible steps, and get clear answers from the right organisation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the first hours or day. Later decisions may need help from the hospital bereavement service, a funeral director, the executor, a solicitor, the medical school or anatomy office, or the relevant Scottish university bequest co-ordinator.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Body donation programmes have their own acceptance criteria and may decline a donation even where the person completed donation paperwork.
