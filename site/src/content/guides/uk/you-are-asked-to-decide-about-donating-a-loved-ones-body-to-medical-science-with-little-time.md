# What to do if… you are asked to decide about donating a loved one’s body to medical science with little time (UK)

## Short answer
Identify immediately if the request is for **anatomical examination** (whole-body donation to a medical school). Under UK law, this specific type of donation requires the deceased person's **written, signed, and witnessed consent** given before they died; family members or executors cannot legally provide this consent after death.

## Do not do these things
- Don't feel pressured to give an immediate "yes" without seeing evidence of prior consent.
- Don't assume that registration on the NHS Organ Donor Register automatically counts as consent for whole-body anatomical examination; they are separate legal processes.
- Don't sign any agreement until you have confirmed which medical school is involved and what costs (such as transport) the estate may be required to pay.
- Don't allow any invasive procedures, embalming, or major body preparation to take place until the receiving medical school has formally confirmed they can accept the donation.
- Don't forget that medical schools can, and frequently do, decline donations even when valid consent is in place (e.g., due to the cause of death or capacity limits).

## What to do now
1. **Verify the identity and purpose of the person calling you.** Ask for their name, the institution they represent (e.g., a specific medical school or a hospital bereavement office), and whether the request is for organ transplantation or whole-body anatomical examination.
2. **Search for a valid "Declaration of Bequest" or written consent form.** In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland (Human Tissue Act 2004) and Scotland (Human Tissue (Scotland) Act 2006), the deceased must have signed a written statement witnessed by at least one other person.
   - Check among the deceased's legal papers, their will, or with their GP.
   - Look for a donor card specifically from a local medical school.
3. **Notify the caller immediately if no written, witnessed consent exists.** If you cannot find evidence of the person's own prior written consent, inform the solicitor or hospital staff that the donation for anatomical examination cannot legally proceed. 
4. **Contact the medical school's Bequest Office directly if consent is found.** Do not rely solely on hospital staff to coordinate this; speak to the medical school to ask if they have the capacity and criteria to accept the body today.
5. **Ask for a clear statement of transport and funeral costs.** Confirm whether the medical school covers the cost of transporting the body from the place of death and whether they will pay for a basic cremation or burial once their examination is complete (usually 1 to 3 years later).
6. **Determine if the Coroner or Procurator Fiscal is involved.** Ask the hospital doctor or the bereavement office if the death must be reported to the Coroner (England/Wales/NI) or Procurator Fiscal (Scotland), as this may delay or prevent the possibility of donation.
7. **Request a "hold" on any funeral director involvement until acceptance is confirmed.** If the medical school accepts the donation, they will often use their own contracted funeral director or transport service; using your own before confirmation may result in unnecessary costs for the estate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to plan a memorial service or a "celebration of life" in the next few hours.
- You do not need to decide on a permanent headstone or final resting place for ashes, as the anatomical process can take several years.
- You do not need to justify your decision to stop the donation process if the legal requirements for consent are not met.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed by the technicalities of the law while grieving. The requirement for prior written consent exists specifically to protect individuals' wishes and to relieve families of the burden of making this specific decision under pressure. If you cannot find a form, the law simply prevents the donation from happening, and you can proceed with a standard funeral without guilt.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate procedural and legal hurdles of body donation in the UK within the first 24 hours of death. It does not cover long-term estate administration or the emotional aspects of bereavement.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, the Human Tissue Authority (HTA) regulates this sector; in Scotland, it is overseen by HM Inspector of Anatomy. Medical schools set their own specific acceptance criteria which may change without notice.
