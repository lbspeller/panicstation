# What to do if… you find water pooling along a wall and it seems to be spreading (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as an active leak. Keep away from any electrical risk, shut off the water at the internal stop tap if you can do that safely, and slow the spread while you call the right help.

## Do not do these things
- Do not step into pooled water if it could be reaching sockets, extension leads, appliances, or the consumer unit.
- Do not touch electrical switches while standing in water.
- Do not keep using taps, toilets, washing machines, dishwashers, boilers, or heating to see if the leak stops.
- Do not drill into the wall, lift flooring, or pull off skirting while water is still spreading.
- Do not use heaters, electrical fans, or mains-powered drying equipment in the wet area until the electrics have been checked or are clearly safe.
- Do not stand under a sagging ceiling, bulging plaster, or cracking wet plaster.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safer.** Move children and pets out of the room. Put on shoes. If you smell burning, hear buzzing or crackling, see sparks, or someone may have had an electric shock, keep everyone out and call **999**.

2. **Deal with electricity cautiously.**
   - If you can reach the consumer unit without stepping in or over water, switch off the electricity at the main switch.
   - If reaching it means going through water, do not try. Keep people out of the area and get urgent help.
   - Do not unplug or move wet electrical items unless the electricity is off and it is safe to do so.

3. **Shut off the water if you can.**
   - Find the internal stop tap, also called a stopcock or stop valve. It is usually under the kitchen sink, in a cupboard, under the stairs, in a utility area, or near where the water supply enters the home.
   - Turn it clockwise slowly to close it. Do not force it if it will not move.
   - If one appliance is clearly involved, such as a washing machine or dishwasher, close its local isolation valve if you can reach it safely. Keep the main stop tap off if water is still spreading.

4. **If you live in a flat, alert nearby people quickly.** If water may be coming from above, next door, or a shared riser, contact the concierge, building manager, managing agent, or neighbour above if you can do that safely.

5. **Contain the spread without taking risks.** Put towels at the edge of the pool to slow it. Place a bucket or container under any drip. Move rugs, cardboard, papers, soft furnishings, valuables, and electrical items away from the wet edge.

6. **Avoid contact if the water might be dirty.** If the water may involve a toilet, drain, sewage, outside floodwater, or you are not sure, keep pets away and avoid splashes. Wash hands and exposed skin after any contact.

7. **Call the right help.**
   - If you rent, report it as an urgent repair to your landlord, letting agent, housing provider, or out-of-hours repair number.
   - If you own, contact an emergency plumber.
   - If water is still coming in after the stop tap is fully off, contact your water company’s emergency contact route.
   - If electrics may have been affected but there is no immediate danger, keep the area out of use and contact a qualified electrician or the relevant electricity network contact route.

8. **Record only what is safe to record.** Take quick photos or short videos of the water, the wall line, and any visible source for your landlord, insurer, plumber, or building manager. Do not delay safety steps to do this.

9. **Start drying only after the leak is stopped and electrics are safe.** Open windows or internal doors if practical and safe. Do not use electrical drying equipment in a wet or uncertain electrical area.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to redecorate, replace flooring, or open the wall.
- You do not need to work out fault, liability, or insurance wording right now.
- You do not need a perfect diagnosis before calling help; “water is pooling along a wall and spreading” is enough.
- You do not need to clean the whole room before the water source is controlled.

## Important reassurance
Water can travel behind skirting, under flooring, and through plaster, so the visible pool may not show where the leak started. The useful first moves are to keep people away from electrical risk, shut off the water if you can, slow the spread, and report it.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and reduce avoidable harm. Repairs, drying, mould treatment, insurance, landlord responsibilities, and building safety decisions may need a plumber, electrician, building professional, landlord, insurer, or other specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, insurance, electrical, plumbing, building, medical, or other professional advice. If anyone is in immediate danger, the structure seems unsafe, electrics may be affected, or you cannot stop the water, keep people out of the area and contact emergency services or qualified professionals.
