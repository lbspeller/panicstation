# What to do if… you need to tell a child that a relative is dying soon (UK)

## Short answer
Ask one calm, trusted adult to tell the child today, using simple direct words, then stay with them while they react and ask questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use confusing euphemisms, such as “gone to sleep”, “lost”, or “passed away”, if you can avoid it; children can take these literally.
- Do not overload them with medical detail, timelines, or adult conversations about treatment choices.
- Do not promise exact times or make guarantees that no one can be sure of.
- Do not tell them right before school or bedtime if you can help it, unless time forces it; they need time to settle with you afterwards.
- Do not make them responsible for comforting adults, being brave, or not upsetting other people.
- Do not force a visit or a goodbye moment on the spot; offer choices.

## What to do now
1. **Choose the adult and the place.**  
   If possible, the child should be told by a parent, carer, or another adult they trust, in a quiet place, with no one rushing away straight after. If you are too upset to speak clearly, ask one other calm adult to sit with you.

2. **Start by asking what they already know.**  
   You can say: “I need to tell you something important about [Name]. What have you noticed or heard?” This helps you correct rumours and match the words they already understand.

3. **Say the core truth simply, then pause.**  
   Use one short sentence first. For example: “[Name] is very ill, and the doctors think they are likely to die soon.” Or: “[Name]’s body is stopping working, and they will not get better.” Then stop and let the child react before adding more.

4. **Use clear words for death if they ask what it means.**  
   You can say: “When someone dies, their body stops working. They are no longer alive, and they cannot feel pain, hunger, cold, or tiredness in the way living people do.”

5. **Answer the urgent fears directly.**  
   Say the things many children need to hear:
   - “Nothing you did, said, or thought caused this.”
   - “You cannot catch this illness like a cold.”
   - “Grown-ups will keep looking after you.”
   - “Here is what will happen today and tonight.”

6. **Offer an optional way to say goodbye.**  
   Give choices, not pressure. The child may want to visit, make a phone or video call, send a voice note, draw a picture, write a card, or send a small item. If a visit is possible, describe what they may see, such as the person looking very tired, sleeping a lot, speaking differently, or having tubes or monitors. Agree who will go, roughly how long you will stay, and a signal the child can use if they want to leave.

7. **Make the next 24 hours predictable.**  
   Tell the child who is staying with them, who is picking them up, where they will sleep, what will happen about school or nursery, and who they can go to with questions.

8. **Tell the school or nursery today if you can.**  
   Send a brief message and ask for one named contact, such as the class teacher, head of year, or pastoral lead. This helps the child have predictable support without the story being repeated to several people.

9. **Ask for specialist support if you are stuck for words.**  
   Contact the hospice, hospital team, GP surgery, Child Bereavement UK, Marie Curie, or another UK bereavement support service. You can ask for help choosing age-appropriate wording quickly.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain the full medical history, family disputes, or funeral plans now.
- You do not need to decide today whether the child will attend the funeral.
- You do not need to get the conversation perfect; you can return to it in small pieces.
- You do not need to answer every question immediately. It is OK to say, “I do not know yet, but I will tell you when I do.”

## Important reassurance
A child’s reaction may not look how you expect. They may cry, go quiet, ask practical questions, change the subject, or want to play. This does not mean they do not care; children often take in frightening news in small pieces.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first conversation and the next day or two. Later decisions may need help from bereavement support, healthcare staff, school staff, or safeguarding services.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, safeguarding, therapeutic, or professional advice. If there is immediate danger, call 999. If you need urgent health advice and it is not life-threatening, use NHS 111. If you are worried you cannot keep the child safe, contact emergency services or local children’s social care.
