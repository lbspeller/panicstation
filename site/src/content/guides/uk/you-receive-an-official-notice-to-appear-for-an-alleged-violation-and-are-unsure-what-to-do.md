# What to do if… you receive an official notice to appear for an alleged violation and are unsure what to do (UK)

## Short answer
Confirm what the notice is and do not give a rushed explanation before getting legal advice if it involves an interview, caution, criminal allegation, or hearing. Preserve what you already have and write a simple timeline today.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice, miss the date, or assume it will go away.
- Do not use contact details on the notice if you are worried it may be fake; find the organisation’s contact details through its official website.
- Do not explain your side in a rushed email, phone call, message, or social media post.
- Do not alter, delete, crop, rename, or tidy messages, files, photos, or videos in a way that changes context or timestamps.
- Do not contact the complainant, witnesses, officer, official, or other party to sort it out unless a solicitor or the issuing body has told you it is appropriate.
- Do not pay, sign, admit, plead, or accept an informal outcome just to make the panic stop.

## What to do now
1. **Read only for the basics first.** Write down the issuing body, reference number, date, time, place, whether attendance is required in person or remotely, and any deadline for replying.

2. **Confirm what kind of notice it is.** Use official contact details from the organisation’s website and ask:
   - “Can you confirm this reference number?”
   - “What exactly does this notice require me to do?”
   - “Is this a court hearing, tribunal hearing, interview under caution, voluntary interview, summons, requisition, penalty matter, or something else?”
   - “Are there documents or evidence already filed or served that I can request?”

3. **If it involves an interview under caution, a voluntary interview, arrest, or police custody, ask for a solicitor before answering questions.** Say clearly: “I want legal advice before any interview.” Free legal advice is generally available for police-station questioning, but the exact route and terminology differ across the UK.

4. **If it is a court or tribunal hearing, ask how to get the papers.** Ask the court, tribunal, regulator, local authority, or other issuing body how to obtain copies of the application, charge, allegation, witness statements, exhibits, case papers, or bundle. Keep a record of who you contacted, when, and what they said.

5. **Contact a solicitor or official advice route with the notice in front of you.** Tell them the reference number, date, issuing body, and whether the notice mentions caution, summons, requisition, tribunal, prosecution, penalty, breach, or attendance. Ask what legal aid or free advice routes may apply in your part of the UK.

6. **Start one evidence folder without editing anything.** Put in:
   - The notice, envelope, and every page or attachment.
   - Any earlier letters, emails, forms, licences, permits, penalty notices, or reference numbers.
   - Full message threads and emails, not just selected parts.
   - Original photos, videos, documents, downloads, and files where possible.
   - Receipts, bank entries, invoices, travel records, delivery confirmations, rota records, appointment confirmations, or access logs you already have.

7. **Make a one-page timeline.** Use neutral facts only: date, time, place, who was involved, what happened, and what document or file supports it. Mark anything uncertain as “not sure” rather than guessing.

8. **List possible evidence, but do not chase people yet.** Write down names of people who may confirm key facts, what they may know, and how they could be contacted. Do not contact them about the allegation until you have advice or clear permission to do so.

9. **Write a short question list.** Include: “What exact rule or allegation is being relied on?”, “What date and time do they allege?”, “What evidence do they already have?”, “Is this about identity, ownership, location, permission, payment, behaviour, or paperwork?”, and “What is the next deadline?”

10. **Ask early for access needs.** If you need an interpreter, disability adjustment, hearing loop, step-free access, help reading documents, extra time at the hearing, or remote attendance where allowed, contact the issuing body and keep a copy of the request.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your whole defence today.
- You do not need to produce a perfect evidence bundle today.
- You do not need to write a long statement before you know what the allegation and process are.
- You do not need to contact witnesses, gather character references, or argue with anyone today.
- You do not need to understand every legal term before preserving the notice, dates, papers, and originals.

## Important reassurance
An official notice can feel urgent and frightening, especially when the allegation is unclear. The safest early move is usually not to prove everything at once, but to avoid missed dates, preserve originals, confirm the process, and get the right advice before speaking in detail.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about evidence, statements, attendance, pleas, settlement, appeals, or representation may need specialist legal help and will depend on the type of notice and where in the UK the matter is being handled.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. UK legal processes, legal aid, police interview rules, court rules, tribunal rules, and official notice requirements vary by nation, case type, and issuing body.
