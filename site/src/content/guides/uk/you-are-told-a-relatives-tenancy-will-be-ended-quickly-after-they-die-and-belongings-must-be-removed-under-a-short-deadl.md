# What to do if… you are told a relative’s tenancy will be ended quickly after they die and belongings must be removed under a short deadline (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign any surrender documents or hand over keys immediately; request the landlord’s demand in writing and inform them that only the legal personal representative (executor or administrator) can formally end the tenancy or authorize the removal of belongings.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a "notice to quit" or "surrender of tenancy" form under pressure from a landlord or agent.
- Do not hand back keys until a formal end date is agreed upon in writing by the person with legal authority.
- Do not personally pay for rent or clearance costs from your own pocket; these are liabilities of the deceased's estate.
- Do not allow a landlord to dispose of belongings without them first serving a formal "Notice to Collect" under the Torts (Interference with Goods) Act 1977.
- Do not enter the property if the landlord has already changed the locks; this requires immediate legal advice rather than forced entry.

## What to do now
1. **Request the landlord's demand and legal justification in writing immediately.** 
   - Ask for the specific date they are demanding the property be vacated.
   - Ask which section of the tenancy agreement or law they are relying on to end the tenancy quickly.
2. **Identify the person with legal authority to manage the estate.**
   - If there is a will, this is the **executor**.
   - If there is no will, a relative must apply to be the **administrator** (letters of administration).
   - In cases of "intestacy" (no will) in England or Wales, the tenancy temporarily vests in the **Public Trustee**, and the landlord must serve a Notice to Quit on them (Form NL1).
3. **Notify the landlord in writing that the tenancy has not yet ended.**
   - State: "The tenancy forms part of the deceased's estate and can only be ended by the Personal Representative or through a court-ordered process."
   - If there is a surviving joint tenant, notify the landlord that the tenancy passes to them automatically through "survivorship."
4. **Identify the specific tenancy type and the nation’s rules.**
   - **England/Wales (Private):** Most tenancies are transitioning to Assured Periodic Tenancies under the Renters' Rights Act 2025; Section 21 "no-fault" evictions are being phased out.
   - **Wales (Social/Private):** These are now "Occupation Contracts" with specific succession rights for "contract-holders."
   - **Scotland:** Different rules apply for Scottish Secure Tenancies and Private Residential Tenancies regarding succession.
5. **Secure the property and belongings without removing them yet.**
   - Take a full photo or video inventory of every room and the condition of the furniture.
   - Record all meter readings (gas, electricity, water).
   - Do not remove high-value items until the executor has noted them for probate purposes.
6. **Request a "Notice to Collect" period for belongings.**
   - If the landlord threatens to clear the property, remind them in writing of their duty under the Torts (Interference with Goods) Act 1977 to store items for a "reasonable period" (usually 14 to 21 days) after serving notice.
7. **Contact the local council's Tenancy Relations Officer if locks are threatened.**
   - If a landlord threatens to change locks without a court order, this may constitute an illegal eviction or harassment.
   - Report the threat to the council's housing department immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide which items to keep, sell, or donate today.
- You do not need to settle the final utility bills or rent arrears until the estate's total value is calculated.
- You do not need to agree to "professional cleaning" or "redecoration" charges demanded by the landlord right now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed by administrative demands during bereavement. Landlords often push for quick turnarounds to minimize "void periods," but the law provides specific protections to ensure estates are handled properly and belongings are not discarded without notice.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization to prevent the loss of a tenancy or belongings. It does not cover the long-term probate process or complex succession disputes which require a solicitor.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Housing laws vary significantly between England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland. If a landlord is acting aggressively or has already blocked access, contact Shelter or Citizens Advice immediately.
