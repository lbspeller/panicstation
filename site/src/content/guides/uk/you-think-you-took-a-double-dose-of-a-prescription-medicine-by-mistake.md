# What to do if… you think you took a double dose of a prescription medicine by mistake (UK)

## Short answer
Do not take any further doses until you have received professional advice: contact **NHS 111** or a **community pharmacist** immediately with the medicine packaging in hand. **Call 999** if any severe symptoms develop.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "balance" the medication by skipping your next scheduled dose or changing your routine without professional instruction.
- Do not take another dose "to be sure" if you are uncertain whether you took the first one.
- Do not use over-the-counter products that may contain the same active ingredients (such as cold / flu remedies containing paracetamol) until advised.
- Do not try to induce vomiting or use home remedies / "antidotes" like milk or water to "flush" the system.
- Do not drive, operate machinery, or consume alcohol if you feel any symptoms like dizziness, confusion, or shakiness.
- Do not stop a medication suddenly if the label states "do not stop suddenly" unless a clinician tells you to do so.

## What to do now
1. **Locate the medicine packaging and the Patient Information Leaflet (PIL) immediately.** 
   - Check the "If you take more than you should" section of the leaflet for specific instructions regarding a double dose.
2. **Determine the likelihood of the error without guessing.** 
   - Count the remaining tablets / capsules in the blister pack or pill organiser and check any digital logs or reminder apps.
   - If you cannot confirm either way, **treat the situation as a confirmed double dose**.
3. **Write down exactly what was taken before making the call.** 
   - Record the medicine name, the strength (mg / micrograms), the exact number of units taken, the time of the error, and any other medications taken in the last 24 hours.
4. **Call NHS 111 or contact a community pharmacist for urgent advice.** 
   - Call **111** (available 24 / 7) or speak to a pharmacist if they are open; keep the packaging in front of you during the call to answer specific questions.
   - For those with hearing or speech loss, use the **18000** emergency text service or the **999 BSL** app if symptoms are severe.
5. **Monitor for red flag symptoms and call 999 if they appear.** 
   - Seek emergency help if you experience: 
   - difficulty breathing or wheezing
   - chest pain or palpitations
   - seizures / fits
   - sudden confusion or extreme drowsiness
   - swelling of the face, lips, or tongue
   - loss of consciousness.
6. **Ensure someone is with you if the medicine causes sedation.** 
   - If the medication is a sedative, opioid, or strong antihistamine, ask another person to stay with you or check in every 15 minutes until a professional has assessed you.
7. **Prioritise an immediate call for high-risk medications.** 
   - Contact **NHS 111** immediately even if you feel fine if the medicine is for:
   - blood sugar (e.g. insulin, sulfonylureas)
   - blood thinning (e.g. warfarin, DOACs)
   - heart rhythm or blood pressure
   - epilepsy / seizures.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a new pill-tracking system (like a dosette box or app) right now.
- You do not need to notify your GP surgery today unless the error involves a controlled drug or long-term prescription adjustment.
- You do not need to worry about "wasting" professional time; dosing errors are a standard clinical priority.

## Important reassurance
Accidental double dosing is a frequent occurrence caused by routine changes or distractions. Healthcare professionals are trained to provide specific "next-step" plans for these exact situations without judgment. 

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the minutes following a suspected medication error. Long-term management and dose adjustments must be handled by your prescribing clinician.

## Important note
This information is not a medical diagnosis. Every medication interacts differently with individual health conditions; always follow the direct instructions provided by NHS 111 or a qualified pharmacist.
