# What to do if… you smell gasoline or fuel around your parked vehicle (UK)

## Short answer
Assume a possible fuel leak. Do not start the vehicle; move people away from the smell, get into fresh air, and arrange recovery or a mechanic. If there is visible dripping, pooling, strong indoor fumes, or anyone feels unwell, call 999 from a safe place.

## Do not do these things
- Do not start the engine to move the vehicle.
- Do not smoke, vape, light matches, or use naked flames near the vehicle.
- Do not use jump leads, chargers, power tools, or anything that could spark near the smell.
- Do not stay in a garage, underground car park, or enclosed space breathing fumes.
- Do not crawl under the vehicle or touch liquid that may be fuel.
- Do not try to patch a fuel line or tank yourself while fumes are present.
- Do not drive the vehicle until it has been checked.

## What to do now
1. Create distance. Move yourself, passengers, children, pets, and bystanders several metres away from the vehicle; move further back if the smell is strong.
2. Get to fresh air. If the vehicle is in a garage or enclosed parking area, leave the space first. If you can do it without going near strong fumes or switching electrics beside the vehicle, open the main door or exit to ventilate.
3. If the fumes are strong indoors, or the vehicle is in an attached garage, get everyone out of the building and call 999 from outside or another safe place.
4. If the smell is not strong and there is no visible flowing fuel, do one quick check from standing height only:
   - look for a fresh puddle, drips, or a damp trail under the vehicle;
   - notice whether the smell is strongest near the fuel filler, under the rear of the vehicle, under the middle, or near the engine bay;
   - check whether the fuel cap or filler flap looks loose, especially if you have just refuelled.
5. If you have just refuelled and it is safe to approach briefly, close or reseat the fuel cap, then step away again. If the smell stays strong, or you see liquid fuel, stop checking.
6. Arrange help without driving the vehicle. Contact your breakdown provider, a recovery service, a garage, or a mobile mechanic, and say that you suspect a petrol or diesel leak.
7. If fuel is on a public road, keep people away from it. Call 999 if there is immediate danger. If there is no immediate danger, report the spillage to the relevant road authority or local council; National Highways covers motorways and major A-roads in England, Traffic Scotland covers Scotland’s trunk roads and motorways, and local roads are normally handled by the relevant local authority. Northern Ireland has its own road-spillage reporting route.
8. If fuel may be entering a drain, stream, river, soil, or another environmental route, report it to the environmental incident service for your UK nation. Use 999 first if there is an immediate risk to people, fire, traffic, or property.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact part that may be leaking right now.
- You do not need to decide repair costs, parts, or the best garage until the vehicle is safe and assessed.
- You do not need to clean up a significant spill yourself.
- You do not need to prove whether it is petrol, diesel, oil, or another fluid before taking basic safety steps.

## Important reassurance
A fuel smell can come from something simple, such as a loose filler cap after refuelling, but it can also mean a real leak. The safest first move is the same either way: do not start or drive the vehicle, step away from fumes, and get the vehicle checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce fire risk, avoid breathing fumes, and prevent a bad first action. Later decisions about inspection, repair, recovery, insurance, or reporting may need help from a mechanic, recovery provider, road authority, environmental regulator, or emergency services.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, engineering, environmental, or professional advice. If you think there is immediate danger, call 999.
