# What to do if… you need to decide whether an ex-partner should be invited to a service and conflict is escalating (UK)

## Short answer
Identify the legal decision-maker (usually the executor or nearest relative) and instruct the funeral director to implement a managed attendance plan that minimises the risk of disruption to the service.

## Do not do these things
- Do not engage in detailed negotiations via group chats or social media where statements can be screenshotted and shared.
- Do not promise "final" attendance decisions while in the immediate peak of an emotional confrontation.
- Do not use the funeral invitation as a tool for resolving long-standing relationship grievances or inheritance disputes.
- Do not assume that venue staff or the officiant will manage a conflict on the day without prior, specific instructions.
- Do not make threats regarding the exclusion of others unless you have the legal authority and a practical means to enforce it.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the person with the legal "right to the body" and funeral arrangements.** 
   - In England and Wales, the executor named in the Will has the primary legal right to arrange the funeral; if there is no Will, the "administrator" (priority follows intestacy rules) holds this right.
   - In Scotland, the Burial and Cremation (Scotland) Act 2016 provides a statutory hierarchy: 1) person nominated by the deceased, 2) surviving spouse / civil partner, 3) cohabitant of at least 6 months, 4) children, 5) parents, 6) siblings.
2. **Appoint a single point of contact for the funeral director and the officiant.**
   - **This individual should be the only person authorised to give or change instructions regarding the guest list, seating, and service flow.**
   - **Notify the funeral director in writing that only this person’s instructions are to be followed to avoid conflicting orders from different family members.**
3. **Determine if the service is legally "public" or "private" before making guest list demands.**
   - **A service in a public space (such as some church services) may be difficult to legally restrict; focus on managing seating and conduct rather than entry.**
   - **A private service at a crematorium or funeral home allows the "client" (the person who signed the contract) to specify an invite-only list.**
4. **Request specific conflict-management measures from the funeral venue and director.**
   - **Ask about staggered arrival and departure times to ensure different parties do not meet in the foyer.**
   - **Request a reserved seating plan that places conflicting parties in separate sections of the venue.**
   - **Identify a "quiet room" or exit route where individuals can be directed if they become overwhelmed or disruptive.**
5. **Issue a single, factual statement regarding the attendance decision and expected conduct.**
   - **Communicate the decision via a one-way channel (such as a formal email) rather than a back-and-forth discussion.**
   - **State clearly that the priority is a peaceful service and that anyone causing a disruption will be asked to leave by venue staff.**
6. **Assign "stewards" or neutral friends to act as greeters at the entrance.**
   - **Brief these individuals on the names of parties involved in the conflict.**
   - **Instruct them to guide people to their pre-arranged seats immediately upon arrival to prevent loitering in communal areas.**
7. **Contact the police on 101 if you receive credible threats of violence or harassment before the service.**
   - **In an immediate emergency where a life is in danger or violence is occurring at the venue, call 999.**
   - **Provide the police with the location and time of the service if you have been threatened with a breach of the peace.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the moral "validity" of an ex-partner's grief or their past actions today.
- You do not need to resolve disagreements regarding the headstone, ashes, or inheritance at this stage.
- You do not need to respond to every message or accusation regarding the guest list decisions.

## Important reassurance
Heightened conflict is a common reaction to the powerlessness felt during bereavement. Choosing to prioritise a stable and peaceful service is a practical safety measure, not an emotional judgment. Following a clear legal hierarchy provides a boundary that can help stop the escalation of family arguments.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for escalating guest-list conflicts. It focuses on identifying legal authority and physical service management. It does not cover long-term mediation or legal disputes over estate administration.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Legal rights regarding funeral arrangements differ between Scotland and the rest of the UK. Funeral wishes expressed in a Will are generally persuasive but not legally binding in England and Wales. If a dispute prevents the funeral from proceeding, you may need to seek urgent legal advice from a solicitor specialising in probate or family law.
