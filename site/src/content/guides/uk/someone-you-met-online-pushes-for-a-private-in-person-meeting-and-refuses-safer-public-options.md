# What to do if… someone you met online pushes for a private in-person meeting and refuses safer public options (UK)

## Short answer
Do not agree to the meeting and end all contact immediately. Block the person on all platforms and call 999 if you feel you are in immediate danger or believe they are looking for you.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree to a "compromise" meeting in a car, hotel, or any secluded space.
- Don’t allow them to book your transport, send a taxi to your house, or see your live location.
- Don’t share your home address, workplace, or the specific names of places you visit daily.
- Don’t keep explaining your boundaries; if they have been ignored once, further negotiation often increases risk.
- Don’t meet them "one last time" to explain why you are ending contact.
- Don’t feel obligated to be "polite" if your safety feels at risk.
- Don’t delete the conversation history before taking screenshots.

## What to do now
1. **Terminate the conversation immediately without further debate.** Send a final message stating: "I am not comfortable with this and I am ending contact," then stop responding.
2. **Take screenshots of the entire conversation and the person’s profile.** Ensure you capture their username, photos, any threats, and any personal information they claimed about themselves.
3. **Block and report the user within the dating app or social media platform.** Use the specific reporting tool for "safety concern," "harassment," or "coaxing/coercion" to alert the platform's safety team.
4. **Update your digital privacy settings immediately.**
   - Disable location services for all social and dating apps.
   - Set social media profiles to private and remove any public "check-ins" or workplace details.
   - If they have your phone number, use your phone’s settings to silence or block unknown callers.
5. **Alert a trusted friend or family member about the situation.** Share the person's profile name and a summary of the pressure they applied so someone else is aware of the contact.
6. **Report the behaviour to the police if you feel threatened.**
   - **Call 999** if the person is threatening immediate violence, claims to be at your location, or you are in immediate danger.
   - **Call 101** or report via your local police force website if you are being harassed or stalked but are not in immediate danger.
7. **Contact specialist support if you feel overwhelmed or unsafe.** If you are 16 or over in England or Wales, call the **24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line** on **0808 500 2222** for confidential guidance.
8. **Report concerns about children or grooming to CEOP.** If you believe the person is targeting a minor or you are under 18, use the **CEOP Safety Centre** online reporting tool.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether you want to press charges today.
- You do not need to figure out if this person was "dangerous" or just "persistent."
- You do not need to respond to any "apologies" or "explanations" they send from new accounts.
- You do not need to justify your decision to friends or family if you don't feel ready.

## Important reassurance
It is a major warning sign when someone refuses a public, safe meeting in favour of a private one. Your instinct to feel uneasy is a valid safety response. You are never required to "prove" your interest by putting your physical safety at risk.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for online safety and harassment. It does not replace a formal police investigation or a long-term safety plan for stalking or domestic abuse.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance and is not legal advice. In any emergency where life or safety is threatened, always dial 999. Specialist organizations like Rape Crisis and the Suzy Lamplugh Trust can provide ongoing support for stalking and harassment.
