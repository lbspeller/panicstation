# What to do if… you are contacted by police about a missing person and asked to meet urgently (UK)

## Short answer
Pause, verify the contact through 101 or the police force’s published contact route, and ask what kind of meeting this is before you attend. Share urgent safety information about the missing person, but do not go to an unverified private location or start a formal interview without legal advice.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t rush to a private address, car park, vehicle, or “neutral” place before you have verified the officer and the request.
- Don’t use a phone number, link, or callback route supplied only by the caller.
- Don’t hand over money, bank details, cards, PINs, one-time codes, gift cards, passwords, or remote access.
- Don’t answer detailed questions on the phone while you are stressed or unsure who you are speaking to.
- Don’t guess, speculate, or fill silence with theories about the missing person.
- Don’t scroll through private messages or photos in front of someone on the spot; keep any relevant information brief and ask how it should be provided.
- Don’t attend an interview “just to clear things up” before you know whether you are being treated as a witness, a source of information, or a suspect.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the caller before doing anything else.**  
   Say: “I’m going to verify this through 101 or the force’s published contact route.” End the call. Use 101, the force website, or another trusted route to ask whether the officer, reference number, and meeting request are genuine. Where practical, use another phone; otherwise wait five minutes before calling back.

2. **Write down the details.**  
   Note the officer’s name, collar or warrant number, police force, station or unit, reference number, phone number shown, time of contact, and exactly what they asked you to do.

3. **Ask what your status is.**  
   Ask: “Am I being asked for information as a witness, or is this a formal interview under caution?” If the answer is unclear, slow the meeting down and ask to speak to a solicitor before any formal interview.

4. **Move the meeting to a safer official setting.**  
   If you agree to meet, ask for a police station or another official setting that can be confirmed through 101 or the police force’s published contact route. If they propose somewhere else, ask why and ask for that reason to be recorded.

5. **Pass on urgent safety facts briefly.**  
   If you believe the missing person is in immediate danger, call 999. Otherwise, give concise facts you personally know, such as last confirmed contact, last known location, vehicle details, serious medical risks you know about, or immediate threats. Make clear what you know directly and what you have only heard from someone else.

6. **Ask for legal advice before any formal interview.**  
   In England and Wales, voluntary interview rights include being asked whether you want free legal advice, and you can ask to delay the interview to speak to a solicitor. In Scotland and Northern Ireland, procedures differ, but you can still say: “I want to speak to a solicitor before any formal interview.”

7. **Arrange support before attending.**  
   Tell a trusted person where you are going, who you are meeting, and when you will check in. If you are under 18, vulnerable, disabled, distressed, or struggling to understand what is happening, ask the officer what support or appropriate adult arrangements apply before any formal interview.

8. **Be careful with phones, devices, and accounts.**  
   Take ID and your notes. If asked to hand over a phone, unlock a device, share account access, or consent to a search, ask what legal power or process is being used and ask to take legal advice before agreeing. If you are told something is compulsory, ask for it to be explained clearly and recorded.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to produce a full timeline immediately.
- You don’t need to search every message, photo, location record, or account while under pressure.
- You don’t need to decide on the spot whether to hand over devices or account access.
- You don’t need to agree to a same-day meeting before verifying the request and arranging support or legal advice.
- You don’t need to contact the missing person’s family or friends unless police ask you to and it feels safe.

## Important reassurance
Missing-person enquiries can move quickly, and police may contact several people urgently. Taking a few minutes to verify identity, pass on urgent safety facts, and avoid unsafe or irreversible steps is a reasonable response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: verify the contact, share urgent safety information, and avoid unsafe meetings or formal interviews without advice. Later decisions about statements, interviews, searches, devices, or arrest may need specialist legal help for your part of the UK.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Police powers and interview procedures vary across England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. If you feel unsafe, pressured, or unsure of your status, prioritise verification through a trusted police contact route, an official meeting setting, and independent legal advice before any formal interview.
