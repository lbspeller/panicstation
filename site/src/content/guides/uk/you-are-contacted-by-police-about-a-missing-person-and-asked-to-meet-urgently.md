# What to do if… you are contacted by police about a missing person and asked to meet urgently (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the caller’s identity by hanging up and calling 101 from a different phone or after waiting five minutes. Do not agree to an immediate meeting in a private or unknown location; request that any meeting takes place at a police station and clarify if you are a witness or a suspect.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attend a meeting at a private residence, car park, or any location that is not a verified police station.
- Don’t provide bank details, passwords, one-time codes, or make any payments (genuine police never ask for money or "safe accounts").
- Don’t feel pressured to answer complex questions or provide a timeline immediately if you are in shock or confused.
- Don’t guess or speculate about the missing person’s whereabouts, habits, or mental state.
- Don’t hand over your mobile phone or laptop for "cloning" or "searching" without first seeking legal advice.
- Don’t agree to provide DNA samples or fingerprints "just to rule yourself out" without a solicitor present.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the identity of the caller through an independent channel.**  
   **England / Wales / Scotland / NI:** Tell the caller you will call them back via the official 101 number to verify their identity. Hang up and wait at least five minutes (to ensure a fraudster hasn't kept the line open) or use a different phone / mobile to call **101**. Ask the operator to verify the officer’s name, collar number, and force.
2. **Record the specific details of the request and the officer.**  
   Write down the officer’s name, rank, collar number, station, and the incident reference number (CAD or Log number). Note exactly what they have asked you to do and where they want to meet.
3. **Ask the officer to clarify your legal status in the investigation.**  
   **England / Wales / NI:** Ask clearly, "Am I being treated as a witness or a suspect?" If they mention a "voluntary interview under caution" or "Caution Plus Three," you are being treated as a suspect and have the right to free legal advice.
   **Scotland:** Ask if you are a witness or if you are "under suspicion." Note that the police in Scotland can question witnesses, but suspects have specific rights under the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995.
4. **Insist that any meeting takes place at a police station.**  
   **UK-wide:** If the officer suggests meeting at your home or a "neutral" place, politely decline and ask to meet at a local station. This ensures the environment is recorded, official, and verifiable by the 101 operator.
5. **Request a solicitor if there is any mention of an interview under caution.**  
   **England / Wales / NI:** You are entitled to free, independent legal advice for any voluntary interview under caution. Tell the officer, "I am happy to cooperate, but I wish to have a solicitor present for the interview." Do not let them persuade you that it will "take longer" or "make you look guilty."
   **Scotland:** You have a right to a private consultation with a solicitor before and during any questioning if you are a suspect.
6. **Provide only immediate, factual safety information if the person is in danger.**  
   If you have information that could save the missing person's life right now (e.g., they mentioned self-harm, a specific dangerous location, or a medical crisis), provide these facts clearly and briefly. Stick to what you personally saw or heard.
7. **Arrange for an Appropriate Adult if you are vulnerable or under 18.**  
   **UK-wide:** If you have a mental health condition, learning disability, or are under 18, the police must ensure an "appropriate adult" is present for formal procedures. State this requirement clearly during the initial contact.
8. **Set a time for the meeting that allows you to prepare.**  
   Unless there is a life-threatening emergency, you do not have to meet "right this second." Agree on a time later in the day or tomorrow that allows you to contact a solicitor or a support person.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to provide a comprehensive life history of your relationship with the missing person right now.
- You don’t need to grant access to your social media accounts or private messages on the spot.
- You don’t need to search your house or property for the missing person’s items without a search warrant or legal advice.
- You don’t need to inform other friends or family members about the police contact unless specifically instructed to do so for safety reasons.

## Important reassurance
Being contacted by the police in a missing person case is common for anyone in the person's social or professional circle. It does not automatically mean you are in trouble or that the person has come to harm. Taking the time to verify the officer and secure legal advice is a standard procedure that professional officers will respect.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and identity verification for the initial hour of contact. It does not provide legal strategy for long-term investigations or criminal defense.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. Police procedures and legal rights differ between England/Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. Always prioritize verification through 101 and seek professional legal representation if asked to attend an interview under caution.
