# What to do if… an after-school program says pickup permissions must be updated immediately after a parent dies (UK)

## Short answer
Ask the setting to supervise your child while you provide a temporary, signed collection instruction and photo identification. Identify the adult with Parental Responsibility (PR) who can legally authorize this change today.

## Do not do these things
- Don't attempt to access or use the deceased parent's digital accounts to authorize a new collector.
- Don't attempt to remove the child from the premises without the manager's or Designated Safeguarding Lead's (DSL) approval.
- Don't send a third party to collect the child without first confirming the specific identification the setting requires.
- Don't hand over original legal documents such as Wills or Grant of Probate; provide photocopies or digital versions.
- Don't engage in prolonged disputes within the sight or hearing of the child.

## What to do now
1. **Request that the child is supervised in a quiet area while the administration is resolved.**  
   This ensures the child is safe and away from any administrative tension.
2. **Ask to speak with the manager or the Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) immediately.**  
   Identify the person with the authority to make an emergency safeguarding decision.
3. **Verify who currently holds Parental Responsibility (PR) for the child.**  
   A surviving parent named on the birth certificate (or via marriage) typically has PR and can authorize collection unless a specific Court Order (such as a Child Arrangements Order) states otherwise.
   - If you are not the parent but are a Testamentary Guardian, explain that your authority may have commenced upon the parent's death if no one else has PR.
4. **Provide a temporary, written collection instruction for today’s session.**  
   Ask if they will accept a signed note or an email from the person with PR that includes:
   - The child’s full name and date of birth.
   - The full name of the person collecting the child today.
   - A specific "collection password" if the setting uses them.
   - A contact number for the person with PR to verify the instruction.
5. **Ensure the person collecting the child has valid photo identification.**  
   The setting cannot release the child to an unknown adult without verifying their identity against the temporary instruction.
6. **Consult the local authority Children’s Social Care team if a safe adult cannot be verified.**  
   If the setting refuses release and no authorized adult is available, ask them to trigger their "Uncollected Child Procedure."
   - You can contact the local council's Multi-Agency Safeguarding Hub (MASH) for advice on emergency placement or collection.
   - Call 101 only if you need police assistance to mediate a non-violent dispute over legal custody.
7. **Ask for a written summary of the required permanent updates.**  
   Request an email listing exactly what documentation they need (e.g., birth certificate, death certificate, or court orders) so you can provide it tomorrow rather than during the crisis.

## What can wait
- You do not need to finalize the permanent "Approved Collection List" this afternoon.
- You do not need to provide a death certificate or Grant of Probate in the next few minutes to secure a safe handover.
- You do not need to resolve disputes regarding long-term custody or residency today.

## Important reassurance
Childcare settings are legally required to follow strict safeguarding protocols to prevent unauthorized collection. While their request may feel insensitive during bereavement, it is a standard safety measure designed to protect the child during a period of family instability. A temporary solution is almost always possible once a person with legal responsibility is identified.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate child collection. It does not cover long-term custody law or probate.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Requirements for evidence of Parental Responsibility vary between settings and local authorities. If a dispute over Parental Responsibility exists, consult a family law solicitor.
