# What to do if… you are told you must repay training costs when you try to leave (UK)

## Short answer
Avoid giving verbal or written agreement to any new dates immediately. Locate your original employment contract or written statement of particulars to verify the specific notice requirements and start-date rules that apply to your resignation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any "variation of contract" or "notice acknowledgement" documents under immediate pressure.
- Do not assume that an HR portal or staff handbook overrides the specific terms in your signed employment contract.
- Do not stop attending work before your calculated contractual last day without written agreement, as this may be treated as a breach of contract.
- Do not withdraw your original resignation or submit a second one, as this can reset the notice clock.
- Do not download or forward company data to personal accounts "for evidence," which may violate security policies.

## What to do now
1. **Locate and review your primary employment documents to verify the notice terms.** 
   - Gather your employment contract and your "written statement of employment particulars."
   - Check the section titled "Notice" or "Termination" for the specific number of weeks or months required.
   - Look for clauses regarding the "start date" of notice (e.g., "notice begins the day after receipt").
   - Identify if there is a "Payment in Lieu of Notice" (PILON) clause or a "Garden Leave" provision.

2. **Calculate your statutory minimum notice to ensure you have met the legal floor.** 
   - Note that in the UK, if you have been employed for more than one month, the statutory minimum notice an employee must give is 1 week.
   - Confirm if your contract requires more than this (contractual notice), as the higher of the two typically applies.

3. **Establish a clear timeline of the resignation events in writing.** 
   - **Record the exact date and time you submitted your resignation and the method used (e.g., email, hand-delivered letter).**
   - **Write down the specific last day you originally proposed based on your contract interpretation.**
   - **Note the exact time and person who informed you that the notice period had "changed."**

4. **Request the employer’s specific justification for the change in writing.** 
   - **Email HR or your manager to ask which specific clause in your contract or which specific policy they are relying on to justify the new date.**
   - **Ask them to clarify if they are asserting your original calculation was factually incorrect or if they are proposing a change to your existing terms.**

5. **Send a formal "record-setting" response to confirm your position.** 
   - **State clearly that you do not agree to a unilateral change to your notice period.**
   - **Quote the specific clause from your contract that supports your original end date.**
   - **Request a written response confirming your agreed final working day and final day of employment.**

6. **Clarify the status of pay and benefits if they are asking you to leave earlier than planned.** 
   - **Ask for written confirmation of whether you are being placed on Garden Leave or if they are triggering a PILON clause.**
   - **Verify that your final payment will include all accrued but untaken holiday pay up to your last day of employment.**

7. **Secure your personal employment records before your access is revoked.** 
   - **Save copies of your original contract, your resignation email, and all correspondence regarding the notice dispute.**
   - **Take screenshots of HR portal submission confirmations if available.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file an employment tribunal claim or contact the Fair Work Agency today.
- You do not need to finalize your handover notes until the end date is clarified in writing.
- You do not need to explain the dispute to your new employer until you have a confirmed final date.

## Important reassurance
Notice period disputes are frequently the result of administrative errors or "stale" contract templates that have not been updated. Reverting to the written word of the contract usually resolves the issue. Feeling anxious about a confrontation is a standard response to a sudden change in your exit plan.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for notice period disputes. It does not cover complex "fire and rehire" scenarios or specific collective bargaining agreements which may require specialized union or legal intervention.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. UK employment law, including the Employment Rights Act 1996 and updates through 2026, involves specific time limits for claims. If you are being pressured or threatened with withholding pay, contact Acas, your trade union, or an employment solicitor immediately.
