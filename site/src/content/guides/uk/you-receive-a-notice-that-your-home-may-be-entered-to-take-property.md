# What to do if… you receive a notice that your home may be entered to take property (UK)

## Short answer
Keep the notice, do not let anyone in while you are panicked, and verify the notice through an independent route before using any phone number on paperwork you do not trust. If someone is at the door now, speak through the closed door and ask them to show ID and proof of authority through a window or letterbox.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume a debt collector has the same powers as an enforcement agent, bailiff, sheriff officer, messenger-at-arms, Enforcement of Judgments Office officer, or police officer.
- Don’t open the door just because someone threatens entry or waves paperwork.
- Don’t argue on the doorstep, block the doorway, or physically intervene.
- Don’t sign a controlled goods agreement, payment plan, or any other document while panicked.
- Don’t hand over cash or card details until you have checked who the person is and what authority they have.
- Don’t hide, sell, transfer, or dispose of belongings to try to frustrate enforcement.
- Don’t ignore the notice because you think it must be fake; check it.

## What to do now
1. **Identify exactly what the notice says.**  
   Put the notice somewhere safe and write down the reference number, the name of the court or creditor, the enforcement company or public office, the person named, the date, and any deadline. Look for wording such as “Notice of Enforcement”, “warrant of control”, “writ of control”, “High Court Enforcement Officer”, “County Court bailiff”, “certificated enforcement agent”, “sheriff officer”, “messenger-at-arms”, “Enforcement of Judgments Office”, or “police warrant”.

2. **Work out which UK process it appears to be.**  
   In England and Wales, debt enforcement paperwork may refer to bailiffs or enforcement agents. In Scotland, it may refer to sheriff officers or messengers-at-arms, and the rules are different. In Northern Ireland, civil judgment enforcement may involve the Enforcement of Judgments Office. Do not rely on England and Wales bailiff rules if the notice is Scottish or Northern Irish; use the relevant public guidance and get local advice.

3. **Verify the notice using an independent route.**  
   Do not rely only on a phone number printed on a notice you are unsure about. Use independent contact details:
   - For a certificated enforcement agent in England and Wales, use GOV.UK’s bailiff identity guidance and its linked register.
   - For a High Court Enforcement Officer, use the HCEO Association directory linked from GOV.UK guidance.
   - For a county court bailiff, family court bailiff, civilian enforcement officer, warrant of control, or writ of control, contact the court named on the paperwork using an independent court-finder route.
   - For a police claim, call 101 to check it unless there is immediate danger, in which case call 999.
   - For a sheriff officer or messenger-at-arms in Scotland, ask to see their identity details and check Scottish public guidance or a Scottish advice service.
   - For Northern Ireland Enforcement of Judgments Office paperwork, use nidirect or Northern Ireland Courts and Tribunals Service information to check the route.

4. **If someone is at the door, keep the door closed while you check.**  
   Say: “I am verifying this with the court or issuing authority now. Please show your ID and authority through the window or letterbox.” Ask for photo ID, the company or authority they work for, the warrant, writ, or order details if relevant, and a breakdown of the amount claimed. Write down the time, name, company or office, reference number, and what they say.

5. **Use the England and Wales doorstep limits only if the paperwork is for bailiffs or enforcement agents there.**  
   You usually do not have to open your door or let them in. Bailiffs generally cannot enter by pushing past you, enter if only children under 16 or vulnerable people are present, enter between 9pm and 6am, or enter through anything except a door. There are limited exceptions where force may be used, such as some unpaid criminal fines, Income Tax, or Stamp Duty, usually as a last resort. If someone says an exception applies, keep the door closed if safe and verify it urgently with the court, creditor, police, or a debt advice charity.

6. **If children, a vulnerable person, or someone unwell is the only person present, say so through the closed door.**  
   Use a simple sentence: “Only children under 16 or a vulnerable person are present. I am getting advice now.” Then call a trusted adult, Citizens Advice, National Debtline, StepChange, the relevant court, or the relevant public authority.

7. **Gather proof and essentials without moving goods out of the way.**  
   Put together the notice, envelopes, court papers, receipts or finance agreements for items that belong to someone else, tenancy or mortgage papers, ID, essential medication, mobility aids, and anything needed for immediate health, disability, childcare, or work needs. In England and Wales, bailiffs generally cannot take basic essentials such as clothes, a cooker, or a fridge, and cannot take work tools and equipment that together are worth less than £1,350.

8. **Get same-day help if the notice is about debt enforcement.**  
   Contact Citizens Advice, National Debtline, StepChange, or a local advice service with the notice in front of you. Use this wording: “I have received a notice saying someone may enter my home to take property. I need help checking what it is and what I should do before any visit.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your whole repayment plan right now.
- You do not need to negotiate on the doorstep.
- You do not need to prove ownership of every item immediately; start by keeping obvious proof together.
- You do not need to decide whether to complain, apply to court, or dispute the debt until the notice and authority have been checked.
- You do not need to let anyone in just to “sort it out” while you are overwhelmed.

## Important reassurance
A notice like this is meant to make you act quickly, but you can still slow the moment down. Keeping the door closed, checking identity, and using independent advice are normal, reasonable steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: checking the notice, staying safe during any visit, and avoiding panic decisions. Later decisions about payment, disputes, court applications, complaints, Scottish diligence, or Northern Ireland enforcement may need specialist debt, legal, or local advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, professional, or debt advice. Rules and routes differ across the UK and depend on the exact paperwork, the type of debt or authority, and where you live. If you feel unsafe or threatened, move away from the door if you can and call 999.
