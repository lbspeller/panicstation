# What to do if… your new job start date is postponed after you have already resigned (UK)

## Short answer
Ask the new employer to confirm the revised start date and that the role is still offered to you in writing as soon as you can today. Then ask your current employer, also in writing, whether they will let you withdraw your resignation or extend your leaving date.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the new job is secure until you have written confirmation of the new start date and the offer status.
- Do not stop attending your current job early unless your current employer agrees; leaving without agreement may affect pay and can create breach-of-contract problems.
- Do not sign anything labelled variation, new contract, termination, or settlement agreement while panicking.
- Do not send angry or threatening messages. Keep everything calm and factual, because you may need a clear paper trail.
- Do not spend your final salary or holiday pay assuming your next pay will arrive on the original schedule.
- Do not give the new employer extra personal or financial details unless you are sure the request is genuine and connected to the job.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and collect the key documents.** Find your offer letter, contract or offer emails, the original start date, the postponement message, your resignation notice, and any messages about checks or onboarding. Save copies somewhere you control.

2. **Check whether the offer is conditional or unconditional.** Look for words such as conditional, subject to, references, right to work, DBS, medical, background checks, qualifications, or satisfactory checks. Note anything you are not sure has been completed.

3. **Ask the new employer for clear written confirmation.** Send one short message to HR or the hiring manager asking them to confirm:
   - the new proposed start date
   - whether the role is still offered to you
   - whether any offer conditions are still outstanding
   - whether pay, hours, location, job title, and reporting line are unchanged
   - whether there is any paid onboarding, training, or paperwork you can start earlier

4. **Ask your current employer if you can stay longer.** Put the request in writing today if possible. Ask whether they will agree to one of these options:
   - withdraw your resignation and continue as normal
   - extend your leaving date to a specific date
   - agree a short bridge arrangement they are willing to offer
   Your current employer does not have to agree, but asking quickly gives them the best chance to consider it.

5. **Confirm your current-job end details in writing.** Ask HR or payroll to confirm:
   - your last working day
   - whether you are expected to work all of your notice
   - how accrued holiday will be handled
   - when final pay will be paid
   - how final pay will be calculated
   - when and how you will receive your P45

6. **If an income gap is likely, check support promptly.** Check whether you can claim Universal Credit if your income will drop. Also check whether New Style Jobseeker’s Allowance may apply, using the rules for where you live. Keep the postponed start-date message, offer documents, and resignation documents handy.

7. **Get practical guidance before agreeing to changes or threats.** Contact Acas or Citizens Advice with the timeline and documents. Ask what the postponement may mean if you accepted the offer and resigned, and what to do before signing anything new.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to raise a formal dispute or bring any claim.
- You do not need to negotiate perfect long-term terms before you have the revised start date and offer status in writing.
- You do not need to make big financial decisions, such as moving home, taking a loan, or making major purchases, until the start date is clearer.
- You do not need to explain the whole situation to everyone at work; keep messages limited, factual, and written.

## Important reassurance
This is a frightening gap, but there are still practical levers available. The immediate aim is to get written certainty from the new employer, ask the current employer for a bridge, and protect your money position while you check your options.

## Scope note
This is first steps only, focused on stabilising the situation and avoiding irreversible mistakes. Later decisions may need specialist employment, benefits, debt, or legal help once you have the facts in writing.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, benefits, employment, therapeutic, or professional advice. Employment rights and benefit options can depend on your contract, whether the offer was conditional or unconditional, what was accepted, your work status, where you live, and your personal circumstances.
