# What to do if… your pay does not arrive on payday (UK)

## Short answer
Secure physical or digital proof of your hours and earnings immediately, then confirm the employer's formal insolvency status. If they are insolvent, obtain a "CN" case reference number to claim unpaid wages and holiday pay from the government’s Redundancy Payments Service.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for the next scheduled payday to see if "it sorts itself out" if the premises are locked or systems are offline.
- Do not sign any document that waives your right to outstanding pay or redundancy in exchange for a "quick settlement" from a former director.
- Do not return company property (laptops, keys, vehicles) without obtaining a dated, signed receipt or a photo of the handover.
- Do not provide your bank details or sensitive personal data to any "new payroll service" or contact without verifying their identity via the Insolvency Service or Companies House.
- Do not rely on verbal assurances from managers that "the money is coming"; only written communication carries weight in later claims.
- Do not delete work-related emails or messages that prove your hours, commissions, or the date the business closed.

## What to do now
1. **Create a comprehensive record of your outstanding earnings immediately.**
   - Write down your last physical day of work and the exact hours you are owed.
   - List any earned commission, overtime, or bonuses that have not appeared on a payslip.
   - Estimate your accrued but untaken holiday days for the current holiday year.

2. **Secure all available employment evidence before system access is revoked.**
   - Download or screenshot your employment contract, latest P60, and your last three months of payslips.
   - Save copies of rotas, timesheets, or "congratulations" emails regarding commission/bonus targets met.
   - Ensure these files are saved to a personal device or cloud account not controlled by the employer.

3. **Verify the employer’s formal status using official insolvency registers.**
   - Check the Companies House register to see if the status is "Active," "In Administration," or "In Liquidation."
   - Search the Individual Insolvency Register if your employer was a sole trader rather than a limited company.
   - Search the "Gazette" for formal insolvency notices which will list the appointed Insolvency Practitioner (IP).

4. **Obtain the "CN" and "LN" case reference numbers if the employer is insolvent.**
   - Contact the appointed Insolvency Practitioner or Official Receiver to request the CN number for your initial claim (RP1).
   - Ask for the timeline regarding the "LN" number, which is required later for claiming Statutory Notice Pay (RP2).
   - If no IP is listed but the business is gone, contact the Redundancy Payments Service (RPS) for guidance.

5. **Submit a claim via the GOV.UK Redundancy Payments Service for immediate arrears.**
   - Use the RP1 online form to claim for unpaid wages (up to 8 weeks), holiday pay, and statutory redundancy pay (if you have 2+ years of service).
   - Be aware that payments are capped at statutory limits (currently £719 per week for dismissals after April 6, 2025).
   - Ensure your claim for redundancy and wages is submitted within 6 months minus 1 day of your dismissal date.

6. **Start Acas Early Conciliation if the employer is not insolvent but is refusing to pay.**
   - Notify Acas that you intend to start a claim for "unlawful deduction from wages" if the business is still trading in any form.
   - Be mindful that the strict deadline for wage claims is 3 months minus 1 day from the date the pay was due.
   - Use the 12-week conciliation window (standard as of December 2025) to attempt to resolve the issue before a Tribunal.

7. **Apply for Universal Credit immediately to bridge the income gap.**
   - Do not wait for the final pay dispute to be resolved before applying for benefits, as payments are not backdated.
   - Report to the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) that your wages are "in dispute" or "delayed due to insolvency" to ensure your assessment is handled correctly.
   - Request a "New Claim Advance" if you require immediate funds for essentials while waiting for your first assessment period to end.

## What can wait
- You do not need to calculate the exact tax and National Insurance deductions yourself; the Redundancy Payments Service handles these calculations.
- You do not need to hire a solicitor to file an initial RPS claim or start Acas Early Conciliation.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to take a new job that offers lower pay; focus on securing the money already owed to you first.
- You do not need to initiate a formal "Protective Award" claim for lack of consultation today, though you should note if 20+ people were made redundant at once.

## Important reassurance
Sudden workplace closures are high-stress events, and the lack of information often causes more anxiety than the closure itself. The UK has a robust "safety net" through the Insolvency Service designed specifically for this scenario. Following the official "CN" claim process ensures you are on the same track as your colleagues and simplifies the recovery of your wages.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and evidence-gathering instructions only. It does not cover long-term Employment Tribunal litigation, complex TUPE transfers (where the business is sold), or claims for "enhanced" contractual redundancy.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Statutory limits on pay change annually in April. Deadlines for Employment Tribunals are extremely strict; if you miss a deadline, you may lose your right to claim. Always verify the specific status of your employer via the Insolvency Service or a qualified employment specialist.
