# What to do if… a colleague sends you aggressive or intimidating messages outside of working hours (UK)

## Short answer
Stop engaging for now, save the messages, and tell the right person at work so it can be handled through a documented route. If a message suggests immediate danger or a crime in progress, get to a safer place and call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply in anger or try to win the argument in writing.
- Do not delete messages or tidy up the chat history before saving evidence.
- Do not meet them alone to clear the air if you feel intimidated.
- Do not share screenshots widely at work or on social media.
- Do not keep using your personal number or social accounts for the argument if you can move contact back to work channels.
- Do not assume it will blow over if the contact is repeated, escalating, threatening, or linked to who you are.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Stop replying, mute the conversation, and turn on do not disturb for that contact. If there is an immediate threat of violence, a crime in progress, or someone is in immediate danger, call 999 first.
2. **Save the messages before changing settings.** Take screenshots showing the sender, dates, times, and full wording. If the platform allows it, export or save the conversation. Keep the originals if you can.
3. **Make a short factual log.** Write down when the messages arrived, what channel they used, whether it was your personal phone or work chat, whether anyone else saw it, and whether it affected your sleep, safety, or ability to work.
4. **Move away from personal channels.** After saving what you need, silence personal channels and keep any necessary contact to work email or work chat. If you need to block them on a personal account to feel safe, save the evidence first.
5. **Send one boundary message only if it feels safe.** For example: “I am not available outside working hours. Please keep messages work-related and respectful. We can address this during working time.” Then stop replying.
6. **Report it through a work route.** Contact your line manager, HR, another manager named in your policy, or a trade union representative if you have one. Say it is about aggressive out-of-hours messages and ask how it will be recorded and handled.
7. **Be clear if your manager is involved.** If the person sending the messages is your manager, or you do not feel safe raising it with your manager, go to HR, another appropriate manager, or the route named in your workplace policy.
8. **Use the grievance route if needed.** Many workplace problems are raised informally first, but a formal grievance may be appropriate if the messages are serious, repeated, discriminatory, threatening, or informal action has not worked. Follow your employer’s grievance policy and keep your account factual.
9. **Ask about being accompanied in formal meetings.** If a formal grievance meeting is arranged, ask in writing whether you can bring a companion. This is usually a work colleague, a certified workplace trade union representative, or an official employed by a trade union.
10. **Flag protected-characteristic links clearly.** If the aggression refers to something like age, sex, disability, race, religion or belief, sexual orientation, pregnancy and maternity, gender reassignment, or marriage and civil partnership, say this when reporting it and keep the messages.
11. **Use police routes for safety concerns.** If contact becomes persistent and unwanted, includes threats, or makes you fear for your safety, consider reporting it outside work as well. Use 999 for emergencies and 101 or online reporting for non-emergencies.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to resign, take legal action, forgive them, or confront them.
- You do not need to write a perfect statement now; saving the messages and making a short factual log is enough for the first step.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether the correct label is bullying, harassment, misconduct, or a grievance.
- You do not need to meet them face-to-face to prove the messages are serious.
- You can decide later whether to block them everywhere, after you have saved what you need and taken safety advice if needed.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaken, angry, or unable to sleep after aggressive out-of-hours messages is understandable. A calm pause, saved evidence, and a documented workplace route are sensible protective steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and reduce the chance of escalation. Later decisions about grievances, mediation, police reports, employment tribunal deadlines, role changes, or legal action may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999. For workplace guidance, consider ACAS, your union if you have one, HR, or an independent employment adviser.
