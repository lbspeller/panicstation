# What to do if… you feel throat tightness or hoarseness starting after an allergen exposure (UK)

## Short answer
Treat throat tightness or a new hoarse voice after allergen exposure as a medical emergency: **use your prescribed adrenaline (auto-injector or nasal spray) immediately and call 999**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if symptoms improve; throat and voice changes indicate a high-risk reaction.
- Do not rely on antihistamines, inhalers, or water to treat throat swelling.
- Do not stand up or walk around, even if you feel you need "fresh air," as this can cause a dangerous drop in blood pressure.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital.
- Do not go to a private area like a bathroom or bedroom to deal with the symptoms alone.
- Do not eat or drink anything to "test" your ability to swallow.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all further exposure to the trigger immediately.**
   - **Spit out** any remaining food or medicine; do not swallow.
   - If you were stung and the stinger is visible, **flick or scrape it out** instantly with a fingernail or bank card (do not squeeze).

2. **Administer your prescribed adrenaline now.**
   - **Use your auto-injector** (e.g., EpiPen, Jext) in the outer thigh or use your **intranasal spray** (e.g., EURneffy) as directed.
   - Do this **immediately** for any throat tightness, hoarseness, or difficulty swallowing after exposure.

3. **Call 999 and state "anaphylaxis" (ana-fill-ax-is).**
   - If alone, **use the adrenaline first**, then call.
   - Tell the operator what caused the reaction and that your throat or voice is affected.

4. **Adopt a safe physical position.**
   - **Lie flat** on your back and raise your legs if possible.
   - If breathing is very difficult, **sit up slightly** with your back supported, but do not stand.
   - If you are pregnant, lie on your **left side**.

5. **Monitor for improvement over the next 5 minutes.**
   - **Use a second dose** of adrenaline (in the other thigh or other nostril) if symptoms do not improve or if they worsen after 5 minutes.
   - Note the time each dose was administered to tell the paramedics.

6. **Prepare for the arrival of emergency services.**
   - **Unlock your front door** and keep your phone nearby.
   - If someone is with you, ask them to stay by your side and gather your medications or allergy plan.

7. **Observe for skin color changes.**
   - **Check for paleness or blueness**; if you have brown or black skin, this may be easiest to see on the palms of your hands or the soles of your feet.

## What can wait
- Identifying the exact ingredient or trigger (this can be done later via allergy testing).
- Notifying family, work, or school until you are under medical care.
- Worrying about whether the adrenaline was "strictly necessary" (it is always safer to use it if throat symptoms are present).
- Cleaning up the area where the exposure happened.

## Important reassurance
Feeling a sense of panic or "impending doom" is a common physical symptom of a severe allergic reaction. You are doing the right thing by taking these steps immediately; acting quickly with adrenaline is the standard, safe procedure for these symptoms.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the first few minutes of a suspected severe allergic reaction. It does not replace the need for professional emergency medical assessment.

## Important note
Always go to the hospital by ambulance after using adrenaline. Symptoms can sometimes return several hours later (a "biphasic reaction"), even if you feel completely better shortly after the injection or spray.
