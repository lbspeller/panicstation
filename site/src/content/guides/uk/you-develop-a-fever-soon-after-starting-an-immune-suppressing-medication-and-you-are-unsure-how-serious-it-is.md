# What to do if… you develop a fever soon after starting an immune-suppressing medication and you are unsure how serious it is (UK)

## Short answer
Treat any fever while on immune-suppressing medication as an urgent medical matter: call your specialist prescribing team, their 24-hour advice line, or NHS 111 immediately. If you have "Red Flag" signs such as mottled skin, new confusion, severe breathlessness, or a rash that does not fade under a glass, call 999 or go to A&E now.

## Do not do these things
- Don't wait to see if the fever goes away on its own; immune-suppressing drugs can allow infections to become serious very quickly.
- Don't use paracetamol or ibuprofen to mask the temperature without first seeking clinical advice, as this hides a key indicator of your condition.
- Don't stop taking your prescribed medication suddenly unless specifically told to do so by a doctor, as some drugs require a structured tapering process.
- Don't rely on a walk-in centre or a local pharmacy for this specific issue; you require triage that accounts for your immunosuppressed status.
- Don't drive yourself to the hospital if you feel faint, dizzy, or are shivering uncontrollably.

## What to do now
1. **Measure your temperature immediately and record the exact reading.**
   - Use a digital thermometer in the mouth or under the arm.
   - Write down the time, the number, and any other symptoms like shivering (rigors) or a sore throat.
   - If the reading is between 37.5°C and 38.0°C, repeat the check in one hour to see if it is rising.
2. **Call your specialist medical team or their designated 24-hour emergency line.**
   - Use the specific contact number provided when you started the medication (often found in your "Patient Alert Card" or treatment booklet).
   - If you cannot reach them, call NHS 111 and state clearly: "I am on immune-suppressing medication and have a fever."
3. **Check for emergency "Red Flag" symptoms that require 999.**
   - Call 999 immediately if you observe:
     - Mottled, bluish, or very pale skin.
     - A rash that does not fade when a glass is pressed against it.
     - New confusion, slurred speech, or extreme lethargy.
     - Severe breathlessness or rapid breathing.
     - Not passing urine for a whole day.
4. **Locate your medication "Alert Card" or recent prescription details.**
   - Find the exact name of the medicine, the dose, and the date you started it.
   - Check if you have been specifically warned about "neutropenic sepsis" (common with chemotherapy and some biologics).
5. **Prepare a summary of your recent medical history for the clinician.**
   - List any other medications you take, especially steroids like Prednisolone.
   - Note when you last had a blood test and if you were told your "white cell count" or "neutrophils" were low.
6. **If instructed to attend A&E, identify yourself as immunosuppressed at reception.**
   - Say: "I am immunosuppressed on [Medication Name] and I have a fever; I have been advised I may be at risk of sepsis."
   - This ensures you are triaged into the correct pathway for urgent blood tests and potential IV antibiotics.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the source of the infection (e.g., chest, urinary, or skin) before seeking help.
- You do not need to decide on long-term changes to your treatment plan today.
- You can delay notifying employers or non-essential contacts until you have completed your initial medical assessment.

## Important reassurance
It is expected and correct to feel concerned when a fever develops on these medications. Because these drugs change how your body responds to germs, medical teams prefer to assess you early—even if it turns out to be a minor viral issue—rather than wait until an infection is advanced.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions only. It does not replace the specific emergency protocols provided by your oncology, rheumatology, or transplant team.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional medical advice or diagnosis. If your condition is deteriorating or you feel you cannot wait for a callback, seek emergency care immediately via 999 or A&E.
