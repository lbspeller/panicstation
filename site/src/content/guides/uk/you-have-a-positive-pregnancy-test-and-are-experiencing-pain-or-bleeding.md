# What to do if… you have a positive pregnancy test and are experiencing pain or bleeding (UK)

## Short answer
Call 999 now if you are bleeding and have severe tummy or pelvic pain, shoulder pain, fainting, dizziness, feeling sick, heavy bleeding, or you feel very weak or pale. If symptoms are not at that level but you have any bleeding, new pain, one-sided pain, pain or pressure when you pee or poo, or you feel unwell, contact your maternity unit, Early Pregnancy Unit, GP, midwife, or NHS 111 today.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “wait and see” if pain is severe, worsening, one-sided, or you feel faint, dizzy, very weak, pale, or very unwell.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel faint or dizzy, have severe pain, or are bleeding heavily; call 999 or ask someone else to take you if you have been told to go in.
- Do not take ibuprofen or other NSAIDs unless a doctor, pharmacist, or midwife has specifically advised it in pregnancy. If you already took a dose, do not panic; tell the clinician or pharmacist.
- Do not ignore shoulder-tip pain, collapse, sudden severe tummy or pelvic pain, or bleeding that soaks a period pad soon after putting it on.
- Do not drink alcohol or take recreational drugs to cope with shock or pain.
- Do not stop prescribed medicines abruptly unless a clinician tells you to; bring the medicine or a list and ask.

## What to do now
1. Choose the safest urgent-care route based on what is happening:
   - Call 999 now if you are pregnant or might be pregnant and have bleeding with severe tummy or pelvic pain, shoulder pain, feeling sick, fainting, dizziness, loss of consciousness, heavy bleeding, or looking very pale or weak.
   - If you are not in a 999 situation but have any vaginal bleeding, new or increasing cramps or pain, one-sided pain, diarrhoea, pain or pressure when you pee or poo, or you are worried something is not right, contact your maternity unit if you have the number.
   - If you are less than 20 weeks pregnant, ask how to contact your local Early Pregnancy Unit or Early Pregnancy Assessment Service.
   - If you do not have a maternity unit number, cannot contact an early pregnancy unit, or are unsure what to do, call NHS 111. You can also contact your GP or midwife if they are reachable now.
2. Say clearly at the start of the call: “I have a positive pregnancy test and I have concerning symptoms.” Then name the main symptom first, such as heavy bleeding, one-sided pain, shoulder pain, dizziness, fainting, feeling sick, diarrhoea, pain when peeing, or worsening cramps.
3. If you are bleeding, use a period pad if you can, so you can track how much blood there is and describe it clearly.
4. Write a one-minute symptom note for triage: when symptoms started, where the pain is, whether it is one-sided or central, pain severity from 0 to 10, whether it is getting worse, bleeding amount, any clots or tissue, dizziness, fainting, shoulder-tip pain, sickness, diarrhoea, pain or pressure when peeing or pooing, and the date of your last normal period if you know it.
5. Gather only what helps urgent care: the pregnancy test or a photo, medicines and supplements with doses, allergies, your NHS number if you have it, maternity notes if you already have them, your phone, and a charger. Bring someone with you if possible.
6. For pain while arranging care, paracetamol is usually the first-choice painkiller in pregnancy when used as directed on the packet or leaflet. If pain is severe, one-sided, sudden, or worsening, do not rely on painkillers; get urgent medical help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide anything today about the pregnancy beyond getting assessed for your symptoms.
- You do not need to book antenatal care, calculate dates perfectly, or find every previous health detail right now.
- You do not need to work out the cause yourself. Bleeding or pain may have several causes, including miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, infection, or something less serious, and this needs clinical assessment.
- You can deal with messages, work, and family explanations later. “I’m having urgent symptoms and getting checked” is enough.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel panicked or numb when symptoms start after a positive pregnancy test. Bleeding and pain in early pregnancy do not always mean something serious is happening, but some warning signs need urgent assessment, and getting checked is the safest next step.

## Scope note
This is first steps only, focused on immediate safety and getting the right level of urgent care. Follow-up plans and longer-term decisions can come after you have been assessed, and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, or a substitute for professional care. If you feel seriously unwell, your symptoms are severe or worsening, or you are worried about your safety, seek urgent or emergency help.
