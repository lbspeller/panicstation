# What to do if… a process server tries to hand you papers at your home or workplace and you are unsure what they are (UK)

## Short answer
Accept the documents without argument, record the exact date and time of receipt, and then verify the claim’s legitimacy via official court channels before the standard 14-day response deadline.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hide, discard, or ignore the documents once they have been handed to you or left in your presence.
- Do not pay any money or provide bank details to the person delivering the papers.
- Do not engage in an argument or attempt to explain your side of the case to the server.
- Do not sign any document if you do not understand its purpose or if you feel pressured.
- Do not let the person pressure you into leaving your property or entering a vehicle.
- Do not assume that refusing to touch the papers stops the legal process from moving forward.
- Do not post images of the documents on social media, as they contain sensitive personal and case data.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize a calm and brief interaction.**  
   **Maintain physical distance and speak through a door or window if you feel uncomfortable.** If at your workplace, request to move the interaction to a private room or have the person wait in reception while you notify a manager or HR representative.
2. **Request basic identifying information and then stop talking.**  
   **Ask the server for the name of the documents and the specific court they originated from.** You are not required to discuss the merits of the case or confirm any details other than your identity if asked.
3. **Accept the papers if they are offered.**  
   **Take the documents to ensure you have a complete record of the claim and any associated deadlines.** In England and Wales, if a server identifies you and tells you what the papers are, service can be deemed "effective" even if you refuse to physically hold them; taking them simply ensures you are not operating without the necessary information.
4. **Record the precise details of the service immediately.**  
   **Note the date, time, and exact location where the documents were handed to you.** 
   - Write down a physical description of the person who served you.
   - Keep the original envelope and any staples or attachments exactly as they were delivered.
5. **Perform a visual legitimacy check of the first page.**  
   **Look for a claim number, a court name, and the "Court Seal," which is typically a round stamp often featuring a crown.** Check for clear headings such as "Claim Form," "Notice of Proceedings," "Statutory Demand," or "Injunction."
6. **Verify the claim independently through official channels.**  
   **Contact the court named on the papers using a phone number found on the official GOV.UK website, not a number printed on the cover letter.** 
   - For money claims, you can call the Civil National Business Centre on 0300 123 1056 to verify if a claim number is genuine.
   - If the claim provides a "Money Claim Online" (MCOL) login, use the official government portal to check the status.
7. **Identify the first response deadline.**  
   **Locate the section regarding "Acknowledging Service" or "Responding to the Claim."** In most civil cases, you have 14 days from the date of service to acknowledge the claim; missing this can result in a "Default Judgment" (CCJ) being registered against you.
8. **Manage your privacy at your place of employment.**  
   **If served at work, inform your manager or HR only that you have received personal legal correspondence and require a short period to review it.** You are not legally required to share the contents of the lawsuit or the nature of the dispute with your employer unless it directly affects your professional regulatory standing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately if you agree with the claim or intend to defend it.
- You do not need to contact the claimant or their solicitor on the same day you are served.
- You do not need to provide a witness statement or gather evidence in the first few hours.
- You do not need to justify the situation to colleagues, friends, or family members.

## Important reassurance
Receiving legal papers is a procedural step and does not mean you have already lost a case or are in criminal trouble. It is normal to feel a sudden spike in stress or embarrassment, particularly if served in public or at work. By accepting the papers and noting the time, you have taken the most important step toward protecting your legal rights and preventing a surprise judgment.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate first steps for stabilization and verification of service in a civil context. It does not provide legal strategy or advice on how to defend a claim. Procedures in Scotland and Northern Ireland may vary significantly from those in England and Wales.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. If you have been served with court papers, you should seek professional advice from a solicitor, the Citizens Advice Bureau, or a regulated debt advisor as soon as possible, as legal deadlines are strictly enforced by the courts.
