# What to do if… you are asked to share your work login details or approve a multi-factor prompt you did not initiate (UK)

## Short answer
Do not provide substantive information or documents immediately. Request the inquiry in writing, verify the individual's credentials via official regulatory registers, and consult your union or an independent solicitor before responding.

## Do not do these things
- Do not forward, download, or screenshot work emails or documents to share with a third party without explicit internal authorization.
- Do not attempt to guess, speculate, or "fill in the blanks" if you are unsure of specific facts or dates.
- Do not delete, modify, or move digital files or messages after receiving contact, as this may be flagged as interference with an investigation.
- Do not sign any summary of "facts" or witness statements during an initial call or meeting without a full review period.
- Do not use work-issued devices or email accounts to seek private legal advice or communicate with your union.
- Do not record conversations or meetings secretly, as this may violate workplace policies or data protection laws.

## What to do now
1. **Request the full details of the inquiry in writing.** You should politely decline to answer questions over the phone and ask the person to email or mail their request. Ask for:
   - their full name and the firm or consultancy they represent
   - who has instructed them (e.g., your employer, a former colleague, an insurer, or a regulator)
   - the specific purpose of the contact (e.g., witness statement, document request, or formal interview)
   - the legal basis for the request and any associated deadlines
2. **Verify the individual's identity using official UK registers.** Do not rely on email signatures or provided phone numbers.
   - For solicitors, check the **Solicitors Regulation Authority (SRA) Solicitors Register**.
   - For barristers, check the **Bar Standards Board (BSB) Barristers' Register**.
   - For private investigators, verify if they are members of a professional body like the Association of British Investigators (ABI).
3. **Establish if you are required to notify your employer.**
   - If the investigator claims to be acting for your employer, contact your HR or Legal department through official internal channels to confirm they have authorized the contact.
   - If the contact is from a third party (like a claimant's lawyer) and they ask you to keep the contact secret, pause. You may have a contractual duty to inform your employer, but you should seek independent advice (union or solicitor) before doing so if you fear retaliation.
4. **Isolate all requested data and documents.** If you are asked for files, customer data, or internal correspondence, do not send them yourself. 
   - Inform the investigator that data disclosures must be handled by your organisation's Data Protection Officer (DPO) or Legal team to ensure compliance with the Data Protection Act 2018.
5. **Keep a neutral log of all interactions.** Record the date, time, method of contact, and a summary of what was asked and what you said. 
   - Do not include confidential work information in your personal notes; keep them focused on the procedural aspects of the contact.
6. **Determine your right to support for any meetings.**
   - **For disciplinary or grievance hearings:** You have a statutory right to be accompanied by a colleague or union representative.
   - **For investigation meetings:** There is no automatic statutory right, but you should check your employment contract and staff handbook. 
   - **For disabled employees:** You may have a legal right to accompaniment as a "reasonable adjustment" under the Equality Act 2010. 
7. **Address potential whistleblowing protections.** If the inquiry relates to wrongdoing (e.g., fraud, safety risks, or sexual harassment), recognize that as of April 2026, sexual harassment disclosures are formally protected under the updated whistleblowing framework.
   - Contact a specialist service like **Protect** (the whistleblowing charity) before making a disclosure to ensure you maintain your legal protections.

## What can wait
- You do not need to agree to an interview or a deadline during the first minute of contact.
- You do not need to decide if you are "pro-employer" or "pro-claimant"; you are only required to be factual and procedurally safe.
- You do not need to interpret complex legal concepts like "legal professional privilege" or "public interest" without professional assistance.

## Important reassurance
It is standard professional practice to ask for time to verify an investigator and consult with a representative. Taking a pause to ensure you do not accidentally breach data laws or your employment contract is a responsible action, not a sign of guilt or obstruction.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and procedural safety. It does not replace legal advice from a qualified employment solicitor or the formal guidance provided by a trade union.

## Important note
Workplace investigations can have significant implications for your employment status and personal liability under data protection laws. If you feel pressured, or if the investigation involves potential criminal activity or high-stakes litigation, you should secure independent legal representation immediately. Information shared with an investigator may be used in court or tribunal proceedings.
