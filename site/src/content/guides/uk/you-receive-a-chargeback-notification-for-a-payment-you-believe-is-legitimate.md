# What to do if… you receive a chargeback notification for a payment you believe is legitimate (UK)

## Short answer
Confirm the notice inside your normal payment provider or acquirer portal, note the response deadline and dispute reason, then decide whether to accept it or send a focused evidence response.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore it; missing the response deadline may mean you lose the chance to respond.
- Do not click links in the email if anything feels unusual; log in through your normal bookmark, app, or saved dashboard instead.
- Do not contact the customer angrily, threaten them, or post about them publicly.
- Do not upload a large, messy bundle of screenshots; irrelevant or unreadable evidence can weaken the response.
- Do not issue a refund and also challenge the chargeback unless your provider says that is appropriate for the exact stage of the case.
- Do not create new intrusive checks about the customer in a panic; use existing order, payment, fulfilment, and account records you already hold.

## What to do now
1. **Open the case in your provider or acquirer portal.** Check it appears there before you treat the email as genuine.

2. **Write down the key case details in one place:**
   - the dispute reason or category,
   - the response deadline,
   - the amount and any fee,
   - the transaction ID and order reference,
   - the current stage of the dispute.

3. **Decide whether you are accepting or challenging before collecting documents.**
   - Accept it if you cannot answer the stated reason with clear evidence, or if the time and cost are not worth it.
   - Challenge it if you can show, with specific records, why the stated dispute reason is wrong.

4. **Gather only evidence that matches the reason given.**
   - For **unauthorised or fraud** claims: use existing records such as previous successful transactions, account history, delivery to a known address, digital access logs, or other order records that show the cardholder or customer likely took part or benefited.
   - For **item not received** claims: use dispatch records, carrier tracking, delivery confirmation, the delivery address used, and any delivery messages.
   - For **not as described, cancelled, or refund expected** claims: use the checkout description, order confirmation, relevant cancellation or returns terms shown at purchase, customer messages, and any refund record.
   - For all cases: include the order or invoice, date, amount, item or service details, and the clearest customer communications.

5. **Write a short, factual rebuttal summary.**
   - Say that you are challenging the chargeback for the stated reason.
   - List the main evidence in 3–6 bullets.
   - Use a simple timeline: purchase, fulfilment or access, customer contact, dispute notice.

6. **Submit through the provider or acquirer portal before the deadline.** Save the confirmation page, the time submitted, and a copy of exactly what you uploaded.

7. **Check for a wider problem without expanding the task.**
   - Look for other disputes linked to the same email, address, device, product, or order pattern.
   - Check recent admin logins and payout or bank-detail changes in your payment dashboard, because chargeback emails are sometimes used as phishing hooks.

8. **If you suspect fraud beyond a normal card dispute, use the right reporting route.**
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, you can report fraud or cyber crime to Report Fraud online or by calling 0300 123 2040.
   - In Scotland, use Police Scotland’s scams and frauds advice and non-emergency reporting routes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to take legal action, use debt collection, or change your whole checkout process.
- You do not need a long argument with the customer right now; the provider or acquirer deadline comes first.
- You do not need perfect evidence; you need relevant evidence that directly answers the dispute reason.

## Important reassurance
A chargeback notice can feel personal, especially when you believe the payment was legitimate. It is usually a structured card-dispute process, and the safest first response is calm, organised, and deadline-focused.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and prevent avoidable mistakes. Frequent, high-value, or complex disputes may need help from your acquirer, payment provider, accountant, solicitor, or another qualified adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, accounting, or professional advice. Chargeback rules, fees, evidence requirements, and deadlines vary by card scheme, acquirer, payment provider, and dispute reason, so follow the instructions shown in your actual case.
