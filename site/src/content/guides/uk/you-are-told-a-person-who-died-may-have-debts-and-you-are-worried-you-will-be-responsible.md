# What to do if… you are told a person who died may have debts and you are worried you will be responsible (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before paying or agreeing to anything. Most debts are usually dealt with from the person’s estate, but you may be personally responsible if you were a joint borrower, signed a guarantee, or are handling the estate and distribute assets before debts are dealt with.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay a creditor “just to stop the calls” before checking whether you personally owe the debt.
- Do not say in writing, by message, or on a recorded call that you “accept responsibility” or “will clear it” unless you are sure you are liable.
- Do not use your own money for the deceased person’s bills and assume you will definitely be repaid later.
- Do not give away, sell, or distribute assets that belonged to the deceased person in their sole name until funeral costs, debts, and taxes have been checked.
- Do not ignore official letters, but do not treat a phone call as proof that the debt is real or that you owe it.
- Do not let a creditor rush you into applying for probate, letters of administration, or confirmation before you understand your role.

## What to do now
1. Work out your role in the simplest possible way:
   - You are just a relative, partner, friend, or next of kin.
   - You are named as an executor in a will.
   - You are applying, or may apply, to deal with the estate.
   This matters because dealing with the estate is different from personally owing the debt.

2. Check the common reasons you might personally owe something:
   - Your name is on a joint loan, credit card, overdraft, mortgage, hire purchase agreement, utility bill, or tenancy.
   - You signed a guarantee.
   - You are still living in a property where ongoing bills, rent, mortgage payments, council tax, rates, energy, or water charges may now continue in your name.
   Ask the creditor for written evidence showing why they say you are responsible.

3. Move contact to writing:
   - Ask for the creditor name, account reference, amount claimed, copy statements if available, and the legal reason they say you personally owe it.
   - If you are not the executor or person dealing with the estate, say you are not authorised to deal with the estate.
   - Keep a note of calls, letters, emails, names, dates, and reference numbers.

4. If you are handling, or may handle, the estate, make a rough list before paying anyone:
   - Known money and assets, such as bank accounts, property, vehicles, valuables, refunds, and insurance.
   - Known debts, such as credit cards, loans, overdrafts, rent, mortgage, council tax or rates, utilities, tax, benefit overpayments, and funeral costs.
   - Any jointly held accounts or assets, because these may not all be treated in the same way.
   If debts may be more than assets, treat the estate as possibly insolvent and get specialist advice before paying creditors or distributing anything.

5. Use notification services to reduce repeat contact:
   - Use Tell Us Once if it is available where the person lived and you have the information needed after the death is registered.
   - Use the Death Notification Service if the deceased person had accounts with participating banks, building societies, or financial firms.
   - Contact any non-participating bank, lender, landlord, utility company, council, or rates office separately.

6. Before distributing any money or belongings, check the local estate rules:
   - In England and Wales, personal representatives can place a deceased estates notice, often called a Section 27 notice, and should not distribute until the notice period has passed.
   - In Scotland or Northern Ireland, the process and timing can be different, so get local advice before distributing if there may be unknown debts.
   - If you distribute too early and the estate cannot later pay a valid debt, you may create personal risk.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether every debt is valid, whether the estate is solvent, or whether you will take on the formal estate role. You do not need to answer repeated phone calls immediately, and you do not need to apply for probate, letters of administration, or confirmation just because a creditor is pushing.

## Important reassurance
It is common to fear that you automatically inherit a loved one’s debts, especially when calls or letters sound urgent. In many cases, the first safe step is simply to stop, ask for written proof, and avoid using your own money until your role and any personal liability are clear.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if the estate may be insolvent, there are joint or guaranteed debts, property is involved, creditors are threatening action, or you are unsure whether to act as executor or personal representative.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, debt, tax, or professional advice. Debt responsibility depends on the exact contract, the deceased person’s estate, where in the UK the estate is being dealt with, and your role.
