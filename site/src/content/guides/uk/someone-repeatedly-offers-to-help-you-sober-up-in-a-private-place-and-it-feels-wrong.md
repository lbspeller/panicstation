# What to do if… someone repeatedly offers to help you sober up in a private place and it feels wrong (UK)

## Short answer
Trust the feeling and do not go anywhere private with them. Move to a staffed, public place and tell staff, security, or a safe person: “I do not feel safe with that person. Please help me stay away from them.”

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to a private room, car, quiet street, toilet area, or “somewhere to lie down” with them.
- Do not accept a drink, water, vape, gum, tablets, “vitamins,” or anything else from that person or anything left unattended.
- Do not let them hold your phone, bag, keys, coat, drink, or transport details “to help you.”
- Do not spend time arguing, proving they are unsafe, or explaining why you are leaving.
- Do not split from the person or group that feels safest just to avoid making a scene.
- Do not rely on a self-testing kit if you think you may have been spiked; get medical or police advice instead.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a staffed, public place.** Go to the bar, reception, cloakroom, security desk, shop counter, hotel lobby, or another bright, busy place. Put other people and staff between you and the person.

2. **Tell staff or security plainly.** Say: “I do not feel safe with that person. They keep trying to get me somewhere private. Please keep them away from me.” If you can, point them out or describe them.

3. **Use Ask for Angela only if it helps.** In a participating venue, asking staff for “Angela” can discreetly signal that you need help. If staff look unsure, use the plain sentence above.

4. **Make a safe exit through staff or a trusted person.** Ask staff to call a licensed taxi, walk you to your transport, let you wait in a staff area, or help you contact someone you trust. If you are already outside, go into the nearest staffed place and ask for help there.

5. **Contact one safe person with exact information.** Text or call: “I feel unsafe. I am at [venue/address]. Please stay on the phone, come here, book me a taxi, or track my journey.” Share your live location if you can do that safely.

6. **Keep control of your phone, keys, bag, and drink.** Put them close to your body. If the person has any of them, ask staff or security to help you get them back instead of dealing with the person alone.

7. **Get urgent help if danger or illness is immediate.** Call 999 if you are in immediate danger, someone is threatening you, or you feel very unwell. For non-emergency police help, call 101. If you feel suddenly confused, unusually drowsy, sick, unable to stay alert, or much more intoxicated than expected, ask staff or a safe person to help you get medical advice through NHS 111 or the local urgent medical route for your part of the UK.

8. **Use specialist support without deciding about reporting.** If something sexual happened, might have happened, or you are not sure, you can contact a sexual assault support service or a sexual assault referral centre for confidential support. You do not have to decide about police reporting before asking for help.

9. **Only if it is safe and manageable, preserve simple evidence.** If you may want medical, forensic, or police options later, try not to delete messages, wash, change clothes, or throw items away until you have had advice. Your immediate safety matters more than preserving anything.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this “counts” as spiking, coercion, assault, or attempted assault.
- You do not need to work out their intentions.
- You do not need to confront them, warn everyone, gather proof, or explain yourself.
- You do not need to make a police report tonight unless you want to or you are in immediate danger.
- You do not need to make any long-term decisions while you are scared, unwell, intoxicated, or alone.

## Important reassurance
Feeling wrong is enough reason to leave. Freezing, going quiet, smiling to keep someone calm, or struggling to say no are common stress responses. Choosing safety over politeness is allowed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you get to a safer place and reach support. Later decisions about medical care, reporting, evidence, work, school, or housing can be made after the immediate situation has passed and you have support.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or feel very unwell, seek urgent help right away.
