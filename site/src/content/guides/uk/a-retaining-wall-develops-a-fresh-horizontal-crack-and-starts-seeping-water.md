# What to do if… a retaining wall develops a fresh horizontal crack and starts seeping water (UK)

## Short answer
Treat a fresh horizontal crack with water seepage as a possible collapse risk. Keep people away from the wall and the areas above and below it, stop any extra water feeding it if you can do that safely, and contact a structural engineer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stand next to the wall, below it, or on the ground immediately above it to inspect it closely.
- Do not let children, pets, visitors, or neighbours walk near the wall or below it.
- Do not seal the crack with filler, foam, mortar, or waterproofing as a first move; this can hide movement and may trap water pressure.
- Do not dig behind or above the wall to see what is happening.
- Do not park a vehicle, place a skip, stack slabs, store heavy items, or add other weight above the wall.
- Do not direct more water towards the wall with a hose, pressure washer, sprinkler, diverted downpipe, or garden run-off.
- Do not assume it is safe because it is “only a garden wall” if it is holding back soil at different levels.

## What to do now
1. **Make a no-go zone.** Keep everyone away from:
   - the full length of the wall
   - the area below the wall, where it could fall
   - the ground above the wall, which could slump if the wall gives way

2. **Check for urgent warning signs from a safe distance.** Treat the situation as urgent if you see or hear any of these:
   - the wall is leaning, bowing, bulging, or sections look displaced
   - the crack is widening or spreading
   - there are new cracking, grinding, or popping sounds
   - soil is slumping, sinking, or opening above the wall
   - water flow is increasing or carrying soil or sand

3. **Escalate if people or public areas could be at risk.**
   - If collapse seems possible soon and people could be harmed, call **999**.
   - If the wall could affect a public footpath, road, highway, neighbouring property, or shared access, contact your local authority’s dangerous structure route as soon as you can.
   - In England and Wales, use the GOV.UK dangerous building or structure route to find the relevant council.
   - In Scotland, contact your local authority building standards service.
   - In Northern Ireland, contact your local council building control service.

4. **Reduce water feeding the wall only from a safe position.**
   - Turn off sprinklers, hoses, irrigation, and outdoor taps feeding the area.
   - If a downpipe, drain, or garden run-off is sending water towards the wall, divert it away only if you can do that without going near the wall or the ground above it.
   - If water is flowing constantly even in dry weather, consider turning off your internal stop tap and checking whether the seepage slows.

5. **Avoid anything that increases pressure behind the wall.**
   - Do not dig relief holes or remove soil yourself.
   - If you can reach a gully or channel drain safely without going near the wall, clear obvious leaves or debris so water does not pool above it.
   - Keep vehicles, deliveries, bins, stored materials, and foot traffic away from the retained ground.

6. **Record the situation quickly and safely.**
   - Take wide photos or a short video from a safe distance.
   - Include something fixed for scale, such as a fence post, paving edge, or wall end.
   - Note the time, recent weather, whether water is clear or muddy, and whether the crack seems to be changing.

7. **Contact the right help.**
   - Contact a chartered structural engineer or structural engineering practice and say: “fresh horizontal crack in a retaining wall with water seepage.”
   - Contact your buildings insurer and ask what emergency stabilisation or inspection steps they want recorded before any repair work.
   - If you smell gas or suspect gas pipe damage, call the National Gas Emergency Service on **0800 111 999**.
   - If electricity equipment, cables, or overhead lines may be damaged or at risk, stay away. In England, Scotland and Wales, report it through your electricity network operator or call **105**; in Northern Ireland, use the Northern Ireland power emergency route.
   - If you suspect a water main or sewer problem outside your boundary, contact your water company.

8. **Tell affected neighbours in plain terms.**
   - Say there is a new crack and water seepage, you are keeping people away, and you are contacting an engineer, insurer, or the local authority as needed.
   - Do not argue about boundary responsibility while the wall may still be unsafe.

## What can wait
- Choosing a permanent repair design.
- Comparing contractors or accepting quotes.
- Deciding who is legally or financially responsible.
- Cosmetic patching, repainting, pressure washing, or tidying the wall.
- Planning long-term drainage upgrades, unless a structural engineer or local authority tells you an immediate drainage step is needed.

## Important reassurance
Taking this seriously is sensible, not overreacting. The useful first steps are simple: keep people out of the possible collapse path, stop adding water or weight, and get suitable help to assess the wall.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and buy time. Later decisions about propping, drainage relief, repair, removal, insurance, or responsibility may need a structural engineer, your insurer, the local authority, or legal advice.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not engineering, legal, financial, insurance, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you think people could be harmed or a public area could be affected, prioritise keeping people away and contacting emergency or local authority help.
