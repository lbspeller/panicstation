# What to do if… you receive an alert that your account security or recovery details have been changed (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible account takeover. Go to the service by typing the address yourself or using the official app, not the alert link, then secure the account by changing the password, ending other sessions, and restoring your recovery options.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click buttons or links in the alert if you are even slightly unsure it is genuine.
- Don’t keep trying passwords again and again if you are being locked out; use the provider’s official recovery route instead.
- Don’t keep using the same device if you think it may be infected; use a different trusted device if you can.
- Don’t assume it is fine because you can still sign in; someone else may still have an active session.
- Don’t pay anyone offering account recovery through direct messages, adverts, search results, or cold calls.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the alert without using its links.**  
   Open the service in the way you normally trust: a typed web address, a saved bookmark you already use, or the official app. Look for account settings such as **Security**, **Recent activity**, **Devices**, **Recovery**, or **Sign-in methods**.

2. **If you can still sign in, block ongoing access first.**
   - Change the password to a new, unique password you have not used anywhere else.
   - Use the provider’s option to sign out of other devices, end sessions, or log out apps.
   - Review signed-in devices and remove anything you do not recognise.

3. **Restore your recovery details.**
   - Re-add your backup email, recovery email, trusted phone number, or trusted devices if they were removed.
   - Remove any recovery email, phone number, device, or security method that is not yours.
   - Check whether two-step verification or multi-factor authentication was changed; turn it back on if it was removed.

4. **Check for quiet settings that let someone stay in control.**  
   In the affected account, especially if it is an email account, look for anything you did not set up:
   - mail forwarding or redirect addresses
   - rules or filters that delete, archive, hide, or forward messages
   - connected apps, app passwords, browser extensions, or third-party access

5. **Secure the account that can reset your other accounts.**  
   If the affected account is your email account, prioritise it before other services because it may be used to reset passwords elsewhere. After it is secured, check your most important linked accounts, such as banking, shopping, cloud storage, social media, and work accounts.

6. **If your mobile number may be involved, contact your mobile network.**  
   If you see signs of a SIM swap, lost signal, unexpected account changes, or call or text forwarding you did not set up, contact your mobile network provider using its official website, app, or phone number and ask them to check for recent changes.

7. **If you cannot sign in, use the provider’s official recovery process.**  
   Use the recovery page or support route inside the official website or app. If you still have one signed-in device, look for options such as **This wasn’t me**, **Secure your account**, **Account recovery**, or **Review security events**.

8. **Save basic evidence while it is visible.**  
   Take screenshots of the alert and any recent security events showing dates, devices, locations, or changed recovery details. Save them somewhere safe that does not rely only on the affected account.

9. **If the alert may be phishing, report it and do not engage further.**
   - Forward suspicious emails to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.
   - Forward suspicious text messages to **7726**.
   If you clicked, replied, entered details, lost money, or were locked out, continue securing the account and report the incident through official channels.

10. **If money, benefits, work systems, or identity details may be at risk, escalate.**
   - Contact your bank, payment provider, employer, or service provider using an official number or app, especially if new payees, diverted invoices, purchases, or benefit changes may be involved.
   - For fraud or cyber crime reporting in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use **Report Fraud** online. If you are in Scotland, report via **Police Scotland on 101**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly how this happened right now.
- You do not need to message everyone immediately; first stop ongoing access and check for forwarding or rules.
- You do not need to delete the account or wipe your devices in a panic.
- You can review password managers, device updates, and longer-term clean-up after the account is stable.

## Important reassurance
This kind of alert can feel like losing control, but the first steps are clear: use the official route, end other sessions, restore recovery details, and check for hidden forwarding or access. Taking those steps in order can reduce the chance of further harm.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, intended to stabilise the account and prevent immediate damage. Later decisions may need help from the account provider, your bank, your employer, your mobile network, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. Account screens and recovery options differ between providers, so follow the official process for the service you use and avoid third-party recovery offers.
