# What to do if… your voicemail PIN or greeting has changed and you did not update it (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible mobile account takeover. Secure your mobile network account first; if you have lost signal, cannot make calls or texts, or have a SIM, PAC, or porting warning you did not request, contact your network urgently from another phone or safe route. Then reset your voicemail PIN and greeting through your network’s official app, website, voicemail menu, or known support number.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep guessing the voicemail PIN repeatedly, because you may trigger lockouts and lose time.
- Don’t use a reset link from an unexpected text or email; type your network’s website yourself, use the official app, or call a known support number.
- Don’t assume it is just a glitch if you also notice loss of signal, SIM-change messages, PAC or porting messages, new-device emails, or account details changing.
- Don’t ignore it if your number is used for account recovery, banking alerts, or one-time codes.
- Don’t post screenshots of network texts or emails, because they may contain identifiers.
- Don’t factory-reset your phone or change your number in a panic.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether your mobile service still works.**
   - Try to make a call and send a text.
   - If calls, texts, or signal have suddenly stopped, use another phone, trusted device, or safe internet connection to contact your mobile network urgently.

2. **Secure your mobile network account from a safe route.**
   - Sign in only through your network’s official app or website, typed manually or opened from a bookmark.
   - Change the account password.
   - Add or confirm any extra account security your network offers, such as a customer service passcode or extra checks for SIM, number, or account changes.
   - Check recent account activity for SIM changes, PAC or porting requests, email or address changes, new devices, or contact-detail changes.

3. **Contact your mobile network and ask for account-takeover checks.**
   - Say: “My voicemail PIN or greeting changed without me. Please check for account takeover.”
   - Ask whether there has been a recent SIM swap, replacement SIM, PAC request, number port, device change, or customer-service contact on the account.
   - Ask whether the network can stop, pause, or reverse any unauthorised SIM or number change if one is in progress.
   - Ask them to add stronger protection to your account so future changes need extra verification.

4. **Reset voicemail through your network’s official process.**
   - Reset the voicemail PIN using your network’s official voicemail menu, app, website, or support route.
   - Choose a PIN that is not based on birthdays, repeated digits, or sequential digits.
   - Re-record your greeting, or switch to a standard greeting if you do not want a personal greeting.
   - Delete any voicemail messages you no longer need, especially messages containing codes or sensitive information.

5. **Reduce the risk to connected accounts.**
   - Secure your email first, because email is often used to reset other accounts.
   - Check your email recovery phone number, recovery email, forwarding rules, filters, and signed-in devices.
   - Change the passwords for email, banking, phone-network, and other high-value accounts that use your phone number or voicemail for recovery.
   - Where available, move important accounts away from voice-call or SMS-only verification and use an authenticator app, passkey, security key, or app-based approval.

6. **Warn banks or important services if money or identity access could be affected.**
   - Contact banks, payment apps, and other important providers using their official app, website, card number, or known phone number.
   - Say you suspect your phone number or voicemail may have been compromised and ask them to check for suspicious activity and secure access.
   - Keep notes of dates, times, support-reference numbers, and what each provider told you.

7. **Report suspected fraud or cyber crime.**
   - If you live in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report fraud or cyber crime through Report Fraud online or by phone and keep the reference number.
   - If you live in Scotland, report suspected fraud to Police Scotland via 101 and keep the incident details.
   - If you received suspicious texts or calls, you can also forward scam texts or report scam calls to 7726 where appropriate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to change your phone number.
- You do not need to contact everyone you know; prioritise banks, email, phone-network support, and accounts where impersonation or resets could cause harm.
- You do not need to factory-reset your phone or reinstall apps now unless there are separate signs your device itself is compromised or a trusted support channel advises it.
- You do not need to solve every account today; secure the phone network account, voicemail, email, and money-related accounts first.

## Important reassurance
A changed voicemail PIN or greeting can happen because of account compromise, network processes, or a mistake. You do not have to prove which one it is before acting. Securing the network account and resetting voicemail through official routes is a sensible damage-limiting step.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and reduce the chance of further loss. If money, identity documents, business accounts, or several online accounts are affected, later decisions may need help from your network, bank, fraud-reporting services, or another specialist support route.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. Mobile network processes, voicemail menus, and security options vary by provider and phone model; use your network’s official help channels if anything does not match what you see.
