# What to do if… people report receiving spam from your email address and you did not send it (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as either a compromised email account or someone faking your address. Secure the account now, or start your provider’s recovery process if you cannot sign in.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t reply to the spam to explain.
- Don’t click unsubscribe links, attachments, or links in messages you did not sign up for.
- Don’t keep using the same password.
- Don’t share one-time codes with anyone, including someone claiming to be support.
- Don’t assume it is only spoofing until you have checked sign-ins, forwarding, filters, rules, and recovery details.

## What to do now
1. **Pause sending anything important from that email account for the moment.** If someone is in the mailbox, they may be able to see replies, password resets, invoices, and personal details.

2. **If you can still sign in, secure the email account first.**
   - Change the password to a long, unique password.
   - Turn on 2-step verification on the email account.
   - Use your provider’s security settings to sign out of other sessions, devices, and connected apps if that option is available.

3. **If you cannot sign in, or settings keep changing, use account recovery.**
   - Go directly to your email provider’s official recovery page or support page.
   - Regain access before changing other connected accounts where possible.
   - Once you are back in, change the password and turn on 2-step verification.

4. **Check for hidden mailbox changes.**
   - Look for forwarding addresses you did not add.
   - Check filters and rules, especially rules that auto-archive, auto-delete, mark as read, or forward messages.
   - Check send-as, delegated access, connected accounts, and app passwords if your provider offers them.
   - Check your recovery email and phone number, and remove anything you did not set.

5. **Check whether messages were actually sent from your account.**
   - Look in Sent, Outbox, Drafts, Trash, Deleted, and Archive.
   - Check recent sign-in activity, devices, or security alerts if your provider shows them.
   - Treat missing logs as inconclusive; some providers show limited detail.

6. **Secure accounts that rely on this email.**
   - Start with banking, shopping, social media, messaging, cloud storage, and work accounts.
   - Change any password that matches or resembles the email password.
   - Turn on 2-step verification where available.

7. **Warn key people without spreading the scam.**
   - Use a different route if possible, such as phone, text, or another email account.
   - Tell family, colleagues, finance contacts, and anyone likely to act on the message: “My email may have been compromised or spoofed. Please do not open unexpected links or attachments from it. Please verify any payment request or invoice by phone.”

8. **Report a suspicious email if you have a copy.**
   - If you can access the spam from a recipient or from your mailbox, forward it to report@phishing.gov.uk.
   - Do not click links or open attachments in the message before forwarding it.

9. **If this is a custom domain or work email, involve the domain or email administrator.**
   - Ask them to check mail logs for unusual sending.
   - Ask them to check anti-spoofing controls, including SPF, DKIM, and DMARC.
   - Ask them to check whether any mailbox rules, forwarding, delegates, or connected apps were added.

## What can wait
- You do not need to know exactly how it happened right now.
- You do not need to message every contact at once; prioritise people most likely to click, pay, or share sensitive information.
- You do not need to rebuild every device tonight; focus first on account control, hidden mailbox settings, and accounts that use this email for password resets.

## Important reassurance
This can happen even when you are careful. Spam appearing to come from your address can be caused by account access, password reuse, or simple spoofing, and the first steps are the same: secure access, check hidden settings, warn key people, and protect connected accounts.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and reduce further harm. If you find ongoing access, financial loss, business impersonation, or repeated spoofing of a domain you control, later decisions may need specialist IT, security, or provider support.
