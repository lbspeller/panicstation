# What to do if… people report receiving spam from your email address and you did not send it (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the account by changing your password immediately and checking for unauthorized forwarding rules. If you cannot sign in, initiate your provider's official account recovery process and report the incident to the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC).

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply to the reports or the spam itself to explain the situation, as this confirms your address is live to attackers.
- Do not click any links or "unsubscribe" buttons in the messages found in your Sent or Trash folders.
- Do not use the same password for any other accounts; your email is often the master key for password resets.
- Do not share multi-factor authentication (MFA) or two-step verification (2SV) codes with anyone claiming to be support.
- Do not assume the issue is resolved just by changing your password without checking for hidden mailbox rules.

## What to do now
1. **Change your email password immediately to a strong, unique phrase.** **Use three random words or a password manager to ensure the new password has not been used elsewhere.** 
2. **Enable two-step verification (2SV) or multi-factor authentication (MFA) on the account settings page.** **This provides a critical second layer of security that prevents attackers from re-entering even if they have your password.**
3. **Check your mailbox for unauthorized forwarding addresses and auto-delete rules.** **Attackers often set up "silent" rules to forward your incoming mail to themselves or to automatically delete replies from people complaining about the spam.**
   - Check "Rules" or "Filters" in your mail settings.
   - Check "Forwarding and POP/IMAP" settings.
4. **Sign out of all other sessions and devices through your account security dashboard.** **This forces any active attacker sessions to disconnect and requires the new password and 2SV code for re-entry.**
5. **Verify your account recovery information is still correct.** **Ensure the backup email address and phone number listed in your security settings belong to you and have not been changed by an intruder.**
6. **Notify your key contacts through a different medium such as a phone call or text.** **Warn them not to click links or open attachments from you until you confirm the account is secure.**
7. **Report the incident to the NCSC Suspicious Email Reporting Service (SERS).** **Forward any examples of the spam sent from your account to report@phishing.gov.uk.**
8. **Contact Action Fraud if you believe you have lost money or sensitive personal data.** **If you are in Scotland, report the matter to Police Scotland by calling 101.**
9. **Audit your other high-priority accounts for suspicious activity.** **Check your bank, social media, and shopping accounts for unauthorized changes, focusing on those that use the compromised email for logins.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific malware or phishing link that caused the initial breach right now.
- You do not need to notify every person in your address book immediately; prioritize financial and professional contacts first.
- You do not need to replace your hardware or devices tonight unless you see physical evidence of tampering.

## Important reassurance
Mailbox compromises and spoofing are common occurrences and do not necessarily mean you have made a mistake. Modern attackers use automated tools to guess passwords or "spoof" addresses to make them look legitimate. Taking these steps quickly is the most effective way to stop the disruption and protect your digital identity.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a suspected email breach or impersonation. It focuses on regaining control and preventing further spread. Complex recovery for enterprise-level domain spoofing may require specialist IT intervention.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or technical advice. Security protocols for email providers change frequently; always follow the specific recovery instructions provided by your service provider (e.g., Google, Microsoft, or Apple). If you suspect your identity has been stolen, contact CIFAS or a similar identity protection service.
