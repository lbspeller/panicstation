# What to do if… you are pressured to attend a private work meeting and feel unsafe (UK)

## Short answer
Do not go to the private location alone. Move the meeting to a safer setting, such as a visible room, an open-door meeting, or a remote call, and put that boundary in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go somewhere isolated, such as a locked room, car, stairwell, outside area, or offsite place, just to keep the peace.
- Do not let anyone rush you with “it will only take a minute” if you feel unsafe or pressured.
- Do not switch off your phone or allow yourself to be separated from your bag, keys, or exit route.
- Do not resign, agree to anything, or sign documents while you feel pressured or scared.
- Do not delete messages, calendar invites, or chat logs connected to the request.
- Do not rely on secret recording as your main safety plan. Workplace policies and legal issues can be complicated; written follow-ups, a safer location, or another person present are usually safer first steps.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause before you respond.** If this is happening right now, stay where other people are, such as an open office, reception area, or corridor near colleagues. Use a neutral line such as: “I need to get my notebook first” or “I need to send one message before we meet.”
2. **Set a clear boundary.** Say: “I’m not comfortable meeting in a private location. I can meet in Meeting Room X with the door open, or on Teams.”
3. **Put the boundary in writing.** Reply to the calendar invite or message with one sentence confirming the safer option and proposing times. Example: “I can meet at 2pm in Meeting Room 3, or by Teams. I’m not able to meet in [private location].”
4. **Ask for the purpose and format.** Ask: “Can you confirm what this meeting is about, and whether it is informal or part of a formal process?”
5. **Ask for another person to be present.** Ask for HR, another manager, a trade union representative, or a suitable colleague to attend as support or as a witness. If they refuse, repeat the safer options: “Then I can do this by email, with the door open, or with HR present.”
6. **If it may be a formal disciplinary or grievance meeting, ask about accompaniment before attending.** You may have a statutory right to be accompanied by a fellow worker, a trade union representative, or a trade union official in some formal disciplinary or grievance meetings. Ask in writing to bring a companion and, if needed, ask for the meeting to be rearranged so they can attend.
7. **Escalate inside the organisation if the pressure continues.** If the person pressuring you is your manager, or you fear retaliation, contact HR, another manager, your trade union representative, or a designated safeguarding or people contact if your workplace has one. Keep it simple: “I’m being pressured into a private one-to-one meeting and I do not feel safe. I’m asking for the meeting to be moved and for another person to be present.”
8. **Make a quick factual record while it is fresh.** Note the date, time, who asked, the exact location requested, the words used, and any witnesses. Save the invite, messages, and chat logs somewhere safe and policy-compliant.
9. **If you feel in immediate danger, leave and get help.** Go to a public area, ask reception or security for help if available, and leave the building if needed. If there is immediate risk of harm, call 999. If you need police help but it is not an emergency, call 101.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to file a formal grievance, contact a solicitor, or take legal action.
- You do not need to produce a perfect written statement right now; preserve the basic facts and messages.
- You do not need to confront the person or explain your reasons in detail. “I’m not comfortable meeting privately” is enough.

## Important reassurance
Feeling unsafe is a valid reason to change the meeting conditions. Asking for a visible location, an open door, a remote meeting, or another person present is a normal and proportionate safety boundary, especially when you are being pressured.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce risk and prevent irreversible mistakes in the moment. Later decisions, such as formal complaints, medical support, legal advice, or trade union action, can come after you are safe and supported.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you believe you are at immediate risk of harm, prioritise getting to a safer place and contacting emergency services. Workplace policies and the best reporting route vary, so it is reasonable to ask HR or your trade union what process applies before attending any meeting.
