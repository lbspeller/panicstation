# What to do if… you receive an urgent penalty notice or fine demanding immediate payment and threatening enforcement action (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay through the link, QR code, phone number, or bank details shown on an unexpected notice. Verify the notice today using official contact details you find yourself, especially if it mentions bailiffs, sheriff officers, enforcement agents, court fines, or a very short payment deadline.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click payment links in unexpected texts or emails.
- Do not scan a QR code on a notice unless you have separately confirmed the notice is genuine.
- Do not call the phone number on the notice until you have checked it against an official source you found yourself.
- Do not pay by gift card, cryptocurrency, bank transfer to a personal name, or any method that feels unusual or pressured.
- Do not give your bank details, passwords, one-time codes, date of birth, or National Insurance number to someone who contacted you.
- Do not open the door or let someone in just because they use words like “bailiff”, “enforcement agent”, “sheriff officer”, or “final notice”.
- Do not ignore it completely; verification is the safe middle step between panic-paying and doing nothing.

## What to do now
1. **Keep the notice, but do not engage with it yet.** Screenshot the message, photograph the letter and envelope, and write down the date received, amount, deadline, reference number, sender name, phone number, website, and payment details.

2. **Identify what it claims to be.** Look for the issuing body and notice type without using any link on the notice. It may claim to be:
   - a council or transport Penalty Charge Notice for parking, bus lane, road-user charging, or moving traffic;
   - a court fine or HMCTS-related enforcement notice;
   - a private parking charge notice from a parking company;
   - a debt collector, bailiff, enforcement-agent, or sheriff-officer notice;
   - a fake notice using official-looking wording.

3. **Check for scam warning signs.** Treat the notice as suspicious if it arrived only by text or email, demands immediate payment through a link, uses a QR code, threatens fast enforcement without clear paperwork, asks for unusual payment, contains vague vehicle or case details, or does not clearly name the authority or company.

4. **Verify through a route you find yourself.** Type the council, court, transport authority, parking company, or tribunal website into your browser yourself, or search GOV.UK for the official service. Use the official website or published switchboard number to check whether the reference number, vehicle registration, name, amount, and deadline match.

5. **For a council or transport PCN, do not rely on a text link.** Go to the council or transport authority website yourself and find its PCN payment or challenge page. Local-authority PCN payment demands sent by SMS are a strong scam warning.

6. **For a court fine or enforcement notice, verify the court route separately.** Use GOV.UK or the official court contact route you find yourself. Do not use a phone number, payment page, or bank details supplied only in the unexpected message.

7. **If bailiffs, enforcement agents, or sheriff officers are mentioned, treat it as urgent but still verify.** Check whether the paperwork gives your correct name and address, the debt, the amount, the creditor or issuing authority, and the enforcement company. Rules and terminology vary within the UK; in Scotland, sheriff officers are different from bailiffs.

8. **If someone is at your door, keep the door closed while you verify.** Ask them to put identification, the company name, the creditor, the reference number, and the reason for the visit through the letterbox or show it at a window. Use an official route you find yourself to check who they are before making any payment or letting anyone in.

9. **If you already clicked a link or entered details, protect your money first.** Contact your bank immediately using the number on the back of your card or the official banking app. Say you may have entered details into a scam payment page and ask what needs blocking, cancelling, or monitoring.

10. **Report suspicious messages once you are safe to do so.** Forward suspicious texts to 7726. Forward suspicious emails to report@phishing.gov.uk. If you lost money or were hacked and you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report it through Report Fraud; if you are in Scotland, report it to Police Scotland on 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the whole appeal, dispute, or repayment position before checking whether the notice is real.
- You do not need to write a long explanation today unless an official source confirms a real deadline that requires action.
- You do not need to stay on a call, reply to texts, or keep using a website that pressured you.
- You do not need to pay first just to stop feeling scared.

## Important reassurance
A notice that uses urgent words can make anyone feel rushed. Slowing down to verify is not ignoring the problem; it is the safest first step before money, personal details, or access to your home are put at risk.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the notice is genuine, later decisions about payment, challenge, appeal, vulnerability, debt advice, or enforcement may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, debt, or professional advice. Penalty and enforcement processes vary by notice type and within the UK, so use official sources and get advice before acting on any deadline you are unsure about.
