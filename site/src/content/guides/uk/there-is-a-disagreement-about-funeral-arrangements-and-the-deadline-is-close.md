# What to do if… there is a disagreement about funeral arrangements and the deadline is close (UK)

## Short answer
Pause anything irreversible, especially cremation, burial, or moving the body to another funeral director, burial place, crematorium, or outside the UK. Tell the funeral director or mortuary there is a dispute, ask them not to proceed on competing instructions, and ask who they need authority from under the rules for the UK nation where the funeral is being arranged.

## Do not do these things
- Do not rush into cremation or burial just because a date is close.
- Do not rush a disputed release or transfer to another funeral director, burial place, crematorium, or outside the UK.
- Do not assume “next of kin” automatically has the final say in every part of the UK.
- Do not sign funeral contracts, cremation forms, burial forms, transport forms, or payment agreements while authority is unclear.
- Do not let several people give the funeral director different instructions.
- Do not remove the will, identity documents, funeral-plan papers, or death paperwork from someone’s home unless the person with proper authority agrees.
- Do not frame the immediate task as winning the family argument; the immediate task is to prevent an irreversible step until authority is clear.

## What to do now
1. **Put a clear hold on irreversible steps**
   - Say: “There is a dispute. Please do not cremate, bury, release, move, or book final arrangements until authority is confirmed.”
   - Ask the funeral director or mortuary to record the hold in writing.
   - If anyone says “we have to go ahead today,” ask exactly what legal or practical deadline they mean.

2. **Find the document or person that may show who can instruct**
   - Look for a will, codicil, funeral plan, prepaid funeral documents, or written funeral wishes.
   - In Scotland, also ask whether there is any written declaration or arrangement naming someone to organise the funeral.
   - Ask the funeral director what proof they need before accepting instructions in the relevant part of the UK.

3. **Identify the safest temporary point of contact**
   - Ask the funeral director to accept calls from one named contact only until authority is clear.
   - That contact should only pass on agreed information, not make disputed final decisions.
   - Send relatives one short message: “There is a dispute, so we are pausing irreversible decisions and confirming who can instruct. Please do not sign, book, pay, or authorise anything until this is clear.”

4. **Check whether an official process is controlling timing**
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, ask whether the death has been reported to the coroner.
   - In Scotland, ask whether the Procurator Fiscal is involved.
   - If so, ask the funeral director, mortuary, coroner’s office, or Procurator Fiscal contact what can and cannot happen before the body is released or the death paperwork is complete.

5. **Separate temporary care from final funeral decisions**
   - It may be possible to agree only where the person is being respectfully cared for while the dispute is paused.
   - Do not treat agreement about temporary care, storage, or a provisional date as agreement to cremation, burial location, ashes, service format, or payment.
   - Ask for any booking to be marked provisional if authority is not yet settled.

6. **Reduce the dispute to the few points that matter today**
   - Write down the contested decisions: cremation or burial, place, ashes, religious or cultural requirements, timing, and who is authorised to instruct.
   - Park readings, music, flowers, notices, wake plans, and memorial choices.
   - Ask whether everyone can agree to a short pause while the authorised person or people are confirmed.

7. **If the funeral is imminent and the hold is not being respected**
   - Contact a solicitor urgently and say: “A disputed cremation or burial may happen imminently. I need urgent advice about a temporary court order or other urgent remedy to pause it.”
   - Keep the request narrow: pause the irreversible step and confirm who can instruct.
   - Save written messages, booking confirmations, forms, and names of people you spoke to.

## What can wait
- You do not need to finalise the service format, readings, music, flowers, order of service, obituary wording, or wake plans right now.
- You do not need to decide who was right in past family disputes.
- You do not need to choose a memorial, headstone, inscription, or scattering arrangement now.
- You do not need to commit to major spending while authority is unclear.
- You do not need to solve the whole estate or probate position before making the immediate funeral pause clear.

## Important reassurance
A pause is not disrespectful. It is a way to protect the person who has died, prevent a step that cannot be undone, and give everyone a safer chance to confirm who can make the decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the next few hours and the earliest safe pause. Later decisions may need specialist help from a funeral director, bereavement support, mediation, or a solicitor.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Funeral disputes can be urgent and fact-specific, especially where there is a will, more than one possible decision-maker, disagreement about cremation or burial, or coroner or Procurator Fiscal involvement.
