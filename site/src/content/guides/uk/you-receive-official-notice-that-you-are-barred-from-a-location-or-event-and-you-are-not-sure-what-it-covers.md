# What to do if… you receive official notice that you are barred from a location or event and you are not sure what it covers (UK)

## Short answer
Do not return to the location or event until you have obtained written confirmation of the exact boundaries, duration, and issuing authority of the notice. If the notice mentions a court, police bail, or a specific statute, treat any entry as a high-risk action that could lead to arrest or a finding of contempt.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t return to the site "just to ask for a manager" or to get a verbal explanation from security staff.
- Don’t attempt to enter via a different gate, door, or entrance to see if you are recognized.
- Don’t ignore documents referencing the "Licensing Act 2003," "Football Spectators Act," or "Unauthorised Entry to Football Matches Act 2026."
- Don’t assume a ban is invalid just because it contains a minor typo like a misspelt name or address.
- Don’t post photos of the notice on social media, as this can be used as evidence of your awareness of the ban if you later claim you didn't understand it.
- Don’t assume "civil" notices are harmless; breaching a civil injunction can result in a warrant for your arrest for contempt of court.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific type of notice you have received.**  
   **Look for the heading or the statute mentioned in the fine print to determine the consequences of a breach.**
   - **Court Orders:** Look for "Restraining Order," "Criminal Behaviour Order (CBO)," "Non-Molestation Order," or "Football Banning Order (FBO)." Breach is usually a criminal offence.
   - **Police Bail:** Look for "Form MG4," "Section 30A PACE (Street Bail)," or "Section 47 PACE." Breach leads to arrest but is not always a separate criminal charge itself.
   - **Council/Police Notices:** Look for "Community Protection Notice (CPN)" or "Public Spaces Protection Order (PSPO)."
   - **Private/Management Bans:** Look for "Notice of Trespass," "Withdrawal of Implied Permission," or "Licensee Exclusion."

2. **Secure the document and record the context of its delivery.**  
   **Keep the original document and any envelope it arrived in to prove the date of service.**
   - Photograph both sides of the notice.
   - Note the exact time, date, and method of delivery (e.g., handed by a constable, sent by registered post, or taped to a door).
   - Record the name or badge number of the person who served it, if known.

3. **Treat the exclusion zone as the widest possible interpretation.**  
   **If the notice mentions a "site" or "complex," assume this includes car parks, perimeter footpaths, and secondary buildings until told otherwise.**
   - If you are banned from a "stadium," assume the exclusion includes the surrounding streets during the "control period" defined by the Football Spectators Act 1989.

4. **Request a formal "Schedule of Exclusion" or map in writing.**  
   **Contact the issuing body via the official email or address on the document—do not visit them in person.**
   - Ask for the specific GPS coordinates or a highlighted map of the "exclusion zone."
   - Ask for the precise "end date" and "expiry time" of the order.
   - If the ban is for an event, ask if it applies to future iterations of that event at different venues.

5. **Verify if there are mandatory "carve-outs" for essential access.**  
   **Check the document for "excepted purposes" such as attending a specific medical facility, place of work, or legal appointment.**
   - If the notice blocks your only route to your home or workplace, do not test the route; contact the issuer or a solicitor immediately to request a "variation" or a defined "access corridor."

6. **Check for "Penal Notices" or "Warning Notices."**  
   **Look for a box with bold text stating that "disobedience to this order may be held to be a contempt of court."**
   - If this text is present, the order is a high-priority legal document that carries the risk of imprisonment for breach.

7. **Contact a solicitor if the notice is linked to a criminal case or a court.**  
   **Provide your solicitor with a clear scan of the notice and ask them to confirm the "terms of the order" as recorded on the police or court computer systems.**
   - If you do not have a solicitor, use the Law Society "Find a Solicitor" tool to locate one specializing in "Criminal Law" or "Actions Against the Police."

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to appeal the ban within the first 24 hours.
- You do not need to explain your side of the story to the venue manager or the police right now.
- You do not need to worry about "clearing your name" before you have confirmed that you are physically safe from arrest for breach.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a notice that limits where you can go is a complex legal situation. It is normal to feel confused by vague language or unsure about boundaries. By staying away and seeking written clarification, you are taking the most effective step to protect yourself from unintended legal consequences or allegations of "wilful breach."

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate stabilization of your legal position following receipt of an exclusion or barring notice. It does not cover the long-term process of appealing a ban, suing for wrongful exclusion, or defending a criminal charge.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. The laws surrounding exclusion vary significantly between private land (trespass) and court-mandated orders (statutory breach). If your notice mentions a court, a specific police station, or an upcoming hearing, you must consult a qualified legal professional before attempting to enter the restricted area.
