# What to do if… your windscreen cracks and you have pulled over safely to check whether you can continue (UK)

## Short answer
If the damage is larger than 10mm in your direct line of sight (Zone A) or 40mm elsewhere in the swept area, do not continue driving—arrange recovery as the vehicle may be legally considered to be in a dangerous condition.

## Do not do these things
- Do not continue driving to "see if the crack runs" or worsens over time.
- Do not slam the vehicle doors or boot, as the pressure change can cause a crack to spread instantly.
- Do not use the heater/demister on a high heat setting if the glass is cold, as rapid temperature changes expand the crack.
- Do not attempt to "test" the strength of the crack by pushing or tapping on the glass.
- Do not stand on the traffic-facing side of the vehicle while inspecting the damage.
- Do not apply tape or "DIY" kits if the vehicle is currently stopped in a high-traffic area or on a motorway.

## What to do now
1. **Verify your stopping position is as safe as possible before exiting the vehicle.** If you are on a motorway, try to reach an emergency area or service station rather than stopping on the hard shoulder. 
   - **Switch on hazard lights and sidelights if visibility is poor.**
   - **Turn your wheels to the left (away from the carriageway).**
   - **Exit via the left-hand doors only and wait behind the safety barrier if one is present.**
2. **Conduct a visibility assessment from the driver's seat once the vehicle is stationary and safe.** Do not just look at the crack from the outside; sit in your normal driving position.
   - **Assume the vehicle is unsafe to drive if the crack is in your direct line of vision (Zone A - a 290mm wide strip centered on the steering wheel).**
   - **Assume the vehicle is unsafe to drive if you have to move your head to see around the damage.**
3. **Check for "spidering" or "branching" at the edges of the crack.** If the crack reaches the edge of the glass or has multiple lines branching out, it significantly compromises the structural integrity of the windscreen. 
   - **Choose recovery if the crack appears to be actively moving or growing while you are stopped.**
4. **Apply the MOT 10mm / 40mm safety thresholds as a strict limit.** Under the Road Traffic Act 1988 (Section 40A), driving a vehicle in a "dangerous condition" is an offense.
   - **Treat any damage larger than 10mm in Zone A (the driver's view) as an immediate stop.**
   - **Treat any damage larger than 40mm in the rest of the wiper-swept area as an immediate stop.**
5. **Contact your breakdown provider or National Highways if you cannot safely move.** 
   - **Call 0300 123 5000 for National Highways if you are on a motorway and need assistance but are not in immediate danger.**
   - **Call 999 only if you are in a live lane or believe there is an immediate risk of a collision.**
6. **Follow mandatory rejoining procedures if you are in a motorway emergency area.** 
   - **You MUST use the SOS emergency telephone provided in the emergency area to contact the operator.**
   - **Do not attempt to rejoin the motorway until the operator confirms it is safe and, if necessary, closes the left-hand lane to assist you.**
7. **Limit any "limping" journey to the nearest safe off-road location only.** If the damage is minor (outside Zone A and below size thresholds), drive at a reduced speed to the nearest garage or car park. Avoid potholes, speed bumps, and high speeds that increase wind pressure on the glass.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the glass needs a "chip repair" or a "full replacement" right now.
- You do not need to contact your insurance company's claims line until you are at a final safe destination.
- You do not need to clean the glass or remove debris from the crack.

## Important reassurance
A sudden crack in the windscreen is a startling event that can immediately impair your sense of safety. It is common to feel uncertain about whether the vehicle is legal or safe to drive. Choosing to stop and arrange recovery is a standard safety-first response to compromised visibility.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for deciding whether a vehicle with windscreen damage is safe to continue its journey. It does not cover repair techniques, insurance claim procedures, or ADAS (Advanced Driver Assistance Systems) recalibration requirements.

## Important note
This guide is for general information and does not constitute legal or professional advice. Under UK law, the driver is responsible for ensuring the vehicle is in a roadworthy condition; if visibility is impaired, you may face fines and penalty points. Always err on the side of caution and seek professional recovery if you are unsure of the vehicle's safety.
