# What to do if… you receive an official notice that you must appear to address an alleged violation and you are unsure what evidence to gather (UK)

## Short answer
Identify if the notice is a court summons / postal requisition or a police interview request, then secure legal advice before providing any statement. Your immediate priority is to preserve all existing records and organize a factual timeline rather than attempting to prove your case today.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice, miss the deadline, or assume the matter will resolve itself without your participation.
- Do not contact any complainants, witnesses, or other parties involved to "explain" or "sort things out" as this can be interpreted as witness interference.
- Do not delete, edit, or "clean up" digital messages, call logs, or browsing history, as this can be viewed as tampering with evidence.
- Do not post about the allegation or the notice on social media or discuss it in public forums.
- Do not call the number on the letter to argue your side before speaking to a solicitor.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific type of notice and the issuing body.** Look for headings like "Postal Requisition," "Summons," "Notice of Criminal Charge," or "Invitation to Voluntary Interview."
   - Note the exact date, time, and location of the required attendance.
   - Locate the reference number and the name of the court, tribunal, or police station.
2. **Verify the notice is genuine through official channels.** Look up the organization’s contact details on an official .gov.uk website (do not use the phone number provided in the letter if you suspect a scam).
   - Call the official switchboard to confirm the case or reference number exists in their system.
3. **Request free legal advice if the notice is for a police interview.** Whether the interview is "voluntary" or following an arrest, you have a right to free, independent legal advice under PACE Code C.
   - Tell the officer you require a solicitor to be present before the interview begins.
   - Do not answer questions about the allegation until you have consulted your solicitor privately.
4. **Secure a solicitor for court or tribunal hearings immediately.** Use the "Find a Solicitor" service via the Law Society (England/Wales), Law Society of Scotland, or Law Society of Northern Ireland.
   - Ask about Legal Aid eligibility; for criminal cases in the Magistrates' Court, you may be "passported" to free aid if you receive certain benefits (e.g., Universal Credit, JSA, or ESA).
   - If you cannot secure a solicitor before a first hearing, ask to speak to the "Duty Solicitor" immediately upon arrival at the court.
5. **Create a "Master Evidence Folder" for all related documents.** Include every page of the notice, the envelope it arrived in, and any "Statement of Facts" or "Initial Disclosure" provided.
6. **Construct a neutral factual timeline.** Write down a list of events with dates, times, and locations based only on what you can verify from your own records.
   - List the names of any people who were present during key events but do not contact them.
7. **Protect and isolate digital evidence.** Take screenshots of relevant text messages, emails, or social media interactions.
   - Keep the original digital files (do not forward or move them if it changes the "last modified" metadata).
   - Back up relevant photos or videos to a secure cloud service or external drive without applying filters or edits.
8. **Request reasonable adjustments or interpreters early.** If you have a disability, require an interpreter, or have specific access needs, notify the court or police station in writing immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare a "defense statement" or a full rebuttal of the charges today.
- You do not need to gather character references or letters of support at this stage.
- You do not need to decide how you will "plead" (Guilty or Not Guilty) until you have reviewed the full prosecution evidence with a solicitor.
- You do not need to pay for a private investigator or forensic services immediately.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an official notice is a high-stress event, and feeling overwhelmed or anxious is a common reaction to legal paperwork. Most initial procedural risks are managed by simply meeting deadlines and remaining silent until you have professional advice. Following these steps helps ensure you do not accidentally disadvantage yourself before the process truly begins.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and evidence preservation only. It does not provide legal strategy or trial preparation. Procedures vary significantly between criminal, civil, and tribunal systems across the UK.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. You should prioritize speaking with a qualified solicitor or a certified advice body (such as Citizens Advice) as soon as possible. Legal rights and aid thresholds may differ in Scotland and Northern Ireland.
