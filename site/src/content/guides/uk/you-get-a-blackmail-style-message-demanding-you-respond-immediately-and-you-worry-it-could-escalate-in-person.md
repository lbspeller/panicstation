# What to do if… you get a blackmail-style message demanding you respond immediately and you worry it could escalate in person (UK)

## Short answer
Do not reply, do not pay, and do not negotiate. Prioritize your immediate physical safety by moving to a secure or populated location, then report the threat to the police (999 for immediate danger, 101 for non-emergency threats).

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply to the message or attempt to "reason" with the sender, as engagement confirms your account is active and vulnerable.
- Do not pay any money or provide gift cards, cryptocurrency, or bank details.
- Do not open the door to any unexpected visitors or go outside to investigate noises.
- Do not click on links, download attachments, or install any software sent by the blackmailer.
- Do not delete the messages or the account until you have preserved the evidence.
- Do not post about the threat on public social media, as this may reveal your current location or emotional state to the harasser.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a physically secure or populated environment immediately.**  
   If you are currently alone, go to a room with other people, call a trusted neighbor, or move to a staffed public area such as a library or cafe. Ensure all ground-floor windows and doors are locked.
2. **Call 999 if the message contains specific details that suggest an imminent physical arrival.**  
   Specific details include your home address, your current location, or the names of family members combined with a threat to visit. If you have a hearing or speech impairment, use the emergencySMS service (text 999) or the 999 BSL app.
3. **Capture and preserve all evidence of the communication.**  
   Take screenshots that clearly show the sender's username, profile ID, the date and time of the message, and the full text of the demand. 
   - Save the email in its original folder; do not forward it to yourself as this can alter technical metadata.
   - Do not perform your own "investigation" or search for the sender's IP address.
4. **Block the sender and restrict your digital exposure.**  
   Use the platform's "Block and Report" function. Set your social media profiles to "Private" and remove any public information regarding your workplace, school, or frequent locations.
5. **Report the incident to the appropriate UK authorities based on your location.**  
   - **Personal safety and criminal threats:** Report to your local police force via 101 or their online reporting portal. 
   - **Fraud or cyber-enabled extortion (England, Wales, and Northern Ireland):** Report to **Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) via reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.
   - **Fraud or cyber-enabled extortion (Scotland):** Report directly to **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
6. **Forward suspicious technical communications to national security centers.**  
   - **Emails:** Forward to report@phishing.gov.uk.
   - **Texts (SMS):** Forward to 7726 (this spells SPAM on a keypad).
7. **Inform a trusted person about the situation to create a safety buffer.**  
   Tell a friend, family member, or colleague so they are aware you are being targeted. If you live in a building with a concierge or security team, provide them with a description or name if available and instruct them not to allow any unauthorized visitors to your unit.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the person behind the message right now; the police and specialized services handle tracing.
- You do not need to decide today if you should permanently delete your social media presence or move house.
- You do not need to explain yourself or your past actions to the blackmailer.
- You do not need "perfect" or comprehensive evidence to make an initial police report.

## Important reassurance
Blackmail and extortion are designed to create a false sense of extreme urgency and isolation. These tactics rely on triggering a "panic" response to force you into compliance. Taking a moment to stop and prioritize your physical safety is the most effective way to regain control. You are not at fault for being targeted by a predatory communication.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and harm prevention for the first few minutes and hours following a threat. It does not replace a long-term safety plan or legal advice for ongoing harassment or stalking cases.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. In a life-threatening emergency, always dial 999. Local police forces and Report Fraud are the authoritative bodies for assessing the severity of a criminal threat.
