# What to do if… you notice your drink or food was left unattended and you feel unsafe about it (UK)

## Short answer
Stop consuming the item immediately, move to a well-lit area with a trusted person, and alert venue staff or security. If you feel unwell or suspect you have been spiked, seek urgent medical assessment by calling 999 or 111.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep consuming the drink or food to "see if it's okay".
- Do not perform a "taste test" or ask a friend to try it for you.
- Do not stay in a secluded area or leave the venue alone.
- Do not leave with someone you do not know or do not fully trust.
- Do not drink more alcohol or use other substances to "calm down" if you feel physically strange.
- Do not attempt to confront or investigate a suspected person yourself.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the item and move:** Stop eating or drinking immediately and leave the item where it is. **Move to a staffed area such as the bar, reception, or a security point and stay with a trusted friend.**
2. **Alert staff using a code-phrase if needed:** Tell a staff member you feel unsafe or vulnerable. **If the venue uses the "Ask for Angela" scheme, use this phrase to discreetly signal that you need assistance.**
   - Ask for a safe space away from the public floor.
   - Ask for help arranging a safe way home or contacting a trusted family member.
3. **Request evidence preservation:** Without handling the item yourself, **ask the manager or security to set the drink or food aside and preserve it for potential police collection.** 
   - Ask if they can also preserve any CCTV footage covering the area where the item was left.
4. **Monitor your physical symptoms:** Pay close attention to how you feel over the next 20 to 60 minutes. **If you feel suddenly confused, extremely sleepy, nauseous, or significantly more intoxicated than your intake suggests, seek medical help.**
   - Drink small amounts of water for hydration, but do not drink large quantities to "flush" your system.
5. **Contact medical services:** Choose the appropriate level of care based on your symptoms. **If you feel very unwell or are in immediate danger, call 999.**
   - For urgent medical advice that is not an emergency: Call **NHS 111** (England and Wales) or **NHS 24 on 111** (Scotland).
   - In Northern Ireland: Use the **"Phone First"** service for your local trust or contact a GP out-of-hours service.
6. **Report the incident (optional):** If you believe a crime like spiking has occurred, report it as soon as possible. **Call 101 or report online to help police gather time-sensitive forensic evidence, such as urine samples, which are most effective within 12 hours.**
7. **Access specialist support if needed:** If you suspect a sexual assault has occurred, prioritize your immediate safety. **You can contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) for a forensic examination and medical care, regardless of whether you choose to report to the police.**
8. **Secure a safe exit:** Do not walk home alone or take an unbooked taxi. **Ask venue staff to help you book a reputable taxi or wait with you until a trusted person arrives to collect you.**

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to press formal charges or give a full police statement.
- Reviewing social media or messages to figure out who was near your table.
- Filing a formal customer complaint with the venue management.
- Determining exactly what substance may have been involved.

## Important reassurance
Your intuition is a vital safety tool. It is always better to discard a drink and seek help than to ignore a feeling of unease. Venue staff are trained to assist in these situations, and you do not need to "prove" that something happened to justify asking for a safe space or a fresh drink.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate minutes and hours following a safety concern. It does not replace professional medical diagnosis or legal advice.

## Important note
If you are in immediate danger or experiencing a medical emergency, call 999. Procedures for evidence collection and medical reporting are subject to local NHS trust and police force policies.
