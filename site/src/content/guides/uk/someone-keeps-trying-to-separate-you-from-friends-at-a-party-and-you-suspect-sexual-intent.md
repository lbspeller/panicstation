# What to do if… someone keeps trying to separate you from friends at a party and you suspect sexual intent (UK)

## Short answer
Prioritise physical proximity to staff or your group: move immediately to a well-lit, crowded area and inform a third party (friend or venue staff) that you are being targeted for isolation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to go to a "quieter place" (upstairs, outside, to a car, or a side room) to resolve the conversation.
- Do not accept a private interaction as a way to "be polite" or "keep the peace."
- Do not leave your drink unattended or accept a drink you did not see poured; if you suspect tampering, do not finish it.
- Do not try to "handle it yourself" if the person is physically blocking your path or ignoring your verbal boundaries.

## What to do now
1. **Relocate to a high-visibility zone immediately.** Move to the bar, the entrance, or the centre of the dance floor. If you are currently in a hallway or corner, stand up and walk toward staff or a large group without explaining your departure to the person.
2. **Execute a "buddy lock" with someone you trust.** Link arms with a friend or stand shoulder-to-shoulder with them. If you are currently separated, send a text with a specific location and the command: "Meet me at the bar now—do not come alone."
3. **Use the "Ask for Angela" protocol if the venue is a pub or club.** Approach the bar and ask for "Angela." Under 2026 "Official Pledge" standards, trained staff will take you to a safe space (private area or back-of-house) to provide support, such as calling a taxi or alerting security, without the other person seeing.
4. **Inform staff or security directly if "Angela" is not recognised.** Say: "I feel unsafe. That person is trying to isolate me and I need to get back to my group safely." Ask them to watch you while you rejoin your friends or to escort you to your transport.
5. **Report suspected drink or needle spiking within 12 hours.** If you feel suddenly unwell or "more drunk" than expected, tell a friend or staff immediately. Many substances used for spiking leave the system quickly; contacting the police (101 or 999 in an emergency) within this window is critical for forensic testing.
6. **Create a safe exit that does not involve the person.** Do not walk to your car or a bus stop alone if you are being followed. Ask the venue to arrange a licensed taxi and wait inside the venue until the driver arrives and is identified.
7. **Contact 999 if you are being physically restrained or blocked.** If someone is preventing you from moving or leaving a room, this is a criminal matter. State your location and that you are being held against your will.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether you want to file a formal police report about the harassment.
- You do not need to justify your reaction or "prove" the other person's intent to anyone.
- You do not need to worry about "ruining the night" for your friends by prioritising your safety.

## Important reassurance
It is a common tactic for individuals with harmful intent to rely on social pressure and the victim's desire not to "cause a scene." Involving staff or friends is a standard safety procedure in UK venues, and you are entitled to use these resources whenever you feel uncomfortable.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for preventing isolation and potential assault. If a sexual assault has already occurred, or if you require medical attention, specialist services such as SARCs (Sexual Assault Referral Centres) provide confidential forensic and medical care regardless of whether you choose to report to the police.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance only and does not constitute legal or medical advice. In an emergency, always dial 999. For 24/7 confidential support regarding sexual violence in England and Wales, call the Rape Crisis national helpline at 0808 500 2222.
