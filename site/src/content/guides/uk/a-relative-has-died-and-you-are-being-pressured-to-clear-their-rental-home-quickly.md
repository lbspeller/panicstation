# What to do if… a relative has died and you are being pressured to clear their rental home quickly (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign anything, return keys, agree a final end date, or allow belongings to be cleared while you are still unsure who has authority. Ask for the demand, deadline, and legal basis in writing, then identify whether there is a joint tenant, successor, executor, administrator, or other personal representative who can deal with the home.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a surrender, agreed end date, clearance agreement, authority to dispose of goods, or anything similar under pressure.
- Do not assume the tenancy or occupation contract ends immediately just because the tenant has died; the answer depends on the nation, landlord type, agreement type, and whether anyone has succession rights.
- Do not hand back keys as a “temporary” step unless the authorised person has agreed what that means in writing.
- Do not let the landlord, agent, council, housing association, or Housing Executive remove or dispose of belongings without a written explanation of the process, an inventory, and a clear chance for the right person to collect them.
- Do not personally promise to pay rent, clearance costs, storage costs, or damage costs unless you know why you are legally responsible, such as being a joint tenant, guarantor, or authorised estate representative.
- Do not ignore a threatened lock change or same-day clearance if anyone is still living there; get urgent housing advice and contact the council tenancy relations or housing enforcement route where available.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the demand in writing.** Send one message asking the landlord, agent, council, housing association, or Housing Executive to confirm:
   - the deadline they are relying on,
   - whether they say the tenancy or occupation contract has already ended,
   - the legal or policy basis for that,
   - who they believe has authority to deal with the property,
   - what they plan to do with belongings if they remain there.

2. **Pause any surrender or key return.** Reply in writing: “We are identifying the person with authority and any tenancy or succession rights. No one is agreeing to surrender the tenancy, return possession, or authorise disposal of belongings at this stage.”

3. **Identify the immediate authority position.** Check whether there is:
   - a surviving joint tenant or joint contract-holder,
   - someone still living there who may have succession rights,
   - an executor named in a will,
   - someone applying or likely to apply as administrator,
   - a personal representative already accepted by the landlord.

4. **Identify the nation and agreement type.** Ask for a copy of the tenancy agreement, licence, or occupation contract and confirm whether the home is in England, Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland. Also confirm whether the landlord is private, council, housing association, Housing Executive, or another social landlord.

5. **If anyone is still living in the home, put that on record.** Tell the landlord in writing who is living there and that they should not change locks, remove belongings, or treat the home as empty. Ask them to set out the proper legal process they say applies before anyone is made to leave.

6. **If the home is empty, protect access and belongings.** Ask for written confirmation that keys will continue to allow access by agreement, that nothing will be removed without notice, and that any urgent visit will be arranged with the agreed contact person.

7. **Make a quick evidence record before anything is moved.** Take photos or video of every room, meters, keys, valuables, documents, medicines, mobility aids, pets, vehicles, and any items with obvious personal value. Make a simple room-by-room list and save all messages.

8. **Ask for a short holding period without accepting their deadline.** Say that bereavement, authority, and clearance are being sorted and ask them to confirm a written holding period for access and collection. Do not offer a final surrender date unless the authorised person agrees.

9. **Separate urgent items from full clearance.** Arrange safe collection of essentials such as identity documents, medication, mobility equipment, pets, perishables, valuables, and funeral-related papers first. The full estate sorting, rent position, deposit, and disposal decisions can be dealt with after authority is clearer.

10. **Get housing advice using the written facts.** Share the written demand, tenancy or occupation contract, deadline, and any threat about locks or disposal with a housing adviser, Shelter, Shelter Cymru, Shelter Scotland, Housing Rights in Northern Ireland, Citizens Advice, or a housing solicitor.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who gets every item from the home.
- You do not need to clear the whole property before you know who has authority and what process applies.
- You do not need to settle rent, deposit, repair, storage, or clearance disputes before stabilising access and belongings.
- You do not need to decide whether anyone will try to succeed to the tenancy until you know the nation, agreement type, and who was living there.
- You do not need to personally take on estate debts or costs just because you are a relative.

## Important reassurance
A short deadline after a death can feel frightening, but asking for the legal basis in writing, protecting the keys and belongings, and checking who has authority are normal first steps. You are allowed to slow the situation down enough to avoid accidentally ending rights or losing possessions.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist housing, probate, estate, or local authority advice because tenancy, succession, occupation-contract, belongings, and eviction rules differ across England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, probate, housing, or professional advice. If there is a same-day deadline, a threatened lock change, someone still living in the home, or a threat to remove belongings, get urgent specialist housing advice before agreeing to anything.
