# What to do if… someone sends you a screenshot of your home address and demands you do something right now (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay, send codes, click links, or rush to comply. Treat the use of your address as serious enough to slow down, preserve what you can, and contact police on 999 if anyone may be in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay, send gift cards, crypto, bank transfers, login codes, passwords, or identity documents to make the pressure stop.
- Do not keep negotiating, arguing, or trying to prove they are bluffing.
- Do not click links, install apps, scan QR codes, or log in through anything they send.
- Do not give them more details, such as your date of birth, workplace, family names, photos of your street, travel plans, or when you are home.
- Do not post publicly about it in the moment if that could reveal more information or attract attention to your address.
- Do not go outside alone to check whether someone is there if you feel unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause first.**  
   If you are at home, lock the door and stay inside. If you are out, move into a staffed public place or near other people. Keep your phone with you and, if possible, plug it in or preserve battery.

2. **Decide whether this is an emergency.**  
   Call **999** now if they say they are outside or on the way, they threaten violence, you are being followed, someone is trying to get in, or you believe anyone is in immediate danger. If there is no immediate danger but the message is threatening, harassing, or coercive, call **101** or use the official online reporting route for your police force.

3. **Save evidence, but do not delay emergency help to make it perfect.**  
   Take screenshots of the full conversation showing the username, phone number, email address, timestamps, the address screenshot, and the demand. Save the profile page if there is one. Write down what they demanded and any payment details they gave.

4. **Stop the live pressure.**  
   After saving what you can, mute or block the account and report the message in the app or platform. If they contact you again from a new account, do not engage; save it, report it, and block again.

5. **If they demanded money or you sent anything, contact your bank immediately.**  
   Use the number on the back of your card or the bank’s official app. Say you are being extorted or scammed and ask about stopping transfers, securing cards and accounts, and checking for fraud.

6. **Use the right fraud or cyber-crime reporting route if money, hacking, or scam links are involved.**  
   In England, Wales and Northern Ireland, use **Report Fraud** for fraud or cyber crime. In Scotland, report to **Police Scotland** by calling **101** for non-emergencies. If you are unsure, start with **101**.

7. **If it came by email or text, report the message safely.**  
   Do not reply and do not click links. For a suspicious email, forward it to **report@phishing.gov.uk**. For a suspicious text, many phone providers let you forward it to **7726**; if that does not work, check your phone provider’s reporting route.

8. **Do a quick account-safety check only if it is relevant.**  
   If you clicked a link, entered details, installed software, or they claim they hacked you, change any passwords you entered or reused, using the real website or app rather than a link in the message. Secure your email account if it may be affected. If you installed software, run your antivirus software and let it clean up anything it finds.

9. **Make a small home-safety plan for the next few hours.**  
   Tell anyone you live with what has happened. Agree not to open the door to unexpected callers, keep phones on, and call police rather than engaging if someone turns up. If you have a doorbell camera or security camera, turn on notifications and keep any relevant footage.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly how they got your address right now.
- You do not need to write the perfect report before contacting police.
- You do not need to clean up your whole online presence tonight.
- You do not need to decide now whether to confront them, warn everyone you know, or post about it.
- You do not need to prove whether they are nearby before taking basic safety steps.

## Important reassurance
Seeing your home address used as pressure is frightening, and it makes sense to feel shaken. A real address does not automatically mean the person is nearby or able to act; addresses can come from old accounts, public records, shared documents, data leaks, or other online sources. Your priority is to slow the situation down, avoid irreversible payments or disclosures, and bring in official support where needed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, preserving useful information, and avoiding rushed mistakes. Later decisions about privacy clean-up, ongoing harassment, legal options, or longer-term safety planning may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cyber-security, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe, threatened, or unsure whether the situation is serious enough, contact police and ask for guidance.
