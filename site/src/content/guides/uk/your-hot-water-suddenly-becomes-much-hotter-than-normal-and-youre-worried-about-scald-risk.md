# What to do if… your hot water suddenly becomes much hotter than normal and you’re worried about scald risk (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the heat source immediately by switching off the immersion heater or boiler hot water setting and avoid using any hot taps until the system has cooled and been professionally inspected.

## Do not do these things
- Don't use the hot water for baths, showers, or hand-washing, even if you intend to mix it with cold water.
- Don't allow children, elderly individuals, or those with reduced skin sensation to access the hot water taps.
- Don't attempt to "test" the water temperature with your hand or skin.
- Don't remove any panels or covers from the boiler or hot water cylinder.
- Don't set the hot water storage temperature below 60°C once the system is repaired (this is required to prevent Legionella bacteria).

## What to do now
1. **Warn all occupants of the immediate scald risk.**
   **Place a physical note or cover over all hot water taps and shower controls.** Inform everyone in the building that the water is dangerously hot and must not be used.

2. **Isolate the hot water heat source.**
   - **For immersion heaters:** Turn off the power at the dedicated wall switch (often marked 'Immersion') or at the consumer unit (fuse box).
   - **For gas boilers:** Use the control panel to turn the 'Hot Water' setting to 'Off' or its lowest possible setting.
   - **If you cannot identify the specific control:** Turn off the main electrical isolator for the entire heating system if it is safe to do so.

3. **Check for secondary hazards like gas or carbon monoxide.**
   **If you smell gas, hear a CO alarm, or see soot marks on the boiler, stop and evacuate.**
   - Open windows and doors only if you can do so on your way out.
   - **Call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999.**
   - If anyone is confused, breathless, or collapsing, call **999**.

4. **Identify and manage existing injuries.**
   **If a scald has occurred, start first aid immediately.**
   - Run cool or lukewarm water over the burn for **20 to 30 minutes**.
   - Remove jewellery or clothing near the burn unless it is stuck to the skin.
   - After cooling, cover the area loosely with layers of cling film (do not wrap it tight).
   - Call **111** for medical advice, or **999** for large burns (bigger than the person's hand) or burns to the face, hands, or joints.

5. **Contact a qualified professional for repair.**
   **Do not attempt to restart the heating source until it has been inspected.**
   - **Tenants:** Contact your landlord or letting agent immediately to report an emergency safety defect.
   - **Homeowners:** Contact a Gas Safe registered engineer (for boilers) or a qualified plumber (for cylinders/immersion heaters).

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause (such as a failed thermostat or mixing valve) yourself.
- You do not need to drain the hot water cylinder.
- You do not need to perform repeated temperature checks while waiting for a professional.

## Important reassurance
It is a safety priority to treat a sudden temperature spike as an emergency. Taking the system offline is the correct action to prevent severe burns and does not indicate an overreaction to a utility fault.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a hot water temperature emergency in a domestic setting. It does not replace the need for a professional technical inspection or emergency medical care.

## Important note
Scalding water can cause deep tissue damage in less than one second. If the water appears to be boiling or steaming, or if the pipes are vibrating or making loud banging noises, keep all persons away from the area until the system is fully isolated.
