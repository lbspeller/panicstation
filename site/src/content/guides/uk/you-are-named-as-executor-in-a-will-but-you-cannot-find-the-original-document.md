# What to do if… you are named as executor in a will but you cannot find the original document (UK)

## Short answer
Make the next hour about protecting papers and finding the signed original will, not distributing anything. If the original cannot be found, there is a recognised lost-will route in England and Wales, but it is evidence-based and not automatic.

## Do not do these things
- Do not give away, sell, throw away, or “tidy up” valuables or paperwork, because the will may be inside ordinary-looking papers.
- Do not assume there is no will, or that a copy you have seen is definitely the latest version.
- Do not distribute estate money or personal items in a rush.
- Do not let several people take papers away to sort separately.
- Do not write on, staple, unstaple, alter, or “clean up” any will, copy, envelope, or signing note you find.
- Do not post the only original will if it turns up unless you are following the probate service instructions and using a safe tracked method.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the deceased person’s papers and property.** Keep keys controlled, secure obvious valuables, and put paperwork, envelopes, notebooks, diaries, address books, storage receipts, and solicitor letters in one safe place.
2. **Start a search log.** Write down the date, who searched, which rooms, drawers, files, boxes, safes, bags, and storage places were checked, and what was found.
3. **Search likely places carefully before anything is cleared.** Check fireproof boxes, safes, locked drawers, filing cabinets, desk trays, folders labelled “will”, “solicitor”, “deeds”, “house”, “insurance”, or “probate”, and any place where certificates or passports were kept.
4. **Look for clues to the will-holder.** Keep solicitor letters, invoices, business cards, emails, will-writing service paperwork, bank safe-custody references, and any note saying where the will was stored.
5. **Contact likely will-holders.** Ask any identified solicitor, will-writing service, bank storage service, or professional adviser whether they hold the signed original will, know where it was stored, or prepared a later will or codicil.
6. **Ask close family only for location information.** Ask whether anyone knows where the original will was kept or whether a later will was made, but avoid arguments about who should inherit while the correct document is still unknown.
7. **If you only have a copy or scan, preserve it exactly as it is.** Keep the file, printout, envelope, email trail, and any note about signing or storage. Do not retype it or change the formatting.
8. **Consider an optional will search if the will-holder is unknown.** A commercial will search or registry search may help identify a registered will or a likely firm holding the will, but it is not the same as probate approval.
9. **Before applying, check the correct probate route.** The GOV.UK PA13 lost-will process is for people representing themselves in England and Wales where the original will is lost and the will has not been revoked. If the estate is not being handled in England and Wales, or if there is any dispute, check the correct court process before submitting forms.
10. **If the England and Wales lost-will route applies, prepare evidence before sending anything.** PA13 asks for detailed information about the missing original will, the copy, the search carried out, who last had the original, and why you believe the will was not revoked.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who receives belongings, whether property should be sold, or how personal items should be divided.
- You do not need to complete the full estate administration while the correct will and probate route are still unclear.
- You do not need to accept pressure from relatives to act on a copy before the missing-original issue has been checked.
- You can leave non-urgent paperwork sorting until the immediate document search is controlled and recorded.

## Important reassurance
A missing original will is a known probate problem, and there are routes for dealing with it when there is enough evidence. The most useful thing now is to slow the situation down, protect documents, keep a clear search record, and avoid irreversible steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: securing documents, searching for the signed original will, preserving any copy, and avoiding early estate decisions. Later decisions about probate, inheritance tax, disputes, or administering the estate may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, or professional advice. Probate outcomes can depend on the facts, including who last held the original will, whether a later will exists, and whether anyone disputes the position. If there is conflict, suspected destruction of the will, uncertainty about the correct UK process, or significant assets, consider getting advice from a probate solicitor before applying.
