# What to do if… you are told your work email will be deleted soon and you still need access to key records (UK)

## Short answer
Stop attempting to log in and confirm whether the lockout is a technical fault, a security precaution, or a formal suspension. Contact your line manager and IT service desk via personal phone or email to request a written explanation of your current access status.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to bypass security controls using personal email, personal cloud storage, or colleague credentials.
- Do not repeatedly enter passwords or use unofficial password reset links which may trigger permanent security locks.
- Do not delete any files, messages, or browser history on work devices.
- Do not post about the lockout on social media or message groups of colleagues, as this may breach confidentiality or IT policies.
- Do not submit a resignation or send emotive messages to management while the cause of the lockout is unconfirmed.

## What to do now
1. **Document the exact time and nature of the lockout.** 
   - Record the specific systems affected (e.g., Email, VPN, Slack, HR Portal).
   - Take a photograph or screenshot of any error messages or "Account Disabled" notices.
2. **Contact your line manager using an external channel.** 
   - Use your personal phone or email to send a neutral message.
   - Ask: "I have lost access to work systems; can you confirm if this is an IT issue or if there is another reason for the lockout?"
3. **Notify the IT Service Desk or Security Team immediately.** 
   - If you cannot find their number, check the public-facing "Contact Us" section of the company website or your original offer letter.
   - Inform them of the lockout and ask if your account has been flagged for a security incident or a technical error.
   - If you recently clicked a suspicious link or entered credentials on an unusual page, disclose this to the IT team immediately.
4. **Follow official reporting protocols for suspected phishing.** 
   - If you suspect your account was compromised via a scam message, forward any relevant details from your personal account to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.
   - Forward suspicious text messages to **7726**.
5. **Request written confirmation if you are told you are suspended.** 
   - Ask for a letter or email to your personal address confirming the start date of the suspension and whether it is with full pay.
   - Request clarity on "terms of suspension," such as whether you are prohibited from contacting colleagues or clients.
   - Note that under ACAS guidance, suspension should be a neutral act, not a disciplinary penalty.
6. **Ask for status clarity if you suspect termination.** 
   - Request a written statement confirming your employment status and the date of termination.
   - Ask for details on your final pay, including any accrued but untaken holiday pay, and when you will receive your P45.
   - Secure a receipt or written record if you are asked to return company equipment like laptops or ID badges.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to file a grievance or a legal claim.
- You do not need to respond immediately to requests to "sign off" on a resignation or settlement if you are in shock.
- You do not need to contact ACAS immediately; the Early Conciliation period now lasts up to 12 weeks, providing time to seek advice.
- You do not need to explain the situation to your colleagues or professional network right now.

## Important reassurance
A sudden loss of system access is a high-stress event, but it is frequently triggered by automated security protocols, IT maintenance errors, or routine password expirations. Taking a moment to confirm the facts before reacting prevents technical issues from escalating into disciplinary ones. Feeling anxious or confused is a normal response to an abrupt workplace change.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. It does not cover the full legal process for disciplinary hearings, unfair dismissal claims, or complex redundancy procedures.

## Important note
This information is based on UK employment law and ACAS guidance as of March 2026, including phased updates from the Employment Rights Act 2025. Your specific rights depend on your length of service and contract terms. If you are told you are dismissed or suspended, you should consult a qualified legal professional, a trade union representative, or Citizens Advice.
