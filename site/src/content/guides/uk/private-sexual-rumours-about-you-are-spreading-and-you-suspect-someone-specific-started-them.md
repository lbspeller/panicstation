# What to do if… private sexual rumours about you are spreading and you suspect someone specific started them (UK)

## Short answer
Do not confront the person you suspect yet. Slow the spread, save what is already visible without recirculating it, and use the safest relevant route: platform reporting, a workplace or education complaint route, safeguarding, or police if there is danger, threats, stalking, harassment, blackmail, or a pattern.

## Do not do these things
- Do not message, accuse, or confront the person you suspect while you are panicked.
- Do not “clear your name” by posting details of your private life.
- Do not forward screenshots around for proof.
- Do not repost, quote, or joke about the rumour to show people how false it is.
- Do not keep searching alone through every chat or account; if more examples need collecting, ask one trusted person to help.
- Do not threaten retaliation or post identifying details about anyone in anger.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause and choose one ally.** Step away from the thread or conversation for a few minutes. Message one trusted person and say: “Sexual rumours about me are spreading. I need help staying calm and not reacting publicly.”

2. **Privately preserve what is already in front of you.** Save the URL, account names, group name, date, time, and screenshots of the words or posts you can already see. Keep them in one private folder and do not forward them around. If this involves sexual images, intimate images, or anyone who may be under 18, do not repost, send, or keep sharing the image; save links, usernames, dates, and where it appeared, then use a reporting route.

3. **Stop adding to the thread.** Mute the chat, stop replying, and avoid correcting every person individually. If someone asks you directly, use one line: “That rumour is false and I’m dealing with it through the proper channels.” Then stop discussing details.

4. **Use platform or group controls.** Report the post, message, account, or group through the platform’s reporting tools. Block accounts that are contacting you. If it is in a group chat, privately ask an admin or moderator to remove the posts, stop further sharing, and warn members not to repeat it.

5. **If this is at work, use a formal work route.** Contact HR, your manager, or a trade union representative. You can say: “Private sexual rumours about me are being spread. This may be harassment or bullying and I want it handled formally and confidentially.” Ask for immediate protections such as no-contact arrangements, changes to seating or rota, or limits on who can message you about it.

6. **If this is at school or college, ask for safeguarding help.** Ask to speak to the safeguarding lead, pastoral lead, head of year, tutor, or another trusted member of staff. Say: “Sexual rumours about me are spreading and I’m being targeted. I need help stopping the spread and avoiding retaliation.” Ask what can be done today to reduce contact, remove posts, and stop people repeating it.

7. **If this is at university, use student support or the harassment reporting route.** Contact student support, wellbeing, your tutor, students’ union advice service, or the university reporting tool if it has one. Ask for practical steps such as no-contact arrangements, help removing posts, support with classes or accommodation, and a confidential record of what has happened.

8. **Contact police if there is danger, threats, stalking, blackmail, harassment, or a repeated pattern.** If someone is in immediate danger or a crime is happening now, call 999. If it is not an emergency, call 101 or use your local police force’s online reporting route. You do not need to prove who started it before asking for advice or making a report, and you should not delay urgent help just to gather more evidence.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove who started it today, even if you strongly suspect someone.
- You do not need to explain your sexual history, relationships, orientation, or private life to defend yourself.
- You do not need to decide today whether to take legal action.
- You do not need to monitor every mention yourself.
- You do not need to answer people who are trying to pull you into the rumour.

## Important reassurance
Sexual rumours are often meant to create shame, panic, and urgency. Slowing down, keeping proof private, and using a formal route can help you regain control without giving the rumour more attention.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce harm and stabilise the situation. Later decisions about complaints, school or workplace processes, police reports, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
