# What to do if… you receive alerts that accounts are being accessed after a death and you suspect identity misuse (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the situation as an active security breach by immediately securing the email and phone number used for password recoveries, then notify banks and credit agencies to freeze activity.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links in any email or text alert; navigate directly to the service provider's official website or app instead.
- Don’t attempt to "test" the login repeatedly, as this can trigger automated security lockouts that may hinder legitimate recovery efforts.
- Don’t share the deceased person’s full date of birth, middle names, or previous addresses on social media or in public obituaries.
- Don’t assume the alert is a mistake if it specifies a real device name or a location unfamiliar to the deceased.

## What to do now
1. **Document the specific details of the alert.**
   - **Take screenshots of the notification, including any IP addresses, device names, or timestamps shown.**
   - **Start a written log of every phone call or email sent to providers, noting the date, time, and any reference numbers provided.**

2. **Secure the primary recovery accounts.**
   - **Sign in directly to the deceased person's email account (if you have legal access) to change the password and enable multi-factor authentication.**
   - **Contact the mobile network provider immediately if you suspect a SIM swap or unauthorised phone access.**
   - **Request that the network provider place a security block on the SIM to prevent number porting.**

3. **Notify financial institutions of suspected fraud.**
   - **Call the bereavement or fraud departments of any bank or card issuer mentioned in the alerts.**
   - **Tell the provider that the account holder is deceased and that you are seeing unauthorised activity.**
   - **Request an immediate freeze on all online banking access and outward transactions until a full review is conducted.**

4. **Register the death via national notification services.**
   - **Use the Tell Us Once service (available in England, Scotland, and Wales) to notify multiple government departments simultaneously.**
   - **If the death occurred in Northern Ireland, call the Bereavement Service "One Call" on 0800 085 2463.**
   - **Use the Death Notification Service to notify participating private banks and building societies in a single step.**

5. **Apply for identity protection for the deceased.**
   - **Contact the UK's three main credit reference agencies (Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion) to request a "deceased" flag on the credit file.**
   - **Apply for Cifas Protective Registration (specifically for the deceased) to alert lenders to potential impersonation.**
   - **Request a statutory credit report for the deceased to identify any newly opened accounts or recent credit searches.**

6. **Report the incident to the national fraud portal.**
   - **File a report with Report Fraud (formerly Action Fraud) online or by calling 0300 123 2040.**
   - **Keep the crime reference number provided, as banks often require this to process fraud claims.**

7. **Secure physical mail access.**
   - **Ensure that mail at the deceased person's last address is being collected or redirected to a secure location.**
   - **Register the deceased person's details with The Bereavement Register or the Deceased Preference Service to stop marketing mail that could be intercepted.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the person accessing the accounts yourself; this is a matter for the police and fraud teams.
- You do not need to complete the full probate process or finalise the estate today.
- You do not need to contact every single minor subscription or service immediately; prioritise banks, credit agencies, and government bodies.

## Important reassurance
Receiving security alerts for a loved one who has passed is distressing, but these automated systems are designed to help you catch misuse early. Taking these steps quickly is a standard part of modern estate administration and does not mean you have failed in your duties as an executor or next of kin.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilisation for suspected identity misuse. Resolving long-term credit errors or reclaiming stolen funds may require further legal or specialist financial assistance.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Procedures for reporting death and fraud vary by jurisdiction and institution; always follow the specific instructions provided by official bereavement teams or the police.
