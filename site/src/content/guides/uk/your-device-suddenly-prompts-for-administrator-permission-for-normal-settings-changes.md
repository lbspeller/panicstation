# What to do if… your device suddenly prompts for administrator permission for normal settings changes (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as an active account takeover: immediately use the provider's official recovery flow from a familiar device, then lock the account by changing the password and signing out of all other sessions.

## Do not do these things
- Don't use links in "security alert" emails or texts to sign in — manually type the provider's official web address into your browser.
- Don't keep entering old passwords if you are failing — repeated failed attempts can trigger a temporary IP or account lockout.
- Don't "test" or interact with any recovery emails or phone numbers added by the attacker.
- Don't pay any third-party "recovery services" found on social media or search engine ads — these are frequently secondary scams.
- Don't assume the account is safe just because you changed the password; persistence mechanisms like auto-forwarding may still be active.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and switch to a trusted device and network (2 minutes).** Use a device you have previously used to sign in to this account and stay on a known home or work Wi-Fi network if possible.
   - Using a "familiar" device and IP address significantly increases the success rate of automated recovery algorithms.
2. **Access the provider’s official recovery portal immediately (now).** Use the "I can't sign in" or "Forgot password" links on the main login page.
   - If the password was changed, look for a link saying "I didn't make this change" in the original notification email sent to your old recovery address.
3. **If you regain access, secure the account in a specific sequence.**
   - **Change the password** to a long, unique phrase you have never used elsewhere.
   - **Sign out of all other sessions** by selecting the "Sign out of all devices" or "Remove all trusted devices" option in security settings.
   - **Remove any unknown recovery details** (emails or phone numbers) and add back only those you currently control.
   - **Check for "Persistence"** by looking for new email forwarding rules, filters that delete incoming mail, or delegated access permissions.
4. **Enable or reset two-step verification (10 minutes).** If it was already on, the attacker likely bypassed it or changed the secondary method.
   - Re-enable it using an app-based authenticator if possible.
   - Generate a new set of **backup/recovery codes** and save them physically or in a secure offline location.
5. **Contact your mobile network provider if you suspect a SIM swap.** If your phone lost signal around the same time your account was accessed, call your network provider from a different phone.
   - Ask them to check for unauthorised SIM changes or porting requests.
   - Request "High-Level Protection" or a "Port-Out PIN" to prevent future unauthorised transfers.
6. **Report the incident to the appropriate authorities.**
   - If you live in **England, Wales, or Northern Ireland**, report the cyber crime at `reportfraud.police.uk` (the successor to Action Fraud).
   - If you live in **Scotland**, report the incident by calling **101** to speak with Police Scotland.
7. **Forward any phishing messages that may have caused the breach.**
   - Forward suspicious emails to `report@phishing.gov.uk`.
   - Forward suspicious text messages to **7726** (a free reporting service used by UK mobile providers).

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify exactly how the breach happened before you secure the account.
- You do not need to notify the attacker or try to "confront" them via email.
- You do not need to delete the account today unless you are instructed to by your provider’s security team.
- You do not need to scan every single device for malware immediately; focus on securing the account and financial links first.

## Important reassurance
Regaining control of an account where recovery details have been changed is difficult but often possible through official "account recovery forms" that ask for historical data (such as old passwords or creation dates). It is a standard security event, and providers have automated systems designed specifically to handle these takeover attempts.

## Scope note
This guidance is for first-step stabilization and regaining account access. It does not cover the legal recovery of funds or technical forensic analysis of a compromised device.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or technical advice. Account recovery processes are governed by the specific terms of service of your provider (e.g., Google, Microsoft, Yahoo). If you believe your identity or bank accounts are at immediate risk, contact your bank and the police directly.
