# What to do if… you lose access to your financial accounts because your phone number changed or SIM was swapped (UK)

## Short answer
Block your bank accounts immediately using the 159 hotline or official fraud lines, then contact your mobile network provider to regain control of your phone number.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep attempting to log in or reset passwords, as this can lead to permanent account lockouts.
- Don’t follow instructions from any "security" call or text that contacted you first; always hang up and call official numbers you have found yourself.
- Don’t use SMS for any security verification while your number is unstable or showing "No Service."
- Don’t move money to any "safe" or "new" account suggested by a caller, even if they claim to be from the police or your bank.
- Don’t delete any suspicious Porting Authorisation Code (PAC) texts or service alerts, as these are evidence for the investigation.

## What to do now
1. **Call your bank immediately to secure your money.** Use a different, trusted phone or a landline.
   - Dial 159 to see if your bank is part of the secure hotline scheme.
   - If not, use the fraud number on the back of your debit/credit card or the bank’s official website.
2. **Tell the bank specifically that you suspect "SIM swap fraud" or "unauthorised number porting."** Ask them to place a temporary block on all outbound payments, new payee setups, and changes to your contact details.
3. **Request that the bank removes SMS as a verification method for your account.** Ask for an alternative, such as a physical card reader, in-app approval on a different device, or in-person verification at a branch.
4. **Contact your mobile network provider’s fraud department.** Report that your number has been hijacked or ported without your permission.
   - Ask them to disable the current SIM and restore service to a new SIM card in your possession.
   - Ask if a PAC (Porting Authorisation Code) was issued and to which network your number was sent.
5. **Report the crime to the relevant national authority.** This creates a crime reference number which banks often require for fraud claims.
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland: Contact Action Fraud (National Fraud and Cyber Crime Reporting Centre) on 0300 123 2040 or online.
   - In Scotland: Call Police Scotland on 101.
6. **Change your primary email password from a secure device.** Use a strong, unique password (such as three random words) and enable an authenticator app rather than SMS verification. Attackers often use hijacked phone numbers to reset email passwords first.
7. **Report the suspicious messages to 7726.** If you still have access to the texts, forward any suspicious PAC requests or network alerts to 7726 (the UK-wide reporting service for mobile scams).

## What can wait
- You don’t need to determine how the attackers got your data right now; focus on closing the access points.
- You don’t need to cancel every direct debit immediately; focus on blocking large outbound transfers and account changes.
- You don’t need to buy a new phone handset; the issue is usually with the SIM/account, not the physical hardware.

## Important reassurance
Feeling overwhelmed is a common response to a sudden loss of account access. This type of fraud relies on speed, but by contacting your bank and mobile network immediately, you are taking the most effective steps to stop further unauthorised activity.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization only. Long-term identity theft protection and formal financial disputes may require further specialist advice once your accounts are secure.

## Important note
This information is for immediate guidance and does not constitute legal, financial, or cybersecurity advice. If you are in immediate physical danger, call 999.
