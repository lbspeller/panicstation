# What to do if… you smell a strong fuel or solvent odour in a garage or utility area and cannot identify the source (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible flammable vapour. Avoid anything that could spark, get people and pets into fresh air, and call for help from a safe place, especially if there is any chance it could be gas.

## Do not do these things
- Do not switch lights or appliances on or off, including extractor fans, plug sockets, entry buzzers, doorbells, or a powered garage door.
- Do not use a phone in the affected area; only call once you are outside or clearly away from the smell.
- Do not smoke, use naked flames, light candles, or strike matches.
- Do not start a car, run an engine, use power tools, or operate anything that could spark.
- Do not open, pour away, mop up, or move suspected fuel, solvent, paint, or chemical containers while fumes are strong.
- Do not sniff close-up or stay inside to find the source.
- Do not go back inside if anyone feels dizzy, sick, confused, breathless, unusually sleepy, or has a headache.

## What to do now
1. **Leave the affected area without creating a spark.** Walk out calmly. Tell others to leave too, without touching switches, phones, appliances, vehicles, or powered doors in the affected area.
2. **Get everyone into fresh air.** Move people and pets outdoors or well away from the garage or utility area. Keep children away from the smell.
3. **Ventilate only if it is quick and clearly safe.** If you can open an external door or window without using electrics, without leaning into fumes, and without delaying your exit, do that. If not, skip this step and stay out.
4. **If a garage door can be opened manually from a safe position, open it.** Do not use a motor, remote, wall switch, or powered opener.
5. **If it could be gas, or you are unsure, call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999 from a safe place.** Follow the operator’s instructions.
6. **If there is immediate danger, call 999.** This includes fire, a visible spill spreading near ignition sources, someone collapsing, severe breathing difficulty, a seizure, or symptoms that are getting worse.
7. **If you can safely reach the gas emergency control valve, turn off the gas at the meter.** Do this only if you do not have to re-enter a smelly space, use electrical switches, or enter a cellar or basement. If you are unsure, stay out and wait for emergency advice.
8. **If anyone may have inhaled harmful fumes, keep them in fresh air.** Call 999 for severe or worsening symptoms. Call NHS 111 if you are unsure whether the exposure is harmful and the situation is no longer an immediate emergency.
9. **Keep the area clear until it has been made safe.** If you are in a flat or shared building, alert neighbours or building staff from outside if you can do so without going back into the affected area or using entry buzzers there.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact source right now.
- You do not need to clean up, move containers, or air the space out fully.
- You do not need to decide about insurance, repairs, landlord issues, or who is at fault today.
- You do not need to re-enter for belongings unless emergency services say it is safe.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to take an unknown strong fuel or solvent odour seriously. Leaving, avoiding sparks, and calling from fresh air are sensible protective steps, even if the source later turns out to be minor.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce fire, explosion, and inhalation risk. Finding the source, cleaning up, repairs, and decisions about storage may need qualified help later.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, fire safety, engineering, or professional advice. If the smell is strong, spreading, or making anyone unwell, prioritise fresh air and emergency help.
