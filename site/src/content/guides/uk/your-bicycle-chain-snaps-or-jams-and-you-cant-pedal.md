# What to do if… your bicycle chain snaps or jams and you can’t pedal (UK)

## Short answer
Stop pedalling immediately, brake smoothly to a halt, and move yourself and the bicycle completely off the carriageway or path before attempting to touch the drivetrain.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t force the pedals if you feel resistance; this can pull a jammed chain into the spokes or bend the derailleur hanger beyond repair.
- Don’t put your fingers near the chainrings, cassette, or pulley wheels while the bike is moving or the rear wheel is spinning.
- Don’t attempt to ride on the pavement to reach home; under Highway Code Rule 64, you MUST NOT cycle on a pavement unless it is a designated shared-use track.
- Don’t stop in a live traffic lane, in the middle of a narrow cycle track, or on a blind bend.
- Don’t leave broken metal links or debris on the road if it is safe to retrieve them, as these can cause punctures or hazards for other road users.

## What to do now
1. **Brake smoothly and steer toward a safe exit point.** **Keep your hands on the handlebars, stop trying to pedal, and use both brakes to slow down while looking for a verge, lay-by, or pavement where you can safely dismount.**
2. **Move the bicycle entirely off the road or cycle path.** **Once stationary, lift or push the bike to a position where you are not obstructing other cyclists or vehicles, ensuring you are visible to oncoming traffic.**
3. **Switch on your lights if visibility is low or it is after sunset.** **In accordance with Rule 60 of the Highway Code, ensure your front white and rear red lights are active so other road users can identify you as a stationary hazard.**
4. **Inspect the drivetrain for a mechanical lock-up without using force.** **Check if the chain has dropped between the frame and the small chainring, wedged between the cassette and the spokes, or snapped entirely.**
   - If the chain is loose and trailing, wrap it around the frame or put it in a bag to prevent it from tangling in the wheels while you push.
   - If the rear wheel is locked by a jammed chain, do not try to wheel the bike forward; lift the rear of the bike to move it.
5. **Decide if a roadside reset is safe and possible.** **Only attempt to reseat the chain if it is simply "off" the cogs and not wedged tightly or twisted; use a stick or wear gloves to avoid heavy grease and sharp teeth.**
6. **Utilise a "safe pause" to arrange alternative transport if the chain is snapped or wedged.** **If you cannot perform a repair with a chain tool and "quick link," use your phone to locate the nearest railway station or call for a recovery service or taxi that accepts bicycles.**
7. **Contact 101 only if your breakdown creates a significant, unmovable hazard to other road users.** **If you are safely off the road, the police are not required; however, if you are stranded in a dangerous location (such as a dual carriageway with no verge), call 999.**
8. **Follow British Transport Police (BTP) 2026 safety guidance if stopping at a station.** **Move to a well-lit area with CCTV coverage and wait in a designated passenger area rather than on the platform edge while arranging help.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the root cause of the failure (such as chain wear or gear misalignment) right now.
- You do not need to clean the grease from your hands or the bike until you have reached a safe indoor location.
- You can defer the decision to replace the chain or the entire drivetrain until the bicycle is inspected by a professional mechanic.
- You do not need to worry about the legality of your positioning if you have moved to the nearest available safe spot to prevent an accident.

## Important reassurance
A mechanical failure like a snapped chain is a common cycling incident. Once you have moved away from the flow of traffic, the immediate danger is over. Being a stationary pedestrian wheeling a bike is a manageable situation, and most UK urban areas have transport options that accommodate bicycles.

## Scope note
This guide focuses on immediate roadside stabilization and safety. It does not provide technical instructions for permanent mechanical repairs or drivetrain maintenance.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only and does not replace professional mechanical or legal advice. Cyclists are subject to the Highway Code; failure to comply with "MUST NOT" rules can lead to prosecution. If you are on a high-speed road or feel physically at risk, prioritize your personal safety over the recovery of the bicycle.
