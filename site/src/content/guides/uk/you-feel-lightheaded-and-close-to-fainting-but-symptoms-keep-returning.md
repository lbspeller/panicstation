# What to do if… you feel lightheaded and close to fainting but symptoms keep returning (UK)

## Short answer
Lie down flat immediately and raise your legs above the level of your heart to increase blood flow to the brain. If symptoms keep returning, contact NHS 111 or your GP for a same-day assessment, even if you feel better between episodes.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to walk, use stairs, or "push through" the feeling to reach another room.
- Do not stand up quickly or move your head suddenly to see if the feeling has gone.
- Do not drive a car, ride a bike, or operate machinery until you have been medically cleared.
- Do not sit with your head between your knees, as this can restrict breathing and increase fall risk.
- Do not take extra doses of blood pressure or heart medication without clinical advice.
- Do not stay in a hot environment or take a hot bath / shower while feeling unsteady.
- Do not ignore symptoms just because you have not actually lost consciousness.

## What to do now
1. **Lie down flat on your back immediately and raise your legs.**  
   - Use cushions, a chair, or a sofa arm to keep your feet higher than your head.  
   - If you are more than 28 weeks pregnant, lie on your **left side** instead of your back.
2. **Call 999 if specific emergency "red flags" occur.**  
   - Someone faints and does not wake up within 1 minute.  
   - There is chest pain, shortness of breath, or a very fast / irregular heartbeat.  
   - There are signs of a stroke (facial drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty).  
   - A seizure or fit occurs (shaking or jerking).  
   - The faint occurred during exercise or while already lying down.
3. **Cool your environment and loosen restrictive clothing.**  
   - Open a window for fresh air or turn on a fan.  
   - Loosen collars, ties, belts, or any tight waistbands.
4. **Sip water slowly once you are steady and alert.**  
   - Do not attempt to drink if you are still actively feeling like you might lose consciousness.  
   - Avoid caffeine or sugary "energy" drinks for now.
5. **Get someone to stay with you in the room.**  
   - If you are alone, call a neighbor or friend and ask them to stay on the line until you feel stable.  
   - Keep your phone on the floor next to you.
6. **Move in slow stages when you eventually try to get up.**  
   - Only attempt to move after the lightheadedness has completely passed for several minutes.  
   - Move from lying to sitting, wait 2 minutes, then stand up slowly while holding onto furniture.
7. **Note the details of the episodes for a medical professional.**  
   - Record what you were doing right before it started (e.g., stood up quickly, skipped a meal, felt hot).  
   - Note any other symptoms like palpitations, nausea, or "greyed out" vision.
8. **Contact NHS 111 for same-day advice for recurrent symptoms.**  
   - Use the 111 online service or call 111 to report that your near-fainting symptoms are returning.  
   - If you are pregnant, contact your maternity triage or midwife for urgent review.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the underlying cause (such as blood pressure, heart rhythm, or blood sugar) yourself.
- You do not need to review your long-term medication plan until you speak with a clinician.
- You do not need to decide on lifestyle changes or salt intake adjustments today.

## Important reassurance
Feeling like you are about to faint is a physical response to a temporary drop in blood flow to the brain. Lying flat and raising your legs is the most effective way to reverse this process quickly. It is normal to feel tired, shaky, or anxious after these episodes occur.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only for managing acute, recurrent near-fainting. It does not replace a clinical diagnosis or long-term management plan for conditions like syncope or PoTS.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional medical advice. If symptoms are severe, worsening, or you are concerned about your safety, call 999 or go to the nearest Accident & Emergency (A&E) department.
