# What to do if… your child tells you someone online is asking them to keep a secret that feels sexual (UK)
## Short answer

Treat this as a safeguarding concern. Stay calm, thank your child for telling you, and help them stop contact without blaming them or pressing for every detail at once.

## Do not do these things

- Do not tell your child they caused this or should have known better.
- Do not make them keep explaining the story over and over right now.
- Do not contact the other person yourself to warn, threaten, or “test” them.
- Do not ask your child to keep chatting to gather more information.
- Do not delete the messages straight away if you may want support or to report it.
- Do not forward, post, or show sexual material involving a child to other people.
- Do not promise absolute secrecy to your child if someone may be at risk.
- Do not force big decisions about reporting in the same conversation unless there is immediate danger.

## What to do now

1. **Slow the moment down and believe your child.** Say something simple like: “I’m glad you told me. You are not in trouble. We’ll handle this together.”

2. **Stop the contact from continuing.** Sit with your child while they stop replying. If it is safe to do so, save the account name and key details before blocking or using the platform’s report tool, because blocking can sometimes make messages harder to find later.

3. **Check the account and the app without interrogating your child.** Ask them to show you the username, profile, platform, messages, images, gifts, requests, threats, and whether the person asked to move to another app.

4. **Preserve only the basics if you may want support or to report later.** Take screenshots of the profile, username, platform, date, and key messages, then keep them somewhere private. Do not ask your child to recreate, resend, or keep receiving sexual material.

5. **Check for immediate risk.** Ask only what you need to know now: whether the person knows your child’s real name, school, phone number, address, location, or has asked to meet, send images, move apps, or keep things hidden from adults. If there is immediate danger, call **999**.

6. **Use a UK child-protection reporting route for online sexual grooming concerns.** You can make a report to **CEOP** if you are concerned a child is being sexually abused or groomed online. You can also contact the police on **101** if there is no immediate emergency but you need police help.

7. **If sexual images may have been shared by or of your child, use a child-specific removal route.** In the UK, **Report Remove** can help under-18s report sexual images or videos of themselves and seek removal support.

8. **Get specialist support for your child and for you.** If you need help thinking clearly about what to say next, contact the **NSPCC Helpline**. If your child wants confidential support, **Childline** can support young people.

9. **Make the next few hours quieter and safer.** Stay physically near your child if you can, keep the device with an adult overnight if needed, turn off location sharing if it is on, and pause non-essential online activity until you have a clearer picture.

## What can wait

You do not need to decide right now whether this was “serious enough,” what the person intended, or what longer-term online rules should be. You also do not need to resolve every detail tonight.

## Important reassurance

Children are often targeted through secrecy, praise, flattery, gifts, threats, shame, or sexualised attention. Freezing, going along with it, or not telling straight away does not mean your child wanted this or caused it.

## Scope note

This is first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, police, clinical, therapeutic, or professional advice. In the UK, online sexual grooming concerns about a child can be reported to CEOP, and immediate danger should go to 999. If details are unclear, it is still reasonable to treat the situation cautiously and get support.
