# What to do if… you see someone attempting to enter a shared building behind residents without permission (UK)

## Short answer
Do not grant access; step back, allow the door to close and lock, and move to a safe position away from the entrance. If you feel threatened or the person forces entry, call 999 immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hold the door open or use your key / fob to grant entry to anyone you do not know.
- Do not ask the individual to "prove" they live there or show you their keys, as this can escalate a confrontation.
- Do not enter enclosed spaces such as lifts or stairwells with an unknown person who has followed you in.
- Do not physically block the doorway or attempt to restrain someone trying to push past.
- Do not follow an unauthorized person into the building to "see where they are going" if they successfully enter.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize your physical distance from the entrance.** Step back and allow the communal door to close and latch fully. If you are already inside, move toward the exit or a well-lit lobby area rather than deeper into the residential corridors.
2. **Use a neutral, standard boundary if spoken to.** From a safe distance, state: "I cannot let anyone in / building policy requires all residents to use their own keys." Say this once and then stop engaging.
3. **Redirect the person to the official access systems.** Point toward the intercom, entryphone, or concierge desk (if available) so they can contact the resident they claim to be visiting without your intervention.
4. **Intervene indirectly if you see another resident about to let them in.** From a safe distance, remind the other resident of the policy: "Please don't hold the door—the management requires everyone to use their own fob."
5. **Call 999 if the person forces entry or behaves aggressively.** Move to a location where you can lock a door between yourself and the individual (such as your own flat, a shop, or your car). Provide the operator with the exact address and a description of the person’s appearance and location.
6. **Report non-emergency security breaches via digital tools.** Use the "Met Engage" tool (in London) or the "Contact Us" online forms for Police Scotland or your local force to share intelligence about repeated unauthorized access.
7. **Document the incident only when you are in a secure location.** Note the time, a description of the person's clothing, and which specific entrance was used.
8. **Notify building management or the Residents' Association immediately.** Report if the door closer is too slow or if the lock failed to engage, as these are technical vulnerabilities that require urgent repair.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the person had "criminal intent" or was simply visiting a neighbor.
- You do not need to solve the building's overall security strategy right now.
- You do not need to confront the person or ask for their identification.

## Important reassurance
It is standard security protocol in shared buildings to refuse entry to those without their own access devices. While it may feel socially awkward to let a door close on someone, maintaining the "one-key-one-person" rule is a recognized safety boundary that protects all residents.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and harm prevention during a potential unauthorized entry incident. It does not replace formal police investigations or long-term building security management.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. While "tailgating" is now a specific criminal offence at designated sporting events under the Unauthorised Entry to Football Matches Act 2026, in residential settings, unauthorized entry may be treated as civil trespass or associated with other criminal activities. Always prioritize your physical safety.
