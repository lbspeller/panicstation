# What to do if… you are being targeted or bullied in a hostile work group chat (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before replying: mute the chat, save a small amount of evidence, and move the issue to a calmer work route such as your manager, HR, or a union representative.

## Do not do these things
- Do not fight back in the chat to defend yourself point by point; it can escalate and can be screenshot out of context.
- Do not delete messages or try to clean up the thread before saving what you need.
- Do not forward screenshots widely or post them online; it may breach workplace rules and inflame the situation.
- Do not agree to a private one-to-one argument with the most hostile person while you are stressed.
- Do not make legal, disciplinary, or personal threats in the chat, even as a bluff.
- Do not assume it cannot be a workplace issue just because it is on WhatsApp, Signal, or another non-work platform; if it involves colleagues, work matters, threats, bullying, or harassment, your employer may still need to deal with it.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safety pause.** Mute notifications, put your phone down for 2 minutes, and decide not to reply while your heart rate is up.
2. **Save a clean record quietly and securely.** Take screenshots showing:
   - the group name, participants, and your addition to the chat
   - the hostile messages with dates and times
   - any threats, intimidation, discriminatory comments, or repeated public criticism
   
   Keep copies minimal, store them somewhere private and secure, and avoid downloading restricted work material or forwarding screenshots wider than needed.
3. **Stop feeding the escalation.** If you must respond, use one short boundary message, then stop:
   - “I’m not comfortable with the tone here. Please keep work discussion respectful. I’m stepping back now and will follow up separately.”
4. **Move it to one responsible route.** Choose one of these:
   - your line manager
   - HR
   - a union representative or staff representative, if you have one
   
   Send a calm note saying what happened, why it concerns you, and attach 2 to 4 key screenshots rather than the whole thread.
5. **Ask for one specific action.** For example:
   - “Please ask the group to stop using that chat for work matters.”
   - “Please remove me or set clear boundaries for conduct.”
   - “Please confirm how I should raise this through the correct workplace route.”
6. **Check the official work route once.** Look up your employer’s bullying, harassment, dignity at work, social media, IT, or grievance policy so you use the right channel and wording. You do not need to start a formal grievance today.
7. **If you feel unsafe about going into work.** Tell your manager, HR, or another appropriate senior person before you arrive, and ask about practical adjustments such as avoiding a particular meeting, changing seating, working remotely for a short period, or having someone with you.
8. **If there are credible threats of violence, stalking, harassment, or hate crime.** Treat safety as the priority. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If it is not an emergency, you can contact police on 101 or use an online police reporting route.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to submit a formal grievance.
- Working out whether it meets a legal definition or whether you have a case.
- Confronting the main person involved.
- Writing a long statement or compiling every screenshot.
- Deciding whether to change jobs.
- Explaining yourself to everyone in the chat.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel shaky, angry, or humiliated when a group piles on digitally. Pausing, saving a careful record, and moving the issue out of the chat are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and prevent irreversible mistakes. If the behaviour continues, you may need specialist employment advice, union support, or formal support through your workplace processes.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. Workplace policies and the safest next step can vary by employer and role. If you feel in immediate danger, prioritise safety and seek urgent help.
