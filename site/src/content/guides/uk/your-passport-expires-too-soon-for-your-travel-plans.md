# What to do if… your passport expires too soon for your travel plans (UK)

## Short answer
Check the passport rule for every country you will enter or transit, using official entry requirements and your exact passport issue and expiry dates. If your passport falls short, focus on one safe fix now: urgent renewal if eligible, changing the route, changing the dates, or not travelling.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume “my passport is still in date” means it will be accepted for entry.
- Do not check only the final destination if your journey includes a layover, border crossing, cruise stop, or separate ticket.
- Do not rely on a friend’s recent trip, an old screenshot, or a search-result summary.
- Do not book non-refundable changes until you have checked the rule against your exact dates.
- Do not assume airport staff can override document rules at check-in.
- Do not apply for a new passport and forget that visas, eVisas, ETAs, airline details, and booking profiles may still hold your old passport number.

## What to do now
1. Write down the dates that matter. Use your passport expiry date, passport date of issue, nationality, departure date, arrival date, planned exit date, and every country where you may enter, transit, pass border control, or change airports.

2. Check the official entry requirements for each country. Use GOV.UK foreign travel advice and open the “Entry requirements” section for your destination and any transit country. If the page tells you to check with an embassy, consulate, or local authority, do that before relying on the trip.

3. Cross-check the same route through the IATA Travel Centre. Airlines commonly use Timatic-based document checks, so this can show how your passport may be assessed at check-in. If GOV.UK, IATA, the destination authority, or your airline appear to disagree, treat the strictest rule as the one to solve before travel.

4. Contact your airline or travel agent with the exact details. Ask: “With this passport issue date, expiry date, nationality, and full itinerary, will I be accepted for check-in and boarding?” Save the chat transcript or email, but do not treat it as a guarantee.

5. Choose the quickest realistic fix. If you are in the UK, check whether you are eligible for HM Passport Office urgent services. The 1 day Premium service is for eligible adult renewals, including passports issued after 31 December 2001, and the new passport is normally ready to collect 4 hours after the appointment. The 1 week Fast Track service can cover more application types and the passport is delivered 1 week after the appointment. Appointment availability and eligibility matter, so check before making travel changes around it.

6. If renewal will not work in time, reduce the trip to the safest workable option. This may mean moving the travel dates, changing the routing to avoid a stricter transit point, removing a stop, postponing the trip, or not travelling.

7. If you are already abroad, check whether this is now an emergency travel document problem. A UK emergency travel document may be available if you are outside the UK, need to travel urgently, cannot use or renew your UK passport before travel, and meet the eligibility rules. It is usually for one single or return journey and can only cover travel through a limited number of countries, so check the route, destination, transit rules, and airline acceptance before relying on it.

8. After any renewal, replacement, or route change, update the things linked to your passport. Check visas, eVisas, ETAs, Advance Passenger Information, airline booking details, travel agent records, cruise records, and any saved traveller profiles.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, claim compensation, or argue about fairness.
- You do not need to rebuild the whole trip before confirming the passport rule.
- You do not need the perfect renewal method; you need the fastest safe option that fits the rule and your travel date.
- You do not need to solve every future passport issue now.

## Important reassurance
This is a common travel shock because passport expiry, passport issue date, destination rules, transit rules, and airline checks can all matter at the same time. Turning it into a short document check and one practical fix can stop the panic from becoming rushed, expensive decisions.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce the risk of being refused boarding or refused entry. Later decisions about refunds, insurance, complaints, complex nationality questions, or visa disputes may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, immigration, or professional travel advice. Entry and boarding rules can depend on nationality, passport type, transit route, airline checks, and the rules in force on the day you travel.
