# What to do if… you are asked to sign a confidentiality agreement about a sexual misconduct complaint (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign on the spot. Take a copy away if you can, pause the conversation, and get independent advice before agreeing to any confidentiality wording.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign because you feel rushed, cornered, tired, shaky, or desperate to end the meeting.
- Do not rely on “it’s standard” or “it will not be used against you” unless the wording clearly says what is allowed.
- Do not sign if you have not had time to read it, take advice, and ask for changes in writing.
- Do not accept wording that appears to cut you off from legal advice, medical care, counselling, victim support, or a union representative.
- Do not store your only copy or notes on a workplace device or account if you are worried about privacy.
- Do not post details publicly or send the agreement widely while you are unsure what it restricts.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the signing.** Say: “I’m not able to sign today. I need time to read this properly and get independent advice.” If the pressure continues, end the meeting as calmly as you can and ask for the document by email.

2. **Take or request a full copy.** Ask for every page, including schedules, side letters, and any policy it refers to. Do not rely on a summary.

3. **Clarify what the document is.** Ask in writing: “Is this a non-disclosure agreement, a confidentiality clause, a settlement agreement, a COT3, or part of an internal investigation process?” The route matters.

4. **If it is a settlement agreement, do not sign until you have independent advice.** In Great Britain, Acas says a settlement agreement must be in writing, relate to a specific complaint or claim, require advice from a relevant independent adviser, have an insured adviser, name that adviser, and state that the legal conditions are met. If you are being pushed to sign without that advice, pause and get help.

5. **Check the confidentiality wording for safe exceptions.** Before signing, ask an independent adviser to check whether the wording clearly lets you speak to:
   - a solicitor, barrister, or other relevant independent adviser
   - your union representative, if you have one
   - police, if you choose to report a crime
   - medical professionals, counsellors, sexual violence support services, or victim support services
   - close family or other support people, where the law or the agreement allows it

6. **Be careful with broad wording.** If it says you must not discuss the matter “with anyone”, “in any circumstances”, or “forever”, ask for the wording to be narrowed before you consider signing. A confidentiality clause should be clear about what can be shared and with whom.

7. **If the complaint may involve a crime, ask specifically about permitted disclosures.** An NDA cannot stop a person in England, Scotland or Wales from reporting a crime to the police. Wider sharing for advice and support has specific rules, especially for victims of crime in England and Wales, so ask your adviser before sharing outside the listed safe routes.

8. **If this involves an English university or higher education provider, raise that immediately.** Acas says English universities and other higher education providers cannot use an NDA or confidentiality clause to stop workers disclosing sexual misconduct, abuse, harassment, or other bullying or harassment. This rule came into effect on 1 August 2025.

9. **Make a private, minimal note of what happened today.** In a safe place, write down who asked you to sign, when it happened, what deadline was given, what pressure was used, what you asked for, and how they replied.

10. **Keep what you already have without investigating.** Save relevant emails, messages, drafts, calendar invites, and screenshots somewhere safe if you can do that without increasing risk or distress. You do not need to gather more evidence right now.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to report to the police, start a formal legal process, leave your job, or settle.
- You do not need to negotiate wording while you are distressed.
- You do not need to answer questions about what outcome you want before you have advice.
- You do not need to accept a same-day deadline just because someone presents it as final.

## Important reassurance
Pausing before signing is reasonable. A confidentiality agreement connected to sexual misconduct can affect who you feel able to speak to, so taking time and getting independent advice is a protective step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: slow the process, keep access to support, and avoid being boxed in by wording you have not had checked. Later decisions may need specialist legal, employment, union, or sexual violence support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe right now, move to a safer place if you can and contact emergency services.
