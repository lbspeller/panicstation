# What to do if… you are told a warrant may be issued if you do not respond by a deadline (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all engagement with the message, do not pay any money, and verify the claim independently using the official GOV.UK "Find a court or tribunal" service. If the threat involves a real ongoing case or bail, contact your solicitor immediately via their verified office number.

## Do not do these things
- Do **not** make any payment to "cancel," "stop," or "clear" a warrant, especially via bank transfer, gift cards, or cryptocurrency.
- Do **not** press "1" or follow any automated prompts during a phone call claiming to be from HM Courts and Tribunals Service (HMCTS).
- Do **not** call back the number that contacted you or use any contact details provided in a suspicious email, text, or letter.
- Do **not** share your National Insurance number, bank details, or date of birth to "verify your identity" with an unsolicited caller.
- Do **not** ignore the message entirely if you are currently involved in a court case; instead, verify its status through official channels.

## What to do now
1. **End the communication immediately and record the details.** Hang up the phone, close the email, or put down the letter.
   - Note the name of the agency claimed (e.g., HMCTS, National Crime Agency, or a specific local court).
   - Note any reference number or specific deadline mentioned.
2. **Verify the caller ID or sender address through official guidance.**
   - Be aware that HMCTS will never call you to ask for payment from **0300 0132 088** (this is an inbound-only number).
   - Recognize that official agencies do not use WhatsApp to contact the public about warrants.
3. **Use the official GOV.UK "Find a court or tribunal" service to get a verified phone number.**
   - Search for the specific court named in the message.
   - Call that court directly and ask the staff to verify the reference number or the status of any warrants in your name.
4. **Call 159 if the message demanded a bank transfer or immediate payment.** This service connects you directly to your bank's fraud department to verify if a payment request is legitimate.
5. **Contact a criminal defence solicitor if the message relates to an existing criminal case or bail.**
   - Do not use the solicitor's number from the message; look up their firm independently.
   - Tell them: "I have received an urgent notice about a warrant with a deadline of [date]. Can you verify if this is a genuine court requirement?"
6. **Report the contact to the appropriate national service.**
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland: Report the incident to **Report Fraud** (the national reporting centre) online or by calling 0300 123 2040.
   - In Scotland: Report the incident to **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
   - Forward suspicious text messages to **7726** (a free service to report smishing).
7. **Notify your bank if you have already shared financial information.** Ask them to freeze your accounts and monitor for unauthorized transactions immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare a legal defence or explanation before verifying if the warrant exists.
- You do not need to contact the police to "surrender" until a legal professional or the court has confirmed the warrant is real.
- You do not need to gather evidence for your original case right now.

## Important reassurance
Threats of immediate arrest are a primary tactic used by scammers to trigger a "fight or flight" response. Taking ten minutes to verify a claim through an official GOV.UK website is a safe and standard procedure. Genuine court and police processes allow for verification through established legal representatives or court staff.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling unexpected legal threats. It does not provide legal advice for active criminal proceedings or immigration matters.

## Important note
Government agencies like HMCTS and the police follow strict procedural rules for issuing warrants. They do not demand immediate payment over the phone to prevent arrest. If you believe someone is physically at your door or you are in immediate danger, call 999.
