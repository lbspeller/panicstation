# What to do if… you hear someone trying keys or a keycard on your door while you are inside (UK)

## Short answer
Do not open the door. Use the locks and latches already fitted, move away from the doorway, and call 999 now if someone is still trying to get in or you feel unsafe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door to check.
- Do not put the chain on and open the door through the gap.
- Do not go into the hallway or communal area to confront them.
- Do not shout details such as “I’m alone” or “I don’t have my phone”.
- Do not stand close to the door, letterbox, glass panel, or window where you can be seen or reached.
- Do not spend time filming or looking for proof if you need help now.
- Do not buzz anyone into the building unless you are sure who they are.

## What to do now
1. Lock the door using the locks, bolts, chain, bar latch, or secondary lock already fitted. If you have a door-entry system, do not release the main door.
2. Move away from the front door. Go to a room further inside if you can, close the room door, and keep yourself out of line with the front door, letterbox, or any glass panel.
3. Bring your phone, keys, and shoes with you. If children, guests, or vulnerable people are with you, bring them into the same safer room and keep everyone quiet.
4. Call 999 if the person keeps trying the lock or keycard, rattles the handle, pushes the door, tries other nearby doors, refuses to leave, or you feel in danger. Say: “Someone is trying keys or a keycard on my door and I am inside.” Give your full address, flat number, floor, building entrance details, and what you can hear.
5. If you cannot safely speak, call 999 anyway and stay connected. Listen to the operator. If you can, cough, tap, or answer quietly. If you are on a mobile and you are prompted, press 55 so the call can be transferred to police.
6. If you can check without moving close to the door or being seen, use a peephole, door camera, intercom screen, or window from a safe position. Note only quick details: number of people, clothing, direction they went, sounds, and whether they tried other doors.
7. If your building has a concierge, security desk, warden, or on-site staff, contact them after calling 999 if the situation is urgent. Ask them to check the corridor safely and preserve door-entry or CCTV information, not to confront anyone.
8. Stay away from the door after the noise stops. Do not open it until police, building staff, or another trusted person has confirmed it is safe, or until you are clearly no longer at risk.

## What can wait
- Working out whether it was a mistake, a neighbour, a landlord, a delivery worker, or an attempted break-in.
- Reviewing CCTV, doorbell footage, or door-entry logs in detail.
- Posting in a building chat or on social media.
- Contacting your landlord, letting agent, housing provider, or managing agent about locks or keycards.
- Writing a full timeline or making longer-term security decisions.

## Important reassurance
A key or keycard attempt at your own door can feel frightening because it is close, sudden, and unclear. You do not have to solve who it is right now. Your immediate job is to stay behind a locked barrier, create distance, and get help if there may be danger.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes only. Later decisions about reporting, locks, keycards, building management, or specialist safety advice can wait until the immediate risk has passed.

## Important note
This guide is general information for immediate first steps, not legal, security, emergency-services, or other professional advice. If you believe someone is trying to enter your home or you feel unsafe, contact emergency services.
