# What to do if… you receive notice of conditions you must follow before your first court date (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the conditions as active from the time stated on the notice. If the start time is unclear, act as if they apply now until the issuing authority or a criminal defence solicitor confirms otherwise.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the conditions “do not count yet” because you have not been to court.
- Don’t contact a person you have been told not to contact, including through friends, family, social media, shared accounts, or indirect messages.
- Don’t test a distance, address, or area ban by going near it to see what happens.
- Don’t ignore a curfew, reporting time, residence requirement, monitoring instruction, or document-surrender requirement because it feels unrealistic.
- Don’t change the conditions yourself; ask for clarification or a variation through the proper route.
- Don’t rely on informal reassurance if it conflicts with the written notice.

## What to do now
1. **Read the notice and turn it into a plain checklist.**  
   Write down:
   - who you must not contact
   - where you must not go
   - any curfew or time limits
   - any reporting place, date, and time
   - where you must live or stay
   - any passport, phone, device, or document requirement
   - the exact return date, police station, court, or contact point shown on the notice

2. **Use the exact wording on your paperwork.**  
   The notice may use terms such as bail, police bail, pre-charge bail, court bail, release conditions, or undertaking. These terms and routes are not identical across the UK, so do not guess from a different form or a different part of the UK.

3. **If anything is unclear, ask the issuing authority for written clarification.**  
   Use the phone number, email address, custody desk, police station, court office, or named contact shown on the notice. Ask:
   - “From what date and time do these conditions apply?”
   - “Does this condition include indirect contact?”
   - “Does this address, route, workplace, school, or childcare handover fall inside the condition?”
   - “Who can confirm the correct route if I need the condition changed?”

4. **Get criminal defence advice and send the exact notice.**  
   If you already have a solicitor, send clear photos or a PDF of every page and ask what you must do today. If you do not have a solicitor, use an official legal advice or solicitor-finder route for your part of the UK and say the conditions may already be active.

5. **Put practical barriers in place before you are stressed or tired.**  
   For a no-contact condition, mute or block routes that make accidental replies easy. For a stay-away condition, choose a route that avoids the area completely. For a curfew or residence condition, set alarms and make sure you can get into the address in time. For reporting or surrendering documents, put the time, place, and item in your calendar now.

6. **If a condition affects childcare, housing, work, medication, caring duties, or collecting belongings, do not improvise contact or entry.**  
   Ask your solicitor or the issuing authority how to handle that specific need without breaching the condition. If the answer is not immediate, choose the option that best avoids contact, avoids the restricted place, and keeps you within the written wording.

7. **Keep a simple compliance folder.**  
   Keep the notice, any envelopes or emails, screenshots of clarification requests, solicitor messages, travel plans for avoiding restricted places, and short factual notes of steps you took to comply. Do not write arguments about the case in this folder.

8. **If the condition seems impossible to follow tonight or before the next working day, contact help before acting outside it.**  
   Try your solicitor, the number on the notice, the police station or custody contact, the court contact named on the paperwork, or an official legal advice route for your part of the UK. Explain the practical problem briefly and ask what compliant step you should take now.

9. **If you need a condition changed, use the proper route.**  
   Do not self-adjust the condition. In England and Wales, police-imposed pre-charge bail conditions can usually be raised with the police first, and there is a magistrates’ court route to ask the court to reconsider police bail. In Scotland or Northern Ireland, or if your notice uses different wording, ask a local criminal defence solicitor or the issuing authority for the correct route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your whole legal strategy today.
- You do not need to explain your side to anyone involved in the case today.
- You do not need to work out whether the conditions are fair before you follow them.
- You do not need to predict what will happen at a later court date.
- You do not need to contact a protected person to sort out practical issues yourself.

## Important reassurance
Receiving conditions before a court date can feel shocking and unfair. The safest first move is simple: follow the written wording, reduce the chance of accidental breach, and get clarification through the official contact route or a solicitor.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about challenging, varying, or responding to the conditions may need specialist criminal defence advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Bail rules, forms, and terminology are not identical across all parts of the UK, so follow your own notice and get advice from a qualified criminal defence solicitor if anything is unclear or difficult to follow.
