# What to do if… someone is lingering in your building lobby or entrance and you feel unsafe (UK)

## Short answer
Put distance and a locked door between you and them, and get help from someone with authority in the building, such as a concierge, security guard, building manager, or managing agent. If you think anyone is in immediate danger, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront them alone or try to “test” them by walking past repeatedly.
- Don’t buzz them in, hold the door, or let them tailgate behind you through a secure entrance.
- Don’t reveal your flat number, name, or routine while you are deciding what to do.
- Don’t go into isolated areas, such as stairwells, basement bike stores, car parks, or bin stores, to check what they are doing.
- Don’t dismiss specific behaviour such as trying doors, following residents, blocking the entrance, or waiting by the intercom.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause point.** If you are in your flat, lock your door and use the peephole or door chain if you answer at all. If you are in a corridor, step back into a secure area, such as your flat, a trusted neighbour’s flat, or a staffed area.
2. **Get a second person involved immediately.** Call or message a neighbour, concierge, security, building manager, or managing agent and ask them to check the lobby or entry area. If there is no staff, ask a trusted neighbour to stay on the phone while you decide your next step.
3. **If you need to leave the building, avoid leaving alone.** Wait until you can leave with another resident, staff member, or a small group. If you must go out, choose a route that keeps you near other people and avoid basements, garages, and other isolated areas.
4. **Choose 999, 101, or online reporting based on immediate risk.**
   - **Call 999** if a serious incident is happening or about to happen, someone is in immediate danger, the person is trying doors or following residents, or you feel threatened right now.
   - **If you can’t safely speak**, call **999** and try to communicate if you can, even quietly. If you are calling from a **mobile** and cannot speak, press **55** or tap when prompted to be transferred to police. It is still safest to give your location if you can.
   - **If you are calling from a landline** and cannot speak, stay on the line. If the operator can hear background noise, they may transfer the call to police, and a landline call usually gives location information automatically.
   - **For non-emergencies**, call **101** or report online, for example if the behaviour seems suspicious but no one appears to be in immediate danger.
5. **Give clear, practical details.** When you contact staff or police, share the exact location, including the building name or number and which entrance; what the person is doing, such as watching residents, trying to follow in, or waiting by the intercom; a description of clothing, height, build, and distinguishing features; and whether they have a bag, bicycle, or vehicle nearby.
6. **Ask the building to preserve evidence without putting yourself at risk.** If your building has CCTV, entry logs, a fob system, or a concierge record, ask management or staff to save the relevant time window and provide it to police if requested. Don’t approach the person to get close-up photos.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide now whether to move, change your routine long-term, or buy new locks or cameras.
- You do **not** need to work out who they are or prove criminal intent before asking for help.
- You can deal later with formal complaints to the managing agent, residents’ association, landlord, or freeholder.
- If you only want to pass information on anonymously, and there is no immediate danger, you can do that later through Crimestoppers.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel alarmed by someone watching residents in a controlled entry area. Taking a cautious, low-contact approach, locking in, getting staff or police support, and avoiding isolated areas can buy time without forcing you into confrontation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the next minutes to hours. If the behaviour continues or escalates, you may need building management, the managing agent, landlord, freeholder, or police to take further action.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, security, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe or believe someone may be at risk, prioritise immediate safety and contact emergency services.
