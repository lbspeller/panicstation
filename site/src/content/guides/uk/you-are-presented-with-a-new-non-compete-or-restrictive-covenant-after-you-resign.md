# What to do if… you are presented with a new non-compete or restrictive covenant after you resign (UK)

## Short answer
Do not resign immediately. Secure written confirmation of the changes to your role, initiate a factual timeline of events, and use the formal grievance process to request a written explanation for the removal of your duties.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign as a reactive measure or in a state of panic, as this may forfeit your right to claim unfair or constructive dismissal later.
- Do not send aggressive or accusatory emails; keep all communications neutral and focused on requesting factual clarity.
- Do not sign any "updated" job descriptions or contract variations on the spot; ask for at least 48 hours to review the documents.
- Do not download or remove confidential company data or client lists to "build a case," as this is usually a gross misconduct offense.
- Do not rely on verbal explanations from your manager; if a reason is given orally, follow up with an email to confirm your understanding of what was said.
- Do not stop performing your remaining work duties, as this could be framed as a performance or conduct issue.

## What to do now
1. **Document every observable change in a private timeline (today).**  
   **Record specific instances where duties were reassigned, access to software or folders was revoked, or you were removed from recurring calendar invites.** Include dates, times, the names of people involved, and exactly what was said or done. Keep this log on a personal device, not a work computer.

2. **Send a formal request for role clarity (within 24 hours).**  
   **Email your manager to ask for a written explanation of why your responsibilities have changed and how your performance will be measured moving forward.** Use neutral language: "I have noticed I am no longer involved in [Project X] and my access to [System Y] has been removed; please clarify my current priorities and whether my job description has been officially varied."

3. **Secure copies of your existing employment records.**  
   **Save your original signed contract, current job description, latest performance review, and any recent emails praising your work.** Ensure you only access documents you are legally entitled to see and do not violate data protection policies.

4. **Explicitly state you are "working under protest" if you disagree with the changes.**  
   **If the employer imposes a significant change to your duties without your consent, notify them in writing that you are continuing to work but do not accept the new terms.** To maintain this legal position, you must repeat this statement in writing regularly, such as every time you receive a payslip.

5. **Initiate a formal grievance if informal requests are ignored.**  
   **Follow your company’s written grievance policy to raise a formal complaint about the removal of your duties and the lack of explanation.** Reference the Acas Code of Practice on Disciplinary and Grievance Procedures, as failure to follow this can lead to a 25% uplift in compensation if the case reaches a tribunal.

6. **Exercise your right to be accompanied in formal meetings.**  
   **If you are called to a formal grievance meeting, notify your employer that you wish to bring a colleague or a trade union representative.** This is a statutory right for grievances concerning a legal or contractual duty owed to you by the employer.

7. **Monitor the 12-week Acas Early Conciliation window.**  
   **Be aware that if you intend to take the matter further, you must usually notify Acas to start Early Conciliation.** As of late 2025/2026, this process can last up to 12 weeks to allow for settlement discussions before a tribunal claim is filed.

8. **Track the statutory tribunal deadlines.**  
   **Ensure you are aware that most employment tribunal claims currently have a strict deadline of 3 months minus 1 day from the act of discrimination or the date of termination.** Note that for acts occurring after October 2026, this limit is scheduled to increase to 6 months under the Employment Rights Act 2025.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to leave the company today.
- You do not need to prove "malice" or "intent" right now; the focus is on documenting the physical removal of duties.
- You do not need to hire a solicitor immediately; a union representative or Acas advisor can provide initial procedural guidance.
- You do not need to notify your colleagues or "clear the air" with the team while the situation is unstable.

## Important reassurance
Feeling sidelined or "frozen out" is a high-stress experience that often triggers a "flight" response. It is normal to feel an urgent need to quit to regain control. However, slowing down and moving to a written, factual footing protects your legal rights and forces the employer to justify their actions in a way that "quiet" changes do not.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for UK employment disputes involving the removal of duties. It does not cover specific procedures for redundancy, disciplinary action for misconduct, or the nuances of Northern Ireland’s industrial tribunal system.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment law in the UK is subject to rapid change in 2026 due to the Employment Rights Act 2025. You should consult a qualified legal professional, a trade union, or Acas before making decisions that could affect your employment status or potential legal claims.
