# What to do if… you are pressured to choose between burial and cremation and feel unsure (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign burial or cremation paperwork while you are unsure or while close family are giving conflicting instructions. Ask the funeral director, hospital bereavement office, mortuary, or registrar what pause is possible while you check recorded wishes and who can give instructions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign cremation, burial, or funeral paperwork just to end the pressure if you are unsure or there is active disagreement.
- Do not assume burial or cremation must happen immediately because someone says it is faster, cheaper, or simpler.
- Do not let several relatives give different instructions to the funeral director; choose one temporary point of contact.
- Do not pay a large deposit or agree to extras until you have a written estimate and know what is essential now.
- Do not argue through staff messages or rushed calls; ask for a call-back time so you can regroup.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for a pause and a clear care plan.** Say: “We are not ready to choose burial or cremation yet. What can be paused, where will they be cared for while we decide, and are there any holding or care costs we need to know about in writing?”

2. **Ask who they can take instructions from.** Say: “Who are you able to accept funeral instructions from for this case, and what do you need to see before acting?” If there is a will, ask whether an executor is named. If there is no will, ask the funeral director or registrar what they need before taking instructions.

3. **Do a quick check for recorded wishes, not a full search.** Spend a short, fixed time looking for:
   - a prepaid funeral plan or funeral contract
   - a will, solicitor letter, or written funeral wishes
   - a note in a life-admin folder
   - recent messages to a trusted person about burial, cremation, ashes, or a grave

4. **Tell the funeral director where the death happened and where the burial or cremation would happen.** Paperwork can differ across England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, and it can also differ if the death happened in one UK nation but the cremation or burial would happen in another. Ask them to confirm the current forms and documents for that exact situation.

5. **Ask what document or permission is missing.** Say: “Is this waiting for registration, a certificate for burial or cremation, cremation forms, a coroner in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, or the Procurator Fiscal in Scotland?” This can show whether the pressure is about paperwork rather than a decision that must be made this minute.

6. **Use a temporary no-irreversible-decision rule.** If wishes are unknown and people disagree, say: “No burial or cremation instruction until we have checked written wishes and confirmed who can give instructions.”

7. **If cost pressure is driving the rush, ask for the simplest written options.** Ask for the simplest burial option, the simplest cremation option, and any short-term care or holding cost. Keep ceremony details out of the decision for now.

## What can wait
- Coffin style, flowers, cars, music, readings, catering, printed orders of service, and who attends.
- A large ceremony versus a small ceremony; a separate memorial can usually be arranged later.
- Urn style, ashes jewellery, scattering plans, or a permanent memorial.
- Telling everyone at once; tell only the people needed to stop conflicting instructions and check wishes.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel frightened of making the wrong choice when burial or cremation feels irreversible. A calm pause to check wishes, authority, paperwork, and costs is a reasonable first step and often prevents avoidable conflict.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you slow the situation down and avoid an irreversible decision while overwhelmed. Later decisions about disputes, costs, paperwork, religious requirements, coroner or Procurator Fiscal involvement, or legal authority may need help from a funeral director, registrar service, religious or community leader, or solicitor.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Rules, forms, and who can give instructions can differ across the UK and by circumstance.
