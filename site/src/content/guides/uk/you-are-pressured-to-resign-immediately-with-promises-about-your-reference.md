# What to do if… you are pressured to resign immediately with promises about your reference (UK)

## Short answer
Do not resign on the spot. Ask for time, ask for the proposal in writing, and treat any reference promise as unsafe to rely on until the wording and exit terms are written down.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hand over a resignation letter or email today just to end the pressure.
- Do not sign any settlement agreement, waiver, or full and final document in the meeting.
- Do not rely on a verbal promise that you will get a good reference.
- Do not argue the whole case in the moment; keep it calm, factual, and focused on buying time.
- Do not delete messages, calendar invites, notes, or documents about what is happening.
- Do not agree that the meeting is informal if they are discussing resignation, discipline, performance, settlement, or your exit.

## What to do now
1. **Create a pause.** Say: “I am not resigning in this meeting. I need time to consider this and get advice. Please put your proposal in writing.” Ask for a later time or date after you have had the written proposal.
2. **Clarify what kind of meeting this is.** Ask: “Is this a disciplinary hearing, a grievance meeting, a performance meeting, a settlement discussion, or are you asking me to resign?” Write down their answer, the time, and who is present.
3. **If it is a disciplinary hearing, ask for the proper information and a companion.** Say: “Please send me the allegation or performance issue, the evidence, the possible outcomes, and the information about my right to be accompanied. I need reasonable time to prepare.”
4. **If it is a grievance meeting about legal or contractual duties, ask to be accompanied.** Say: “I want to make a reasonable request to be accompanied. Please allow enough time for my companion to attend and prepare.” A companion is usually a colleague, a certified workplace trade union representative, or an official employed by a trade union.
5. **Treat the reference promise as not settled until it is written.** In the UK, an employer usually does not have to give a work reference unless there is a written agreement, the role is in certain regulated financial services situations, or it is agreed in another way such as a settlement agreement.
6. **Ask for the reference terms in writing.** Ask who will provide the reference, whether it will be basic or detailed, whether it will include dates and job title only, and whether they are offering exact agreed wording.
7. **Ask for the full exit proposal in writing.** Request a written summary covering notice, whether notice would be worked or paid, holiday pay, final pay date, benefits end date, bonus or commission position, and whether any disciplinary, grievance, or performance process would stop.
8. **If they mention a settlement agreement, take it away unsigned.** Say: “I am not signing today. Please send the draft so I can take independent advice.” A UK settlement agreement has to meet legal validity conditions; these include being in writing and the worker getting advice from a relevant independent adviser.
9. **Make a clean paper trail.** As soon as you can, write a dated note of what was said, especially any pressure to resign today and any reference promise. If it feels safe, email HR a neutral summary: “Confirming today’s meeting: I was asked to resign today and a reference was mentioned. I asked for the proposal and any reference terms in writing, and for time to consider.”
10. **Contact rapid support.** If you are in a union, contact the union now. If not, contact Acas for guidance before you resign or sign anything.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign.
- You do not need to raise a grievance today.
- You do not need to decide today whether to bring any claim or take legal action.
- You do not need to negotiate every detail in the meeting.
- You do not need to write a long statement or defend yourself in full right now.

## Important reassurance
This situation can feel urgent, but you are allowed to slow the conversation down. Asking for written terms and time to get advice is a reasonable first step, not an escalation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to buy time and avoid irreversible decisions. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if there is a settlement agreement, disciplinary process, discrimination issue, whistleblowing concern, health issue, or dispute about pay or notice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employment situations are fact-specific, and deadlines or options can depend on your contract, employment status, and what has happened.
