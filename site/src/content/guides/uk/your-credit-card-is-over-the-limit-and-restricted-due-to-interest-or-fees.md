# What to do if… your credit card is over the limit and restricted due to interest or fees (UK)

## Short answer
Stop trying to spend on the card. Check what you can safely pay without missing essentials, then contact the card provider and ask exactly what payment or clearance is needed to lift the restriction and what financial difficulty support they can offer.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep trying the card to see if it works; repeated declined payments can create problems with merchants and will not usually fix the restriction.
- Don’t take a cash advance to fix this unless you fully understand the cost; cash advances can add fees and higher interest.
- Don’t set up a card payment that could make your bank account go short for rent, mortgage, council tax, utilities, food, or travel to work.
- Don’t cancel a direct debit in panic if it is what prevents a missed minimum payment; change it only after checking what you can afford and what the card provider expects.
- Don’t claim a genuine transaction is fraud just to buy time.

## What to do now
1. **Check what pushed the balance over the limit.** In the app or online account, separate interest, fees, new purchases, pending transactions, and card holds. Write down the amounts and dates so you can explain the problem clearly.

2. **Check what you can safely pay today.** Only pay what you can afford after essentials. If paying the card would leave you unable to cover rent, mortgage, council tax, utilities, food, medication, childcare, or travel to work, tell the provider you are in financial difficulty instead of forcing the payment.

3. **Make a safe payment if you can.** Use the provider’s app, website, or phone payment option. Then check whether the payment reduces the balance or available credit straight away, or only after it has cleared.

4. **Contact the card provider and ask the exact restriction rules.** Use the number on the back of the card or in the app. Ask:
   - “How far under the limit do I need to be before the restriction can lift?”
   - “Does the payment need to clear first?”
   - “Is the restriction only on new spending, or could it affect recurring card payments too?”
   - “Are any further interest, fees, or pending transactions due before my next statement?”

5. **Ask for financial difficulty support.** Say: “I went over my limit because of interest and fees, and I am trying to stabilise the account.” Ask them to consider:
   - waiving or reversing any over-limit or late fees if they have been applied,
   - reducing, pausing, or cancelling further interest or charges for a period,
   - agreeing a temporary payment arrangement you can actually keep,
   - confirming the arrangement in writing by secure message, email, or letter.

6. **Protect bills linked to the restricted card.** Check subscriptions, insurance, phone bills, travel, or other recurring payments that use this card. Move essential payments to a debit card, direct debit, or bank transfer if you can, and pause nonessential recurring payments so they do not keep trying the restricted card.

7. **If you live in England or Wales and debts are escalating, ask a free debt adviser about Breathing Space.** A debt adviser can decide whether it is suitable and can apply for you. Standard Breathing Space can give temporary protection for up to 60 days on included debts, including stopping creditor contact about those debts, pausing enforcement action, and stopping added interest and charges.

8. **If you live in Scotland or Northern Ireland, get local debt advice early.** In Scotland, a money adviser can explain whether the Debt Arrangement Scheme could help. In Northern Ireland, the England and Wales Breathing Space scheme is not currently available, so ask a free debt adviser what options are available where you live.

9. **If the provider will not help or something looks wrong, use the complaints path.** Complain to the card provider first and keep notes, screenshots, dates, and copies of messages. If you get a final response you disagree with, or you do not get a response within eight weeks, you can ask the Financial Ombudsman Service to look at it. You usually need to contact the Ombudsman within six months of the final response letter.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to close the card, consolidate, transfer balances, or take a new loan.
- You do not need to negotiate the perfect repayment plan right now; the priority is stopping extra costs where possible and understanding what lifts the restriction.
- You do not need to chase your credit score today. Focus first on avoiding missed priority bills, missed minimum payments where possible, and further charges.

## Important reassurance
This can happen when a balance is already close to the limit and interest or fees are added. A restriction does not mean you have run out of options; the provider should explain what is needed and consider support if you are in financial difficulty.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repayment plans, debt options, complaints, or borrowing may need help from a free debt adviser or another suitable specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not financial, legal, debt, or professional advice. Card providers’ policies differ, and payment clearing times and restriction rules vary.
