# What to do if… someone at your door claims to be a worker or official but you cannot verify them (UK)

## Short answer
Keep the door closed and locked. Verify them using a phone number you find yourself, not a number they provide. If you feel in immediate danger, or they try to force entry, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door “just to talk” if you feel pressured or unsure.
- Do not let them inside “for a quick look” or to “use the toilet” or “use the phone”.
- Do not use any phone number, QR code, link, or ID-check method they provide.
- Do not hand over a bank card, passport, bill, phone, keys, or other personal documents at the doorstep.
- Do not go to another room to “get something” while they are still at the door.
- Do not sign anything, pay anything, or agree to work at the doorstep.
- Do not argue on the doorstep if they become pushy; end the conversation and verify from inside.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Keep the door closed and locked. Speak through the door, an intercom, a doorbell camera, or a window if you can.
2. **Use one clear line.** Say: “I can’t let anyone in without verifying. Please wait outside while I check.” If they push back, repeat it once and stop engaging.
3. **Collect details without opening the door.** Ask them to hold ID up to a window, peephole, or camera. Note their name, organisation, and any reference number or work order number. Treat the ID as unconfirmed until you check it yourself.
4. **Verify using a route you trust.**
   - **Utility company:** call your supplier using a number from your bill, statement, app, or the official website you type in yourself.
   - **Council, landlord, or housing repairs:** call the council switchboard, landlord, or housing provider using a number you already have or find independently.
   - **Police:** if there is no immediate danger, call 101 and ask whether someone should be at your address. If you feel threatened or they try to force entry, call 999.
5. **Do not let urgency force the door open.**
   - If you smell gas or suspect carbon monoxide, move to fresh air if needed, open doors or windows if safe, and do not use electrical switches or flames. In Great Britain, call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999 yourself. In Northern Ireland, use the emergency gas number shown by your local gas network or supplier, found independently.
   - If they claim there is an electricity network issue or power cut, call 105 yourself in England, Scotland or Wales. In Northern Ireland, call 03457 643643 for NIE Networks.
6. **If they will not leave, try to push past you, threaten you, or you feel unsafe, call 999.** Say that someone is at your door trying to gain entry and you cannot verify who they are.
7. **Bring in another person if you can.** Call a friend, neighbour, family member, building manager, or concierge and keep them on speaker while you verify. Do not say bank details, passwords, or payment information out loud at the door.
8. **After they have gone, secure and record.** Lock doors, check accessible windows, and write down or voice-note the time, what they said, the organisation they claimed to be from, and a description. Save doorbell or CCTV footage if you have it.
9. **If you shared details, let them in, or paid anything, act quickly.** Contact your bank using the number on your card, banking app, or another trusted route. You can use 159 if your bank supports it. Then report the incident to Report Fraud when you are safe.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether it was definitely genuine or definitely a scam.
- You do not need to confront them, prove anything, or catch them out.
- You do not need to book repairs, sign paperwork, make payments, or arrange another visit at the doorstep.
- You do not need to explain why you are checking. Keeping the door closed is enough.

## Important reassurance
Feeling rude, frozen, or unsure is a normal reaction when someone appears official and says something is urgent. A genuine worker or official can wait while you verify them through a trusted route.

## Scope note
These are first steps to help you stay safe and buy time. If someone gained access, money was taken, threats were made, or you are still worried, later decisions may need specialist help from the police, your bank, your housing provider, or a fraud-reporting service.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.
