# What to do if… you lose your company ID badge and you are worried it could be misused (UK)

## Short answer
Take immediate steps to recall the message or revoke link access, then report the incident to your manager and your organization's Data Protection Officer (DPO) or IT security team to ensure the breach is assessed within the legal 72-hour window.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to hide the mistake by deleting the email from your sent folder or ignoring it.
- Do not engage in lengthy or defensive arguments with the recipient.
- Do not send further sensitive information while trying to explain the error.
- Do not wait for the recipient to respond before reporting the incident internally.
- Do not assume a "recall" request was successful without secondary confirmation.
- Do not attempt to contact the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) yourself unless you are the designated reporting officer.

## What to do now
1. **Attempt an immediate recall or access revocation if the system allows it.**
   - Use the "Recall Message" feature in Outlook (only works if the recipient is internal and has not opened it).
   - If a link was sent via SharePoint, OneDrive, or Google Drive, **immediately change the permissions to 'Restricted' or delete the link entirely.**
2. **Contact the unintended recipient with a neutral, clear request.**
   - **Ask them to delete the email and any attachments immediately without reading, downloading, or sharing them.**
   - Request that they **confirm in writing** once the deletion is complete, including the deletion of the item from their "Deleted Items" or "Trash" folder.
3. **Notify your line manager and the Data Protection Officer (DPO) immediately.**
   - **Use your organization's official incident reporting form or send a high-priority email stating "Possible Personal Data Breach" in the subject line.**
   - Include the **time sent, recipient address, and the nature of the data** (e.g., names, financial details, or health information).
4. **Identify the specific categories of data involved for the risk assessment.**
   - Determine if the data includes **Special Category Data** (health, race, religion, sexual orientation) or criminal offense data, as these carry higher reporting triggers.
   - Note if the file was **encrypted or password-protected**, as this significantly reduces the risk profile.
5. **Start a local incident log to document your response actions.**
   - **Record every action you took and the exact time you took it, including when you attempted the recall and when you notified your manager.**
   - Keep a record of the **confirmation of deletion** from the recipient if they provide it.
6. **Check for sector-specific reporting requirements if you work in regulated industries.**
   - If you work for a **Trust Service Provider**, be aware that your organization may have a **24-hour notification window** to the ICO for significant impacts.
   - If you work in **Financial Services**, your DPO may need to assess the incident against the **2026 FCA operational incident thresholds** for consumer harm.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether the incident meets the legal threshold for an ICO notification right now.
- You do not need to provide a detailed explanation of why the error happened (e.g., "I was tired" or "Autofill failed") during the initial containment phase.
- You do not need to contact the individuals whose data was leaked; this is a strategic decision for the DPO.
- Long-term preventative measures, such as disabling Outlook's autocomplete, can wait until after the immediate incident is contained.

## Important reassurance
Mistakes involving misdirected emails are among the most common security incidents in UK workplaces. Promptly reporting the error is considered a proactive security action, not a failure. Most organizations prioritize containment and harm reduction over blame, and early reporting is the most effective way to prevent a mistake from becoming a legal liability.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and internal reporting for misdirected information in a UK workplace context. It does not cover long-term disciplinary procedures, litigation defense, or the technical forensics of a data breach.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Data protection requirements under the UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018 are complex and fact-specific. Always follow your employer’s specific Data Protection Policy and the instructions of your DPO or IT Security team. If you are a business owner, you should consult the ICO’s official website for reporting thresholds.
