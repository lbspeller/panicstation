# What to do if… you receive a call or message claiming to be from an embassy about your passport and it asks for personal details (UK)

## Short answer
Break contact immediately without providing any information and verify the request by calling the embassy switchboard or the FCDO using a number you have looked up independently.

## Do not do these things
- Don't provide your passport number, expiry date, full name, address, or bank details during the initial contact.
- Don't click links, download "official forms," or scan QR codes sent via SMS, WhatsApp, or email.
- Don't call back the number that contacted you or use "press 1" options to speak to an agent.
- Don't assume the call is real because the caller ID says "Embassy" or matches a real government number; these are easily spoofed.
- Don't share one-time passcodes (OTP) or account recovery codes, even if the caller claims they are for "identity verification."
- Don't pay any "fines" or "expedited fees" using bank transfers, cryptocurrency, or gift cards.

## What to do now
1. **End the interaction immediately.** Hang up the phone or stop replying to the message thread.
2. **Record the contact details.** Take a screenshot of the message or note the incoming phone number and the exact time of the call.
3. **Find the official switchboard number.** 
   - Search for the specific embassy or high commission on the official GOV.UK website.
   - Use the "Contact" or "Consular services" section to find their public switchboard number.
4. **Verify the request through the official switchboard.** 
   - Call the number you found independently and ask to be put through to the Consular section.
   - State: "I received a message claiming to be from this embassy regarding my passport. Can you confirm if this is a genuine request?"
5. **Contact the FCDO if you are a British national abroad.** 
   - **Call the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office (FCDO) 24/7 on +44 (0)20 7008 5000.**
   - Select the option for "Consular services for British nationals" to speak with a verified officer.
6. **Secure your digital accounts if you shared any details.** 
   - If you shared passwords, change them immediately and enable passkeys or two-step verification.
   - If you use WhatsApp or Signal, enable "Registration Lock" or "Two-Step Verification" in your app settings to prevent account hijacking.
7. **Report the incident to the appropriate UK authorities.** 
   - **Forward suspicious text messages to 7726.**
   - **Forward suspicious emails to report@phishing.gov.uk.**
   - **Report the fraud to Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) online at `reportfraud.police.uk` or by calling 0300 123 2040 if you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland.
   - **Contact Police Scotland on 101** if you live in Scotland and have lost money or sensitive data.

## What can wait
- You do not need to respond to "urgent" deadlines or threats of passport cancellation today.
- You do not need to report the call to the local police in a foreign country unless you have suffered a physical or financial loss.
- You do not need to apply for a replacement passport immediately unless you have confirmed with the FCDO that your current one is compromised or cancelled.

## Important reassurance
Impersonation scams are highly sophisticated and often use "spoofing" technology to make calls appear as though they are coming from a legitimate government office. Taking a moment to pause and verify through a separate, trusted channel is a standard safety procedure and will not result in the cancellation of your documents if the matter is genuine.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for suspected impersonation scams. It does not cover the legal process of replacing a stolen passport or managing long-term identity theft.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. If you believe your physical safety is at risk while abroad, contact the local emergency services immediately.
