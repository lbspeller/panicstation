# What to do if… a neighbour repeatedly tries to start sexual conversations and you worry they may escalate (UK)

## Short answer
Create immediate physical distance and keep a detailed log of every interaction, as legal protection for harassment usually requires proving a pattern of two or more incidents. If you are in immediate danger or the person is attempting to enter your home, call 999; if you cannot speak, press 55 when prompted.

## Do not do these things
- Do not meet them privately to discuss their behaviour or try to negotiate boundaries.
- Do not feel obligated to be polite, "keep them sweet," or laugh at suggestive jokes to keep the peace.
- Do not wait for a physical incident to occur before seeking support or recording what is happening.
- Do not delete any digital messages, notes, or recordings they may have sent.
- Do not confront them if you are alone or in a space where you cannot easily exit.

## What to do now
1. **Create physical distance and avoid being cornered (immediately).**  
   If you see them in communal areas like hallways, lifts, or bin stores, it is safer to turn back, wait inside, or wait for another resident before proceeding. Carry your phone in your hand and stay on an active call with a friend while moving through shared spaces if you feel uneasy.
2. **Set one clear, brief boundary only if you feel safe to do so.**  
   State clearly: "I do not want to have sexual conversations with you. Stop." Do not explain why or stay to hear a response; immediately walk away and close your door.
3. **Start a contemporaneous incident log (today).**  
   Record the date, time, exact location, and as close to their word-for-word comments as possible. Include details of any witnesses and the specific physical sensations or feelings of fear you experienced, as this documentation is vital for police or housing reports.
4. **Identify and contact your housing provider to create a paper trail.**  
   Contact your landlord, housing association, or council housing officer to report that a resident is making you feel unsafe. Request that they log this as a formal concern and ask what physical safety measures they can provide, such as improved lighting or checking communal CCTV.
5. **Contact the 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line for confidential guidance.**  
   Call **0808 500 2222** or use the online chat at 247sexualabusesupport.org.uk. They support anyone aged 16+ in England and Wales who has experienced sexual harassment or unwanted sexual comments, regardless of whether a physical crime has occurred.
6. **Use the National Stalking Helpline if the behaviour feels persistent or fixated.**  
   Call **0808 802 0300** for specialist advice on safety planning and whether the neighbour's behaviour meets the legal threshold for stalking or harassment.
7. **Report to the police via 101 or online reporting for non-emergencies.**  
   Tell the operator you wish to report a "course of conduct" involving unwanted sexualised comments and that you fear escalation. Ask for a crime reference number and request advice on local "StreetSafe" reporting if the harassment happens in specific public areas nearby.
8. **Agree on a check-in protocol with one trusted person.**  
   Tell a friend or family member exactly which flat the person lives in and agree to a "home safe" text routine. If you live alone, ensure someone knows to call you if they do not hear from you by a specific time after you return home.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether you want to press charges or go to court right now.
- You do not need to start looking for a new place to live or looking into moving immediately.
- You do not need to notify all your other neighbours or post about the situation on local social media groups.
- You do not need to have "perfect" evidence like a video recording; your own written log is a valid starting point.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel "hyper-vigilant" or trapped when harassment happens where you live. Feeling uncomfortable or worried about escalation is a valid reason to take safety steps, even if the neighbour claims they are "just joking" or "being friendly."

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for managing immediate safety and creating a record of harassment. Longer-term legal injunctions under the Protection from Harassment Act 1997 or housing transfers require further consultation with police or legal professionals.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. If you are in immediate danger or someone is trying to force entry to your home, call 999. If you are in England or Wales and are under 16, contact Childline or a trusted adult.
