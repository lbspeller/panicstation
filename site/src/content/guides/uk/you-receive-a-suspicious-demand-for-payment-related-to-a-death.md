# What to do if… you receive a suspicious demand for payment related to a death (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay, click, call back, or share details through the message. Pause and verify the demand using contact details you find independently, then report it as suspected fraud.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money just to make the pressure stop, even if they say it is urgent or time-limited.
- Do not click links, scan QR codes, open attachments, or log in through the message.
- Do not call back the number in the message or keep replying to clear it up.
- Do not share bank details, card details, copies of ID, National Insurance numbers, passwords, one-time codes, or extra details about the deceased person through an unverified route.
- Do not pay any fee until you have verified it independently, especially to a personal account, gift card, crypto wallet, money transfer app, or unfamiliar payment link.
- Do not keep it secret because the sender tells you to.

## What to do now
1. **Mark it as unverified.** Put the phone or email down for a few minutes. If you are shaking, sit down, put both feet on the floor, and slow your breathing before you do anything else.
2. **Preserve the message without interacting with it.** Take screenshots, save the email, text, voicemail, phone number, account details, payment link shown on screen, and the date and time. Do not open the link to get more information.
3. **Check what the demand claims to be about.**
   - If it claims to be from a **funeral director**, use paperwork you already have, the funeral director’s official website, or the firm’s main switchboard found independently.
   - If it mentions a **coroner, hospital mortuary, hospital bereavement office, or release of the body**, use the relevant NHS hospital website, local council website, or paperwork you already have to find the right contact route.
   - If it claims to involve an **estate, inheritance, insurance policy, solicitor, executor, or funds being held**, contact the executor, solicitor, insurer, or firm using a number or website you already trust, not the one in the demand.
4. **Ask the verified contact one plain question.** Say: “I have received a demand for release fees linked to this death. Is this genuinely from you, and what official reference should it have?” Do not use any payment details from the suspicious message unless the verified organisation confirms them independently.
5. **If the message mentions a funeral livestream, donation page, or tribute page, verify it with the family or funeral director first.** Fake livestream and donation links can copy real names, photos, and funeral details.
6. **If you already paid or shared details, contact your bank immediately.** Use the number on your bank card, official app, or official website and ask for the fraud team. Ask whether the payment can be stopped or recalled, whether your card or account needs protecting, and what record or reference number they can give you.
7. **Report the suspected fraud.**
   - If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report it to Report Fraud online or by calling 0300 123 2040.
   - If you are in Scotland, report it to Police Scotland online or by calling 101 for a non-emergency.
   - If someone is in immediate danger, the incident is happening now, or you are being coerced in person, call 999.
8. **Report the message route as well.**
   - If it was a scam text, forward it to 7726.
   - If it was a scam email, forward it to report@phishing.gov.uk.
9. **Warn one other person involved in the bereavement.** Send a simple message such as: “I got a suspicious release-fee demand linked to the death. Please do not pay, click, or share details unless we verify it directly.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out today how the sender got the details.
- You do not need to confront the sender or keep messaging them.
- You do not need to post publicly while you are still shaken.
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a complaint about a bank or payment firm.
- You do not need to handle every call yourself; a trusted person can sit with you or make practical calls while you listen.

## Important reassurance
Scams can target people after a death because grief and urgency make it harder to slow down. Feeling unsure, angry, or embarrassed does not mean you did anything wrong; the safest move is to stop the pressure and verify through a route you control.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: they are meant to prevent an immediate loss, protect information, and get the incident logged. Later decisions about recovering money, complaints, estate administration, or legal issues may need help from your bank, the relevant organisation, or a suitable specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. Processes can vary by UK nation and local area. If money has gone and you are unhappy with how your bank or payment firm handles it, you can use their complaints process and may be able to contact the Financial Ombudsman Service.
