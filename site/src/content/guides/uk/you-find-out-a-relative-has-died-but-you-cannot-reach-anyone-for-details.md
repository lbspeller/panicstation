# What to do if… you find out a relative has died but you cannot reach anyone for details (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and verify the news through an official medical or legal channel (the hospital bereavement office, the relative's GP, or the local coroner) before taking any physical or financial action.

## Do not do these things
- Don't announce the news on social media or in group chats until you have official confirmation.
- Don't send money, provide bank details, or share ID with anyone offering "help" with arrangements via unverified messages.
- Don't use contact numbers or links provided in an unexpected text or social media message; find official numbers independently.
- Don't assume silence from other family members implies a specific outcome; they may be occupied with emergency logistics or have limited phone access.
- Don't travel to a location until you have confirmed where the relative is or which authority is holding them.
- Don't make multiple repeated calls to the same individuals, as this can impede their ability to manage the situation or communicate with officials.

## What to do now
1. **Document the source and content of the information.**
   - **Record who contacted you, the time, the platform used, and the exact wording of the message.**
   - **Note any specific locations mentioned, such as a hospital name, a specific town, or a police station.**

2. **Call the Bereavement Office of the most likely hospital in the area.**
   - **Search for the hospital switchboard and ask specifically for the "Bereavement Office" or "Bereavement Manager" rather than PALS.**
   - **Ask if they have a record of the individual and what the official process is for confirming a death to a relative.**
   - **Note that if you are not the registered next of kin, they may be limited in what they can disclose, but they can often confirm if a person is a patient.**

3. **Contact the relative's GP surgery if they lived at home.**
   - **Call the surgery and explain that you have received an unconfirmed report of a death.**
   - **Ask if the GP has been notified of a death or if they can provide guidance on which medical examiner is reviewing the case.**

4. **Inquire with the local Coroner’s Office for the district where they lived.**
   - **Check the local authority website for the relevant area to find "Coroner Services" contact details.**
   - **Ask if a death has been reported for that individual; coroners are involved in most sudden or unexplained deaths where a doctor cannot immediately sign a certificate.**

5. **Request a "Safe and Well" check via 101 only if there is a risk to life.**
   - **Use 101 (non-emergency) to request a check if you believe the person may be at home and in need of medical help, rather than already deceased.**
   - **Clarify to the operator that you have received a report of a death but cannot confirm it and are concerned for the person's immediate safety.**

6. **Verify the status of the death registration via the local Register Office.**
   - **Search the "Find a Register Office" tool on GOV.UK for the area where the person lived.**
   - **Ask the registrar if a death has been registered for that name; in England and Wales, this usually happens within 5 days of the medical examiner’s authorisation.**

7. **Send a single, neutral message to a known family contact.**
   - **Send one text or leave one voicemail stating: "I have received a report regarding [Name]. I am trying to confirm the details through official channels. Please call me when you are able to provide an update on their location."**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on funeral directors or service arrangements today.
- You do not need to notify banks, pension providers, or the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) until you have a confirmed fact of death.
- You do not need to book travel or accommodation until you know exactly where the deceased is located.
- You do not need to provide proof of your relationship to anyone until you are dealing with the official Registrar or Coroner.

## Important reassurance
An information vacuum is common during the first few hours of a family crisis. Other relatives may be focused on immediate tasks, or the official "Medical Examiner" process may be causing a delay in formal notifications. This silence is a result of the process, not necessarily an indication of further trouble.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for verifying a report of death. It does not cover long-term probate, estate management, or funeral planning.

## Important note
Procedures differ across the UK. In England and Wales, the Medical Examiner system now reviews all non-coroner deaths. In Scotland, the Death Certification Review Service may be involved. In Northern Ireland, "Tell Us Once" is not available, and you must contact organizations individually once the death is confirmed.
