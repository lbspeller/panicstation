# What to do if… you learn a loved one has died abroad and you do not know who to contact first (UK)

## Short answer
Use an official consular route first: call the FCDO 24/7 consular line or the nearest British embassy, high commission or consulate in the country where the death happened. Ask them to help you confirm what is known and who the local official contact is.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money, passport details, bank details, or copies of documents to a stranger who contacts you about the death until you have checked through official channels.
- Do not rely on social media posts, messages, or second-hand claims as confirmation.
- Do not agree to urgent funeral, cremation, burial, or repatriation arrangements offered out of the blue before you know who is officially handling the case locally.
- Do not post identifying details publicly online while you are still checking identity, location, and family contact details.
- Do not cancel the person’s passport while repatriation is still being considered.
- Do not feel you have to decide about repatriation, funeral arrangements, travel, or legal steps in the first hour.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the basic facts you have.**  
   Note the person’s full name as shown on their passport, date of birth, nationality, where they were thought to be, who told you, when they told you, and any phone numbers, emails, case numbers, hospital names, police names, hotel details, employer details, or tour operator details you have been given.

2. **Make one official consular contact.**  
   If you are in the UK, or you cannot quickly find the right overseas post, call the FCDO 24/7 on **+44 (0)20 7008 5000** and select **“Consular services for British nationals.”**  
   Say: “I have been told my loved one has died abroad. I do not know who to contact first. Please help me check what is known and who the official local contact is.”  
   Ask for a case reference if one is opened.

3. **If you are already in the country where the death happened, contact the nearest British embassy, high commission or consulate.**  
   Ask for consular assistance with a death abroad. Ask which local authority, such as a hospital, police service, coroner, prosecutor, registrar, or equivalent office, is the official point of contact.

4. **Ask what has and has not been confirmed.**  
   Ask whether the person has been formally identified, which local authority reported the death, where the person is now, and what information is still unconfirmed. If you are a relative or close contact, ask what they need to record or confirm your family relationship or contact role.

5. **If a tour operator, employer, hotel, hospital, police officer, or friend contacts you, slow the call down.**  
   Write down their name, role, organisation, country, phone number, email address, and any case reference. Say you will call back after speaking to consular staff or another official contact. If it was a package holiday, the tour operator may have a welfare team, but still check any urgent request through an official route before agreeing to arrangements.

6. **Prepare the minimum information that may help consular staff.**  
   Have ready what you can: the person’s full name, date of birth, passport number if known, last known address, travel dates, travel insurance details if known, and names and contact details of close family. A recent photo may help with identification questions in some situations. If you do not have these details, still make the call.

7. **Ask what needs to happen locally before any body, ashes, belongings, or documents can be moved.**  
   Ask about local death registration, when a local death certificate may be available, whether a post mortem or investigation is involved, and whether personal belongings are being held by local authorities, a hospital, a hotel, or another organisation.

8. **Ask which decisions can safely wait.**  
   Ask whether you need to do anything today about a local funeral director, an international funeral director, burial or cremation abroad, repatriation to the UK, travel to the country, or insurance. Unless there is a verified official reason to act now, keep the first call focused on confirmation and official contacts.

9. **Use a call-back rule if anything feels pressured or fake.**  
   End the call, then call the FCDO number or the embassy, high commission or consulate using contact details you found yourself on GOV.UK. Treat urgency, secrecy, payment pressure, and requests for passport or bank details as reasons to re-check before acting.

## What can wait
- Choosing a UK or overseas funeral director, unless an official contact explains a verified reason it cannot wait.
- Deciding between repatriation, burial abroad, or cremation abroad.
- Telling banks, insurers, employers, subscriptions, landlords, or other organisations, unless one of them is needed to identify travel insurance or contact details.
- Sorting probate, estate matters, inheritance, pensions, benefits, tax, or property.
- Making a public announcement.
- Booking travel for yourself before you know where you would need to go and who you would meet.

## Important reassurance
You do not have to solve the whole situation now. The first steps are to get one official contact, check what is actually confirmed, and avoid being pushed into money, document, travel, or funeral decisions before you have reliable information.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from consular staff, local authorities abroad, an insurer, an international funeral director, a translator, or a lawyer familiar with the country where the death happened.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes vary by country and by the circumstances of the death. If anything feels unclear, rushed, or pressured, slow down and re-check through official consular channels.
