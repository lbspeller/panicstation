# What to do if… your child tells you an adult made a sexual comment or request (UK)

## Short answer
Make your child safe and supported first, and do not investigate it yourself. If anyone is in immediate danger, call 999; otherwise get safeguarding advice today from the NSPCC Helpline, children’s social care at the relevant local council or local authority, CEOP for online sexual abuse concerns, or the police if you believe a crime may have happened.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the adult or warn them; it can increase risk and affect what happens next.
- Do not question your child repeatedly, test them, or push for details.
- Do not promise “I won’t tell anyone”; instead say you may need to speak to someone whose job is to help keep children safe.
- Do not ask your child to recreate messages, pose, resend anything, or show you sexual images.
- Do not forward, post, copy, save elsewhere, or share sexual images or messages.
- Do not delete conversations, block accounts, wipe devices, or change passwords unless you need to do this for immediate safety.
- Do not share the story in group chats, on social media, or with people who do not need to know.
- Do not send the adult angry texts or calls to “get it in writing”.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a calm, private moment with your child.** Say something simple, such as: “Thank you for telling me. You’re not in trouble. I’m glad you told me.”
2. **Make an immediate safety boundary.** Stop contact with that adult for now: no visits, lifts, babysitting, private messages, calls, or one-to-one contact. If the adult is in your household or will be nearby soon, keep your child with a safe adult and move to a safer place if you can.
3. **Write down what your child has already told you, once.** Note the date, time, who was present, what prompted the conversation, and your child’s words as closely as you can remember. Keep it factual.
4. **If there are messages, accounts, or devices, preserve what already exists without digging.** Do not forward, copy, or ask your child to resend anything. Keep the device, account, chat, username, profile, and any URLs as they are, if it is safe to do so, and ask NSPCC, CEOP, children’s social care, or police what to do next.
5. **Get safeguarding advice today, even if you are unsure whether it “counts”.** You can contact the NSPCC Helpline on 0808 800 5000, usually 10am–4pm Monday to Friday, or email help@nspcc.org.uk at any time. You can also contact children’s social care at the relevant local council or local authority.
6. **Use the right urgent route if risk is immediate.** Call 999 if your child or another child is in immediate danger, if the adult is trying to reach them now, or if you cannot keep them safely apart.
7. **If the concern happened online, consider CEOP.** CEOP can receive reports about online sexual abuse or worrying online communication with a child. This can sit alongside urgent safety action if someone is at immediate risk.
8. **If it is not an emergency but you believe a crime may have happened, contact police on 101 or report online.** Keep what you say brief and factual: who the adult is, how they know your child, what was said or requested, whether there are messages or accounts, and whether the adult still has access to children.
9. **If the adult is linked to a school, club, faith group, tutor, activity, or workplace with children, contact their safeguarding lead.** Ask for the designated safeguarding lead at a school, or the safeguarding lead or officer in other settings. Say you are raising a safeguarding concern and need immediate advice about keeping your child away from that adult.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to make a formal police report or statement.
- You do not need to prove what happened before asking for safeguarding advice.
- You do not need to decide today whether it was “serious enough”.
- You do not need to tell extended family, other parents, or the adult’s friends today.
- You do not need to make a full plan for therapy, school changes, or long-term support right now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shocked, unsure, numb, angry, or worried about overreacting is common. Your job in this moment is not to investigate or decide everything; it is to keep your child safer, respond calmly, preserve what already exists without spreading it, and bring in people whose role is child safeguarding.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about reporting, support, school or activity arrangements, contact with the adult, and longer-term care may need specialist safeguarding, police, legal, clinical, or therapeutic help.

## Important note
This is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe a child is at immediate risk, call 999.
