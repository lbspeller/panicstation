# What to do if… you receive calls from creditors or debt collectors after someone has died (UK)

## Short answer
Do not agree to pay anything on the phone. Tell the caller the person has died, say you will only deal with the matter in writing, and ask them to send the claim to the estate.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay from your own money just to stop the calls unless you are sure you are personally liable, such as for a joint debt or a guarantee.
- Don’t confirm you are the executor, administrator, or personal representative if you are not sure.
- Don’t give your date of birth, bank details, workplace, or other personal information to a caller you did not contact first.
- Don’t accept a same-day phone deadline or agree to pay by card during the call.
- Don’t argue about the debt amount on the phone.
- Don’t ignore official court papers if any arrive; keep them safe and get advice promptly.

## What to do now
1. **End the call and move everything into writing.** Say: “They have died. I cannot discuss this by phone. Please send everything in writing to the estate.” Then end the call.

2. **Write a call log straight away.** Record the date, time, company name, caller name, phone number, account reference, what they claimed, and what they asked you to do.

3. **Ask for proof of the claim.** If you speak to them again, ask for:
   - the original creditor’s name,
   - the account or reference number,
   - the balance they say is owed,
   - the type of debt,
   - the address or email for their bereavement team.

4. **Do not send original documents unless you have checked the route.** Many firms accept a copy or scan of the death certificate. Keep originals safe and only send copies unless the firm clearly explains why an official copy is needed.

5. **Keep the debt separate from your own money.** If they ask you to pay, repeat: “Please put the claim in writing to the estate.” Do not agree to a payment plan, settlement, or card payment while you are still working out who is responsible.

6. **Work out who should deal with the correspondence.** The right person may be a personal representative, executor, administrator, or the person applying to deal with the estate. If that is not you, say: “I am not the person dealing with the estate. Please address correspondence to ‘The Estate of [Name]’ and I will pass it on.”

7. **Send a short notification when you can.** Write to the creditor or collector saying the person has died, asking them to confirm the claim in writing, and asking them to pause calls while the estate is being sorted.

8. **If calls become aggressive or excessive, complain in writing.** Tell the firm to stop phone contact and use written contact only. Keep copies. For complaints about debt collection by a financial business, complain to the firm first and ask whether the Financial Ombudsman Service can consider it if the firm does not resolve it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today which debts will be paid.
- You do not need to decide today whether the estate can pay everything.
- You do not need to apply for probate or letters of administration just because a caller is pressuring you.
- You do not need to find every account, statement, or policy immediately.
- You do not need to explain family details to a collector on the phone.

## Important reassurance
A call from a creditor or collector does not automatically mean you personally owe the debt. Debts usually need to be checked against the estate first, and it is reasonable to slow the situation down and insist on written contact.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent panic payments and keep the situation stable. Later decisions about estate debts, probate, insolvency, joint debts, guarantees, and complaint routes may need debt advice, probate advice, or legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, debt, probate, or professional advice. Rules and options depend on the type of debt, where in the UK the estate is being dealt with, whether you had any joint liability, and who is responsible for administering the estate.
