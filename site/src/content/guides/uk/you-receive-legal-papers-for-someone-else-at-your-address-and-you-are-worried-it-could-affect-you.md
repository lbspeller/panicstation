# What to do if… you receive legal papers for someone else at your address and you are worried it could affect you (UK)

## Short answer
Open the envelope specifically to identify the sender and reference number, then immediately contact them to state the named person does not live at your address.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore documents that look like court claims, statutory demands, or enforcement notices.
- Don’t throw the papers away or shred them until the issue is resolved with the sender.
- Don’t pay any money or agree to payment plans for a debt that is not yours.
- Don’t let enforcement agents or bailiffs into your home to "talk about it."
- Don’t assume that writing "not at this address" on the envelope and reposting it will stop a legal process already in motion.

## What to do now
1. **Open the mail if it appears to be a legal or debt-related document.** While you should usually return mail unopened, the Postal Services Act 2000 allows for a "reasonable excuse" to open mail; resolving a delivery error that could lead to bailiffs at your door is a valid reason.
   - Look for the name of the court, the solicitor, the claimant, or the enforcement agency.
   - Find the case reference number or account number.
2. **Contact the sender immediately using independently verified details.** Do not only use the phone number on the letter; search for the official website of the court or company to ensure you are speaking to the correct entity.
   - Tell them: "I am the current occupier. The person named on these papers does not live here."
   - Ask them to put a "hold" on the account or case while you provide proof of residency.
3. **Prepare a digital copy of your proof of occupancy.** Take a clear photo or scan of a recent document in your name at that address.
   - Use a Council Tax bill, a utility bill, or a signed tenancy agreement.
   - Redact your bank details or account numbers, but ensure your name, the address, and the date are visible.
4. **Send a formal notification in writing.** Email or use the online contact form of the sender to create a paper trail of your communication.
   - State clearly: "I am not the individual named in [Case Reference]. I have no connection to them. Please update your records and cease sending documents to this address."
   - Attach your proof of occupancy.
5. **Mark the original envelope and return it via Royal Mail.** Write **"NOT KNOWN AT THIS ADDRESS — RETURN TO SENDER"** in large, clear letters on the front of the envelope.
   - Put it in any red postbox or hand it to a postal worker.
   - If the documents were hand-delivered by a process server, do not post them; keep them for the sender to collect or follow their specific instructions.
6. **Secure your property against potential bailiff visits.** Keep your doors and windows locked, especially if the letter was a "Notice of Enforcement."
   - If an agent knocks, speak to them through the door or a window.
   - Show your ID and proof of occupancy through the glass; do not open the door.
   - Ask for their name and the company they represent.
7. **Contact Citizens Advice or a debt charity if the letters continue.** If you have notified the sender and letters still arrive, it may be a case of persistent error or identity misuse.
   - Keep a log of every letter received and every person you have spoken to.

## What can wait
- You do not need to pay for a solicitor unless you are also named as a defendant.
- You do not need to find the forwarding address for the previous tenant; that is the claimant's responsibility.
- You do not need to prove where the named person *is* currently living, only that they are *not* at your address.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a legal document for a stranger or former tenant is a common administrative error caused by outdated databases or "last known address" rules. You are not legally responsible for the debts or legal obligations of another person simply because mail was sent to your home. Taking these steps creates the necessary evidence to stop the process before it escalates to your doorstep.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only for individuals receiving mail for third parties. If you are personally named on the papers, or if the debt is yours but the name is misspelled, this guide does not apply and you should seek urgent debt or legal advice.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. While opening mail to resolve a crisis is generally protected under "reasonable excuse," you should never do so with the intent to act to the named person's detriment. Always verify enforcement agents' identities via the Certificated Bailiff Register.
