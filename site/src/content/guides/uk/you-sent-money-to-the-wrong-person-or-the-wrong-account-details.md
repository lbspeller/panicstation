# What to do if… you sent money to the wrong person or the wrong account details (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately and say you have made a misdirected payment to the wrong person or wrong account details. Ask them to start a recovery attempt, often called Credit Payment Recovery or a payment recall, and ask for a case reference.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send a second payment to “fix it” unless your bank tells you to.
- Don’t wait to see if the recipient replies before contacting your bank.
- Don’t assume nothing can be done once the money has left your account.
- Don’t delete banking messages, screenshots, emails, texts, or app notifications about the payment.
- Don’t pay anyone who offers to recover the money for an upfront fee.
- Don’t give security codes, passwords, or remote access to anyone who contacts you about the payment.

## What to do now
1. **Save the transaction details.** Screenshot or write down the amount, date and time, payee name shown, sort code, account number, payment reference, and any confirmation screen.

2. **Check whether the payment is still pending or scheduled.** In your banking app or online banking, look for words like “pending”, “scheduled”, “future-dated”, or “standing order”. If it has not been sent yet, cancel it in-app if your bank allows this.

3. **Contact your bank straight away.** Use the number on your card, your banking app, or your bank’s official website. Say: “I sent money to the wrong person or wrong account details. Please start a misdirected payment recovery or Credit Payment Recovery attempt now.”

4. **Ask for the recovery case details.** Ask for the case or reference number, the time and date it was logged, and what the bank will do next. Recovery is not guaranteed, but your bank should make a reasonable effort to help.

5. **Follow up in writing the same day.** Use secure message, email, or your bank’s complaint/contact route. Restate the amount, time, sort code, account number, payee name, and that you are asking for recovery of a misdirected payment.

6. **Match the next step to how you paid.**
   - **Faster Payments:** ask your bank to start Credit Payment Recovery. Faster Payments are usually too fast to cancel once sent, but banks that offer Faster Payments follow recovery procedures.
   - **Standing order or future-dated payment:** cancel any future payments to the wrong details, then ask about recovering any payment already sent.
   - **Bacs or CHAPS:** ask your bank what recovery or recall route applies to that payment type.
   - **Debit or credit card:** ask your card provider what dispute route applies, such as chargeback or Section 75 if it was a credit-card purchase.
   - **PayPal or another payment app:** open the platform’s official help or dispute process and keep messages inside the platform.

7. **If recovery fails, ask what details you can request in writing.** Ask your bank what recipient details they can provide, what evidence they need from you, and what options they say are available. Keep copies of every response.

8. **If you think you were tricked, treat it as possible fraud rather than only a typo.** Tell your bank you may be a victim of authorised push payment fraud and ask what reimbursement or claim route applies to your payment.
   - If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report it through Report Fraud.
   - If you are in Scotland, report it to Police Scotland online, by phone, or in person.
   - Keep any crime or report reference number.

9. **Reduce the chance of a second loss.** If you entered details after clicking a link, replying to a message, or speaking to someone unexpectedly, change your banking password, check for new payees, and remove any payee details you do not recognise.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to take court action.
- You do not need to argue with the recipient now; get the bank recovery process moving first.
- You do not need to work out the full complaint or ombudsman route immediately. That can wait until your bank has responded or the relevant complaint deadline becomes important.
- You do not need to decide whether to move banks today.

## Important reassurance
This is a common and stressful mistake, especially with long numbers, saved payees, and autofill. Acting quickly, keeping records, and using your bank’s official route gives you the best chance of recovery without creating a second problem.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and start the recovery process. Later decisions about complaints, the Financial Ombudsman Service, or court action may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Processes and outcomes vary by bank, payment type, and the facts of the transaction.
