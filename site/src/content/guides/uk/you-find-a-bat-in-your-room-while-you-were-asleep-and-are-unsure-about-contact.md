# What to do if… you find a bat in your room while you were asleep and are unsure about contact (UK)

## Short answer
If you were asleep and cannot confidently rule out a bite, scratch, or saliva contact, get urgent same-day medical advice and do not touch the bat with bare hands.

## Do not do these things
- Do not handle a live or dead bat with bare hands.
- Do not try to catch a flying bat.
- Do not wait for symptoms before asking for medical advice.
- Do not dismiss the risk just because there is no obvious bite mark.
- Do not let children or pets back into the room while the bat is still there.
- Do not dispose of a grounded, injured, or dead bat without first getting advice.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** Put on shoes, leave the room, close the door, and keep children and pets away.

2. **If the bat is flying, do not try to catch it.** If you can do this without going near the bat, open an outside window as wide as possible, dim the lights, close the door, and leave the bat alone in the room.

3. **If the bat is grounded, still, injured, or dead, do not touch it.** Keep it away from people and pets. If it can be safely covered without touching it or going near its mouth, use a box or similar container, then get advice before moving it.

4. **Check only for immediate exposure clues.** Look for any bite, scratch, bleeding, unexplained mark, new skin pain, or possible saliva contact with your eyes, mouth, or an open cut. Do not spend a long time searching your skin.

5. **Seek urgent same-day medical advice and say this plainly:** “I woke up and found a bat in my bedroom. I was asleep and I’m not sure whether there was contact.” Use NHS 111 where available, your urgent GP or out-of-hours GP route, or A&E or an urgent treatment centre if you are directed there.

6. **If there is any possible bite, scratch, or saliva contact, wash the area now.** Use soap and running water, then follow the medical advice you are given.

7. **If there is a visible mark, take one clear photo if it is easy.** Do not delay medical advice to get a better photo.

8. **For the bat itself, contact the Bat Conservation Trust National Bat Helpline if the bat is contained, grounded, injured, dead, or you need safe bat-specific guidance.** Keep the description simple: where the bat is, whether it is flying or grounded, and whether any person or pet may have had contact.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you were definitely bitten.
- You do not need to solve how the bat got in tonight.
- You do not need to deep-clean the room immediately.
- You do not need to arrange property checks before you have dealt with medical advice and immediate bat safety.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to feel shaken after waking to a bat in the room. The useful next step is not to prove what happened while you were asleep; it is to avoid touching the bat and get a prompt risk assessment.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: immediate safety, avoiding direct bat contact, and getting urgent assessment. Later decisions about entry points, repeat bat visits, pets, or property work may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, veterinary, legal, or professional advice. A health professional should assess possible bat contact, especially when you were asleep and cannot confidently rule it out.
