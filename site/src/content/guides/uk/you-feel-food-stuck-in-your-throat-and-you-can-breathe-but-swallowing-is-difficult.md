# What to do if… you feel food stuck in your throat and you can breathe but swallowing is difficult (UK)

## Short answer
Stop eating, sit upright, and do not try to force food or drink down. If breathing becomes difficult, you cannot speak normally, or you cannot manage your saliva, call 999. If you can breathe but swallowing remains difficult, get urgent same-day medical advice by calling 111 where available or using your local urgent-care route.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep eating to try to push it down.
- Do not chug water, fizzy drinks, or any other drink.
- Do not try to make yourself vomit.
- Do not put fingers, utensils, or objects into your throat.
- Do not lie down.
- Do not take tablets, capsules, or extra medicines while swallowing is difficult.
- Do not ask someone to do abdominal thrusts if you can breathe, speak, and cough strongly; those are for severe choking when the airway is blocked.

## What to do now
1. **Stop and sit upright.** Put down any food or drink. Sit straight up or stand, keep your head level, and take slow steady breaths.
2. **Check for airway danger.** Call 999 now if you have trouble breathing, noisy breathing, wheezing, blue or grey colour, cannot speak normally, or feel the airway is closing.
3. **Check whether saliva is going down.** If you cannot swallow your saliva, are drooling, or have to keep spitting because saliva will not go down, call 999 or go to A&E now if you can travel safely.
4. **If saliva is going down, do not rush it.** Stay upright and avoid more food. If you can swallow normally enough to take a tiny sip without coughing, you may try one or two small sips of still water. Stop immediately if it causes coughing, a wet or gurgly voice, pain, vomiting, or the fluid comes back up.
5. **Get urgent same-day advice if it does not settle quickly.** Call 111 where available, or use your local urgent-care route, and say: “Food feels stuck after eating. I can breathe, but swallowing is difficult.”
6. **Use urgent care sooner if there are red flags.** Seek urgent medical help now if you have significant chest or neck pain, repeated retching or vomiting, blood in saliva or vomit, worsening weakness or faintness, or symptoms that are getting worse.
7. **Arrange follow-up if this has happened before.** If the food clears and you feel safe, book a GP appointment for recurrent episodes, because repeated food-stuck symptoms can be linked to inflammation, narrowing, reflux, or other swallowing problems that may need checking.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause right now.
- You do not need to test different foods or drinks.
- You do not need to decide today whether you need tests or a procedure.
- You do not need to finish the meal.

## Important reassurance
This can feel frightening even when you are still breathing. A food-stuck feeling and sudden swallowing difficulty are recognised symptoms, and asking for same-day help is appropriate if it is not clearly settling or if saliva is hard to swallow.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety and getting the right level of urgent care. Later decisions may need assessment by a GP, urgent-care clinician, emergency department, or specialist, and may sometimes involve tests or endoscopy.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, diagnosis, or a substitute for urgent care. If symptoms are worsening, you cannot manage saliva, breathing changes, or you are unsure what to do, seek urgent medical help now.
