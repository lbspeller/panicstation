# What to do if… you feel food stuck in your throat and you can breathe but swallowing is difficult (UK)

## Short answer
Stop eating immediately and sit as upright as possible. If you cannot swallow your own saliva (drooling) or have a wet, gurgly voice, go to A&E immediately as this indicates a complete blockage. If you can still swallow sips of water but the sensation remains, contact NHS 111 for same-day urgent advice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep eating or swallow bread to try and "push" the food down.
- Do not force large amounts of liquid or use fizzy drinks, as these can increase the risk of breathing the liquid into your lungs (aspiration).
- Do not try to induce vomiting or put your fingers down your throat.
- Do not lie down or lean back.
- Do not take muscle relaxants or over-the-counter tablets to try and clear the blockage.
- Do not ignore the sensation even if it feels "minor" but persists for more than an hour.

## What to do now
1. **Sit as upright as possible.** Move to a firm chair and keep your head and neck straight. **This helps gravity and prevents saliva from entering your airway.**
2. **Assess your ability to swallow saliva.** Try to swallow your own spit.
   - If you cannot swallow saliva and find yourself drooling or having to spit into a tissue, **go to A&E immediately**.
   - If your voice sounds "wet" or gurgly when you speak, **go to A&E immediately**.
3. **Monitor your breathing.** Check for any new wheezing, whistling noises (stridor), or a persistent "barking" cough.
   - If breathing becomes difficult, noisy, or you cannot speak, **call 999 and follow choking instructions**.
4. **Try one small sip of water only if you can swallow saliva.** Take a tiny sip of still, room-temperature water.
   - **Stop immediately** if this causes coughing, pain, or if the water comes straight back up.
5. **Note what you were eating.** Remember the type of food (e.g., steak, dry bread, chicken bone) and the time it happened. **This information is vital for doctors to decide if they need to use a camera (endoscopy) to remove it.**
6. **Contact NHS 111 if the sensation persists.** If you can breathe and swallow sips of water but the "stuck" feeling does not go away within 30–60 minutes, **call 111 or use 111 online**. 
   - State clearly: "I have food stuck in my throat; I can breathe, but swallowing is difficult."
7. **Seek emergency care for chest or neck pain.** If you feel sharp pain in your chest or neck, or if you begin to feel feverish or weak, **go to A&E**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify why this happened right now; focus only on clearing the current blockage.
- You do not need to try different "home remedies" or textures to see if they pass.
- You do not need to worry about long-term dietary changes until you have been assessed by a professional.

## Important reassurance
The sensation of food being "stuck" is physically distressing and can cause significant anxiety. If you can breathe normally, your airway is currently clear. It is very common for the sensation of a "scratch" or a "lump" to remain for several hours even after the food has actually passed into the stomach.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization of a suspected food bolus obstruction where breathing is not currently compromised. It does not replace a clinical assessment.

## Important note
A complete inability to swallow saliva is a medical emergency. Always seek professional medical advice if symptoms do not resolve quickly or if you are in significant pain.
