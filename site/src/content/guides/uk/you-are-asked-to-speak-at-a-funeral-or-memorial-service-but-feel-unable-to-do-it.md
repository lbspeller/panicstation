# What to do if… you are asked to speak at a funeral or memorial service but feel unable to do it (UK)

## Short answer
Tell the person organising the service as soon as you can that you may not be able to speak, and ask for a backup plan. That could mean someone else reads your words, the celebrant or minister includes them, or the contribution is shortened.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force yourself to push through in silence if you already feel unable to manage it.
- Do not leave the organiser without warning if you already know you may not be able to speak.
- Do not apologise repeatedly or explain in detail; “I’m not able to do this” is enough.
- Do not write something long hoping it will feel easier on the day; shorter words are usually easier to hand over or read.
- Do not rely on your phone as the only copy, because battery, screen lock, or notifications can add pressure.

## What to do now
1. **Send one clear message to the organiser.**  
   “I’m not sure I’ll be able to speak on the day. Can we plan for someone else to read it, or use another option?”

2. **Tell the right person for this service.**  
   This may be the funeral director, celebrant, minister, faith leader, or the family member coordinating the service. Ask what change is easiest at this stage.

3. **Choose one fallback that does not depend on you speaking.**  
   Pick one:
   - Someone else reads your words.
   - The celebrant, minister, or faith leader includes your words in their tribute.
   - A short poem or reading is used instead of a personal speech.
   - A short written message is added to the order of service, if printing or changes are still possible.

4. **Prepare a hand-over copy even if you still hope to speak.**  
   Print one page in large font. At the top, write: “If I cannot continue, please read from here.” Give it to the backup reader before the service starts.

5. **Make the contribution very small.**  
   Aim for a few lines or about one minute, if that helps: who they were to you, one small memory, and one line of thanks or goodbye.

6. **Ask for practical support before the service starts.**  
   Ask where you will stand, whether there is a lectern or microphone, and whether water will be nearby. Ask one person to sit or stand close enough to take over if you stop.

7. **Step back cleanly if you need to.**  
   Say: “I can’t do the reading, but I can help by choosing a poem, sharing a written memory, helping with photos, or greeting people.” One alternative is enough.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find the perfect words now.
- You do not need to predict exactly how you will feel on the day.
- You do not need to decide family roles or who should speak.
- You do not need to turn this into a long tribute.
- You do not need to explain your grief to anyone in detail.

## Important reassurance
Many people find speaking at a funeral or memorial service much harder than they expected. Having someone else read your words, or making the contribution shorter, still lets your care be present without putting all the pressure on you.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce pressure and prevent a last-minute scramble. Later decisions about the service, family expectations, grief support, or faith and community traditions may need help from the funeral director, celebrant, minister, faith leader, bereavement service, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, pastoral, or other professional advice. Practices vary by venue, funeral director, faith or community tradition, and by what has already been printed or scheduled.
