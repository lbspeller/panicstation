# What to do if… you find a locked safe or strongbox and you suspect vital documents are inside (UK)

## Short answer
Do not attempt to force the safe open. Preserve the safe in its current location and contact the named executor, who should oversee a professional opening by an accredited safe engineer to ensure documents are not destroyed or compromised.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use drills, prying tools, or heat (such as a blowtorch) on the safe; these can ignite paper documents or trigger internal relocking devices.
- Don’t attempt to guess combinations repeatedly, as many digital or high-security mechanical safes will permanently "deadlock" after multiple failed attempts.
- Don’t remove the safe from the property alone or without documentation, as this can lead to accusations of asset concealment or theft.
- Don’t use generic "handyman" services; opening high-security safes without damage requires specialized tools and expertise.
- Don’t overlook potential toxic hazards; some older fireproof safes (pre-1980s) may contain asbestos or chemical fire-retardants that are dangerous if the casing is breached.

## What to do now
1. **Stabilize and secure the immediate area.** Put the safe in a secure room if it is portable, or lock the room it is in; ensure only the likely executor or administrator has access.
2. **Document the safe as found.** Take high-resolution photos of:
   - The safe in its original location.
   - The make, model, and serial number (usually on a plate on the back, side, or door).
   - Any signs of prior damage or tampering.
   - Any keyrings, envelopes, or notebooks nearby that might contain codes.
3. **Verify the legal authority to open it.**
   - If a Will exists elsewhere naming executors, they must authorize the opening.
   - If no Will is found, the "Administrator" (usually the next of kin) should lead the process.
   - If the safe itself is suspected to contain the only copy of the Will, search the National Will Register ("Certainty") first to see if a copy is held by a solicitor.
4. **Conduct a focused, non-invasive search for keys.** Spend no more than 15 minutes checking:
   - Labeled key hooks or drawers in the same room.
   - Address books or digital "password managers" belonging to the deceased.
   - Folders labeled "Warranties," "Manuals," or "House Papers" where keys or codes are often taped.
5. **Report suspected theft or external tampering.** If the safe appears to have been forced or items are clearly missing from the surrounding area, call **101** to report a potential crime and get a reference number before proceeding.
6. **Instruct an MLA-approved safe engineer.**
   - Look for a locksmith accredited by the Master Locksmiths Association (MLA) who specifically lists "Safe Opening" as a skill.
   - Request "Non-Destructive Entry" (NDE) where possible to preserve the safe and its contents.
   - Ensure at least two people (the executor and a witness) are present during the opening.
7. **Inventory the contents immediately upon opening.**
   - Record the opening on video if there is family tension.
   - List every item found; take photos of documents as they sit inside the safe before moving them.
   - Place all documents flat in a moisture-proof folder for preservation.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to value any jewelry or cash found inside immediately.
- You do **not** need to decide which family member "gets" specific items today.
- You do **not** need to contact banks regarding the contents until you have the original documents in hand.
- You do **not** need to dispose of the safe itself if it was damaged during opening; it may be repairable or needed for insurance evidence.

## Important reassurance
Finding a locked safe when you need urgent paperwork is a common and stressful hurdle in estate administration. Taking a few hours to find an expert rather than a few minutes to use a hammer prevents the permanent loss of the very documents you are trying to retrieve.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate physical and procedural stabilization of a locked safe. It does not provide legal advice on the distribution of assets or the long-term probate process.

## Important note
Opening a safe without the permission of the legal executor or administrator can be viewed as intermeddling in the estate. If there is a dispute over who has the right to the contents, keep the safe locked and seek advice from a probate solicitor.
