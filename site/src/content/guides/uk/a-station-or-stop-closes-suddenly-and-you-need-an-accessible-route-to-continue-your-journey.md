# What to do if… a station or stop closes suddenly and you need an accessible route to continue your journey (UK)

## Short answer
Pause where you are safe, then ask station or stop staff, or use a help point, to confirm a step-free route to the next workable station or stop before you move.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t follow a diversion blindly if it involves stairs, footbridges, long ramps, heavy crowding, or a lift that is out of service.
- Don’t let anyone rush you onto an alternative route you cannot safely leave unless staff confirm the next interchange and exit are step-free now.
- Don’t separate from your wheelchair, mobility aid, buggy, assistance dog, medication, or essential bag unless you choose to and it is genuinely safe.
- Don’t assume a “step-free station” is fully step-free today; lift faults, gaps, platform changes, crowd control, and partial step-free access can change what is workable.
- Don’t leave the station or stop area until you know your next step-free entry point and what to do if that entry point is also blocked.

## What to do now
1. **Stop at the nearest safe point where staff can see or hear you.** If the station or stop is being cleared, follow staff instructions only as far as you can do safely, then position yourself near staff, a help point, or a clear edge of the crowd.
2. **Ask for an accessible route continuation, not just another route.** Say:
   - “I need step-free routes only.”
   - “Can you confirm the next interchange and exit are step-free right now?”
   - “If the lift, ramp, or step-free entrance is not available, what accessible alternative is available now?”
3. **If you are on National Rail, ask staff to coordinate assistance for the rest of your journey.** Ask them to contact the next station, arrange a boarding ramp if needed, and confirm who will meet you at the next interchange or exit. Last-minute assistance is usually possible where staff are available, but it may take longer during disruption.
4. **If you are in London, ask staff to check live step-free options before you commit.** On TfL services, ask staff to confirm lift status, the correct entrance or exit, whether a boarding ramp or mini-ramp is needed, and whether an alternative step-free route is available.
5. **If the rail or Tube route is no longer step-free, ask for suitable accessible alternative transport.** Say: “This route is not accessible for me. What accessible alternative can you arrange to get me to a step-free station, stop, or destination point?” Availability depends on the operator, location, and disruption, so ask what can be arranged now.
6. **If you need to leave and re-enter the network, lock in the re-entry point first.** Ask: “Which station or stop should I re-enter at, which entrance should I use, and is that entrance step-free right now?”
7. **If you are travelling with someone else, split the tasks.** One person stays with you, your mobility aid, and belongings; the other speaks to staff, checks live accessibility updates, or finds the correct help point.
8. **If you feel overwhelmed, repeat one clear script.** Say: “I need step-free access. Please tell me the nearest accessible option and confirm it is working.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide who is at fault right now.
- You do not need to file a complaint, claim compensation, or document everything while you are still stranded.
- You do not need to find the fastest route; prioritise a route you can reliably complete step-free.
- You can wait to rearrange meetings, tickets, or onward travel until you are moving again or safely somewhere calm.

## Important reassurance
Sudden closures are disorienting, and accessibility disruptions add extra pressure. It is reasonable to slow the situation down and ask for a confirmed step-free plan before you move.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate disruption: stabilise, get accurate accessibility information, and secure a step-free continuation. Later decisions about complaints, refunds, compensation, or longer-term access planning may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, transport, or professional advice. Accessibility availability can change quickly because of lift faults, crowding, staffing, vehicle changes, or operational decisions. If an instruction would require you to do something unsafe or inaccessible, say so clearly and ask for a step-free alternative you can complete.
