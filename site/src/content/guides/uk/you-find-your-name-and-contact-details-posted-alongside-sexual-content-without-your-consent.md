# What to do if… you find your name and contact details posted alongside sexual content without your consent (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause, reduce ways strangers can reach you, and save basic proof before starting removal reports. Do not confront the poster, pay anyone, or delete evidence in a rush.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t reply to the poster, negotiate, threaten them, or warn them that you are collecting proof.
- Don’t pay money, send more images, or “prove” anything to anyone contacting you.
- Don’t delete posts, messages, accounts, voicemails, or notifications in a rush if they may contain evidence.
- Don’t share the links widely to warn people, because this can spread the content further.
- Don’t assume this is your fault or that you have to handle it alone.
- Don’t spend hours searching every site while you are panicking; start with the places causing contact or immediate exposure.

## What to do now
1. **Get a safety buffer first.**  
   If people are trying to locate you, coming to your home, threatening you, or you feel physically unsafe, stop engaging and move somewhere safer, such as near trusted people or staff. If there is immediate danger, call 999.

2. **Preserve simple proof.**  
   Take screenshots or screen recordings that show the page, your name or contact details, the account or username, the URL, and the date or time if visible. Write down where you found it and when.

3. **Reduce the routes to you.**  
   Silence unknown callers if your phone allows it, filter messages, tighten privacy on social accounts, remove visible phone numbers or email addresses, and limit who can message or tag you.

4. **Secure the accounts that could be used against you.**  
   Change passwords for your main email and social accounts. Turn on two-step verification, especially for email, because email often controls password resets for other accounts.

5. **Report the content to the site or app.**  
   Use the platform’s reporting tools. Report it as non-consensual intimate content or intimate image abuse if that fits, and also report the exposed personal information as doxxing, harassment, or personal information misuse.

6. **Use specialist takedown support.**  
   If you are 18 or over and in the UK, contact the Revenge Porn Helpline for free confidential help with intimate image abuse and takedown routes. You can also use Report Harmful Content for help finding reporting routes across platforms, and StopNCII if intimate images or videos of you may be re-uploaded on participating services.

7. **If any sexual image or video shows someone under 18, use child-protection routes.**  
   Do not share the image or video onward. Report online child sexual abuse images or videos to the Internet Watch Foundation, and report online sexual abuse or concerning online contact involving a child to CEOP or the police.

8. **Consider police reporting without pressuring yourself.**  
   Sharing, threatening to share, or creating fake intimate sexual images without consent may be a criminal offence. You can report through your local police force online, by calling 101, or in person; if you report, keep any incident or crime reference number.

9. **If strangers keep contacting you, treat the contact as part of the harm.**  
   Save abusive messages, voicemails, call logs, usernames, and links. Block and report accounts. If you are already in contact and it feels safe, one clear boundary such as “Do not contact me again” is enough; otherwise, do not respond.

10. **Get medical or emotional support if this connects to sexual assault or you feel overwhelmed.**  
   If you need medical support after rape or sexual assault, an NHS sexual assault referral centre can offer medical, practical, and emotional support. If you feel at risk of harming yourself or cannot stay safe, call 999 or go to A&E.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to pursue a court process, a solicitor, a public statement, or a formal complaint.
- You do not need to tell your employer, school, family, or friends right now unless there is an immediate safety or practical reason.
- You do not need to change your phone number or email immediately; filtering, privacy settings, and evidence preservation can come first.
- You do not need to identify the person yourself or prove exactly which offence applies.
- You do not need to collect perfect evidence from every site before asking for help.

## Important reassurance
This kind of posting is a known tactic used to humiliate, frighten, expose, or control people. Feeling shocked, shaky, angry, numb, or ashamed does not mean you did anything wrong. The first priority is to slow the contact, keep basic proof, and use reporting routes that do not require you to handle everything alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about police, legal options, workplace or school impact, ongoing harassment, and digital cleanup may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, digital security, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
