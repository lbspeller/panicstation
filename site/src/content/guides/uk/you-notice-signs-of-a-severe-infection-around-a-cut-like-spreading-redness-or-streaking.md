# What to do if… you notice signs of a severe infection around a cut like spreading redness or streaking (UK)

## Short answer
Seek urgent medical advice from NHS 111 or your GP practice immediately if redness is spreading or you see red streaks. If you also feel very unwell, have difficulty breathing, or show signs of confusion, call 999 or go to A&E.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait for symptoms to improve on their own if you see red streaks or spreading redness.
- Don’t try to pop, squeeze, or drain any blisters or pus-filled areas yourself.
- Don’t apply topical creams, oils, or "home remedies" like butter or honey to a suspected infection.
- Don’t scrub the area with harsh antiseptics or alcohol, as this can damage healthy tissue.
- Don’t take leftover antibiotics from a previous illness.
- Don’t ignore a high temperature or shivering if you have a red wound.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency "red flag" symptoms.** **Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if the person has a spreading infection plus any of the following:**
   - Difficulty breathing or breathing very fast.
   - Blue, grey, pale, or blotchy skin, lips, or tongue (on brown or black skin, this may be easier to see on the palms, soles of the feet, or inside the lips).
   - A rash that does not fade when a glass is rolled over it.
   - Confusion, slurred speech, or acting very drowsy/difficult to wake.
   - Feeling extremely cold, shivering, or feeling "like they might die."
2. **Contact NHS 111 for urgent same-day assessment.** **If the emergency signs above are not present, call 111 or use 111 online (for those aged 5 and over) to report "spreading redness" or "red streaks" from a wound.**
   - Tell the operator if you have a fever, severe pain, or if the redness is moving rapidly (tracking).
   - If your GP surgery is open, you can request an urgent "same-day" appointment instead.
3. **Track the progress of the redness.** **Use a clean pen to draw a line around the outer edge of the red area and write the time next to it.**
   - This allows you and medical staff to see exactly how fast the infection is moving.
   - Take a clear photo of the area in bright, natural light for comparison later.
4. **Remove restrictive items from the affected limb.** **Take off any rings, watches, or bracelets on the arm or leg where the infection is located.**
   - Swelling can occur rapidly, and jewellery can become a dangerous constriction or cut off circulation.
5. **Elevate and rest the area.** **Keep the affected limb raised on pillows above the level of the heart where possible.**
   - This helps reduce swelling and discomfort while you wait for medical advice.
6. **Perform basic cleaning if the wound is dirty.** **If the original cut is still dirty, rinse it gently under cool, clean running tap water for a few minutes and pat dry with a clean towel.**
   - Do not apply a tight bandage; use a loose, clean, non-stick dressing if available.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you need a hospital stay or IV treatment now; the clinician will decide after assessment.
- You do not need to identify the specific bacteria causing the infection yourself.
- You do not need to clean the wound repeatedly; once is sufficient before medical review.

## Important reassurance
Seeing a red line or spreading redness is a clear signal from your body that it needs help, and noticing it now allows for early intervention. Most skin infections like cellulitis or lymphangitis are highly treatable with antibiotics once identified by a professional.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage guidance for the first few hours of noticing a spreading infection. It does not replace clinical diagnosis or long-term wound management.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional medical advice. If you are immunocompromised, have diabetes, or the infection is on the face or near an eye, treat the situation with increased urgency.
