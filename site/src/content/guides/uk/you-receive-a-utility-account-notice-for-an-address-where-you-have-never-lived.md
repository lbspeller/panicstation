# What to do if… you receive a utility account notice for an address where you have never lived (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the utility company using independently verified details to report the account as fraudulent or an administrative error, and request an immediate "hold" on collections while you check all four UK credit reference agencies for unauthorized activity.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay any amount "under protest" to stop the letters; payment can be interpreted as an admission of liability or a link to the address.
- Do not use the phone numbers, links, or QR codes provided on the suspicious notice until you have cross-checked them against the company's official website.
- Do not ignore the notice; utility debts can lead to "default" markers on your credit file or court action (CCJs) even if the address is incorrect.
- Do not provide more personal data than is strictly necessary to identify the error (such as your full bank details or original passport).

## What to do now
1. **Verify the supplier and find their official contact details independently.** **Search for the company’s official website using a secure search engine to find their fraud or "new accounts" department phone number.**
   - Do not trust the "Contact Us" section of the letter you received.
   - If the company name is unfamiliar, check the Ofgem (Energy) or Ofcom (Broadband) registers to ensure they are a legitimate provider.
2. **Contact the utility company to formally dispute the account.** **State clearly that you have never lived at the address mentioned and that you did not authorize the opening of this account.**
   - Request that the account be marked as "not mine" or "suspected fraud."
   - Ask for a "billing hold" or "collections pause" while they investigate.
   - Request a written confirmation (email or letter) that you have raised this dispute and ask for a case reference number.
3. **Capture evidence of the incorrect notice.** **Take a clear photograph or scan of the notice and the envelope (including any postmarks) before you send any copies to the utility.**
   - Keep a log of every interaction, including the date, time, the name of the person you spoke to, and what was promised.
4. **Check all four UK credit reference agencies.** **Obtain your statutory credit report from Experian, Equifax, TransUnion, and Crediva to see if the unknown address or any associated debts have been linked to your name.**
   - Look for "searches" (applications) you did not make or "defaults" for services you do not use.
   - If you find the incorrect address on your file, use the "Notice of Correction" or "Dispute" tool on the agency’s website.
5. **Report the incident to the national fraud reporting service.** **File a report with the appropriate authority based on your location to obtain a crime reference number, which the utility may require to close the account.**
   - If in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland: Report via **Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) online or by calling 0300 123 2040.
   - If in Scotland: Report to **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
6. **Apply for a Cifas Protective Registration.** **Register for Protective Registration via the Cifas website (costing £30 for 2 years) to place a warning flag on your credit file.**
   - This requires lenders and service providers to carry out extra identity checks whenever your details are used to apply for credit or services.
7. **Escalate the complaint if the utility company fails to resolve the issue.** **If the company does not confirm the account is closed or continues to pursue you after 8 weeks (or 6 weeks for broadband), escalate to the relevant Ombudsman.**
   - **Energy:** Use the **Energy Ombudsman** after 8 weeks or upon receipt of a "deadlock letter."
   - **Broadband / Phone:** Use the **Communications Ombudsman** or **CISAS** after 6 weeks (for complaints raised from April 2026).
   - **Water:** Contact the **Consumer Council for Water (CCW)**; they may further refer you to **WATRS** if the company is a member.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify who opened the account or how they got your details; your priority is removing your liability.
- You do not need to hire a solicitor or legal representative for the initial dispute and Ombudsman process.
- You do not need to pay for a full "credit repair" service; you can dispute errors with credit agencies for free.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a bill for a strange address is a common symptom of either an administrative "mismatch" (where a name is incorrectly linked to a property in a database) or identity misuse. Most UK utilities have established protocols for "Gone Away" or "Identity Theft" cases. Once you have a case number and have notified the credit agencies, the burden of proof for the debt lies with the utility company, not you.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for UK-based utility disputes and identity theft. It does not cover legal defense for active court summons or long-term debt management strategies.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Jurisdictional rules regarding ADR (Alternative Dispute Resolution) timelines and reporting bodies are subject to change; always verify the current ombudsman for your specific provider.
