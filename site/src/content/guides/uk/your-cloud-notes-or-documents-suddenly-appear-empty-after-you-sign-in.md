# What to do if… your cloud notes or documents suddenly appear empty after you sign in (UK)

## Short answer
Immediately disconnect the device from all internet and network connections to stop the spread of the infection. Do not pay the ransom or engage with the attacker, as this does not guarantee the return of your data and may lead to further demands.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay the ransom, negotiate, or reply to the message to ask for proof or time.
- Do not click any links, buttons, or QR codes contained within the ransom note.
- Do not connect any external drives, USB sticks, or backup devices to the infected computer.
- Do not run unverified "decryption" or "removal" tools found online, as these can permanently corrupt your files.
- Do not restart the computer if you can avoid it, as some ransomware deletes decryption keys upon reboot.
- Do not log into sensitive accounts (banking, email, or password managers) using the infected device.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the infected device immediately.** 
   - Turn off the Wi-Fi using the hardware switch or software menu if accessible without clicking the note.
   - Unplug the Ethernet (network) cable from the back of the computer or the wall socket.
   - Disconnect Bluetooth and any docked connections to prevent the ransomware from spreading to other devices on your home or office network.
2. **Document the screen without interacting with the malware.** 
   - Use a separate mobile phone or camera to take clear photos of the ransom note and any specific "ID" or "Key" displayed.
   - Note down the exact time you first saw the message and any unusual file extensions you see on your desktop (e.g., .crypt, .locky, or random strings).
3. **Report the incident to the appropriate authorities.** 
   - **In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland:** Use the **Report Fraud** online portal or call 0300 123 2040.
   - **In Scotland:** Report the crime to **Police Scotland** by calling 101 or using their online cybercrime reporting form.
   - **For organizations:** Use the **NCSC (National Cyber Security Centre)** "Report a Cyber Incident" tool for technical guidance and signposting.
4. **Notify your organization or IT provider if the device is for work or school.** 
   - Contact your IT helpdesk using a different, clean device or phone.
   - Inform them that the device is already isolated from the network and that no contact has been made with the attackers.
5. **Secure your primary digital accounts from a clean device.** 
   - Change the passwords for your email, online banking, and cloud storage providers (e.g., iCloud, OneDrive, Google Drive).
   - Ensure Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is enabled on all these accounts to prevent the attacker from using stolen credentials.
6. **Check for data breach notification requirements.** 
   - If you are a business owner or sole trader and personal data of others was on the device, you may have a legal obligation to report the breach to the **Information Commissioner's Office (ICO)** within 72 hours.
7. **Seek specialist Scottish support if applicable.** 
   - If located in Scotland, contact the **Cyber and Fraud Centre - Scotland** (0800 1670 623) for specialized incident response and recovery advice.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to attempt a data recovery service today.
- You do not need to worry about the "countdown timer" shown on the screen; these are often psychological tactics to force a payment.
- You do not need to notify your entire contact list until you have confirmed if your email or address book was actually accessed.
- You do not need to buy a new computer immediately; the hardware is likely reusable after a professional wipe and reinstall.

## Important reassurance
Finding a ransom note is a high-stress event designed to cause panic. By isolating the device and refusing to engage with the attacker, you have already prevented the most common escalations of this crime. Most ransomware is opportunistic, and following a methodical recovery process is the safest way forward.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction only. It does not cover the technical process of file decryption, data restoration from backups, or forensic investigation.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal, technical, or professional security advice. Ransomware variants change rapidly; always prioritize instructions from the NCSC or your organization's official IT security policy.
