# What to do if… you are told you must be formally “served” but people keep contacting you without giving details (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the contact as a scam until you verify the claim independently through official government channels. Do not provide personal data or make payments to "stop" the process; genuine legal service does not require payment from you.

## Do not do these things
- Don't pay any money over the phone to "cancel service," "stop a warrant," or "settle a claim" to avoid being served.
- Don't give out your National Insurance number, date of birth, or bank details to an unsolicited caller or texter.
- Don't click links or download PDF attachments in emails or texts claiming to be from "the court" or "service department."
- Don't use the phone numbers or links provided by the person contacting you to "verify" their identity.
- Don't trust the Caller ID; scammers use "number spoofing" to make calls appear as if they are coming from local courts or 0300 numbers.
- Don't engage with automated messages (e.g., "Press 1 to speak with an advisor about your warrant"); HMCTS does not use automated messages for this purpose.
- Don't respond to any messages about legal service received via WhatsApp, as HMCTS and official process servers do not use this platform for formal contact.

## What to do now
1. **Switch to a passive evidence-gathering mode immediately.** **Document the specific date and time of the contact, the number used, the exact name of the caller or firm, and any "case reference" or "warrant number" they provided.**
2. **Ask for three specific identifiers and then end the call.** **Request the name of the court where the claim is filed, the full claim number, and the name of the claimant or solicitor firm.**
   - If the caller refuses to provide these or becomes aggressive, hang up immediately.
3. **Verify the existence of the claim using independent contact details.** **Look up the official contact number for the specific court mentioned via GOV.UK and call them directly to ask if that claim number exists and if your name is attached to it.**
   - You can also use the 159 service if you are concerned the contact is a financial scam targeting your bank account.
4. **Notify others in your household or place of work about the suspicious contact.** **Instruct them not to confirm your address, schedule, or employment details to anyone calling about "legal papers" or "service."**
5. **Report the suspicious activity to the correct national authority.** **In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, report the contact to "Report Fraud" (the successor to Action Fraud) online or at 0300 123 2040.**
   - If you are in Scotland, you must report the incident to Police Scotland by calling 101.
   - Forward suspicious text messages to 7726.
6. **Check for genuine civil judgments if you suspect a real debt might exist.** **Use the "TrustOnline" register to search for any active County Court Judgments (CCJs) or orders registered against your name and address.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you owe money or want to "settle" until you have physically seen and verified the paperwork.
- You do not need to hire a solicitor or seek legal representation until you have confirmed a genuine court case exists.
- You do not need to worry about being arrested "immediately"; civil process servers and enforcement agents follow strict procedural rules that do not involve surprise arrests for debt.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel startled or pressured when someone mentions "the court" or "legal service." Scammers rely on this urgency to bypass your logic. In the UK legal system, if a claim is real, it will be verifiable through a court office, and taking 24 hours to check the facts will not result in an immediate legal penalty.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for suspicious or vague legal contact only. If you receive genuine, verified court documents, you must follow the specific deadlines listed on those forms.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. If you are being harassed or threatened with physical harm, contact the police on 101, or 999 in an emergency.
