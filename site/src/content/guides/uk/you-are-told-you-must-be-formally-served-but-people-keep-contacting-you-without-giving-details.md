# What to do if… you are told you must be formally served but people keep contacting you without giving details (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and treat the contact as unverified until you have paperwork, a court name, or a reference you can check through an official route you found yourself. Do not pay, click links, or share personal details with the person contacting you.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay “to stop service”, “cancel a warrant”, or “avoid court” over the phone, by text, or by email.
- Don’t confirm your date of birth, National Insurance number, bank details, full address, employer, or daily routine to an unexpected caller.
- Don’t click links or open attachments in messages claiming to be about “service”, “court action”, a “warrant”, or a “claim”.
- Don’t use a phone number, link, email address, or payment route they provide to “verify” the case.
- Don’t assume repeated calls mean it is genuine; pressure, urgency, and vague details are common warning signs.
- Don’t agree to meet someone because a caller says you “must”, unless you have independently verified what the case is and you can do it safely.

## What to do now
1. **Save what you have.** Write down the date, time, phone number, email address, what they claimed, any reference number, and whether they asked for money or threatened arrest. Screenshot texts and emails, and save voicemails.
2. **Ask once for basic identifiers, then stop the exchange.** Use one calm sentence: “Please give me the court name, claim or reference number, and the name of the claimant or firm.” If they refuse, pressure you, or move to payment, end the contact.
3. **Verify through a route you choose.** Search for the court or tribunal on GOV.UK, not through the contact details they gave you. If they gave a court name and case number, ask that court whether the reference exists and whether you are named on it; the court may be limited in what it can tell you.
4. **Be cautious about claims that you must meet someone.** Court and legal documents can sometimes be served without a meeting. In England and Wales civil claims, service can include post, leaving documents at a permitted address, certain electronic methods where allowed, personal service, or another method authorised by the court. Rules can vary by court, case type, and part of the UK, so verify the specific claim rather than relying on the caller’s explanation.
5. **Stop giving away information.** Tell household members, reception staff, or anyone who may answer calls for you not to confirm your address, date of birth, employer, shift pattern, or whether you are at home. Let unknown calls go to voicemail.
6. **Report it if it looks like impersonation or fraud.** Forward suspicious texts to 7726 if your phone provider supports it. If you think it is fraud or impersonation, report it through Report Fraud if you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland; if you are in Scotland, report it to Police Scotland by calling 101.
7. **If real papers arrive, deal with them the same day.** Look for the court name, claim number, issue date, claimant, response instructions, and deadline. Before responding or paying anything, confirm anything unclear with the court using contact details you found independently.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to pay, admit anything, defend anything, or hire a solicitor.
- You do not need to keep answering unknown calls in case they are important.
- You do not need to explain your situation to the caller beyond asking for the identifiers needed to verify it.
- Deeper steps, such as identity checks or legal advice, can wait until you know whether there is a real case, unless you have already shared money or sensitive details.

## Important reassurance
A genuine process should be capable of being checked through a court, tribunal, solicitor, or official route. Taking a pause, refusing pressure, and verifying independently is a normal and sensible response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the situation, avoid a panic mistake, and find out whether anything genuine exists. If real paperwork exists, deadlines may be strict and later decisions may need help from a solicitor, advice agency, court staff, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, security, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe or you are being harassed, contact police on 101; if you are in immediate danger, call 999.
