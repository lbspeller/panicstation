# What to do if… you hit your head and now have a worsening headache, nausea, or unusual sleepiness (UK)

## Short answer
Get urgent medical help now because symptoms are worsening after a head injury. Go to A&E now for a worsening or persistent headache, vomiting, unusual sleepiness, confusion, memory problems, or any other worsening symptoms; call 999 instead if the person is hard to wake, cannot stay awake, has a seizure, has new weakness, numbness, speech, walking, vision or hearing problems, has signs of skull injury, had a high-force injury, or cannot get to A&E safely.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “sleep it off” alone if you feel unusually sleepy, your symptoms are worsening, or someone is finding it hard to wake you.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you feel sleepy, dizzy, confused, sick, or your symptoms are worsening.
- Do not drink alcohol or use recreational drugs after a head injury.
- Do not take sleeping tablets or other medicines that make you drowsy unless a clinician tells you to.
- Do not take aspirin for pain after a head injury unless a clinician has specifically told you to.
- If you are prescribed aspirin, antiplatelet medicine, or blood thinners, do not stop them without medical advice.
- Do not dismiss worsening symptoms because the hit “wasn’t that bad”.

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 now if any emergency danger sign applies.** This includes: the person was knocked out and has not woken up; they cannot stay awake or keep their eyes open; they have a seizure or fit; they have weakness or numbness in part of the body; they have problems walking, balancing, understanding, speaking, writing, seeing, or hearing; clear fluid is coming from the nose or ears; there is bleeding from the ears or bruising behind the ears; there is a black eye without direct injury to the eye; there is a dent to the head or a head wound with something inside it; the head was hit at speed, such as in a car crash, bicycle collision, being hit by a vehicle, or a diving accident; or they fell from more than 1 metre or more than 5 stairs.
2. **Go to A&E now if symptoms are worsening but none of the 999 signs apply.** This includes a worsening or persistent headache, vomiting after the injury, unusual sleepiness, confusion that is new for the person, memory problems, behaviour that is not normal for the person, any loss of consciousness that has now passed, or a head wound, swelling, or bruise that concerns you.
3. **If worsening nausea is the only symptom and none of the A&E or 999 signs apply, contact NHS 111 now for urgent direction.** Say: “head injury with worsening nausea,” and tell them when the injury happened. Go to A&E if 111 tells you to, if you cannot get advice promptly, or if any symptom worsens.
4. **Do not stay alone.** Ask a responsible adult to stay with you or the injured person, especially during the first 24 hours after the injury, and to call 999 or get you to A&E if symptoms worsen.
5. **Treat it as higher risk and choose A&E promptly if any apply:** you take anticoagulants, antiplatelet medicine, or aspirin; you have a bleeding or clotting disorder; you are age 65 or over; you had brain surgery in the past; the impact was high force; you had any loss of consciousness; or there is a memory gap from before or after the injury.
6. **Reduce strain while you are getting help.** Rest quietly, avoid alcohol and drugs, keep screens and bright light low if they worsen symptoms, and sip water only if you are fully awake and not repeatedly vomiting. For pain, paracetamol is the usual first choice unless you have been told not to take it.
7. **Have key details ready for 999, 111, or A&E.** Note when and how it happened, what has worsened, whether there was any blackout or memory gap, any vomiting, current medicines, allergies, major medical conditions, and whether the injured person is a child, pregnant, or medically complex.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether this is “concussion” or something else right now; the priority is being assessed because symptoms are worsening.
- You do not need to write a detailed symptom diary; just keep the injury time and the main changes clear.
- You do not need to decide about work, school, sport, driving, or travel today. That can wait until after you have been checked.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaken and unsure after a head injury is common, especially when symptoms appear or worsen later. Getting urgent assessment is a protective step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and get you to the right level of care. Later decisions about recovery, sport, work, school, driving, medicines, or follow-up may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not a diagnosis or a substitute for medical care. If you are unsure whether someone is more sleepy than usual, or the injured person is a child, pregnant, on blood-thinning medicine, or medically complex, choose urgent assessment rather than waiting.
