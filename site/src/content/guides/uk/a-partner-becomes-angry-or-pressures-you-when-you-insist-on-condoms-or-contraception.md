# What to do if… a partner becomes angry or pressures you when you insist on condoms or contraception (UK)

## Short answer
Pause the situation and get to a safer, calmer place before anything sexual continues. If a partner is angry, pressuring you, or blocking contraception, prioritise safety and support over continuing the discussion.

## Do not do these things
- Do not have sex without condoms or contraception to calm them down if that is not what you want.
- Do not keep negotiating in private if you feel intimidated, cornered, or scared.
- Do not accept guilt, threats, or “proof of love” arguments as a reason to continue.
- Do not let them control your phone, your contraception, or your access to medical care.
- Do not stay in the argument to prove you are right; if anger is escalating, leaving or getting help matters more.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safe pause immediately.** Use a short line such as “Stop. I’m not continuing.” Move to another room, the bathroom, a public area, or anywhere you can put space between you and them.
2. **If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.** If you cannot speak safely, call 999 from a mobile, listen to the operator, and press 55 when prompted so the call can be put through to police.
3. **Move the situation out of isolation.** Text or call someone you trust: “Can you call me now?” or “Can I come over?” If a simple reason helps you leave, use one, such as needing the bathroom, feeling unwell, or needing to pick something up.
4. **Keep contraception and health information away from them if you safely can.** Put pills, patches, rings, condoms, or emergency contraception somewhere private or take them with you. If you use an app, consider switching off notifications and using a passcode.
5. **If unprotected sex happened, a condom was refused, or a condom was removed, get health advice as soon as you safely can.** Emergency contraception is time-limited; it usually needs to be used within 3 to 5 days, depending on the method. Ask a pharmacy, sexual health clinic, GP surgery, 111 where available, or your local urgent health advice service. Emergency contraception is often free through NHS services.
6. **Get specialist support even if you are unsure what to call it.** If anything sexual happened without your consent, or you are unsure, Rape Crisis England & Wales can be contacted on 0808 500 2222. Rape Crisis Scotland and Northern Ireland sexual violence support routes are listed in the resources below. For domestic abuse support, GOV.UK lists current helplines for where you are in the UK; in England, the National Domestic Abuse Helpline can be contacted on 0808 2000 247. You do not have to report to police to ask for support.
7. **If sexual contact happened without your consent and you may want medical care or to report later, safety comes first.** If it is safe and you want to, avoid washing or cleaning up straight away and keep clothes or condoms separate in a clean bag.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to label this as abuse, assault, coercion, or a bad argument.
- You do not need to confront them, persuade them, or explain yourself perfectly.
- You do not need to make a big relationship decision today.
- You do not need to report to police before getting medical or specialist support.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to insist on condoms or contraception. If a partner responds with anger, intimidation, or pressure, that is not your fault. Freezing, complying, or going along to stay safe in the moment are common survival responses.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate harm and help you stabilise. Later decisions about healthcare, reporting, housing, safety planning, or the relationship may need specialist support at your pace.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and immediate first-step options in the UK. It is not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
