# What to do if… an official asks for next of kin and you are not sure who qualifies (UK)

## Short answer
Ask what they mean by “next of kin”: a practical contact person, or someone with formal authority for a specific purpose. Give the best reliable contact details you have, and clearly say what you are unsure about so it is recorded accurately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t guess and present it as certain if you are not sure.
- Don’t give private contact details until you have checked who is asking, which organisation they represent, and why they need them.
- Don’t assume “next of kin” by itself gives someone decision-making authority; in many UK settings, it is usually a contact role.
- Don’t get pulled into settling a family argument while the official is waiting for contact details.
- Don’t delay giving any contact at all because you cannot be completely certain; give what you know, with clear caveats.

## What to do now
1. **Ask what they mean by “next of kin”.** Say: “Do you mean the best person to contact, or someone with formal authority, such as an executor or power of attorney, if that is relevant here?”

2. **Check the requester before sharing details.** Ask for their name, role, organisation, and the reason they need the contact details. If this is by phone and you are unsure, call back using the organisation’s main public number or switchboard rather than a number they give you in the moment.

3. **Separate “best contact” from “closest relative”.** If the immediate need is communication, give the person who is most likely to answer, pass on messages, and handle the conversation calmly. Ask the official whether they will record that person as the contact rather than assuming they are the legally closest relative.

4. **Use a clear uncertainty script.** Say: “I am not sure who you treat as next of kin. The best reachable contact I know is [name, relationship, phone number]. Please record that I am unsure whether they should be treated as primary.”

5. **Offer more than one contact if there is family complexity.** Say: “There may also be [name, relationship, phone number]. Please record both relationships and ask your organisation which person should be contacted first.”

6. **If this relates to a death investigation, ask which contact role they are recording.** In England and Wales, a coroner’s office may use a primary family contact for communication, and that person does not have to be the closest relative. In Scotland, COPFS may use nearest-relative or family-contact routes during a death investigation. If you are unsure, say so and ask the official to decide what they need to record.

7. **Check for an existing nominated contact if the person is alive or in hospital.** Ask whether the organisation already has a nominated contact on admission, GP, care, or hospital records. Do not unlock a phone, search private messages, or share more than contact details unless you already have permission or staff confirm it is appropriate.

8. **Write down exactly what you gave.** Note the date and time, the requester’s name and organisation, why they said they needed the details, and the names, relationships, and phone numbers you provided.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide every possible legal meaning of “next of kin” right now.
- You do not need to resolve disputes between relatives before giving an initial contact.
- You do not need to find every possible family member before passing on the best known contact.
- Paperwork about wills, executors, powers of attorney, estate administration, or formal decision-making can usually wait until the immediate contact problem is calmer.

## Important reassurance
This is a common point of confusion. In many practical UK settings, “next of kin” is used to mean a main contact, and it is normal to need clarification. Being unsure does not mean you have done anything wrong; what matters is being honest about what you know and what you do not know.

## Scope note
These are first steps to help you respond safely and accurately in the moment. Later decisions about formal authority, medical decisions, death investigations, or estate administration may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Practice can vary by organisation and by UK nation, so it is reasonable to slow down, verify the requester, and share only the minimum contact details needed at this stage.
