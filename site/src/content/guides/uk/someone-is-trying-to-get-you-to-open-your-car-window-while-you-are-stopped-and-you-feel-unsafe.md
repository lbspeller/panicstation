# What to do if… someone is trying to get you to open your car window while you are stopped and you feel unsafe (UK)

## Short answer
Keep all doors locked and windows fully closed while remaining inside the vehicle. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999 and drive steadily to a busy, well-lit public area if it is safe to do so.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t open the window even slightly to hear the person better.
- Don’t unlock the doors or exit the vehicle to engage in conversation.
- Don’t hand over any items such as phones, keys, or wallets through a gap.
- Don’t follow the person's directions to move to a quieter or "more private" location.
- Don’t perform dangerous driving maneuvers like driving onto pavements or through red lights unless directed by emergency services.
- Don’t assume a person is a police officer just because they claim to be or are wearing high-visibility clothing.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the vehicle immediately.** **Check that all doors are locked, all windows are rolled up, and your seatbelt remains fastened.**
2. **Create a safe exit path.** **If you are stopped behind another vehicle, roll forward or backward slightly to ensure you have enough room to steer around them if you need to leave suddenly.**
3. **Move to a populated area.** **If the vehicle is mobile and you can move safely, drive to the nearest busy and well-lit location such as a staffed petrol station, supermarket forecourt, or police station.**
   - Do not stop in isolated areas.
   - Keep moving at a steady pace until you reach witnesses.
4. **Contact emergency services via 999.** **Dial 999 immediately if you feel threatened, if the person is attempting to open your doors, or if they are blocking your path.**
   - You are legally permitted to use a handheld mobile phone to call 999 or 112 in a genuine emergency if it is unsafe or impractical to stop.
   - If you cannot speak, listen to the operator and press 55 when prompted on your mobile to use the Silent Solution system.
5. **Attract external attention.** **Use your vehicle’s horn and flash your main beam headlights repeatedly to alert bystanders to the situation.**
6. **Limit communication to the glass.** **If you must respond, do so with a short, firm statement through the closed window such as "I am calling the police" and then cease all engagement.**
7. **Verify police identity if applicable.** **If the person claims to be an officer but is in plain clothes or an unmarked car, keep your doors locked and ask them to show their warrant card through the window.**
   - You have the right to ask them to radio their control room to confirm their identity.
   - You can also call 999 yourself to verify that a legitimate police stop is taking place at your location.
8. **Manage "bump and run" scenarios.** **If the person is approaching after a minor collision and you feel unsafe, stay inside the locked car and signal that you are calling the police.**
   - Note the registration and make of their vehicle if possible without opening your window.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the person's motive or whether their "emergency" is real right now.
- You do not need to apologize for being cautious or for refusing to open your window.
- You do not need to assess vehicle damage from a minor bump until you are in a safe, well-lit location.
- You do not need to check your social media or report the incident to your insurance provider in this moment.

## Important reassurance
Prioritizing your physical safety over social politeness is a correct and necessary response. It is normal to feel a "flight or fight" response in these situations; staying inside a locked vehicle provides a significant physical barrier that keeps you in control of the situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate minutes of a perceived threat while inside a vehicle. Subsequent legal reporting or insurance claims should be handled once you have reached a verified safe location.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. In any situation involving potential crime or immediate physical threat, always contact the police via 999 as your primary action.
