# What to do if… you develop pelvic pain with fever and unusual discharge starting (UK)

## Short answer
Seek a same-day medical assessment via your GP or NHS 111. If the pain is severe, you have a temperature of 38°C or above, you feel faint, or you might be pregnant, go to A&E or call 999 immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delay seeking medical advice to see if symptoms improve overnight.
- Do not douche or use internal vaginal "cleansers" as this can push infection further into the womb.
- Do not use tampons or menstrual cups; use external pads if there is discharge or bleeding.
- Do not have any sexual contact (including oral or with a condom) until a clinician confirms it is safe.
- Do not take any leftover or shared antibiotics; this can prevent accurate testing.
- Do not assume you cannot be pregnant because you are bleeding or using contraception.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel faint, dizzy, or are in severe pain.

## What to do now
1. **Identify emergency symptoms that require an immediate 999 call or A&E visit.**  
   **Act now if you have any of the following:**
   - Severe or rapidly worsening pain in the lower tummy or pelvis.
   - A temperature of 38°C or higher, or feeling very hot, cold, and shivery.
   - Pain in the tip of your shoulder or sharp pain under your ribs on the right side.
   - Feeling faint, dizzy, lightheaded, or passing out.
   - Repeated vomiting or feeling very unwell.
   - Heavy vaginal bleeding.
2. **Contact your GP or NHS 111 for a same-day urgent assessment.**  
   **Explain that you have the "triad" of symptoms: pelvic pain, fever, and unusual discharge.** Request a same-day appointment or speak to a clinician via 111 (phone or online) for urgent triage. If you are unable to see a GP, a sexual health (GUM) clinic can provide specialist testing for these symptoms, but they may not offer emergency care for severe pain.
3. **Take a pregnancy test immediately if there is any possibility you could be pregnant.**  
   **Perform a home test now and share the result with the clinician.** This is critical to rule out an ectopic pregnancy, which can present with similar symptoms to a pelvic infection and is a life-threatening emergency.
4. **Prepare a brief summary of your symptoms for the clinician.**  
   **Write down the following details to ensure nothing is missed:**
   - When the pain started and its exact location (e.g., left, right, or central).
   - Your highest recorded temperature.
   - The color, consistency, and smell of the discharge.
   - The date of your last period and your current method of contraception (especially if you have an IUD or coil).
   - Any recent new sexual partners or known exposure to STIs.
5. **Isolate the potential infection by avoiding sexual contact.**  
   **Do not have sex until you have completed treatment and any partners have also been tested.** This prevents re-infection or spreading a potential infection while waiting for results.
6. **Use simple pain relief if safe for you to do so.**  
   **Take paracetamol or ibuprofen according to the packet instructions if you have no contraindications.** Avoid alcohol or other substances that could mask a worsening of your condition or interfere with clinical assessment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific type of bacteria or infection yourself.
- You do not need to notify past partners until a clinician provides a diagnosis and advises on the partner notification process.
- You do not need to collect your own discharge samples at home; these must be taken by a professional during an examination.

## Important reassurance
Pelvic pain accompanied by fever and discharge is a common clinical presentation with established treatment pathways. While these symptoms require urgent investigation to prevent complications like Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID), most infections are highly treatable with a course of antibiotics. Seeking help now is the most effective way to resolve the pain and protect your long-term health.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions for the initial hours of symptom onset. It does not cover long-term treatment plans, antibiotic side effects, or fertility counseling.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not replace professional medical diagnosis. If symptoms are severe or if you are pregnant, prioritize emergency medical intervention over the steps listed here.
