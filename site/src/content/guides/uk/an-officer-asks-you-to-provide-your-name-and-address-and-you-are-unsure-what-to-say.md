# What to do if… an officer asks you to provide your name and address and you are unsure what to say (UK)

## Short answer
Ask the officer if you are being detained and if you are legally required to provide your details. If you are not legally required (such as during a standard stop and search or stop and account), you may decline; if you are required (driving, anti-social behaviour, or under Section 60), provide accurate details only.

## Do not do these things
- Don't provide false details or an alias.
- Don't walk away before the officer confirms you are free to go.
- Don't offer information about your destination, employment, or companions.
- Don't attempt to physically resist a stop or search.
- Don't argue about the law or the officer's grounds in the moment.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the legal status of the interaction immediately.** Say:  
   "**Am I being detained, or am I free to go?**"
2. **Determine if the request is a legal requirement.** If the officer says you are not free to go, ask:  
   "**Am I legally required to provide my name and address?**"
3. **Ask for the specific power being used.** If they say "yes," ask:  
   "**Under what power are you requiring this?**"
4. **Identify the situation to determine your obligation (England and Wales).**
   - **If you are driving or supervising a learner:** You are required to provide your name and address and produce documents (or produce them at a station within 7 days) under the Road Traffic Act 1988.
   - **If the officer suspects Anti-Social Behaviour (Section 50):** A constable in uniform (or a designated PCSO) can require your name and address if they believe you are acting in an anti-social manner.
   - **If you are in a Section 60 area:** If a Section 60 (suspicionless search) is in place, you are required to provide your name and address.
   - **If you are being issued a Dispersal Order (Section 35):** You are required to provide your name and address if you are being directed to leave an area.
5. **Provide only the minimum required information if obligated.** Say:  
   "**My name is [Name]. My address is [Address].**"
6. **End the interaction once the requirement is met.** If the officer continues to ask questions, say:  
   "**I have provided my details as required. Am I now free to leave?**"
7. **Request legal advice if the situation escalates to arrest.** Say:  
   "**I wish to speak to a solicitor.**"
   - Do not answer further questions until legal advice is available.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to lodge a formal complaint right now.
- You do not need to check your own records to verify the officer's claims on the street.
- You do not need to provide proof of identity (like a passport) unless you are driving and do not have your licence.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel nervous or find it difficult to speak clearly when stopped by the police. You are allowed to take a moment to process the question. Asking for the legal power being used is a standard way to clarify your situation and does not imply guilt.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for police interactions involving requests for identification in England and Wales. Powers in Scotland and Northern Ireland differ, particularly regarding Section 50.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Police powers can change; if you are detained or arrested, always seek professional legal representation.
