# What to do if… you are served with a protection or restraining order (UK)

## Short answer
Assume the paperwork is serious and time-sensitive: read the conditions, stop contact with the applicant unless the order clearly allows a specific method, and check the next court date or deadline.

## Do not do these things
- Do not contact the applicant “to clear it up”, including through friends, family, social media, shared accounts, or indirect messages.
- Do not go to their home, workplace, school, or usual places if the paperwork restricts you from doing so.
- Do not assume the order is unenforceable because you disagree with it, because you have not told your side, or because another hearing is listed.
- Do not post about the case online or share screenshots of the order or court papers.
- Do not ignore a hearing date, response deadline, police instruction, or court instruction in the pack.
- Do not “test” the boundary with one message, one visit, one call, or a message through someone else.

## What to do now
1. **Create a no-contact buffer immediately.** Put your phone on Do Not Disturb, mute or block the applicant’s number and social profiles so you do not reply impulsively, and tell one trusted person: “Please do not pass messages between us.”

2. **Read the paperwork once, slowly, and write down the must-know details.** Note:
   - the type of order, such as a non-molestation order, occupation order, restraining order, domestic abuse protection order, or another named order;
   - the exact restrictions on contact, distance, places, third-party messages, social media, shared property, childcare, or work routes;
   - the start date, end date, and whether it says “until further order”;
   - the court name, case number, hearing date, hearing time, and whether the hearing is in person or remote;
   - any deadline for filing a statement, acknowledgement, response, or application.

3. **Act as though the restrictions are active once you have been served or clearly told about them.** If the wording is unclear, take the stricter route until you have advice. Do not rely on the applicant saying contact is “fine” unless the order itself, the court, or your legal adviser confirms that route is allowed.

4. **Verify the hearing and deadlines with the court named on the papers.** Use official court contact details found independently, not just a number in a message. Ask the court office:
   - “Is there a hearing listed, and what is the date, time, location, or remote-link process?”
   - “Is there a deadline for me to file or send anything?”
   - “How should I send documents to the court?”
   - “Is there any instruction about service on the other party?”

5. **Get legal help quickly, even for one urgent appointment.** Ask for a solicitor or legal adviser experienced in the exact order named on the paperwork. If money is a worry, ask about legal aid eligibility, fixed-fee urgent advice, and any urgent advice or support available for people attending court without a lawyer.

6. **Plan today so you do not miss court.** Put the hearing in your calendar with reminders. If you need an interpreter, disability adjustment, remote attendance information, or help finding the courtroom, contact the court office as soon as you can.

7. **Change daily routes and routines to avoid accidental breach.**
   - If the order names streets, buildings, schools, workplaces, or shared places, plan a different route before you leave home.
   - If childcare, school runs, pets, shared bills, or belongings are affected, do not improvise direct contact. Use a solicitor, legal adviser, the court process, or another route clearly allowed by the order.
   - If the order affects where you can live or enter, arrange a temporary alternative and do not “just pop in”.

8. **Handle essential belongings through a safe formal route.** Do not attend in person unless the order clearly permits it and the arrangement is documented. Ask a solicitor, legal adviser, the court, or another authorised channel how collection should happen.

9. **Keep a simple evidence folder without investigating.** Save the order, envelope, emails, texts already in your possession, receipts, work logs, location records you already have, and a short factual timeline of dates and times. Do not contact the applicant or potential witnesses to gather statements.

10. **If police contact you about an alleged breach, stay calm and get legal advice before giving a detailed account.** Follow any immediate safety instruction, ask what order or incident they are referring to, and ask for a solicitor if you are interviewed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to contest, accept, vary, discharge, or negotiate the order.
- You do not need to write a long statement tonight unless the paperwork gives an immediate deadline.
- You do not need to contact mutual friends, employers, relatives, or online groups to explain your side.
- You do not need to understand every legal label right now; follow the exact restrictions written on the order and confirm deadlines with the court.

## Important reassurance
Being notified about a protection order can feel shocking, frightening, or humiliating. The safest first step is not to argue with the applicant or rush into explanations; it is to prevent any possible breach, confirm the court timetable, and get advice. Breach consequences can be serious and may lead to arrest, charge, or further court action, but careful calm steps now can reduce risk.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours and days after you are notified. Later decisions may need specialist legal help, especially if the order affects your home, children, work, immigration position, criminal case, or existing family court proceedings.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Protection orders and court processes vary by order type, wording, court, and which UK legal system applies. If you are unsure about a condition, take the cautious route and get qualified advice.
