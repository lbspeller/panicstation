# What to do if… you notice sudden swelling in one leg (UK)

## Short answer
If sudden one-sided leg swelling comes with severe breathlessness, chest or upper back pain, fainting, collapse, or feeling severely unwell, call 999 now. If you have any new breathing symptom, coughing up blood, or no emergency signs but the swelling is sudden and only in one leg, get same-day medical advice through 111 or the urgent-care route for where you are.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “wait and see” overnight if swelling appeared suddenly and is only in one leg.
- Do not massage, deeply rub, or strongly stretch the swollen or painful area.
- Do not do strenuous exercise to “work it out.”
- Do not take leftover blood thinners or someone else’s medication.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you feel unwell, dizzy, breathless, faint, or have chest pain.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency warning signs right now. Call 999 if any are present:**
   - severe, new, or worsening breathlessness
   - chest pain or upper back pain, including pain that is worse when breathing in
   - fainting, collapse, or someone passing out
   - a very fast heartbeat with chest or breathing symptoms
   - feeling severely unwell

   If you need A&E urgently, do not drive yourself. Ask someone to take you or call 999 for an ambulance.

2. **If you are coughing up blood, have sudden breathing difficulty that is not severe, or do not have emergency signs but the leg swelling is sudden and one-sided, use the same-day urgent-care route for where you are:**
   - **England:** use **111 online** if you are aged 5 or over, or **call 111**.
   - **Scotland:** call **NHS 24 on 111**.
   - **Wales:** call **NHS 111 Wales**, or use its online tools if you prefer.
   - **Northern Ireland:** use your **GP out-of-hours** route, usually through your GP practice voicemail or website, or a local **Phone First** urgent-care route where available or requested locally. If you cannot access urgent advice promptly or you are getting worse, go to the nearest Emergency Department.

   When you speak to someone, say clearly: “sudden swelling in one leg.” Tell them whether there is pain, warmth, redness, darkened skin, tenderness, breathlessness, chest pain, coughing up blood, fainting, or feeling severely unwell.

3. **If you are pregnant or have recently given birth:** treat sudden one-leg swelling as same-day urgent. Contact your maternity unit or triage line. If you cannot reach them promptly, use the urgent-care route above.

4. **Make the situation easier to assess:**
   - Note when the swelling started and how quickly it changed.
   - Look for pain, warmth, redness, darkened skin, tenderness, or swollen surface veins.
   - If practical, take two photos from the front and side so both legs can be compared.
   - Write down recent details that may matter: long travel, being immobile, a recent hospital stay, recent surgery, recent injury, hormone contraception, HRT, previous clots, cancer treatment, pregnancy, or recently giving birth.
   - Have your medication list, allergies, and NHS number ready if you know it.

5. **While waiting for advice or transport:**
   - Remove tight socks or tight clothing around the swollen area.
   - Rest comfortably and avoid strenuous activity.
   - Do not massage the leg.
   - Change position gently if you need to, but do not try to exercise the swelling away.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to decide about scans, blood tests, or treatment options before you are assessed.
- You do not need to contact several services at once: use 999 for emergency signs, otherwise use one same-day urgent-care route for your UK nation.
- You do not need to search for every possible explanation online before asking for help.

## Important reassurance
Sudden swelling in one leg can have causes that are not dangerous, but it can also be a sign of a blood clot that needs prompt assessment. Getting checked the same day is a calm and sensible step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stay safe and reach the right urgent assessment. Later decisions may need a clinician after examination and, if needed, tests.

## Important note
This guide is general information only, not medical or professional advice, and not a diagnosis. If you feel rapidly worse, develop breathing symptoms or chest pain, faint or collapse, or cannot safely get help another way, use emergency services.
