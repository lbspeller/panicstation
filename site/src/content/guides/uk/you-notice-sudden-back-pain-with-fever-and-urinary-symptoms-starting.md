# What to do if… you notice sudden back pain with fever and urinary symptoms starting (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your GP for an urgent same-day appointment or call NHS 111 immediately. Fever, shivering, or vomiting combined with pain in your back (just under the ribs) and urinary symptoms suggests a kidney infection that requires prompt medical assessment today.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait until tomorrow to seek help if you have a fever, shivers, or flank pain.
- Do not take non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) like ibuprofen or naproxen unless specifically told to by a doctor, as these can increase the risk of kidney problems during an infection.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics or medication prescribed for someone else.
- Do not drive yourself to a medical facility if you feel dizzy, confused, or the pain is severe.
- Do not ignore symptoms because you have already started over-the-counter UTI treatments (like cranberry or cystitis sachets).

## What to do now
1. **Call your GP practice for an urgent same-day appointment or contact NHS 111 now.**
   - Tell the operator you have: **“fever/shivers + pain in my back just under the ribs + urinary symptoms like burning or frequency.”**
   - If you cannot reach your GP, use the NHS 111 online service or call 111.
2. **Check for sepsis "red flags" and call 999 or go to A&E immediately if any are present.**
   - **Confusion, slurred speech, or not making sense.**
   - **Difficulty breathing or breathing very fast.**
   - **Blue, pale, or blotchy skin, lips, or tongue** (on brown or black skin, this may be easier to see on the palms of the hands or soles of the feet).
   - **A rash that does not fade** when a glass is rolled over it.
3. **Identify if you are in a high-priority group while waiting for a callback.**
   - Seek immediate help if you have these symptoms and:
   - **You are pregnant.**
   - **You have a weakened immune system or diabetes.**
   - **You have not peed all day** (or for the last 12 hours).
   - **You are vomiting** and cannot keep fluids or medications down.
4. **Prepare essential information for the clinician.**
   - Note your temperature if you have a thermometer.
   - Confirm if the pain is on one side of your back or both.
   - List any allergies, especially to antibiotics like penicillin or trimethoprim.
   - Have a list of any regular medications ready.
5. **Stabilise your physical state while awaiting assessment.**
   - **Sip water frequently** to stay hydrated, unless you are vomiting.
   - **Take paracetamol** for pain and fever if it is safe for you to do so, but avoid ibuprofen.
   - **Rest in a comfortable position** and ask someone to stay with you or check on you every 30 minutes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the exact cause (such as a kidney stone versus an infection) before seeking help; a clinician must do this.
- You do not need to provide a urine sample until you are at the clinic or have been told how to collect it.
- You do not need to decide on long-term work or childcare arrangements until your immediate clinical status is confirmed.

## Important reassurance
Sudden back pain with a fever is physically draining and can feel alarming. Taking the step to contact 111 or your GP now is the most effective way to manage the pain and prevent the infection from worsening. Most kidney infections are effectively treated with a course of antibiotics.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and urgent triage for the first few hours of symptoms only. It does not replace a clinical diagnosis or a full treatment plan.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a medical diagnosis. If symptoms are severe, you feel very unwell, or you suspect sepsis, call 999 or go to your nearest A&E immediately.
