# What to do if… you are asked to declare cash or valuables and you are unsure what counts as a declaration (UK)

## Short answer
State clearly to the officer that you wish to make a full and correct declaration and ask them to clarify if they are requesting a "cash declaration" or a "goods/valuables declaration." If you are carrying £10,000 or more in any form of currency or cash-equivalent, you must disclose the total immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not say "nothing to declare" if you are uncertain; this can lead to immediate seizure of funds and a penalty of up to £5,000.
- Do not attempt to split cash between group members or different bags to stay below the £10,000 or €10,000 threshold.
- Do not hide, move, or conceal cash once you have entered the customs area or have been approached by an officer.
- Do not assume that "cash" only refers to physical banknotes; it includes cheques, bearer bonds, and in some regions, gold or prepaid cards.
- Do not argue about the definitions of "used" or "personal" items; provide the information requested and ask for the official form to record your statement.

## What to do now
1. **Ask the officer for a specific definition of their request:** **Request clarification by saying, "I want to ensure I comply with the rules—are you asking for a declaration of physical cash or a declaration of goods and valuables?"**
   - A cash declaration relates to the money you are physically carrying.
   - A goods/valuables declaration relates to items you have purchased abroad or are bringing into the UK for the first time.
2. **Apply the correct threshold for your specific travel route:** **Identify whether you are subject to the Great Britain or Northern Ireland reporting limits.**
   - For Great Britain (England, Scotland, Wales): You must declare £10,000 or more (or equivalent) if travelling to or from any country outside the UK (including the EU).
   - For Northern Ireland: You must declare €10,000 or more (or equivalent) if travelling to or from a non-EU country, or if arriving in Northern Ireland from Great Britain.
3. **Calculate your total based on the broad legal definition of "cash":** **Include all physical notes and coins, but also include specific "cash-like" instruments.**
   - Include: banknotes, coins, cheques (including travellers' cheques), and bearer bonds.
   - If in Northern Ireland: You must also include gold coins, bullion, nuggets, and prepaid cards in your total.
4. **Utilize the online declaration service if the time window allows:** **Check if you are within the 72-hour window for cash or the 120-hour window for goods.**
   - Cash must be declared online no earlier than 72 hours before travel.
   - Goods/valuables can be declared online up to 5 days (120 hours) before arrival.
5. **Request a paper form if you are already at the port or airport:** **Tell the Border Force officer you need to complete a "CASH/C88" or "BOR9011" form immediately.**
   - If no officer is visible, go to the Red Channel or use the "Red Point" telephone provided in the customs hall.
   - If you cannot use online services, call the HMRC helpline at 0300 322 9434 for assisted digital support (available Monday to Friday, 8am to 6pm).
6. **Provide clear documentation for high-value personal items:** **State which items were already yours before you left the UK and which are new purchases.**
   - Have receipts for new purchases ready.
   - For items you took out of the UK (e.g., an expensive watch or engagement ring), explain they are personal used property if asked.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a professional currency conversion; a reasonable estimate based on recent market rates is acceptable for the initial verbal disclosure.
- You do not need to provide an exhaustive history of the funds in the first ten seconds; the immediate priority is the fact of the declaration itself.
- You do not need to prove ownership of every personal item you are wearing unless specifically challenged; standard personal effects are usually exempt from "goods" declarations if they are within personal allowances.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel pressured or confused when questioned at a border. Customs officers are trained to deal with travelers who are unsure of the rules. By proactively stating you want to declare and asking for guidance, you are demonstrating "good faith," which is the most effective way to avoid penalties or the seizure of your property.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for travelers at the UK border. It covers the immediate actions required to avoid non-declaration penalties but does not cover the specific tax or duty rates for commercial "merchandise in baggage."

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Customs laws, particularly those involving the movement of goods between Great Britain and Northern Ireland, are subject to the Windsor Framework and may change. Always follow the direct instructions of a Border Force officer.
