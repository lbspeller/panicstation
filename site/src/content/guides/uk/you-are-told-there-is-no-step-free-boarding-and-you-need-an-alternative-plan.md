# What to do if… you are told there is no step-free boarding and you need an alternative plan (UK)

## Short answer
Do not try to board in a way that feels unsafe. Ask staff to arrange a step-free alternative now, such as a different platform, a different accessible station, a different route, a later train with boarding help, or suitable accessible transport.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to use stairs, be lifted, or cross a gap or step if it is not safe for you.
- Do not let anyone rush you into boarding without a ramp, level access, or another confirmed safe method.
- Do not leave your mobility aid behind unless you choose to and it is genuinely safe.
- Do not leave the staffed area without telling staff if you still need help completing the journey.
- Do not throw away receipts, screenshots, or messages if you have to pay for something to get unstuck.

## What to do now
1. **Move to the safest nearby place you can.** Get away from platform edges and crowds if possible, such as to a waiting room, concourse, ticket office, information desk, or staffed point.

2. **Ask for assisted travel support now.** Speak to station staff, the ticket office, the information desk, gate line staff, or use a Help Point if you cannot find anyone.

3. **Use a clear request.** Say: “I can’t board here without step-free access. Please arrange a step-free alternative now: an accessible platform, an accessible route, or suitable accessible transport to an accessible station.”

4. **Ask staff to contact control or a supervisor if needed.** Say that you need a practical travel plan, not just directions, because the planned boarding point is not accessible to you.

5. **Ask for the most workable alternative.** Depending on the station and service, ask for:
   - a different platform at this station that has step-free boarding;
   - a different train or route that is step-free now;
   - a later train where a ramp or safe boarding method can be provided;
   - transport to the nearest or most convenient accessible station, without extra charge where this applies; or
   - permission to use another operator or route if staff can confirm that your ticket can be accepted.

6. **Make the ticket check simple.** Ask staff what you should show at barriers, onboard checks, or the next station. If possible, ask for a message, reference number, written note, or the staff member’s name and the time of the agreed plan.

7. **If you have to pay to get unstuck, keep proof.** Take a photo of the station name, time, any lift or access notice, and any relevant app message. Keep receipts and note who you spoke to.

8. **Say clearly if waiting is making things worse.** If pain, fatigue, medication timing, toileting needs, temperature, anxiety, or battery charge are becoming urgent, tell staff and ask for somewhere seated, warm, accessible, and safe while the alternative is arranged.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a formal complaint.
- You do not need to argue about blame or policy at the platform.
- You do not need to explain medical details beyond what staff need to know to help you travel safely.
- You do not need to solve the whole journey at once; the next safe step is enough.

## Important reassurance
Access failures can be frightening and humiliating, especially when other people are watching or waiting. You are not being difficult by refusing unsafe boarding and asking for a step-free alternative.

## Scope note
These are first steps for getting safe, getting help, and finding a practical alternative for this journey. Later decisions about refunds, redress, complaints, or legal rights may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. What can be arranged immediately may depend on the operator, station staffing, train type, disruption, and what is safe at the time.
