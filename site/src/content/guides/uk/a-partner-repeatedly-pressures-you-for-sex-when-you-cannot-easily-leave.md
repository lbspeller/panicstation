# What to do if… a partner repeatedly pressures you for sex when you cannot easily leave (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause first: create physical space, contact someone, or move towards other people. Pressure is not consent, and you do not owe sex to stay safe or keep the peace.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to prove you have a good enough reason to say no.
- Do not agree to go somewhere more private to calm them down.
- Do not send long explanations or accusations while you are still with them or relying on them for a lift, access to your home, or a way out.
- Do not make your phone use more visible if you think they monitor you.
- Do not blame yourself for freezing, going quiet, going along, or changing your mind to reduce danger.
- Do not assume you must report to police to get support or medical care.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** If you are with them now, use a neutral interruption if it helps you move away: “I need the toilet,” “I feel unwell,” “I need to make a call,” or “I’m stepping outside for air.” Move towards other people, staff, a public area, or a room with a lock if that is the safest available option.
2. **Keep your way out close.** If you can do it without increasing risk, keep your phone and keys on you, keep shoes or an outer layer on, and stay nearer to an exit. If you are in a car, ask to be let out somewhere public, such as a petrol station, shop, transport stop, or well-lit area.
3. **Bring in another person quietly.** Text or call someone you trust with a simple line such as “Can you call me now?” or “I need picking up.” If you cannot speak freely, send a pre-agreed word, phrase, or emoji. If you are at a venue, hotel, bar, gym, workplace, or station, ask staff to help you leave discreetly.
4. **Use emergency help if you feel in immediate danger.** In the UK, call 999 if a crime is in progress or someone is in immediate danger. If you call from a mobile and cannot speak, listen to the operator and press 55 if prompted. For non-emergency police contact, call 101.
5. **Contact specialist sexual violence support, even if you are unsure what to call it.** In England and Wales, the Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line is 0808 500 2222, free and open 24/7 for people aged 16 and over. In Scotland, Rape Crisis Scotland is 08088 01 03 02, open every day from 5pm to midnight. In Northern Ireland, the Domestic & Sexual Abuse Helpline is 0808 802 1414 and is available at any time.
6. **Consider medical and practical support if any sexual contact happened that you did not freely choose.** A Sexual Assault Referral Centre can usually help with injury checks, STI prevention or testing, emergency contraception where appropriate, and support options. The NHS SARC finder is for England; for Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland, a specialist helpline can help you find the right local route.
7. **Use domestic abuse support too if this is part of a wider pattern.** The National Domestic Abuse Helpline is 0808 2000 247 and is free and open 24/7. Men’s Advice Line is 0808 8010327 and is open Monday to Friday, 10am to 5pm.
8. **Get urgent medical help if needed.** If you have serious injury, feel at risk now, or need emergency help, call 999 or go to A&E. If it is not an emergency but you are not sure what medical help you need, use your local NHS urgent advice route or out-of-hours service.
9. **Make a private note only if it is safe and useful for you.** If you want to, write down the date, time, place, what happened, and anything you want to remember, in a place they cannot access. This is for your memory and choices later, not something you have to do.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to call it assault, abuse, coercion, or anything else.
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report to police.
- You do not need to confront your partner today.
- You do not need to collect evidence or make a formal statement now.
- You do not need to work out the whole relationship today; focus on immediate safety and support first.

## Important reassurance
Many people freeze, fawn, go quiet, or go along when they feel trapped. Those are common survival responses, not consent and not a failure. Repeated pressure in situations where you cannot easily leave is serious, and it makes sense if you feel panicked, confused, or unsure.

## Scope note
These are first steps to help you get safer and supported. Later decisions about housing, money, reporting, medical follow-up, or the relationship may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information for immediate harm-reduction and support, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call emergency services. If you think your phone or internet use may be monitored, try to use another device, move to a public place, or ask staff or a trusted person to contact help for you.
