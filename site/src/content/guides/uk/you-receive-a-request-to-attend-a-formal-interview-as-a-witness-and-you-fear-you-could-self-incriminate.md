# What to do if… you receive a request to attend a formal interview as a witness and you fear you could self-incriminate (UK)

## Short answer
Do not attend or provide any information until you have obtained independent legal advice. State clearly that you are not refusing to cooperate but will only engage after consulting a solicitor to ensure your privilege against self-incrimination is protected.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attend "for a chat" or to "clear things up" alone, even if the police insist you are only a witness.
- Do not sign a Section 9 witness statement or any written document without a solicitor’s review.
- Do not answer "preliminary questions" over the phone or in person before legal advice is secured.
- Do not offer to unlock mobile devices, provide passwords, or consent to searches.
- Do not delete any digital communication, call logs, or photos that may be relevant to the investigation.
- Do not speculate or guess during any interaction with officers.

## What to do now
1. **Identify your specific UK jurisdiction immediately.** Procedures and rights regarding witness testimony and cautions differ significantly between England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. **Tell the officer you need to confirm the legal framework of the request before proceeding.**
2. **Request all interview details in writing from the officer.** Ask for the officer's name, rank, collar number, station, and a direct callback number. **Ensure the written request specifies if the interview is voluntary, what the alleged offence is, and whether you will be treated as a witness or a suspect.**
3. **Ask the officer explicitly if the interview will be conducted under caution.** If they state you are a witness but you fear your answers may involve you in illegal activity, the privilege against self-incrimination applies. **Record their response to this question in your own notes immediately.**
4. **Arrange for free legal representation before any police station meeting.** In England and Wales, you are entitled to the duty solicitor for voluntary interviews at a police station; in Scotland, you can request the Solicitor Contact Line. **Inform the police that you are exercising your right to legal advice and will provide a date for the interview once a solicitor is instructed.**
5. **Establish if you are free to leave at the start of any encounter.** If the police visit you or you attend a station, ask: "Am I under arrest, and am I free to leave at any time?" **If you are told you are free to leave, you may end the interaction at any point if you feel the questioning is moving toward self-incrimination.**
6. **Use a neutral holding statement if pressured for an immediate response.** Say: "I am not refusing to assist, but I require legal advice before making any formal statement or attending an interview." **Do not allow the officer to bypass this by framing the matter as "urgent" or "minor."**
7. **Create a private, factual record of all contact for your solicitor.** Document the time of the call or visit, the exact words used by the officer, and any documents they requested you bring. **Keep this record secure and do not share it with anyone other than your legal representative.**
8. **Maintain strict digital privacy regarding the investigation.** Avoid discussing the police contact or the underlying facts on social media, messaging apps, or with third parties. **Limit your communication to stating that you are seeking legal advice regarding a witness request.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to provide a full account today.
- You do not need to review your own files or evidence before speaking to a solicitor.
- You do not need to justify why you want a solicitor to the police.
- You do not need to worry about "looking guilty" by requesting legal protection; it is a standard procedural right.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel anxious when contacted by the police, regardless of your involvement. The transition from "witness" to "suspect" can happen quickly during an investigation; requesting a solicitor is a professional, protective measure used by many people in this situation to ensure the process remains fair.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for initial contact with UK law enforcement. It does not cover trial procedures, the specific laws of evidence, or long-term legal defense strategies, which must be managed by a qualified solicitor.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Police powers and individual rights are subject to complex legislation including PACE (England and Wales), the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Act, and PACE NI. Always consult a qualified solicitor before participating in a formal interview.
