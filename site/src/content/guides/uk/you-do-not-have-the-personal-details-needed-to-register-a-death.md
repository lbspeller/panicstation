# What to do if… you do not have the personal details needed to register a death (UK)

## Short answer
Tell the register office exactly what you do and do not know, and do not guess. Bring the best confirmed information you can find quickly, then ask the registrar how unknown or uncertain details should be handled.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t guess dates, spellings, addresses, names, occupations, or family details just to get through the appointment.
- Don’t delay contacting the register or registration office once you have been told registration can go ahead.
- Don’t assume you must have every document before you speak to the registrar.
- Don’t give original documents to anyone unless you are sure they are part of the official registration, medical, care, or funeral process.
- Don’t try to notify every bank, pension provider, utility, insurer, or subscription service before you have handled the registration question.

## What to do now
1. **Make a short “known / not known” list.**  
   On one page, write:
   - details you are sure of, and where you got them from
   - details you are unsure about
   - details you do not know and will not guess

2. **Contact the register or registration office handling the death.**  
   Say: “I need to register a death, but I do not have all the personal details.” Ask:
   - whether the registration can go ahead yet
   - which details they need for the appointment
   - what they want you to do if a detail is unknown
   - whether any documents would help, and whether copies are enough for now

3. **Check only quick, reliable places for the missing details.**  
   Look for:
   - passport, driving licence, birth certificate, marriage certificate, or civil partnership certificate
   - recent official letters showing name, address, date of birth, National Insurance number, pension, benefits, or NHS details
   - care home, hospital, GP, or hospice paperwork you have already been given
   - funeral director paperwork, if a funeral director is already involved

4. **Ask the person or office already involved what they have.**  
   Depending on the situation, this may be the hospital bereavement office, care home manager, GP surgery, hospice, funeral director, medical examiner’s office, coroner’s office, procurator fiscal, or registration office. Ask only for the specific missing details needed for registration.

5. **If official paperwork is not ready, ask where the delay is.**  
   A death generally cannot be registered until the required medical, registrar, coroner, procurator fiscal, or other official process allows it. Ask: “Has the registration office received what it needs, or who should I contact next?”

6. **At the appointment, use calm, exact wording.**  
   If you are asked for something you cannot confirm, say: “I do not know and I do not want to guess. Can this be recorded as unknown, or can I provide it later if I find it?”

7. **After registration, ask about the simplest notification route.**  
   Ask the registrar whether Tell Us Once is available in your situation, or what the equivalent next notification step is. Tell Us Once is not available for every situation in the UK.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find every personal document before you contact the register or registration office.
- You do not need to solve probate, the estate, house contents, pensions, or all accounts today.
- You do not need to make final funeral decisions just to answer the registrar’s personal-detail questions.
- You do not need to contact every organisation at once; handle the registration step first unless something is urgent.

## Important reassurance
People often do not have all the details when someone has died, especially after a sudden death, estrangement, a hospital or care-home death, or scattered paperwork. It is acceptable to say “I do not know” and ask the registrar what to do next.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you get through the registration questions without guessing or taking on everything at once. Later decisions about corrections, certificates, probate, money, property, and the estate may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Registration processes can vary across England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, so check with the register or registration office handling the death.
