# What to do if… you learn a data protection incident may be blamed on you and you are asked for an immediate response (UK)

## Short answer
Cease all engagement with the sender immediately and secure digital copies of the messages. Prioritise personal safety and follow your organisation’s internal reporting procedures to address the conduct formally.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply while feeling distressed or attempt to counter-attack.
- Do not delete any evidence or clear the message history.
- Do not attempt to meet the individual alone to resolve the matter.
- Do not forward the messages to other colleagues or post them on social media.
- Do not assume the behaviour is "just work stress" if it feels hostile or threatening.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safe distance from the device.** Silence notifications for the specific contact or application to prevent further immediate exposure. If a message contains a direct threat to your physical safety, **call 999 immediately**.
2. **Preserve every interaction exactly as it appears.** Take screenshots of the messages, ensuring they show the sender’s name, the date, the timestamp, and the full text. Save these files to a personal device or drive rather than a work-controlled account if possible.
3. **Log the context of the messages.** Record the details of when the messages arrived, the platform used (e.g., WhatsApp, Slack, personal SMS), and the physical or emotional impact the contact had on you at the time.
4. **Isolate your personal communication channels.** If the colleague is using your personal phone number or social media, do not respond on those platforms. Move any future communication to official work channels only.
5. **Review your employer's Bullying and Harassment policy.** Locate the specific procedure for raising a concern, which may include contacting a designated "Speak Up" officer, HR, or a manager not involved in the situation.
6. **Report the incident formally.** Submit a factual account of the messages to your manager or HR. State clearly that the conduct is unwanted and is creating an intimidating or hostile environment. 
7. **Request a formal grievance meeting if informal steps are insufficient.** If the behaviour is severe, repeated, or involves discrimination, follow the formal grievance procedure outlined in your contract.
   - You have a statutory right to be accompanied to a formal grievance hearing by a "companion."
   - A companion must be a work colleague, a trade union representative, or an official employed by a trade union.
8. **Contact the Acas helpline for independent guidance.** Call 0300 123 1100 (Monday to Friday, 8am to 6pm) to discuss your rights and the correct procedural steps for your specific situation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether you will stay in your current role.
- You do not need to determine the legal classification of the behaviour (e.g., "harassment" vs "bullying") before reporting it.
- You do not need to respond to any "urgent" work-related requests contained within aggressive messages.
- You do not need to provide a face-to-face explanation to the sender.

## Important reassurance
It is a common and valid reaction to feel anxious, hyper-vigilant, or physically shaken when your personal time is interrupted by hostility. These reactions are biological responses to a breach of safety and boundaries. Moving the issue into a structured, documented process is the most effective way to regain control.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling immediate workplace-related hostility outside of hours. It does not cover long-term legal claims, employment tribunal representation, or detailed whistleblowing procedures.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Workplace rights can vary based on your specific contract and length of service. If you are a member of a trade union, contact them immediately for specific representation. If the behaviour involves persistent stalking or threats of violence, report the matter to the police via 101 or their online reporting tool.
