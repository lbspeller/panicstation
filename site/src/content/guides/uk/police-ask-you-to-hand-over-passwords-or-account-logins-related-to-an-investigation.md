# What to do if… police ask you to hand over passwords or account logins related to an investigation (UK)

## Short answer
Do not provide any passwords or unlock devices immediately; instead, request a solicitor and ask the officer to confirm in writing whether they are serving a formal Section 49 RIPA disclosure notice.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t provide a PIN or password "just to be helpful" — once access is granted, police can often search much more than the specific item mentioned.
- Don’t assume an officer's verbal request or a printed "advisory" sheet is a legal command that requires immediate compliance.
- Don’t guess or provide "close" passwords if you are unsure — repeated failed attempts can permanently lock or wipe the device.
- Don’t provide two-factor authentication (2FA) codes, SMS prompts, or hardware security keys without legal advice.
- Don’t attempt to remotely wipe, delete, or "clean" accounts or devices after they have been seized, as this may constitute perverting the course of justice.
- Don’t sign into any cloud service, email, or social media account on a device currently in police possession.

## What to do now
1. **Request a solicitor immediately.** **Ask for legal advice before answering any questions about your devices, passwords, or accounts.** 
   - If you are at a police station, you are entitled to free, independent legal advice from a duty solicitor.
2. **Clarify the legal basis of the request.** **Ask the officer: "Are you serving me with a formal Section 49 notice under the Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000?"**
   - Most initial requests are informal or accompanied by an "Advisory Notice" which explains the law but does not yet compel you to act.
   - A formal Section 49 notice usually requires judicial permission and must be provided to you in writing.
3. **Identify the document provided.** **Check if the paper you have been handed is an "Advisory Notice" or a "Section 49 Disclosure Notice."**
   - An Advisory Notice is a warning that they *may* apply for a power to compel you later; it does not carry a criminal penalty for refusal at that moment.
   - A Section 49 Disclosure Notice is a legal requirement that carries a penalty of up to 2 years in prison (or 5 years in cases of national security or child indecency) for non-compliance.
4. **Maintain your right to silence regarding passwords.** **Tell the officer: "I am not refusing to comply with the law, but I will not provide any access until I have consulted with my solicitor."**
   - You do not have to "prove" you don't know a password on the spot. 
5. **Secure your physical devices if they are still in your possession.** **Keep devices locked and do not use biometric features (FaceID / Fingerprint) if you are being asked to unlock them under pressure.**
   - Police can seize devices regardless of whether you provide the password.
6. **Note the details of the interaction.** **Write down the officer’s name, collar number, the exact time of the request, and which specific accounts or devices they asked to access.**
7. **Leave if you are not under arrest.** **If you are attending a voluntary interview or are stopped in public and are told you are free to leave, do so and contact a solicitor immediately.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to comply with a future court order right now.
- You do not need to explain why you have a password or why you are choosing to wait for a solicitor.
- You do not need to worry about the police "breaking into" the phone immediately; forensic extraction usually takes time.
- You do not need to notify your service providers (Apple, Google, etc.) about the seizure until you have had legal advice.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel intimidated when an officer demands a password or warns you about "obstruction." Requesting a solicitor and insisting on the proper service of a legal notice is a standard and protected procedural right. It is not an admission of guilt.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization when a request for digital access is first made. It does not cover the long-term legal strategy for contesting a RIPA notice or the specific procedures for Terrorism Act (Schedule 7) stops at ports and airports, which have different rules.

## Important note
This information applies to the UK but legal procedures can vary between England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. This is not legal advice. If you are served with a formal Section 49 notice, you must seek immediate specialist legal representation, as failure to comply is a criminal offence.
