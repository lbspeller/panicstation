# What to do if… messages start auto-archiving or skipping your inbox and you did not change any settings (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the account by changing the password and enabling two-step verification, then audit and remove all unauthorized filters, rules, and forwarding addresses.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use the affected email account to request password resets for other services (banking, social media) until the account is secured.
- Do not click links in "Security Alert" emails that arrive while this issue is occurring; navigate directly to the provider's official login page instead.
- Do not delete the contents of your "Archive" or "Deleted Items" folders immediately, as these contain the messages you are missing and evidence of when the changes began.
- Do not assume a technical glitch is the cause if you also see "sent" messages you did not write or unknown devices in your login history.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the account credentials immediately.** 
   **Change your email password to a strong, unique phrase that you do not use elsewhere.** 
   - Ensure two-step verification (2SV) or multi-factor authentication (MFA) is active. 
   - Use the "Sign out of all other sessions" or "Sign out everywhere" option provided in your account security settings to terminate any active unauthorized access.

2. **Audit and remove unauthorized mail rules or filters.** 
   **Check for any instruction that tells the server to "Skip the Inbox," "Archive it," "Mark as read," or "Move to folder."** 
   - In Gmail, look under Settings > Filters and Blocked Addresses for rules applying to "all mail" or broad keywords.
   - In Outlook, check Settings > Mail > Rules for any automation moving mail to "Conversation History" or "Archive."

3. **Disable unauthorized email forwarding.** 
   **Check your "Forwarding and POP/IMAP" settings for any email addresses you do not recognize.** 
   - Attackers frequently set up a "silent forward" so they receive a copy of every email you get, even after you change your password.
   - Remove any unknown addresses and disable the forwarding function entirely.

4. **Verify global service status and "Smart Features."** 
   **Check the Google Workspace Status Dashboard or Microsoft Service Health page to rule out a known platform outage.** 
   - In early 2026, some users experienced filtering failures due to "Smart Features" updates. 
   - If no outage is listed, proceed with the assumption that settings were changed manually by an external party.

5. **Review third-party app and "Delegate" access.** 
   **Revoke access for any apps, add-ons, or "productivity tools" that you do not actively use or remember authorizing.** 
   - Check "Account Delegation" (Gmail) or "Mailbox Permissions" (Outlook) to ensure no other user has been granted the right to read or manage your mail.

6. **Report the compromise to the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC).** 
   **Forward any suspicious "security alert" or "account change" emails that you did not initiate to report@phishing.gov.uk.** 
   - This helps the NCSC identify and block malicious infrastructure used in account takeovers.

7. **Report fraud or financial risk to the appropriate UK body.** 
   **If you have lost money or your identity has been used for fraud, contact your bank immediately and report the incident.** 
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland: Use the **Report Fraud** service (formerly Action Fraud) at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040. 
   - In Scotland: Call **Police Scotland** on 101 or contact the **Cyber and Fraud Centre Scotland** on 0800 1670 623.

## What can wait
- Organizing or re-filing the messages that were moved to the Archive folder.
- Responding to non-urgent emails that were missed during the period of misdirection.
- Investigating the specific IP addresses or locations of the unauthorized logins, as this information is for reporting rather than immediate stabilization.

## Important reassurance
It is a recognized pattern for unauthorized users to set "auto-archive" rules to hide their activity, such as password reset notifications from other accounts. Noticing this change early is a critical step in preventing further loss. Standard security procedures are effective at stopping this behavior once access is revoked.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and account recovery. It does not cover forensic investigation of a device or the recovery of third-party accounts (e.g., social media or banking) that may have been accessed using the compromised email.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional IT or legal advice. If this is a work or school account, you must contact your organization's IT department or Data Protection Officer immediately, as they may have specific reporting requirements under UK GDPR.
