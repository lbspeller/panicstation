# What to do if… you receive a credible warning that someone is coming to confront you at your location (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer position, create distance from doors and entrances, and involve other people who can help. If you feel at risk of harm, someone is threatening violence, or they may be arriving imminently, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go outside, open the door, or meet them to calm things down.
- Do not get drawn into arguing by text or call while you are trying to stay safe.
- Do not stand at windows, doorways, communal entrances, or car parks to check whether it is them.
- Do not broadcast your location, movements, or plans in group chats or on social media.
- Do not stay isolated if you can safely avoid it.
- Do not ask an unprepared friend, neighbour, or colleague to confront them for you.

## What to do now
1. **Move to the safest available place.**
   - If you can leave safely before they arrive, go to a staffed public place nearby, such as a shop, reception area, workplace lobby, or transport hub.
   - If leaving could increase risk, stay inside, move away from doors and ground-floor windows, and choose a place with a solid barrier between you and the entrance.
   - Keep your phone, keys, charger, and any essential medication with you.

2. **Secure access points without putting yourself in danger.**
   - Lock doors and accessible windows if you can do this quickly and safely.
   - If you are in a shared building, do not go into hallways, stairwells, car parks, or communal entrances to investigate.
   - If there is an intercom, door camera, or reception desk, use that instead of going to the door.

3. **Bring in on-site help immediately.**
   - At work or in a venue, tell reception, security, or a manager: “I’ve had a credible warning someone may come to confront me. Please do not let anyone in to see me, and call police if they try to force contact.”
   - At home, call or message a trusted neighbour, friend, or nearby family member so someone knows what is happening. Ask them to stay on the phone, alert building staff, or help you contact police if needed.
   - If children, older people, disabled people, or pets are with you, move them away from the entrance and keep them with a safe adult if possible.

4. **Decide which police route fits the risk right now.**
   - Call 999 if there is immediate danger, someone is threatening violence, someone is trying to get in, a crime is happening now, or the situation could become violent very soon.
   - Use 101 or police online reporting if it is serious but not immediate, so there is a record and you can ask for advice.
   - If you are unsure whether it is immediate, treat safety as the priority and call 999.

5. **Prepare the essentials for a clear call.**
   - Write down the exact address or location, including floor, flat number, entry instructions, and any safe access point.
   - Note who may arrive, how you know, what they said or did, whether weapons were mentioned, and any description or vehicle details.
   - Say what you need plainly, such as: “I need officers to attend,” “I need advice,” or “I need this logged because they may be on their way.”

6. **If you cannot speak safely on a 999 call, use the Silent Solution.**
   - Dial 999.
   - Listen to the operator’s questions and respond by coughing or tapping if you can.
   - If prompted, press 55 on a mobile to show it is a genuine emergency and to be connected to police.
   - Try to give your location in any safe way you can, because a silent call alone may not be enough to identify exactly where help is needed.

7. **Make it harder to be intercepted.**
   - Do not go to predictable exit points such as the front door, car park, or usual smoking area unless you are leaving safely with a clear destination.
   - Pause non-essential location sharing and avoid live updates in messaging apps for now.
   - Keep any threatening messages, missed calls, voicemails, doorbell footage, or screenshots, but do not stay in danger to collect evidence.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to make a formal statement, pursue charges, move address, change jobs, or take legal action.
- You do not need to explain the full history to multiple people. Focus only on what helps keep you safe in the next minutes to hours.
- You do not need to respond to further messages from the person or anyone relaying messages, unless responding clearly helps immediate safety.
- You do not need to prove that your fear is reasonable before asking for help.

## Important reassurance
A credible warning can trigger shaking, racing thoughts, nausea, tunnel vision, or feeling stuck. That does not mean you are overreacting. The safest first approach is to increase distance, reduce access, and get support involved.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance for the next minutes to hours. Later decisions may need support from police, victim support services, workplace safety staff, housing services, or legal advice, depending on what happened.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, security, or professional advice. If you think harm is possible or imminent, prioritise immediate safety and contact emergency services.
