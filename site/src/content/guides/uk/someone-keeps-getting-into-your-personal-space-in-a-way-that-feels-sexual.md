# What to do if… someone keeps getting into your personal space in a way that feels sexual (UK)

## Short answer
Create distance and get to a safer spot near staff, friends, other passengers, or a busier area. Use one clear boundary if you can; if it continues, you can treat it as a safety issue and get help without having to prove their intent.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t stay isolated with them in a quiet corner, empty carriage, stairwell, lift, stockroom, or parked car.
- Don’t keep being polite if it is stopping you from moving away or asking for help.
- Don’t negotiate, apologise, or explain at length; short phrases are enough.
- Don’t put yourself in danger to record them or challenge them.
- Don’t force yourself to decide right now whether it was harassment, assault, or a misunderstanding.

## What to do now
1. **Create immediate space.** Step sideways or back, change seats, switch queues, move carriages, leave the dance floor, or move towards a staffed or busy area.
2. **Use one clear boundary line if it feels safe.** Say it loudly enough for nearby people to hear: “Step back.” “Don’t touch me.” “Give me space.” Repeat the same line rather than debating.
3. **Move towards help, not towards privacy.** Go to bar staff, security, a conductor, station staff, reception, a manager, or venue staff. Say: “This person keeps getting into my space and touching me. I need help moving away from them.”
4. **Bring in a witness quickly.** Stand next to someone and say: “Can I stand with you? Someone is bothering me.” If you are with friends, use their name and give a direct instruction: “Stay with me. Let’s move.”
5. **If you are on a train or at a station,** move carriage if you can, stand near other passengers or staff, use a help point if there is one, and alert staff at the next safe opportunity. For a non-emergency rail incident, you can text British Transport Police on 61016; if a crime is happening now, someone is nearby and threatening you, or someone is in danger, call 999.
6. **If you are at work,** move to a safer location such as a break room, reception, a manager’s office, or a more visible workstation. Tell a manager, HR, or a trusted supervisor what is happening and ask for an immediate practical change, such as not working one-to-one with them, moving workstation, changing task, or having another person present. In England, Scotland or Wales, Acas can give workplace guidance about sexual harassment; in Northern Ireland, use your workplace policy, trade union, or local employment advice route.
7. **Make a short record while it is fresh, if you can.** Write the time, date, location, what happened, who was nearby, and any CCTV locations such as a platform, aisle, entrance, bar, lift, or corridor. This is for you; you do not have to decide what to do with it now.
8. **If you feel in danger right now, call 999.** If it is not an emergency but you want to contact police, you can call 101 or use online reporting through the relevant police route.
9. **Get confidential specialist support, with no pressure to report.** If you are in England or Wales, Rape Crisis England & Wales has a free 24/7 support line on 0808 500 2222 and online chat. If you are elsewhere in the UK, The Survivors Trust or a local sexual violence support service can help you find support.

If you think you might want to report later, try not to delete messages or wash or discard items connected to what happened, but only if keeping them does not make you feel less safe or more distressed.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide right now whether to make a formal report.
- You do not have to confront them again.
- You do not have to gather proof, record them, or make other people agree with you.
- You do not have to label what happened before you ask for help.
- You can decide later whether to speak to management, transport police, venue operators, or a specialist support service.

## Important reassurance
Your discomfort is enough reason to move away and ask for help. Freezing, feeling confused, doubting yourself, or reacting later are common responses to unwanted sexual-feeling contact. A short boundary, moving to safety, and getting another person involved are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the immediate situation and the next few hours. Later decisions about workplace complaints, formal reports, health support, or ongoing safety planning may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. Specialist sexual violence support can help you think through your options without pressure to report.
