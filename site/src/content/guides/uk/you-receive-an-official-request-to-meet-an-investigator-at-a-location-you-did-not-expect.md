# What to do if… you receive an official request to meet an investigator at a location you did not expect (UK)

## Short answer
Decline to agree to any meeting or location immediately and state that you require time to verify their identity and seek legal advice. Do not provide any information or confirm any facts until you have spoken to a solicitor and confirmed the request is legitimate through an official switchboard.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attend any location "just to find out what it’s about" before completing verification and getting legal advice.
- Don’t use any phone number, link, or QR code provided in the suspicious message to verify the person’s identity.
- Don’t answer any questions about the matter or "explain your side" over the phone or in person until a solicitor is present.
- Don’t bring unlocked digital devices, passcodes, or original documents to a meeting unless specifically instructed to by your own legal representative.
- Don’t ignore the request entirely; if it is a genuine police request, failing to respond in a controlled manner may lead to a decision to arrest you.

## What to do now
1. **End the immediate contact by stating you need time to verify the request and seek legal counsel.** You can say: "I am not prepared to agree to a meeting right now. I need to verify your identity independently and speak with a solicitor. Please provide your name, collar number, and station, and I will contact you through the official switchboard."
2. **Wait at least five minutes after hanging up before making any outgoing calls to verify the identity.** Fraudsters can "hold the line open" so that when you think you are dialing 101, you are actually still connected to the scammer; waiting ensures the line has cleared.
3. **Verify the caller’s identity using the official national non-emergency number or the organisation's public switchboard.** 
   - Dial 101 to verify a police officer.
   - Ask for the specific force’s control room or switchboard.
   - Ask them to confirm the officer’s name, collar number, and whether a voluntary interview has been requested under a specific reference number.
4. **Identify the legal status of the request by asking specifically if you are being treated as a suspect, a witness, or a victim.** 
   - If you are a suspect, ask if they intend to conduct a "voluntary interview under caution" (often called "voluntary attendance" or "VA").
   - Ask for the specific "disclosure" or "summary of allegations" in writing before any meeting is scheduled.
5. **Contact a solicitor immediately and inform them of the request, especially if the interview is to be "under caution".** 
   - In England and Wales, legal advice for any police interview under caution is free and non-means-tested, regardless of whether it happens at a station or elsewhere.
   - You can find a firm using the Law Society "Find a Solicitor" service or ask for the duty solicitor when the officer tries to book the appointment.
6. **Request that any meeting take place at an official police station or the organisation’s headquarters rather than a "neutral" location.** 
   - While investigators sometimes suggest cafes or homes for convenience or "informality," these locations lack the recorded safeguards and legal oversight of an official interview room.
   - State: "For my safety and to ensure all legal procedures are followed, I will only meet at a police station with my solicitor present."
7. **Document the original request and keep all evidence of how you were contacted.** 
   - Save copies of letters, emails, or screenshots of text messages.
   - Note the exact time of the call and the number displayed on your caller ID.
8. **Follow your solicitor's instructions regarding "pre-interview disclosure" before you attend.** 
   - Your solicitor is entitled to see the basic evidence against you before the interview begins.
   - Do not attempt to negotiate this disclosure yourself; let your legal representative handle all communication with the investigator.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a statement or an account today.
- You do not need to decide whether to "cooperate" or stay silent until you have been briefed by your solicitor on the evidence.
- You do not need to check your own records, messages, or bank statements until your solicitor tells you what is relevant.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an unexpected official request is a high-stress event that often triggers an immediate "fight or flight" response or a desire to "clear things up" quickly. This reaction is common and does not mean you have done something wrong. Taking time to verify and secure legal representation is a standard, professional way to handle the situation that protects your rights.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for receiving an unexpected request. It does not cover the specifics of interview strategy, the differences in Scottish or Northern Irish law (which have different detention powers), or long-term legal defense.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional legal advice. Procedures regarding voluntary attendance and caution vary by jurisdiction and the specific enforcement body involved. If you are suspected of a crime, you should always have a solicitor present during any questioning.
