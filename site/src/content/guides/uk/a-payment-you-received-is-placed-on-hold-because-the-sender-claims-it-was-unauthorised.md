# What to do if… a payment you received is placed on hold because the sender claims it was unauthorised (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the money as unavailable, stop any further fulfilment you can safely stop, and respond only inside the payment provider’s official dispute or chargeback process. Do not send money back by a separate transfer.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t refund or “send it back” using a separate bank transfer, another app, gift cards, crypto, or a personal payment route.
- Don’t click links in emails or texts about the dispute; log in through the provider’s app or by typing the provider’s address yourself.
- Don’t send any more goods, download links, account access, or extra work while the hold is unresolved, unless your provider or marketplace tells you to.
- Don’t reroute a parcel to a new address because the sender asks you to.
- Don’t accept off-platform offers such as “I’ll cancel the dispute if you send part of it back.”
- Don’t delete messages, invoices, tracking, delivery proof, screenshots, access logs, or order records.
- Don’t assume it is only a misunderstanding; an “unauthorised” claim can involve a stolen card, account takeover, or someone denying a payment.

## What to do now
1. **Freeze the transaction on your side.**  
   Treat the held amount as not settled. Do not spend it, withdraw it, or rely on it for another payment until the provider closes the hold or dispute.

2. **Stop anything you have not already delivered.**  
   - Physical goods: pause dispatch if you still can. If the item has already shipped, keep the tracking and do not change the delivery address because of a message from the sender.
   - Digital goods or services: pause new access, new files, extra sessions, licences, or further work. Keep existing proof of what was already provided.

3. **Open the dispute safely.**  
   Log in through the official app or website and read the dispute details. Write down:
   - the dispute type, such as “unauthorised transaction”, “chargeback”, or “item not received”
   - the response deadline
   - the exact evidence the provider asks for
   - whether the provider says you should or should not release the item

4. **Submit evidence only through the official portal.**  
   Use clear, relevant proof. This may include:
   - order details, invoice, item description, price, and date
   - the name, email, account ID, address, or other details shown in the payment or order record
   - proof that you shipped to the recorded address, such as tracking, delivery confirmation, or signature if available
   - messages showing what was agreed before payment
   - for digital goods or services, access logs, download timestamps, delivery emails, handover messages, or completion records
   - screenshots of the provider’s own transaction page if it shows the payment status or recorded delivery details

5. **Keep your response short and factual.**  
   State what was sold, when payment was received, what was delivered, and what proof you are attaching. Do not speculate about the sender’s motive.

6. **Keep communication inside the platform.**  
   If you need to reply to the sender, keep it brief: “Please use the platform’s dispute process. I have submitted the delivery evidence there.” Do not argue, threaten, or share extra personal data.

7. **Secure the accounts connected to the payment.**  
   If the provider offers passkeys, 2-step verification, or another extra login check, turn it on. Change any reused password for your payment account and your email account, and check for unfamiliar devices, new users, changed payout details, or new bank details.

8. **If there are signs of fraud, use the UK reporting route.**  
   If the messages, account activity, or payment pattern suggest fraud or account compromise:
   - in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report it through Report Fraud
   - in Scotland, report fraud to Police Scotland via 101
   Use 999 only if there is immediate danger.

9. **If the provider’s decision seems wrong, use complaints escalation.**  
   Complain to the payment provider or bank first. If you receive a final response you disagree with, or the firm has not responded within the usual complaint timescale for that type of complaint, you may be able to ask the Financial Ombudsman Service to look at it if the firm and complaint are covered.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to pursue the sender directly for money can wait until the dispute outcome is known.
- Writing a long case history can wait; send the specific evidence the provider asks for first.
- Changing your whole business process can wait; right now, focus on stopping further loss and preserving proof.
- Negotiating with the sender can wait, and should only happen in writing and through a route you trust.

## Important reassurance
A hold does not automatically mean you did anything wrong. It usually means the provider is pausing the money while it checks the payment or deals with a dispute. Clear records, safe login, and no separate refund are the most useful first moves.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and reduce the chance of irreversible loss. Later decisions may need specialist help from your payment provider, marketplace support, merchant services provider, accountant, solicitor, or another qualified adviser.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, professional, or business advice. Payment holds, chargebacks, seller protection, complaint routes, and deadlines vary by provider, card scheme, marketplace, and the facts of the transaction.
