# What to do if… you believe you were drugged and something sexual happened while you were impaired (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer place and seek medical or specialist sexual assault support as soon as you can. If you are in immediate danger, seriously unwell, injured, very drowsy, or struggling to breathe, call 999 now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive yourself if you feel drowsy, confused, sick, unsteady, or detached.
- Do not confront or message the person you are worried about right now.
- Do not force yourself to work out every detail while you are in shock.
- Do not let anyone pressure you into reporting to police before you are ready, unless there is immediate danger.
- Do not assume it does not matter because you had been drinking, used drugs, froze, or cannot remember clearly.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Move to a public, well-lit place, a staffed venue, a hospital, or a trusted person’s home. If someone may still be near you or you feel unsafe, call 999.
2. **Get urgent medical help if your body feels unsafe.** Call 999 or go to A&E if you have breathing problems, collapse, seizures, repeated vomiting, severe confusion, heavy bleeding, serious injury, or you keep drifting out of consciousness.
3. **Contact specialist sexual assault support as soon as you can.** A Sexual Assault Referral Centre, or the equivalent sexual assault service in your nation, can offer medical care, emotional support, and options about forensic care without forcing you to report to police.
4. **Use the right urgent health route for where you are.** In England, Scotland or Wales, call 111 if you need urgent health advice and it is not a 999 emergency. In Northern Ireland, use Phone First, GP out-of-hours, or 999 if it is an emergency.
5. **Ask one trusted person to stay with you or help you get care.** They can help with transport, make a call with you, wait nearby, and write down what professionals say.
6. **Make a short note only if it feels manageable.** Write the place, rough times, what you remember drinking or taking, who was nearby, how you got home, symptoms, injuries, and any missing time. Save messages, location history, bank payments, or taxi receipts if they are easy to save.
7. **Keep medical and forensic options open if you can, but do not delay care.** If you may want forensic options later, try not to wash, change clothes, brush teeth, eat, drink, smoke, or use the toilet until you have spoken to a SARC or medical professional. If you need to do any of these, or already have, you can still get help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to report to police.
- You do not need to prove what happened before asking for medical or specialist support.
- You do not need to contact the venue, the suspected person, or mutual friends today.
- You do not need to make decisions about relationships, work, study, housing, or telling wider family while you are in the immediate aftermath.

## Important reassurance
Memory gaps, numbness, fear, shame, anger, sickness, and uncertainty can happen after intoxication, spiking, shock, or sexual violence. Not remembering clearly does not make your concern less valid, and it does not mean you did anything wrong.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, health, and keeping choices open. Later decisions about medical follow-up, reporting, statements, complaints, or ongoing support can be taken one step at a time with specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or seriously unwell, call 999 or go to A&E.
