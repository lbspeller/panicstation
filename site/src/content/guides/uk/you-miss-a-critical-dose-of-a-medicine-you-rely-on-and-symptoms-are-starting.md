# What to do if… you miss a critical dose of a medicine you rely on and symptoms are starting (UK)

## Short answer
Do not attempt to "make up" the dose by guessing; immediately check the patient information leaflet for specific missed-dose instructions and contact a pharmacist or NHS 111 for same-day clinical advice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take a double dose or "double up" to catch up unless a healthcare professional specifically instructs you to.
- Do not take a dose if you are unsure whether you already took one.
- Do not "borrow" medicine from someone else or take an old version of the prescription.
- Do not suddenly stop other medications in an attempt to balance the effects.
- Do not drive, operate machinery, or perform complex tasks if you feel dizzy, confused, drowsy, or shaky.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency symptoms immediately.** If you experience severe difficulty breathing, chest pain, a seizure, sudden confusion, or you collapse, **call 999** (or **112**) now.
2. **Identify the exact details of the medication.** Locate the original packaging or the pharmacy label and note:
   - the medicine name and strength (e.g., milligrams)
   - the exact time the dose was due
   - any doses you have taken since the missed one.
3. **Read the "If you forget to take..." section of the Patient Information Leaflet (PIL).** Follow the printed instructions exactly as they apply to your specific timeframe; if the leaflet says to skip the dose and wait for the next, do not override this advice.
4. **Contact a community pharmacist for an urgent assessment.** If you are symptomatic, a pharmacist can provide immediate advice on whether a late dose is safe or if you need an emergency supply.
   - Use the "Find a pharmacy" tool on the NHS website if your usual one is closed.
   - Explain that symptoms have already started when you speak to them.
5. **Call NHS 111 if you cannot reach a pharmacist or your GP.** They can provide clinical triage and, if necessary, arrange for an out-of-hours prescription or direct you to an Urgent Treatment Centre.
6. **Request an emergency supply if you have run out of the medicine.** Under the Pharmacy First / Urgent Supply of Medicines service, a pharmacist can often provide a small supply of your regular medicine without a prescription if they are satisfied there is an immediate need.
   - Note that Schedule 1, 2, and 3 Controlled Drugs (such as strong painkillers like morphine) usually cannot be supplied this way.
7. **Record your symptoms and timings while waiting for advice.** Write down what you feel and when it started so you can give a clear history to the clinician.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose why the symptoms are occurring or determine if they are "real" or caused by anxiety; let a professional assess this.
- You do not need to create a new medication schedule or buy pill organisers today.
- You do not need to notify your specialist immediately if your GP or 111 provides a solution, though you should update them later.

## Important reassurance
Missing a dose is a common occurrence, and it is normal to feel anxious when symptoms reappear. Most medication errors can be managed safely by following professional guidance rather than acting on instinct. Taking a moment to find the leaflet and call for advice is the most effective way to regain control.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and advice-seeking protocols only. Long-term management of your condition or adjustments to your treatment plan must be discussed with your prescribing clinician or specialist.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace the advice of your doctor or the specific instructions provided with your medication. If you feel very unwell or your condition is deteriorating rapidly, seek emergency medical care.
