# What to do if… you are pressured to stay silent after reporting sexual harassment or misconduct at work (UK)

## Short answer
Do not agree to, sign, delete, or explain anything in the moment. Create a pause, ask for the request in writing, keep brief factual records, and get independent advice or specialist support before you respond.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a confidentiality clause, non-disclosure agreement, settlement agreement, or “full and final” document on the spot.
- Do not rely only on what the person pressuring you says about your options.
- Do not delete messages, emails, screenshots, notes, calendar entries, or meeting invites connected to the report or the pressure to stay quiet.
- Do not post details publicly while you are under pressure and before you have independent advice; it can reduce your control and increase risk.
- Do not share confidential workplace data widely or casually, even when you are trying to protect yourself.
- Do not let a “quick informal chat” replace a written record if you feel pressured, isolated, or outnumbered.
- Do not assume you are forbidden from speaking to a union representative, Acas, a solicitor, or medical or emotional support about the effect on you.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** If the pressure is happening now, say: “I’m not able to discuss or agree to anything right now. Please put what you are asking for in writing.” Then end the conversation if you can.

2. **Write a short private note for yourself.** Record the date, time, who was present, what was said, what you were asked to do, and how you were told to keep quiet. Keep it factual.

3. **Secure the records you personally received.** Save relevant emails, messages, meeting invites, screenshots, and documents sent to you. Keep copies minimal and do not take or share sensitive workplace data you do not need.

4. **Ask one clear question in writing.** If someone is pushing “confidentiality”, send one calm message such as: “Please confirm exactly what you are asking me not to discuss, and whether you are saying I cannot speak to my union representative, Acas, a solicitor, or medical or emotional support about the effect on me.” Do not argue in the message.

5. **Get independent advice before agreeing to anything.** Acas guidance says a non-disclosure agreement must not be used to stop a worker whistleblowing or reporting sexual harassment to the police, and should not be used to stop a worker reporting discrimination. If you are unsure what applies to you, depending on where you work, contact Acas, a union representative, a solicitor, or a specialist sexual harassment support service.

6. **If you are being punished after reporting, name it neutrally.** Write to HR or a senior manager not involved: “Since I reported sexual harassment or sexual misconduct on [date], I have experienced [specific pressure or treatment]. Please log this as a concern about victimisation or retaliation.” Keep it short and factual.

7. **If you are offered a settlement agreement, slow everything down.** A settlement agreement must be in writing and the worker must get advice from a relevant independent adviser for it to be legally valid. Your first step is not to negotiate under pressure; it is to get independent advice.

8. **If there is immediate danger, prioritise safety.** If you fear violence, stalking, coercion, or being stopped from leaving, move to a safer place if you can and contact emergency services on 999. If it is not an emergency, you can contact police on 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to leave your job, make a tribunal claim, report to police, or go public.
- You do not need to write a perfect statement now.
- You do not need to reply quickly to pressure messages.
- You do not need to understand every legal label before taking the first protective steps.
- You do not need to negotiate money, wording, references, or resignation terms in the same conversation where you are being pressured.

## Important reassurance
Pressure to stay quiet can feel isolating and disorienting, especially after you have already reported something serious. Pausing, keeping factual records, and getting independent support are reasonable protective steps. You are allowed to take time before responding.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about complaints, settlement agreements, police reports, employment tribunal deadlines, resignation, or wider disclosure may need specialist legal, union, workplace, or support-service advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, contact emergency services.
