# What to do if… a colleague is spreading your personal information at work and it is escalating (UK)

## Short answer
Save what you can see, write a short factual timeline, then tell your manager or HR in writing today and ask them to stop the sharing and contain the spread.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply-all, confront them publicly in a team channel, or start correcting every rumour yourself.
- Do not retaliate by sharing their personal information.
- Do not delete messages, screenshots, emails, or notes.
- Do not start secretly recording conversations before checking your workplace policy or getting advice.
- Do not resign while you are overwhelmed. That can wait.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause first.** If the information being shared could put you at risk, such as your home address, location, rota, phone number, or threats, move to a safer place and tell a manager immediately. Ask to work somewhere else, leave for the day, or have contact reduced. If you are in immediate danger, call **999**. For non-emergency police reporting, use **101** or online reporting.
2. **Save evidence quietly.** Keep screenshots or photos of messages, emails, chat posts, shared documents, rota entries, and any replies that show the spread. Keep the originals where possible and do not edit the files.
3. **Make a short incident log.** Write the date and time, what was shared, where it appeared, who saw it, who told you, and what effect it is having at work. Keep it factual and brief.
4. **Ask your manager or HR for containment in writing.** Send a short message such as: “A colleague is sharing personal information about me, it is escalating, and I need it stopped and contained. Please confirm what immediate steps will be taken today.” Ask for specific steps, such as removing posts, telling the colleague to stop, limiting contact, moderating the channel, and telling others not to repeat or circulate the information.
5. **Ask HR or IT to reduce exposure.** If the shared details appear in a staff directory, rota tool, profile, email signature, HR record, or internal chat, ask for urgent practical changes: hide personal contact details, remove non-essential fields, restrict who can view the profile, and preserve relevant logs.
6. **Flag any possible internal-records leak.** If you think the colleague got the information from HR files, a rota system, a staff directory, or another workplace system, say this plainly. Ask who is checking it, who can access those records, and what temporary access restrictions can be put in place.
7. **Send one short stop message only if it feels safe.** If it is unlikely to inflame the situation, send: “Please stop sharing personal information about me. I do not consent to it being shared. I have asked management or HR to address this.” Keep a copy. If you think this will escalate things, skip it.
8. **Use the workplace route if it does not stop quickly.** Find the bullying, harassment, dignity at work, or grievance policy. If the behaviour continues or the response is weak, raise it formally and keep it tight: what happened, when, where, who was involved, what evidence exists, and what you need now.
9. **If personal data or private details were mishandled, make a written data-handling complaint.** Send it to HR, the data protection contact, the person your employer names for data protection issues, or your manager if you cannot find one quickly. Ask them to contain the spread, preserve records, restrict access, and explain what they will do next.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to involve a solicitor, make an external complaint, or take legal steps, unless there is immediate danger or an urgent deadline you already know about.
- Writing a long account. A short timeline plus saved evidence is enough for now.
- Deciding whether to move teams, resign, or make a public statement.
- Correcting every rumour one by one. Focus first on containment through your employer.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to feel exposed, angry, or shaky when private information is being spread at work. You do not need to solve the whole situation at once. A calm written request, saved evidence, and practical containment steps are a strong first move.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if the sharing involves threats, discrimination, harassment, personal data from workplace systems, or ongoing retaliation.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe or threatened, prioritise immediate safety and urgent help.
