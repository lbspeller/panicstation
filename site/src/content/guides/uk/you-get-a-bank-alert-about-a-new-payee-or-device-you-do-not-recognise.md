# What to do if… you get a bank alert about a new payee or device you do not recognise (UK)

## Short answer
Do not approve the request, do not click any links, and contact your bank immediately through a trusted route to secure your account. Use the official number on the back of your card or the 159 service to reach your bank’s fraud department.

## Do not do these things
- Do not tap links in the alert message, and do not call any phone number provided within a text, email, or notification.
- Do not share one-time passcodes (OTP), security codes, card PINs, or online banking passwords with anyone, even if they claim to be from the bank.
- Do not approve a "new device," "new payee," "payment," or "card added to wallet" prompt unless you are the one who initiated the action.
- Do not move money to a "safe account" or any other account because someone on the phone told you to.
- Do not keep a suspicious caller on the line or allow them to "guide you" through your banking app or website.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the interaction immediately.** Close the banking app, exit the webpage, or hang up the phone if you are currently speaking to someone about this alert.
2. **Contact your bank through a verified channel.**
   - Call the number on the back of your physical bank card or the official number listed on the bank's website.
   - Use the 159 hotline by dialing **159** from your phone; this service connects you directly to the fraud departments of most major UK banks.
   - If using a mobile app, navigate to the "Help" or "Contact Us" section manually rather than clicking a notification.
3. **Request an immediate security freeze on your account.** Tell the bank you have received an unrecognised payee or device alert and ask them to:
   - **Freeze** all cards and online banking access temporarily.
   - **Remove** any new beneficiaries or payees that you did not personally add.
   - **Revoke** all active sessions and unrecognised devices from your online banking profile.
   - **Verify** that your contact details (email, phone number, and home address) have not been changed.
4. **Report any missing or pending funds.** If money has already been moved or is marked as "pending," ask the bank to start a fraud "recall" or "trace" immediately.
   - Note that under mandatory reimbursement rules (2026), most victims of "authorised push payment" (APP) fraud are eligible for reimbursement up to £85,000, provided gross negligence is not involved.
5. **Secure your associated accounts.**
   - Change the password for the **email account** linked to your banking, as attackers often gain access to bank alerts through compromised email.
   - If your phone loses signal unexpectedly or you receive messages about a "SIM swap," contact your **mobile network provider** immediately using a different phone.
6. **Gather evidence without engaging the hazard.** Take screenshots of the suspicious alert, the unrecognised payee details, or the device login notification, ensuring the date and time are visible.
7. **Report the incident to the authorities.**
   - If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report the fraud via the **Report Fraud** portal (the service replacing Action Fraud) at reportfraud.police.uk.
   - If you are in Scotland, report the incident to **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
   - Forward suspicious emails to **report@phishing.gov.uk** and suspicious texts to **7726**.
8. **Check your credit file for identity misuse.** Use a service like Experian, Equifax, or TransUnion to see if any new credit applications have been made in your name.
   - You can apply for **Cifas Protective Registration** (approx. £30 for 2 years) to place a warning flag on your credit file, requiring lenders to perform extra identity checks.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to close your account permanently or switch banks right now.
- You do not need to perform a full factory reset of your phone or computer immediately unless the bank confirms a malware infection.
- You do not need to write a detailed statement or timeline in the first few minutes; simple screenshots and the bank's reference number are sufficient for now.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a security alert means your bank's protection systems are working. These notifications are designed to give you a window of time to act before a loss occurs. Feeling a sense of panic is a normal biological response to a perceived threat; taking the step to call your bank through a trusted number will immediately transfer the technical burden to their security professionals.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for UK banking security alerts. It does not cover the long-term legal process of fraud reimbursement, formal complaints to the Financial Ombudsman Service, or civil litigation.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute financial or legal advice. If you are in immediate physical danger or feel threatened by a caller, dial 999. Always independently verify contact details for financial institutions.
