# What to do if… you notice a ceiling beam, joist, or rafter is cracked or out of line (UK)

## Short answer
Treat a newly cracked, sagging, or shifted ceiling beam, joist, or rafter as a possible structural safety issue. Keep people away from underneath and above it, and arrange urgent assessment by a competent structural professional. If there is immediate danger, injury, fire, or active movement, leave the home and call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stand underneath it, test it, push it, pull it, or hit it.
- Do not go into the loft or attic for a closer look if the ceiling, roof, or timber already looks sagged, shifted, or newly cracked.
- Do not remove plasterboard, ceilings, floorboards, insulation, or timber to inspect it.
- Do not add weight above it, including loft storage, people walking on joists, or moving furniture over the affected area.
- Do not attempt DIY propping, bracing, jacking, or temporary supports unless a qualified professional tells you exactly what to do.
- Do not switch electricity back on after sparks, buzzing, burning smells, water near wiring, or exposed wiring until it has been checked.
- Do not turn gas back on after a gas smell, hissing, a struck pipe, or emergency shut-off until the gas emergency service or a Gas Safe registered engineer says it is safe.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safety zone immediately.** Move everyone out of the room and out of the area directly above or beside it, such as the loft or upper room. Close the door, keep pets and children away, and do not let anyone walk above the suspected beam, joist, or rafter.

2. **Check from a safe position for signs to leave now.** Leave the home and call 999 if there is active sagging, a dropping ceiling, widening cracks, loud ongoing creaks or snaps, falling plaster or dust, visible separation at supports, fire, injury, or any sign that something is moving while you watch.

3. **Reduce load and vibration without going near it.** Stop running, heavy movement, loft access, furniture moving, or storage over the affected area. Do not re-enter the room to tidy up, retrieve possessions, or take better photos if doing so would put you underneath the suspect area.

4. **If you smell gas, hear hissing, or think a pipe may be damaged, treat that as a separate emergency.** Leave immediately. Do not smoke, use flames, or operate electrical switches. Open doors and windows only if you can do that without delaying your exit or going near the suspect area. Once safely outside, call the gas emergency number for where you are: 0800 111 999 in England, Wales or Scotland, or 0800 002 001 in Northern Ireland.

5. **If there are electrical danger signs, make power safer only if you can do it safely.** If you notice sparks, buzzing, burning smells, water near electrics, or exposed wiring, and the consumer unit is reachable without going under or near the damaged area, turn off the affected circuit or the main switch. If it is not safely reachable, stay out and call 999 if there is immediate danger.

6. **Arrange urgent structural assessment.** If you own the property, contact a chartered structural engineer and say a ceiling beam, joist, or rafter looks newly cracked or out of line and you are keeping people away from it. A chartered building surveyor experienced in structural defects may also help assess the situation or advise whether a structural engineer is needed.

7. **If you rent, tell the responsible property contact immediately.** Contact your landlord, letting agent, managing agent, or freeholder and say you believe there may be a structural safety risk. Tell them what you have isolated, whether anyone has left the property, and whether any gas or electrical danger signs are present.

8. **If it may endanger people outside your home, contact the local authority route.** This includes a roof, wall, chimney, beam, or ceiling that could affect a public pavement, shared hallway, neighbour, shop, workplace, or communal area. In England and Wales, GOV.UK can direct you to your local council dangerous building route; in Scotland and Northern Ireland, use your local authority building standards or building control route. If life is at immediate risk, call 999.

9. **Record only what you can from safety.** From outside the safety zone, take a few photos or a short video showing the crack or misalignment, the wider room context, and any new wall or ceiling cracks. Note the time, what changed suddenly, and any recent trigger such as a leak, impact, storm, building work, or heavy load above.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today how it will be repaired.
- You do not need to get repair quotes before the area is made safe and assessed.
- You do not need to open up the ceiling, roof, or floor to investigate.
- You do not need to decide fault, insurance responsibility, neighbour responsibility, or landlord responsibility before making the area safe.
- You do not need to move everything out of the property unless emergency services, the local authority, your landlord, your insurer, or a structural professional advises it.

## Important reassurance
A sudden-looking crack or shift is frightening, but it does not automatically mean the whole home is about to collapse. The safest first move is to reduce risk, keep people away from the affected area, and get the structure checked before anyone uses that space again.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and buy time. Later decisions about repair, insurance, landlord duties, building control, or legal responsibility may need help from a structural engineer, building surveyor, insurer, landlord, local authority, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, engineering, surveying, gas, electrical, or professional safety advice. If you think there is an immediate risk of collapse, fire, injury, gas danger, electrical danger, or danger to the public, leave the area and use the relevant emergency route.
