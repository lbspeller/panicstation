# What to do if… you notice swelling in your face or hands that develops over a day and your urine output drops (UK)

## Short answer
Get urgent same-day medical assessment. Call 999 now if your lips, tongue, mouth or throat are swelling, you are struggling to breathe, you are very drowsy or confused, you have chest pain, or you cannot pass urine at all and feel very unwell.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait overnight if your urine output has clearly dropped or the swelling is spreading.
- Do not take extra anti-inflammatory painkillers such as ibuprofen or naproxen to bring swelling down while you are peeing less, unless a clinician has specifically told you to.
- Do not take someone else’s water tablets or diuretics.
- Do not stop prescribed medicines suddenly unless a clinician tells you to, but do tell urgent-care staff exactly what you take.
- Do not force large amounts of water quickly, especially if you are breathless or the swelling is worsening.
- Do not drive yourself to urgent care if you feel dizzy, very weak, confused, faint, or short of breath.

## What to do now
1. Check for emergency signs. Call 999 now if any of these are happening:
   - swelling of your lips, tongue, mouth or throat
   - trouble breathing, wheezing, choking sensation, tight throat, hoarse voice, or trouble swallowing
   - blue, grey or pale colour around the lips, tongue or face
   - collapse, fainting, severe chest pain, new confusion, or extreme drowsiness
2. Treat no urine at all as urgent. If you cannot pass urine at all, or you have severe lower belly pain or fullness with very little urine, go to A&E now. Call 999 if you are too unwell to travel safely.
3. If you are pregnant or have given birth in the last few weeks, phone your maternity unit, midwife, or hospital triage line now. If you cannot reach them, use the urgent-care route for where you are; call 111 where available, or call 999 if you have emergency symptoms.
4. Otherwise, get same-day urgent medical advice today. Call your GP practice and say: “My face or hands have swollen over a day and my urine output has dropped.” If you cannot get prompt help from your GP, use the urgent-care route for where you are; call 111 where available.
5. If you are getting worse while waiting, switch to emergency care. Go to A&E or call 999 if you become breathless, very weak, faint, confused, severely drowsy, or develop chest pain, throat symptoms, or spreading facial swelling.
6. Make a short facts list to take with you:
   - when the swelling started and how fast it has changed
   - how much you have peed compared with normal, such as “only once since waking”
   - whether your urine is very dark, bloody, painful, or hard to pass
   - any breathlessness, cough, fever, vomiting, diarrhoea, rash, hives, throat tightness, or lip swelling
   - any recent sore throat, skin infection, sting, new food, or new medicine
   - all medicines, painkillers, cold remedies and supplements you have taken, including ACE inhibitors such as ramipril
7. If you have a home blood pressure monitor, take one reading now after sitting quietly for a few minutes. Write the number down and take it with you.
8. While you are waiting to be seen, sip fluids normally if you can and avoid alcohol. If throat, lip, tongue or breathing symptoms start at any point, call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to start a strict diet, detox, kidney cleanse, or supplement.
- You do not need to decide about long-term treatment today.
- You do not need to gather a perfect record of every symptom; a short list and your medicines are enough for now.

## Important reassurance
Swelling with reduced urine can feel frightening, and it is a sensible reason to seek urgent help. The aim right now is not to diagnose yourself; it is to be assessed quickly so serious causes can be ruled out or treated early.

## Scope note
This guide covers first steps only to help you get safely assessed. Later tests, treatment and decisions depend on what clinicians find.

## Important note
This is general information, not a diagnosis, and not medical or professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, develop breathing or throat symptoms, or cannot pass urine at all and feel very unwell, call 999.
