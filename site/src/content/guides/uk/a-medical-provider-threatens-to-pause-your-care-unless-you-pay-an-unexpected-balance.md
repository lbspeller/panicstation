# What to do if… a medical provider threatens to pause your care unless you pay an unexpected balance (UK)

## Short answer
Ask them to pause the payment pressure and put the balance, the reason for it, and the service they may pause in writing. If the care may be urgent today, ask for a clinician to make that decision before any billing decision is treated as final.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay the full amount under pressure if you do not understand what the charge is for.
- Do not give card details to someone who contacted you unexpectedly; call back using a number you already trust.
- Do not accept “you owe a balance” without dates, services, prices, and the name of the organisation billing you.
- Do not sign new payment terms or new prices while you are unwell, rushed, or unsure.
- Do not let a billing dispute stop you seeking urgent or emergency care if your symptoms are worsening.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the demand in writing.**  
   Say: “Please put in writing what this balance is for, the date and service, who is billing it, and what service you are saying may be paused.” Ask for an itemised statement, any estimate or terms they say you agreed to, and direct contact details for the billing team.

2. **Ask whether this is a clinical decision or an admin hold.**  
   Say: “Is a clinician saying this care is urgent or immediately necessary today, or is this an administrative billing hold?” If they say the care can be paused, ask for the clinician, clinic lead, or ward lead to confirm the clinical position in your notes.

3. **If this is overseas visitor charging in England, ask for the Overseas Visitor Manager or team.**  
   Say clearly: “I am concerned that payment pressure may delay care. Please ask a clinician to confirm whether the care is urgent, immediately necessary, or non-urgent.” In England, urgent or immediately necessary care under overseas visitor charging should not be delayed because of payment, although charges may still be pursued later if they apply.

4. **Use the right support route for the UK nation you are in.**  
   - **England:** ask for PALS or the hospital patient experience team.
   - **Scotland:** contact PASS, the Patient Advice and Support Service.
   - **Wales:** for a new concern raised on or after 1 April 2026, ask for the NHS Wales “Listening to People” process; older concerns may still be handled under “Putting Things Right”. You can also ask Llais about complaints advocacy.
   - **Northern Ireland:** contact the Patient and Client Council, or the Health and Social Care Trust complaints department.

5. **If you dispute the charge, ask for a temporary hold.**  
   Say: “I dispute this balance. Please place the account on hold while you send the itemised breakdown and evidence of what I agreed to. Please confirm in writing that this will not be escalated while it is under review.”

6. **If this is private care, separate old invoices from future care.**  
   Ask: “Is this an old invoice, a deposit for future care, or a charge not covered by my insurer?” Then ask for the written complaints process, whether the provider is an ISCAS subscriber, and whether a payment plan or temporary hold is available while the balance is checked.

7. **If you choose to pay something, label it clearly.**  
   Before paying, ask: “If I pay £X today, will the service continue while the rest is reviewed, and will you confirm that in writing?” Keep the receipt and ask them to record what the payment is for.

8. **If you need medical help now, use the medical route rather than arguing about billing.**  
   For life-threatening emergencies, call 999 or go to A&E. For urgent but not life-threatening advice, use the urgent-care route for where you are, such as 111, GP out of hours, the provider’s clinical team, or local urgent-care services.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you will pay the full amount.
- You do not need to argue about blame at reception.
- You do not need to sign new payment terms while you are under pressure.
- You do not need to start a formal complaint before you have asked for written details, the correct support route, and a hold on the account.

## Important reassurance
Being asked for unexpected money while trying to get healthcare can feel frightening and urgent. Asking for written details, a clinical urgency decision, and the correct patient-support route is a reasonable way to slow the situation down.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if this involves overseas visitor charging, private healthcare terms, insurance cover, debt collection, or a formal complaint.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Healthcare charging and complaints routes differ across England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland, and depend on whether the care is publicly funded, privately funded, insured, or assessed under overseas visitor charging.
