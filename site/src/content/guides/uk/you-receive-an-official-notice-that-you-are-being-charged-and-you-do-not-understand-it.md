# What to do if… you receive an official notice that you are being charged and you do not understand it (UK)

## Short answer
Do not ignore it. Find any deadline and any court date, then verify the notice using official court or issuer contact details and get criminal defence advice so you understand what you must do next.

## Do not do these things
- Do not bin it, hide it, or wait and see; missing a deadline can make things worse.
- Do not plead guilty, pay, or send a written explanation just because you feel rushed.
- Do not call numbers, scan QR codes, or follow payment links from a notice you have not checked.
- Do not post photos of the paperwork online or share it widely; it can contain case identifiers and personal data.
- Do not contact anyone involved in the case if the notice mentions bail conditions, restrictions, or no-contact rules.
- Do not assume the process is the same everywhere in the UK; use the court, police force, prosecutor, or issuer named on the notice as the starting point.

## What to do now
1. **Do a 2-minute scan for the must-do bits and write them down.**  
   Look only for your name, date of birth, address, offence wording, case or reference number, response deadline, and any court date, time, or location.

2. **Treat this as a deadline problem first, not a solve-the-case problem.**  
   If it says **Single Justice Procedure Notice** or **SJPN**, you generally have **21 days from the date shown on the notice** to respond. If it gives a hearing date, plan to attend unless a criminal defence solicitor or the court tells you something different.

3. **Verify the notice using independent, official contact details.**  
   Use an official court finder, official GOV.UK page, police force website, or prosecutor website rather than a number or payment link you do not trust. When you contact them, give only the case or reference number at first and ask:
   - “Can you confirm this notice is genuine?”
   - “What type of notice is it?”
   - “What do I have to do, and by what date?”
   - “Is there a court hearing, and do I need to attend?”

4. **Get criminal defence help to read it with you as soon as you can.**  
   If you already have a solicitor, send clear photos or scans of every page, including the envelope. If you do not, contact a criminal defence solicitor and ask about legal aid if money is tight. If a court date is close and you cannot get a solicitor in time, attend court and ask court staff on arrival whether a duty solicitor is available for your type of case that day; do not assume this is guaranteed.

5. **If you think it is meant for someone else, act quickly anyway.**  
   Contact the court or issuer using official details and say: “I may be the wrong person. How do I correct this?” Ask what proof they need, such as ID or address history. Keep it factual and avoid long explanations until you have advice.

6. **If it mentions bail or conditions, follow them while you get advice.**  
   Conditions can include not contacting certain people, living at a particular address, giving up a passport, or reporting to a police station. If you genuinely cannot comply for a practical reason, tell a solicitor promptly and ask what to do before taking action.

7. **Make a simple document pack now.**  
   Keep the notice, envelope, attachments, photos or scans, and one page where you record dates, deadlines, names of people you spoke to, and what they said.

8. **If language, disability, or literacy is a barrier, ask for help early.**  
   When you contact the court, issuer, or solicitor, ask for what you need, such as an interpreter, accessible format, hearing loop, or help understanding the paperwork.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide your plea right now unless a verified deadline requires a response.
- You do **not** need to write your full account before getting advice.
- You do **not** need to contact anyone involved to clear it up.
- You do **not** need to gather every possible document right now; first confirm the notice type, the deadline, and where to get legal help.

## Important reassurance
Official legal wording can be hard to understand, especially when you are frightened. Your first job is not to solve the whole case; it is to confirm what the document is, avoid missing deadlines, and get the right person to help you read it.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for stabilising the situation and avoiding irreversible mistakes. Later decisions may need help from a criminal defence solicitor or another specialist who can see the actual notice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. If you are unsure what the notice is, whether it is genuine, or what to do by the deadline, contact the court, issuer, and/or a criminal defence solicitor using official contact details.
