# What to do if… you smell a strong chemical or solvent odour indoors and cannot identify the source (UK)

## Short answer
Get everyone, including pets, into fresh air immediately. If the odour is strong, spreading, or anyone feels unwell, call 999 from a safe place.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay inside trying to identify the exact source.
- Do not go room-to-room breathing it in.
- Do not use electrical switches, plugs, appliances, doorbells, or buzzers if there is any chance it could be gas, fuel, or solvent vapour.
- Do not light candles, smoke, vape, use matches, or use any naked flame.
- Do not mix cleaning products or spray anything to “neutralise” the smell.
- Do not re-enter if you feel dizzy, faint, sick, confused, breathless, or your eyes, throat, or skin are stinging.

## What to do now
1. Move to fresh air first. Get everyone out of the affected room or building if you can do that safely. Take your phone and keys if they are already with you, but do not delay leaving to collect things.
2. As you leave, open an outside door or window if it is directly on your route and does not make you stay inside. Do not remain indoors to air the place out.
3. If anyone has breathing trouble, chest tightness, fainting, confusion, seizures, severe vomiting, burns, or strong eye or skin irritation, call 999 and say you suspect chemical fumes indoors.
4. If you suspect gas at all, or you are not sure, call the gas emergency number for your area once you are outside. In Great Britain, call 0800 111 999. In Northern Ireland, call 0800 002 001.
5. If the gas meter shut-off valve is safe and easy to reach on your way out, turn it off. Do not go into a cellar, basement, garage, cupboard, or affected area to do this.
6. If you are in a shared building, such as a block of flats, leave the affected area and call 999 if the smell is strong, spreading, or could affect other homes. Tell them it may involve a communal hallway, garage, plant room, meter cupboard, or neighbouring property.
7. If liquid, mist, powder, or residue may be on you, move away from the source first. If it is safe, remove contaminated outer clothing without pulling it over your face, keep it away from other people, and blot or brush visible residue off your skin. Rinse irritated skin or eyes with plenty of clean water.
8. If nobody is seriously unwell but you feel “not right”, call NHS 111 where available, or your local urgent medical advice service, once you are in fresh air. If symptoms get worse, call 999.
9. From outside, note only the essentials: when you first noticed the smell, where it seemed strongest, whether it was like petrol, solvent, gas, burning plastic, or cleaning chemicals, and whether there has been recent painting, fuel storage, cleaning, pest control, or building work.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact chemical now.
- You do not need to clean, deodorise, or neutralise the smell now.
- You do not need to decide about repairs, landlord messages, insurance, complaints, or testing until people are safe and urgent advice has been taken.
- You do not need to re-enter for belongings.

## Important reassurance
Leaving first is a sensible protective step, not an overreaction. Strong unexplained odours can cause real symptoms, and treating unknown fumes as potentially hazardous buys time for safer advice.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repairs, landlord contact, environmental health, specialist testing, or replacing damaged items may need help from the relevant service or a qualified professional.

## Important note
This is general first-step safety information, not medical, legal, environmental, engineering, or other professional advice. If there is immediate danger, call 999. If you suspect a gas leak, call the gas emergency number for your area: 0800 111 999 in Great Britain, or 0800 002 001 in Northern Ireland.
