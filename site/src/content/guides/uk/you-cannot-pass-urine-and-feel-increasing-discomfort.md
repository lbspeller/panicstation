# What to do if… you cannot pass urine and feel increasing discomfort (UK)

## Short answer
If you cannot pass urine at all and are in increasing pain, go to your nearest A&E department immediately. This is a medical emergency called acute urinary retention that requires a clinician to drain the bladder. If you cannot travel safely, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep straining or forcing yourself to urinate, as this can increase internal pressure and pain without clearing a blockage.
- Do not drink large amounts of water to "flush the system," as this will only fill the bladder further and increase discomfort.
- Do not take over-the-counter medications or someone else's prescription (such as prostate medicine or antibiotics) to resolve the issue yourself.
- Do not attempt to insert any objects or home-made catheters into your urethra.
- Do not wait until the morning or try to "sleep it off" if you have passed zero urine for several hours and feel pain.

## What to do now
1. **Go to an A&E department immediately if you are unable to pass any urine and your bladder feels painful or distended.** This condition requires urgent clinical intervention to prevent bladder or kidney damage.
2. **Call 999 if you have additional emergency symptoms or cannot get to the hospital.** 
   - These include feeling faint / collapsing.
   - Severe confusion or agitation.
   - High fever or shivering (rigors).
   - Severe, uncontrollable pain.
3. **Perform one gentle attempt to urinate and then stop.** 
   - Try a warm bath or running water nearby to see if it assists a natural release. 
   - If nothing happens, do not repeat the attempt; leave for the hospital immediately.
4. **Check for "Red Flag" neurological symptoms that require 999/Immediate A&E.**
   - New numbness or "pins and needles" in your "saddle area" (the region that would touch a bicycle saddle, including genitals, anus, and inner thighs).
   - Any change in feeling when you wipe with toilet paper after using the loo.
   - New weakness in your legs or difficulty walking.
   - Recent sudden back pain combined with an inability to pee.
5. **Prepare essential information for the medical team while you are in transit.**
   - Note the exact time you last passed a normal amount of urine.
   - Check if you have any blood in your urine or if you are currently pregnant.
   - List all current medications, especially any new antidepressants, antihistamines, or decongestants.
6. **If you already have a urinary catheter, check for kinks but do not remove it.**
   - Ensure the tubing is not twisted or blocked by your clothing.
   - If the catheter is not draining and your bladder is painful, go to A&E immediately. Do not attempt to flush or "fix" the catheter yourself unless previously trained.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the underlying cause (such as an enlarged prostate, infection, or medication side effect) before seeking help.
- You do not need to contact your GP first if you are in acute pain and unable to void; A&E is the appropriate setting.
- You do not need to decide on long-term treatments or surgery right now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant anxiety or "panic" when you cannot perform a basic bodily function like urinating. The discomfort and pressure you feel are real physical signals that your body needs assistance. Seeking emergency care for this is the standard and expected medical response.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for acute urinary retention only. It does not cover chronic (painless) urinary retention or long-term urological management.

## Important note
Acute urinary retention is a time-sensitive medical issue. This information is for guidance and does not replace the assessment of a doctor or nurse. If you are unsure of your symptoms, contact NHS 111, but do not delay if you are in severe pain.
