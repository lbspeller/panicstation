# What to do if… your credit card is suddenly cancelled or your credit limit is reduced and bills are due (UK)

## Short answer

Contact your card issuer using the number on the card, statement, or official banking app to find out whether this is a security block, limit reduction, or account closure. Then move any essential bills due in the next few days to another payment method or contact the biller before the due date.

## Do not do these things

- Don’t call phone numbers or click links from a text or email about the cancellation; use the card, statement, or official app.
- Don’t keep testing the card with repeated declined payments; this may trigger more blocks or merchant checks.
- Don’t ignore bills because you feel stuck; tell the provider early that the payment method has failed.
- Don’t take out high-cost quick credit in a panic.
- Don’t cancel essential services such as energy, phone, insurance, rent, or mortgage payments before asking about a short extension or payment arrangement.
- Don’t assume a new credit application is the fastest or safest fix today.

## What to do now

1. **Check what has actually changed.** Open the issuer’s official app or website and look for the exact status: card blocked, suspected fraud, limit reduced, account closed, missed payment, or review. Note the time, the message shown, your available credit, and any transactions you do not recognise.

2. **Contact the issuer through an official route.** Ask:
   - Is this a security block, fraud check, missed-payment issue, affordability review, limit reduction, or closure?
   - Can the card be unblocked after checks, or is the decision permanent?
   - What is the quickest safe way to make at least the minimum payment if one is due?
   - If you cannot pay on time, what support, temporary help, a payment arrangement, or a route to free debt advice can they offer?
   - Can they send the reason or next steps in writing?

3. **Separate the bills due in the next 7 days.** Write down the amount, due date, provider, and usual payment method for rent or mortgage, council tax or rates, energy, water, or other utilities, phone, insurance, childcare, loan payments, and any credit-card minimum payment.

4. **Move urgent payments away from the cancelled or reduced card.** For each bill due soon, use a method the provider confirms will arrive in time, such as debit card, bank transfer, or an online account payment. Direct Debit or standing order may be better for future bills, but it may not activate quickly enough for a bill due today or tomorrow.

5. **Contact the billers before the due date.** Say that your credit card payment method has failed and you are arranging another way to pay. Ask for a short extension, payment plan, late-fee pause, or confirmation of the latest time they can receive payment. Keep a screenshot, email, chat transcript, or note of the call.

6. **Fix recurring card payments one by one.** For subscriptions, insurance, utilities, or other recurring card payments, log in to the merchant account or contact the provider and replace the card with a debit card, bank transfer, Direct Debit, or other accepted method. Cancelling or changing a card payment does not usually cancel the underlying bill or contract.

7. **If fraud or identity misuse might be involved, tell the issuer immediately.** Ask them to secure the account and explain their fraud process. If you decide to report fraud or cyber crime, use Report Fraud in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland; in Scotland, report it through Police Scotland.

8. **If the issuer’s decision seems wrong, start a complaint trail after the urgent bills are stabilised.** Ask for the reason in writing and submit a formal complaint to the card provider. If you receive a final response and remain unhappy, or the provider has not responded within the relevant complaint timeframe, you can ask the Financial Ombudsman Service whether it can look at the complaint. For most complaints, firms have up to 8 weeks to respond; some payment-services complaints have shorter timeframes. A final response will usually explain the time limit for going to the ombudsman, often 6 months.

## What can wait

- You do not need to decide today whether to close the account permanently, switch banks, or rebuild your credit score.
- You do not need to apply for new credit immediately.
- You do not need to argue every detail with the issuer before protecting bills due today or tomorrow.
- You do not need a full long-term budget tonight; the immediate task is to keep essential payments from failing silently.

## Important reassurance

A sudden block or credit-limit change can happen because of security checks, automated reviews, missed payments, or issuer risk decisions. This does not mean you have to solve everything at once; the practical goal is to confirm what happened, protect the most urgent bills, and create a written trail.

## Scope note

These are first steps only, aimed at stabilising the next few days. Later decisions about complaints, debt help, credit files, switching products, or affordability support may need specialist help.

## Important note

This is general information, not financial, legal, debt, or professional advice. If housing, essential services, or unaffordable debt are involved, contact the provider as early as possible and consider free debt advice.
