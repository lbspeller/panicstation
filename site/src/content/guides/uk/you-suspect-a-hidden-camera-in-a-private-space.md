# What to do if… you suspect a hidden camera in a private space (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer place first and do not confront anyone alone. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999; otherwise, report from a safe place if you choose to, and contact specialist support even if you are not sure what happened.

## Do not do these things
- Do not search the room thoroughly or dismantle fixtures, vents, alarms, chargers, sockets, mirrors, or furniture.
- Do not confront the owner, host, staff member, flatmate, colleague, or suspected person in private or alone.
- Do not throw away, move, delete, reset, or take home a suspected device.
- Do not post images or allegations on social media.
- Do not message lots of people in the property about it while you are still there.
- Do not blame yourself or wait until you can prove exactly what happened before asking for help.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** Leave the bathroom, bedroom, changing area, or room and go somewhere with other people around, such as reception, a lobby, a public area, a neighbour, your car, or a trusted person’s home.
2. **Call 999 if there is immediate danger.** This includes feeling threatened, being unable to leave safely, or a crime happening now.
3. **For non-emergency reporting, use 101 or the relevant police website from a safe place.** In England and Wales, Police.uk can route online reports. In Scotland, use Police Scotland. In Northern Ireland, use the Police Service of Northern Ireland.
4. **Stop touching the area.** If you noticed a specific object, lens, hole, charger, alarm, vent, mirror, or light, leave it where it is. If you already touched something, stop there and do not try to put it back.
5. **Make a short factual note.** Write the date, time, exact location, what you noticed, where the object was pointing, and any booking, access, check-in, keypad, or room-change messages you already have.
6. **If it is safe, take only a simple record from where you are.** A photo of the object or room layout can help later, but do not move items, open fixtures, or stay in the room to investigate.
7. **If this is a hotel, rental, gym, workplace, school, venue, or shared accommodation, ask for a safer space.** Keep the request simple: ask to move rooms, leave the area, or have another person stay with you. If it feels safe, ask in writing that access records or CCTV for the relevant time are not deleted.
8. **If the suspected person may be at home or nearby, leave first if you can.** Go to a trusted person, public place, or police station, then decide what to do next from there.
9. **Contact specialist sexual violence support.** In England and Wales, the 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line is for people aged 16+ and is on 0808 500 2222 or online chat. In Scotland, Rape Crisis Scotland can help. In Northern Ireland, The Rowan is the regional Sexual Assault Referral Centre.
10. **If anyone threatens to share footage or asks for money, do not negotiate.** Save the messages if you can do so safely, then contact police from a safe place.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to make a formal statement.
- You do not need to identify the device or confirm whether it was recording before asking for help.
- You do not need to work out who placed it before you leave or report.
- You do not need to tell everyone, confront anyone, or handle the property dispute immediately.
- You do not need to handle this perfectly for it to be taken seriously.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shocked, frozen, embarrassed, angry, or unsure is a normal response to a frightening privacy violation. Suspicion is enough reason to get safer, stop touching the area, and ask for help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce risk, avoid irreversible mistakes, and buy time. Later decisions about police reporting, accommodation, workplace action, housing, evidence, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, forensic, financial, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
