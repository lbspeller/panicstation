# What to do if… you are asked to backdate documents or signatures to fix an audit issue (UK)

## Short answer
Do not backdate, sign, or approve anything you believe is inaccurate. Pause the request, keep the wording factual, and ask for the compliant way to correct the audit record without making it look as if something happened earlier than it did.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign or approve a document with a date, timestamp, or statement you believe is untrue.
- Do not change version history, audit logs, email trails, metadata, or system records to make the issue look earlier or cleaner.
- Do not delete messages, drafts, files, task records, or comments connected to the request.
- Do not send confidential work documents to your personal email, personal cloud storage, messaging apps, or a USB stick.
- Do not accuse anyone in the moment; keep your response calm, brief, and written.
- Do not let an audit deadline rush you into creating a misleading record.

## What to do now
1. **Pause before signing or editing.** Say: “I need to check the correct way to record this because I cannot sign or date something inaccurately.”
2. **Clarify the exact document and date being requested.** Ask which document, which date, whose approval, and what audit issue the change is meant to address.
3. **Ask for the instruction in writing.** If the request was verbal, send a short confirmation: “You asked me to sign or date this as if it happened earlier. I am not comfortable doing that. Please confirm the compliant way to record the correction.”
4. **Offer a truthful correction route.** You can usually sign and date today, then add a clear note saying when the work or decision actually happened, what evidence supports that timing, and why the document is being completed now.
5. **Check the internal route for audit corrections.** Look for your organisation’s document control, records management, audit response, code of conduct, fraud, compliance, or speak-up policy.
6. **Escalate through the function that owns record integrity.** Use Compliance, Legal, Internal Audit, Risk, the company secretary, or the whistleblowing or speak-up channel if your organisation has one.
7. **Keep a factual timeline inside approved systems.** Record who asked, when, the document name, the date requested, who was present, and what you replied. Do not copy bundles of documents or personal data for yourself.
8. **Ask how to preserve records if you fear deletion.** Use Compliance, Legal, Internal Audit, or an approved records hold process rather than taking files outside the organisation.
9. **If the pressure continues, treat it as a possible whistleblowing concern.** In the UK, protection may apply to certain protected disclosures made with reasonable belief and in the public interest. Use your employer’s whistleblowing route, a legal adviser, or the correct prescribed person for your sector.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, raise a grievance, or start legal action.
- You do not need to prove anyone’s intent right now.
- You do not need to investigate the whole audit issue yourself.
- You do not need to contact an external regulator immediately unless there is immediate danger or urgent risk; first avoid creating an inaccurate record and use a safe advice or escalation route.

## Important reassurance
Being pressured to “just fix the paperwork” can feel intimidating, especially during an audit. You are allowed to pause, ask for a written instruction, and insist that any correction shows the real date and the real sequence of events.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to help you avoid irreversible mistakes and buy time. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if you work in finance, healthcare, regulated services, public bodies, charities, or a formal signatory role.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, employment, regulatory, therapeutic, or professional advice. Follow your organisation’s policies and confidentiality rules, and consider independent advice from Acas, a union, a legal adviser, or the correct prescribed person if you are unsure how to raise the concern safely.
