# What to do if… a merchant says they refunded you but the card transaction is still showing as reversed (UK)

## Short answer
First check whether the card payment actually left your account or whether a pending authorisation was cancelled. Then ask the merchant for traceable refund details and ask your card provider to trace the missing credit or explain the card dispute route if you are still out of pocket.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume “reversed” and “refunded” mean the same thing; a reversal can mean a pending hold was cancelled, while a refund may appear as a separate credit.
- Don’t keep sending daily messages with no new information; send one clear request for the details your card provider can use.
- Don’t close the account or cancel the card just to “reset” the refund.
- Don’t rely only on a merchant screenshot if it does not show the refund amount, date, payment method, and reference.
- Don’t throw away receipts, return proof, postage proof, emails, or chat transcripts.
- Don’t start a card dispute if the refund has already reached you, because you cannot be refunded twice for the same purchase.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether money actually left your account.**
   - Open the transaction details in your banking app.
   - Check whether the transaction is still **pending** or has **posted** to the account.
   - Look for a separate refund or credit line for the same merchant and amount.
   - Compare your available balance with your statement or account balance, because a cancelled hold can affect the available balance before it disappears.

2. **Save what you can see today.**
   - Take screenshots showing the merchant name, amount, date, and the word “reversed”.
   - Save the receipt, order confirmation, cancellation or return confirmation, and any message where the merchant says they refunded you.
   - Write down the date you first noticed the issue.

3. **Ask the merchant for refund details that can be traced.**
   Send one clear message asking for:
   - the refund amount;
   - the date and time the refund was processed;
   - confirmation that it went back to the same card, wallet, or payment method;
   - the last 4 digits of the card, or confirmation of the wallet token if you used Apple Pay or Google Pay;
   - any refund reference, ARN, or payment-provider trace number, if available;
   - confirmation whether they issued a refund or only reversed, voided, or cancelled the authorisation.

4. **Contact your card provider and ask them to trace the missing credit.**
   Use this wording: **“The merchant says they refunded me, but I cannot see the credit. Please trace a missing refund credit.”**
   Give them:
   - the original transaction date and amount;
   - the merchant name;
   - the refund date and amount;
   - the merchant’s refund reference, ARN, or trace number, if you have one;
   - screenshots or written confirmation from the merchant.

5. **Ask about card changes or wallet payments.**
   Ask your provider to check whether the refund may be linked to:
   - an old or replacement card;
   - an expired card;
   - a closed card account;
   - Apple Pay, Google Pay, or another tokenised wallet payment;
   - a pending authorisation that was never actually charged.

6. **If the money has left your account and no refund has arrived, ask about the dispute route.**
   Say: **“I am still out of pocket. The merchant says they refunded me, but no credit has posted. What card dispute, chargeback, or Section 75 route applies, and what evidence and deadline do you need?”**
   Chargeback is not guaranteed, and the merchant may dispute it, but your card provider can tell you what route is available for your card and purchase.

7. **If the provider does not resolve it, make a formal complaint in writing.**
   - Use the card provider’s formal complaints route.
   - State that the issue is a missing refund credit after a merchant-confirmed refund.
   - Attach the merchant’s refund details and your account screenshots.
   - Ask for a written final response.

8. **If the complaint is still unresolved, check whether you can go to the Financial Ombudsman Service.**
   You can usually ask the Financial Ombudsman Service to look at it after a final response you disagree with, or if the provider has not replied within the relevant complaint deadline. Check the current time-limits page, especially because some payment-service complaints may have shorter response deadlines than other financial complaints. If you have a final response letter, you normally need to complain to the Financial Ombudsman Service within six months of the date on that letter.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide today whether to take legal action.
- You don’t need to post publicly or threaten the merchant to force a response.
- You don’t need to cancel the card unless you suspect fraud or ongoing unauthorised activity.
- You don’t need to work out every complaint deadline now; first get the refund details and a clear answer from your card provider.

## Important reassurance
This situation is common because card apps use confusing words for pending holds, reversals, voids, and refunds. Getting the refund date, amount, and any trace reference gives your card provider something concrete to check.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation, preserve useful evidence, and avoid losing time. Later decisions may need specialist help if the money remains missing after tracing, a card dispute, or a formal complaint.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Card providers, merchants, and card schemes may use different wording and processes, so keep records and follow your provider’s written instructions where they differ.
