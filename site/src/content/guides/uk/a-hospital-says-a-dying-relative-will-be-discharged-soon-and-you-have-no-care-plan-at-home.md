# What to do if… a hospital says a dying relative will be discharged soon and you have no care plan at home (UK)

## Short answer
State clearly to the ward's Nurse in Charge and the Discharge Coordinator: "This discharge is unsafe because there is no care plan or caregiver at home." Request an immediate multidisciplinary meeting (MDM) to confirm exactly what clinical and personal support will be active from the hour they arrive home.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to a discharge date or time just to reduce pressure from the ward staff.
- Do not assume that community services (like District Nurses) are automatically notified; verify they have accepted the referral.
- Do not promise to provide physical care (such as lifting or 24-hour monitoring) if you are unable to do so safely.
- Do not leave the hospital without a written list of "Out of Hours" emergency contact numbers for symptom control.
- Do not accept "we will sort it once they are home" as a valid plan for a dying patient.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the Discharge Coordinator and name the risk.**  
   **Ask the ward clerk or nurse to identify the specific staff member managing the discharge.** Speak to them directly and use this specific phrase: "I am raising a formal safety concern because a safe care plan is not in place for [Name]."
   - Ask for this statement to be recorded in the patient’s clinical notes.

2. **Request a "Discharge Huddle" with the relevant specialists.**  
   **Ask for a 10-minute meeting with the ward doctor, the Occupational Therapist (OT), and a member of the Palliative Care Team.** In this meeting, ask:
   - "What is the clinical 'Path of Care' for the next 48 hours?"
   - "Who is the single named person coordinating this discharge today?"
   - "Has a 'Home First' or 'Discharge to Assess' assessment been completed?"

3. **Verify the funding and support pathway for your jurisdiction.**  
   **Ask which specific urgent funding route is being used to provide care at home.**
   - In **England and Wales**, ask: "Does the patient meet the criteria for NHS Continuing Healthcare (CHC) Fast Track funding?"
   - In **Scotland**, ask: "Has the Hospital Based Complex Clinical Care assessment been completed, and what is the community handover plan?"
   - In **Northern Ireland**, ask: "How is the Trust-funded domiciliary care or Hospice at Home service being triggered for day one?"

4. **Confirm the "Equipment and Meds" checklist.**  
   **Do not allow the patient to leave until you have confirmation that equipment has been delivered and meds are in hand.**
   - Ask: "Is the hospital bed / pressure-relief mattress physically inside the house yet?"
   - Ask: "Are 'Just in Case' or 'Anticipatory' medications (for pain, breathlessness, or agitation) provided in the discharge pack?"

5. **Ensure the digital handover is complete.**  
   **Ask the doctor to update the patient's electronic records so community teams have the correct information.**
   - In **Scotland**, ask for the Key Information Summary (KIS) to be updated.
   - In **England and Wales**, ask if the ReSPECT form or Coordinate My Care (EPACCS) record is live.

6. **Escalate to patient advocacy services if the pressure continues.**  
   **Contact the hospital’s patient support office immediately if you are being forced to accept an unsafe discharge.**
   - In **England**, contact PALS (Patient Advice and Liaison Service).
   - In **Scotland**, contact PASS (Patient Advice and Support Service).
   - In **Wales**, contact the Llais (the patient voice body).
   - In **Northern Ireland**, contact the Patient and Client Council (PCC).

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on long-term care homes or permanent funding arrangements today.
- You do not need to finalize the funeral or "place of death" if the immediate priority is getting through the first 24 hours.
- You do not need to answer every phone call from non-essential relatives; focus only on the hospital staff and the care coordinator.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant fear or guilt when telling a hospital you cannot cope at home. These feelings are a byproduct of the crisis, not a sign that you are failing your relative. Stating that home is unsafe is a protective act that ensures your relative receives professional clinical support rather than an abandoned or chaotic discharge.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a discharge crisis in the UK. It focuses on the immediate transition from hospital to home and does not cover long-term social care legislation or bereavement support.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or medical advice. Procedures vary between NHS Trusts and local authorities; always insist on seeing a written care plan before discharge occurs.
