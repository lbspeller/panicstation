# What to do if… you are removed from a client account suddenly and the client asks what happened (UK)

## Short answer
Request the meeting purpose and details in writing and ask to schedule the call for a time when you can be in a private space with access to your records and a note-taker or companion.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign immediately or "to get ahead of it" before you know the specific allegations or concerns.
- Do not speculate or offer "off the record" explanations during the initial contact.
- Do not admit to any wrongdoing or apologize for specifics until you have seen the evidence or allegations in writing.
- Do not record the call without the explicit permission of all participants.
- Do not delete, modify, or move work-related documents, emails, or messages after receiving the request.
- Do not discuss the request with colleagues or witnesses while the matter is potentially under investigation.

## What to do now
1. **Send a written response immediately to establish a paper trail.** Reply via email or the workplace messaging system stating: "I am available to discuss this. Please confirm the specific nature of the concern, whether this is an informal chat, an investigatory meeting, or a formal disciplinary hearing, and who will be attending."
2. **Request a specific start time that allows for preparation.** If HR pushes for an immediate "drop-in" call, state: "I need to ensure I am in a private location where I can take notes and focus on the discussion. I will be ready to join the call at [Time, e.g., in 60 minutes or tomorrow morning]."
3. **Verify the meeting type against the ACAS Code of Practice.** Check if the invitation mentions "disciplinary," "investigation," or "grievance," as these labels trigger different statutory rights under UK law.
4. **Identify your right to be accompanied based on the meeting's status.**
   - If the meeting is a **disciplinary hearing** (which could result in a formal warning or dismissal) or a **grievance hearing**, you have a statutory right to be accompanied by a colleague or trade union representative.
   - If the meeting is an **investigation meeting**, there is no automatic statutory right to a companion, but you can still request one as a "reasonable adjustment" if you have a disability or if your employment contract allows it.
5. **Locate and review your staff handbook or employment contract.** Find the sections regarding disciplinary and grievance procedures to see if your employer provides rights beyond the statutory minimum, such as longer notice periods or wider companion choices.
6. **Prepare a notebook and pen to record the discussion manually.** During the call, write down the names of everyone present, the specific allegations or concerns raised, and any deadlines mentioned for follow-up actions.
7. **Maintain a factual and concise communication style throughout the call.** If asked a question you cannot answer with certainty, state: "I need to check my records/the timeline before I can provide an accurate answer; I will follow up in writing."
8. **Request a written summary or minutes once the call concludes.** Ask the HR representative: "When will the notes from this call be shared, and what is the next step in the process?"

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a full defense or a written statement during the first call.
- You do not need to decide whether to involve a lawyer or file a counter-grievance immediately.
- You do not need to agree with HR's interpretation of events or their proposed timeline during the initial conversation.

## Important reassurance
It is standard procedural practice for HR to use neutral or vague language when initiating a discussion to maintain confidentiality. Receiving such a request does not automatically mean a formal sanction is inevitable. Taking a moment to move the conversation to a written format and a scheduled time is a professional and legally sound way to manage the situation.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate stabilization of the first 60 minutes following a request for an urgent HR call. It does not provide guidance on the long-term conduct of disciplinary hearings, tribunal claims, or settlement negotiations.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is based on UK employment principles and the ACAS Code of Practice as of 2026. Workplace policies vary, and specific legal rights may depend on your employment status (employee vs worker) and length of service. If you are a member of a trade union, contact your representative immediately. For formal proceedings, consider seeking advice from Citizens Advice or a qualified employment solicitor.
