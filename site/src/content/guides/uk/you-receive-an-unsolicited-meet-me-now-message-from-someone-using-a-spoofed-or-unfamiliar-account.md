# What to do if… you receive an unsolicited “meet me now” message from someone using a spoofed or unfamiliar account (UK)

## Short answer
Do not go to the requested location and do not share your current coordinates. Stay in a safe, populated area and verify the sender’s identity through a pre-existing, trusted communication channel before responding further.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to the location to "see if they show up" or "just to check" if it is actually your friend.
- Do not click any links, "proof of identity" photos, or location pins sent by the unfamiliar account.
- Do not share your live location, workplace, or intended travel route to "prove" why you cannot meet.
- Do not engage in a defensive or emotional argument to "out" the person as a fake.
- Do not delete the message thread or call logs until you have captured screenshots of the profile and content.

## What to do now
1. **Immediately secure your physical location and stop moving toward any requested destination.** If you are currently in transit to meet them, change direction toward a well-lit, public space or return to your home if it is safe to do so.
2. **Treat any extreme sense of urgency or "emergency" framing as a major risk signal.** Scammers and malicious actors use "meet me now" or "I'm in trouble" narratives to bypass your critical thinking; assume the urgency is manufactured until proven otherwise.
3. **Verify the sender’s identity using a channel you have used successfully in the past.**
   - Contact the person they claim to be via a saved phone number, an old message thread, or a face-to-face check if they are nearby.
   - Use a specific "out-of-band" question that only the real person would know the answer to, or simply ask: "Did you just message me from a new account asking to meet?"
4. **Preserve all digital evidence of the contact without further engagement.**
   - Take screenshots of the message, the account handle, and any "About" or profile information (including phone numbers or IDs).
   - Do not forward the message to others as this can sometimes trigger automated malicious links or hide original metadata.
5. **Report the contact through official UK safety and cybercrime channels.**
   - **For immediate danger:** Call **999** if you believe someone is following you or if you feel a direct threat to your life.
   - **Non-emergency safety concerns:** Call **101** to report suspicious behavior or potential stalking.
   - **Reporting the message (England, Wales, NI):** Contact **Report Fraud** (the successor to Action Fraud) at 0300 123 2040 or via reportfraud.police.uk if you shared data or money.
   - **Reporting in Scotland:** Call **101** to report the incident to Police Scotland directly.
6. **Use automated reporting tools for the specific medium used.**
   - **SMS Text:** Forward the message to **7726** (a free service for all major UK mobile providers).
   - **Suspicious Emails:** Forward the message to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.
   - **Fake Websites:** Report any links sent to the NCSC via their online "Report a suspicious website" tool.
7. **Adjust your digital privacy settings immediately to prevent further tracking.**
   - Disable "Live Location" in WhatsApp, Find My, or Google Maps if it was active.
   - Set your social media profiles to "Private" to prevent the sender from seeing your recent posts or "Stories" that might reveal your current location.
8. **Inform one trusted person about the situation and your current location.** Tell them you have received a suspicious request and are ignoring it; ask them to stay on the phone with you or check in within 15 minutes.

## What can wait
- Investigating who "really" sent the message or trying to find their IP address.
- Replying to explain why you aren't coming or apologizing for "missing" them.
- Deleting your social media accounts entirely; muting and setting to private is sufficient for immediate stabilization.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sudden spike of adrenaline or panic when receiving an "urgent" request from a known name. This physical reaction is exactly what the sender is trying to exploit. By pausing and refusing to move, you have already regained control of the situation. Your safety is more important than being "polite" to a potentially fake account.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the minutes following a suspicious "meet me now" message. It does not cover long-term stalking protection or forensic recovery of hacked accounts.

## Important note
This information is for general safety stabilization and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. If you feel you are being targeted or followed, prioritize contacting the police via 999 or 101.
