# What to do if… you receive a notice that your property may be searched or inspected as part of an investigation (UK)

## Short answer
Do not agree to entry, unlock devices, or share access details on the spot unless you understand whether the person is asking for consent or relying on a legal power. Verify the notice through an independently found official route, contact a solicitor, and if lawful powers are being used, do not obstruct; calmly check what is being relied on and keep a record.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice; there may be deadlines, appointments, or follow-up action.
- Do not hide, move, delete, destroy, or “tidy away” anything connected to the issue.
- Do not give consent to search or inspect just to get it over with.
- Do not let someone in only because they show an ID card, uniform, or badge.
- Do not argue on the doorstep or physically block entry if they say they are using a legal power to enter.
- Do not sign anything you do not understand, especially a consent form, statement, or note of interview.
- Do not discuss the notice in group chats, social media posts, or messages that could be shared.
- Do not guess answers to questions about other people, devices, documents, or accounts.

## What to do now
1. **Treat the notice as unverified until you check it independently.**  
   Do not use phone numbers, payment links, QR codes, or email links from the notice. Use an official website, main switchboard, or known public contact route to check:
   - whether the notice is genuine,
   - whether the reference number exists,
   - who is responsible for the visit,
   - what property or area is involved,
   - whether the visit is a request for consent, an arranged inspection, or the use of a legal power.

2. **Ask for the exact authority being relied on.**  
   Ask, preferably in writing: “What legal power, warrant, order, licence condition, inspection power, or consent request are you relying on?” Different police, regulatory, immigration, housing, environmental, trading standards, prison, probation, and other official powers can have different rules.

3. **Contact a solicitor before the visit if you can.**  
   Tell them: “I have received a notice that my property may be searched or inspected as part of an investigation. I need urgent advice before I agree to entry or answer questions.”  
   If it looks criminal or police-led, ask for a criminal defence solicitor. If it is a regulator, landlord-related official body, council, immigration, environmental, business, or professional investigation, ask for someone with that area of experience.

4. **Tell other people at the property one clear thing.**  
   Say: “Do not consent to entry or hand over access details on the spot. Ask what authority they are using and call me or the solicitor.” This is especially important if you live with family, flatmates, tenants, staff, or a partner.

5. **If someone attends, keep the first conversation calm and at the threshold where possible.**  
   Ask for their name, role, organisation, and ID. Write it down. Then ask: “Are you asking for my consent, or are you relying on a legal power to enter?” If they are only asking for consent, you can say: “I am not consenting to entry now. Please put the request in writing so I can take legal advice.”

6. **If they say they have a warrant or other legal power, do not obstruct.**  
   Ask to see and read the warrant, order, written authority, licence condition, or other paperwork. Check the address, date, named person or organisation, what areas may be entered, what they are looking for, and whether anything may be seized or copied. Ask for the Notice of Powers and Rights or equivalent paperwork if one is available.

7. **Switch to record-keeping mode during any search or inspection.**  
   Note the time they arrive and leave, who is in charge, which rooms or areas are searched, what questions are asked, and what is taken, copied, photographed, or damaged. Ask for a search record, receipt, inventory, or written note of anything seized or copied.

8. **Protect urgent safety needs without interfering.**  
   Tell the person in charge if there are children, vulnerable adults, pets, medical equipment, essential medication, mobility needs, religious items, confidential work material, or anything dangerous on the property. Ask when you may call your solicitor if you have not already done so.

9. **Be careful with phones, computers, keys, passwords, and accounts.**  
   Do not volunteer passcodes, unlock devices, or hand over account access just to be helpful. If you are told you must provide something, stay calm, ask what legal power is being used, and tell a solicitor as soon as you can.

10. **If the notice is linked to bail, licence, probation, prison, or supervision conditions, find that paperwork now.**  
   Check the exact wording of your conditions and contact your solicitor or supervising contact to clarify what you must allow, what requires notice, and what should be put in writing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a long explanation, complaint, or statement today.
- You do not need to decide now whether to challenge the notice, warrant, inspection, or seizure.
- You do not need to contact neighbours, your employer, clients, or relatives unless there is an immediate practical need.
- You do not need to prepare the property beyond making it safe for people and pets.
- You do not need to answer wider investigation questions without advice.

## Important reassurance
A notice like this can make everything feel urgent, but the safest first steps are usually simple: verify who sent it, identify whether consent or legal authority is being used, get legal advice, avoid changing or destroying anything, and keep a clear record.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the period after receiving a notice or when someone attends. Search, inspection, seizure, and entry powers vary by agency, legal system within the UK, type of property, and the exact authority being used, so later decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. If you may be arrested, questioned, searched, inspected, or asked to hand over devices, documents, passwords, keys, or account access, get urgent advice from a solicitor before agreeing, signing, or answering investigation questions.
