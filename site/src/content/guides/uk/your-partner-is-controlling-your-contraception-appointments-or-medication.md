# What to do if… your partner is controlling your contraception, appointments, or medication (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a safety and healthcare privacy issue. Get a private moment, use a safer phone or device if you can, and contact a confidential domestic abuse or healthcare service without your partner present.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront them in the moment if that could increase danger.
- Do not let them insist on being in the room, speaking for you, or holding your phone during healthcare calls or appointments.
- Do not stop essential medication suddenly just to avoid conflict.
- Do not use a device, account, browser, or location-sharing service they monitor if that could put you at risk.
- Do not assume you have to decide today whether to leave, report to police, or explain everything.

## What to do now
1. **Get to the safest private pause available.** If you can, step into a room with a lock, go for a short walk, make an errand excuse, or use a private moment at work, a pharmacy, a GP practice, or a clinic.
2. **If you are in immediate danger, call 999.** If you cannot speak, stay on the line and listen. If prompted, press **55** to show it is a genuine emergency and to be put through to police.
3. **Use a safer contact route.** If you think your phone is monitored, use a trusted person’s phone, a work phone, a clinic phone, or another device they do not control. Ask services not to leave voicemail or send letters or texts unless you say it is safe.
4. **Contact specialist support for where you are.**
   - England: National Domestic Abuse Helpline, **0808 2000 247**.
   - Northern Ireland: Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline, **0808 802 1414**.
   - Scotland: Domestic Abuse and Forced Marriage Helpline, **0800 027 1234**.
   - Wales: Live Fear Free, **0808 80 10 100**.
   - If you are in England or Wales and anything sexual happened without consent, or you are not sure, Rape Crisis England & Wales 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line: **0808 500 2222**.
5. **Arrange healthcare contact that excludes your partner.** Call your GP practice and ask for a private appointment, or contact a sexual health or contraception clinic directly. Say: “I am experiencing controlling behaviour and I need to speak to a clinician alone.”
6. **Use a one-sentence privacy script.** Repeat it if needed: “I am not safe discussing this with anyone else present. Please speak to me alone and only contact me on this safe number.”
7. **Tell the clinician the specific interference.** Say if your partner has hidden pills, controlled prescriptions, blocked appointments, pressured unprotected sex, tampered with condoms, watched messages, or insisted on being present. Ask about discreet contraception, emergency contraception if relevant, and safer ways to receive medication or appointment messages.
8. **Reduce immediate sabotage risk only if it is safe.** If you can do it without being discovered, keep contraception or medication somewhere your partner cannot access, such as with a trusted person, at work, or in a bag you control.
9. **Keep a small facts list only if safe.** Write dates and short facts such as “hid pills,” “blocked appointment,” or “threatened me if I used contraception.” Do not keep this on a device or account they can access.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this “counts as abuse.”
- You do not need to decide today whether to end the relationship or report to police.
- You do not need to choose the perfect contraception now; the first goal is a private clinical conversation and immediate safety.
- You do not need to gather evidence or explain the whole relationship history before asking for help.

## Important reassurance
Wanting private healthcare, private appointments, and control over contraception or medication is reasonable. Feeling confused, embarrassed, or unsure is common when someone is controlling your body, phone, appointments, or prescriptions.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, private healthcare access, and specialist support. Later choices about housing, legal options, reporting, or longer-term safety planning may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in danger or fear escalation, prioritise immediate safety and contact emergency or specialist support.
