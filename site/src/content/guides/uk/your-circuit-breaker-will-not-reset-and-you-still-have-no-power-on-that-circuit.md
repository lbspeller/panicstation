# What to do if… your circuit breaker will not reset and you still have no power on that circuit (UK)

## Short answer
Stop trying to force the breaker back on. Leave the affected circuit off, keep away from anything hot, damp, buzzing, scorched, smoky, or smelling of burning, and contact a registered electrician or your landlord or letting agent.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep flicking the breaker back on repeatedly to see if it holds.
- Do not tape, wedge, bypass, replace, or modify any breaker, fuse, RCD, RCBO, socket, switch, cable, or appliance wiring.
- Do not remove the consumer unit cover beyond the normal front flap.
- Do not keep using an appliance you suspect caused the trip, even if it appears to work elsewhere.
- Do not touch sockets, switches, plugs, cables, or the consumer unit if they are hot, buzzing, crackling, scorched, damp, smoky, or smell of burning.
- Do not use water near the consumer unit, sockets, switches, plugs, extension leads, or the suspected fault area.
- Do not run extension leads under rugs, through doors, through windows, or across wet areas to replace the dead circuit.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger.** If you see smoke or flames, smell burning, hear crackling or arcing, or the consumer unit feels hot, move people away. Switch off the main switch only if you can do that without touching anything unsafe, then call **999** for the fire service.

2. **Leave the affected circuit switched off for now.** A breaker that will not reset may be cutting power because of a fault in wiring, a socket, a light fitting, water ingress, or an appliance.

3. **Check whether this is a single-circuit fault or a wider power cut.** If only one circuit is off, treat it as a fault in your home. If several circuits or the whole home are off, or neighbours are also affected, treat it as a power cut. In England, Scotland and Wales you can call **105** for power-cut help. In Northern Ireland, use your electricity network operator’s published power-cut contact route.

4. **Unplug what you safely can on the affected circuit.** Unplug appliances, chargers, heaters, kettles, irons, tumble dryers, and other plug-in items in the rooms or sockets that lost power. If there is a safe external switch or fused spur for an appliance, switch it off without opening covers.

5. **Make one careful reset attempt only if there are no danger signs.**
   - Move the tripped switch fully to **OFF**, then back to **ON**.
   - If an RCD has tripped and you can clearly identify the individual circuit switches, turn the individual circuits off, turn the RCD on, then turn circuits back on one at a time.
   - Stop as soon as the problem circuit trips again, anything feels unsafe, or you are not sure what you are looking at.

6. **If it will not reset, trips immediately, or trips again, keep that circuit off.** Do not keep testing it. Put a note near the consumer unit saying the circuit has been left off because it will not reset.

7. **Do a no-dismantling check of what changed.** Think about whether the trip followed a new appliance, a heater or high-power appliance, DIY drilling, a bulb change, outdoor socket use, water near a socket, a leak, condensation, or recent work. Write this down for the electrician or landlord.

8. **Contact the right person.** If you rent, report it to your landlord or letting agent as an electrical fault and say the breaker will not reset and the circuit has been left isolated. If you own the home, contact a registered electrician and say whether there are burning smells, damp sockets, medical equipment, heating problems, or vulnerable people at home.

9. **Keep the home usable without creating another hazard.** Use battery torches rather than candles. Keep fridge or freezer doors closed where possible. Use other sockets only lightly, and do not overload extension leads or multi-way adapters.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find the exact fault tonight.
- You do not need to test wires, remove sockets, open light fittings, move heavy appliances, or take the consumer unit apart.
- You do not need to decide about rewiring, upgrades, or replacement appliances now.
- You do not need to prove whether the fault is your appliance, the wiring, or the consumer unit before asking for help.

## Important reassurance
A breaker that will not reset is often protecting you by keeping power off when something may not be safe. Leaving the circuit off and getting it checked is a sensible way to buy time and reduce risk.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and avoid common electrical-safety mistakes. A registered electrician or your landlord’s contractor may need to test the circuit, sockets, fittings, and appliances before the power can safely be restored.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional electrical, legal, tenancy, insurance, or emergency-service advice. If you suspect fire, overheating, smoke, arcing, or anything unsafe to touch, prioritise immediate safety and emergency help.
