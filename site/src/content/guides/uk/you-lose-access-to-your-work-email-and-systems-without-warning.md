# What to do if… you lose access to your work email and systems without warning (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and treat it as either an IT issue, a security hold, or a workplace instruction until you are told otherwise. Use a neutral non-work channel to contact your manager and IT or the service desk, and ask what has happened and what you should do next.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to work around access controls by using personal cloud storage, personal email, a colleague’s login, forwarding rules, or remote-access tools.
- Do not repeatedly guess passwords, use random password-reset links, or sign in on unfamiliar pages.
- Do not delete messages, wipe a work device, remove work files, or change settings unless IT tells you to.
- Do not post about it publicly or message lots of coworkers for explanations.
- Do not resign, accept blame, or send an angry message while you are trying to understand what has happened.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the basics before you make more attempts.** Note the time access stopped, what you cannot access, such as email, VPN, HR portal, shared drives, or your work device, and any exact error message. Take a photo or screenshot if you can do that without bypassing security.

2. **Contact your line manager through a neutral channel you already use.** Use your personal phone, text message, or a known non-work contact route. Send: “I’ve suddenly lost access to my work email and systems. Can you confirm whether this is an IT or security issue, or whether there is another instruction I need to follow?”

3. **Contact IT, the service desk, or security through an official route.** Say: “My account appears inaccessible. Please confirm whether it is locked for security and tell me the official reset or verification steps.” Do not use reset links from unexpected emails or texts.

4. **Treat it as a possible security issue until IT says otherwise.** If you recently clicked a link, approved a sign-in, entered a password somewhere unusual, lost a work device, or saw an unexpected multi-factor prompt, tell IT or security plainly. If you reused the same or a very similar password on personal accounts, change those personal passwords from a trusted device.

5. **Report suspicious messages only through official channels.** If the suspicious message was in a personal email account, you can forward it to report@phishing.gov.uk. If it was a suspicious text message, you can forward it to 7726. If the suspicious message is inside a work account you cannot access, tell IT rather than trying to get back in by unsafe routes.

6. **If you are told you are suspended or under investigation, switch to simple paper-trail mode.** Ask for written confirmation, if they can provide it, of whether you are suspended, when it started, whether pay and benefits continue, who your HR contact is, and what you are allowed to do while locked out. Ask whether you may contact colleagues about work, support, or the issue itself.

7. **If you are told your employment has ended, keep it practical.** Ask HR for a short written statement of your employment status, how you will receive any pay owed, how holiday pay will be handled if relevant, whether a P45 will be issued, and how to return company property with a record.

8. **Do not agree to anything important on a rushed call.** If someone asks you to accept blame, confirm resignation, sign something, or hand over property informally, say: “I’m not able to agree to anything right now. Please send it to me in writing and I’ll respond.”

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to raise a grievance, resign, accuse anyone, or start a claim. If this becomes a formal employment dispute, deadlines and processes may matter, but the first task is to find out what has happened and keep a clear record.

## Important reassurance
A sudden lockout feels alarming, but it can happen because of ordinary IT faults, automated account controls, security precautions, admin changes, or a workplace process. The safest first move is to avoid workarounds, keep communications calm, and get clear instructions through proper channels.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, for the first hours after an unexpected work email or systems lockout. Once you know whether it is an IT fault, security hold, suspension, investigation, or termination, you may need specialist employment, union, cyber security, or legal support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, cyber security, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Workplace procedures and rights depend on your contract, workplace policies, location, and the specific facts.
