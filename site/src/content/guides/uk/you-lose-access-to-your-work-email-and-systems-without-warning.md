# What to do if… you lose access to your work email and systems without warning (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the discrepancy against your records, notify payroll or your manager in writing immediately to request a correction, and confirm the specific date the shortfall will be paid.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the error will be automatically corrected in the next pay cycle without a written agreement.
- Do not sign any new contracts, deduction waivers, or hours-change documents while in a state of distress.
- Do not use language like "theft" or "illegal" in your initial communication; treat it as a technical error first.
- Do not stop working or walk out mid-shift, as this can be treated as a breach of contract or resignation.
- Do not accept "under the table" cash payments or informal IOUs that bypass your official pay statement.

## What to do now
1. **Gather and compare your primary evidence immediately.**  
   Locate your latest payslip, bank statement, and time-tracking records (rotas, clock-in logs, or sent emails). Compare the hours worked and the agreed rate in your contract against the "gross pay" shown on the slip.
2. **Identify the exact nature of the shortfall.**  
   Determine if the missing amount is due to:
   - unpaid basic hours or overtime
   - missing bonuses, commissions, or shift premiums
   - an incorrect hourly rate (check if you recently had a birthday or if the April 2026 minimum wage increase applies)
   - an unauthorized or unexplained deduction
3. **Contact your manager or payroll department in writing.**  
   **Send a neutral, factual message stating the discrepancy.**  
   Example: "I have reviewed my payslip for the period ending [Date] and it appears I have been paid for 30 hours instead of 37. Please confirm when the remaining £[Amount] will be transferred."
4. **Request a specific "out-of-cycle" payment if the shortfall causes hardship.**  
   **Ask for an immediate BACS transfer rather than waiting for the next scheduled payday.**  
   If the employer refuses, ask for a written explanation of why the correction must wait and how they will ensure your tax and National Insurance (NI) records are adjusted.
5. **Check if the underpayment takes you below the National Minimum Wage (NMW).**  
   **Calculate your average hourly pay for the period after the deduction.**  
   If your pay falls below the statutory minimum (£12.71 for age 21+ from April 2026), your employer is in breach of NMW law. Note this for any future report to the Fair Work Agency (FWA).
6. **Note the relevant legal time limits for your records.**  
   **The deadline to start a claim for "unlawful deduction from wages" is usually 3 months minus 1 day from the date the pay was due.**  
   Be aware that under the Employment Rights Act 2025, these limits are scheduled to extend to 6 months starting in October 2026; use the shorter 3-month window to remain safe.
7. **Initiate the formal grievance process if the informal request is ignored.**  
   **Follow your company’s written grievance procedure exactly.**  
   Submit a formal letter detailing the dates, amounts, and your previous attempts to resolve the issue informally. Attach copies of your evidence.
8. **Contact Acas for Early Conciliation if the deadline approaches.**  
   **Notify Acas before the 3-month time limit expires to "pause the clock."**  
   Early Conciliation is a mandatory step before you can take an employer to an Employment Tribunal. From December 2025, this process can last up to 12 weeks.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to resign or claim "constructive dismissal" today.
- You do not need to audit your entire employment history right now; focus on the current pay period first.
- You do not need to hire a solicitor to send an initial internal inquiry.
- You do not need to worry about the Fair Work Agency's long-term enforcement powers if this is a simple one-off administrative error.

## Important reassurance
Payroll errors are common and are frequently resolved with a single polite email. Most employers prefer to fix a mistake quickly rather than face a formal grievance or an external audit. Feeling anxious about your finances is a normal response to an unexpected shortfall; taking the first step of documenting the error often reduces this stress.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a single pay period shortfall. It does not cover long-term pay disputes, complex pension contributions, or insolvency-related non-payment.

## Important note
This guide is for information purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Employment laws and enforcement bodies, such as the Fair Work Agency, are subject to change in 2026. If you are a member of a trade union, contact your representative immediately for specific advocacy.
