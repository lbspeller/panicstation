# What to do if… someone you know keeps showing up at places you regularly go (UK)

## Short answer
Prioritise safety over certainty: get to a safer place, tell someone what is happening, and start a simple record of repeated sightings or contact. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront them alone, agree to meet, or try to “clear it up” face to face.
- Don’t follow them, test them, bait them, or try to catch them out yourself.
- Don’t dismiss the pattern just because each single incident could be explained away.
- Don’t post real-time location or routine details, including stories, check-ins, gym tags, work tags, or commute updates.
- Don’t delete messages, call logs, voicemails, DMs, photos, or notes that may help show a pattern.
- Don’t block them before saving what you may need; muting may be safer while you save records.
- Don’t make big irreversible changes in a panic, such as quitting your job or moving, before you have support and a safer plan.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If you have just seen them, go into a staffed public place such as a shop, café, reception area, transport hub, or workplace entrance. Stay near other people. If you feel threatened, think they may follow you, or believe you are in immediate danger, call 999. If it is unsafe to speak, call 999 and, if prompted, press 55.

2. **Tell one trusted person now.** Message or call someone you trust and say where you are, who you have seen, and what you need. Ask them to stay on the phone, meet you, or check that you arrive safely.

3. **Set a simple check-in for the next journey.** Agree one practical check-in, such as “I’ll text when I leave work and when I get home.” Share your route only with someone you trust.

4. **Tell staff at the places where this is happening.** If it is safe to do so, tell workplace reception, security, campus security, gym staff, a building manager, or a regular venue manager. Give the person’s name if you know it, a brief description, and a clear request, such as: “Please do not confirm my schedule,” “Please call me if they appear,” “Please walk me to my car or bus stop,” or “Please call police if I ask.”

5. **Start a short incident log.** Write down each sighting or contact with the date, time, exact place, what happened, who saw it, and what you did next. Save screenshots, messages, call logs, DMs, voicemails, and photos you already have. Do not delay getting help just to collect more proof.

6. **Report repeated incidents if you feel unsafe or unsure.** If there is immediate danger, call 999. If it is not an emergency, you can contact police through 101, online reporting, or a police station. Say clearly that this has happened more than once, that it involves places you regularly go, and that it is affecting your sense of safety. Ask how to record updates and what reference number to use.

7. **Contact specialist stalking support.** The National Stalking Helpline can help you think through safety, records, reporting, and next steps. You can contact them on 0808 802 0300 or through their website.

8. **Ask what protective options apply where you live.** When speaking to police, the helpline, a domestic abuse service, or a legal adviser, ask what protective options may be available in your part of the UK. If the person is an ex-partner or family member, say that clearly, because it can affect which support routes are relevant.

9. **Reduce how predictable you are for now.** Make small changes that do not create more risk: use a staffed entrance, avoid isolated car parks or quiet corridors, ask someone to walk with you, leave with a colleague, vary a route when safe, and park or wait in well-lit public areas.

10. **Check routine leaks on your phone and accounts.** Turn off location sharing you do not need, review who can see your posts and stories, and change key passwords from a trusted device, starting with email and then social media. Use two-factor authentication where you can.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to move, change jobs, stop going to familiar places, or confront them.
- You do not need perfect proof before seeking help; a pattern and your safety concerns are enough to ask for support.
- Longer-term steps, such as legal advice, housing changes, workplace adjustments, or formal safety planning, can wait until you have reached a safer pause and spoken to police or a specialist service.

## Important reassurance
It is common to second-guess yourself when each incident could be explained away on its own. You are allowed to take the pattern seriously and protect yourself early.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and reduce immediate risk. Later decisions may need help from police, a stalking specialist, a domestic abuse service, a legal adviser, your workplace, or another appropriate professional.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.
