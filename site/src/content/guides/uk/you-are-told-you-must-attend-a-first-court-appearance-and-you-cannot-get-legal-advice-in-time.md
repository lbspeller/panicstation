# What to do if… you are told you must attend a first court appearance and you cannot get legal advice in time (UK)

## Short answer
Attend the court as scheduled and immediately request the "duty solicitor" upon arrival at the court building. If no duty solicitor is available or eligible, ask the court for an "adjournment" (a delay) to obtain legal advice before entering any plea or making decisions.

## Do not do these things
- Don't miss the court date — failure to appear is a separate criminal offence and can lead to an immediate arrest warrant.
- Don't enter a "guilty" or "not guilty" plea until you have spoken with a legal professional.
- Don't agree to specific bail conditions or "restraining orders" if you do not understand the exact restrictions.
- Don't discuss the details of your case with anyone in the court waiting areas, corridors, or canteen.
- Don't assume court staff or the prosecutor can give you legal advice; they represent the court and the state respectively.

## What to do now
1. **Gather every piece of paperwork related to your charge or summons and take it with you to the court.**
   - Include the charge sheet, postal requisition, or summons.
   - Note down the date, time, and the specific name of the court (e.g., Magistrates’, Sheriff, or JP Court).
2. **Collect proof of your financial status to assist with immediate legal aid applications.**
   - Bring a recent bank statement, a payslip, or proof of state benefits (on your phone or paper).
   - This is necessary for the "means test" if you apply for a representation order.
3. **Arrive at the court building at least 45 minutes before your scheduled hearing time.**
   - This provides the only window to locate and speak with the duty solicitor.
4. **Identify yourself to the court usher or the reception desk immediately after passing security.**
   - Use this exact phrasing: "I am unrepresented for my first appearance today. I need to see the duty solicitor."
5. **Wait in the designated area for the duty solicitor to call your name or approach you.**
   - If they are busy, stay visible; if the usher tries to call you into court before you have had advice, tell the usher: "I have not had legal advice yet and I am waiting for the duty solicitor."
6. **State clearly to the Magistrates or Judge if you are called into the courtroom without advice.**
   - Say: "I am unrepresented and have not been able to obtain legal advice. I request an adjournment to seek legal representation before I enter a plea."
7. **Provide a "bail address" if the court discusses releasing you until the next hearing.**
   - Have a clear address written down where you can live and receive mail.
   - Mention any "caring responsibilities" or "medical needs" if they might affect bail conditions (like a curfew).
8. **Request an interpreter or assistance for a disability immediately if required.**
   - Tell the court staff or the Judge: "I require an interpreter in [language]" or "I have a disability and require [specific adjustment] to take part."

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare a full "defence" or gather witnesses for this first hearing.
- You do not need to decide whether you want a "Jury Trial" or a "Magistrates Trial" (either-way offences) in the first minutes.
- You do not need to pay any legal fees today if you are seeing a duty solicitor for the first time.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard part of court business for people to arrive without a lawyer for their first hearing. The court system has specific rotas (duty schemes) designed to catch these situations. Requesting more time to get advice is a normal and responsible action that the court is accustomed to hearing.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for the initial appearance only. It does not cover trial preparation, sentencing hearings, or appeals.

## Important note
Availability of duty solicitors varies by jurisdiction and is often restricted to "imprisonable" offences in England and Wales. In Scotland, the duty solicitor has exclusive jurisdiction for those in custody or on undertakings for summary cases. This information is correct as of March 2026.
