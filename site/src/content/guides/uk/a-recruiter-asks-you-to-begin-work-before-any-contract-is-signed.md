# What to do if… a recruiter asks you to begin work before any contract is signed (UK)

## Short answer
Request the purpose of the meeting and any relevant documents in writing before attending, and do not sign or agree to any proposals during the discussion.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign, accept an exit package, or agree to any terms during the meeting.
- Do not sign any documents, including settlement agreements or confidentiality waivers, on the spot.
- Do not assume "confidential" means the meeting is informal or low-stakes.
- Do not send messages to colleagues or post on social media about the meeting invite.
- Do not feel forced to negotiate or respond to offers immediately.
- Do not assume you must pay for legal advice yourself; employers typically provide a contribution for the mandatory legal review.

## What to do now
1. **Send a written request for the meeting agenda and a list of attendees.** Ask specifically for the purpose of the meeting, the names and roles of everyone attending, and copies of any documents that will be discussed.
2. **Request to bring a companion to the meeting even if not explicitly offered.** While there is no automatic statutory right to accompaniment for settlement-only meetings, ACAS Code of Practice recommends it as good practice, and you should ask to bring:
   - a work colleague
   - a trade union representative
   - a support worker (if required as a reasonable adjustment for a disability).
3. **Clarify the legal basis of the "confidentiality" at the start of the meeting.** Ask the employer to confirm if they are treating the discussion as a "protected conversation" under Section 111A of the Employment Rights Act 1996 or as "without prejudice" negotiations.
4. **State clearly that you are attending to listen and take notes only.** Inform the attendees at the outset that you will not be making any decisions or providing a formal response during the session.
5. **Ask for any offer or proposal to be provided in a formal written document.** Ensure the document includes the proposed termination date, the financial breakdown, and any deadlines for your response.
6. **Request a minimum of 10 calendar days to consider the proposal.** The ACAS Code of Practice states that parties should be given a minimum of 10 calendar days to consider the formal written terms of a settlement agreement.
7. **Verify if the meeting is part of a formal disciplinary or grievance process.** Ask the employer to confirm if the meeting is a formal hearing, as this triggers a statutory right to be accompanied by a colleague or union representative.
8. **Take a detailed written record immediately after the meeting concludes.** Document what was said, any pressure applied, and the names of those present while the details are fresh.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to accept an offer or leave the company today.
- You do not need to hire a solicitor before you have seen the written proposal.
- You do not need to explain the situation to your team or manager right now.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of legal advice yet, as this is usually funded by the employer if an agreement is reached.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an invitation to a confidential settlement discussion is a standard procedural step in many UK workplaces. Feeling uncertain or startled is a common response to an unexpected meeting request. Taking time to seek clarity and refusing to make immediate decisions is a professional and protective course of action.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling an unexpected meeting invitation. It does not cover the legal merits of a specific settlement offer or the strategy for long-term employment tribunal claims.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. In the UK, a settlement agreement is only legally binding if you have received advice from a relevant independent adviser, such as a qualified solicitor or a certified trade union official. Do not sign a settlement agreement without this independent advice.
