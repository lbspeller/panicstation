# What to do if… you are considering a medical or forensic exam after a suspected sexual assault and you feel overwhelmed (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safe place and contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) to discuss medical care and forensic options. You can access these services and have evidence stored without involving the police right now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel pressured to decide about police reporting, making a statement, or defining the incident immediately.
- Do not assume it is too late to seek help; medical care and support are available regardless of when the incident occurred.
- Do not wash, shower, or change clothes if you are considering a forensic exam within the next 7 days.
- Do not brush your teeth, eat, or drink if an oral assault is suspected.
- Do not confront the person involved or post details on social media while in a state of shock.
- Do not use a "self-swab" kit at home, as these may not be admissible in a future legal case.

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 immediately if you are in danger or require urgent medical attention for serious injuries.**
   - If you are safe but want to speak to the police about a non-urgent matter, call 101.
2. **Contact a SARC to speak with a specialist who can explain your options without pressure.**
   - In England and Wales: Find your nearest SARC by searching the NHS website or calling 111.
   - In Scotland: Call the national 24/7 self-referral phone service at 0800 148 88 88.
3. **Ask the SARC staff directly if you can "self-refer" for medical care and evidence collection without a police report.**
   - This allows you to have a forensic medical examination (FME) and have the evidence stored securely while you decide what to do next.
4. **Follow specific steps to preserve potential evidence if you are within 7 days of the incident.**
   - Place any clothing worn at the time in a clean paper bag (not plastic).
   - If you need to use the toilet, try to do so into a clean container and bring this with you.
   - Avoid washing or cleaning any part of your body if possible.
5. **Request an Independent Sexual Violence Adviser (ISVA) or support worker to assist you.**
   - You have the right to ask for a support worker to be present or for the professional to explain every step of the process before it happens.
6. **Use "control" phrases if you feel overwhelmed during a phone call or appointment.**
   - "I need you to explain the options slowly."
   - "I would like to take a break now."
   - "I want medical care but I am not sure about a forensic exam."
   - "I am stopping this process now."

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you will ever report this to the police.
- You do not need to provide a chronological or detailed account of what happened right now.
- You do not need to speak to anyone other than healthcare or support professionals if you are not ready.
- You do not need to decide if you want a full forensic exam until you have spoken to a professional about what it involves.

## Important reassurance
Feeling numb, disconnected, frozen, or unable to focus is a common and normal response to a suspected sexual assault. These reactions are biological responses to shock and do not mean you are failing to handle the situation. You are allowed to move at your own pace and change your mind at any point in the process.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only, focused on immediate safety and preserving options for medical and forensic care. Later decisions may require specialist legal or clinical advice.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and is not a substitute for professional medical or legal advice. If you are unsure of your safety or health, contact emergency services or a specialist SARC provider immediately.
