# What to do if… you receive a request to hand over CCTV, doorbell camera, or dashcam footage to authorities (UK)

## Short answer
Pause, verify who is asking, and get the request details in writing before you share footage or hand over any device. Some requests are voluntary; some may be backed by a court order, warrant, or other legal requirement, so ask which it is.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hand over your phone, camera, SD card, NVR, DVR, or cloud account access on the spot unless you are clearly legally required to or have had advice.
- Don’t share passwords, unlock codes, two-factor codes, or login details.
- Don’t delete, trim, enhance, rename, or re-export footage in a way that overwrites the original recording.
- Don’t share a wider time period, extra cameras, private indoor footage, or unrelated clips when a narrow segment is enough.
- Don’t post the footage online or send it to neighbours, group chats, or social media while an incident is being dealt with.
- Don’t rely only on a phone number, email address, QR code, or upload link given by the person asking.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the request details.** Note the person’s name, force or agency, collar number or staff number if given, contact details, date and time of the request, and any incident, log, or crime reference number.

2. **Ask whether it is voluntary or legally required.** Use plain wording:
   “Is this a voluntary request, or are you requiring it under a court order, warrant, statutory power, or other legal requirement? Can you provide the reference or a copy?”
   If it is voluntary, it is generally reasonable to say you need time to check the request before sending anything.

3. **Verify the requester through an official route.**
   - If they are at your door, ask to see official identification, such as a warrant card for a police officer, and write down the details.
   - If the request came by phone, email, text, QR code, or upload link, do not use only the contact details in that message.
   - Contact the force or agency through an official public number or website and ask them to confirm the person, the reference number, and the footage request.

4. **Pause for legal advice before sharing if there is risk to you.** Get advice from a qualified UK solicitor before providing footage, devices, passwords, or broad access if you might be a suspect, the footage could implicate you, the footage could put you or someone else at risk, or the request asks for “everything” rather than a specific clip.

5. **Keep the footage request narrow.** Ask for the exact camera, date, start time, end time, and reason the footage is needed. If you decide to share, provide only the relevant segment and flag any sensitive material, such as footage inside a home, children, neighbours, medical situations, or unrelated private activity.

6. **Preserve the relevant original.** If your system overwrites recordings, make a copy of the exact relevant window as soon as you can, but keep the original recording or device untouched. Avoid screen recordings if your system can export the original file or a standard export.

7. **Record what you provided.** Keep a note of the file names, camera source, date and time window, transfer method, recipient, and reference number. If physical media or a device is taken, ask for a written receipt or inventory.

8. **Treat non-police official requests the same way.** For another authority or agency, verify identity, ask what legal power or purpose they rely on, keep the request narrow, and get advice before sharing anything broad, risky, or unclear.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to cooperate beyond the specific footage request.
- You do not need to give a statement about what you think happened.
- You do not need to edit, label, enhance, narrate, or explain the footage unless you later choose to or are formally required to.
- You do not need to give account access if a narrow export or copy can answer the request.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to slow the moment down. Verifying the requester, preserving the original, and sharing only what is necessary can help without giving away more access than the situation calls for.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the request becomes a formal legal demand, involves a search or seizure, or could affect you personally, later decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. In the UK, data protection law can allow sharing footage with law enforcement where it is necessary and proportionate, but that does not automatically mean every request is mandatory or that broad access is required. If you are unsure whether you must comply, or you may be at risk of arrest, charge, civil action, or retaliation, get advice from a qualified UK solicitor before handing over devices, credentials, or broad access.
