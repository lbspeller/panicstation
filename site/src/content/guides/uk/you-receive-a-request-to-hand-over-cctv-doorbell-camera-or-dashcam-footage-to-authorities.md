# What to do if… you receive a request to hand over CCTV, doorbell camera, or dashcam footage to authorities (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the identity of the requester and confirm whether the request is voluntary or a formal seizure under the Police and Criminal Evidence Act (PACE). You are generally permitted to share footage for the "prevention or detection of crime" under the Data Protection Act 2018, but you should narrow the scope to the specific incident and time window.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hand over your physical device (phone, tablet, or DVR) or storage media (SD card) unless a formal seizure is in progress or a warrant is presented.
- Don’t allow anyone to "scroll through" your full library of recordings without a specific legal requirement.
- Don’t delete, crop, or modify the original high-resolution footage before you have made a secure backup.
- Don’t post the footage to social media while an active investigation is ongoing, as this can prejudice legal proceedings.
- Don’t assume a request via email or text is genuine without verifying it through an official police force channel (e.g., calling 101).

## What to do now
1. **Identify the officer and the case details immediately.** **Ask for the officer’s name, collar number, police force, and the specific crime reference number (CRN).**
   - Note these details down on paper or in a secure digital note.
   - If the request is made in person, ask to see their warrant card.
2. **Determine the legal basis for the request.** **Ask explicitly if the request is voluntary or if the officer is exercising powers under PACE 1984 or a court warrant.**
   - Most initial requests for doorbell or dashcam footage are voluntary.
   - If an officer is lawfully on your premises and believes the footage is evidence that may be lost or destroyed, they may have the power to seize it under Section 19 of PACE.
3. **Verify the requester through official channels if the request was remote.** **Contact the relevant police force via their official 101 number or public website to confirm the officer and the investigation exist.**
   - Do not use contact details provided solely in an unsolicited email or text link.
   - Ask the 101 operator to verify that the officer is currently dealing with the incident number provided.
4. **Isolate and preserve the relevant footage segment.** **Identify the exact time window requested and export only that specific portion of the video.**
   - Do not provide access to your entire camera history or cloud account.
   - Use the "export" or "download" function of your system to maintain the original metadata and quality.
5. **Utilize official digital evidence portals for the transfer.** **Ask the officer to send an official link to a secure evidence management portal (such as Axon Citizen or Nice Investigate).**
   - This ensures the chain of custody is logged and provides a digital record of what you shared.
   - Avoid sending raw video files via unencrypted personal email or messaging apps if possible.
6. **Apply the Data Protection Act (DPA) 2018 exemption.** **Confirm for your own records that you are disclosing the data under Schedule 2, Part 1, Paragraph 2 of the DPA 2018.**
   - This exemption allows you to share personal data (like images of neighbors or passersby) with law enforcement for the prevention or detection of crime without breaching GDPR.
7. **Obtain a receipt for any physical items taken.** **If an officer seizes a physical device or storage card, insist on a written receipt or an entry in their pocket notebook that you can photograph.**
   - This should include the date, time, officer details, and a description of the item seized.

## What can wait
- You do - not - need to provide a formal witness statement at the same moment you hand over the footage.
- You do - not - need to allow an officer into your home to "fix" or "configure" your camera system.
- You do - not - need to explain your presence or actions at the time of the recording if you were not involved in the incident.

## Important reassurance
It is standard procedure for police to canvas a neighborhood for video evidence after an incident. Asking for verification and requesting a formal upload link is a professional way to handle the situation and does not imply that you are being uncooperative or have something to hide.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and the immediate handover process for video evidence. It does not cover legal defense if you are a suspect or the long-term storage requirements for evidence used in court.

## Important note
Under UK law (PACE 1984), police have specific powers to seize evidence if they have reasonable grounds to believe it is necessary to prevent its concealment or destruction. However, most doorbell and dashcam requests begin as voluntary "appeals for footage." If you believe the footage could implicate you in a crime, you should seek immediate legal advice from a qualified solicitor before disclosing any data.
