# What to do if… you receive a message threatening to share intimate webcam recordings of you (UK)

## Short answer
Stop engaging, do not pay, and do not send anything else. Save a minimal record of the threat, secure your email and key accounts, then use the right UK reporting route.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay, negotiate, or try to prove anything to them; paying does not guarantee the threats will stop.
- Do not send more images, videos, live video, passwords, codes, or “verification”.
- Do not click their links, open unknown attachments, or install anything they send as “proof”.
- Do not screenshot, save, forward, or share sexual images or videos of anyone who may be under 18, even for reporting.
- Do not delete your account, delete the thread, or wipe your device just to make it go away.
- Do not spend hours checking whether the recording is real; one calm set of basic details is enough for now.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Put the device down for a moment, take a few slow breaths, and move away from anyone pressuring you to reply quickly. Messages like this are often sent to many people, even when they include personal details.

2. **Stop contact.** Do not reply. Do not argue, bargain, apologise, or ask for proof. If the threat is on a platform, use the platform report tool after you have saved the basic details.

3. **Save a minimal record.** Keep the message, sender details, dates, platform name, usernames, email addresses, phone numbers, profile links, payment details, wallet addresses, and any threat to contact your friends, family, followers, employer, or school. If an intimate image or video might involve someone under 18, do not screenshot, save, forward, or share it; record only the non-image details.

4. **Do not delete the account or thread.** If you need to stop seeing the messages, mute, archive, or block after saving the basic details. If you feel you must remove a profile, deactivating is usually safer than deleting because it may preserve information for reporting.

5. **Secure your email first.** If the message included a password you still use anywhere, change it wherever it is reused. Change your email password first because email is often used to reset other accounts.

6. **Secure the accounts they mention.** Turn on two-factor authentication for your email and main social accounts. Check recovery phone numbers and email addresses, remove any you do not control, and save recovery codes somewhere you can find later.

7. **Do quick device checks without panicking.** Update your operating system and browser. Check camera and microphone permissions and remove access for apps you do not recognise. Run a scan with reputable security software if you already have it. Covering the webcam or closing the laptop is fine if it helps you feel safer.

8. **Report through the right UK route.**
   - If there is immediate danger, violence, or the crime is happening now and you need urgent help, call 999.
   - For sextortion or threats to share intimate images, use the Police.uk sextortion reporting guidance or contact your local police on 101 if it is not an emergency.
   - If the incident is mainly cyber crime or fraud and you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud.
   - If you live in Scotland, or the crime happened in Scotland, contact Police Scotland on 101 unless it is an emergency.
   - If money has already been sent, contact your bank or payment provider as soon as you can and ask what steps are available, then report.

9. **If you are under 18, or this involves someone under 18, use CEOP.** CEOP is the UK reporting route for online sexual abuse concerns involving children and young people. You do not have to decide alone whether the threat is real before asking for help.

10. **If intimate images or videos actually exist or have been shared, use specialist takedown support.** UK adults can contact the Revenge Porn Helpline. StopNCII.org may help if you are currently over 18, were 18 or older when the image was taken, still have the image or video, and want to create a case for participating platforms.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to tell friends, family, your employer, or your school.
- You do not need to prove tonight whether the recording is real.
- You do not need to change every account at once; start with email and the accounts they mention.
- You do not need to buy new software, wipe devices, or replace your phone before taking the basic safety steps.
- You do not need to keep reading their messages to “understand” the threat.

## Important reassurance
A message like this can cause a rush of shame, fear, and urgency. That reaction is exactly what the sender is trying to use. The safest first moves are calm and practical: stop contact, do not pay, save basic details, secure key accounts, and report through the right route.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and prevent irreversible mistakes. Later decisions, especially if money was sent, threats escalate, or images are shared, may need specialist support.

## Important note
This guide gives general information and safety steps, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel at risk, overwhelmed, or unsafe, contacting police or a specialist support service is a valid next step.
