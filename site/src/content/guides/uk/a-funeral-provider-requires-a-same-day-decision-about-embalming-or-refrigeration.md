# What to do if… a funeral provider requires a same-day decision about embalming or refrigeration (UK)

## Short answer
You are not legally required to authorise embalming for a burial or cremation in the UK. Tell the provider: “I do not consent to embalming at this time; please ensure the person is kept in refrigeration while we finalise our decisions.”

## Do not do these things
- Don't sign any "Authorisation for Embalming" or "Care of Deceased" form until you have read every line and understood the costs.
- Don't accept the claim that embalming is a legal requirement (it is only required for international repatriation).
- Don't agree to embalming solely because you wish to view the person; refrigeration and basic hygienic care are often sufficient for early viewing.
- Don't feel forced to commit to a full funeral package just to secure safe storage of the deceased.
- Don't believe a provider can legally refuse to release the deceased to another funeral director if you decide to switch.

## What to do now
1. **State your immediate decision clearly to the staff.**
   **Tell the provider: “I am not authorising embalming today. Please use refrigeration only.”** Repeat this statement if the provider offers justifications for the procedure.
2. **Identify the person with the legal right to give consent.**
   **The Executor (if there is a Will) or the next of kin (if there is no Will) has the legal right to decide on the care of the body.** If you are not that person, state that no decision can be made until the legal representative is consulted.
3. **Request the legally mandated Standardised Price List.**
   **Ask for the 'CMA Standardised Price List' and the 'Additional Options Price List' in writing.** Under the Funerals Market Investigation Order 2021, these must be provided and must show the specific cost for both refrigeration and embalming.
4. **Ask for the specific reason for the same-day deadline.**
   **Ask: “Why is a decision required today? Is it due to your storage capacity, the condition of the deceased, or your company policy?”** If it is a capacity issue, they must inform you if they are unable to provide appropriate refrigerated storage.
5. **Verify the current location of the deceased.**
   **Ask if the person is currently at the funeral home, a hospital mortuary, or a coroner’s mortuary.** If they are still at a hospital or with the coroner, they are already being kept in refrigerated conditions, and you have more time to decide.
6. **Check for existing written instructions or a pre-paid plan.**
   **Search the deceased’s paperwork for a funeral plan or specific wishes regarding embalming.** If a plan exists, it may already cover these costs or state a preference.
7. **Document the conversation in writing.**
   **Send a brief email or text to the provider confirming: “As discussed at [time], I have not authorised embalming and expect the deceased to be kept in refrigeration.”** This creates a paper trail if the procedure is performed without consent.
8. **In Scotland, cite the Funeral Director Code of Practice.**
   **If the provider is in Scotland, remind them that the 2025 Code of Practice mandates informed, written consent before any embalming takes place.** They are required by law to provide clear information to help you make this decision.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on the type of coffin, flowers, or transport today.
- You do not need to settle on a date for the funeral service immediately.
- You do not need to pay the full funeral bill today; most directors only require a deposit for disbursements (third-party fees) initially.
- You do not need to decide whether you want a permanent viewing or an open-casket service right now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when asked to make technical decisions while grieving. Asking for refrigeration is the safest "default" choice because it is a reversible decision that preserves your options. You are not "disrespecting" the deceased by refusing an invasive chemical procedure.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and rights regarding the care of the deceased. It does not cover long-term funeral planning, the legalities of the coroner’s inquest process, or international repatriation requirements.

## Important note
This is general information and does not constitute legal advice. In the UK, funeral directors are currently not subject to a single statutory regulator in England and Wales, although they must comply with Consumer Rights legislation and CMA orders. Always insist on seeing a written contract before committing to high-cost services.
