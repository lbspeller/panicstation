# What to do if… you are told a loved one is likely to die within days and you need to alert close family (UK)

## Short answer
Pause to record the clinician's exact words, designate one trusted person to handle secondary notifications, and send a single clear message to the immediate inner circle. Do not attempt to manage all communications alone while in the initial period of shock.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide exact "hour by hour" predictions to relatives, as clinical timelines are estimates and subject to change.
- Do not attempt to make dozens of individual phone calls if you are feeling overwhelmed or physically unsteady.
- Do not share sensitive clinical data, ward security codes, or financial requests in unencrypted group chats.
- Do not engage in disputes regarding who "deserves" to be told first; focus on the immediate legal next of kin and the person’s stated wishes.
- Do not assume "next of kin" status grants you legal authority to make medical decisions; it is a communication role only.

## What to do now
1. **Record the basic facts in a single digital or paper note.**
   - Include the loved one’s full name and date of birth.
   - Note the specific ward, hospice name, or home address.
   - Record the name and role of the clinician who provided the update.
2. **Clarify the communication records with the nursing or palliative team.**
   - **Ask who is currently listed as the primary contact person for medical updates.**
   - **Confirm that the team has the correct, most direct phone number for that person.**
   - **Ask if there is a preferred time or method for the family to request further updates.**
3. **Appoint a "Family Coordinator" to protect your own energy.**
   - **Delegate the task of updating the wider circle to one reliable person (e.g., a sibling or close friend).**
   - **Provide this person with a specific list of names to contact.**
   - **Instruct them to act as a "buffer" for repeated questions from extended family or friends.**
4. **Send a factual initial alert to the smallest appropriate inner circle.**
   - **Use a short, written script to ensure consistency and reduce the need for repeat explanations.**
   - **Example script:** "[Name] is now in their last days and is currently at [Location]. If you wish to visit or speak with them, please make arrangements to come today. I will provide further updates when they are available."
5. **Verify the current visiting status with the ward or hospice staff.**
   - **Ask if there are any practical limits on the number of people at the bedside at once.**
   - **Inquire about facilities for overnight stays or if video calls can be facilitated by staff.**
   - **Note that under 2026 CQC Regulation 9A, providers must facilitate visits for end-of-life care and should not enforce fixed "visiting hours."**
6. **Notify your employer using the correct statutory terminology.**
   - **State clearly: "I am taking emergency time off for dependants to deal with a family crisis."**
   - **Be aware that under the Employment Rights Act, you have a day-one right to a "reasonable" amount of unpaid time off for such emergencies.**
   - **Request a follow-up from HR regarding the 2026 statutory Bereavement Leave if the situation progresses.**
7. **Maintain a "No Money" rule for all group communications.**
   - **Explicitly tell family members that you will never ask for urgent bank transfers or money via message.**
   - **Instruct them to call you directly to verify any unusual requests to prevent scams.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to make decisions about funeral directors or probate services today.
- You do not need to reply to every individual message of sympathy or "how are they?" query.
- You do not need to provide deep medical explanations; "the clinical team says they are dying" is sufficient.
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family conflicts or estrangements.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of "brain fog," numbness, or an inability to process complex instructions. These are standard physiological responses to acute stress. Taking a few minutes to sit in silence or write down a single task does not mean you are failing your loved one; it is a necessary part of stabilization.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and communication management for the first 24 to 48 hours. It does not cover long-term bereavement support, legal estate administration, or medical diagnosis.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal, medical, or employment advice. Statutory rights like "Time Off for Dependants" are generally unpaid unless specified in your contract. If you require immediate emotional support, contact the Marie Curie Support Line at 0800 090 2309.
