# What to do if… you receive a call saying someone in custody wants you to store their property and you feel unsure about it (UK)

## Short answer
End the call immediately without agreeing to anything or providing personal details. Verify the request by calling 101 (for police) or the prison's official enquiry line found on GOV.UK, using a different phone or waiting five minutes to ensure the line is clear.

## Do not do these things
- Don't give out your bank details, address, or any one-time security codes to the caller.
- Don't agree to meet a "courier" or "agent" to hand over or collect any items.
- Don't assume the call is genuine because the caller knows the name of your relative or friend.
- Don't trust the number on your screen, as scammers frequently spoof official police or prison numbers.
- Don't allow the caller to pressure you with "urgent" deadlines or legal threats.
- Don't pay any "release fees" or "storage insurance" requested over the phone.
- Don't use a phone number provided by the caller to "verify" their identity.

## What to do now
1. **Terminate the call immediately without providing an explanation.** Scammers often use "social engineering" to keep you talking; simply hanging up is the safest first step.
2. **Wait at least five minutes or use a different phone to make your next call.** Fraudsters can sometimes "stay on the line" even after you hang up, meaning when you try to call 101, you are actually still speaking to the scammer.
   - If available, use a mobile phone if the suspicious call came to your landline.
3. **Contact the police via 101 to verify the claim if it involves police custody.** **Ask the operator to check the name and collar number of the officer who supposedly called you, or to confirm if the named individual is actually in custody at that station.** 
   - Note the reference number provided by the 101 operator for your report.
4. **Use the "Find a Prison" tool on GOV.UK if the caller claimed to be from a prison.** **Search for the specific prison named and call their general enquiries or reception number directly to ask about their property release procedures.**
   - Official prison staff will never ask you to store property privately or pay money via a phone call.
5. **Contact your bank's fraud department immediately if you shared any financial information.** **Call the number on the back of your debit or credit card or use the official banking app to report a potential compromise.**
6. **Report the attempted scam to Action Fraud.** **Use the official online reporting tool at actionfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040 to provide details of the caller's claims and the number used.**
7. **Secure your property and home if the caller mentioned your address.** **Ensure all doors and windows are locked, and if anyone arrives at your door claiming to be a courier or officer, do not open it and call 999 immediately.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to help the person in custody right now.
- You do not need to contact the person in custody to confirm the story until you have verified the caller's identity through official channels.
- You do not need to worry about "obstructing justice" by hanging up on a suspicious caller.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel panicked when a caller mentions a loved one in trouble. Legitimate police and prison services have strict, slow-moving protocols for property that involve written authorization and physical ID checks at official buildings. They do not conduct business through urgent, unsolicited storage requests to the public.

## Scope note
This guide is for immediate stabilization and fraud prevention. It does not cover the long-term legal processes for acting as a property nominee or the rules regarding "prohibited items" in prisons.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance only. Police and prison property procedures can vary by force and establishment. If you believe a crime is in progress or you are in physical danger, call 999.
