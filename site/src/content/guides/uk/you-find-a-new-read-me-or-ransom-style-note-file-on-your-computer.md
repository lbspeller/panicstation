# What to do if… you find a new “read me” or ransom-style note file on your computer (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the device by disconnecting it from all internet and network connections immediately to prevent further encryption or data theft. Use a separate, uninfected device to report the incident and secure your primary accounts.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay the ransom demand or communicate with the attackers via links, email, or chat windows provided in the note.
- Don’t restart the computer if it is actively encrypting files; power it down entirely instead.
- Don’t connect backup drives, USB sticks, or shared network storage to the affected machine.
- Don’t attempt to use "free decryptor" tools found through search engines, as these may contain additional malware.
- Don’t log into banking, work, or social media accounts from the compromised device.
- Don’t delete the ransom note or any strange files, as these may be needed for forensic analysis or decryption later.

## What to do now
1. **Sever all network connections immediately.**
   **Turn off the Wi-Fi using a physical switch or through software settings, and unplug any ethernet cables.**
   - If you cannot quickly disable the connection, or if files are visibly changing/renaming in real-time, press and hold the power button to force a shutdown.
2. **Disconnect all peripheral storage devices.**
   **Unplug external hard drives, USB flash drives, and SD cards that are currently connected to the machine.**
   - Do not plug these into any other computer until they have been professionally scanned for infection.
3. **Document the ransom demand using a different device.**
   **Use a smartphone or camera to take clear photos of the ransom note on the screen, any unusual file extensions, and the "About" screen of the computer (showing the OS version).**
   - Record the exact time you first noticed the note and any specific programs that were running at the time.
4. **Secure high-priority accounts from a clean device.**
   **Log into your email, online banking, and primary identity accounts (Microsoft, Google, Apple) from a known-safe device to change passwords and verify Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).**
   - Prioritize changing your email password first, as it is often the gateway for resetting other services.
5. **Report the incident to the national reporting centre.**
   **Contact Report Fraud (formerly Action Fraud) online or by calling 0300 123 2040.**
   - The phone line is available Monday to Friday, 8am–8pm. 
   - If you are an organisation, use the NCSC Cyber Incident Signposting Service (CISS) to determine if you must also notify the National Cyber Security Centre.
6. **Assess the data breach reporting requirements.**
   **If the compromised device contains personal data of others, determine if you must notify the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO).**
   - Notifiable breaches under UK GDPR must generally be reported within 72 hours of you becoming aware of the incident.
7. **Consult official decryption resources.**
   **Visit the "No More Ransom" website (an official project by law enforcement and tech companies) from a safe device to see if a legitimate decryption tool exists for your specific ransomware variant.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to pay the ransom immediately; the initial focus is on containment and account security.
- You do not need to wipe or "factory reset" the computer right now; keeping the system as-is allows for professional data recovery attempts.
- You do not need to notify every contact in your address book immediately; start with your bank and IT department if applicable.

## Important reassurance
Finding a ransom note is a high-stress event designed to trigger a panicked reaction. By disconnecting the device, you have already taken the most effective step to stop the attack from spreading to your backups or other devices on your network.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a suspected ransomware infection. Full recovery, including system cleaning and data restoration, typically requires specialized technical support and verified clean backups.

## Important note
This information is for guidance purposes and does not constitute legal, financial, or professional cybersecurity advice. If you are handling a cyber incident for a business or public body, you should follow your organization’s formal incident response plan and seek qualified technical assistance.
