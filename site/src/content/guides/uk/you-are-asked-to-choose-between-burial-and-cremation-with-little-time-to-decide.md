# What to do if… you are asked to choose between burial and cremation with little time to decide (UK)

## Short answer
Request a pause from the funeral director or hospital bereavement office to confirm who has legal authority to decide and to check for recorded wishes. Ask for the person to be kept in refrigerated care while you take 24 to 48 hours to confirm the choice.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign any cremation or burial application forms if you are currently in a state of high distress or if there is active family conflict.
- Don’t assume that choosing cremation is mandatory due to time or cost; ask for a price comparison for the simplest versions of both.
- Don’t allow multiple people to provide conflicting instructions to the funeral director; nominate one person to communicate the final decision once reached.
- Don’t pay a full deposit or agree to non-essential services (flowers, cars, stationery) until the fundamental choice of burial or cremation is settled.
- Don’t rush the decision because of "paperwork deadlines" without first asking what the specific legal or medical delay is.

## What to do now
1. **Request an immediate "hold" on instructions for at least 24 hours.** **Inform the funeral director or hospital mortuary that you need a safe pause to locate a will or confirm the personal representative.** 
   - Ask: "What is the procedure and cost for keeping the person in refrigerated care for an additional 48 hours while we confirm authority?"
2. **Identify the person with the legal right to decide.** **Confirm who is the named Executor in the will or, if there is no will, who is the Administrator under the Rules of Intestacy.** 
   - The Executor has the legal right and duty to arrange the disposal of the body.
   - If there is no will, the hierarchy generally follows: spouse or civil partner, then children (over 18), then parents, then siblings.
3. **Conduct a focused search for a Will or a Letter of Wishes.** **Check physical "life admin" folders, safe-deposit boxes, or contact the person's solicitor to see if a preference for burial or cremation was recorded.** 
   - Note: Funeral wishes in a will are not legally binding in the UK, but they are the primary guide for Executors and the courts in cases of dispute.
4. **Clarify if the death has been referred to the Coroner (England, Wales, NI) or Procurator Fiscal (Scotland).** **If an investigation is active, the authorities will control when the body is released and what certificates are issued.** 
   - Ask: "Has the Coroner/Fiscal issued a certificate for burial or cremation yet?"
   - In England and Wales, the Medical Examiner system (reformed in 2024) must also provide clearance before the registrar can issue the "Green Form."
5. **Verify the correct statutory forms for your specific jurisdiction.** **Ensure you are using the mandatory forms required for 2026, particularly if the death occurred in Scotland.** 
   - In Scotland, new statutory burial and cremation application forms under the 2025 Amendment Regulations must be used for all applications from 2 March 2026.
   - In England and Wales, ensure the funeral director is following the 2024 death certification reforms regarding cremation forms.
6. **Ask for a "Basic/Simple Service" quote for both options in writing.** **Request a breakdown that excludes all ceremony extras (flowers, celebrant, viewing) to see the true cost difference between the two options.** 
   - This helps remove financial panic from the immediate decision-making process.
7. **Set a "no-action" boundary if family members are in active conflict.** **State clearly to the funeral director that no irreversible steps (such as cremation) should be taken until legal authority is confirmed.** 
   - If the conflict remains deadlocked, a court application can be made to appoint a person to handle the disposal, though this is a last resort.

## What can wait
- Deciding on a funeral date, time, or location.
- Choosing a specific coffin, urn, or headstone.
- Selecting music, readings, or a celebrant.
- Paying for "repatriation" or "limousines" before the core decision is made.
- Notifying the wider circle of friends and colleagues.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when asked to make a permanent decision while in shock. Most funeral providers and hospitals are equipped to hold a body safely in refrigerated care for several days. Taking a 24-hour pause to find a will or talk to family will not usually cause a significant problem and often prevents long-term regret.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate decision. It does not cover long-term estate administration, the details of coronial inquests, or the legalities of property rights in human remains.

## Important note
Legal authority for funeral arrangements is determined by the status of the person (Executor or Administrator) and the Rules of Intestacy. Statutory forms and medical certification processes changed significantly in 2024 (England/Wales) and March 2026 (Scotland). Always confirm the current version of forms with your funeral director.
