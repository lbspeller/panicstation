# What to do if… a garage ceiling or roof structure starts sagging and you’re unsure if it’s safe to stay beneath (UK)

## Short answer
Evacuate the garage immediately and establish a clear exclusion zone around the structure. Contact your local council's Building Control "Dangerous Structures" team for an emergency assessment if the risk is public or imminent.

## Do not do these things
- Do not enter or remain inside the garage to investigate, "watch" the sag, or retrieve belongings.
- Do not attempt to prop up the ceiling or roof with jacks, timber, or supports yourself.
- Do not open or close any garage doors, especially electric ones, if the frame appears twisted or under strain.
- Do not ignore sudden new sounds such as loud cracks, pops, or groans coming from the structure.
- Do not climb onto the roof to inspect or attempt to drain water pooling on top.

## What to do now
1. **Evacuate the area immediately.** Ensure all people and pets are out of the garage and clear of the potential "fall zone" (the area where debris would land if the structure failed).
2. **Secure the site to prevent entry.** Lock internal doors leading to the garage and use physical barriers (bins, tape, or chairs) to stop others from accidentally entering the space.
3. **Check rooms adjacent to or above the garage.** If the garage is attached to the house, keep people out of any rooms directly sharing a wall with or positioned above the sagging section until it is assessed.
4. **Call 999 if there is an immediate threat to life.** Use the emergency services if you believe the structure is about to collapse onto a public highway, or if anyone is trapped or injured.
5. **Contact your local council's Building Control department.** Ask for the **Dangerous Structures team**; most UK councils operate a 24-hour service to assess buildings that pose a risk to the public or occupants.
6. **Identify the responsible party.**
   - If you rent the property: notify your landlord or letting agent **immediately** that you have declared the garage a no-go zone.
   - If the property is leasehold: contact the freeholder or managing agent to report a structural failure.
7. **Isolate electrical power only if it is safe.** If you can access the consumer unit (fuse box) from a safe part of the house and the garage is on a separate circuit, switch it off—especially if water leaks are visible near light fittings.
8. **Document the situation from a safe distance.** Take photographs or video of the sag from outside the garage or through a doorway without stepping inside to assist with insurance or professional surveys.
9. **Commission a professional structural inspection.** Once the immediate danger is stabilized, arrange for a **Chartered Structural Engineer** (MIStructE) or **Chartered Building Surveyor** (MRICS) to provide a formal safety report.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause of the sagging (such as rot, overload, or structural failure) right now.
- You do not need to clear out the contents of the garage today; safety of life takes precedence over property.
- You do not need to obtain repair quotes or select contractors until a professional structural assessment is complete.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel hesitant about closing off a functional part of your home, but structural movement is unpredictable. Taking the "overcautious" step of evacuating is the only way to ensure safety when structural integrity is in question.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a potential structural emergency only. It does not replace the mandatory on-site assessment required from a qualified structural professional or local authority officer.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional engineering advice. Local authorities have statutory powers under the Building Act 1984 to take action on dangerous structures; always follow the instructions given by Building Control officers or emergency services on-site.
