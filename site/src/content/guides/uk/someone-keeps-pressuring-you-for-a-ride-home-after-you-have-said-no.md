# What to do if… someone keeps pressuring you for a ride home after you have said no (UK)

## Short answer
Treat repeated offers of rides home after you have said no as a safety signal. Do not get into their car; move toward a safer, more public way home and tell someone what is happening.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t get into their car “just this once” to avoid awkwardness.
- Don’t tell them your address, exact route, or what time you will be leaving.
- Don’t let them move you to a quiet place, such as a car park, side street, stairwell, or empty room, “to talk”.
- Don’t try to handle it alone if you feel uneasy; involve someone else early.
- Don’t post real-time location updates or regular commute patterns on social media.
- Don’t assume you need proof before asking for help or changing your route.

## What to do now
1. **Get yourself home in the safest, most public way available today.**  
   Go with a trusted colleague or friend, wait in a staffed or public area, take a well-lit route, or use a licensed taxi or private-hire vehicle that you book yourself. If you feel at risk right now, stay inside and ask staff, security, or a trusted person to come to you.

2. **Use one short boundary line and stop explaining.**  
   You have already said no. Use something like: “No, I’m not getting a lift. Please stop asking.” Then end the conversation and move toward other people.

3. **Tell one trusted person what has been happening.**  
   Say who is offering the rides, where it happens, how often it has happened, and what you need today, such as being met at the exit, walking to transport together, or checking in when you get home.

4. **Set up a simple check-in for the next few journeys.**  
   Message or call someone when you leave, when you reach transport, and when you arrive. If your plan changes, send one short update so someone knows what to expect.

5. **If this is linked to work, study, housing, or a regular venue, ask for practical safety support.**  
   You can ask a manager, HR, a safeguarding lead, reception, campus security, building security, or venue staff for help leaving safely, being accompanied to transport, changing an exit point, adjusting a rota, or stopping the person from approaching you there.

6. **If it continues, escalates, or you feel followed, threatened, or unsafe, contact a specialist support route.**  
   You can call police on 101 for non-emergency advice or reporting, report online through Police.uk where available, or call 999 if you or someone else is in immediate danger.

7. **Preserve options quietly if it is safe to do so.**  
   Keep texts, direct messages, voicemails, notes of dates and places, and names of anyone who saw what happened; do not put yourself at risk to collect anything.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide today whether to make a formal report or complaint.
- You don’t need to decide whether to call it harassment, stalking, or something else.
- You don’t need a perfect explanation for why it feels wrong; “I said no and it keeps happening” is enough.
- You don’t need to confront them alone or prove that they meant harm.

## Important reassurance
Feeling uneasy is a valid reason to change your plan and ask for help. Repeated pressure after a clear no is not something you have to smooth over or manage alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stay safer in the immediate situation and keep your options open. Later decisions may need support from a specialist service, workplace or education safeguarding route, police, or another trusted professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999. If you are not in immediate danger but want police advice or to report, call 101 or use Police.uk. If you want confidential specialist support, Rape Crisis England & Wales’ 24/7 Support Line is available on 0808 500 2222 for people aged 16+ in England and Wales, and the National Stalking Helpline is available on 0808 802 0300. If that service does not cover where you are, use Police.uk, GOV.UK, or a local specialist support service.
