# What to do if… your laptop is locked by an unknown firmware or BIOS password (UK)

## Short answer
Stop guessing passwords. If you can still reach your files, back them up first, then use an official proof-of-ownership route: your IT admin, the retailer, or the manufacturer’s support.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep trying random passwords; repeated failed attempts may create lockouts, delays, or extra recovery steps.
- Do not pay “BIOS unlock” sites or follow bypass guides from forums; they may be scams, may damage the laptop, and may be unlawful if you are not clearly authorised to access the device.
- Do not open the laptop or attempt hardware “clears” unless you are competent to do that work; it can damage the device, affect support, or make data recovery harder.
- Do not change BIOS or UEFI security and boot settings while drive encryption is enabled, unless you already have the recovery key.

## What to do now
1. **Record exactly what you are seeing.**  
   Take a clear photo of the screen showing the exact prompt text and any code or message. Note the laptop make, model, and serial number or service tag from the case.
2. **Make one deliberate attempt to recall the real password, then stop.**  
   Check whether it may belong to a workplace or school IT team, a repair or refurbishment provider, a previous owner, a family member, or a password manager entry. If you are guessing, stop.
3. **Identify the type of lock as best you can.**  
   - If the prompt appears before Windows, macOS, or Linux starts, it is usually a power-on, firmware, BIOS, or UEFI password.  
   - If it explicitly says **HDD password** or **SSD password**, it may be a drive-level lock, so moving the drive to another computer may not help.
4. **If you can still reach the operating system, back up your important files now.**  
   Copy the most important files to an external drive or cloud storage before changing anything else. If Windows device encryption or BitLocker is on, look for the recovery key in your Microsoft account, work or school account, printout, USB drive, or through the organisation that manages the device.
5. **If this is a work, school, or managed laptop, contact IT first.**  
   Tell them the exact prompt text and send the photo. Ask whether the password is policy-enforced and ask them to resolve it through their authorised process.
6. **If it is your personal laptop, contact the manufacturer’s official support.**  
   Use the manufacturer’s support site, not an unlock advert. Be ready to provide the model, serial or service tag, the on-screen code if there is one, and proof of purchase or ownership.
7. **If you bought it second-hand and it arrived locked, contact the seller or platform now.**  
   Ask for a return or refund through the platform or payment method. Keep the listing, receipt, messages, and your photo of the lock screen.
8. **If a retailer sold it to you recently, keep the complaint simple and in writing.**  
   Explain that the laptop may be faulty or not as described because it is locked by a firmware, BIOS, or drive password you were not given. Attach the photo and order details, and ask the retailer for its repair, replacement, or refund process.
9. **If the seller stalls and you paid by card, gather evidence before contacting your card provider.**  
   Keep a short timeline, the order details, photos, and messages. Ask your card provider about chargeback; if you paid by credit card and the purchase qualifies, Section 75 may also apply, usually for qualifying purchases over £100 and up to £30,000.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to pay for a repair, replace the motherboard, or replace the laptop.
- You do not need to attempt a reset procedure yourself right now.
- You do not need to change encryption, secure boot, TPM, or boot-order settings until you have secured backups and found any recovery keys you may need.

## Important reassurance
A firmware or BIOS password prompt can appear for ordinary reasons: a management policy, a repair or refurbishment mistake, a second-hand laptop that was not properly cleared, or a setting changed by someone with access. Going slowly and using official routes is the safest way to avoid turning a lockout into data loss.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation, protect data where possible, and avoid irreversible mistakes. Later decisions may need manufacturer support, retailer help, card-provider support, or a reputable repair professional.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. Some firmware and drive locks are intentionally designed not to be bypassable; the correct route is usually authorised support, proof-of-ownership service, or returning the device to the seller. If you are not sure the laptop is yours to access, pause and resolve ownership or authorisation first.
