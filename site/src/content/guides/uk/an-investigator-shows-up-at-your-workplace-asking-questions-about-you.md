# What to do if… an investigator shows up at your workplace asking questions about you (UK)

## Short answer
State that you cannot answer questions without legal advice and ask for the investigator's contact details to provide to a solicitor. Request a more private setting or ask to reschedule the interaction once you have a representative present.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide immediate verbal explanations or "off-the-record" comments to anyone, including managers.
- Do not hand over personal mobile phones, passwords, or private notebooks without a confirmed legal warrant.
- Do not consent to a search of personal bags, vehicles, or lockers without first seeing written authorization or a warrant.
- Do not sign any statements, notes of the meeting, or consent forms during the initial encounter.
- Do not discuss the situation with colleagues or use work email / messaging systems to communicate about the investigation.
- Do not assume that a "voluntary" interview has fewer legal consequences than an interview following an arrest.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the investigator and their agency immediately.**
   - Request to see a formal ID card or warrant card.
   - Record the individual's full name, rank or role, and the organization they represent (e.g., Police, HMRC, HSE, or an external HR consultant).
2. **Move the conversation to a private room away from the main workspace.**
   - If the investigator is external to the company, ask a member of HR or a manager to facilitate a private space.
   - If the investigator is your employer, request that the discussion be paused until you can move to a confidential office.
3. **State your requirement for legal advice clearly.**
   - Say: "I am not prepared to answer questions or provide a statement at this time without first consulting a solicitor."
   - Request a written summary of the allegations or the purpose of the questioning.
4. **Determine the nature of the meeting if it is employer-led.**
   - **Investigation Meeting:** Ask if this is a fact-finding exercise; check your employment contract for a contractual right to be accompanied, as there is no statutory right for investigations.
   - **Disciplinary Hearing:** If the meeting could lead to a formal warning or dismissal, invoke your statutory right to be accompanied by a trade union representative or a workplace colleague.
5. **Handle requests for physical evidence with a "slow-down" protocol.**
   - If asked for a personal device, ask: "Under what legal authority or specific company policy are you making this request?"
   - Request that the demand be put in writing before you comply.
6. **Request that any "voluntary police interview" be scheduled for a later date.**
   - A voluntary interview under PACE Code C gives you the right to free legal advice; do not start the interview until a solicitor is present.
   - Ask for the investigator's email address so your solicitor can arrange the time and location.
7. **Create a factual log of the interaction on a personal, non-work device.**
   - Note the time the investigator arrived and left.
   - List exactly what was asked and your response (e.g., "I stated I needed legal advice").
   - List any documents or items that were viewed or taken by the investigator.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to cooperate fully or remain silent until you have spoken to a solicitor.
- You do not need to provide a defense or a timeline of events during the first encounter.
- You do not need to explain your actions to your line manager or HR department immediately.
- You do not need to review work emails or files to "prove" your innocence right now.

## Important reassurance
The arrival of an investigator is a high-pressure event, and feeling overwhelmed or startled is a standard response. Taking time to seek advice is a protected procedural step and does not imply guilt. You have the right to ensure that any process followed is lawful and consistent with ACAS standards or PACE codes.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate moments of a workplace investigation. It does not cover long-term legal strategy, employment tribunal procedures, or criminal defense.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Rules regarding the Fair Work Agency (operational April 2026) and specific police powers under PACE may vary based on the specific allegations involved. Always verify the identity of anyone claiming to be an enforcement officer.
