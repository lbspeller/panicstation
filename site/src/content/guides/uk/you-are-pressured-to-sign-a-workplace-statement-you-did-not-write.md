# What to do if… you are pressured to sign a workplace statement you did not write (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign it on the spot as accurate. Ask for a copy, ask what your signature would mean, and ask for time to check it or provide your own words.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign anything you have not fully read and understood, including attachments, appendices, or later pages.
- Do not sign a blank, partly completed, or “we’ll fill it in later” document.
- Do not accept “it’s just a formality” unless they put in writing what your signature means.
- Do not argue through every allegation in the room; focus on getting the document, time to check it, and a clear record of your position.
- Do not delete texts, emails, chat messages, meeting invites, notes, or documents related to this.
- Do not resign, accept a warning, or agree an outcome just to end the pressure in that moment.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and use one clear sentence.**  
   “I can’t sign this today because I did not write it. Please give me a copy and time to check it for accuracy.”

2. **Ask what your signature would mean.**  
   Ask: “Does signing mean I agree this is accurate, or only that I have received it?” If they say it is receipt only, ask them to write “signed only to acknowledge receipt, not agreement or accuracy” on the document or confirm that by email before you sign.

3. **Ask for the whole document pack.**  
   Ask for the statement, any attachments or exhibits it refers to, the relevant workplace policy, and any deadline for comments. If they will not give you a copy, say: “I cannot responsibly sign or confirm accuracy without a copy to review.”

4. **If it is presented as your statement, ask to correct it before signing.**  
   In many workplace investigations, a witness statement is often a signed copy of investigation meeting notes. You should usually be given a copy to check it is accurate. Mark or list the parts that are wrong or incomplete, including names, dates, quotes, missing context, and anything that changes the meaning.

5. **Offer your own accurate version.**  
   Ask to provide a separate signed and dated statement in your own words, or to attach corrections to the employer’s version. Keep it factual: what you saw, heard, said, did, or did not say.

6. **If they will not correct it, do not sign it as accurate.**  
   Say: “I do not confirm this as an accurate statement. Please record that I refused to sign because it does not reflect what I said, and please attach my corrections or my own statement.” Write that down for yourself immediately afterwards.

7. **Check what kind of meeting this is.**  
   Ask: “Is this an investigation meeting, a disciplinary hearing, or a grievance meeting?”  
   - At a disciplinary hearing, you generally have the right to be accompanied by a fellow worker, a certified or trained workplace trade union representative, or an official employed by a trade union.  
   - At a grievance meeting, the right to be accompanied applies where the grievance is about a legal or contractual duty; if it is unclear, you can still ask for a companion.  
   - At a disciplinary investigation meeting, there is usually no legal right to be accompanied, but Acas says allowing it is good practice and your workplace policy may allow it.

8. **Make your own record straight away.**  
   As soon as you can, write a short factual note: date, time, who was present, what you were asked to sign, whether you were given a copy, what they said your signature would mean, what deadline they gave, and the exact wording you used when you refused or asked for changes. Email it to yourself or save it somewhere safe.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to raise a formal grievance, contact Acas, or take legal advice.
- You do not need to answer every allegation immediately if you have not been given the full document and evidence.
- You do not need to discuss resignation, settlement, warnings, or outcomes in the same meeting where you were surprised with paperwork.
- You do not need to make a perfect statement on the spot; a short request for a copy, time, and corrections is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
Being put on the spot to sign wording you did not write is a pressure moment, not a deadline to give up control of your own account. Asking for a copy, time to check accuracy, and the chance to give your own words is a reasonable protective step.

## Scope note
These are first steps to slow the situation down, avoid accidental admissions, and create a clear record. Later decisions may depend on whether this is an investigation, a disciplinary process, a grievance, or something that needs specialist employment advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, professional, or other specialist advice. Workplace procedures and rights can vary by contract, policy, role, union status, and the facts of the situation.
