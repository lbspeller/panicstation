# What to do if… you are told you must surrender your passport or travel documents due to a legal order (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and request the written order or bail notice to verify the exact deadline and location for surrender, then hand over the document only at that designated location in exchange for a formal written receipt.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not** hand your passport to any person or official without first viewing their identification and the specific written authority requiring the surrender.
- **Do not** attempt to leave the UK "one last time" or book new international travel while an order is active, as this is often treated as a criminal breach of conditions.
- **Do not** apply for a replacement passport or emergency travel document while a surrender order is in force, as this may be a separate legal offence.
- **Do not** give away the document before you have recorded the passport number, expiry date, and taken clear photographs of every page including current visas.
- **Do not** argue with officials or refuse a valid court order on-site; instead, document your compliance and seek legal variation through the proper court channels.

## What to do now
1. **Request the written legal order or bail notice that specifies the surrender requirement.**  
   Verify the exact wording of the order to confirm which documents are required (e.g., "all travel documents" or a specific passport), the strict deadline for handover, and the named authority or location that must receive them.
2. **Contact the designated receiving office to confirm their opening hours and exact desk for surrenders.**  
   If you are not already at the court or station, verify the location through the official police force website or the HM Courts and Tribunals Service (HMCTS) directory rather than relying on a third party.
3. **Take high-resolution photographs or scans of the document photo page and any valid visas.**  
   Ensure these copies are stored in a secure digital location you can access without the physical document, such as an encrypted cloud folder.
4. **Hand over the documents only in exchange for a formal, dated receipt.**  
   **The receipt must include:**
   - Your full name and the passport number(s).
   - The date and time of the handover.
   - The name and badge number (or staff ID) of the person receiving it.
   - The specific department or court office address.
   - A reference number or case number linked to the order.
5. **Clarify whether the order applies to all travel documents if you hold dual nationality.**  
   Ask the official to confirm in writing if they require all foreign passports in addition to any British passport; do not assume a foreign document is exempt unless the order specifically states so.
6. **Inquire about the specific process for the return of the documents.**  
   Ask what event triggers the return (such as the end of a trial or a specific date) and which office you must contact to initiate the retrieval once the order expires.
7. **Notify your legal representative immediately once the document has been surrendered.**  
   Provide them with a copy of the receipt and the written order so they can begin the process of an "Application to Vary Bail" if the restriction is no longer proportionate.
8. **Verify the 2026 entry requirements if you are a dual national with a surrendered UK passport.**  
   Be aware that under 2026 rules, British dual nationals must normally present a British passport to enter the UK; if yours is held, you may need to discuss a "Certificate of Entitlement to the Right of Abode" with your solicitor for future travel.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to appeal the restriction at the moment of handover.
- You do not need to cancel non-refundable travel booked for months in the future today; wait until you have the written order and have spoken to a solicitor about variation timelines.
- You do not need to explain your personal circumstances to the administrative staff at the counter; keep the interaction focused on the paperwork and receipt.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard legal procedure for passports to be held during active investigations or court proceedings to ensure a person remains available for the process. Taking time to verify the paperwork and insisting on a receipt is a correct and responsible way to handle official documents.

## Scope note
This guide covers only the immediate first steps of surrendering a document under a UK legal order. It does not provide legal strategy for challenging orders or advice on criminal proceedings.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Surrendering a passport is a significant legal event; you should seek advice from a qualified solicitor or the duty solicitor at court as soon as possible.
