# What to do if… someone repeatedly pressures you to remove clothing for photos and calls it harmless fun (UK)

## Short answer
Treat repeated pressure to remove clothing for photos as a safety and boundary issue, not a joke you need to keep explaining. Stop the photo situation, move away from them if you can, and tell one trusted person what has been happening.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to “just one photo” because you feel worn down or want the pressure to stop.
- Do not keep debating whether it was “harmless fun” if they have ignored your no, your discomfort, or your silence.
- Do not meet them alone to “clear the air” if they have already pushed past your boundaries.
- Do not send, copy, save, or forward sexual images of anyone under 18.
- Do not forward intimate images of yourself to someone else for “advice” unless you are sure it is safe and you choose to do that.
- Do not blame yourself for freezing, laughing it off, going quiet, or trying to keep things calm.

## What to do now
1. End the immediate pressure in the simplest safe way available. You can say, “No photos. Stop asking.” Then leave the room, end the call, put your phone down, mute them, or block them if that is safer.

2. Put one barrier between you and the next request. Move communication to text only, avoid being alone with them, change lift or seating plans, leave a chat where they pressure you, or ask someone you trust to stay nearby.

3. Keep a small record of what is happening. Save messages, usernames, dates, times, locations, links, and what was said. Keep this somewhere they cannot access. Do not copy, save, or forward sexual images of anyone under 18.

4. Tell one trusted person in plain words today. Say, “They keep trying to get me to remove clothing for photos and they call it harmless fun. I want help keeping distance.” Ask for one practical thing, such as walking with you, being present when they are nearby, screening messages, or helping you contact support.

5. If you are under 18, tell a trusted adult as soon as you can. This could be a parent, carer, teacher, tutor, youth worker, school nurse, or another safe adult. You do not need to prove it first. Ask them to help stop further contact and to help you use child-safe support if an image exists or might be shared.

6. If a sexual image of you was taken, shared, or threatened and you are under 18, use Childline Report Remove or ask a trusted adult to help you. Do not send the image to friends, staff, or adults for proof.

7. If an intimate image was taken, shared, or threatened and you are 18 or over, contact the Revenge Porn Helpline. Use StopNCII if you meet its criteria and the image may be shared online. Keep messages, links, usernames, and threats where you can, but avoid forwarding the image around.

8. If this is happening at work, report the repeated photo pressure through your manager, HR, union representative, or workplace harassment route. Ask for practical steps that reduce contact while it is being looked at.

9. If you want specialist support without deciding whether to report anything, contact a specialist sexual violence support service. You can talk about the pressure, the photo requests, and what would help you feel safer.

10. If you are in immediate danger, they are trying to stop you leaving, or you think they may hurt you, call 999.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to confront them, report to police, make a formal complaint, explain the whole story neatly, or decide what label fits. The first job is to stop the pressure, reduce contact, and get one safe person or service beside you.

## Important reassurance
Repeated pressure to remove clothing for photos is not made harmless by jokes, flattery, teasing, or being told you are overreacting. Feeling confused, embarrassed, frozen, or unsure is a common response when someone keeps pushing past your boundaries.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about reporting, safeguarding, workplace action, image removal, or ongoing support may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Your immediate safety matters more than collecting proof, and you can ask for help even if you are not ready to report anything.
