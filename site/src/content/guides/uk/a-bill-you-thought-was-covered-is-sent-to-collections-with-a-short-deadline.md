# What to do if… a bill you thought was covered is sent to collections with a short deadline (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay immediately under pressure. Send a **written dispute** to both the collection agency and the original creditor stating the debt is in dispute and requesting an **immediate suspension of recovery** while you provide proof of coverage or payment.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not pay "just to stop the letters"** if you believe the bill is incorrect, as reclaiming money from a collection agency is significantly harder than pausing the process.
- **Do not agree to a repayment plan** or admit liability until you have seen a full breakdown of the debt and confirmed it was not covered by your insurance or contract.
- **Do not handle this solely by telephone;** verbal agreements are difficult to prove, and you need a "paper trail" if this affects your credit file or goes to court.
- **Do not ignore a formal "Letter of Claim"** or court forms, as these have strict legal deadlines that can lead to a default judgment regardless of whether the debt is valid.
- **Do not ignore the original company** just because an agency is now involved; the original creditor often has the power to "recall" the debt if an error is found.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the debt and the collector (2 minutes).**  
   **Check the name of the agency against the Financial Services Register if they claim to collect consumer credit debt.** Do not use links or phone numbers provided in a suspicious text or email; find the official contact details for the company they claim to represent to ensure the demand is not a scam.

2. **Send an immediate "Dispute and Request for Information" notice.**  
   **Write to the agency and the original creditor today via email or tracked post.** Clearly state: 
   - "I dispute this debt in its entirety." 
   - "I believe this amount was covered by [Insurance/Warranty/Service Plan] or has already been paid." 
   - "Under FCA CONC 7.14.1, please suspend all recovery activity while this dispute is investigated." 
   - Request a full "Statement of Account" and a copy of the original agreement or invoice.

3. **Identify if the debt is "Regulated" under the Consumer Credit Act.**  
   **Check if the bill relates to a credit card, personal loan, or "buy now pay later" service.** If it is a regulated debt, the collector must legally pause enforcement if you raise a valid dispute. If it is a utility bill or service invoice, they are still expected to follow "Fair Treatment" and "Consumer Duty" guidelines which discourage aggressive collection of disputed sums.

4. **Gather your "Proof of Coverage" or "Proof of Payment" (10 minutes).**  
   **Locate the specific document that proves the bill should have been handled by someone else.** 
   - Screenshots of insurance claim approvals or "paid" status in a portal. 
   - Email confirmations from the provider stating the balance was zero. 
   - Policy numbers and dates of service. 
   - Bank transaction references if the payment was already made.

5. **Formally request a "Recall" from the original creditor.**  
   **Contact the original company’s billing or "Vulnerable Customer" department and explain the administrative error.** Ask them to formally recall the debt from the agency. If they refuse, tell them you are raising a formal complaint under their internal procedures, which may force a stay on the collection process.

6. **Check for "Pre-Action" legal status.**  
   **Determine if the letter you received is a "Letter of Claim" (England & Wales) or a "Notice of Intention" (Scotland / NI).** 
   - In England and Wales, you generally have **30 days** to respond to a formal Letter of Claim before they can start court action. 
   - Use the "Reply Form" included with such letters to request documentation and state your dispute.

7. **Monitor your credit files immediately.**  
   **Log into Experian, Equifax, or TransUnion (via free services like Checkmyfile or Credit Karma).** Look for any "Default" or "Late Payment" markers related to this bill. If they appear, you can add a "Notice of Correction" (up to 200 words) to your file explaining that the debt is currently in dispute.

## What can wait
- **You do not need to settle the balance today,** even if the letter threatens "immediate" action; legal "immediate" usually allows for a period of response.
- **You do not need to hire a solicitor yet;** most billing disputes can be resolved through the company’s internal complaints process or an Ombudsman.
- **You do not need to worry about "bailiffs" coming today;** in the UK, a collector cannot send a bailiff to your home without a court order and a failed judgment.

## Important reassurance
Administrative errors where "System A" doesn't talk to "System B" are extremely common in insurance and utility billing. Getting a letter from a collection agency is a standard automated step and does not mean you have "failed" or that your credit is permanently ruined. Once a formal dispute is logged, the burden of proof shifts to the creditor to prove you actually owe the money.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for disputed UK debts sent to third-party collectors. It does not provide long-term debt management advice or legal defense strategies for active court proceedings.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Debt laws differ between England/Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. If you receive a "Claim Form" from the County Court or Sheriff Court, seek immediate advice from Citizens Advice or StepChange.
