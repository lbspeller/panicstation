# What to do if… a bill you thought was covered is sent to collections with a short deadline (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay in a panic if you genuinely think the bill is wrong, already paid, or should have been covered. Respond in writing today: say you dispute the debt, ask them to pause collection activity while it is investigated, and request the details and documents behind the amount.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay “just to stop it” while you are still checking whether it is yours, accurate, or covered; paying may make the dispute harder to unwind.
- Do not handle this only by phone. Use email or letter as well, so there is a written record.
- Do not click payment links from texts or emails, and do not give a collector your online banking login.
- Do not admit you owe the debt or agree to a repayment plan while you are still checking coverage and accuracy.
- Do not ignore a Letter of Claim in England and Wales, or any court papers anywhere in the UK. Those can have real deadlines.

## What to do now
1. **Check the message is real before you pay.**  
   Use the company name and reference number, then independently find verified contact details. Do not rely on links in texts or emails. If the message has unusual urgency, odd payment links, or threats, treat it as a possible scam and only use verified contact routes.

2. **Write to the collector today.**  
   Keep it short and factual. Include:
   - your name, address, and their reference number
   - “I dispute this debt because I believe it was covered, already paid, or billed in error.”
   - “Please provide a breakdown of the amount, the dates, the original bill or invoice, and the name of the original creditor.”
   - “Please confirm you will pause collection activity while this is investigated.”

   Send it by email if an email address is provided, and keep a copy. If you post it, get proof of postage.

3. **If it may be a regulated consumer credit debt, say that clearly.**  
   Many debt collectors for consumer credit debts are FCA-authorised. For regulated consumer credit debts, FCA rules generally require a firm to suspend recovery steps where the customer disputes the debt on valid, or potentially valid, grounds while the firm looks into it. If you are unsure whether this debt is regulated, still send the written dispute and document request.

4. **Make a small proof pack showing why you think it was covered.**  
   Save or screenshot:
   - policy, plan, warranty, or service-contract documents
   - claim reference, authorisation number, emails, or portal messages
   - receipt, invoice marked paid, or bank payment reference
   - dates of service and the original provider’s name

   Put these in one folder so you can attach or quote them accurately.

5. **Contact the original provider or creditor as well as the collector.**  
   Ask them in writing:
   - whether they instructed the collection agency
   - whether they can recall or pause the account while it is reviewed
   - what was billed, what remains outstanding, and why
   - what decision was made about cover, payment, warranty, insurance, or authorisation

6. **If the short deadline is a Letter of Claim, treat it as formal.**  
   In England and Wales, a Letter of Claim under the Debt Claims Protocol normally includes an Information Sheet, Reply Form, and Financial Statement form. The Reply Form must usually be sent back within 30 days from the date at the top of the letter. Use the Reply Form to say the debt is disputed and to ask for documents you need. In Scotland or Northern Ireland, the labels and process differ, so respond promptly in writing and get debt advice quickly if court action is threatened.

7. **Check whether it has appeared on your credit files.**  
   Check the three main UK credit reference agencies: Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion. If you see a new default or collection entry you believe is wrong, save screenshots showing:
   - who is reporting it
   - the dates and amounts
   - the account or reference number

   You can dispute incorrect entries with the organisation reporting the data and with the credit reference agency.

8. **Escalate if they keep pressing after a clear dispute.**  
   Use the firm’s complaints process in writing and attach the short dispute trail. If it relates to consumer credit and the business is covered, you may be able to complain to the Financial Ombudsman Service if the firm does not resolve it. If you are in problem debt and need immediate breathing room, ask a debt adviser whether Breathing Space is available to you in England and Wales; a debt adviser must start it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to negotiate, settle, or start a repayment plan.
- You do not need to fix your credit score right now. First, get the account checked and stop avoidable mistakes.
- You do not need to send a long explanation. A short written dispute and document request is enough for the first response.
- You do not need to decide whether to complain to an ombudsman until you have first complained to the firm and it has had the required chance to respond, unless formal court papers arrive.

## Important reassurance
A bill that was supposed to be covered can reach collections because of an admin error, a missed payment allocation, a duplicated invoice, or a mismatch between the provider and the insurer, warranty company, or plan administrator. The safest immediate move is to slow the process down in writing and make them show exactly what they say is owed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and prevent avoidable damage. If the letters escalate toward court, the debt is large, or you have several debts at once, you may need tailored debt advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Debt collection, credit reporting, and court processes vary by the type of bill and where you are in the UK. If you receive formal court paperwork, get independent advice promptly.
