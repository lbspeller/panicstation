# What to do if… someone is repeatedly trying to get residents to open the main entrance (UK)

## Short answer
Do not let them in. Get yourself behind a secure door, then alert the concierge, building manager, landlord, managing agent, or housing association; call 999 if someone may be in immediate danger or a crime may be happening now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t buzz them in to be polite, even if they say they live there, are visiting someone, have lost a key, or are delivering something.
- Don’t go down to challenge them face-to-face or try to physically block them.
- Don’t follow them if they walk away or try to keep them there.
- Don’t hold the door open for anyone you do not recognise in a controlled-entry building.
- Don’t share entry codes, fobs, routines, or details about when someone else might open the door.
- Don’t put yourself back in the lobby to check the door, take a photo, or prove what happened.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause and secure your own door.** If you are in a hallway, lobby, car park, or entrance area, go back into your flat or another secure place and lock the door before doing anything else.
2. **Do not grant entry.** If they buzz your flat, do not open the main door unless you can confirm who they are. If you speak, keep it brief: “I can’t let you in. Please contact the person you’re visiting or building management.”
3. **Alert the on-site or building contact now.** Call or message the concierge, security desk, building manager, managing agent, landlord, or housing association. Say the person is repeatedly trying to get residents to open the main entrance.
4. **Call police if the situation feels urgent.** Call 999 if someone is trying to force entry, threatening anyone, has got inside and you feel at risk, or a crime may be happening now. If it is not an emergency, use 101 or the police online reporting route for your area.
5. **Write down only what you can safely remember.** Note the time, entrance, what they said, which flats or buzzers they tried if you saw this safely, and a basic description such as clothing and direction of travel.
6. **Report any entry-system fault without checking it yourself.** If you already noticed the door not latching, being propped open, a broken lock, or the intercom failing, tell management. Mention any CCTV so they can preserve or check it if appropriate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a formal complaint, ask for upgrades, or speak to neighbours in detail.
- You do not need to investigate who they are, search social media, take photographs, or gather proof yourself.
- Building-wide fixes such as door closers, fob controls, access reminders, or repairs can be dealt with after the immediate situation is stable.

## Important reassurance
It can feel awkward not to help someone at the door. In a controlled-entry building, refusing entry to an unknown person is a normal safety step that protects residents, visitors, and staff.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment you notice repeated attempts to get residents to open the main entrance. If it becomes a pattern, later decisions about repairs, security measures, complaints, or police follow-up may need help from the building manager, landlord, managing agent, housing association, police, or another relevant specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. If you think someone is in immediate danger or a crime is happening now, call 999.
