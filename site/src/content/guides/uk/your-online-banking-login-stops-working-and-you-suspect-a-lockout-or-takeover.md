# What to do if… your online banking login stops working and you suspect a lockout or takeover (UK)

## Short answer
Stop attempting to log in immediately and call your bank’s 24/7 fraud department using the number on the back of your physical card. If you cannot find your card, use the official "159" hotline from a safe phone to be connected to your bank's security team.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep trying to log in or reset your password if you suspect a takeover (this may alert the attacker or further lock the account).
- Do not click links in any text messages or emails that claim to be "security alerts" about the lockout.
- Do not call phone numbers provided in suspicious emails, texts, or by an unexpected caller.
- Do not share your Full PIN, card reader codes, or One-Time Passcodes (OTPs) with anyone, even if they claim to be from the bank.
- Do not move money to a "safe account" or "security account" at the request of a caller.
- Do not install remote-access software (like AnyDesk or TeamViewer) to "fix" the issue.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact symptoms of the failure.** Note if your password was rejected, if the app says "account locked," or if you have lost mobile phone signal entirely (which may indicate a SIM swap).
2. **Contact your bank’s fraud department using a verified route.** 
   - Call the **number on the back of your bank card** (most are 24/7).
   - If you do not have your card, **dial 159** (the Stop Scams UK hotline) to be securely routed to your bank.
   - If you are calling back after a suspicious call, **wait at least 15 seconds** after hanging up or use a different phone to ensure the line is clear.
3. **Request an immediate security freeze.** Tell the agent: **"I suspect my account is being taken over and I cannot log in. Please place a temporary block on all outbound payments and digital banking access immediately."**
4. **Review recent account activity with the agent.** 
   - Ask them to check for **new payees**, **pending transfers**, or **changes to your contact details** (phone number or email).
   - If unrecognised changes are found, ask for a **case reference number** for your report.
5. **Secure your primary email account.** If an attacker has access to your email, they can reset your bank passwords. 
   - **Change your email password** immediately using a different device if possible.
   - **Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)** on your email if it is not already active.
6. **Address mobile signal loss.** If your phone has suddenly lost all service and you suspect a SIM swap, **contact your mobile network provider** immediately to report the fraud and secure your SIM.
7. **Report the incident to the national authorities.**
   - For users in **England, Wales, or Northern Ireland**: Report to **Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.
   - For users in **Scotland**: Report to **Police Scotland** by calling **101**.
8. **Consider protective registration.** Visit **cifas.org.uk** to apply for **Protective Registration**. This places a flag on your credit file that requires lenders to carry out extra checks when your details are used to apply for products.

## What can wait
- Finding out exactly how the compromise happened (focus on the lockdown first).
- Updating all other non-financial passwords (prioritize bank and email only).
- Filing a formal complaint about bank service or app reliability.
- Deciding whether to switch banks or close the account permanently.

## Important reassurance
Digital lockouts and security freezes are standard procedures designed to stop theft before it happens. Most banks have dedicated teams available 24/7 specifically for these moments. Taking the step to freeze the account through a verified number is the most effective way to prevent financial loss, regardless of the cause of the lockout.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a suspected digital banking compromise. It does not cover long-term identity restoration or the legal process for reclaiming funds if they have already been moved.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Banking security protocols vary by institution; always prioritize the specific instructions given by your bank's official fraud team once you have reached them through a trusted channel.
