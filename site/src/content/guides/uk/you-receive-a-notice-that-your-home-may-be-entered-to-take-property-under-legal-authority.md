# What to do if… you receive a notice that your home may be entered to take property under legal authority (UK)

## Short answer
Keep your doors locked and verify the notice immediately through independent official channels before allowing anyone entry. In most UK civil debt cases, you are not legally required to open the door to an enforcement agent.

## Do not do these things
- Don't open the door or invite the person inside while you are still verifying their identity.
- Don't leave doors unlocked, as enforcement agents may legally enter through any unlocked door.
- Don't sign a "controlled goods agreement" if you do not understand the terms or feel pressured.
- Don't hide or move goods after receiving a notice, as this can lead to further legal penalties.
- Don't physically obstruct or argue with an enforcement agent; if you feel unsafe, call 999.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact name and reference number on the notice.**  
   - Look for terms like "Notice of Enforcement", "Writ of Control", or "Warrant of Control".
   - Locate the court name, creditor name, and the specific reference number for the debt.

2. **Verify the enforcement agent’s credentials through independent registers.**  
   - **For a standard bailiff:** Search the Ministry of Justice "Certificated Bailiff Register".
   - **For a High Court Enforcement Officer (HCEO):** Check the "High Court Enforcement Officers Association" (HCEOA) directory.
   - **For a County Court bailiff:** Call the specific court named on the paperwork using a number from the official GOV.UK court finder.

3. **Check the "7 clear days" notice period is valid.**  
   - **Confirm that the notice was received at least 7 clear days (excluding Sundays and bank holidays) before any scheduled visit.**
   - **Note that if this notice period has not passed, the visit may be premature and can be challenged.**

4. **Communicate only through a closed door or window if someone arrives.**  
   - **Ask the agent to pass their ID and the warrant or writ through the letterbox.**
   - **Check that the address and name on the paperwork are exactly correct.**
   - **Inform the agent that you are currently verifying the debt and seeking advice.**

5. **Disclose any "vulnerability" immediately to the agent and the creditor.**  
   - **State clearly if there are children under 16, elderly persons, or people with disabilities or mental health conditions in the home.**
   - **Ensure the agent knows if the only person present is considered vulnerable, as they are generally prohibited from entering in these circumstances.**

6. **Gather basic evidence for exempt items if you have them ready.**  
   - **Identify items that cannot be taken, such as "tools of the trade" (equipment needed for work/study) up to a value of £1,350.**
   - **Locate receipts or ownership proof for items belonging to someone else who does not live there.**

7. **Contact a free debt advice service the same day.**  
   - **Call National Debtline, StepChange, or Citizens Advice to discuss stopping the visit or applying for "Breathing Space" (a 60-day pause on enforcement).**

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle the full debt immediately on the doorstep.
- You do not need to prove the ownership of every item in your house during the first interaction.
- You do not need to negotiate a long-term repayment plan until you have verified the notice is genuine.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an enforcement notice is a high-pressure situation, and feeling startled or anxious is a normal reaction. These notices are standardized legal documents; receiving one does not mean your belongings will be taken today. You have the right to a pause while you verify the facts and seek professional advice.

## Scope note
This guide covers the first-step stabilization and verification process for property entry notices in the UK. It does not cover the long-term management of debt, court applications to set aside judgments, or the specific laws of Scotland or Northern Ireland.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. Enforcement powers vary significantly depending on the type of debt (e.g., Magistrates' Court fines and HMRC debts have different entry rules). Always prioritize personal safety.
