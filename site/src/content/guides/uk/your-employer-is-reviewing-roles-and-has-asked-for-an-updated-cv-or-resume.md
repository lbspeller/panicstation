# What to do if… your employer is reviewing roles and has asked for an updated CV or resume (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and ask, in writing, what the review is, what decisions are being considered, and what your CV will be used for. Do not resign, sign anything, or agree to a final change today.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign “to get ahead of it” or accept pressure to leave quickly.
- Do not sign anything on the spot, including a new contract, resignation letter, voluntary redundancy form, or settlement agreement.
- Do not assume it is definitely redundancy, but do treat it as a formal risk and keep a clear record.
- Do not send a rushed CV with guesses, exaggerations, or dates you are not sure about.
- Do not vent in company-wide Slack, Teams, email, or public channels in a way that could be framed as misconduct.
- Do not delete messages, wipe your work device, or remove employer documents out of panic.

## What to do now
1. **Reply calmly and ask for the basics in writing.** Send a short email to the person who asked, copying HR if appropriate:
   - What is happening: a role review, reorganisation, redundancy consultation, redeployment exercise, performance process, or something else?
   - What is the timeline for decisions and next meetings?
   - What will the updated CV be used for: internal matching, selection, redeployment, client work, or another purpose?

2. **Ask whether you are personally at risk.** Use plain wording such as:
   - “Am I currently considered at risk of redundancy?”
   - “Is my current role proposed to change, be removed, or be mapped to a different role?”
   - “Will I receive a written role profile or proposal before I am asked to respond?”

3. **If selection could be involved, ask about the pool and criteria.** Ask:
   - “What is the proposed selection pool?”
   - “What criteria will be used?”
   - “How will the criteria be scored and checked for consistency?”
   - “Will I be able to see and comment on any scoring about me?”

4. **Create your own record of what has happened.** Write down the date, who spoke to you, what was said, and any deadlines mentioned. If there has already been a verbal conversation, send a short email saying “To confirm my understanding…” and list the key points.

5. **Update your CV carefully against your real work.** Ask for your current job description and any proposed new role profile. Keep the CV factual, include the work you actually do, and avoid claims you could not evidence if asked.

6. **Ask what alternatives are being considered.** If redundancy or role removal may be possible, ask whether the employer is looking at ways to avoid or reduce redundancies, including redeployment, suitable alternative vacancies, retraining, different teams, reduced hours, or other practical options.

7. **If you are pregnant, on relevant family leave, or recently returned from it, flag this in writing if relevant.** In UK redundancy situations, some employees with pregnancy or family-leave redundancy protection must be offered any suitable alternative vacancy as a priority if one is available. You do not need to argue the law in the moment; just make sure HR has the correct information and records it.

8. **If a settlement agreement is mentioned, slow it down.** Say that you need a copy to review and that you will take independent advice before responding. In the UK, a settlement agreement must meet legal conditions, including advice from a relevant independent adviser, before it can validly settle employment claims.

9. **If you later need records, consider a subject access request carefully.** A subject access request can be used to ask for copies of your personal information, such as some notes or records about you. It can affect the tone of the situation, so consider advice before sending one if the process is already tense.

10. **Keep working normally while you gather facts.** If you are asked to do something unusual, such as a handover, knowledge transfer, returning equipment early, or training someone to do your tasks, ask why and get the reason confirmed in writing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you would accept voluntary redundancy, a different role, or a settlement.
- You do not need to threaten legal action to ask reasonable process questions.
- You do not need to perfect your CV in one sitting; accuracy matters more than polish right now.
- You do not need to disclose personal plans beyond what is needed to explain availability, request adjustments, or confirm protected leave.

## Important reassurance
Being asked for an updated CV during a role review can happen in restructures, redeployment exercises, and role-mapping work. It can be a sign of risk, but it is not proof that you have already been selected or singled out. Your immediate aim is to slow the conversation down, get the purpose in writing, and protect your options.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and avoid irreversible mistakes. If you are formally told you are at risk, given notice, offered a new role, or asked to sign a settlement agreement, later decisions may need specialist help based on your contract, length of service, and the employer’s process.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Workplace situations vary, and small details can change what applies. If you think you are being treated unfairly or pressured to sign or leave quickly, consider getting independent advice before agreeing to anything.
