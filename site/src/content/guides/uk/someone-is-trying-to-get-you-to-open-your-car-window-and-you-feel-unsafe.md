# What to do if… someone is trying to get you to open your car window and you feel unsafe (UK)

## Short answer
Keep your doors locked, keep the windows up, and do not get out. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999 and, if you can move safely and legally, go to a busy, well-lit public place.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t open the window “just a crack” or unlock the door to talk.
- Don’t get out of the car to explain, argue, or “sort it out”.
- Don’t hand anything out through a gap, including your phone, keys, wallet, or bank card.
- Don’t let a stranger direct you to a quieter place “to talk”.
- Don’t make risky driving moves, such as mounting kerbs, running lights, or sudden swerves.
- Don’t hold and use your phone while driving unless you need to call 999 or 112 in an emergency and it is unsafe or impractical to stop.

## What to do now
1. **Lock down the car.** Keep the doors locked, windows up, and your seatbelt on. Stay in the driver’s seat.
2. **Create space if you safely can.** If traffic allows, leave or create enough room in front of you to pull away without hitting anything.
3. **Move towards people and light.** If it is safe and legal to move, drive steadily to a busy, well-lit place such as a petrol station, supermarket forecourt, or staffed car park. If you cannot move safely, stay inside with the car secured.
4. **Call police when you can do it safely.**
   - Call 999 if you feel threatened, the person is trying the handles, blocking you in, or a crime may be happening now.
   - If you cannot speak, call 999 and follow the operator’s prompts. On a mobile, pressing 55 when prompted can transfer the call to police; this only works on mobiles and does not let police track your location.
   - If you are already safe and it is not urgent, call 101 or report online.
5. **Attract attention without opening the car.** If the situation escalates, use the horn and flash your lights briefly to draw witnesses.
6. **If you respond, keep it short and through the glass.** Say, “I can’t open the window. I’m calling the police.” Then stop engaging.
7. **If they claim there is an emergency, call for help for them.** Stay locked in the car and offer to call 999, 112, or another appropriate service on their behalf. Do not unlock the door to assist.
8. **If they claim to be police, especially in plain clothes or an unmarked vehicle:**
   - Keep the doors locked and windows up.
   - If you are unsure and can safely call, call 999 and explain that you are concerned the stop may not be genuine.
   - If you need to move before stopping, drive steadily to the nearest busy public place, such as a late-open petrol station or police station. Do not speed away.
   - You can ask to see identification through the closed window once you are stopped somewhere safer.
9. **If this follows a bump or road incident:** if you feel unsafe, stay in the car and call police. If you can safely move out of danger, drive to a nearby public place and tell police where you are. Only note the other vehicle’s registration, colour, make, or direction if you can do it without lowering the window, leaving the car, or delaying your own safety.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to work out their motive right now.
- You don’t need to debate, apologise, or prove that you are “not overreacting”.
- You don’t need to call your insurer, post online, or write a full account in the moment.
- You don’t need to decide whether to make a full report until you are somewhere safer.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to prioritise your safety over politeness. Staying locked in your car, moving towards people if you can, and calling for help are normal responses when you feel unsafe.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the next few minutes. Later decisions, including reports, insurance, complaints, or legal questions, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, driving, insurance, or professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, prioritise getting to a safer public location without taking driving risks and contact emergency services.
