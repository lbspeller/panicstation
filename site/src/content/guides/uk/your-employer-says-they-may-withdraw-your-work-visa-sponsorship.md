# What to do if… your employer says they may withdraw your work visa sponsorship (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as time-sensitive: get the facts in writing from your employer and get regulated immigration advice quickly. Make sure your UKVI account contact details are up to date, and avoid rushed work, travel, resignation, or settlement decisions until you know your exact position.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign, sign a settlement agreement, or accept redundancy terms on the spot if sponsorship is part of the situation.
- Do not assume you have “60 days” in every case, or that any time period starts from when you first heard a rumour.
- Do not keep working informally after your employer says your sponsored employment has ended unless regulated advice or an official right-to-work check confirms you may.
- Do not agree informally to a changed role, hours, work location, or different employer without checking whether your visa must be updated first.
- Do not rely only on verbal reassurance such as “we will not report it yet”; ask for dates and confirmation in writing.
- Do not leave the UK until you understand how sponsorship withdrawal, cancellation, curtailment, or a pending application could affect re-entry and your options.
- Do not ignore post or email from the Home Office or UKVI, even if opening it feels overwhelming.

## What to do now
1. **Get the key facts in writing today.** Email HR and, if there is one, the employer’s immigration contact. Ask:
   - Are they ending your employment, considering ending it, or only reviewing sponsorship?
   - Are they withdrawing sponsorship or reporting a sponsorship change, and when?
   - What is your final working day, if one has been decided?
   - Has any change already been reported through the sponsor system?
   - If they mention performance, conduct, restructuring, redundancy, or absence, what process are they following?

2. **Secure your core documents now.** Save copies in a folder you can access outside your work account:
   - Passport ID page, any BRP or expired BRP you still use for sign-in, eVisa details, and visa decision emails or letters
   - Certificate of Sponsorship reference number and any sponsorship emails
   - Employment contract, job description, payslips, rota, timesheets, HR letters, and notice letters
   - Any messages about sponsorship withdrawal, changed role, changed hours, changed work location, redundancy, dismissal, or unpaid leave

3. **Check your online immigration status.**
   - Sign in to view your eVisa or immigration status.
   - Get a share code if the service lets you do so.
   - Save the share code, expiry date, and the status wording you see for your own records.
   - If you cannot access your account, start account recovery straight away.

4. **Make sure UKVI can contact you.**
   - Check that your UKVI account has your current email address, mobile number, home address, postal address, and passport or travel document details.
   - Update those details through the official GOV.UK eVisa account guidance where needed.
   - Do not rely on an old work email address if you might soon lose access to it.

5. **Ask work for a safe pause while facts are clarified.** You can ask HR for:
   - Written confirmation that you remain employed while they clarify the position
   - Paid leave, garden leave, or another temporary arrangement if work is unsafe or unclear
   - One named contact for sponsorship and employment communications
   - Written confirmation before any report is made or any final employment date is set

6. **Get regulated immigration advice quickly.** Contact a UK immigration solicitor or a registered immigration adviser. Tell them your route, visa expiry date, Certificate of Sponsorship details, employer name, final working date if any, and exactly what the employer has said in writing.

7. **Contact your union or employment adviser if you have one.** Sponsorship, dismissal, redundancy, settlement agreements, and notice can interact, so keep immigration and employment advice coordinated.

8. **If you receive a Home Office or UKVI notice, deal with the document first.**
   - Photograph or scan every page.
   - Note the date on the notice and the date you received it.
   - Keep the envelope if it arrived by post.
   - Send it to your regulated adviser promptly and ask what deadline, if any, is stated.

9. **Keep the situation stable while you clarify.**
   - Do not change job, employer, role, hours, or location informally.
   - Avoid travel unless a regulated adviser has checked the immigration risk.
   - Keep a simple timeline of events with dates, names, and copies of messages.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today which future visa route, employer, or long-term plan is best.
- You do not need to explain the situation to colleagues, friends, or social media.
- You do not need to start a grievance, tribunal claim, or negotiation in the first hours unless a clear written deadline is already close.
- You do not need to pack, move, book flights, or make housing decisions until you have written facts and advice.
- You do not need to answer pressure from work immediately if you have asked for time to get advice.

## Important reassurance
This is frightening, but a rumour or first email is not the same as a final immigration decision. The useful first steps are narrow: get written facts, save documents, make sure UKVI can contact you, and speak to a regulated adviser before making irreversible decisions.

## Scope note
This guide covers first steps only. Later decisions about a new sponsor, changing job, a new application, travel, dismissal, redundancy, settlement, or challenging what happened are case-specific and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, employment, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Immigration and employment deadlines can be strict, so get regulated UK immigration advice promptly if you are unsure about your right to work or stay.
