# What to do if… you receive a notice to attend a meeting about a complaint involving your child (UK)
## Short answer
Do not ignore the notice, but do not rush into a detailed account while shocked. Ask in writing what the meeting is, who is running it, whether police are involved, and whether you or your child are being interviewed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice or miss the meeting without contacting the organiser; decisions may be made without your account.
- Do not call, message, confront, or post about the person you think complained.
- Do not ask your child to explain, rehearse, delete messages, or change their account.
- Do not give detailed answers to police, or to anyone saying “interview” or “under caution”, before asking about free legal advice.
- Do not send a long emotional email, group chat message, or social media post about the complaint.
- Do not bring several people to the meeting or anyone likely to argue; ask first what support person is allowed.

## What to do now
1. **Read the notice once and identify the organiser.**  
   Write down the name, role, organisation, phone number, email address, meeting date, meeting place, and any words such as “child protection conference”, “safeguarding”, “strategy meeting”, “police”, “voluntary interview”, or “under caution”.

2. **Ask for clarification in writing.**  
   Send a short message asking:
   - What is the formal name of the meeting?
   - Who will attend, by role or organisation?
   - What are the specific concerns or allegations, in brief?
   - Is the meeting about your child, you, another adult, or more than one person?
   - Are you or your child being asked to give an account or be interviewed?
   - Will the meeting be minuted or recorded, and how can you get a copy?
   - What happens if you cannot attend the proposed time?

3. **If police are mentioned, ask for free legal advice before any interview.**  
   If the notice mentions police, a police station, “voluntary interview”, “interview”, “suspect”, or “under caution”, say: “I want legal advice before any interview.” If your child is being interviewed by police, ask who the appropriate adult will be and do not let the interview feel informal just because it was arranged by appointment.

4. **If it sounds like a child protection conference, ask for the reports and the chair’s details.**  
   Ask when you will receive the social work report or written concerns, whether other reports can be shared, who the conference chair is, and how you can correct factual errors before the meeting.

5. **Ask for practical support needs now.**  
   If you need an interpreter, disability adjustment, hearing support, a remote attendance option, or a short delay so a solicitor or supporter can attend, ask for it in writing. Keep the request practical and calm.

6. **Choose one steady support person if allowed.**  
   Ask whether you may bring a solicitor, advocate, friend, or family member. Choose someone who can help you stay calm, take notes, and listen; avoid anyone who may argue or speak over you.

7. **Make a one-page factual pack.**  
   Put together dates, letters, emails, school messages, medical appointment dates, professional names, and any relevant documents. Keep it factual. Do not add accusations, guesses, or long explanations.

8. **Prepare three calm sentences to use if you freeze.**  
   You can say:
   - “I am shocked and I want to understand exactly what process this is.”
   - “I want to cooperate, but I need the concerns in writing before giving a detailed account.”
   - “My priority is my child’s safety and wellbeing.”

9. **If you think your child is in immediate danger, treat that separately from the meeting notice.**  
   Call 999 if there is immediate danger. If the worry is serious but not immediate, contact the local children’s social care team or the NSPCC for urgent safeguarding advice.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out who complained today.
- You do not need to write a full statement tonight.
- You do not need to decide whether anyone is lying.
- You do not need to explain the whole family history in your first reply.
- You do not need to agree to a plan on the spot before you understand it; ask for it in writing and ask for time to take advice.

## Important reassurance
A notice about a complaint involving your child is frightening, but it does not automatically mean your child will be removed or that you have failed as a parent. The safest first move is to slow the situation down, get the process named clearly, and avoid rushed detailed answers.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours or days after receiving the notice. Later decisions may need specialist help from a solicitor, children’s social care adviser, advocate, or other relevant professional, depending on what the meeting actually is.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes, names, and rights can vary within the UK, so ask the organiser to confirm what rules apply to your meeting in writing.
