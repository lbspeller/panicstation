# What to do if… someone you know becomes aggressive during a conversation and you feel unsafe (UK)

## Short answer
Stop trying to resolve the conversation and focus on getting to a safer pause: create space, move towards an exit or other people, and leave if you can. If you think violence is imminent, someone is threatening violence, or you are being prevented from leaving, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to win the argument, prove a point, or correct them in the moment.
- Do not match their volume, insults, sarcasm, or pace.
- Do not touch them, grab them, or try to physically calm them.
- Do not block their exit or corner them.
- Do not follow them into a more private space, such as a bedroom, garage, stairwell, toilet, or car.
- Do not move into a kitchen or another room with obvious weapons or heavy objects if you can avoid it.
- Do not stay to smooth it over if you feel the situation is turning unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Change your goal from talking to leaving safely.**  
   Take one or two steps back if you can. Angle your body towards the door and keep a clear path to an exit.

2. **Use one short line, then stop debating.**  
   Say, “I’m going to take a break now,” “I’m stepping outside,” or “I’m not continuing this while it feels unsafe.” Keep your voice low and do not add a long explanation.

3. **Move towards a safer place.**  
   Head towards a hallway, front step, open doorway, reception area, shop floor, pavement, or somewhere other people can see you. If you are in a workplace or venue, move towards staff, reception, security, or a staffed area.

4. **Create space without making a challenge.**  
   Keep your hands visible, avoid sudden movements, and avoid staring them down. If it helps while you leave, keep a table, sofa, counter, car door, or other solid object between you and them.

5. **Bring in another person early if you can do it safely.**  
   Message or call someone nearby with a short line such as, “Can you come here now?” At work, ask a colleague, manager, receptionist, or security staff member to stay with you or walk with you out.

6. **Call 999 if you feel violence is likely or you cannot leave safely.**  
   Say where you are, what is happening, and that you feel at risk of immediate violence. If you cannot speak safely on a mobile, call 999, listen to the operator, and respond by coughing, tapping, or pressing 55 if prompted. Give your address, room, entrance, or nearby landmark if you safely can; do not rely on a silent call alone.

7. **Once you get away, go somewhere harder to reach.**  
   Choose a place with other people, staff, CCTV, or a lockable door. If you are outside, go to a busy, well-lit place rather than stopping just outside the building.

8. **Use a non-emergency route only once the immediate risk has passed.**  
   If it did not become an emergency but you are worried it could happen again, you can contact police on 101 or use the Police.uk contact route for non-emergency advice or reporting.

9. **If this involves a partner, ex-partner, family member, or someone you live with, use specialist domestic abuse support when it is safe to do so.**  
   Use GOV.UK domestic abuse support information to find the right confidential support route for where you are. If there is immediate risk of harm, call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to end the relationship, report formally, complain at work, or explain everything.
- You do not need to write the perfect message or justify why you left.
- You do not need to confront them later today about their behaviour.
- You can deal with apologies, blame, or what really happened after you are safe and calmer.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to leave a conversation as soon as it feels like it could turn physical. Creating distance is not rude or dramatic; it is a practical way to reduce risk.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes and the first safer pause. Later decisions, especially if this is part of a pattern, may need help from police, domestic abuse support, workplace support, housing support, legal advice, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or think violence is about to happen, call 999.
