# What to do if… you notice increasing redness, pain or swelling around an IV site after an infusion or injection (UK)

## Short answer
Stop any ongoing infusion immediately and contact the clinical team that treated you, or NHS 111, to screen for infection, phlebitis, or extravasation (fluid leaking into tissue). Seek emergency help if the redness is spreading rapidly or you feel systemic symptoms like shivering or high fever.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore symptoms that are worsening; these issues rarely resolve on their own once inflammation starts spreading.
- Do not massage, rub, or apply pressure to the painful or swollen area.
- Do not apply a hot or cold compress unless a clinician has confirmed what substance was infused, as the wrong temperature can worsen some tissue injuries.
- Do not attempt to remove the cannula (IV line) yourself at home unless specifically instructed to do so by a healthcare professional.
- Do not use or flush the line if it is still in place and you notice pain, resistance, or swelling.
- Do not apply creams, oils, or ointments to the puncture site.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact clinical unit that provided the treatment.** Look at your discharge paperwork or appointment letter for a direct number for the ward, day unit, or clinic.
   - If no direct number is listed, call the hospital switchboard and ask for the specific unit (e.g., "Ambulatory Care," "Oncology Day Unit," or the named ward).
2. **Check for emergency "Red Flag" symptoms and call 999 immediately if present:**
   - **Difficulty breathing, chest pain, or swelling of the face, neck, or tongue.**
   - **Sudden collapse, confusion, or feeling faint.**
   - **Severe, escalating pain that does not respond to elevation.**
   - **Numbness, tingling ("pins and needles"), or inability to move your fingers or hand on the affected arm.**
3. **Screen for systemic infection (Sepsis) or severe tissue injury and seek urgent (same-day) medical review via NHS 111 or A&E:**
   - **High temperature (above 38°C) or very low temperature (below 36°C).**
   - **Uncontrollable shivering or "rigors" (even if you do not have a fever).**
   - **Pus, yellow discharge, or a foul smell coming from the site.**
   - **The skin looks blistered, dusky/purple, or feels unusually cold to the touch.**
   - **Red streaks or "lines" tracking up your arm from the site.**
4. **Mark the area of redness to track changes over the next hour.** Use a ballpoint pen to **draw a line around the current edge of the redness** and write the time next to it.
5. **Take a clear photograph of the site.** Ensure there is good lighting and include a common object (like a coin) for scale to show clinicians if the area grows.
6. **Support and elevate the limb.** Keep the arm raised on pillows so it is higher than the level of your heart to help reduce swelling.
7. **If you are a chemotherapy patient, call your 24-hour Oncology Emergency Hotline immediately.** Use the "Red Card" or "Chemo Hotline" number provided by your cancer center; do not wait for a callback from a general GP or 111 if this specific service is available to you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact medical cause (e.g., "phlebitis" vs. "cellulitis") yourself; this requires a physical clinical assessment.
- You do not need to worry about the "bruising" color right now, provided it is not accompanied by spreading redness or heat.
- You do not need to decide on long-term dressings or scar management in the first few hours.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a high level of concern when a medical site becomes painful after treatment. While many IV site reactions are mild "mechanical" irritations that settle with rest, the clinical protocol in the UK is always to verify that an infection or a "leak" (extravasation) is not occurring. Taking these steps is the standard safety procedure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and escalation advice for the immediate hours after noticing a reaction at an IV site. It does not replace a clinical examination or provide a treatment plan for confirmed infections or injuries.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and is not a medical diagnosis. If you feel very unwell, or if symptoms are progressing rapidly while you wait for advice, go to your nearest Accident and Emergency (A&E) department.
