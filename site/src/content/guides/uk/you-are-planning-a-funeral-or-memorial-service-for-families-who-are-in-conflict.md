# What to do if… you are planning a funeral or memorial service for families who are in conflict (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before booking or paying for anything. Ask the funeral director who they can accept instructions from, what proof or written authority they need, and whether one named person can be recorded as the point of contact.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t try to mediate live in group chats, group emails, or in-person arguments.
- Don’t book anything, sign contracts, or pay deposits unless the person giving instructions has clearly authorised it and you can keep a record.
- Don’t promise either family that their preferred date, speakers, music, burial, cremation, seating, photos, or wake plans will happen.
- Don’t share private details, such as cause of death, finances, family history, or old disputes, to justify service decisions.
- Don’t relay insults, defend one side to the other, or let yourself become the messenger for conflict.
- Don’t treat “next of kin” as a simple trump card. Ask the funeral director how they will handle authority and objections in this case.
- Don’t ignore a credible threat of disruption or intimidation at the service.

## What to do now
1. **Find out who the funeral director can take instructions from.**
   - Ask: “Is there a will, and are any executors named?”
   - If there is no will, ask who is applying, or likely to apply, to deal with the estate.
   - If authority is unclear, say: “I cannot make service decisions until the funeral director confirms who they can accept instructions from.”

2. **Ask for one named point of contact.**
   - If there are multiple executors or several close relatives, ask them to nominate one person for day-to-day instructions to the funeral director.
   - Keep the wording practical: “This is to prevent mistakes, not to shut anyone out.”
   - If no one will agree, ask the funeral director whether they need to pause arrangements until authority or instructions are clearer.

3. **Get the boundary recorded by the funeral director, not just by you.**
   - Ask the funeral director: “Please note who you are currently accepting instructions from and where any objection should be sent.”
   - Ask what they will do if a different relative, executor, or other person objects.
   - Do not personally decide whether an objection is valid; pass it to the funeral director and step back from that decision.

4. **Move requests into one controlled channel.**
   - Send one neutral message to both sides if you are helping: “To prevent errors, all requests need to go through [name]. I will not make decisions in side conversations.”
   - Do not respond to repeated argument messages. Reply once with the channel and stop there.
   - Keep copies of key messages, estimates, approvals, and cancellations.

5. **Reduce the service to a small set of decisions.**
   - Ask the funeral director, officiant, celebrant, minister, or venue for a simple service outline.
   - Limit choices to essentials: date and time, burial or cremation arrangements, who speaks, readings, music, arrival, committal, exit, and any wake or gathering.
   - Use closed choices where possible, such as three readings, two music options, or a fixed number of speakers.

6. **Set rules for flashpoint parts of the service.**
   - Ask the officiant or celebrant to confirm that only pre-agreed speakers will speak.
   - Set time limits for tributes.
   - Agree what will happen if someone interrupts, arrives intoxicated, refuses to sit where planned, or tries to speak without agreement.

7. **Offer a split-format option if the authorised person agrees.**
   - Suggest a brief main service and a separate gathering later.
   - Another option is one formal service and two separate family gatherings.
   - Present this as a pressure-reduction option, not as a judgment about either family.

8. **Protect yourself from blame and financial risk.**
   - Keep a simple decision log: what was decided, who decided it, and when.
   - If you are asked to handle money, ask: “Whose account is paying, who is authorising the spend, and who will receive invoices?”
   - Do not put costs on your own card unless you have calmly decided you can afford that risk and you understand who is responsible for repayment.

9. **Treat possible confrontation as a safety issue.**
   - Tell the funeral director, venue, and officiant if there may be disruption.
   - Ask about practical controls, such as staff awareness, ushers, reserved seating, arrival timing, and how they handle disruption.
   - If you personally feel unsafe, step back from the planning role and tell the funeral director you cannot be the contact person.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle historic grievances, decide who was right, or make both families agree on one version of the relationship.
- You do not need to finalise photos, flowers, order of service wording, catering, transport details, or the wake until authority and communication boundaries are clearer.
- You do not need to decide where you will sit, whether you will attend every gathering, or how to respond to every message today.
- You do not need to explain every decision to every relative.

## Important reassurance
Family conflict around funerals can feel personal, but clear boundaries often reduce the pressure quickly. You are allowed to slow the process down enough to avoid mistakes, unclear spending, and avoidable confrontation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the planning, reduce harm, and prevent expensive or irreversible mistakes. Later decisions may need specialist help if authority, payment, burial, cremation, ashes, or access to the body remains disputed.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Funeral authority, estate roles, payment responsibility, and objections can depend on the facts and on where in the UK the arrangements are being made. If authority is unclear or someone objects, slow down, keep things in writing, and ask the funeral director what they need before anything is booked or changed.
