# What to do if… you get a message that includes details about your location that you did not share (UK)

## Short answer
Move to a secure location with other people immediately and do not reply to the message. If you believe the sender is nearby or you are in immediate danger, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don't reply to the sender to ask how they got the information or to "test" them.
- Don't click any links, download files, or view images sent by the unknown person.
- Don't confirm or deny the accuracy of the location details they provided.
- Don't meet the sender or go to a secondary location they suggest.
- Don't pay any money, cryptocurrency, or gift cards if the message includes a threat.
- Don't delete the message thread until you have taken screenshots for evidence.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause point immediately.** **If you are alone, go to a public space like a shop, café, or a trusted neighbour's house.**
   - Ensure all doors and windows are locked if you choose to stay at home.
   - If you must move between locations, stay on a phone call with a trusted person until you arrive.
2. **Contact emergency services if you feel unsafe.** **Call 999 if the message implies the sender can see you, is nearby, or if you feel at risk of immediate harm.**
   - If you are in danger but cannot speak, call 999 and then press 55 when prompted (the "Silent Solution").
   - For non-emergencies where you still feel harassed, call 101 or report via your local police force's website.
3. **Preserve evidence without responding.** **Take screenshots that clearly show the sender's username, phone number, or handle, alongside the timestamp and the specific location details mentioned.**
   - Save the screenshots to a cloud service or send them to a trusted friend so they are not only on one device.
   - Keep the original message thread intact in the app; do not delete it.
4. **Disable all active location sharing.** **Check your device settings to turn off "Find My," "Live Location," or "Emergency Sharing" features in messaging and map apps.**
   - Review app permissions and set location access to "Never" or "Only While Using" for all non-essential apps.
   - Check if you have any Bluetooth tracking tags (like AirTags) registered to your account that you did not authorize.
5. **Secure your primary digital accounts.** **Change your email password first, as this is the "master key" for resetting other accounts.**
   - Use a "Passkey" or a strong, unique password that you have not used elsewhere.
   - Enable two-step verification (2SV) using an authenticator app rather than SMS codes where possible.
   - Check for any new "Forwarding Rules" in your email settings that might be sending your messages to an unknown address.
6. **Report the incident to the appropriate authorities.** **If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report cyber-enabled threats or extortion to "Report Fraud" (formerly Action Fraud) via reportfraud.police.uk.**
   - If you are in Scotland, report the incident to Police Scotland by calling 101 or using their online stalking reporting form.
   - Use the "Report" or "Block" function within the specific app where the message was received.
7. **Brief a trusted contact on the situation.** **Tell one person exactly what happened, where you are now, and what your next steps are.**
   - Ask them to check in with you at a specific time.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact technical method they used to find you right now.
- You do not need to delete your entire social media presence immediately.
- You do not need to notify your employer or school until you have reached a safe location.
- You do not need to decide whether to press charges or take legal action in the first few minutes.

## Important reassurance
Receiving your location details from a stranger is designed to cause a "freeze" or "panic" response. In many cases, these details are gathered from public records, old data breaches, or broad IP address info rather than active physical tracking. Taking these safety steps is a proactive way to regain control and does not mean the sender has ultimate power over you.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions. It is not a replacement for a formal police safety plan or professional cybersecurity audit.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or security advice. Always prioritize your physical safety and follow the directions of emergency services if they are involved.
