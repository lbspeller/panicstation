# What to do if… you feel like strangers are coordinating to follow or watch you in public (UK)

## Short answer
Move to a staffed, public place and involve a third party rather than trying to handle it alone. If you think you are in immediate danger, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront them, accuse them, or try to prove what is happening.
- Don’t go straight home, to your car, or to another private destination if you feel you may be followed.
- Don’t use an isolated shortcut, stairwell, alley, quiet carriage, or empty car park to get away quickly.
- Don’t expose your phone PIN, banking apps, wallet, keys, or address if you suspect distraction or theft.
- Don’t let embarrassment stop you from asking staff, security, or police for help.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a staffed place.**  
   Go into the nearest staffed shop, café, hotel lobby, reception, transport ticket office, or station help point. Stand where staff can see you, such as near a counter or entrance.

2. **Tell staff clearly and briefly.**  
   Say: “I feel unsafe. Two people seem to be coordinating around me. Can I stay here while you call security or police?” If you are at a train, Tube, tram, or station location, ask staff to contact British Transport Police if that seems appropriate.

3. **Choose emergency or non-emergency help.**  
   Call 999 if a crime is happening now, someone is in immediate danger, you are being blocked, you are threatened, or they follow you into the staffed place. If it does not feel like an emergency but you want advice or to report what happened, call 101 or use online reporting through your local police force.

4. **Use a silent 999 call if you cannot speak safely.**  
   Call 999 and stay on the line. If you are on a mobile and cannot talk, listen for the prompt and press 55 when asked so the call is transferred to police. If you do not respond to the prompt, the call could be ended.

5. **Use rail-specific reporting for non-emergency rail concerns.**  
   In an emergency, call 999. For a non-emergency concern on trains or at stations, text British Transport Police on 61016 or call 0800 40 50 40.

6. **Reduce the chance of being isolated while you wait.**  
   Stay in a busy, well-lit place. Keep your bag closed and in front of you. If you call someone you trust, say your current location, such as “I’m inside the shop on this street,” and ask them to stay on the line. Avoid saying your home address or next destination out loud.

7. **Record details only once you have a safe pause.**  
   Without staring or following anyone, note clothing, distinguishing features, direction of travel, and any vehicle details. Write the details down for staff or police. Do not film if it could escalate the situation or make you less safe.

8. **If they leave, let staff or police help you decide what next.**  
   You can still ask staff, security, 101, or transport police whether it is worth making a report. You do not need to decide alone whether it was deliberate.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide right now whether it was real targeting or a misunderstanding.
- You don’t need to confront anyone or gather proof.
- You don’t need to post about it, warn others online, or contact several agencies at once.
- You don’t need to make big decisions about changing your routines today.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to go into high alert when something feels coordinated or off. Moving towards staff, witnesses, and official help is a sensible first safety step, even if the situation later turns out to have another explanation.

## Scope note
These are first steps to help you reach a safer pause in public. If this happens repeatedly, or you think it may be linked to stalking, harassment, theft, or another specific risk, later decisions may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in danger or a crime is in progress, call 999.
