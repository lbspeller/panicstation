# What to do if… you suspect someone is using tracking or monitoring to control you (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a safety issue first: get to a safer pause, then use a device or account they cannot access to contact specialist support. Avoid confronting the person or making sudden device changes that could alert them and increase risk.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront them about tracking, try to “prove it” to them, or threaten to report them while you are still within their reach.
- Do not do a factory reset, mass password changes, delete apps, or turn off location sharing if there is any chance they will notice immediately and retaliate.
- Do not accept “help” from them to check your phone, fix settings, or explain what you have found.
- Do not use a device you think may be monitored to plan where you are going, search for help, or contact support if you have a safer option.
- Do not go alone to a private place to search for a tracker if you feel unsafe, pressured, or watched.
- Do not handle or destroy a suspected tracker unless you need to move away from immediate danger.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If you are with them or feel watched, move to a public place, a trusted person, or somewhere you can make a call without being overheard. If you are in immediate danger, call **999**.
2. **Use safer communications.** Use a friend’s phone, a work device, a library computer, or another device they have never handled. If you must use your own phone, keep messages neutral and avoid searching for safety plans until you can use a safer route.
3. **Contact specialist support without pressure to report.** You can contact:
   - **National Domestic Abuse Helpline: 0808 2000 247** for safety planning, including tech-abuse concerns.
   - **National Stalking Helpline: 0808 802 0300** for stalking or harassment advice.
   - **Rape Crisis England & Wales 24/7 Support Line: 0808 500 2222** or online chat, if you are aged 16+ in England or Wales and want sexual violence support.
4. **If reporting feels right, choose the safest route.** If it is not an emergency, you can call **101** or contact your local police force online. You can also speak to a specialist service first and decide later.
5. **Check the most dangerous location leaks only from a safe device or account.** If it is safe, look for live location sharing through Apple or Google accounts, social apps, shared calendars, family accounts, car apps, fitness trackers, or messaging apps. If you find sharing switched on, do not rush to switch it off if that could alert them; note what you found and decide with support how to change it safely.
6. **Create one clean contact route if you can do it safely.** On a device they have never handled, you may set up a new email address, passcode, or support-service contact route. Do not log into it from a device, browser, or account they may monitor.
7. **If you suspect a physical tracker, get help to check safely.** Ask a trusted person, a specialist service, a garage, or police for help if that feels safe. If you find a device, prioritise getting yourself away from risk; you do not need to destroy it or investigate it yourself.
8. **Write down only what helps you stay oriented.** On paper or on a safe device, briefly note dates, times, places, tracker alerts, unexpected appearances, comments about your movements, sexual pressure linked to where you have been, and anything you found in settings. This is for you and any support worker; it does not need to be perfect.
9. **If there has been recent sexual assault or injury, consider medical support.** A sexual assault referral centre, sexual health clinic, GP, NHS 111, or A&E can help with health needs. You do not have to report to police to ask for medical or specialist support.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to report to police, leave the relationship, end contact, or confront them.
- You do not need to solve every device, account, app, or tracker tonight.
- You do not need to gather proof before asking for help.
- You do not need to explain yourself to the person who may be monitoring you.
- You do not need to make long-term plans before you have a safer way to communicate.

## Important reassurance
If someone is using tracking, monitoring, or access to your accounts to sexually control you, your unease makes sense. Feeling confused, hyper-alert, or unsure what is real can happen when someone is using coercion and surveillance. Support should focus on your safety, choices, and pace.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety and reducing harm. More detailed tech checks, reporting routes, medical choices, legal options, and longer-term safety planning may need specialist help because sudden changes can sometimes increase risk.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, technical, financial, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If you want medical care, sexual violence support, stalking advice, or domestic abuse support, specialist services can help you talk through options without pressure to report.
