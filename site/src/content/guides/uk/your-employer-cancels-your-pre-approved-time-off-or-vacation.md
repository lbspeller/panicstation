# What to do if… your employer cancels your pre-approved time off or vacation (UK)

## Short answer
Get the cancellation clearly in writing, check the notice rule in your contract or holiday policy, and reply calmly before deciding what to do next. Do not simply fail to turn up while the dispute is unresolved.

## Do not do these things
- Do not rely on a verbal conversation only; keep a written record.
- Do not simply fail to attend work unless the position has been resolved or you have taken proper advice.
- Do not resign, threaten resignation, or send an angry message in the moment.
- Do not share private personal details unless they are necessary for the request.
- Do not spend more non-refundable money until you know where the leave stands.
- Do not argue about staffing generally; keep the issue to your approved dates, the notice given, and what happens next.

## What to do now
1. **Save proof of the original approval and the cancellation.** Keep screenshots, emails, calendar entries, rota records, app notifications, or messages showing the dates approved, who approved them, when they were approved, and when you were told the leave could not be taken.

2. **Confirm what type of time off this is.** If it is annual leave or holiday, your employer can usually refuse or cancel particular dates if they give the required notice and follow any contract or policy rules. If it is sickness absence, maternity leave, paternity leave, adoption leave, parental bereavement leave, or another statutory leave type, do not treat it as an ordinary holiday dispute; check the specific workplace process or ask HR.

3. **Check the notice rule before replying.** Look at your contract, staff handbook, holiday policy, rota policy, or any workplace agreement. If there is no different rule, GOV.UK says an employer cancelling leave must give notice equal to the amount of leave requested, plus 1 day. Acas describes the cancellation notice as at least the same amount of time as the holiday requested. If you are unsure, use the longer GOV.UK wording for your first check.

4. **Count the dates simply.** Write down:
   - the first day your approved leave was due to start;
   - how many calendar days or working days of leave are being cancelled, matching how your employer records holiday;
   - the date and time you were told it was cancelled;
   - whether the notice seems to meet the rule your employer is relying on.

5. **Reply once, calmly, in writing.** Keep it short:
   - “Please confirm in writing that my approved annual leave from [date] to [date] is being cancelled, and the reason.”
   - “Please confirm the contract, policy, or default notice rule being relied on.”
   - “The notice appears short because [brief date count]. Can the original dates still be honoured, or can we agree a limited cover plan such as [specific task] while preserving the rest of the leave?”

6. **If you will lose money, say so early and neutrally.** Write: “I booked non-refundable costs after the leave was approved. Is there any reimbursement, exception, or date-adjustment process that could avoid the loss?” Keep receipts and booking confirmations, but do not assume reimbursement is guaranteed.

7. **Ask for alternative dates in writing.** If the employer will not honour the original dates, ask them to propose replacement dates and confirm how you will still be able to take your holiday entitlement within the leave year.

8. **Escalate through the workplace route if your manager will not engage.** Contact HR, a more senior manager, or your union representative if you have one. Say you need urgent confirmation of the cancellation notice basis, the affected dates, and the proposed solution.

9. **Start a short timeline if the situation feels targeted or unusual.** Record dates, times, who said what, witnesses, and copies of messages. If you think the cancellation may be linked to pregnancy, disability, whistleblowing, union activity, or another protected concern, keep the timeline factual and get specialist advice before making a formal allegation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to raise a formal grievance.
- You do not need to write a long argument about fairness; first get the written cancellation and the notice rule.
- You do not need to solve the staffing problem for your employer.
- You do not need to decide yet whether to seek external advice, unless the leave starts very soon or you are being told to choose immediately.

## Important reassurance
Having approved leave pulled back can feel very destabilising, especially if you have already made plans or paid money. A short written record, a clear notice check, and one practical written reply can protect your position without escalating the situation unnecessarily.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on your contract, workplace policy, the reason for the cancellation, the timing, your employment status, and whether any protected issue is involved.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. For tailored help, speak to HR, your union, Acas, or a qualified employment adviser.
