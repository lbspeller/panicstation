# What to do if… your rescue inhaler is not relieving symptoms the way it usually does (UK)

## Short answer
Sit upright, use your reliever inhaler according to your personal action plan (or take 1 puff every 30–60 seconds up to 10 puffs), and call **999** immediately if symptoms do not improve quickly or you are too breathless to speak.

## Do not do these things
- Do not lie down or curl up; staying upright keeps your airways as open as possible.
- Do not "wait and see" for more than a few minutes if your breathing is significantly restricted.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital; paramedics can provide life-saving treatment during transit.
- Do not exceed the maximum dose stated on your action plan or by the NHS (usually 10 puffs for blue inhalers).
- Do not ignore a "silent chest"; if wheezing stops but breathing remains difficult, this is a medical emergency.

## What to do now
1. **Sit upright and loosen any tight clothing.** Avoid lying down, as this increases the effort required to breathe. 
2. **Follow your personal asthma action plan immediately if you have one.** If you do not have a plan, follow the standard NHS emergency dosing below.
3. **Check your inhaler device for physical issues.** 
   - Ensure the mouthpiece is not blocked by lint or debris.
   - Shake the inhaler (if it is a metered-dose/pMDI type) before use.
   - Check the dose counter; if it is at zero, it may only be delivering propellant without medication.
4. **Use your reliever inhaler using the "emergency" protocol.**
   - **For blue reliever inhalers (Salbutamol):** Take 1 puff every 30–60 seconds for up to 10 puffs. Use a spacer if you have one.
   - **For AIR or MART inhalers (e.g., Symbicort, Fostair):** Take 1 puff every 1–3 minutes until you feel better, up to a maximum of 6 puffs.
5. **Call 999 or 112 for an ambulance immediately if any of the following occur:**
   - You are too breathless to speak in full sentences.
   - Your symptoms are getting worse despite using your inhaler.
   - You do not feel better after taking the maximum number of puffs (10 for blue, 6 for AIR/MART).
   - You do not have an inhaler with you or the one you have is empty.
   - Your lips, tongue, or fingernails look blue, grey, or pale.
6. **Repeat the dosing if help has not arrived.** If symptoms have not improved after 10 minutes, you may take the same maximum dose again (up to 10 puffs for blue / 6 for AIR/MART) while waiting for the ambulance.
7. **Contact your GP for a same-day urgent review if symptoms improve.** Even if the inhaler eventually works, any time your asthma is "not relieved as usual," it indicates your underlying control needs immediate medical adjustment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify what triggered the symptoms (e.g., pollen, pets, cold air) until you are stable.
- You do not need to clean your spacer or inhaler thoroughly right now; a quick check for blockages is sufficient.
- You do not need to worry about being a "nuisance" to emergency services; breathing difficulties are a priority.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of panic or a racing heart when your breathing is difficult or when you use a high dose of a reliever inhaler. These are known side effects of the medication and the body's natural response to breathlessness; they do not mean the situation is out of control, but they are a signal to follow your safety steps.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute worsening of asthma symptoms. It does not replace a personal asthma action plan or professional medical diagnosis.

## Important note
Call 999 immediately if you are struggling to breathe. If your symptoms are not an emergency but your inhaler is less effective than usual, you must see a GP or asthma nurse within 48 hours to prevent a future severe attack.
