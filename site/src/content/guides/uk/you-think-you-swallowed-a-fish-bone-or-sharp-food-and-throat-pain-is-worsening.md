# What to do if… you think you swallowed a fish bone or sharp food and throat pain is worsening (UK)

## Short answer
Stop eating or drinking immediately and seek an urgent medical assessment at an Accident and Emergency (A&E) department or via NHS 111. Worsening pain or difficulty swallowing after ingesting a sharp object requires physical examination to rule out a lodged object or internal injury.

## Do not do these things
- Do not eat bulky foods like bread, rice, or marshmallows to try and push the object down.
- Do not use your fingers, tweezers, or other tools to try and remove the object.
- Do not try to make yourself vomit.
- Do not take pain-relief medication before being assessed, as this can mask the worsening of symptoms.
- Do not lie flat if you are struggling to swallow or are drooling.
- Do not wait for the pain to go away on its own if it is becoming more intense.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency airway signs.** If you are struggling to breathe, making a whistling noise when breathing (stridor), choking, or are unable to speak, call 999 immediately.
2. **Stop all intake of food and drink.** Avoid putting anything further down your throat, including water, as this can push a sharp object deeper into the tissue or complicate an endoscopic removal if one is needed.
3. **Go to an A&E department immediately if you have high-risk symptoms.** Seek immediate emergency care if you experience any of the following:
   - an inability to swallow your own saliva or sudden drooling
   - severe or rapidly increasing pain in the throat, neck, or chest
   - blood in your spit or vomit
   - a change in your voice, such as sudden hoarseness
   - any swelling or redness in the neck area.
4. **Contact NHS 111 if pain is worsening but you can still swallow fluids.** If you do not have the emergency signs listed above but the sensation of something being stuck is persistent or the pain is increasing, call 111 for a same-day urgent referral to an Ear, Nose, and Throat (ENT) specialist or a minor injuries unit.
5. **Prepare your information for the clinician.** Write down the following details to provide to the medical team:
   - the exact time you swallowed the object
   - what the object was (e.g., a specific type of fish bone or a sharp shard of crackling)
   - exactly where you feel the sensation (e.g., left side, high up, or behind the breastbone)
   - any existing conditions involving your gullet or swallowing.
6. **Arrange safe transport.** If the pain is severe or you feel faint, do not drive yourself to the hospital. Ask someone to drive you or use a taxi.

## What can wait
- You do not need to confirm if the bone is "actually" there before seeking help; a scratch can feel identical to a lodged bone, but only a clinician can differentiate the two.
- You do not need to identify the exact species of fish or the specific meal once you have identified the object was sharp.
- You do not need to decide if you want a "camera down the throat" (endoscopy) now; that is a clinical decision made after examination.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for a sharp object to cause a scratch (mucosal abrasion) and then pass through to the stomach safely. However, because a scratch feels exactly like a lodged bone, and because a lodged bone can cause infection or injury if left, medical staff expect and prefer to see people in this situation. Seeking help is a standard safety precaution.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage guidance only. It is intended to help you navigate the first minutes and hours after a suspected ingestion and does not replace professional clinical diagnosis or treatment.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional medical advice. If you suspect a sharp object is lodged and your symptoms are worsening, you must be assessed by a healthcare professional to prevent potential complications like infection or perforation.
