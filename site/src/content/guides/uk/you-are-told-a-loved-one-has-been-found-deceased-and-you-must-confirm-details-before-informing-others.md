# What to do if… you are told a loved one has been found deceased and you must confirm details before informing others (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and verify the identity of the deceased person and the caller through an official switchboard (101 for police or the hospital main line) before taking any further action.

## Do not do these things
- Do not post any information on social media or in group chats until you have official confirmation.
- Do not assume the caller is who they say they are, even if they have personal details about the deceased person.
- Do not provide your own sensitive data (National Insurance number, bank details, or passport numbers) to an incoming caller.
- Do not rush to drive or travel if you are in shock; wait until a support person can assist you.
- Do not begin a "cascade" of phone calls to relatives until the identity of the deceased has been formally confirmed to you by authorities.

## What to do now
1. **End the incoming call and wait at least five minutes before calling back.** Fraudsters can sometimes "stay on the line" to intercept your next outgoing call; waiting allows the connection to clear completely.
2. **Call the official switchboard of the organisation the caller claimed to represent.** Do not use a direct number provided by the caller; find the number independently.
   - For police, dial **101** and ask for the specific force (e.g., "Metropolitan Police" or "Greater Manchester Police").
   - For hospitals, call the main switchboard number found on the official NHS website and ask for the **Patient Affairs** or **Bereavement Office**.
3. **Request a formal verification of the officer or staff member's identity.** If you are speaking to the police, ask the 101 operator to verify the officer’s name, collar number, and whether they are currently dealing with the incident number you were given.
4. **Confirm the exact details used to identify the deceased person.** Ask the official contact for the specific facts currently held to ensure there is no case of mistaken identity.
   - Ask: "What information was used to identify this person (e.g., photo ID found at the scene, or formal hospital records)?"
   - Ask: "Is this a confirmed identification or an 'initial' identification based on items found?"
5. **Ask for the name and contact details of the Medical Examiner or Coroner’s Officer.** In 2026, most deaths in the UK are reviewed by a Medical Examiner (ME) or reported to a Coroner; these officials are your primary points of contact for factual updates.
6. **Write down the specific reference numbers and names provided.** In shock, memory for numbers and names is often the first thing to fail.
   - Record the **Police Incident Number** (CAD/Log number).
   - Record the **Coroner’s Case Reference** if applicable.
   - Record the **full name and direct extension** of your single point of contact.
7. **Identify one trusted person to support you while you wait for further facts.** Ask this person to come to your location or stay on the phone with you, but instruct them clearly that the information is not for wider sharing yet.

## What can wait
- You do not need to notify the "Tell Us Once" service or any government departments today.
- You do not need to choose a funeral director or make any arrangements for the body immediately.
- You do not need to respond to any inquiries from distant friends or colleagues right now.
- You do not need to look for a will or legal documents until the formal identification process is complete.

## Important reassurance
Taking time to verify the facts is a protective act, not a sign of disbelief or lack of care. It is very common to feel a sense of "unreality" or numbness; these are standard psychological responses to sudden, high-stress news.

## Scope note
This guide is for the immediate minutes and hours following a notification. It focuses on verification and stabilization only.

## Important note
Official processes for death notification and identification vary depending on whether the death was expected (in a hospital/hospice) or sudden (involving the police). Always follow the instructions of the official Coroner’s Officer or Medical Examiner once their identity is verified.
