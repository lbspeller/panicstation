# What to do if… you lose your visa, residence permit, or immigration documents while abroad (UK)

## Short answer
Secure your remaining identity documents first, then use the official UKVI route to prove or recover your UK immigration status. If your passport or travel document is also missing, replace that first because you will usually need a valid travel document before a carrier can board you.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go straight to the airport hoping check-in staff will sort it out.
- Don’t rely on screenshots, scans, or old photos as your only proof if a carrier asks for official evidence.
- Don’t report a card as lost or stolen until you have checked bags, pockets, accommodation safes, reception desks, taxis, and lost property; once reported, a card may not be usable if found later.
- Don’t hand over your only remaining passport, national identity card, or emergency travel document to anyone unofficial.
- Don’t pay “fixers” or social media accounts that claim they can get you a faster UK visa, BRP, or eVisa outside official routes.
- Don’t destroy an expired BRP before checking whether you still need it for UKVI account access or another official task; if GOV.UK or UKVI tells you to destroy it, follow that instruction.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe, calm base and list exactly what is missing.**  
   Write down whether you have lost your passport, visa vignette, expired BRP, residence card, Home Office travel document, phone, email access, or UKVI account sign-in details. Re-check bags, pockets, hotel safes, reception, transport apps, and lost property before making reports that may cancel a document.

2. **Secure the documents and accounts you still have.**  
   Keep your remaining ID with you, not as a deposit. If your phone, wallet, or bag was stolen, protect your email, UKVI account email, banking apps, and mobile number as soon as you can.

3. **Create a small proof pack.**  
   Save offline copies of your passport bio page if you have one, any image of the missing visa or card, your UKVI customer number, application reference numbers, Home Office emails, travel booking, police or lost-property reference, and a short note of when and where you last had the document.

4. **If your passport or national travel document is missing, contact your own embassy, consulate, or passport authority.**  
   Ask how to get a replacement passport or emergency travel document in the country you are in. UKVI or a visa application centre will usually need a valid passport or travel document before you can complete any return-to-UK permission route.

5. **If a BRP is missing, report the lost or stolen BRP through the official GOV.UK route.**  
   BRPs have been replaced by eVisas and cannot be used for travel, but GOV.UK still says a lost or stolen BRP must be reported. Save any confirmation, reference, or Home Office message you receive.

6. **If a UK residence card is missing, use the official GOV.UK residence card reporting route.**  
   Residence cards are being replaced by eVisas, but a lost or stolen card still needs reporting. Keep the police or report reference if one is available.

7. **Try to access your eVisa and UKVI account.**  
   Sign in to your UKVI account, check that your passport or travel document details are current, and confirm that you can view your eVisa. If you have a new passport or emergency travel document, update or add it in the UKVI account if the service allows you to do so.

8. **If you cannot access your eVisa or UKVI account, use the official UKVI help route.**  
   Use GOV.UK’s eVisa and UKVI account help pages to recover access, report an eVisa error, or ask what travel evidence you need. Avoid guessing which application to make if your document type is unclear.

9. **If you lost a passport containing a UK visa vignette or wet ink stamp, replace the passport first.**  
   If the old passport with a valid visa still exists, GOV.UK says you may be able to travel with both the old and new passports. If the passport and vignette are lost, use the official GOV.UK route to transfer or replace the visa evidence if GOV.UK says it applies to your document type.

10. **If you lost a Home Office travel document abroad, follow the specific GOV.UK lost travel document route.**  
   Depending on your status, GOV.UK says you may need a temporary visa or a Returning Resident visa to return to the UK. Do not assume this is the same as replacing a passport issued by your own country.

11. **Contact your airline or carrier before travelling to the airport.**  
   Tell them exactly what you still have, such as a replacement passport, emergency travel document, eVisa access, share code, or UKVI confirmation. Ask what they will need to see for boarding and keep their answer in writing if possible.

12. **If you are sponsored as a student or worker, tell your sponsor contact.**  
   Your university or employer may need to record the loss and may be able to point you to the right UKVI route for your visa type.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, claim compensation, or change future travel plans.
- You do not need to rebuild your whole immigration history right now; focus on identity, official reporting, eVisa or UKVI access, and the minimum evidence needed for travel.
- You do not need to replace every supporting document immediately unless there is a clear identity theft risk.
- You do not need to choose a long-term immigration strategy while you are still trying to get safe, documented, and able to travel.

## Important reassurance
This is stressful because carriers can be strict at check-in, but losing a card or visa evidence does not automatically mean you have lost your UK immigration status. Work through one official route at a time: identity document, report, UKVI account or eVisa access, then travel evidence.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, such as correcting UKVI records, replacing documents after you return, dealing with a sponsor, or making an immigration application, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. What you need can depend on your nationality, the document you lost, your UK immigration status, the country you are in, and carrier checks; use official GOV.UK and UKVI routes or a regulated immigration adviser if you are unsure.
