# What to do if… your airline says your travel documents do not meet the destination entry requirements (UK)

## Short answer
Ask the airline to state the exact rule they think you fail, for the exact destination or transit point, then check that rule against official entry guidance. Before the flight closes, ask how to protect the rest of your booking while the document issue is checked or fixed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep debating without getting the specific reason, country, flight segment, and rule.
- Do not check in hold luggage while the document issue is unresolved, unless the airline tells you it is safe to do so.
- Do not rely on a forum, chatbot, travel agent summary, or old screenshot over official entry rules and the airline’s documented check.
- Do not cancel the whole itinerary in panic unless the airline has explained what will happen to each remaining flight.
- Do not leave the airport desk without asking how the refusal will be recorded and what it means for onward or return legs.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the exact failed rule.** Say: “Which exact destination or transit requirement do you think I do not meet, and where is that rule shown?” Ask whether the issue is passport validity, passport issue date, visa, electronic authorisation, transit permission, name mismatch, onward ticket, residence permit, document condition, or something else.

2. **Ask the airline to re-check the document system.** Ask them to check the itinerary again, including every transit point, your nationality, passport type, residence status if relevant, and the passport number used for any digital approval. Many airline document checks are based on Timatic-style data, but the details entered still matter.

3. **Check authoritative sources quickly.** Use the destination government’s own entry-requirements page first. Use GOV.UK Foreign Travel Advice for that country as a UK traveller cross-check, and use the IATA Travel Centre as another check of passport, visa, transit, and health-document requirements.

4. **Check the common mismatch points in front of the agent.**
   - Passport expiry date and any required validity after arrival or departure.
   - Passport issue date, if the destination applies an issue-date rule.
   - Passport damage, missing pages, water damage, detached cover, or unreadable details.
   - Name order, spelling, hyphens, spaces, and middle names on the ticket and passport.
   - Transit rules, especially if you change airports, pass border control, or collect bags.
   - Electronic visa, eVisa, ETA, ESTA-style approval, or visa waiver linked to the wrong passport number, old passport, wrong nationality, or different name.

5. **Ask for escalation while staying at the desk.** Say: “I believe I meet the requirement. Can a supervisor or document-check team re-verify this before the flight closes?” Show approval emails, reference numbers, residence permits, onward or return bookings, and official guidance on your phone, but keep original documents in your possession.

6. **If a quick fix is possible, do it before the flight closes.** If the issue is a name correction, ask whether the airline can correct it at the airport and what the cut-off is. If the issue is a missing or mismatched electronic permission, apply or correct it only through the official government route if you are eligible, and ask whether the airline can accept the status you have; do not assume pending status will be accepted.

7. **If boarding is still refused, move to damage control.** Ask for the reason in writing, even if it is a short note, boarding refusal printout, app message, or email. Ask what options are available now under your fare, booking conditions, and the airline’s reason for refusal: rebooking, rerouting, refund, or keeping later flights active.

8. **Preserve evidence before anything disappears.** Screenshot your booking, boarding pass or check-in status, app messages, visa or authorisation status, passport details page, and the official rule you relied on. Note the time, airport, desk or gate number, staff name or ID if visible, and what you were told.

9. **If you are abroad and your UK passport is unusable, check the emergency route.** If you are a British national outside the UK and your UK passport is lost, stolen, damaged, full, recently expired, held by HM Passport Office or a foreign embassy, or otherwise unusable for urgent travel, check the official UK Emergency Travel Document process. This is for urgent passport problems abroad, not for fixing a destination visa or entry-permission refusal.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a formal complaint or claim; first protect the booking, route, documents, and evidence.
- You do not need to prove every detail at the airport if the flight is closing; focus on the exact rule, a supervisor check, and the next workable travel option.
- You do not need to write a long complaint from the airport; a short written reason, screenshots, and times are enough for the first moment.
- You do not need to book a completely new journey until you have asked what happens to the current ticket, return legs, baggage, and any connected flights.

## Important reassurance
This situation is often caused by one narrow mismatch: passport dates, transit rules, name format, document condition, or a digital authorisation linked to different details. Getting the airline to name the exact rule usually makes the next step clearer, even if the answer is rebooking rather than boarding that flight.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the airport moment: clarify the rule, check it against authoritative sources, fix only what can be fixed safely, and preserve your booking and evidence. Later decisions, complaints, insurance claims, or passenger-rights arguments may need specialist help and depend on the exact reason for refusal.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, financial, or professional advice. Entry requirements can change and depend on nationality, passport type, residence status, route, transit points, and airline policy. Airlines may refuse boarding if they believe travel documentation is inadequate, and any later refund, rerouting, or compensation position depends on the facts.
