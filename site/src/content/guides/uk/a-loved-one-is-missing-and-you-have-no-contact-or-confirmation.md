# What to do if… a loved one is missing and you have no contact or confirmation (UK)

## Short answer
Report them missing to the police now if you are worried for their safety. Call 999 if they may be in immediate danger, are a young child, or are vulnerable to harm; otherwise call 101 or use the Police.uk online reporting route. You do not need to wait 24 hours.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait “to see if they turn up” if this is out of character, they are vulnerable, or you are seriously worried.
- Don’t assume it is “not serious enough” for police; missing-person reports are assessed for risk.
- Don’t post that they are dead, harmed, or responsible for something without confirmation.
- Don’t share sensitive identifiers publicly, such as date of birth, medical information, home address, or daily routine. Share relevant information with police instead.
- Don’t chase leads alone, go to unsafe locations, or confront someone you suspect.
- Don’t delete messages, call logs, travel details, or location-sharing history that could help establish a timeline.
- Don’t pay anyone who contacts you claiming they can find them or demanding money. Pause and tell police about the contact.

## What to do now
1. **Make the police report now.**
   - Call 999 if you think they may be in immediate danger, are a young child, or are vulnerable to harm.
   - Otherwise call 101 or use the Police.uk online reporting route.
   - Tell police clearly why this is unusual or worrying.
   - Ask for an incident or reference number and write it down.

2. **Get the key facts ready before or while you call.**
   Have: full name, age or date of birth if known, recent photo, height and build, clothing last seen in, last known location and time, usual routes and places, phone number, vehicle details, medical needs, medication, and any risks such as dementia, cognitive impairment, coercive control, domestic abuse, severe distress, or needing medication.

3. **Do a brief, safe check of the last known place.**
   Look for obvious clues such as whether keys, wallet, medication, bags, passport, phone charger, or a note are present or missing. Do not put yourself at risk, delay the police report if you are worried, or disturb things unnecessarily. Leave their room and things alone where possible.

4. **Do the fastest “expected to be here today” checks.**
   Contact the place they were meant to be today, such as work, school, college, a care setting, a booked appointment, or a travel contact. Write down who you spoke to, the time, and the last confirmed sighting or contact.

5. **Preserve information that may help the timeline.**
   Screenshot or save last messages, missed calls, voicemail details, recent “last seen” information, travel confirmations, taxi or delivery notifications, and location-sharing history if you already have access to it. Do not try to break into accounts.

6. **Check close contacts and local hospitals only if it is safe and does not delay police.**
   Ring close family, friends, neighbours, or anyone they were expected to see. You can also try local hospitals if relevant, but keep police updated and do not rely on this instead of making the missing-person report.

7. **Start one simple timeline and one running log.**
   Write down the last confirmed contact, last known location, missed commitments, checks already made, people contacted, possible sightings, and the police reference number.

8. **Choose one person to update police and one person to keep the log.**
   This helps prevent duplicate calls, lost details, and rumours. Give police any new information using the reference number.

9. **Contact Missing People if you need support while police act.**
   Missing People supports families, friends, and carers of missing people in the UK and can help you think through safe next steps.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to go to the media or post widely on social media.
- You do not need to notify everyone at once. Start with police and the quickest checks that could confirm where they were today.
- You do not need to clear their belongings, cancel everything, access their accounts, or make long-term decisions unless police advise it.
- You do not need to work out every possible explanation before asking for help.

## Important reassurance
It is common for your mind to jump to the worst outcome when contact suddenly breaks and there is no confirmation. The most useful thing now is to act early, keep information clear, and let police assess the risk.

## Scope note
This is first-steps guidance for the first hours. Search activity, public appeals, media contact, and longer-term decisions may need police-led advice and specialist support.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or professional advice. If you believe anyone is in immediate danger, call 999.
