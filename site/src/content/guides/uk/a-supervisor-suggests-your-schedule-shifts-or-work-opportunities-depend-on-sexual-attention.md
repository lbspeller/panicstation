# What to do if… a supervisor suggests your schedule, shifts, or work opportunities depend on sexual attention (UK)

## Short answer
End the interaction immediately and move to a public or shared workspace, then create a private record of exactly what was said and preserve any evidence of rota or schedule changes.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to any "private meetings" off-site or after hours to discuss your schedule.
- Do not delete any communication from the supervisor, even if the content is distressing.
- Do not feel pressured to "play along" with suggestive comments to protect your income.
- Do not sign any documents regarding your performance or conduct without independent advice.
- Do not investigate the supervisor's history with other staff yourself.

## What to do now
1. **End the current interaction and move to a shared area.** If the supervisor makes a suggestion, state: "I am going to get back to my tasks now." Physically relocate to a space where other staff or customers are present.
2. **Write a factual account of the interaction immediately.** Record the date, time, location, and the specific words used regarding shifts or opportunities.
   - Include any witnesses who were nearby.
   - Note exactly how you responded.
3. **Preserve all digital and physical evidence immediately.** Save screenshots of messages, emails, or call logs.
   - Take photos of any physical rotas or schedule changes.
   - Store these copies in a personal account or device, not on work-owned systems.
4. **Identify a safe contact for confidential support.** You can call the 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line on **0808 500 2222** for specialist guidance.
5. **Request that all future scheduling communications occur in writing.** If the supervisor attempts to discuss your shifts verbally, tell them: "Please send my shift details via email or the official work system."
6. **Seek specialist advice on your employment rights before taking formal action.** Contact **ACAS** (0300 123 1100) to understand how "protected disclosures" work under 2026 whistleblowing laws.
7. **Identify the safest reporting route within your organisation if you choose to escalate.** Review the staff handbook for a "Safeguarding", "Whistleblowing", or "Grievance" policy.
   - You can request that scheduling be handled by a different manager while a concern is investigated.
   - If you are a member of a trade union, contact your representative today.
8. **Contact the police if you have been physically touched or feel in immediate danger.** Call **999** for emergencies or **101** to report an incident. Reporting is entirely your choice.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign or stay in the role.
- You do not need to have "perfect" evidence before seeking help or advice.
- You do not need to determine the legal category of the behaviour yourself.
- You do not need to confront the supervisor about their conduct right now.

## Important reassurance
What you are describing is a serious breach of UK employment law and a recognised form of sexual harassment. Feeling hesitant, fearful of losing work, or shocked by the supervisor's behaviour is a normal response to workplace coercion. You have the right to work in an environment free from sexual pressure, and there are protections in place to prevent retaliation when you raise these concerns.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute workplace situation. It does not cover long-term legal strategies, tribunal procedures, or therapeutic recovery.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and is not legal advice. Laws regarding workplace harassment and whistleblowing are subject to change. For specific legal protection, consult an employment solicitor or ACAS. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
