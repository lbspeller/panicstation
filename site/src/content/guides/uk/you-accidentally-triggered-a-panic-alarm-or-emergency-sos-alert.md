# What to do if… you accidentally triggered a panic alarm or emergency SOS alert (UK)

## Short answer
If there is a real emergency or you are unsafe, do not cancel the alert; say what is happening. If it was accidental and you are safe, stay on the line or stay reachable, and clearly say: “Accidental activation. I’m safe. No emergency.”

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hang up on a 999 or 112 call just because it was accidental; stay on the line and say you are safe.
- Don’t keep pressing the panic button, SOS button, or alarm keypad to try to cancel it unless you know that is the correct cancel action.
- Don’t give a passcode, password, or safe word to an unexpected caller you cannot verify.
- Don’t leave the address just because you feel embarrassed, unless staying there is unsafe.
- Don’t reset your phone, delete call history, unplug panels, or change settings while the alert may still be live.
- Don’t apologise at length or explain the whole story first; start with the simple fact that it was accidental and you are safe.

## What to do now
1. **Pause for ten seconds and identify what happened.**
   - If your phone called 999 or 112, deal with that first.
   - If a home, workplace, or personal alarm is sounding, use the normal disarm method.
   - If an SOS alert went only to your contacts, stop the sharing or alert in the phone app and message those contacts.

2. **If your phone called 999 or 112 by accident:**
   - Stay on the line.
   - Say clearly: “Accidental call. I’m safe. No emergency.”
   - Answer any questions the operator asks.
   - If you already hung up, keep the phone with you, keep it off silent if you can, and answer any call-back.
   - If you are actually in danger but cannot speak, follow the operator prompts; from a mobile, you may be asked to press 55 or tap when prompted.

3. **If a monitored home, workplace, or panic alarm was triggered:**
   - Disarm or silence the alarm using the normal code, fob, app, or control panel.
   - Check the keypad, panel, or app for the alarm name or zone, such as panic, police, medical, entry door, or motion.
   - Answer the monitoring centre or alarm provider if you recognise the call.
   - If you are not sure the caller is genuine, end the call and call back using a known number from the alarm app, contract paperwork, keypad sticker, workplace procedure, or provider website.
   - Once you are sure you are speaking to the right provider, give the requested password, passcode, or account details if your provider uses them, and ask them to cancel any call-out or escalation.
   - If this happened at work, tell the duty manager, reception, security desk, or named keyholder in one sentence: “False activation. I’m safe. I’m contacting the alarm provider now.”

4. **If a phone SOS alert went to your emergency contacts:**
   - Open the phone’s SOS, emergency sharing, or safety notification and choose the option to stop sharing, cancel, or mark yourself safe if it is shown.
   - Send one clear message to the people who received the alert: “Accidental SOS. I’m safe. Please ignore.”
   - If someone says they already called 999, stay reachable and explain it was accidental if emergency services contact you.

5. **If responders, police, security, or a keyholder arrive or call:**
   - Keep your phone nearby and answer calls.
   - Go to the door or meeting point only if it is safe.
   - Say: “False alarm or accidental activation. I’m safe.”
   - Show ID or account details if they ask and you can do so safely.
   - Let them finish any checks they need to make.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly why it happened right now.
- You do not need to decide who is at fault.
- You do not need to change phone SOS settings, alarm codes, app permissions, staff training, or device placement until the alert is cancelled.
- You do not need to contact neighbours, colleagues, or relatives beyond the people needed to stop the response or reassure alerted contacts.
- You do not need to make a complaint or write a full explanation in the moment.

## Important reassurance
Accidental panic alarms, emergency calls, and phone SOS alerts happen. Clear, brief communication is enough: say it was accidental, say you are safe, and stay reachable while any checks are closed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and reduce unnecessary emergency response. Later decisions about alarm servicing, phone settings, workplace procedure, or repeated false activations may need help from the alarm provider, device maker, employer, landlord, or another relevant specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, technical, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are unsure whether there is a real emergency, treat safety as the priority and tell the emergency operator, alarm provider, or responder what is happening.
