# What to do if… you find a bat in your room after you were asleep and you are unsure about contact (UK)

## Short answer
Seek urgent medical advice from NHS 111 (or NHS 24 in Scotland) today if you cannot rule out physical contact with the bat while you were asleep, and contain the bat if possible without touching it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not handle the bat with bare hands, even if it appears dead or motionless.
- Do not release the bat outdoors if you have already contained it; medical professionals may need it for assessment.
- Do not wait for symptoms to appear before seeking medical help.
- Do not attempt to catch a flying bat using your hands or a towel.
- Do not ignore the situation because there are no visible marks; bat bites can be microscopic.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a separate room and secure the area.** 
   - Ensure all people and pets are out of the room where the bat was found.
   - Close the door to the room.
   - Check the floor before placing a towel at the base of the door to ensure you do not crush the bat if it is grounded near the gap.
2. **Perform a brief visual check for injuries.**
   - Look for any small pinprick marks, scratches, or areas of redness on your skin.
   - **Wash any suspected bite or scratch immediately with soap and running water for at least five minutes.**
3. **Contact NHS 111 for a rabies risk assessment.**
   - **Call 111** from any phone (or **NHS 24** in Scotland).
   - Tell the operator: "I woke up and found a bat in my bedroom; I was asleep and cannot be certain I wasn't bitten or scratched."
   - Follow their instructions regarding attending an Urgent Treatment Centre, GP, or A&E.
4. **Identify the bat's location only if it is safe to do so.**
   - If the bat is stationary on a wall or floor, place a small box or container over it using gloves or a thick cloth to avoid direct contact.
   - Slide a piece of stiff cardboard under the box to secure the bat inside.
   - Keep the container in a quiet, dark place until you receive further instructions from health officials.
5. **Contact the National Bat Helpline for advice on the animal.**
   - **Call 0345 1300 228** (Bat Conservation Trust).
   - Provide factual details: where the bat was found, its current behavior, and whether you have sought medical help.
6. **Take photos of the bat and any marks on your skin.**
   - Use a clear light source and include a common object (like a coin) for scale in the photo.
   - This may help specialists identify the species of bat involved.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the bat species yourself.
- You do not need to clean the room or bedding immediately; wait until you have spoken to a professional.
- You do not need to decide whether to get a vaccine; the medical risk assessment will determine this for you.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel anxious when discovering a bat in a sleeping area. While UK bats can carry rabies-like viruses, the risk of transmission is very low if prompt medical assessment and treatment are sought. Health professionals have established protocols for these specific "unseen contact" scenarios.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for potential bat exposure and containment in the UK. It does not replace clinical diagnosis or long-term pest control advice.

## Important note
This information is based on UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA) and Public Health Scotland protocols for 2026. Rabies is a serious condition that is preventable with prompt post-exposure treatment but is fatal once symptoms appear. Always prioritize the advice given by NHS 111 or your attending clinician.
