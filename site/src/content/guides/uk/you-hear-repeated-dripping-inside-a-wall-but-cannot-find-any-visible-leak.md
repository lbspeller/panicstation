# What to do if… you hear repeated dripping inside a wall but cannot find any visible leak (UK)

## Short answer
Treat repeated dripping inside a wall as a possible hidden leak. If it is safe and you can reach it without forcing anything, turn off your water at the inside stop tap, then keep away from any sockets, switches, lights, boiler, or consumer unit that could be damp.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep running taps to “test” the sound; it may worsen the leak or damage.
- Don’t cut into walls, ceilings, or boxed-in pipework in a panic.
- Don’t ignore it because you cannot see water yet.
- Don’t touch or use sockets, switches, lights, or appliances near the suspect area if moisture might be present.
- Don’t force a stuck stop tap; call a plumber or the responsible repair contact instead.
- Don’t leave the property with the water still on if the dripping continues and you can safely turn it off.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safer first.** Keep people and pets away from the suspect wall, floor, ceiling, sockets, switches, light fittings, boiler, and consumer unit. If there is visible water near electrics, do not touch nearby electrical fittings.
2. **If it is safe, turn off the water at the inside stop tap.** It is often under the kitchen sink, in a utility area, bathroom, airing cupboard, under the stairs, garage, cellar, or near where the supply enters the home. Turn it clockwise slowly. Do not force it.
3. **If electrics may be affected, reduce electrical risk only if you can do it safely.** If the consumer unit is dry, away from the damp area, and you know how to use it, switch off the affected circuit or the main switch. If the consumer unit or your route to it is wet, damaged, sparking, buzzing, or unsafe, stay clear and get urgent professional help.
4. **Listen again after a few minutes.**
   - If the dripping stops, it may be a leak from a pressurised pipe or fitting.
   - If it continues, it may be from a waste pipe, tank, cistern, roof or loft leak, appliance, heating pipe, or water travelling from another flat.
5. **If you have a water meter, do only a brief leak check if safe.** Make sure taps, appliances, toilets, and showers are not using water. If you can safely see the meter, check whether the dial or indicator moves. If it moves when nothing is using water, treat it as a possible active leak and keep the stop tap off.
6. **Limit spread without opening the wall.** Put towels or bowls where water might appear, such as skirting boards, pipe boxing, under radiators, or below the suspect ceiling line. Move valuables, electrical items, documents, and furniture away from the suspect wall or floor edge.
7. **Stay clear of unstable surfaces.** If plasterboard is bulging, a ceiling is sagging, a damp patch is growing, or cracking appears, keep out from underneath and do not poke or cut it.
8. **Contact the right repair route now.**
   - If you rent, report it as an urgent repair to your landlord, agent, council landlord, housing association, or out-of-hours repair line.
   - If you own the home or are responsible for repairs, contact an emergency plumber and say it is a suspected hidden leak inside a wall.
   - If you are in a flat, tell the building manager, freeholder, managing agent, or neighbours above and below, because water can travel between homes.
9. **If the leak may be on the supply pipe, contact your water company as well.** Responsibility and help can vary, but your water company can advise on possible supply-pipe leaks and any support route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now about redecorating, replacing plaster, or making an insurance claim.
- You do not need to trace pipe routes yourself.
- You do not need to open walls or ceilings before a plumber or responsible repair person has assessed the risk.
- You do not need to start drying equipment until the leak is stopped or assessed and the electrics are safe.

## Important reassurance
A hidden dripping sound can be frightening because you cannot see the source. Taking simple first steps now, especially water off where safe, staying away from possible wet electrics, and reporting it urgently, is a sensible way to limit risk while the source is found.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate damage and safety risk. Finding the source, repairing it, checking electrics, drying the area, and deciding who pays may need a plumber, electrician, landlord, managing agent, insurer, water company, or other specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, electrical, plumbing, insurance, structural, or other professional advice. If there is fire, smoke, sparking, a burning smell, serious ceiling movement, structural collapse risk, or immediate danger to people, move away and call 999.
