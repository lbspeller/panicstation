# What to do if… you get a needle-stick injury from a discarded needle in a public place (UK)

## Short answer
Wash the wound immediately under warm running water while gently squeezing to encourage bleeding, cover it with a waterproof dressing, and seek medical assessment via NHS 111 or A&E today.

## Do not do these things
- Do not scrub the wound or use bleach, alcohol, or disinfectants on the broken skin.
- Do not suck the wound or put the affected area in your mouth.
- Do not try to recap, break, or bend the needle.
- Do not delay seeking medical advice even if the wound looks clean or minor.
- Do not assume you need HIV PEP immediately; clinicians rarely recommend it for discarded needles, but a professional assessment is still required.

## What to do now
1. **Perform immediate first aid by washing the area thoroughly.**  
   Hold the wound under warm running water and **gently squeeze it to encourage bleeding**. Use plenty of soap to wash the area, then pat dry and **cover with a waterproof plaster or dressing**.
2. **Note the specific details of the injury and the needle.**  
   Record the **date and time** it happened, where on your body you were hit, and whether the needle went **through clothing or footwear**. Note if you saw **visible blood** on the needle or inside the syringe.
3. **Contact NHS 111 or go to an Urgent Treatment Centre or A&E immediately.**  
   Tell the operator or triage nurse you have had a **"community needlestick injury"** so they can prioritise your assessment. This must happen **the same day** to ensure any necessary vaccinations or treatments are started within the effective window.
4. **Identify your previous vaccination history if possible.**  
   - **Tetanus:** Check if you are up to date with boosters (usually 5 doses total in a lifetime).
   - **Hepatitis B:** Check if you have ever completed a 3-dose course of the Hepatitis B vaccine.
   - **Childhood Vaccines:** For children, confirm if they received the 18-month hexavalent booster (introduced in the 2025/2026 schedule).
5. **Report the discarded needle to the local council for safe removal.**  
   Do not attempt to pick up the needle yourself unless you have a proper sharps container and tongs. **Search for your local council's "street cleaning" or "discarded needles" reporting page** to ensure it is removed from the public area.

## What can wait
- You do not need to bring the needle with you to the hospital; clinicians do not test discarded needles for viruses as results are unreliable.
- You do not need to decide on long-term blood testing schedules today; the clinician will provide a follow-up plan (usually at 6 weeks and 3 months).
- You do not need to search for symptoms of bloodborne viruses; these infections do not show symptoms in the first few hours or days.
- You do not need to report the incident to the police unless you believe the needle was used in a specific assault or crime.

## Important reassurance
It is natural to feel high levels of anxiety after a needlestick injury. While the worry is significant, the actual risk of contracting a serious infection like HIV or Hepatitis C from a needle found in a public place is statistically very low in the UK. Medical professionals take these incidents seriously and will provide the necessary boosters or monitoring to keep you safe.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only. Medical decisions regarding Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP), Hepatitis B immunoglobulin, or specific testing intervals must be made by a qualified clinician.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and does not replace professional medical advice. If you notice signs of a spreading skin infection, such as increasing redness, heat, or red streaks moving away from the wound, seek urgent medical care.
