# What to do if… your stools turn very pale while your urine becomes very dark (UK)

## Short answer
Get urgent NHS advice today. Very pale stools together with very dark urine can happen when bile is not moving or being processed normally, and it is safer to be assessed promptly.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume dehydration alone explains very pale or clay-coloured stools together with very dark urine, especially if it has happened more than once.
- Do not deliberately put off same-day advice if this is new for you or clearly different from your usual.
- Do not drink alcohol to relax or to see if symptoms change.
- Do not start, stop, double-up, or add medicines, supplements, or herbal products to try to fix this yourself. If you take prescribed medicines, do not stop them suddenly unless a clinician tells you to.
- Do not ignore yellowing of the whites of your eyes or skin, fever or chills, worsening tummy pain, confusion, collapse, or repeated vomiting.

## What to do now
1. Do a quick red-flag check:
   - Look at the whites of your eyes and your skin in good light for any yellow tinge.
   - Notice whether you have itching, new upper or right-sided tummy pain, fever or chills, repeated vomiting, unusual drowsiness, confusion, fainting, or you feel rapidly worse.

2. Get urgent NHS advice today:
   - Use your local urgent NHS route: NHS 111 where available, NHS 24 111 in Scotland, NHS 111 Wales where appropriate, or your GP out-of-hours service in Northern Ireland.
   - Say clearly: “My stools have turned very pale and my urine is very dark.”
   - Ask where you should be assessed today, such as an urgent GP appointment, out-of-hours service, urgent treatment centre, or A&E.

3. Use emergency help now if you seem seriously unwell:
   - Call 999 if you have severe or worsening abdominal pain, confusion, collapse or fainting, repeated vomiting, inability to keep fluids down, or you feel very unwell.
   - If symptoms are severe but you are safe to travel, A&E is the right emergency place.
   - If you might be seriously unwell, call 999 rather than driving yourself.

4. If you are pregnant or recently gave birth, contact maternity triage or your maternity unit today:
   - Tell them about the pale stools and dark urine.
   - Also mention itching, yellow eyes or skin, tummy pain, reduced baby movements, or feeling unwell.
   - Use urgent NHS advice if you cannot reach your maternity unit.

5. Keep the details simple for the clinician:
   - Note when it started and whether the pale stools or dark urine happened more than once.
   - Write down any itching, pain, fever, nausea, vomiting, yellowing, weight loss, or recent illness.
   - Have a list of all medicines ready, including prescribed medicines, over-the-counter medicines, paracetamol, antibiotics, hormones, supplements, and any recent dose changes.
   - You can take a photo of the stool or urine colour in normal light if it helps you describe it, but you do not have to.

6. While waiting for advice or assessment:
   - Avoid alcohol.
   - Drink water normally, using small sips if you feel nauseated.
   - Eat lightly if you feel able.
   - If a red-flag symptom appears or you feel worse, re-contact urgent NHS services or use 999 for emergency symptoms.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause today.
- You do not need to compare photos online or keep searching symptoms.
- You do not need to decide about work, travel, diet changes, or long-term treatment right now.
- Focus on getting same-day clinical advice and following the route they give you, unless emergency symptoms develop.

## Important reassurance
This combination can feel frightening because it is unusual. Seeking urgent advice is a protective step, not an overreaction, and it is the safest way to find out what needs to happen next.

## Scope note
This guide covers first steps for sudden very pale stools with very dark urine. Testing, diagnosis, and treatment decisions may need a clinician or specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not a diagnosis, and not medical or professional advice. If you feel very unwell, rapidly worse, confused, faint, or unsafe to travel, use emergency services.
