# What to do if… you are being treated unfairly at work after raising a complaint or reporting wrongdoing (UK)

## Short answer
Slow things down and start a clear paper trail today: write a timeline of what you raised and what changed afterwards, and keep your own notes and non-confidential key documents somewhere secure off work systems. If any disciplinary, dismissal, settlement, or tribunal deadline might be in play, get advice early.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign “to get away from it” without pausing and getting advice; leaving can affect your options.
- Do not sign or agree to settlement terms, new contract terms, warning paperwork, or resignation wording on the spot; ask for time to review.
- Do not delete emails, messages, calendar entries, or notes that may show what happened.
- Do not remove confidential, client, patient, commercially sensitive, or restricted files to prove your case.
- Do not secretly record meetings without checking your workplace policy and getting advice; take notes and confirm key points in writing instead.
- Do not vent publicly or on social media; keep communications factual, limited, and work-related.

## What to do now
1. **Make a fast timeline.** Note the date you raised the grievance or reported wrongdoing, who you told, how you raised it, and what changed afterwards. Include concrete events such as discipline, demotion, hours cut, shift changes, exclusion from meetings, hostile treatment, suspension, or new performance concerns.
2. **Preserve evidence safely.** Save copies of your own letters, emails, meeting invites, grievance documents, outcome letters, rota changes, payslips, and screenshots of messages showing dates and times. If something is only on work systems and you cannot copy it safely, write down where it is: date, sender, subject line, channel, and who may have access.
3. **Keep confidential material out of your evidence bundle for now.** If the proof involves confidential or restricted information, write a neutral note describing what it is and where it can be found instead of forwarding or downloading it.
4. **Put the process in writing.** If you are being performance-managed, investigated, suspended, or disciplined, ask for the concerns, allegations, evidence relied on, and process timetable in writing. After meetings, send a short email recap of what was discussed, what was agreed, and the next dates.
5. **Ask to be accompanied where a formal meeting is involved.** For many formal disciplinary or grievance meetings, workers can be accompanied by a trade union representative, a certified union official, or a fellow worker. Make the request clearly and ask for enough notice, copies of evidence, and any adjustments you need to take part safely and clearly.
6. **Label the issue cautiously for your own notes.** Keep a note of whether your original report may have been whistleblowing, a grievance, a discrimination complaint, or another protected workplace issue. You do not need to argue the legal label at work today; the immediate goal is a clear factual record.
7. **Raise the suspected retaliation promptly and briefly.** Send a short follow-up grievance or addendum to your existing grievance. Say what you raised, what happened afterwards, why you think it may be linked, and what you are asking for now, such as pausing the action while it is reviewed, using a different manager, or giving written reasons and evidence.
8. **Protect tribunal deadlines early.** For most employment tribunal claims, you usually need to notify Acas within the tribunal time limit, commonly 3 months minus 1 day from the act you are complaining about. A grievance, disciplinary, or appeal process does not usually stop that clock.
9. **Treat dismissal as urgent.** If you have been dismissed and think whistleblowing may be part of the reason, interim relief may have a very short deadline: generally within 7 days of the effective date of termination. You might not need to notify Acas for that application, but you should get advice urgently because the route is technical.
10. **Get support before making a pressured decision.** If you have a union, contact your rep and share your timeline. If not, contact Acas for early guidance and consider practical employment advice before signing, resigning, settling, or missing a deadline.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to leave, settle, or bring a tribunal claim.
- You do not need a perfect evidence pack before raising the retaliation concern or getting advice.
- You do not need to confront the person you suspect is retaliating.
- You can postpone wider career planning until the immediate process, documents, and deadlines are under control.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel panicked and second-guess yourself when work becomes difficult after you speak up. A simple timeline and written trail can reduce confusion and help you get clearer advice without relying on memory under stress.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise, document, and protect options. Later decisions may need specialist employment advice, especially if there is a live disciplinary process, dismissal, settlement discussion, or tight time limit.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Rights and deadlines can depend on your employment status, the type of complaint or disclosure, and the specific action taken. If dismissal, suspension, settlement terms, or a tribunal deadline may be in play, get advice as soon as you can.
