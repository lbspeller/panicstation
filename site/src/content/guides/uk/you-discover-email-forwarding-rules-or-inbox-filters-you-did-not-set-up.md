# What to do if… you discover email forwarding rules or inbox filters you did not set up (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the mailbox as compromised. Use a trusted route into the account, remove the forwarding and hiding rules, change the password, then sign out other sessions and turn on 2-step verification.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using the inbox normally “until later”; forwarding rules can let someone intercept password resets.
- Don’t click through links in suspicious emails to change settings; open the provider’s website or official app yourself.
- Don’t reuse an old password or a password used on any other account.
- Don’t assume deleting one rule is enough if it reappears; treat that as possible ongoing access.
- Don’t ignore recovery options such as backup email addresses and phone numbers; these may have been changed.
- Don’t delete suspicious messages or logs before you have checked whether money, work, or other accounts are affected.

## What to do now
1. **Use a clean route into the account.** Use a device you trust if possible, then open your email provider by typing the address or using the official app. Avoid links in emails or messages.

2. **Remove the suspicious forwarding and hiding rules.**
   - Delete forwarding addresses you do not recognise.
   - Check rules or filters that auto-delete, archive, mark as read, or move messages.
   - Pay extra attention to rules involving words such as security, password, bank, payment, invoice, code, or login.

3. **Change the email password to a new unique password.** Do not use a password you have used before. If the provider offers app passwords, security questions, or passkeys, review those settings too and remove anything unfamiliar.

4. **Sign out other sessions and revoke unknown access.** Use the provider’s security settings to sign out of other devices and sessions. Remove unknown devices, connected apps, mail clients, and third-party access.

5. **Turn on 2-step verification and check recovery details.** Confirm the recovery email address and phone number are yours. Remove anything unfamiliar and save backup codes if the provider offers them.

6. **Look for signs of what was targeted.**
   - Check Sent, Deleted, Trash, Archive, and spam folders for messages you did not send or password resets you did not request.
   - Search for recent messages about banks, shopping accounts, benefits, work systems, invoices, or verification codes.
   - Change passwords on linked accounts that used the same password, starting with money, work, and shopping accounts.

7. **If money, banking, or payments may be involved, contact the bank or payment provider now.** Use the number on the back of your card, the official app, or the official website. Do not use contact details from a suspicious email.

8. **If this is a work, school, or organisation account, tell the IT or security team now.** They may need to remove rules server-side, check sign-in logs, revoke tokens, and protect other accounts.

9. **Report what fits your situation.**
   - Forward suspicious emails to report@phishing.gov.uk.
   - If you have lost money or need to report fraud or cyber crime in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud.
   - If you are in Scotland and need to report fraud or cyber crime, contact Police Scotland on 101 or use their non-urgent online reporting route.
   - If someone is in immediate danger or a crime is happening now, call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly how they got in before you secure the mailbox.
- You do not need to message every contact unless you find messages were sent from your account.
- You do not need to do a full device deep clean before you stop forwarding, change the password, and sign out other sessions.
- You do not need to decide every linked-account password at once; start with money, work, shopping, and any account that reused the old password.

## Important reassurance
Finding a forwarding rule you did not create is a serious warning sign, but it is also a clear thing to act on. The immediate aim is to stop the forwarding, cut off access, and prevent password resets or security codes being intercepted.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. After the account is stable, you may still need help from your email provider, your organisation’s IT team, your bank, or a fraud and cyber crime reporting service.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, IT, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you cannot remove the rule, it keeps coming back, you cannot regain access, or fraud is active, use your provider’s official support route and the appropriate UK reporting route.
