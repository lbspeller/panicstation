# What to do if… you are pressured to work off the clock or without pay (UK)

## Short answer
Pause, ask for the overtime request and how the hours will be recorded in writing, and start logging every extra hour today. Do not keep doing extra hours without written clarity on whether they are paid, time off in lieu, or contractually unpaid.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep working after clocking out without making your own clear record of the extra time.
- Do not sign an opt-out, contract change, new hours agreement, or overtime agreement on the spot while you feel pressured.
- Do not rely only on verbal promises such as “we’ll sort it next month”.
- Do not leave real hours off a timesheet to “help the team” or avoid conflict.
- Do not post about the dispute on social media or in group chats with colleagues or managers.
- Do not resign in the heat of the moment unless you are safe and sure you can manage the immediate impact.

## What to do now
1. **Create a calm written record of what is being asked.**  
   Send a short message: “To confirm, you are asking me to work from __ to __ on __. Please confirm how these hours should be recorded and whether they are paid overtime or time off in lieu.” Keep it factual.

2. **Start a private overtime log today.**  
   Record the date, start and finish times, breaks taken, what work you did, who asked, and how they asked. Save rotas, texts, emails, app screenshots, and clock-in records somewhere you control, not only on a work device.

3. **Check your contract, written statement, and any overtime policy.**  
   Look for:
   - your normal working hours and breaks
   - any “reasonable overtime” or “as required” wording
   - whether overtime is paid, unpaid, or given as time off in lieu
   - how overtime must be authorised and recorded

   If the wording is unclear, ask HR, payroll, or your manager to confirm the rule in writing.

4. **Check your payslip against your real hours.**  
   Compare your payslip and work records with your own log. If hours appear to be missing, raise it in writing: “My record shows __ hours worked; my pay appears to reflect __ hours. Please check and correct this.”

5. **Do a quick minimum wage reality check, even if you are salaried.**  
   Overtime does not always have to be paid separately, but your average pay for the total hours you work must not fall below the legal minimum wage rate. If your real hours may push you below that, keep the evidence and consider using the official pay and work rights complaint route.

6. **Use the safest escalation path.**  
   Start with an informal raise, then send a written follow-up summarising what was said and what you are asking for, such as payment, time off in lieu, or correct recording going forward. If it is not resolved, use your employer’s grievance process.

7. **If the pressure is about extreme hours, check the 48-hour average rule.**  
   Even if your contract mentions overtime, you usually cannot be forced to work more than an average of 48 hours a week unless you have signed a written opt-out. If you are being asked to opt out, you can refuse and should keep the refusal in writing. If you already opted out, you can usually cancel it by giving at least 7 days’ notice, or up to 3 months if your agreement says so.

8. **Protect your options if pay is still missing.**  
   Employment tribunal time limits can be strict. For many pay claims, the time limit is 3 months minus 1 day, and you usually need to notify Acas for early conciliation within the relevant time limit. Contact Acas early if you want to keep that option open.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, make a formal claim, or take legal action.
- You do not need a perfect calculation before you start recording hours and asking for written confirmation.
- You do not need to confront your manager in the moment; a calm written follow-up can be enough for now.
- You do not need to agree to a new overtime arrangement before you have read it properly.

## Important reassurance
Being pressured to work unpaid overtime can make the situation feel urgent and personal. Keeping records and asking for written confirmation is a normal, reasonable step when hours, pay, and working-time limits are unclear.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and prevent rushed decisions. Later decisions about grievances, settlement discussions, tribunal claims, or resignation may need specialist employment advice based on your contract and role.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Employment rights can depend on your contract terms, pay type, job role, industry rules, and whether you are a worker or employee. If you feel at risk of retaliation or sudden loss of income, get confidential advice promptly before taking actions that cannot easily be undone.
