# What to do if… you develop sudden ringing in your ear with dizziness or nausea (UK)

## Short answer
Get medical help today. Call 999 now if stroke is possible, if you cannot stay safe because of the dizziness, or if vertigo comes with hearing loss, vision changes, speech trouble, or arm or leg weakness; otherwise ask for an urgent GP appointment or contact NHS 111 today.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive, cycle, climb, or use machinery while dizzy or unsteady.
- Do not wait it out if you notice sudden hearing loss, one-sided hearing change, new weakness or numbness, speech problems, vision changes, a sudden severe headache unlike usual, fainting, or symptoms that are getting worse.
- Do not put drops, oils, cotton buds, fingers, or other objects in your ear unless a clinician has told you to.
- Do not aggressively clean or flush your ear.
- Do not take someone else’s prescription medicine.
- Do not keep walking around if you are unsteady; sit or lie down to reduce the chance of a fall.

## What to do now
1. Get steady first. Sit or lie down with your head supported. Move slowly. If you feel sick, lie on your side and keep a bowl or bag nearby. Ask someone to stay with you if possible.
2. Check for emergency warning signs. Call 999 now if you have face drooping, arm or leg weakness or numbness, speech problems, new confusion, blurred vision or loss of sight, a sudden severe headache unlike usual, dizziness with falling over, fainting, or you feel too unwell to stay safe.
3. Notice whether your hearing has changed. In a quiet room, cover one ear and then the other and compare. If one ear is suddenly muffled, quieter, blocked, or different, say clearly: “I have sudden hearing change with ringing and dizziness.”
4. Choose the NHS route today. If any emergency signs are present, call 999 or go to A&E without driving yourself; ask someone else to take you, or call 999 if you cannot travel safely. If there are no emergency signs, ask for an urgent GP appointment or use NHS 111 today and say: “sudden ringing in my ear with dizziness or nausea,” plus whether you have any hearing change.
5. Write down the key facts before you speak to anyone. Note the exact time it started, which ear is affected, whether hearing changed, whether the room is spinning or you feel lightheaded, whether you have vomited, any headache, fever, neck stiffness, weakness, numbness, speech or vision changes, and any recent head injury, loud noise, flying, diving, cold, or ear pain.
6. While waiting for advice, keep your phone nearby, sip water if you can keep it down, avoid alcohol, keep lights on if you need to move, and ask someone else to arrange transport if you are told to attend in person.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause right now.
- You do not need to try internet manoeuvres, balance exercises, ear cleaning, supplements, or private tests before urgent triage.
- You do not need to decide whether this is wax, infection, migraine, vertigo, Ménière’s disease, or something else.
- You do not need to make long-term decisions about work, driving, hearing tests, or treatment plans during the first urgent step.

## Important reassurance
Ringing with dizziness or nausea can feel frightening and disorientating. The important first step is not to diagnose it yourself; it is to stay safe, avoid falls, and get the right level of help quickly in case the cause needs urgent treatment.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stabilise the moment and reach appropriate care. Later decisions about tests, diagnosis, treatment, driving, work, or follow-up may need a GP, NHS 111, A&E, ENT, audiology, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not medical or professional advice and cannot diagnose the cause of sudden ear ringing, dizziness, nausea, vertigo, or hearing change.
