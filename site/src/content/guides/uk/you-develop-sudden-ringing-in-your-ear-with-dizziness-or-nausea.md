# What to do if… you develop sudden ringing in your ear with dizziness or nausea (UK)

## Short answer
Lie down immediately to prevent falls and **seek urgent medical assessment now**. If the symptoms include sudden hearing loss, severe headache, or difficulty walking, **call 999 or go to A&E immediately**.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not drive**, cycle, or operate machinery—vertigo can cause sudden loss of control.
- **Do not delay seeking help** if you have noticed any change in your hearing levels.
- **Do not put any liquids**, oils, or cotton buds into the ear canal.
- **Do not perform "head maneuvers"** found online without a professional diagnosis.
- **Do not take leftover or someone else's vestibular suppressants** (such as prochlorperazine).
- **Do not attempt to "walk it off"** or balance through the dizziness, as this increases injury risk.

## What to do now
1. **Stabilize your physical position to prevent injury.** Sit or lie down on a flat surface immediately. Keep your head supported and try to keep your eyes fixed on a single, non-moving object.
   - If you feel you may vomit, lie on your side.
   - Keep a bowl or bag within reach.
2. **Check for emergency "red flag" symptoms. Call 999 immediately if you experience:**
   - **Sudden hearing loss** in one or both ears.
   - **Face drooping** on one side or an inability to smile.
   - **Arm or leg weakness** or numbness, especially on one side.
   - **Slurred speech** or difficulty finding words.
   - **Double vision** or sudden loss of vision.
   - **Inability to stand or sit up** unaided due to poor coordination.
   - **A sudden, severe headache** unlike any you have had before.
3. **Conduct a basic hearing self-check if safe to do so.** In a quiet environment, rub your fingers together near each ear or listen to a ticking watch. 
   - Note if the sound is significantly quieter, muffled, or absent on the side with the ringing. 
   - Report "sudden hearing loss" specifically to medical staff if found.
4. **Contact the correct NHS service for your current state.**
   - **Call 999 or go to A&E** for any of the red flags listed in Step 2.
   - **Contact your GP for an urgent "same day" appointment** if you have tinnitus and vertigo but hearing seems normal.
   - **Call 111 or use 111.nhs.uk** if your GP surgery is closed or you cannot get an immediate appointment.
5. **Prepare a summary for the clinician.** Document the following facts:
   - The exact time the symptoms started.
   - Whether the vertigo is "spinning" (the room moves) or "lightheadedness."
   - Whether the ringing is in the left, right, or both ears.
   - Any recent illnesses, head injuries, or new medications started in the last 48 hours.
6. **Minimize sensory input while waiting.** Dim the lights, reduce loud noises, and avoid sudden neck or head movements. Keep your mobile phone within reach but avoid scrolling, as screen movement can worsen nausea.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to identify the specific cause (such as Labyrinthitis or Ménière's) right now.
- You do **not** need to schedule a routine hearing test or see an optician.
- You do **not** need to buy over-the-counter travel sickness medication until advised.

## Important reassurance
Sudden onset ear symptoms and dizziness are often caused by temporary issues in the inner ear, such as viral inflammation. While the sensation is intense and can cause high levels of distress, medical professionals have clear protocols to stabilize these symptoms and protect your hearing.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. It is intended to help you navigate the NHS urgent care system and reduce the risk of injury during an acute episode.

## Important note
This information is not a substitute for professional medical diagnosis. Sudden hearing loss with vertigo is a time-sensitive medical event; always prioritize immediate clinical assessment over self-monitoring.
