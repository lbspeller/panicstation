# What to do if… a landlord demands immediate access to a home after a tenant dies and you are unsure what to allow (UK)

## Short answer
Refuse immediate entry unless there is a life-threatening emergency, such as a fire or a gas leak. Request all access proposals in writing with 24 hours' notice, and ensure any visit is supervised by the deceased person's personal representative or a trusted witness.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hand over keys or allow entry "right now" based solely on the landlord's verbal demand.
- Do not sign any documents on the spot, including "surrender" forms or "permission to enter" agreements.
- Do not allow the landlord to remove, bag up, or dispose of any belongings without the personal representative's formal consent.
- Do not feel obligated to argue legal rights at the door or on the telephone.
- Do not assume the tenancy ended automatically at the time of death; it usually continues as part of the deceased's estate.

## What to do now
1. **Identify if there is a genuine physical emergency.**  
   - Immediate access is typically only permitted for emergencies such as the smell of gas, a major active water leak, or fire.
   - If the landlord is attempting to force entry or you feel physically unsafe, call **999** immediately.
2. **Move the conversation to a written format.**  
   - Tell the landlord: "Please put your request for access in writing, including the specific reason and the proposed time."
   - Refuse to make decisions at the door or during an unannounced visit. 
3. **Assert the right to 24 hours' written notice for non-emergencies.**  
   - For repairs or inspections that are not life-threatening, state that you require at least 24 hours' written notice as per standard UK housing law.
   - Specify that any agreed entry must be at a reasonable time and must be supervised.
4. **Determine who has legal authority for the estate.**  
   - Ask the landlord to direct broad requests to the executor (if there is a will) or the administrator (if there is no will).
   - If no one is yet appointed, the tenancy in England and Wales temporarily vests in the Public Trustee; you can inform the landlord that they must wait for a formal representative.
5. **Set boundaries for purpose-limited visits.**  
   - If you agree to a visit, insist that you or a witness is present the entire time.
   - State clearly that belongings are not to be touched or moved during the visit.
6. **Document the current state of the property.**  
   - Take timestamped photos or video of every room, the contents, and the condition of the door locks.
   - Keep a written log of every time the landlord contacts you or visits the property.
7. **Contact the local council if harassment continues.**  
   - If the landlord continues to pressure you or makes threats, call **101** to report harassment.
   - Contact your local council’s Tenancy Relations Officer or Private Sector Housing team to report the landlord’s behavior.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today when the tenancy will end or how the deposit will be returned.
- You do not need to move or clear out any furniture or personal items immediately.
- You do not need to pay any "exit fees" or rent arrears on the spot.
- You do not need to provide the landlord with a copy of the death certificate or the will during the first interaction.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when facing property demands during a period of grief. You have the right to pause, close the door, and insist on formal communication. Taking these steps protects the deceased person's estate and ensures that all actions taken are legal and documented.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate access disputes only. It does not cover the long-term process of probate, formal tenancy termination, or the legal distribution of an estate's assets.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding tenancy and succession differ between England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. If a landlord attempts to illegally evict an occupier or force entry, contact the police or a housing solicitor immediately.
