# What to do if… you discover false information about you in an HR record or personnel file (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the account immediately by contacting your IT or security department to request a forced sign-out and password reset. Treat all unrecognised sent items as active security threats and do not interact with the account until cleared by professionals.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete any sent messages, security notifications, or login logs, as these are vital evidence for the investigation.
- Do not attempt to "Recall" the sent emails, as this often alerts the attacker that they have been discovered.
- Do not use the same device to change passwords if you suspect the device itself is infected with malware.
- Do not reply to any messages the attacker has sent or engage in conversation with them.
- Do not send a global apology or explanation email to all contacts until your IT security team has authorised the communication.
- Do not click on any links or download attachments in the suspicious sent folder to "verify" what was sent.

## What to do now
1. **Switch to a trusted communication channel immediately.** Stop using the compromised email or messaging app and use a known-safe phone, a different device, or an in-person meeting to report the issue.
2. **Contact your IT or Cyber Security helpdesk to report a suspected account compromise.** State clearly that you are seeing unauthorised messages being sent from your account. Request that they:
   - **Perform a forced sign-out of all active sessions** across all devices.
   - **Temporarily lock or disable the account** while the source of the breach is identified.
   - **Review and remove any new mailbox rules**, forwarding addresses, or "Sweep" rules.
   - **Check for unauthorised delegated access** or third-party app permissions.
3. **Change your password only after IT has confirmed the account is secure.** Use a strong, unique password (such as three random words) and ensure Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is reset if the attacker managed to bypass or modify it.
4. **Notify your immediate manager via phone or a secure alternative.** Briefly inform them that your account is compromised and that they should ignore any unusual requests or links coming from your name until further notice.
5. **Report the incident to the national 'Report Fraud' service if you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland.** Use the official online portal at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040. If you are in Scotland, report the matter to Police Scotland via 101.
6. **Alert the finance or payments team if the unauthorised messages involve invoices or banking.** If the attacker sent messages regarding changing bank details or requesting urgent payments, the finance department must be alerted via a trusted phone number to freeze or verify pending transactions.
7. **Forward any original phishing emails that may have led to the hack to the NCSC.** If you found the source phishing email, forward it to `report@phishing.gov.uk`. If the compromise started via a text message (smishing), forward that message to 7726.
8. **Check other work-related accounts where you may have used the same password.** Work systematically with your IT department to secure connected systems like HR portals, CRM software, or cloud storage.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the specific technical method the attacker used (e.g., session hijacking vs. password theft).
- You do not need to provide a formal written statement or incident report until the account is fully contained.
- You do not need to worry about the long-term forensic report or "lessons learned" documents in the first few hours.

## Important reassurance
Account compromises are highly common and often occur through sophisticated social engineering or automated attacks. Taking rapid action to report the issue to your IT department is the most effective way to protect your organisation and your professional reputation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Your organisation’s specific Internal Incident Response Plan (IRP) and data protection policies (GDPR/DPA 2018) will dictate the subsequent investigative and notification steps.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional cybersecurity advice. Always prioritize the specific instructions and security policies provided by your employer. If you are a self-employed contractor, seek professional assistance from a certified cybersecurity provider.
