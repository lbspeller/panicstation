# What to do if… your employer or provider tells you your benefits or health coverage have been cancelled unexpectedly (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as urgent to verify, not as something to accept on the spot: get the end date and reason in writing today, and ask for immediate reinstatement if it appears to be a mistake.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume you definitely have no cover because someone told you verbally; check directly with the provider or insurer.
- Do not sign anything saying you accept a change to benefits or terms while you are panicking.
- Do not cancel important appointments or stop medication without first checking what care you can still access, including NHS routes.
- Do not rely only on screenshots; ask for written confirmation by email or letter.
- Do not post about it on public workplace channels or social media while the facts are unclear.
- Do not resign, threaten legal action, or buy replacement cover before you have the dates, documents, and current cover status.

## What to do now
1. **Find out exactly what has been cancelled, in writing.**  
   Email HR, Payroll, or the benefits team and ask:
   - which benefit ended, such as private medical cover, dental cover, life assurance, income protection, pension, or another benefit
   - the effective end date
   - the reason shown on their system, such as admin error, eligibility change, missed payment, provider switch, or contract change
   - whether any dependants were affected
   - what notice or policy document they are relying on

2. **Save your immediate evidence.**  
   Download or screenshot:
   - the cancellation message
   - the benefits portal page showing inactive cover
   - your last 2 or 3 payslips
   - any benefit selection confirmation
   - any membership number, policy number, or provider message

3. **Check your payslips for deductions or missing contributions.**  
   Look for:
   - ongoing deductions for the benefit
   - any salary-sacrifice line
   - employer pension contributions
   - any sudden change from the previous month  
   If money is still being deducted for a benefit that appears cancelled, ask Payroll to explain where it has gone and whether the provider has received it.

4. **Confirm your status directly with the provider or insurer.**  
   Using your member number, card, app, or portal, ask:
   - “Is my cover active right now?”
   - “What date does your system show as the end date?”
   - “Who requested the cancellation or change?”
   - “Can cover be reinstated if this is an employer or administration error?”
   - “What should I do about any appointment, treatment, or claim already booked?”  
   Write down the date, time, person or team you spoke to, reference number, and what they said.

5. **If you need medical help now, use NHS routes while the admin is sorted.**
   - If it is life-threatening or you have serious emergency symptoms, call 999 or go to A&E.
   - In England, use NHS 111 online or call 111 if you need medical help now and it is not a life-threatening emergency.
   - In Scotland, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use the local NHS urgent-care route listed by NHS services, or call 999 in an emergency.
   - If a prescription, appointment, dental issue, or planned treatment is affected, ask the NHS or the provider what safe next step is available today.

6. **Ask your employer for a fix-first action.**  
   If HR says it is a mistake or system issue, reply with a clear request:
   - reinstate the cover immediately
   - backdate it if there has been a gap
   - confirm who will decide what happens to claims or appointments during any gap
   - give you the name and contact details of the benefits administrator, broker, or provider team handling it
   - confirm the next update time in writing

7. **If they say it is a package or terms change, slow it down.**  
   Ask for:
   - the written change
   - when it was communicated or consulted on
   - what your contract, handbook, or benefits policy says about changes
   - whether you are being asked to agree to a variation  
   You can say: “I do not agree to any change today. Please send the documents and the reason in writing so I can consider them.”

8. **If this affects your workplace pension, check the records before reporting.**  
   - Check your pension provider account for the last contribution received.
   - Ask Payroll what was deducted, when it was sent, and which pension scheme received it.
   - Raise the missing payment with your employer and pension provider first.
   - The Pensions Regulator says members should usually wait 90 days before reporting missing workplace pension payments, because it can take up to three months for money to be paid into a pension.

9. **Escalate if informal correction is not working.**  
   If the issue is not corrected quickly, or if the employer says the cancellation was intentional, use the workplace grievance procedure. Keep it factual: what benefit was promised, what changed, when you found out, what evidence you have, and what fix you are asking for. If you are in a union, contact your union representative.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to change jobs.
- Renegotiating your whole package.
- Buying replacement private cover.
- Writing a long complaint before you know the exact end date and reason.
- Deciding on any legal route.
- Making a final decision about treatment plans, unless a clinician says you need to decide today.

## Important reassurance
A sudden benefits or private medical cover interruption can be caused by payroll errors, provider admin, eligibility flags, renewal problems, or system migrations. You are not overreacting by treating it as urgent; the first job is to confirm the facts, protect your immediate care, and create a written record.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Once you know whether this was an error, a payment issue, an eligibility issue, or a genuine contract change, later decisions may need help from a union representative, Acas, a pension provider, an insurer, a clinician, or an employment adviser.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. Employment benefits, insurance cover, pension contributions, and medical access can be fact-specific. If you feel seriously unwell or unsafe, seek urgent medical help immediately.
