# What to do if… someone repeatedly asks for sexual “favours” in exchange for help, rides, or introductions (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the interaction immediately and get to a safe location; do not meet the person alone or accept any "help" that is tied to sexual access. This behaviour is a form of sexual harassment and you can access confidential support from a specialist service without having to report it to the police.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to any sexual contact to "get it over with" or to stop the pressure.
- Do not get into their vehicle or meet them in a private home to discuss the situation.
- Do not try to negotiate or give "maybe" answers as this can be interpreted as an opening for more pressure.
- Do not delete messages, emails, or call logs out of panic, as these may be needed if you choose to seek support or report later.
- Do not blame yourself for any past help, lifts, or money you have accepted from this person.

## What to do now
1. **Create immediate physical distance.** If you are currently with the person, use a neutral exit line such as "I need to leave now" or "I'm late for an appointment" and move to a public space or a place with people you trust.
2. **Cease all one-on-one contact.** Stop responding to personal messages and do not agree to meet in person. If you must communicate for work or study, keep all messages short, factual, and via professional channels only.
3. **Issue a single, clear boundary (if you feel safe doing so).** You can send one written message stating: "Do not ask me for sexual favours or make sexual comments; I only want a professional / platonic relationship." You do not need to wait for a reply or engage in a debate.
4. **Identify and remove points of leverage.** Arrange alternative transport, find a different mentor, or use different community resources so you are no longer dependent on this person for "favours" or help.
5. **Secure existing evidence.** Take screenshots of any texts, DMs, or emails where the demands were made. If the requests were verbal, write down the date, time, location, and exactly what was said while it is fresh in your mind.
6. **Contact a specialist support service for confidential advice.**
   - **England & Wales:** Call the 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line on 0808 500 2222.
   - **Scotland:** Call the Rape Crisis Scotland Helpline on 08088 01 03 02 (daily, 5:00 PM to midnight).
   - **Northern Ireland:** Call the 24/7 Domestic & Sexual Abuse Helpline on 0808 802 1414.
7. **Consider visiting a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC).** These centres provide medical and emotional support and can collect forensic evidence even if you have not decided to talk to the police. You can find your nearest centre by searching the NHS website.
8. **Inform a trusted third party.** Tell a friend, family member, or a professional (such as a GP or a workplace safeguarding lead) about what is happening so you are not carrying the situation alone.
9. **Call 999 if you are in immediate danger.** If you are being followed, threatened, or feel you cannot safely leave your current location, contact emergency services immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you want to make a formal police report or a workplace grievance.
- You do not need to "prove" to the person that their behaviour is wrong or wait for them to apologise.
- You do not need to figure out how this will affect your long-term career or social standing right this second; the priority is immediate safety and distance.

## Important reassurance
Being pressured for sexual access in exchange for help is a form of coercion, not a "deal." It is common to feel confused, to "freeze," or to try and be polite to keep the peace when someone is exploiting their power over you. These are survival responses and do not mean you have consented to the behaviour.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for ending coercive interactions and accessing immediate UK support. It does not cover long-term legal strategies or formal disciplinary procedures.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or medical advice. If you are at risk of harm, contact 999. Specialist support services can help you understand your rights under the Equality Act 2010 regarding sexual harassment.
