# What to do if… lights are flickering in multiple rooms and you suspect an electrical fault (UK)

## Short answer
Treat flickering across more than one room as a possible wiring fault or electricity supply problem. Reduce electrical load now, check for danger signs such as heat, burning smell, buzzing, crackling, sparks, smoke, or scorch marks, and switch off at the consumer unit only if danger signs appear and it is safe to do so.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore repeated flickering across rooms, especially if it is new or getting worse.
- Don’t keep running high-power appliances such as a kettle, tumble dryer, portable heater, microwave, washing machine, dishwasher, or hair dryer to see if it settles.
- Don’t touch or investigate hot sockets, scorch marks, a buzzing socket, or a buzzing consumer unit with the power on.
- Don’t remove the consumer unit cover or attempt DIY electrical repairs.
- Don’t repeatedly reset a tripping breaker.
- Don’t approach or touch external electrical equipment, damaged cables, or anything that is arcing. If you look outside, do it only from a safe distance.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger.**  
   Look and listen without opening or touching anything. If you notice burning or plastic smell, buzzing or crackling from sockets or the consumer unit, sparks, smoke, heat, or scorch marks:
   - turn off power at the consumer unit main switch only if it is safe to reach and the consumer unit is not hot, smoking, sparking, or wet;
   - leave the property if there is fire, smoke, sparks, or immediate danger;
   - call **999** if there is fire, smoke, sparks, or you feel unsafe.

2. **Reduce the electrical load.**  
   Turn off and unplug high-draw items such as portable heaters, tumble dryers, washing machines, dishwashers, microwaves, kettles, hair dryers, and non-essential chargers. Leave only essential lighting or essential devices on.

3. **Check the consumer unit only for an obvious trip.**  
   If it is safe and dry to do so, look for a tripped RCD, RCBO, or MCB switch. If there are no danger signs, you can reset it once. If it trips again, leave it off and do not keep testing it.

4. **Check whether it may be the local supply.**
   - Look at whether the flickering affects lights in several rooms and whether other devices, such as the router or TV, are also cutting out.
   - If safe, ask a neighbour whether they are affected, or look from inside at nearby street lighting.
   - If it looks like an area supply problem, contact the electricity network operator:
     - **England, Scotland, and Wales:** call **105**.
     - **Northern Ireland:** call **03457 643643**.

5. **If it seems limited to your home, stop testing and make a brief note.**  
   Without opening sockets, switches, or the consumer unit, write down:
   - which rooms are affected;
   - whether it is constant or comes in bursts;
   - whether it happens when a particular appliance switches on;
   - whether any breaker trips;
   - whether there is heat, smell, buzzing, crackling, sparks, smoke, or scorch marks.

6. **Contact the right person.**
   - **If you rent:** tell your landlord, agent, or housing provider in writing that lights are flickering in multiple rooms. Include any heat, burning smell, buzzing, crackling, tripping, sparks, smoke, or scorch marks, and ask for a qualified electrician to check it.
   - **If you own:** arrange a qualified electrician promptly, especially if the flickering is recurring, affects several rooms, or happens with tripping, heat, smell, buzzing, crackling, sparks, or scorch marks.

7. **Escalate if anything worsens.**  
   If danger signs appear, switch off at the consumer unit only if safe, leave the property if needed, and call **999** for fire, smoke, sparks, or immediate danger. If you think the problem is with the external supply or damaged outside equipment, contact the electricity network operator instead of trying to inspect it yourself.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to open sockets, switches, light fittings, or the consumer unit.
- You do not need to replace bulbs, fittings, sockets, or appliances today.
- You do not need to decide perfectly whether it is the landlord, an electrician, or the network operator. Reduce load, check for danger signs, then contact the network operator if it seems area-wide; otherwise contact your landlord, housing provider, or an electrician.

## Important reassurance
Whole-home or multi-room flickering can feel alarming because it may come from the electricity supply or from a fault inside the property. The safest first move is to reduce load, avoid touching or opening electrical parts, and bring in the right help rather than trying to prove the cause yourself.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and avoid making the situation worse. A qualified electrician or the electricity network operator may be needed to diagnose and fix the underlying problem.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you see smoke, fire, sparks, or you feel unsafe, leave the property and call emergency services.
