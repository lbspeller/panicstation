# What to do if… you are blocked in your car and someone is approaching you (UK)

## Short answer
Stay inside with the doors locked, windows up, and your phone in hand. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999 or 112 and ask for the police; if there is a clearly safe gap to leave without hitting a person or object, drive to a busier, well-lit place.

## Do not do these things
- Do not get out to confront the person or try to “sort it out” face to face.
- Do not unlock the doors or fully open a window to talk.
- Do not reverse, accelerate, or turn blindly in panic.
- Do not drive to a quieter or more isolated place because someone tells you to.
- Do not focus on proving what is happening while you still need to get safe.
- Do not end a 999 call just because you cannot speak.

## What to do now
1. **Make the car a locked room.** Lock the doors, keep the windows up, keep your phone in hand, and keep the seatbelt on if you are about to move. If it is safe, start the engine so you can leave without delay.
2. **Check for a clearly safe exit.** Look for people first, then use mirrors, cameras, and a slow head check for vehicles, posts, walls, trolleys, kerbs, and other obstacles.
3. **Leave only if the gap is clearly safe.** If you can drive out without contact and without moving towards the person, leave steadily and go to a busy, staffed, well-lit place such as a petrol station, supermarket entrance, or open drive-through.
4. **If you cannot leave safely, call 999 or 112 early.** Ask for the police. Say you are blocked in, someone is approaching or trying the doors, and you are staying locked inside the car. Give the car park name, nearest shop or entrance, level, row, and any visible landmark.
5. **If you cannot speak safely, still call 999.** Listen to the operator. Cough, tap the phone, or tap keys if you can. If you are prompted on a mobile, press 55 so the call can be treated as a genuine emergency and put through to police.
6. **Draw attention without opening the car.** Use short horn bursts, flash the headlights, switch on hazard lights, or use the car alarm or panic button if you have one.
7. **Keep the person outside the barrier.** If you need to respond, do it through closed glass and keep it short: “Step back. I am calling police.” Do not debate, explain, or negotiate.
8. **Use small movements only if they clearly make you safer.** If there is room, you may inch forward or adjust angle to improve visibility or line up with the clearest exit. Stop immediately if a person moves into your path or you are unsure.
9. **If the person leaves but you are worried they may follow, do not go straight home.** Drive to a busy, staffed, well-lit place and keep the 999 call going if you are already connected.
10. **When you are safe, note details only if it does not pull you back into danger.** Useful details may include registrations, vehicle colour and make, descriptions, direction of travel, the exact location, and the time.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a formal statement or pursue anything later.
- You do not need to confront anyone, leave notes, take photos, or prove what happened while you are still unsafe.
- Insurance, complaints to the car park operator, and detailed written notes can wait until you are somewhere safe.
- If the immediate danger has passed and you still want to report it, you can usually call 101 or use your local police online reporting route. If danger resumes, use 999.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, shaking, or feeling unreal can happen during a sudden threat. Staying locked inside, getting help early, making noise, and moving only when there is a clear safe path are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first minutes only. Later decisions about reporting, insurance, vehicle damage, statements, or legal issues may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, driving, or other professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger or someone is trying to get into your car, call 999 or 112.
