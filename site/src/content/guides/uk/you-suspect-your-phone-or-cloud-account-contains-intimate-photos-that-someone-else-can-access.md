# What to do if… you suspect your phone or cloud account contains intimate photos that someone else can access (UK)

## Short answer
Find a private space, then secure your primary account (Apple or Google) by changing your password and signing out all other devices immediately to stop active viewing.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront the person you suspect immediately, as this may trigger them to share or move the images to a location you cannot reach.
- Don’t delete the images or the entire account in a panic, as this removes the evidence needed for takedowns and police investigations.
- Don’t use a device you believe is being monitored to perform security resets or contact help services.
- Don’t click security alert links in emails or texts; always navigate directly to the official service website to change settings.
- Don’t share the suspected images with anyone else, including friends, to "show them what happened," as this technically constitutes further sharing.

## What to do now
1. **Find a private and secure location where your screen cannot be seen.**  
   If you believe you are in immediate physical danger or are being threatened with violence, call **999** now.

2. **Access your account from a trusted device and connection.**  
   If you suspect your phone is compromised, use a friend's device or a public computer and your own mobile data rather than shared home Wi-Fi.

3. **Secure the account that manages your photo backups (Apple Account / Google Account).**  
   - **Change your password** to a completely new, complex phrase you have never used before.
   - **Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)** if it is not already active.
   - **Navigate to the "Devices" or "Security" section** and select "Sign out of all other sessions" or manually remove any device you do not recognise.

4. **Audit specific photo-sharing permissions within the app settings.**  
   Check for:
   - Shared Albums or "Shared with Partner" features in Google Photos.
   - Shared Libraries or "Shared with You" settings in Apple Photos / iCloud.
   - Any external email addresses with "Viewer" or "Editor" access to cloud folders.

5. **Contact the Revenge Porn Helpline if you are over 18.**  
   Call **0345 6000 459** (10:00–16:00, Monday to Friday, excluding bank holidays) for specialist help with image takedowns and legal advice.

6. **Use StopNCII.org to prevent the images from being uploaded elsewhere.**  
   **Create a case on the StopNCII website** to generate "digital fingerprints" of your images; this allows participating platforms to block the content automatically without you having to upload the actual images to the service.

7. **Use Report Remove if you are under 18 (or the images were taken when you were).**  
   **Navigate to the Childline "Report Remove" tool** to have sexual images of minors identified and removed from the internet by the Internet Watch Foundation (IWF).

8. **Preserve evidence of the intrusion or threats without saving the explicit content.**  
   **Capture screenshots of login alerts, messages, or URLs** where you believe the images are being held; do not take new screenshots of the explicit images themselves, as this can create new legal risks.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you want to pursue a full criminal prosecution.
- You do not need to contact every individual website or platform where an image might appear; helplines can often batch these reports.
- You do not need to explain the situation to your family, employer, or friends today.
- You do not need to replace your physical phone immediately if you have secured the cloud accounts.

## Important reassurance
The feeling of violation you are experiencing is a normal response to a serious breach of privacy. Intimate image abuse is a crime in the UK, and the responsibility for this situation lies entirely with the person accessing or sharing the content without your consent. There are established legal and technical pathways to help you regain control.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step technical stabilisation and UK-specific reporting routes for non-consensual intimate image (NCII) situations. It does not provide long-term legal strategy or forensic device analysis.

## Important note
This information is for harm reduction only and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. UK law (including the Online Safety Act) provides specific protections and requires major platforms to remove flagged intimate content within 48 hours, but these rules may vary by platform.
