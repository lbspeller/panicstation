# What to do if… your fever returns after you seemed to be improving (UK)

## Short answer
If your fever returns after you seemed to be improving, get urgent health advice today. In England, use 111 online or call 111; in Wales or Scotland, call 111; in Northern Ireland, use nidirect, your GP out-of-hours route, or local urgent-care advice. Call 999 now if you have signs of a life-threatening emergency.

## Do not do these things
- Do not carry on as normal just because you felt better yesterday.
- Do not take extra doses or combine medicines beyond what the label says; many cold and flu remedies already include paracetamol.
- Do not assume it is definitely viral, definitely nothing, or definitely the same illness.
- Do not rely on a forehead strip or a single uncertain reading if you can recheck with a suitable thermometer.
- Do not ignore new symptoms, rapidly worsening symptoms, or feeling much more unwell than before.

## What to do now
1. **Re-check your temperature and write it down.**  
   Note the reading, the time, how you measured it, and any fever-reducing medicines you have taken, including the name, dose, and time.

2. **Check for emergency signs.**  
   Call **999** now if you have severe or rapidly worsening symptoms such as trouble breathing, chest pain, new confusion, a seizure, a stiff neck with severe headache, repeated vomiting you cannot control, a new rapidly spreading rash or purple spots, or you are barely passing urine.

3. **If it is not immediately life-threatening, get urgent health advice today.**  
   In England, use **111 online** or call **111**. In Wales or Scotland, call **111**. In Northern Ireland, use **nidirect**, your GP out-of-hours route, or local urgent-care advice. Say clearly: “I had a fever, I was improving, and now the fever has returned and I am getting worse again.”

4. **Mention anything that raises risk.**  
   Tell the adviser or clinician if you are pregnant, have a weakened immune system, have diabetes, have heart, lung, kidney, liver, neurological, or metabolic disease, are on immune-suppressing medicines, have recently had antibiotics, or have recently travelled abroad.

5. **Watch for dehydration while you arrange help.**  
   Sip fluids regularly if you can. Tell the adviser or clinician if you have severe thirst, feel faint or lightheaded, are peeing much less than usual, or your urine is much darker than usual.

6. **Reduce spread while you still have a high temperature.**  
   Stay home and avoid close contact with other people if you can until you no longer have a high temperature.

7. **Prepare a simple timeline.**  
   Write down when the illness started, when you improved, when the fever returned, the highest temperature, new symptoms, current medicines, recent antibiotics, and any major health conditions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether you should have rested more or replay what you did wrong.
- You do not need to do major chores or errands while you are arranging advice.
- Return-to-work, exercise, travel, and social plans can wait until you are clearly improving again and you have followed any health advice.

## Important reassurance
A fever returning after you seemed to be improving can feel alarming. Getting advice today is a sensible safety step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the next few hours. Later decisions, tests, treatment, or follow-up may need help from a GP, 111 where available, an urgent treatment centre, or another medical professional.

## Important note
This guide is general information only and is not a diagnosis, medical advice, or professional advice. For a baby or child with a fever, use child-specific NHS advice or seek urgent health advice.
