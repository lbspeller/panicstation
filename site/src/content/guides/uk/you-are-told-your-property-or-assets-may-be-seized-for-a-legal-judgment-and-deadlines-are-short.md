# What to do if… you are told your property or assets may be seized for a legal judgment and deadlines are short (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay, sign, or let anyone in until you have checked what this is using independent contact details. Find the case number and court name, verify the enforcement with the court or official guidance, then ask the court or a free debt adviser which urgent application or payment request is needed.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay or share bank details just because someone says “today” or “by close of business”.
- Don’t call a phone number, click a link, or scan a QR code from the letter, text, or email until you have cross-checked it.
- Don’t let someone in just to “verify” them; ask them to show ID and paperwork through a window or letterbox.
- Don’t sign a controlled goods agreement if you do not understand it, cannot afford it, or feel pressured.
- Don’t move, hide, sell, or give away goods in a panic before getting advice.
- Don’t assume sending a form automatically stops enforcement; the court usually has to make an order.

## What to do now
1. **Put the papers in one place and treat the contact as unverified.**
   - Keep the letter, envelope, text, email, screenshots, and any card left at the door.
   - Write down the time, date, caller number, visitor name, company name, and exact deadline they gave.

2. **Find the key details on the paperwork.**
   - Look for the court name, claim or case number, judgment date, creditor name, amount claimed, and any enforcement reference.
   - Check whether it says “warrant of control”, “writ of control”, “Notice of Enforcement”, “County Court”, “High Court”, “bailiff”, or “enforcement agent”.

3. **Verify the court details independently.**
   - Use GOV.UK court finder, not the contact details in the message, to find the court.
   - Ask the court whether the case number exists, whether a warrant or writ has been issued, and what enforcement step is next.
   - If the court cannot identify the case, do not pay the person contacting you until you have taken advice.

4. **Verify the person or company separately.**
   - If someone visits, keep the door closed and ask for their ID, company name, telephone number, enforcement reference, and breakdown of the amount claimed.
   - If they say they are a certificated enforcement agent, use official guidance to check the register.
   - If they say they are a County Court bailiff, contact the court that sent them using independently found details.

5. **Use the Notice of Enforcement deadline as urgent.**
   - A Notice of Enforcement is a warning that an enforcement agent may visit after the notice period.
   - Check the deadline on the notice and ask a debt adviser quickly if the days have been counted correctly or if more time can be requested.
   - Do not wait for a doorstep visit before asking for help.

6. **If it is a County Court warrant of control, ask about suspending the warrant.**
   - If you accept you owe the judgment debt but need affordable instalments, ask the court or adviser whether Form N245 is the right form to apply to suspend the warrant and vary payments.
   - If a judge set the payment rate, or if the situation is not straightforward, ask whether Form N244 is needed instead.
   - Put “urgent enforcement action threatened” on any covering email or message to the court.

7. **If it is a High Court writ of control, ask about a stay of execution.**
   - Ask which court you must apply to, how to file the urgent application, and what evidence they need.
   - If you are applying because you cannot pay immediately, prepare a short income and spending summary.
   - If you dispute the judgment, were not served, are the wrong person, or did not know about the case, say that clearly when asking what urgent application is needed.

8. **Contact free debt or legal help the same day.**
   - Say: “I have a judgment enforcement threat and property may be seized; the deadline is short.”
   - Give the adviser the case number, court name, creditor name, enforcement company, amount claimed, and deadline.
   - Tell them if you are ill, disabled, pregnant, recently bereaved, caring for children, or otherwise vulnerable, because this can affect how enforcement should be handled.

9. **If someone is already at the door, keep the situation calm and documented.**
   - Speak through the closed door if you can.
   - Ask them to leave paperwork through the letterbox.
   - If you feel unsafe, end the conversation and contact the court, the enforcement company, or emergency services if there is immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to challenge the whole judgment or negotiate the debt long term.
- You do not need to sell a car, borrow from family, empty accounts, or move valuables before you have verified the enforcement and taken advice.
- You do not need to write a long history of the dispute for the first call; the case number, deadline, paperwork type, and what is about to happen are enough.
- You do not need to open the door to sort this out.

## Important reassurance
Words like “seizure”, “warrant”, “writ”, and “enforcement” are frightening, but there are still checks you can make and urgent steps you can take. The safest first move is to verify the paperwork, avoid rushed agreements, and get help focused on the immediate enforcement deadline.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The court forms and enforcement routes in this guide are for England and Wales judgment enforcement within the UK; if your paperwork is from Scotland or Northern Ireland, use the court details on official local court websites and get jurisdiction-specific advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Court enforcement depends on the type of judgment, the court, the paperwork already served, and your circumstances.
