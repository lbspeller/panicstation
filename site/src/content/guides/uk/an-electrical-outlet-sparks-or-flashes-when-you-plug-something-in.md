# What to do if… an electrical outlet sparks or flashes when you plug something in (UK)

## Short answer
Stop using that socket immediately. If there is smoke, flame, a burning smell, heat, buzzing, crackling, visible damage, or repeated or large sparking, move away, switch off power at the consumer unit only if you can do it safely, and call 999 or 112 if there may be a fire or immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep trying to plug it in to see if it happens again.
- Don’t touch the socket faceplate, plug, or cable if it feels warm or hot, or if you see scorch marks, melting, smoke, or damage.
- Don’t reset an RCD or breaker repeatedly if it trips again straight away.
- Don’t turn the breaker back on to check if there is heat, a burning smell, visible damage, buzzing, crackling, or repeated sparking.
- Don’t use water near the socket, plug, appliance, or any electrical equipment.
- Don’t try to fight a fire unless you are trained, it is clearly safe, and you already have a clear way out.
- Don’t open the socket, poke anything into it, or attempt a DIY repair unless you are qualified.
- Don’t use an extension lead or adaptor to work around the faulty socket.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Step back from the socket, keep children and pets away, and keep hands dry.
2. **If you see smoke or flames, or smell burning:** leave the room, close the door if you can, get everyone away from the area, and call 999 or 112. Switch off power at the consumer unit only if you can do that without going near danger or delaying getting out.
3. **If there is ongoing sparking, buzzing, crackling, heat, or visible damage:** do not touch the socket, plug, or cable. Go to the consumer unit and switch off the affected circuit, or use the main switch if you are not sure which circuit it is.
4. **If there is no smoke, flame, heat, smell, buzzing, crackling, or visible damage, and it is safe to approach:** turn the socket switch off. If a plug is in place, pull it out by gripping the plug, not the cable.
5. **Leave power off if anything seems wrong.** Keep the circuit off if the socket is warm, loose, discoloured, scorched, cracked, smells hot, or sparked more than once.
6. **Stop using the appliance too.** Put it somewhere safe and unplugged. Do not test it in another socket until the socket and appliance have been checked.
7. **Do a visual check only.** Look for blackening, scorch marks, melted plastic, a cracked faceplate, a loose socket, or a damaged plug. Do not remove the faceplate.
8. **Arrange a registered electrician.** Tell them what happened, which socket it was, what was plugged in, whether an RCD or breaker tripped, and whether there was heat, smell, smoke, noise, or visible damage.
9. **If you rent or live in shared accommodation:** contact your landlord, agent, or building maintenance and say you have stopped using the socket and isolated the circuit because of a possible electrical fault. Ask for urgent attendance.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide right now whether it was a harmless flash or a fault.
- You don’t need to replace the socket or appliance yourself.
- You don’t need to work out the exact circuit if switching the main switch off was the safest option.
- You can deal later with insurance, product recalls, labelling circuits, or wider electrical checks.

## Important reassurance
A small single flash is not always an emergency, but heat, smell, smoke, noise, damage, tripping, or repeated sparking are reasons to stop and isolate power. Moving away from the socket and getting it checked is a sensible safety-first response.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce fire and electric shock risk. Fault-finding, testing, and repairs may need a registered electrician or other specialist help.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, electrical, fire-safety, or professional advice. If you believe there is immediate danger, prioritise getting everyone away from the area and calling emergency services.
