# What to do if… you realise you may have overstayed your permission to stay by mistake (UK)

## Short answer
Check your actual permission-to-stay end date from an official Home Office record, not just a BRP card date. If your permission has ended, or you cannot confirm it quickly, pause work and other status-dependent steps and get regulated immigration advice urgently.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the expiry date printed on a BRP card is the same as your permission-to-stay end date.
- Don’t leave and re-enter the UK to try to “reset” the problem.
- Don’t keep working if your right to work is unclear or you have confirmed your permission has ended.
- Don’t start a rushed application using guessed dates, missing evidence, or a route you have not checked.
- Don’t rely on social media, friends, or unregulated advisers for deadlines or eligibility.
- Don’t ignore official emails, decision letters, or online status messages because you feel overwhelmed.

## What to do now

1. Verify your actual permission end date from official records. Check your Home Office decision email or letter, your UKVI account, and your eVisa or online immigration status if you have one. Write down the permission end date, the type of permission, and the passport or identity document linked to the grant.

2. Check whether this is a false alarm before assuming you are an overstayer. A BRP card date may not match your permission end date. If you made an application before your permission expired and it is still pending, your permission may continue in some circumstances, often called section 3C permission; check the submission date, payment confirmation, and any Home Office acknowledgement.

3. If you confirm your permission has ended, or you cannot confirm your status today, pause status-dependent activity. Do not work, take paid self-employment, start a new job, or agree new work until you have clear confirmation that you are allowed to work. For renting in England, pause any new tenancy step that depends on passing a right-to-rent check until you have checked your position.

4. Get regulated immigration advice urgently. Ask specifically whether any application is still possible, whether section 3C permission applies, and whether the limited overstayer exception in Part Suitability, SUI 13.1, could apply to your facts.

5. If you are within a short time after expiry, act quickly but do not guess. UK rules can allow some short overstays to be disregarded in limited circumstances, including some applications made within 14 days where there is a good reason beyond the applicant’s or representative’s control and evidence is provided with the application. This is not an automatic grace period and does not by itself restore your previous rights.

6. Save evidence before it disappears. Keep copies of your decision letter, UKVI account screenshots, eVisa or status page, application receipts, payment confirmations, emails, travel records, and anything that may show why you could not apply on time. Keep originals unchanged and save copies in one folder.

7. Keep your contact details stable while you get advice. Make sure you can access the email address, phone number, UKVI account, and postal address connected to your immigration record. Do not delete messages, close accounts, or change numbers without keeping access to the old details.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether you will leave the UK, switch routes, challenge a decision, or explain everything to everyone. You also do not need to write a long personal statement immediately; first confirm the dates, pause anything that could make the problem worse, and get regulated advice.

## Important reassurance
Many people panic because they have looked at the wrong date, missed a message, or confused a card expiry date with permission to stay. If there has been a real overstay, quick action, accurate dates, and regulated advice are the safest first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the first hours or day after you realise there may be an overstay. Immigration outcomes depend on your exact route, dates, documents, and circumstances, so later decisions may need help from a regulated immigration adviser or solicitor.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, immigration, or professional advice. UK immigration rules and Home Office practice can change, and your position may depend on details not covered here.
