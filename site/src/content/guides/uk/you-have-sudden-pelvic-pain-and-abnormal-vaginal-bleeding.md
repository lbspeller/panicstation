# What to do if… you have sudden pelvic pain and abnormal vaginal bleeding (UK)

## Short answer
If pain is severe, sharp, or worsening, if you feel faint or dizzy, have pain at the tip of your shoulder, or are bleeding heavily, call **999** or go to **A&E** immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel dizzy, faint, lightheaded, or are bleeding heavily—call 999 or have someone else drive you.
- Do not wait to see if the pain improves if you are or could be pregnant, or if you feel sweaty, cold, shivery, or very unwell.
- Do not use tampons or menstrual cups until you have been medically assessed; use sanitary pads to monitor blood loss instead.
- Do not take aspirin or ibuprofen for the pain unless specifically advised by a clinician, as these can mask worsening symptoms or increase bleeding risk.
- Do not have sex, douche, or put anything in the vagina until an infection or other cause has been ruled out by a professional.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate emergency red flags.** Call **999** or go to **A&E** now if you have pelvic pain and any of the following:
   - Pain that is **severe**, **sharp**, **sudden**, or **getting worse**
   - Pain that makes it **difficult to move** or hurts when the area is **touched**
   - Feeling **faint**, **dizzy**, or **lightheaded**, or if you have **passed out**
   - **Pain in the tip of your shoulder** (where the shoulder ends and the arm begins)
   - **Heavy vaginal bleeding** (flooding through pads or passing large clots)
   - **Difficulty breathing**, **confusion**, or a **very high temperature**
   - **Nausea** or **vomiting** combined with intense pain
2. **Perform a pregnancy test immediately.** 
   - If the test is **positive** and you have any pain or bleeding, you require **urgent same-day medical assessment**.
   - If you have red-flag signs (Step 1) and a positive test, seek emergency care immediately as this can indicate a life-threatening ectopic pregnancy.
3. **Note specific details of the bleeding and pain.** This information is required for triage:
   - The time the bleeding started and the colour (e.g., bright red, dark brown, watery).
   - The frequency of pad changes and whether you are passing tissue or clots.
   - The exact location of the pain (e.g., one-sided, central, or under the ribs) and if it is persistent or comes and goes.
4. **Access the appropriate urgent care pathway.**
   - If you do not have "999" symptoms but have abnormal bleeding and pain, call **NHS 111** or contact your GP for a **same-day appointment**.
   - If you are currently under maternity care, contact your **maternity triage** line or **Early Pregnancy Assessment Unit (EPAU)**.
5. **State clearly if you have signs of infection.** Mention if you have a fever, chills, unusual-smelling discharge, or if the pain started after recent sexual activity or a recent procedure (like IUD insertion).
6. **Prepare for a physical examination.**
   - Bring a list of all current medications, including contraception and blood thinners.
   - **Do not eat or drink** until you have been told it is safe to do so by hospital staff, in case you require a procedure or surgery.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the underlying cause yourself, as this requires professional diagnostic tools like ultrasound or blood tests.
- You do not need to decide on long-term treatment plans or changes to contraception today.
- You do not need to worry about non-urgent appointments or routine screenings while seeking emergency assessment.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed or frightened when experiencing sudden pain and bleeding. These symptoms are common reasons for urgent medical consultations, and seeking professional help immediately is the standard safety procedure to ensure you are monitored correctly.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage guidance only. It is intended to help you reach the correct level of care and does not replace clinical diagnosis or treatment.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute medical advice. If your symptoms are severe, if you are pregnant, or if you feel very unwell, you must seek emergency medical attention immediately.
