# What to do if… you are told not to contact someone but you share urgent responsibilities with them (UK)

## Short answer
Do not contact them directly or indirectly unless the written restriction clearly allows it or an approved route has confirmed it. Put urgent shared responsibilities through a solicitor, the named investigator, or the contact route on the paperwork.

## Do not do these things
- Do not message, call, email, DM, react to posts, or “just check in”.
- Do not ask friends, family, colleagues, children, or mutual contacts to pass messages unless that route has been clearly approved.
- Do not discuss the investigation with them, compare accounts, or try to clear things up.
- Do not rely on the other person saying it is fine to contact them.
- Do not turn up at a home, workplace, school gate, appointment, or shared place where contact is likely unless the restriction and an agreed plan clearly allow it.
- Do not delete messages, call logs, emails, or paperwork connected to the restriction.

## What to do now
1. **Stop contact and write down what you were told.**  
   Note who told you, their role, the exact words used, the date and time, and any case, custody, bail, court, workplace, prison, or probation reference. Keep copies of any bail notice, court order, letter, email, or message.

2. **Identify the exact source of the restriction.**  
   Check whether it is a police bail condition, a court order such as a restraining order, a prison or probation instruction, or an employer or institution investigation instruction. Use the wording on the paperwork, because the route for questions or changes depends on the exact source.

3. **Find the named safe contact route.**  
   Look for the investigating officer, custody contact, court contact, solicitor, probation contact, prison contact, HR contact, or named case handler. If you do not have paperwork, ask the organisation that gave the instruction to send the restriction in writing before you act.

4. **Make a short list of responsibilities that cannot wait.**  
   Keep it practical and do not include arguments or case details. List only the next few days of needs, such as childcare handovers, school runs, medication, rent or mortgage payments, utilities, insurance, keys, essential belongings, vehicles, work handovers, rota cover, or access to shared systems.

5. **Ask for a permitted workaround before using one.**  
   Through your solicitor, the named investigator, or the named official contact route, ask how each urgent responsibility should be handled without contact. Options you can ask about may include solicitor-to-solicitor communication, a named third party for one specific task, a written handover through HR, or a scheduled collection through an agreed route. Do not use any workaround until it is clearly approved.

6. **If children are involved, separate logistics from safety.**  
   For school runs, handovers, appointments, or belongings, use the approved workaround. If a child is in immediate danger, call 999. If you have a non-emergency safeguarding concern, contact the child’s local authority children’s social care team, the police on 101, or the official online reporting route rather than contacting the other person.

7. **If they contact you, do not reply.**  
   Save screenshots, voicemail details, call logs, emails, envelopes, or messages. Note the date and time, then tell your solicitor, the investigating officer, or the named contact on the restriction.

8. **If this affects work, use a manager or HR as the handover point.**  
   Ask for a written plan saying who handles shared tasks, which systems you can use, what communication is not allowed, and what channel must be used instead.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain yourself to them right now.
- You do not need to fix the long-term parenting, housing, money, or work arrangement today.
- You do not need to answer messages, defend yourself, or resolve disputed facts in the moment.
- You do not need to decide whether to challenge the restriction before you have the exact written wording and specialist advice.

## Important reassurance
This situation can feel impossible because real responsibilities may still exist. Slowing everything down and using a traceable approved route is usually safer than trying to solve the practical problem quickly.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce the risk of accidental contact and stabilise urgent responsibilities. Later decisions about changing conditions, challenging an order, making a complaint, parenting arrangements, housing, employment, or the investigation may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Rules and consequences depend on the exact restriction, the investigation, and the paperwork you have. If anything is unclear, assume direct and indirect contact may be covered until your solicitor or the named official contact route confirms otherwise.
