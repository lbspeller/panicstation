# What to do if… your rental car reservation is cancelled at pickup and you need transport urgently (UK)

## Short answer
Get moving first: secure a workable alternative, such as another branch, another hire company, rail, coach, or a licensed taxi, then deal with refunds and complaints. Before you leave the desk, calmly collect written proof that you attended and the car was not supplied.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t accept a much higher walk-up price under pressure without first asking what alternatives they can offer at the booked rate, or as close to it as possible.
- Don’t let the booking be treated as a no-show if you are physically there; ask them to record that you attended and they could not supply the vehicle.
- Don’t agree to extra deposits, insurance, or upgrades you do not understand just to unlock a car; ask for the total cost in writing first.
- Don’t leave without evidence you can safely collect, such as your booking confirmation, cancellation message, receipts, names, times, and any written note from the desk.
- Don’t photograph staff, other customers, or private desk screens without permission.

## What to do now
1. **Pick the safest immediate transport aim.** Decide what matters most right now: arriving by a fixed time, carrying people or luggage, or keeping the cost manageable. This helps you choose between another hire car, rail, coach, or a licensed taxi.

2. **Ask the desk for a written record before you walk away.** Ask for:
   - confirmation that the reservation was cancelled, not honoured, or could not be supplied,
   - the stated reason, such as no vehicles, overbooking, payment issue, ID issue, or licence issue,
   - the date, time, branch location, and staff name or desk reference,
   - confirmation that you attended in person and should not be recorded as a no-show.

3. **Ask for the fastest same-company fix.**
   - Ask the desk to call nearby branches of the same rental company and hold a vehicle for you.
   - Ask whether they can supply a different vehicle class at the booked price, or with the smallest written price difference.
   - If they offer a more expensive replacement, ask them to write down the original booking price, the new total price, and why the original car was not supplied.

4. **Contact the booking route while you are still there.**
   - If you booked direct, call the rental company’s central reservations or customer service and ask for a same-day replacement booking at a nearby branch.
   - If you booked through a broker, travel site, airline, or voucher provider, contact that company too and ask them to rebook you or confirm the cancellation in writing.
   - Keep call notes: time, number called, who you spoke to, and what they said.

5. **Run a fallback transport search at the same time.**
   - Check nearby rental branches, including those at another terminal, station area, or city-centre location.
   - Check National Rail in Great Britain, Translink in Northern Ireland, or coach routes if you can carry your luggage and reach the destination without a car.
   - Check a licensed taxi or private-hire app for the shortest safe move, such as to your destination, a major station, or another rental cluster.
   - If you are at an airport or large station, use signed official transport areas and avoid unsolicited offers from people approaching you.

6. **Protect your money without turning it into a full dispute yet.**
   - Save the booking confirmation, cancellation message, payment receipt, replacement transport receipts, and any written desk note.
   - If you paid by credit card, you can ask your card provider whether Section 75 may apply. It generally depends on factors including the cash price, usually more than £100 and not more than £30,000, how you paid, and whether the required link between you, the credit provider, and the supplier exists.
   - If you paid by debit or credit card, you can ask your bank or card provider about chargeback for a service not provided. Chargeback is scheme-based, not a legal right, and is not guaranteed.
   - Ask your provider what evidence they need and what time limit applies.

7. **Start the complaint path in the simplest order.**
   - Contact the rental company first and keep it short: “I attended the branch, the car was not supplied, and I want a refund of all charges plus a written explanation.”
   - If you used a broker or travel site, send the same short message to them and ask who is responsible for refunding you.
   - If the rental company is a BVRLA member, the rental took place in the UK, and you have exhausted the company’s complaints process and received its final written decision, you can consider BVRLA ADR.
   - Before opening BVRLA ADR, check whether you have already started court action, another ADR complaint, or a bank or credit-card provider investigation for the same dispute, because BVRLA may not be able to deal with the complaint in those situations.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to use ADR, contact a consumer advice service, or consider legal action.
- You do not need to write a detailed timeline now; key evidence, names, times, and receipts are enough for the moment.
- You do not need to prove blame at the counter. Focus on getting moving, avoiding unnecessary extra costs, and preserving proof.

## Important reassurance
A cancelled or unavailable hire car can leave you feeling suddenly stuck, especially at an airport or station. You do not have to solve the whole dispute at the desk; getting safe onward transport and a simple written record is enough for the first stage.

## Scope note
These are first steps to get you moving and preserve your options. Refunds, extra costs, card claims, and complaints can depend on who you booked with, what the terms say, what was charged, and what evidence you have.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, professional, or transport advice. Policies and outcomes vary by company, booking channel, payment method, and the facts of what happened.
