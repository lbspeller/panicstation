# What to do if… you are told you must submit to an official drug or alcohol test by a deadline and logistics are unclear (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the deadline as an absolute priority and **immediately contact the issuing authority to get written confirmation of the location and time**, while **maintaining a timestamped log** of every attempt you make to comply.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait for a return call without also sending a written message (email or text) to create a paper trail of your effort.
- Don’t assume a "technical breach" (missing the test because of bad instructions) will be overlooked; it can lead to immediate arrest or prison recall.
- Don’t guess the location or attend an unverified private clinic; if they are not the official contracted provider, the test may not be recognized.
- Don’t attempt to use "detox" products or masking agents, as interference with a sample is often treated as a separate, serious offence.
- Don’t rely on verbal assurances from staff that "it will be fine if you come tomorrow" unless you have it in writing.
- Don’t ignore a vague message; under the Crime and Policing Bill 2026, police powers for testing now cover a wider range of substances (Class A, B, and C).

## What to do now
1. **Verify the order using established contact details rather than the ones in the unclear message.**  
   **Check your existing paperwork (bail sheets, probation "probation requirement" letters, or court orders) for an official office number.** If the request came via a sudden text or email, do not click links; call the main switchboard of the police station or probation office you usually attend to confirm the instruction is genuine.

2. **Start a "Compliance Log" immediately to record every action you take.**  
   **Open a note on your phone or use a notebook to write down the date and exact time of every call or email you send.** Record who you spoke to, what they said, and take screenshots of any "call failed" or "no answer" screens. This log is your primary protection if you are later accused of non-compliance.

3. **Identify if the order comes from the Police (Custody Officer) or Probation.**  
   **Determine the specific legal authority behind the test.** 
   - If it is pre-charge bail, the Custody Officer at the station where you were bailed has the power to vary conditions (PACE s.47). 
   - If it is a Community Order or Licence, your Supervising Officer (Responsible Officer) is the person who must clarify the "probation requirement."

4. **Send a formal written request for logistics to the issuing officer or office.**  
   **Email or text the office asking for the exact address, the "window" of time for attendance, and the specific forms of ID required.** State clearly that you are ready and willing to comply but require the specific location to do so. Ask what counts as "submitted" (e.g., "does the sample need to be provided by 4 PM, or do I just need to have arrived by then?").

5. **Contact the "Officer in the Case" (OIC) or the Duty Sergeant if this is police-related.**  
   **Ask them to confirm the testing provider's details and note your request for clarification on the electronic custody record.** If the deadline is impossible because of late notice or missing info, ask for a "variation of bail conditions" in writing from the Custody Officer.

6. **Contact your Probation Office immediately if the test is a condition of a sentence or licence.**  
   **Speak to the Duty Officer if your specific supervisor is unavailable.** Under the Sentencing Act 2026, failing to comply with a "probation requirement" (formerly RAR) can trigger immediate breach proceedings. Ask them to confirm which contracted laboratory or clinic you must attend.

7. **Prepare your "Compliance Pack" for the test appointment.**  
   **Gather your photo ID, the original notice/letter, and any current prescriptions or medical evidence.** Many official tests (especially saliva tests under 2026 protocols) require you to disclose medications before the sample is taken to avoid "false positive" complications.

8. **Seek legal advice if you are at a police station or under arrest.**  
   **Request the duty solicitor or contact your own legal representative before agreeing to testing procedures if the logistics remain unconfirmed.** You have a right to private legal advice if you are being detained for a drug/alcohol test as part of a criminal investigation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to challenge the legality or fairness of the test right now; focusing on the logistics is the safest way to avoid immediate arrest.
- You do not need to provide a full statement about your substance history; keep your questions strictly to the "where" and "when" of the deadline.
- You do not need to decide on a formal complaint today; your priority is ensuring you are not recorded as "failed to attend."

## Important reassurance
It is common for administrative errors to occur in high-pressure legal environments. If you have a timestamped log showing you made multiple, active attempts to find the correct location and time, you are in a much stronger position to defend against an allegation of breach. The system prioritizes "good faith" efforts that are backed by evidence.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for logistics confusion in the UK. It does not provide legal strategy for contesting the results of a test or the grounds on which a test was ordered.

## Important note
This is general information and not legal advice. Drug and alcohol testing protocols involve specific statutory requirements under PACE, the Sentencing Act 2026, and the Offender Rehabilitation Act. Always follow instructions from your legal representative or the court.
