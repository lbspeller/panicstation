# What to do if… you are told you must submit to an official drug or alcohol test by a deadline and logistics are unclear (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as time-sensitive: use trusted contact details to ask for clear written instructions now, and keep proof that you are trying to comply. Focus on the deadline, the correct place, what ID to bring, and what counts as submitting the test.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore it or wait silently for someone to call you back.
- Don’t use phone numbers, links, or attachments from an unexpected text or email unless you have checked they are genuine.
- Don’t guess a testing site and assume it will count without confirmation from the issuer or authorised provider.
- Don’t use “detox” products or try to interfere with the test; this can be treated seriously and may make the situation worse.
- Don’t rely on messages passed through friends or family; ask the official contact directly and keep a written record.
- Don’t sign a rushed statement or agreement you do not understand; ask for it in writing and get legal advice if it may affect your liberty, bail, licence, court order, or probation position.

## What to do now
1. **Check that the instruction is genuine.** Use contact details from paperwork you already trust, such as a bail sheet, court document, probation letter, licence paperwork, prison release paperwork, or an official website. Do not rely only on a new text message or email if it is vague or unexpected.

2. **Find the source of the requirement.** Look for the organisation, named officer or team, reference number, case number, deadline date, and deadline time. The notice may come from a justice, custody, court, probation, licence, prison, offender-management, or contracted testing route.

3. **Send one clear written request for the missing logistics.** Ask for:
   - the exact address or authorised testing provider
   - whether you need an appointment or can attend without one
   - opening hours and the latest arrival time that will still count
   - the test type, if they can tell you
   - what ID, paperwork, reference number, or forms to bring
   - what counts as “submitted” by the deadline
   - what proof of attendance or submission you will receive
   - what to do if illness, disability, caring responsibilities, transport disruption, or missing instructions make attendance difficult.

4. **If you have to phone, make the call practical and then write it down.** Ask for the person’s name, role, direct contact route, and the instruction they are giving. Immediately send a follow-up message saying what you understood: the place, time, documents needed, and any agreed next step.

5. **If the paperwork is police-related, contact the named police contact or station contact on the paperwork.** This may be an investigating officer, custody office, bail office, or another named contact. Ask who can confirm the testing instructions and who can deal with the problem if the deadline is not workable because the instructions were missing or unclear.

6. **If this is probation, licence, prison release, or offender-management related, contact the named supervising contact immediately.** Ask them to confirm the provider or site, note that you raised the logistics problem promptly, and send you a written plan for how to meet the requirement or what to do next.

7. **Create a simple compliance log.** In a note on your phone, record the date and time, who you contacted, the number or email used, what you asked, what they said, and any reference numbers. Keep screenshots, sent emails, call logs, voicemails, auto-replies, and travel or attendance evidence.

8. **Prepare to attend as soon as you have a workable instruction.** Take photo ID, the notice or letter, the reference number, any required forms, and details of prescribed medicines for the authorised test provider if needed. Aim to arrive early enough that queues, travel delays, or reception problems do not push you past the deadline.

9. **If you are arrested, detained, questioned at a police station, or being asked to agree to something you do not understand, ask for legal advice.** You can ask for legal advice, including through the duty solicitor route where available, before answering questions or agreeing to procedures that may affect you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to argue about whether the requirement is fair right now; first get the practical instructions and protect your record of trying to comply.
- You do not need to explain your whole history in the first message; keep it short and focused on the deadline and logistics.
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, challenge the requirement, or make a bigger legal application.
- You do not need to predict the test result in your messages; focus on where, when, how, and proof of attendance.

## Important reassurance
Unclear official instructions are stressful, but a prompt, calm paper trail can matter. The immediate aim is not to solve the whole case; it is to show that you acted quickly, asked the right people for specifics, and did not ignore the deadline.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Drug and alcohol testing processes can vary by authority, legal route, contract provider, and where in the UK the requirement was issued, so later decisions may need specialist legal or professional help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If the requirement may affect bail, custody, probation, licence, a court order, employment, immigration status, or your liberty, get advice from a qualified adviser or solicitor as soon as you can.
