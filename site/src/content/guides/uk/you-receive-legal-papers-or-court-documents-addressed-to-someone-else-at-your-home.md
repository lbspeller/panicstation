# What to do if… you receive legal papers or court documents addressed to someone else at your home (UK)

## Short answer
Keep calm, do not accept responsibility, and make a clear record that the named person does not live at your address. Return ordinary wrongly addressed post, but if it looks like court or enforcement paperwork, also notify the sender or official body using contact details you verify independently.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t throw away, shred, or hide papers that look like court, debt, or enforcement documents.
- Don’t pay anything or agree to “sort it” for the named person.
- Don’t call or pay a number from the letter until you have checked the organisation independently.
- Don’t let anyone into your home just because they mention paperwork for someone else.
- Don’t post photos of the documents online or share personal details from them.
- Don’t try to track down the named person yourself.

## What to do now
1. **Keep the packet together for the moment.** Keep the envelope and every page together, and note the date it arrived.
2. **Avoid reading more than you need.** If it is clearly not for you, only check what is needed to identify the sender, any reference number, and whether your own name appears anywhere. If you opened it by mistake, stop there and keep it with the envelope.
3. **Record only the practical details.** Write down or photograph the outside of the envelope, the sender’s name, any reference number, and the arrival date. Do not copy or share private details that are not needed.
4. **Return ordinary wrongly addressed post.** On the envelope, cross out your address, write **“Not known at this address”** or **“No longer at this address”**, circle the return address if there is one, and put it back in the post.
5. **If it looks like a court claim, summons, urgent notice, or enforcement letter, also send a short protective message.**
   - Use an official website or verified public contact route to find the court, solicitor, claimant, creditor, or enforcement company.
   - Say: “I am the current occupier at [address]. [Name on papers] does not live here. Please update your records. I am not the defendant, respondent, debtor, or named person.”
   - Include the reference number if you have one.
   - Keep a copy or screenshot of what you sent and when.
6. **If a process server or enforcement agent comes to the door, keep the door closed or stay at the doorway.**
   - Say: “That person does not live here. Please mark this as not at this address.”
   - Ask them to show ID, company details, and any reference through the window or letterbox.
   - Checking identity does not mean you have to let them in.
7. **If the document is a notice of enforcement or bailiff letter for someone else, contact the enforcement company first.**
   - Say you are not the person named and ask them to mark the case as **not at this address** while they check.
   - Send copies only of simple proof that you live there, such as a council tax bill, utility bill, tenancy agreement, or bank statement in your name.
   - Redact account numbers and financial details where you can. Keep originals.
   - If the letter names a creditor and contact continues, send the same short correction to the creditor using verified contact details.
8. **If your own name appears anywhere, treat it as urgent.** Do not just return it. Contact the issuing body using verified details and ask what the document is, why you are named, and how to correct the record.

## What can wait
- You do not need to understand the whole case today.
- You do not need to decide whether the named person has done anything wrong.
- You do not need to contact the named person.
- You usually do not need a solicitor immediately unless your own name appears, someone is threatening action against you or your home, or you cannot get the sender to correct the record.

## Important reassurance
Wrong-name legal or debt letters often happen because records are old, someone has moved, or an address has been linked incorrectly. Receiving the envelope does not automatically make you responsible for the person, the case, or the debt.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation, create a clear paper trail, and reduce the chance of avoidable escalation. Later decisions may need specialist help if you are named, enforcement continues, or the document affects your home or belongings.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Processes and powers vary by document type and part of the UK, so verify contact details independently and keep copies of anything you send.
