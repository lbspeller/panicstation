# What to do if… a last-minute flight reroute adds a transit stop and you are unsure about document requirements (UK)

## Short answer
Confirm whether the added stop requires you to pass border control, then get the airline to confirm in writing that it will accept you for boarding on the rerouted itinerary.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume “I’m only changing planes” means no extra documents; some routes still need a transit visa, ETA, or other permission.
- Do not rely on other passengers or social media; check-in staff use document-check rules for your passport and route.
- Do not cancel bookings or spend more money until you know whether the reroute can be used safely.
- Do not hand over your passport to anyone offering “help” outside official airline or airport desks.
- Do not continue onto a new leg while the airline cannot confirm document acceptance for the added transit stop.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the new routing exactly.** Include every airport and country, flight numbers, connection times, terminal changes, whether it is overnight, and whether the ticket is one booking or a self-transfer.

2. **Find out whether the added stop is airside or landside.**  
   - **Airside** usually means you stay in the international transit area and do not pass border control before your next flight.
   - **Landside** means you pass border control, even briefly, before continuing.
   A terminal change, airport change, overnight stop, self-transfer, or baggage reclaim can make the stop landside. If you are unsure, treat it as landside until the airline confirms otherwise.

3. **Check the official rules for the added transit country using your passport nationality.** Use that country’s government or immigration site, and check the UK FCDO travel advice page for the country’s entry requirements. Look specifically for transit visa, airside transit, landside transit, eVisa or ETA, passport validity, and rules for children or minors.

4. **If the added stop is in the UK, use the GOV.UK transit and visa checker pages.** GOV.UK separates UK transit into:
   - **airside transit**, where you do not pass UK border control;
   - **landside transit**, where you do pass UK border control and then leave the UK soon afterwards.
   Your airline should be able to tell you whether your reroute passes UK border control.

5. **If you may need a UK ETA, check the current GOV.UK ETA rules.** GOV.UK says you do not need an ETA if you are transiting through a UK airport and will not pass through border control. If you are not sure whether you will pass border control, check with the airline before travelling.

6. **Ask the airline to confirm document acceptance for the new itinerary.** Use app chat or email if possible so you have a written record. Ask:
   - “Will I pass border control at the added transit stop?”
   - “Will you accept me for boarding on this reroute with my passport and current documents?”
   - “Will my bags be checked through, or must I collect and re-check them?”
   - “Is this one protected connection, or is any part a self-transfer?”

7. **If the answer is unclear, ask for a safer reroute.** Ask the airline to remove the uncertain transit country, avoid an overnight stop, avoid a self-transfer, or route you through a country where you already meet the transit requirements.

8. **Prepare fast proof for check-in.** Keep accessible offline: passport bio page, visas, ETAs, residence permits or immigration status documents, onward ticket, booking reference, accommodation address if relevant, and the airline’s written confirmation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to solve the whole trip now; the immediate question is whether you can board and legally complete this reroute.
- You do not need to research every exception; focus on your passport nationality, documents you already hold, whether you pass border control, and whether baggage must be rechecked.
- You do not need to argue at the desk. If the airline cannot confirm acceptance, asking for a reroute that removes the document uncertainty is usually more useful.

## Important reassurance
This is a common travel disruption, and it is reasonable to feel unsettled because the timing is tight. A short check of border-control, baggage, and document rules can prevent a much harder problem later.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from the airline, the relevant embassy or consulate, or a regulated immigration adviser, especially if you have dual nationality, a refugee travel document, prior overstays, name mismatches, or unusual passport validity issues.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Entry and transit rules can change and can depend on nationality, documents held, ticket type, baggage arrangements, and whether you pass border control. Airlines can refuse boarding if their document checks show you do not meet requirements for any point on your itinerary.
