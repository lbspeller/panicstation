# What to do if… a partner pressures you to share your phone passcode and becomes hostile when you refuse (UK)

## Short answer

Do not focus on winning the argument. The immediate priority is to get to a safer pause, keep control of your phone if you safely can, and contact domestic abuse support from a route your partner cannot monitor.

## Do not do these things

- Do not keep arguing about whether you are “allowed” privacy if that is making them more hostile.
- Do not make lots of visible phone, password, or account changes on a device they may already watch if that could trigger escalation.
- Do not unlock the phone “to prove” anything.
- Do not assume this is too minor to matter because they have not hit you.
- Do not threaten them with police or legal language in the moment if your goal is immediate safety.
- Do not use a shared device, shared cloud account, or phone they can check to look for help unless you think that is safe.

## What to do now

1. **Move the interaction toward safety, not resolution.**  
   Try to get to a safer pause: another room with an exit, outside, a neighbour, a shop, work, or anywhere other people are nearby. If you are in immediate danger, call **999**. If you call from a mobile and cannot speak, listen to the operator and press **55** when prompted.

2. **Use a safer device or safer contact route.**  
   If possible, use a phone, computer, or account your partner cannot access. GOV.UK lists domestic abuse helplines and support routes. Refuge’s National Domestic Abuse Helpline is free and open 24 hours a day on **0808 2000 247**.

3. **Keep your passcode private for now if you safely can.**  
   Your phone may contain messages, email, photos, banking, health information, location history, and private conversations. You do not need to hand over that access just because someone is demanding it.

4. **Tell one trusted person something specific.**  
   From a safer route if possible, send a short message such as: “My partner is pressuring me for my phone code and got hostile when I refused. Please check on me today.” Ask for one concrete thing: a call at a set time, a place to go, or for them to stay reachable.

5. **Treat this as a control and safety issue, not just a phone argument.**  
   Pressure to inspect your device, monitor who you talk to, demand passwords, or punish you for privacy can fit domestic abuse, including controlling or coercive behaviour and technology-related abuse. You do not need to label it perfectly right now for it to matter.

6. **Make one low-visibility note only if it is safe.**  
   If it would not be discovered or escalate danger, note the date, time, what access they demanded, and what happened when you refused, especially if they threatened you, blocked your movement, tried to take the phone, or kept repeating the demand. Keep it somewhere safer than your usual notes if possible.

7. **Get health support if you are injured or feel physically unwell.**  
   If you are injured, feel unwell, cannot calm down, or are worried about a stress reaction or panic symptoms, use **111**, local urgent care, or your **GP** where available. If it becomes an emergency, use **999**.

## What can wait

You do not need to decide today whether to stay, leave, report anything, confront them again, or change every password. You also do not need to decide right now whether this “counts enough.” Later, a domestic abuse service can help you think through police, housing, court, and digital safety options without forcing you into a decision.

## Important reassurance

Wanting privacy on your own phone is normal. Someone becoming hostile because you will not hand over your passcode can be frightening and disorienting, and it is common to freeze, minimise it, or wonder whether you are overreacting.

## Scope note

This is first steps only. Later decisions about police, housing, court orders, digital security changes, or the relationship itself may need specialist help.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. In the UK, domestic abuse can include controlling, coercive, and technology-related behaviour, but you do not need to label it correctly right now to ask for help. If you may want to report later, a simple private note can help, but your immediate safety matters more than collecting evidence.
