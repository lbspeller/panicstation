# What to do if… you develop new chest pain that gets worse when you breathe in (UK)

## Short answer
Treat new, sharp chest pain as a medical emergency if it is accompanied by breathlessness, sweating, or spreading pain. **Call 999 immediately** if the pain is severe, lasts more than 15 minutes, or makes you feel very unwell.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital under any circumstances.
- Do not perform physical "tests" such as running, lifting, or climbing stairs to see if the pain returns.
- Do not wait to see if the pain "settles" if you are also feeling short of breath or dizzy.
- Do not take any medication to mask the pain unless you are certain it is safe for you and you have spoken to a medical professional.
- Do not ignore the pain even if it is sharp or "stabbing" (many people wrongly assume heart attacks only feel like "pressure").

## What to do now
1. **Stop all physical activity and sit in an upright, supported position.** This reduces the workload on your heart and lungs.
   - If sitting upright is too painful, try lying on the side that hurts, as this can sometimes stabilize the chest wall.
2. **Determine if you need to call 999 immediately.** Dial 999 if:
   - The pain is severe, crushing, or has lasted longer than 15 minutes.
   - The pain spreads to your arms, back, neck, or jaw.
   - You are struggling to breathe or feel unusually short of breath.
   - You feel sweaty, clammy, sick, or lightheaded.
   - You are coughing up blood.
3. **If you suspect a heart attack and are not allergic to Aspirin, chew one 300mg tablet.** Do not swallow it whole; chewing allows it to enter your bloodstream faster.
4. **Call NHS 111 if the pain is new but you do not meet the 999 criteria.** Tell the operator: "I have new chest pain that gets worse when I breathe in." They will triage you and may direct you to an Urgent Treatment Centre or A&E.
5. **Unlock your front door and keep your phone within reach.** If you are alone, this ensures emergency responders can reach you if your condition worsens.
6. **Prepare a brief summary for the medical team.** Note down:
   - The exact time the pain started.
   - Any recent long-distance travel, surgery, or illnesses.
   - If you have any leg swelling or a cough.
   - Any medications you have taken in the last 24 hours.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause (such as pleurisy, a pulmonary embolism, or a strain) yourself; this requires clinical tests.
- You do not need to contact your regular GP first if the pain is occurring now; use 111 or 999 for immediate triage.
- You do not need to gather medical records or history beyond your current symptoms and medications.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel anxious when experiencing chest pain. Sharp pain that changes with breathing (pleuritic pain) often has treatable causes, but because it can also indicate serious issues with the lungs or heart, seeking an immediate professional assessment is the safest and most appropriate action.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and emergency triage only. It does not provide a diagnosis or long-term management for chest conditions.

## Important note
Chest pain is a complex symptom. Always prioritize the instructions given by 999 or 111 operators over any general guide. If you are in doubt, assume it is an emergency and call 999.
