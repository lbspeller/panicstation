# What to do if… you receive a notice threatening immediate enforcement or legal action over a missed payment (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before paying, clicking, replying, or sharing details. Verify the notice through an independent official route, then ask the original creditor or authority what process is actually in place.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay immediately just because the message sounds urgent or mentions “bailiffs”, “enforcement”, “police”, “arrest”, or “prison”.
- Don’t use the phone number, link, QR code, bank details, or payment page in an unexpected notice until you have checked it independently.
- Don’t give card details, bank details, ID documents, passwords, or one-time passcodes to an unexpected caller or messenger.
- Don’t open attachments from an unexpected enforcement email or text.
- Don’t let someone into your home just because they say they are a bailiff or enforcement agent.
- Don’t ignore a notice after you have confirmed it is genuine; verified enforcement letters can lead to extra fees or visits if nothing is done.

## What to do now
1. Put the notice somewhere safe and create a short pause. Take a photo or screenshot, keep the envelope or email header, and do not delete the message.

2. Check for scam pressure. Treat the notice as suspicious if it demands same-day payment, tells you not to contact anyone else, asks for an unusual payment method, uses a link or QR code as the only payment route, or threatens arrest for an ordinary missed payment.

3. Verify the organisation independently. Use an official website you find yourself, such as GOV.UK, your council’s website, your court’s official contact details, your utility provider’s website, or a known account portal. Do not use contact details only provided in the notice.

4. Ask the original creditor or authority three direct questions:
   - “Is this debt real and in my name?”
   - “Has it been passed to enforcement, and if so, which company or officer has it?”
   - “What is the safest official way to pay, challenge it, or ask for a payment plan?”

5. If the notice says “Notice of Enforcement” or “taking control of goods”, check whether it fits the proper process before relying on the deadline. A notice period should usually apply before a first enforcement visit, but the timing and rules can depend on the debt, document, and UK nation. If the notice says someone will attend immediately, or the deadline is unclear, verify it with the original authority or a free debt adviser before paying the sender.

6. If the notice names a bailiff or enforcement agent, ask for their full name, company, reference number, authority for the debt, and a breakdown of the amount claimed. If they say they are a certificated enforcement agent, use GOV.UK’s bailiff identity guidance to find the official register route or ask the court or original authority to confirm them.

7. If someone is at your door, keep the door closed while you check. Ask them to show ID and authorisation through a window or put it through the letterbox so you can photograph it. You usually do not have to let a bailiff into your home, but they may still be able to take some goods outside, such as a vehicle, depending on the debt and process.

8. If the notice is genuine and you cannot pay in full, ask for a payment plan and ask for any agreement in writing. If you live in England or Wales and you are overwhelmed by debts, ask a free debt adviser whether Breathing Space may be available and suitable; a debt adviser applies if it is right for you.

9. If you think it is a scam, stop engaging with the sender. Report suspicious emails, texts, websites or calls to the National Cyber Security Centre. If you lost money or gave personal details, report the fraud through Report Fraud in England, Wales or Northern Ireland, or contact Police Scotland via 101 in Scotland. If you feel threatened or unsafe right now, call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to borrow money, sell belongings, or agree to a plan you do not understand.
- You do not need to argue the full history of the debt before confirming whether the notice is genuine.
- You do not need to let anyone inspect your home while you are still checking who they are.
- You do not need to choose a long-term debt solution before getting free advice.

## Important reassurance
Urgent enforcement wording is designed to make you act quickly, and scammers also use that pressure. A short pause to verify the sender, the debt, and the safest route to respond is a protective step, not ignoring the problem.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: pause, verify, avoid scam loss, and reduce immediate escalation. Later decisions may need help from a free debt adviser, a legal adviser, the court, the council, the creditor, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, debt, or professional advice. Enforcement rules and options can differ by debt type and by UK nation, so check through official contact details or a regulated advice service before relying on a deadline or making a payment.
