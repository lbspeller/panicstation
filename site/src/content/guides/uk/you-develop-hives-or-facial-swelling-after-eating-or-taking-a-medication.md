# What to do if… you develop hives or facial swelling after eating or taking a medication (UK)

## Short answer
If your lips, mouth, tongue or throat are swelling, your throat feels tight, breathing or swallowing is difficult, you feel faint or confused, or symptoms are getting worse quickly: use your adrenaline auto-injector if you have one and call 999 now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait and see if there is swelling of the lips, mouth, tongue or throat, voice change, breathing trouble, swallowing trouble, dizziness, confusion, collapse, or symptoms are rapidly worsening.
- Do not eat more of the suspected food or take another dose of the suspected medicine to test it.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you feel unwell, lightheaded, breathless, or symptoms are progressing.
- Do not rely on an antihistamine alone if there is facial swelling, throat tightness, breathing trouble, swallowing trouble, faintness, or confusion.
- Do not stand up or walk around if you may be having anaphylaxis, even if you start to feel better.
- Do not restart the same suspected trigger later just to check without medical advice.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency signs. Treat this as an emergency if any apply:** sudden swelling of the lips, mouth, tongue or throat; trouble breathing; wheeze; persistent cough; hoarse voice; throat tightness; difficulty swallowing; blue, grey or pale skin, lips or tongue; sudden confusion; drowsiness; dizziness; fainting; collapse; or rapidly spreading symptoms.

2. **If any emergency sign is present:**
   - Use your adrenaline auto-injector immediately if you have been prescribed one, following the instructions on the device.
   - Call 999 for an ambulance and say you think it is anaphylaxis.
   - If symptoms have not improved after 5 minutes and you have a second adrenaline auto-injector, use the second one.

3. **While waiting for the ambulance, if you can do this safely:**
   - Lie down and raise your legs.
   - If breathing is difficult, raise your shoulders or sit up slowly, then lie down again as soon as you can.
   - Do not stand or walk.
   - If you might vomit, or you become very drowsy but are still breathing, turn onto your side and ask someone to stay with you.

4. **If you have hives or itching only and you are otherwise well, with no facial swelling, no breathing or swallowing symptoms, no faintness and no rapid worsening:**
   - Stop the suspected trigger for now; do not eat more of the food and do not take an extra dose of the medicine.
   - Ask a pharmacist about an antihistamine you can safely take, especially if you have a long-term condition, take other medicines, are pregnant, or the person affected is a child.
   - Get urgent advice from NHS 111 if the rash is spreading, symptoms keep coming back, you feel unwell, or any swelling develops under the skin.

5. **If the reaction followed a medicine:**
   - Do not take another dose of the suspected medicine until you have spoken to a pharmacist, NHS 111, your GP practice, or the prescriber.
   - If the medicine is something you believe you must not stop suddenly, such as medicine for seizures, heart rhythm, transplant treatment, or steroid tablets, get urgent advice before deciding to stop or skip it.
   - If swelling, breathing symptoms, swallowing symptoms, faintness or confusion are present, do not wait for routine advice; call 999.

6. **Keep simple details for the clinician once urgent help is arranged or symptoms are stable:**
   - Take a photo of the rash or swelling if it is safe to do so.
   - Note the time symptoms started.
   - List what you ate, drank or took in the few hours before symptoms began, including over-the-counter medicines and supplements.
   - Keep the food label, packet, bottle, blister pack, or patient leaflet.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact trigger right now.
- You do not need to arrange allergy tests today.
- You do not need to contact a restaurant, manufacturer, or shop immediately; keeping the packaging is enough for now.
- You do not need to decide today whether you will ever take that medicine or eat that food again.
- Updating your GP or pharmacy record with the suspected allergy or adverse reaction can wait until you are safe and urgent symptoms have been dealt with.
- Reporting a suspected medicine or device problem through the MHRA Yellow Card scheme can wait until after you have had any urgent medical advice or treatment.

## Important reassurance
Hives and swelling can feel frightening, and it is reasonable to take them seriously. Calling 999 for possible anaphylaxis is not overreacting when airway, breathing, faintness, confusion or sudden mouth and throat swelling symptoms are present.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on staying safe in the moment and avoiding avoidable repeat exposure. Later decisions about allergy referral, prevention plans, medication alternatives, or food avoidance may need help from a GP, pharmacist, allergy specialist, or other appropriate clinician.

## Important note
This is general information, not a diagnosis or medical or professional advice. If you are unsure whether symptoms are serious, treat them as urgent and seek medical help immediately.
