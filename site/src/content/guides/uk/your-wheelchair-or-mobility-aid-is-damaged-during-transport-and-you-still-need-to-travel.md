# What to do if… your wheelchair or mobility aid is damaged during transport and you still need to travel (UK)

## Short answer
Stop using the aid if it may be unsafe, tell the transport operator straight away, and ask for accessibility support and a safe onward travel plan.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using the aid if anything safety-critical looks damaged, including the frame, wheels, brakes, steering, controls, battery casing, seat, or footplates.
- Don’t force bent parts, tape damaged wiring near a battery, or make a rushed repair that could hide a serious fault.
- Don’t let anyone move a powered chair or scooter again until you have agreed how it should be moved, including powered mode, freewheel mode, and any parts that must not be lifted.
- Don’t leave the station, airport, terminal, stop, or vehicle area without a written report or reference number if you can safely wait for one.
- If you must move on before a full report is made, don’t leave with nothing: get the staff member’s name, time, location, and a quick photo or note of what happened.
- Don’t treat “we can’t help” as the end of the process; ask for the accessibility, special assistance, supervisor, control room, or customer support route.

## What to do now
1. **Make the aid and the area safe first.**  
   If the aid is unstable, stay where you are and ask staff to come to you. If you can move safely, move only a short distance to a safer nearby place. Turn power off if it is a powered device and check for obvious hazards: a wobbling wheel, cracked frame, failed brake, loose seat, damaged battery casing, exposed wiring, leaking fluid, or anything rubbing or dragging.

2. **Tell the operator immediately and ask for accessibility support.**  
   Use the nearest staff member, help point, assistance point, desk, or phone route. Say:  
   “My mobility aid was damaged during transport or handling and I still need to travel. I need help now, a damage report, and a safe onward travel plan.”

3. **Get a basic record before the aid is moved again, if you can.**  
   Ask staff to create an incident or damage report and give you a reference number. Take quick photos of:
   - the damage, close up and from wider away
   - any handling tag, luggage tag, label, ticket, or booking confirmation
   - the make, model, or serial number area, if safely reachable
   - where the aid is now, if that helps show what happened

4. **Ask for a practical way to continue today.**  
   Ask for the specific help you need in the next few hours, such as:
   - staff assistance through the station, airport, terminal, or interchange
   - a ramp, lift, step-free route, or platform-to-platform help
   - help boarding, leaving, or changing service
   - a temporary wheelchair or other equipment if one is available
   - rebooking to an accessible service if your original journey is no longer workable
   - accessible onward transport if the operator can arrange it

5. **If this happened on the railway, ask station staff to arrange Passenger Assist for the rest of the journey.**  
   Ask for help with your next connection, boarding, alighting, ramps, and getting to the exit or next train. Passenger Assist advance requests can be made up to 2 hours before the journey, and you can also ask for last-minute “turn up and go” help at a staffed station or help point. Last-minute help may depend on staff and conditions at the time, so ask staff to tell you clearly what they can arrange now.

6. **If this happened during air travel, speak to both the airline and airport assistance route.**  
   Use the nearest assistance point, special assistance desk, airline desk, ground handling staff, or help point. For flights covered by UK rules, if a wheelchair or other mobility equipment is lost or damaged during travel, speak with the airline and airport immediately so they can make arrangements. This can include temporary equipment, onward transport, repairs, and there may be compensation duties later. Ask them to log the damage now, give you a written reference, and explain what they can provide so you are not left unable to move safely.

7. **If this happened on a bus, coach, or local transport service, ask for an incident record and immediate travel help.**  
   Ask the driver, station staff, terminal staff, or operator contact route to record the damage and help you continue safely. This may mean assistance, an accessible re-route, a later accessible service, or a safe place to wait while options are checked. If it happened on a London bus or at Victoria Coach Station, TfL publishes passenger rights covering disabled access and, where TfL is responsible, loss or damage to mobility equipment.

8. **If you cannot continue safely, change the goal to a safe stop.**  
   Ask staff to help you get to a safe waiting place and to arrange an accessible alternative, such as a later accessible service or an accessible taxi route, rather than pushing on with an unsafe device.

## What can wait
- Deciding exactly who is at fault.
- Arguing about compensation, legal wording, or complaint escalation.
- Getting repair quotes, unless the operator needs one later.
- Insurance calls, social posts, or long email threads.
- Deciding whether to make a formal complaint after the immediate journey is safe.

## Important reassurance
A damaged mobility aid can turn a normal journey into an immediate safety problem. It is reasonable to ask for help, documentation, and a workable plan before the aid is handled again or before you continue travelling.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on safety and continuing travel. Later decisions about repair, replacement, compensation, insurance, or complaints may need specialist help or advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, or professional advice. What is immediately available can vary by operator, location, staffing, equipment, and the transport rules that apply, but you can still ask for accessibility help now, a written incident record, and a safe onward travel plan.
