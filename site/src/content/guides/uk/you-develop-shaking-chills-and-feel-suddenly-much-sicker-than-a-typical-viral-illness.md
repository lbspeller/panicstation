# What to do if… you develop shaking chills and feel suddenly much sicker than a typical viral illness (UK)

## Short answer
Seek a same-day medical assessment immediately, as shaking chills (rigors) can indicate a serious bacterial infection or sepsis. Call **999** or go to **A&E** if you have any red-flag symptoms such as confusion, difficulty breathing, or a non-fading rash.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you are shivering violently, feeling faint, or confused.
- Do not wait for a "normal" viral recovery time if your symptoms have suddenly taken a sharp turn for the worse.
- Do not take antibiotics that were not specifically prescribed for this current illness.
- Do not assume a "shivering fit" is just a standard fever; rigors are often a marker of significant infection.
- Do not ignore a lack of urination; passing no urine all day is a critical warning sign.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency red flags immediately and call 999 or go to A&E if you notice:**
   - Confusion, slurred speech, or acting in a way that doesn't make sense.
   - Blue, grey, pale, or blotchy skin, lips, or tongue (on brown or black skin, check the palms of the hands or soles of the feet).
   - Difficulty breathing, gasping for air, or breathing much faster than usual.
   - A rash that does not fade when you roll a clear glass over it.
   - Feeling like you are going to die or a sense of extreme "impending doom."
2. **If you do not have the emergency signs above, contact NHS 111 immediately for an urgent assessment:**
   - Call 111 or use 111 online and state clearly: "I am having shaking chills and I feel much sicker than a normal viral illness."
   - Mention specifically if you have not passed urine all day.
   - Report if you are vomiting and cannot keep any fluids down.
3. **Ask a healthcare professional directly: "Could it be sepsis?"**
   - This is a standard clinical prompt in the UK that ensures clinicians specifically screen for life-threatening infection.
4. **Prepare a 60-second summary of your status for the clinician:**
   - **Timing:** When the shaking started and how quickly you worsened.
   - **Vitals:** Your temperature if known, and whether you feel abnormally cold to the touch.
   - **Output:** When you last passed urine and what color it was.
   - **History:** Any recent surgeries, dental work, wounds, or if you are pregnant or immunocompromised.
5. **Ask someone to stay with you until you are seen by a medic.**
   - If you are alone, call a friend or neighbor to stay on the line or come to your door, as your condition could change rapidly.
6. **Keep your current medications and any recent prescriptions nearby.**
   - Ensure you have a list or the physical boxes ready to show the ambulance crew or hospital staff, including any paracetamol or ibuprofen you have taken today.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the source of the infection (e.g., urinary, chest, or blood) yourself.
- You do not need to wait for a GP appointment if the GP surgery is closed or cannot see you within the hour; use 111, A&E, or 999 as appropriate.
- You do not need to worry about child care or work logistics until you have initiated the medical contact.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel very frightened when your body shakes uncontrollably. Shivering is a physical reflex to a rising internal "thermostat" setting during infection. Seeking help now is the most effective way to manage this and prevent the situation from escalating.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage guidance for acute, severe illness. It does not replace clinical diagnosis or long-term management of chronic conditions.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is based on UK health protocols as of 2026. Sepsis and severe infections are medical emergencies; if you are in doubt, always seek professional medical help immediately.
