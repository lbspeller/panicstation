# What to do if… you are suddenly excluded from key meetings and decisions at work (UK)

## Short answer
Pause, make a factual record of what has changed, and ask your line manager in writing to confirm your current role, responsibilities, and which meetings or decisions you should be included in.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send an angry or accusatory message to a large group or public channel.
- Do not resign, threaten to resign, or force a confrontation while you are still unclear about what is happening.
- Do not accuse specific people of discrimination, retaliation, or bullying unless you have facts you can point to.
- Do not delete emails, messages, meeting invites, or calendar records.
- Do not copy, export, or forward confidential work material to personal accounts unless your workplace policy clearly allows it.
- Do not record conversations while panicked or if workplace policy forbids it; make a dated written note instead.

## What to do now
1. **Make a simple timeline.** Write down when the exclusion started, which recurring meetings stopped, which decisions you found out about late, whether access was removed, and whether responsibilities changed.
2. **Keep a clean record without breaching policy.** Keep a dated log and retain items you personally received where your workplace rules allow it, such as invite removals, written task changes, or messages about withdrawn work. If you are unsure, do not export anything; write down dates, meeting names, and who was involved.
3. **Send a calm note to your line manager.** Ask them to confirm your current responsibilities, decision rights, named meetings you should attend, whether any change is temporary, and what communication route you should use for work-critical updates.
4. **Ask for decision handoffs.** For decisions affecting your work, request a short written recap covering what changed, who owns the decision, and what you need to do next.
5. **Protect your performance record.** If exclusion blocks delivery, write it plainly: “I cannot complete X without Y decision or meeting update. Please confirm the owner and next step by [date].”
6. **Check the grievance route before you need it.** Find your employer’s written grievance procedure in the handbook, contract pack, or intranet. Acas guidance says it is usually sensible to try informal resolution first unless the issue is serious or informal steps are not suitable.
7. **Know the accompaniment point.** If you later attend a formal grievance meeting about an employer duty owed to you, you can generally make a reasonable request to be accompanied by a workplace colleague, a certified trade union representative, or an official employed by a trade union.
8. **Escalate one step if clarity does not come.** If your manager does not answer or the exclusion continues, contact HR or the designated people team with your timeline and ask what process will confirm your role expectations and restore work-critical communication.
9. **If you suspect bullying, discrimination, or retaliation, keep linked notes separate.** Record the date you raised a concern, requested an adjustment, joined union activity, made a complaint, or did anything else you think may be connected, then record what changed afterwards.
10. **Get independent guidance if it escalates.** If you are in a union, contact them. You can also contact Acas for guidance on workplace processes and options.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to quit, start legal action, or confront colleagues individually.
- You do not need to write a long statement now; capture key examples and keep them date-stamped.
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a subject access request for personal data your employer holds about you. That may be useful later, but it can also escalate tension, so first focus on getting role clarity and preserving a basic record.
- You do not need to prove motive today. Start with what changed, when it changed, and how it affects your work.

## Important reassurance
Being excluded can feel humiliating and alarming, but it may also come from a mistake, a reorganisation, unclear ownership, or poor communication. Your immediate job is to slow the situation down, get expectations in writing, and avoid being judged on decisions or updates you were not included in.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the situation becomes a formal dispute involving grievance, disciplinary action, redundancy, discrimination, whistleblowing, dismissal risk, or a settlement discussion, you may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Workplace rights and procedures depend on your contract, workplace policies, and the specific facts.
