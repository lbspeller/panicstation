# What to do if… you suspect you are being followed while driving and you are approaching home (UK)

## Short answer
Do not drive to your home or stop outside it. Keep driving to a safer, public, staffed place and call 999 if you feel at risk.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pull into your driveway, garage, or usual parking spot “just to see what happens”.
- Do not stop somewhere quiet, such as a side street, lay-by, or near your home, to check your phone or look behind you.
- Do not confront the other driver, signal aggressively, or get out of your car to argue or take photos.
- Do not try to “lose them” by speeding, running lights, or making sudden risky turns.
- Do not lead the car to anyone else’s home, including friends, family, or a partner.
- Do not hold and use a phone while driving. Ask a passenger to call if there is one, use hands-free only if you can stay in proper control, or stop somewhere safe first.

## What to do now
1. **Do not go home. Keep moving on main roads.** Stay on well-lit, busier routes. Keep doors locked and windows up.
2. **Do a quick, safe pattern check without going home.** If it is safe, make a simple loop that still keeps you on main roads, such as taking an extra circuit at a roundabout. If the same vehicle mirrors you, treat it as real and act quickly.
3. **Call the police.**
   - **If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.** Say: “I think I’m being followed in my car. I’m not going home. I need help.”
   - Share road names, junctions, direction of travel, your car description, and the other vehicle’s description or number plate only if you can do so safely.
   - **If speaking would put you at risk or you cannot speak:** call 999 and follow the Silent Solution prompt. On a mobile, this usually means pressing **55** when prompted so the call can be put through to police.
   - **If there is no immediate danger once you are safely stopped, use 101** to report or update the police.
4. **Go to a help location, not your home.** Prefer a busy, staffed place with good lighting, such as a 24-hour petrol station, large supermarket, or hospital A&E entrance. A police station can also be suitable if you know it is nearby and accessible.
5. **If you are speaking to 999 or 101, ask where they want you to go.** Follow the call handler’s directions if they give you a safer route or place to stop.
6. **If you stop, stay in the car unless police or staff tell you otherwise.** Park under lights near an entrance. Keep doors locked. Be ready to move only if you can do so safely and the police or staff have not told you to stay put.
7. **If the other vehicle tries to make you pull over and you are not sure it is police, prioritise a public stop.** Drive steadily, do not speed, and head to a well-lit public place. If safe, call 999, or ask a passenger to call, to say you are being signalled to stop and you are unsure the vehicle is genuine.
8. **Once you are safe, write down what you remember.** Note the time, route, number plate or vehicle description, and any distinctive details. Do this only after you are stopped somewhere safe.

## What can wait
- You do not need to be 100% sure you are being followed before calling for help.
- You do not need to gather proof right now, such as photos, videos, or social posts.
- You do not need to decide tonight whether it was stalking, road rage, mistaken identity, or a misunderstanding. The priority is not leading the vehicle to your home and getting to safety.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaky, angry, or embarrassed is a normal stress response. Choosing the cautious option, by not going home, calling police if you feel at risk, and heading somewhere public and staffed, is reasonable even if it turns out to be nothing.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, for the next few minutes, to avoid being led into a vulnerable spot and to bring help into the situation. If this happens more than once, later decisions may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, safety, driving, or professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 999 and follow the call handler’s instructions. Only stop where it is safe and legal to do so, and avoid using a hand-held phone while driving.
