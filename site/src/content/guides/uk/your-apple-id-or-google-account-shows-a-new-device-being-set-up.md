# What to do if… your Apple ID or Google Account shows a new device being set up (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible account takeover: deny the sign-in if you can, then change the password from a device you already trust. After that, remove any device, session, recovery detail, forwarding rule, or connected app you do not recognise.

## Do not do these things
- Do not tap “Approve” or “Allow” on any prompt you did not start.
- Do not use links in alert emails or texts to “secure your account”; go through Settings, the app, or the account website you type yourself.
- Do not reuse an old password or a small variation of one.
- Do not try lots of different fixes on several devices at once; focus on the account that sent the alert.
- Do not assume two-step verification has handled it; still check devices, sessions, recovery details, and email forwarding.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a trusted device.**
   - Use your own phone or computer, not a shared, borrowed, or public device.
   - Open Apple or Google settings directly, rather than following a link from a message.

2. **Deny the sign-in prompt if it is still on screen.**
   - **Apple:** choose **Don’t Allow** if you see a sign-in notification you do not recognise.
   - **Google:** tap **No** or **No, it’s not me** if the prompt says the sign-in is not yours.

3. **Change the password right away.**
   - **Apple Account:** change your password from Settings on your iPhone, iPad, or Mac, or from the official Apple Account page.
   - **Google Account:** change your password from your Google Account security settings.
   - Use a brand-new password you have not used anywhere else.

4. **Remove unknown devices and sessions.**
   - **Apple:** check your Apple Account device list and remove anything you do not recognise.
   - **Google:** go to Security, or Security & sign-in, then **Your devices → Manage all devices** and sign out of anything you do not recognise.

5. **Check recovery details before the attacker can use them.**
   - **Apple:** check that trusted phone numbers, trusted devices, email addresses, and other sign-in details are yours.
   - **Google:** check that the recovery email and recovery phone are yours.
   - Remove any phone number, email address, device, or sign-in method you did not add.

6. **Check for hidden continued access.**
   - In Gmail, check for unfamiliar forwarding and filters that could send copies of your email elsewhere.
   - Check connected third-party apps and services, then remove anything you do not recognise.
   - If your mobile service suddenly stopped working, or you get SIM or number-change messages, contact your mobile network provider promptly.

7. **Check money-linked activity.**
   - Look at recent purchases, subscriptions, and saved payment methods connected to the Apple or Google account.
   - If you see a payment you did not authorise, contact your bank or card provider immediately.

8. **Use official recovery if you cannot regain control.**
   - Use Apple or Google’s official account recovery flow from a trusted device.
   - Avoid sponsored or lookalike search results; use the provider’s app, settings route, or official help pages.

9. **Report UK fraud or cyber crime if money, identity, or account access has been taken.**
   - If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud.
   - If you live in Scotland, or the crime happened in Scotland, contact Police Scotland on 101 for non-emergency reporting.
   - Use 999 only if there is an immediate emergency.
   - Keep a simple note of the time of the alert, what changed, and screenshots if you can take them safely.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out how they got in right now.
- You do not need to message the unknown device or person.
- You do not need to reset every device today unless the provider tells you to or you keep seeing re-entry.
- You do not need to decide about long-term reporting before you have secured the account, unless money is actively leaving your accounts.

## Important reassurance
A new-device alert can feel urgent because it is designed to make you act quickly. A few focused steps can often stop the immediate sign-in, remove access, and make it harder for someone to get back in.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to interrupt a possible in-progress sign-in and reduce immediate harm. Later decisions may need help from Apple, Google, your mobile network provider, your bank, the police reporting service, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. If you are unsure whether a prompt or message is genuine, close it or deny it, then go directly to your account settings from a trusted device.
