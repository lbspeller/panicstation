# What to do if… your arm becomes swollen or heavy after an IV, line, or injection (UK)

## Short answer
Treat new one-arm swelling or heaviness after an IV, line, or injection as urgent until a clinician has assessed it. Call 999 now if you have severe breathing difficulty, chest or upper back pain, fainting or collapse, a very fast heartbeat, or the arm or hand is cold, blue or pale, very numb, weak, severely painful, or rapidly worsening.

## Do not do these things
- Do not firmly massage, knead, or try to “rub out” the swelling.
- Do not ignore spreading swelling, redness, warmth, increasing pain, blistering, leaking fluid, pus, or a hard sore line along a vein.
- Do not leave rings, watches, tight sleeves, or tight bandages on a swelling hand or arm.
- Do not flush, inject into, or keep using an IV, cannula, or line that may be causing swelling.
- Do not put ice or heat directly on the skin.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you feel faint, short of breath, have chest pain, or feel very unwell.

## What to do now
1. Check for emergency warning signs. Call 999 now if you have severe breathing difficulty, chest or upper back pain, fainting or collapse, a very fast heartbeat, or if the arm or hand becomes cold, blue or pale, very numb, weak, severely painful, or rapidly more swollen.

2. If you have arm swelling plus sudden breathlessness, chest pain, or collapse, treat it as an emergency and call 999. If you are coughing blood or suddenly short of breath but are not sure it is an emergency, get urgent medical advice now through NHS 111 or 999 if symptoms are severe.

3. If any IV, cannula, or line is still in place, do not flush it, inject into it, or use it. Contact the ward, clinic, nurse, line team, or scanning department that placed it and say: “My arm is swollen and heavy after the IV, line, or injection.” Mention burning, tightness, leaking fluid, blistering, redness, warmth, numbness, or colour change.

4. If the line is already out and this is new one-sided swelling or heaviness, get urgent medical advice today. Use NHS 111, an urgent GP appointment, the clinic that treated you, or A&E if symptoms are worsening or you feel very unwell.

5. Remove rings, watches, bracelets, and tight clothing from that arm or hand now, before swelling increases.

6. Raise the arm while you are arranging help. Keep the hand above the elbow and, if comfortable, the elbow above heart level. Keep fingers gently moving if you can, but avoid heavy use of the arm.

7. If this followed a scan or infusion where contrast, saline, or another fluid leaked into the tissues, follow the discharge instructions you were given. If you were told it was a contrast leak, a cold pack wrapped in cloth for up to 15 minutes at a time is commonly advised; do not put ice directly on the skin, and stop if pain or skin changes worsen.

8. If there is redness or swelling that is spreading, mark the outer edge with a pen and write the time next to it. Take a photo if the arm is changing quickly, so clinicians can see the progression.

9. Seek urgent help if you develop fever, rigors, pus, spreading warmth or redness, worsening pain, new blisters, skin breakdown, pins and needles, altered sensation, or a change in arm or hand colour.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this is a clot, phlebitis, infection, infiltration, or extravasation.
- You do not need to write a complaint, chase records, or work out who is responsible right now.
- You do not need to keep checking the arm every minute. Remove tight items, raise the arm, arrange urgent advice, and re-check if symptoms change.

## Important reassurance
A swollen, heavy arm after an IV, line, or injection can feel frightening, especially when it is only on one side. Many causes are treatable, and getting checked early is the safest way to reduce the chance of complications.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to help you stay safer and avoid common mistakes. Later decisions about tests, treatment, line care, medicines, or follow-up may need help from a doctor, nurse, pharmacist, radiology team, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not medical advice, a diagnosis, or a substitute for urgent assessment by a qualified professional.
