# What to do if… your heart suddenly starts racing or pounding while you are at rest (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all physical activity, sit or lie down immediately, and call 999 if you experience chest pain, shortness of breath, severe dizziness, or fainting.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive yourself to a hospital or walk to a clinic.
- Do not perform strenuous exercise to try and reset your heart rate.
- Do not consume caffeine, nicotine, alcohol, or energy drinks.
- Do not take extra doses of heart medication or aspirin unless specifically instructed by a 999 operator.
- Do not take new over-the-counter cold or flu medicines as these can contain stimulants.
- Do not ignore the episode if it is new, even if the symptoms eventually stop.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe seated or lying position immediately.** Sit in a chair with back support or lie flat on the floor if you feel lightheaded.
2. **Check for emergency red-flag symptoms and call 999 if any are present.**
   - Chest pain, tightness, or pressure.
   - Pain spreading to your arms, neck, jaw, or back.
   - Sudden or severe shortness of breath.
   - Feeling faint, blacking out, or collapsing.
   - New or sudden sweating, nausea, or vomiting.
3. **Capture the specific details of the episode to tell a clinician.**
   - Note the exact time the racing or pounding began.
   - Feel your pulse at your wrist or neck to check if the rhythm is steady or "jumping around."
   - Count your pulse for 30 seconds and write down the number.
   - If you use a wearable heart monitor or smartwatch, save the current reading or ECG strip.
4. **Reduce all physical and environmental strain.**
   - Loosen any tight clothing around your neck or chest.
   - Keep your current medications or a written prescription list nearby for paramedics to see.
   - Stay still and avoid standing up or moving quickly until the heart rate settles.
5. **Contact NHS 111 if the episode continues without red flags.**
   - Use 111 online or call 111 if the racing heart persists for more than a few minutes but you do not have chest pain or breathing difficulties.
6. **Use only clinician-approved vagal manoeuvres if you have been previously trained.**
   - If a cardiologist or GP has taught you a specific technique for diagnosed SVT or similar conditions, use it now only if you feel otherwise stable.
   - Do not attempt techniques you have seen online if they have not been prescribed for you.
7. **Arrange a follow-up even if the symptoms stop.**
   - Contact your GP surgery for an urgent appointment if this is the first time this has happened at rest.
   - Seek a review if the episode felt different from previous palpitations, such as being much faster or accompanied by new symptoms.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the underlying cause of the palpitation yourself.
- You do not need to decide on long-term lifestyle changes or treatment plans right now.
- You do not need to keep checking your pulse once you have recorded the initial rate and rhythm.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of dread or anxiety when your heart behaves unusually at rest. This physical sensation is a natural response to a sudden change in heart rhythm and does not always indicate a permanent or life-threatening issue.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute episode of resting heart palpitations. It does not replace a clinical diagnosis or long-term management plan by a healthcare professional.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only. If you believe you are experiencing a medical emergency, or if symptoms are worsening rapidly, call 999 immediately.
