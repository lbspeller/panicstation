# What to do if… you think you are being followed or stalked after repeated coincidental encounters (UK)

## Short answer
Move to a safer public place, tell one trusted person what is happening, and call 999 now if you feel in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to “test” them by going somewhere isolated or changing plans in a risky way.
- Do not confront them alone or agree to talk somewhere private.
- Do not post about the situation in real time, because it may reveal your location or routine.
- Do not delete messages, call logs, photos, screenshots, unwanted gifts, or notes that may later help show a pattern.
- Do not delay asking for help because you are unsure whether it “counts” as stalking.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** Go inside a busy shop, café, station, workplace reception, or other place with staff. If you are driving, do not go straight home if you think you are being followed; go to a well-lit public place such as a supermarket, petrol station, police station, or staffed transport hub.

2. **Call 999 if you feel in immediate danger.** If you can, say where you are and that you are worried you are being followed or stalked. If you cannot speak on a mobile, call 999, listen to the operator, and press 55 or tap when prompted. If you cannot speak on a landline, call 999, stay on the line, and follow any instructions you can.

3. **Tell one person right now.** Call or text a trusted friend, family member, colleague, neighbour, or staff member. Share where you are, what you have noticed, what the person or vehicle looks like, and what you are doing next.

4. **Ask for a practical barrier at the place you are in.** Ask staff, reception, security, building management, or transport staff to stay with you, walk you to transport, note the person or vehicle description, or avoid letting unknown visitors through to you.

5. **Start a simple incident log.** Write down the date, time, exact location, what happened, what the person looked like, any vehicle details, witnesses, and nearby CCTV locations. Use paper or a different device if you are worried your phone or accounts may be monitored.

6. **Keep possible evidence without investigating.** Save messages, call logs, screenshots, photos, voicemails, letters, parcels, or unwanted gifts. Do not put yourself at risk to collect more.

7. **Reduce easy location leaks for today.** Turn off location sharing you do not need, check who can see your posts or stories, and avoid posting your route or live location. If you suspect your phone or account is compromised, use a different device or trusted person’s phone to contact help.

8. **Contact police when you are not in immediate danger.** Call 101 or report online through your local police force. Say you are worried about stalking because of repeated encounters and any unwanted contact. Ask for an incident number and how to share your log or saved evidence. If you are in England or Wales, you can ask whether police can consider protective options such as a Stalking Protection Order.

9. **Contact a specialist stalking or victim-support service.** You do not have to report to police before asking for support. The National Stalking Helpline can give stalking-specific advice. Victim Support can also provide support, including by phone and live chat. Paladin may be relevant for higher-risk stalking advocacy, especially in England and Wales.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a formal statement, go to court, move home, change job, or confront the person.
- You do not need to work out their motive.
- You do not need to prove it is stalking before asking for help or recording what has happened.
- You do not need to make every account, device, route, and routine perfect right now; start with immediate safety, telling someone, and preserving what you already have.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to take repeated unwanted “coincidences” seriously. Writing down the pattern and telling someone early can help you make safer next steps without having to decide everything at once.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you get safer, reduce panic, and preserve a basic record. Later decisions about police reports, protective measures, housing, work, education, devices, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel at risk now, prioritise immediate safety and contact emergency services.
