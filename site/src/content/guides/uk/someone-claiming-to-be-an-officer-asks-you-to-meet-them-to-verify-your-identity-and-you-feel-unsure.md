# What to do if… someone claiming to be an officer asks you to meet them to “verify your identity” and you feel unsure (UK)

## Short answer
End the contact immediately without agreeing to meet or sharing any documents. Call the police non-emergency number 101 from a different phone to verify the individual’s identity and the necessity of the request.

## Do not do these things
- Don't go to any meeting location—including public ones—before you have verified the request through official channels.
- Don't call back using any number provided by the person or shown on your caller ID.
- Don't share photos of your passport, driving licence, or biometric residence permit via text, email, or messaging apps.
- Don't provide "liveness" checks (such as a selfie or video of yourself holding ID) to an unverified contact.
- Don't allow anyone into your home to "collect" or "inspect" your identification documents.
- Don't comply with threats of immediate arrest or fines for "refusing to cooperate" with a verification request.

## What to do now
1. **End the interaction immediately.** Tell the person: "I will verify this request via 101 before proceeding." Hang up the phone or stop replying to messages.
2. **Clear your phone line.** If you were called on a landline, wait at least five minutes or use a different phone (such as a mobile) to make your next call, as fraudsters can keep lines open to intercept your next dial.
3. **Obtain the person's professional details.** If you are still in contact, ask only for their **full name, rank, collar number, and the force they represent**. Do not provide any of your own information in return.
4. **Call 101 to verify.** Dial **101** and ask the operator to confirm whether the named individual is a genuine officer and if there is a recorded reason for them to contact you for "identity verification."
   - If the person is at your door, keep it locked and call **101** while they wait outside.
   - If you feel physically threatened or in immediate danger, call **999**.
5. **Request verification via radio if in Scotland.** For lone officers in Scotland, you can ask them to **provide verification via their police radio**.
   - The officer can put their radio on loudspeaker so a control room operator can confirm their identity and the reason for the interaction.
6. **Use official GOV.UK channels for legitimate ID checks.** Note that official government identity verification (such as for Companies House or DBS) is typically handled via **GOV.UK One Login** or at a **Post Office branch**, not via informal meetings with officers.
7. **Report the suspicious contact.**
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** Report the incident to **Report Fraud** online at reportfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040.
   - **Scotland:** Report the incident to **Police Scotland via 101**.
8. **Alert your bank if you shared details.** If you have already provided bank details or photos of your ID, **contact your bank's fraud department immediately** using the number on the back of your card.

## What can wait
- You do not need to attend a meeting or "verify" anything within the first hour of contact.
- You do not need to explain why you are being "difficult" or "uncooperative" while you perform verification.
- You do not need to decide whether to provide evidence or statements until the officer’s identity is confirmed.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of urgency or fear when someone claiming to be in authority makes a demand. This reaction is expected and is often exploited by impersonators. Taking a moment to pause and verify through a known, trusted channel like 101 is a standard and supported safety procedure.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for suspicious identity verification requests only. If the contact is verified as genuine, you may need to seek legal advice regarding your obligations or rights during a police interaction.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Police procedures can vary by force and specific legal circumstances.
