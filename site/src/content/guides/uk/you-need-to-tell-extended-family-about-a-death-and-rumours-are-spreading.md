# What to do if… you need to tell extended family about a death and rumours are spreading (UK)

## Short answer
Pause the spread by sending one clear holding message from one agreed person. Tell the closest people directly first, and do not share unconfirmed details just to quiet rumours.

## Do not do these things
- Do not correct rumours by arguing in public group chats or on social media.
- Do not guess the cause of death or share unconfirmed details to stop speculation.
- Do not send the news by broadcast to someone who should hear it one-to-one unless there is no safe alternative.
- Do not feel forced to answer questions immediately, especially about circumstances, medical details, or family conflict.
- Do not post publicly just to get ahead of it if the closest people have not been told yet.
- Do not pass on private details that the immediate family has not agreed to share.

## What to do now
1. **Choose one spokesperson and one backup for now.** Decide who will send the first message and who will handle follow-ups so the rest of you are not pulled into dozens of parallel conversations.
2. **Write a short holding message you can reuse.** Keep it factual and minimal. For example:
   - “I’m very sorry to tell you that [Name] died on [date]. We’re focusing on immediate family right now. We’ll share confirmed details about arrangements when we can. Please don’t share this further yet.”
3. **Tell the closest people first, directly.** Start with those most likely to be devastated or blindsided, such as a partner, children, parents, siblings, or very close friends, where safe and appropriate. Use a phone call, voice note, text, or a short message asking them to contact you when they can.
4. **Create a simple tell list and tell tree.** Split extended family into a few branches, such as one side of the family, cousins, or family friends. Ask one person in each branch to contact people and report back with: “Told”, “Not reached”, or “Needs a call”.
5. **Stop the rumour engine with one consistent line.** When asked for details you cannot confirm, repeat:
   - “We don’t have confirmed information we can share yet.”
   - “If anything changes, [Name of spokesperson] will update everyone.”
6. **Use practical boundaries that reduce forwarding.**
   - Use smaller message groups rather than one huge chat.
   - If someone often forwards news quickly, contact them one-to-one and say: “Please don’t share this yet.”
   - Ask branch contacts not to add extra details or guesses.
7. **If rumours include harmful or false claims, correct once, calmly, and stop.** Send one correction from the spokesperson, then step away. For example:
   - “For clarity: the family is not sharing a cause of death at this time. Please stop speculating.”
8. **Protect the immediate family’s privacy.** Agree what is not for group chat, such as medical details, police involvement, family disputes, who found the person, or where they died.
9. **Leave official notifications until there is a safer pause.** If the person who died was living in England, Scotland, or Wales, the registrar can explain Tell Us Once when the death is registered and may complete it with you or give you a unique reference number. Tell Us Once is not used if the person was living in Northern Ireland, where separate official routes apply.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a perfect announcement, obituary, or social media post now.
- You do not need to decide funeral details, attendance rules, or who is allowed to know everything today.
- You do not need to respond to every message, call, or opinion in the first day or two.
- You do not need to resolve family tensions or correct every minor inaccuracy right now.
- You do not need to complete official notifications before the closest people have been told, unless there is an immediate practical reason.

## Important reassurance
When rumours spread, it can feel like you are failing the person who died or the closest family. You are not. In early bereavement, clarity and kindness matter more than completeness. A short, consistent message and a simple contact plan are enough for now.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for stabilising communication in the first hours and days. Later decisions about family dynamics, public notices, funeral arrangements, official notifications, or disputed information may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are unsure what is safe to share, especially where there is police involvement, safeguarding concern, media interest, or intense family conflict, keep details minimal and focus on direct, private contact until you have clearer information.
