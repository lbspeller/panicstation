# What to do if… a loved one’s phone is locked after they die and you need to reach their contacts (UK)

## Short answer
Do not guess the passcode, reset the phone, or cancel the mobile number yet. Preserve the phone and number, use any emergency contacts or visible notifications, and ask the mobile provider’s bereavement team how to keep the number reachable while you notify people.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t repeatedly guess the passcode. It can cause lockouts and, depending on the device and settings, may risk data loss.
- Don’t factory reset, restore, or erase the phone to “get in”. That can remove the contacts, messages, and call history you are trying to preserve.
- Don’t pay an unlocking shop, bypass service, or online tool. These routes carry a high scam risk and may cause permanent data loss.
- Don’t cancel the mobile contract or stop topping up the SIM until you have asked how to keep the number reachable.
- Don’t post their phone number, address, date of birth, or other personal details publicly to find people.
- Don’t access email, cloud accounts, or voicemail by guessing passwords or bypassing security.

## What to do now
1. **Protect the phone and SIM**
   - Put the phone somewhere safe and keep it charged.
   - If the phone is already on, generally avoid switching it off unless you need to for safety. Restarting can sometimes make security prompts stricter.
   - Keep the SIM in place unless the mobile provider tells you otherwise.
   - Gather any useful account details you can find separately, such as a bill, SIM packaging, device receipt, account number, or written instructions.

2. **Check what is visible without unlocking**
   - Look at the lock screen for missed calls, messages, or notification previews that show names or numbers.
   - Do not clear notifications. Write down visible names, numbers, dates, and times.
   - On an iPhone, tap **Emergency** and look for **Medical ID** if it was set up to show when locked.
   - On some Android phones, tap the emergency option and look for emergency information or emergency contacts if they were set up to show when locked.
   - If you find one trusted emergency contact, call them from your own phone and ask whether they can help pass the message to others.

3. **Keep the number working as an inbox**
   - Keep the mobile service active if you can, because people may still call or text the number they already know.
   - If calls come in, answer from the phone only if you can do so without unlocking or bypassing anything.
   - If you can legitimately access voicemail because you already know the PIN or the provider confirms a route, write down caller names, numbers, and key messages.
   - If you cannot access voicemail, do not guess the PIN. Ask the provider’s bereavement team what can be done.

4. **Contact the mobile provider’s bereavement team**
   - Ask how to keep the number active temporarily while you notify people.
   - Ask whether the account can be transferred, whether responsibility for the line can be changed, or whether the number can be kept or moved.
   - Ask what proof they need. Providers commonly ask for the account holder’s name, address, mobile number, your contact details, and evidence of the death.
   - If you do not know the provider, check the SIM, a bill, bank statement, email, or the network name shown on the phone.

5. **Use official Apple or Google routes only**
   - If it is an iPhone and a Legacy Contact was set up, Apple’s Digital Legacy process uses the access key and proof of death to request access.
   - If no Apple Legacy Contact exists, Apple has a formal process to request access to or deletion of a deceased person’s Apple Account data, but it may require legal documentation.
   - Apple cannot remove the passcode from a locked iPhone without erasing the device, so do not treat Apple support as a way to unlock the phone itself.
   - If it is an Android phone linked to a Google account, use Google’s deceased-user request process. Google may provide account content in some circumstances, but it will not provide passwords or login details.

6. **Build a contact list without unlocking the phone**
   - Check address books, diaries, recent post, Christmas cards, club letters, workplace paperwork, and funeral-related paperwork.
   - If there is another device already signed in to email or messages, use it only if you are authorised and it is accessible without bypassing security.
   - Send a short message to a few trusted people you can identify and ask them to pass the news on privately.
   - If a funeral director is involved, ask whether they can act as a contact point for people who hear the news and need details.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to close accounts, memorialise social media, deal with the whole digital estate, or sort every contact. You can start by keeping the number reachable, noting visible callers, and asking one or two trusted people to help spread the message privately. Probate, account closures, and longer digital-estate decisions can usually wait unless there is an urgent fraud, safety, or safeguarding concern.

## Important reassurance
A locked phone after a death is a common and distressing problem. Not being able to unlock it does not mean you have failed; the safest first step is to avoid anything irreversible and use the number, provider, emergency contacts, and paper trails to buy time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on preserving information and contacting people. Later decisions about the estate, disputed access, business accounts, probate, or high-value digital accounts may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. Mobile providers, Apple, Google, and other services have their own rules and may require proof of identity, proof of death, and evidence of authority before they act.
