# What to do if… a stranger claims you damaged their car and insists you pull into a quieter location to talk (UK)

## Short answer
Do not move your vehicle to an isolated or quiet area. Stay in a well-lit public space with your doors locked and call 999 immediately if you feel threatened, blocked, or followed.

## Do not do these things
- Don't follow the other driver to side streets, industrial estates, or "round the back" of buildings.
- Don't exit your vehicle if the other party is aggressive or if the environment feels unsafe.
- Don't hand over your physical driving licence or allow the stranger to take photos of your personal documents.
- Don't agree to pay cash at the roadside or admit liability under pressure.
- Don't drive directly to your home address if you believe the other vehicle is following you.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritise a safe, public location.** If you are in an isolated spot, drive to the nearest busy, well-lit area such as a petrol station, supermarket forecourt, or a main road with foot traffic.
2. **Secure your vehicle.** Once stopped in a public place, keep your engine running (if safe to do so), lock all doors, and keep windows closed or opened only a small gap for communication.
3. **Assess the threat level for emergency services.** 
   - Call **999** if the person is trying to open your door, blocking your exit, acting violently, or if you are being followed.
   - Call **101** if you are in a safe public place and want to report the confrontation or the suspected scam without immediate danger.
4. **Communicate through the window.** State clearly and firmly: "I am happy to exchange details here in this public place. I will not be moving to another location."
5. **Exchange only the legally required information.** Under Section 170 of the Road Traffic Act 1988, if damage or injury has occurred, you must provide:
   - Your name and address.
   - The name and address of the vehicle owner (if different).
   - The vehicle registration number.
   - Insurance details are only legally required at the scene if someone is injured.
6. **Gather evidence discreetly.** Note the make, model, colour, and registration of the other vehicle. If safe, use a smartphone to record the interaction or take photos of any alleged damage from inside the car. 
7. **Leave the scene if you feel unsafe.** If the other driver becomes physically aggressive or you cannot safely exchange details, drive to the nearest police station.
8. **Report the incident as soon as possible.** If you were unable to exchange details at the scene due to safety concerns, you must report the accident to a police station or a constable as soon as is reasonably practicable and, in any case, within 24 hours.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine fault or liability at the roadside.
- You do not need to contact your insurance company until you are in a safe, permanent location.
- You do not need to provide phone numbers, email addresses, or employer details.
- You do not need to inspect the damage thoroughly until the other party has left and you are in a secure environment.

## Important reassurance
It is a common tactic for scammers to use intimidation to move victims to isolated areas. Refusing to move to a quiet location is a standard safety protocol, not a sign of guilt or "fleeing." Feeling shaken or defensive in a roadside confrontation is a natural response.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate safety and stabilization steps for roadside confrontations and potential "crash for cash" scenarios. It does not replace legal advice or the formal claims process required by your insurer.

## Important note
Under UK law (Section 170 Road Traffic Act 1988), you have a legal duty to stop and exchange details if damage or injury occurs. If safety concerns prevent this, you must report the matter to the police as soon as is reasonably practicable. Failing to report within 24 hours is a criminal offence. For suspected insurance scams, you can contact the Insurance Fraud Bureau CheatLine on 0800 422 0421 once you are safe.
