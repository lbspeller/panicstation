# What to do if… a friend seems suddenly disoriented at a social event and you worry someone is targeting them sexually (UK)

## Short answer
Immediately move your friend to a safe, quiet area with a second trusted person, alert venue staff using "Ask for Angela" or by requesting security, and call 999 if they lose consciousness or their health worsens.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave them alone under any circumstances, including "to sleep it off" or to go to the toilet.
- Do not allow them to leave the venue with anyone you do not know or trust.
- Do not try to "sober them up" with coffee, food, or more fluids, as this can increase choking risks if they lose consciousness.
- Do not confront a suspected person directly, which may escalate the danger to you and your friend.
- Do not pressure them to recall details or decide on a police report while they are still disoriented.
- Do not wash their clothes or allow them to bathe immediately if they suspect an assault, as this may destroy forensic evidence.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate your friend from the crowd immediately.** Move them to a quieter space like a foyer or a well-lit area near staff. 
   - Ensure at least one other trusted person stays with you both so you are not alone.
2. **Alert venue staff or security using "Ask for Angela."** Go to the bar and "Ask for Angela" to signal you need discreet help, or tell security: "My friend is suddenly unwell and I am worried they are being targeted."
   - Request that staff prevent any unauthorized persons from approaching or leaving with your friend.
3. **Assess for emergency medical symptoms.** Call **999** (or **112**) immediately if they are:
   - Struggling to breathe or having a seizure.
   - Losing consciousness or becoming impossible to wake.
   - Vomiting repeatedly or severely confused.
   - (Note: If you cannot speak, call **999** and press **55** when prompted to alert the operator you are in danger).
4. **Monitor breathing and prevent choking.** Keep your friend upright if they are awake. 
   - If they become very drowsy or lose consciousness, place them in the **recovery position** (on their side with their head tilted back) and stay with them until help arrives.
5. **Secure potential evidence if safe to do so.** If there is a drink or container they were using, do not throw it away.
   - Handle the glass/bottle by the base only (avoid the rim) and place it in a clean bag if possible for potential police testing.
6. **Seek non-emergency medical or police advice.** Call **NHS 111** for urgent health advice if they are unwell but stable.
   - Call **101** to report the incident to the police if it is no longer an immediate emergency.
7. **Contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC).** If you suspect a sexual assault has occurred, find the nearest **SARC** via the NHS website. 
   - They provide specialist medical care and forensic exams that can be stored even if your friend is not ready to talk to the police.

## What can wait
- You do not need to confirm if they were "spiked" versus "too much alcohol" tonight; medical safety is the priority.
- You do not need to identify the person you suspect or gather witness statements yourself.
- You do not need to make a final decision about a formal police investigation immediately.
- You do not need to explain the situation to your wider social group or social media.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel panicked or hyper-vigilant when you suspect a friend is in danger. Sudden disorientation can be frightening and confusing for both of you. Your only priority right now is physical safety and getting your friend into the care of professionals; you do not need to "solve" what happened right now.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety actions for the immediate duration of a crisis at a social event. It does not replace professional medical assessment or legal advice.

## Important note
If someone is unresponsive or in immediate danger, always call 999. Forensic evidence (urine/blood) for spiking often degrades within 12–24 hours, so early reporting to police or a SARC is necessary if testing is desired.
