# What to do if… you have a new episode of unusual sensations and confusion and you worry it could be a seizure warning (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself to a safe position (sit or lie on the floor) and seek urgent medical assessment. Call **999** if this is a first-time episode, if awareness is not fully returning, or if physical shaking occurs; otherwise, contact your **GP for an urgent appointment** or **NHS 111** today.

## Do not do these things
- Do not **drive** or operate any machinery until you have been cleared by a specialist.
- Do not go near **stairs, open water, baths, heights, or lit hobs** while feeling altered or "spaced out".
- Do not let anyone **restrain you or put objects in your mouth** if you lose awareness or begin to shake.
- Do not attempt to "walk off" the confusion or ignore the episode.
- Do not drink alcohol or use recreational substances to steady your nerves.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe position on the floor immediately.**  
   Sit or lie down away from furniture with sharp edges. If you feel a "warning" (aura), this is the priority to prevent injury from a fall.
2. **Alert someone nearby to stay with you.**  
   Tell them: "I am feeling confused and having unusual sensations; please stay with me until this passes." Ask them to note the time and what they observe.
3. **Call 999 immediately if specific emergency triggers occur.**  
   Request an ambulance if:
   - This is the **first time** you have experienced these symptoms.
   - You have **three or more episodes** within a 24-hour period.
   - Any shaking or loss of awareness lasts **more than 5 minutes**.
   - You are **not returning to your normal self** or remain heavily confused.
   - The episode happened in water or resulted in an injury.
4. **Note the specific sensory cues you experienced.**  
   Write down or record a voice note of exactly what you felt. Common cues include:
   - A "rising" sensation in the stomach.
   - Intense, sudden "deja vu" or a feeling of "jamais vu" (familiar things feeling strange).
   - Sudden unprovoked fear, joy, or anxiety.
   - Strange tastes or smells (often metallic or like burning rubber).
   - Tingling, numbness, or "pins and needles" in one part of the body.
5. **Contact your GP for an urgent same-day appointment or call NHS 111.**  
   If 999 was not required, you still need a clinical assessment today. State clearly that you have had a "new episode of altered awareness and unusual sensations" and need to be screened for a suspected seizure.
6. **Stop driving immediately and prepare to notify the DVLA.**  
   **You are legally required to stop driving following a suspected seizure or blackout.** Place your car keys in a secure location and cancel any planned journeys. You must notify the DVLA of the medical episode; a clinician will advise you on the specific forms (usually FEP1) required.
7. **Position yourself in the recovery position once any shaking stops.**  
   **If you have had a physical seizure, roll onto your side and tilt your chin up to keep your airway clear.** Stay in this position until you feel fully alert and your breathing is regular.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause or "type" of seizure yourself.
- You do not need to worry about long-term medication or diagnosis at this moment.
- You do not need to contact the DVLA in the next hour; the priority is medical assessment, though you must not drive in the interim.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel exhausted, frightened, or "foggy" after an episode of altered awareness. These sensations are physically taxing on the brain. Seeking urgent help is the standard, safest response to a new neurological event.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety advice only. Long-term management, specialist neurology referrals (usually required within 2 weeks for first seizures), and formal driving restrictions must be managed by healthcare professionals.

## Important note
This information is not a substitute for professional medical advice. Always follow the instructions of emergency dispatchers or attending clinicians. If your symptoms worsen or you are alone and concerned, call 999.
