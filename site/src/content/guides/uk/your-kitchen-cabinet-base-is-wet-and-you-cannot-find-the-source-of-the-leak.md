# What to do if… your kitchen cabinet base is wet and you cannot find the source of the leak (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the water first: shut off the nearest isolation valve under the sink if you can do that easily, and if you cannot confidently isolate it, turn off your home’s inside stop valve. Keep water away from electrics and contain the spread.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep running taps, the dishwasher, or the washing machine to see what happens.
- Don’t touch plugs, extension leads, switches, sockets, or appliances if anything around them is wet.
- Don’t force a stuck isolation valve or stop valve, and don’t use extra leverage with tools if you are unsure.
- Don’t loosen pipe fittings unless the water supply is off.
- Don’t leave standing water sitting on the cabinet base if you can safely wipe it up.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Put on shoes, move stored items out of the wet cupboard, and keep children and pets away from the area.
2. **Shut off water at the closest point you can use safely.**
   - If the wetness is under the sink, turn the small isolation valves for the tap feeds to the off position if that is obvious and they are easy to move.
   - If there are appliance feeds for a dishwasher or washing machine, turn those isolation valves off too if you can do that without forcing them.
   - If you cannot find the right valve quickly, or the leak may be from more than one connection, turn off the home’s inside stop valve.
3. **Check the supply is off without trying to recreate the leak.** Open the cold kitchen tap briefly.
   - If it slows and stops, the cold supply is probably isolated.
   - If it keeps running normally, you may have turned the wrong valve. Do not force anything; keep the area contained and get help.
   - Do not run the dishwasher, washing machine, or hot tap to test the leak source.
4. **Protect electrics.**
   - If water is near sockets, switches, built-in appliances, cables, or the consumer unit, do not touch wet electrical items.
   - If you can reach the consumer unit or fuse box from a dry, safe position, switch off the affected kitchen circuit. If you cannot identify it safely, use the main switch.
   - If you would need to stand in water, touch wet surfaces, or reach past wet electrics, step back and call your landlord, managing agent, building emergency contact, or a registered electrician.
5. **Contain the water.**
   - Put a bowl, tray, or bucket under the wettest point.
   - Use towels to stop water spreading across the cupboard base or floor.
   - Wipe up standing water if you can do it safely, then leave the cupboard doors open.
6. **Make a quick visual check only after the water is off.**
   - Look for fresh water around the sink trap or U-bend.
   - Look at the tap tails or flexible hoses that feed the tap.
   - Look at dishwasher or washing machine hoses that pass through the cabinet side or back.
   - Look for water tracks from above, from a pipe joint, or from the waste pipe area; do not dismantle anything.
7. **Contact the right help.**
   - If you rent, contact your landlord, agent, council, or housing association urgently and say what you turned off, whether the water stopped, and whether electrics may be affected.
   - If you own, contact a plumber urgently if the water will not stay off, a valve will not turn, or the leak restarts as soon as you reopen a valve.
   - If you are in a flat or shared building and cannot isolate the supply, contact the landlord, managing agent, building manager, freeholder, or emergency building contact. Your water company may also be able to advise about external stop taps or supply-side issues.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact failed part right now.
- You do not need to run appliances to confirm the source.
- You do not need to choose a permanent repair now.
- Detailed insurance decisions can wait until the water and electrics are stable, but take a few photos and keep a note of what happened when you can.

## Important reassurance
It is normal not to see the exact source straight away. Water can run along pipes, drip from edges, and pool away from the actual leak. Stopping the supply, keeping away from wet electrics, and containing the water are the important first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and reduce further damage. Later decisions about plumbing repair, electrics, tenancy responsibilities, or insurance may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information for urgent home stabilisation, not legal, electrical, plumbing, insurance, or other professional advice. If water may have reached electrics, avoid wet switches, outlets, plugs, and appliances, and isolate power only from a dry, safe location.
