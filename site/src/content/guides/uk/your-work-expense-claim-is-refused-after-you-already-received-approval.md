# What to do if… your work expense claim is refused after you already received approval (UK)

## Short answer
Get the refusal and the reason in writing, then reply with the prior approval evidence and ask what exact document, correction, or authorisation is needed to release payment. Pause any further work spending unless you have fresh written approval.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep incurring more work expenses while the claim is disputed, unless you have fresh written confirmation.
- Do not assume it is personal before checking for admin issues such as coding, cost centre, missing evidence, policy version, or cut-off dates.
- Do not threaten legal action in your first message; it can slow down a practical fix.
- Do not sign anything saying the expense was “unauthorised” or “personal” if you disagree, without time to read it.
- Do not post about it publicly or in wide workplace channels while you still need a clear paper trail.
- Do not rely only on a verbal reassurance that it will be sorted.

## What to do now
1. **Make a short evidence pack.** Save the approval, the submitted claim, receipts or invoices, the expenses policy you relied on, and the refusal message.
2. **Ask for the refusal reason in writing.** Ask which line items are refused, which are payable, the exact reason for refusal, and what would make the claim payable.
3. **Check whether this is an admin problem.** Look for a wrong cost centre, missing receipt, old policy version, rejected category, missed submission cut-off, or missing budget-holder approval.
4. **Confirm any verbal conversation in writing.** Send a short email saying what you understood was said, then ask the person to confirm or correct it.
5. **If evidence is the issue, ask about alternatives once.** If you cannot provide the exact receipt requested, ask whether a bank statement line, booking reference, invoice copy, or other evidence can be accepted on this occasion.
6. **Resubmit once, cleanly.** Only change what they asked you to change, attach or link the evidence pack, and make the resubmission easy to check.
7. **Ask the original approver to restate the approval.** A brief message confirming the date, business purpose, and amount or limit of the prior approval may help finance or payroll release the claim.
8. **Check the policy and contract wording.** Note what they say about prior approval, evidence, limits, exceptions, and reimbursement timing. If there is no clear written policy, note whether this type of expense has usually been reimbursed before.
9. **If it is still refused, raise a formal grievance.** Keep it narrow: what was approved, what you spent, what you submitted, what was refused, the dates, and what outcome you want, such as payment in the next available pay run or a written final decision with reasons.
10. **Do not miss external time limits if you are considering a claim.** Expenses are usually handled as an expenses or contract issue, not simply as a wage deduction. If a contract or policy gives you a right to reimbursement, a breach of contract route may be relevant. Tribunal and court routes can depend on where in the UK you work and whether your employment has ended. If you are thinking about any external claim, check Acas, nidirect, a trade union, or legal advice promptly because time limits can be strict and exact dates can be complicated.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to take court or tribunal action.
- You do not need to write a long history of everything that has gone wrong at work.
- You do not need to resign, make threats, or decide whether to stay in the job.
- You do not need to solve future expenses arrangements until this claim is stabilised.

## Important reassurance
A refusal after prior approval can be caused by process problems such as coding, missing evidence, policy interpretation, or a changed approver. A calm written record and a clear evidence pack usually give you the best chance of getting a practical decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation, document what happened, and use the right internal route. Later decisions may need help from Acas, nidirect, a trade union, Citizens Advice, or an employment adviser, especially if the amount is significant or your job is affected.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, employment, or professional advice. The correct route depends on your contract, the expenses policy, the evidence available, where in the UK you are, and whether you are still employed.
