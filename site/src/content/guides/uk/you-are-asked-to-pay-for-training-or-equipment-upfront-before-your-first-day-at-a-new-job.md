# What to do if… you are asked to pay for training or equipment upfront before your first day at a new job (UK)

## Short answer
Ask for the purpose of the meeting and whether it is a formal disciplinary or grievance hearing. If it is a formal hearing, you have a statutory right to be accompanied and can request a postponement of up to five working days to arrange for a companion to attend.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign or agree to "leave today" during the meeting, as this may affect your right to claim unfair dismissal or receive notice pay.
- Do not sign any documents that you have not had time to read in full, including settlement agreements or meeting minutes you disagree with.
- Do not guess or speculate if you do not know the answer to a question; it is safer to state that you do not recall or need to check records.
- Do not admit to allegations that have not been clearly explained to you in writing.
- Do not hand over personal mobile devices or passwords to personal accounts.
- Do not record the meeting covertly; check your staff handbook first as this can be considered gross misconduct in many UK workplaces.

## What to do now
1. **Pause for 30 seconds to gather a notebook and pen before entering the room.** Having a way to take notes reduces the pressure to memorize everything and signals that you are treating the matter formally.
2. **At the start of the meeting, ask the lead person to clarify the meeting’s status.** Use these specific questions and write down the answers:
   - "Is this an informal chat, a formal investigation, or a disciplinary / grievance hearing?"
   - "Could this meeting result in a formal warning or my dismissal?"
3. **Request a written agenda or a summary of the allegations if they have not been provided.** If the employer refuses, take a note of the refusal and ask them to state the concerns verbally so you can write them down.
4. **Identify everyone in the room and their roles.** Ask who is taking the official minutes and confirm when you will receive a copy of those notes.
5. **Exercise your right to be accompanied if the meeting is a formal disciplinary or grievance hearing.** Under the Employment Relations Act 1999, if your chosen companion (a work colleague or trade union representative) cannot attend, you can propose an alternative time within five working days of the original date.
6. **State clearly if you feel you have had insufficient time to prepare.** If you were given no notice and the meeting is formal, ask for an adjournment to review the evidence and consult a representative.
7. **Keep your responses to questions brief and factual.** If you are unsure of a detail, say: "I would need to check my emails / records before I can confirm that accurately."
8. **Request a short break (5–10 minutes) if the meeting becomes overwhelming or if you need to confer with your companion.** You are entitled to step out of the room to compose yourself or speak privately with your representative.
9. **Confirm the next steps before the meeting concludes.** Ask: "What happens now, what are the timeframes for a decision, and who is my point of contact for follow-up information?"
10. **Write a contemporaneous record immediately after leaving the room.** Record the time the meeting started and ended, who spoke, what was alleged, and your specific responses while the details are fresh.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to appeal a decision that hasn't been made yet.
- Preparing a long-form witness statement or rebuttal.
- Searching for external legal representation or filing a tribunal claim.
- Discussing the meeting with colleagues who were not involved.

## Important reassurance
Being called into an unexplained meeting is a high-stress event, and feeling a physical reaction like a racing heart or shaking hands is a normal response to sudden workplace ambiguity. The meeting is a procedural step; your primary goal is to gather information and protect your right to a fair process, not to solve the entire issue in one sitting.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for an unexpected HR meeting in the UK. It focuses on immediate rights under the ACAS Code of Practice and the Employment Relations Act 1999. It does not cover specific legal strategies for ongoing litigation or specialized sectors with different statutory rules (such as the police or armed forces).

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment rights can vary based on your specific contract, length of service, and employment status (e.g., worker vs. employee). If you are a member of a trade union, contact your representative as soon as possible for specific assistance.
