# What to do if… your phone shows a recovery mode message saying it must be connected to a computer to update or restore (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the situation as a potential SIM-swap fraud or a hardware failure by immediately verifying your account status with your provider from a different device. Do not wait for service to "fix itself" if you suspect unauthorized changes have been made to your mobile account.

## Do not do these things
- Do not follow links in any "service restoration" or "security alert" texts that arrived just before or after the service loss.
- Do not share any one-time passcodes (OTPs) with anyone claiming to be from your network provider’s fraud team.
- Do not factory-reset the handset yet, as this may delete recent logs or messages that serve as evidence of an account takeover.
- Do not delete an existing eSIM profile or keep re-inserting a physical SIM if you smell burning or see smoke from the device.
- Do not ignore the issue if you have Wi-Fi signal but "No Service" for calls/SMS, even if data apps still work.

## What to do now
1. **Establish a secure secondary connection immediately.**
   - Connect to a trusted Wi-Fi network to maintain access to email and internet-based messaging.
   - Use a different phone (a landline or a borrowed mobile) to make outgoing calls to your provider or bank.

2. **Check for local network outages before assuming a personal fault.**
   - Ask anyone nearby on the same network if they have service.
   - Use a browser to check your provider's "network status" page for your current postcode.

3. **Perform basic physical and software resets (maximum 5 minutes).**
   - **Toggle Airplane Mode on and off once to force a network search.**
   - **Restart the device completely.**
   - **Remove and inspect the physical SIM card if applicable.**
     - Look for dirt, scratches, or moisture on the gold chip.
     - Clean the chip with a dry, lint-free cloth and re-seat it firmly.
   - **Check for a Carrier Settings Update in your phone settings.**

4. **Contact your mobile network provider’s official support line from another phone.**
   - Find the number on an old paper bill, the official website, or dial 0333 numbers if you cannot use a freephone line.
   - **Tell the agent: "My SIM has stopped working suddenly. I need to verify if a SIM swap, eSIM reissue, or Porting Authorisation Code (PAC) has been requested today."**
   - Request that a "fraud note" or "porting block" be placed on your account immediately if any changes are found.
   - Ask for a "temporary spend cap" to prevent high-cost fraudulent calls or "charge to bill" purchases.

5. **Secure your primary digital accounts in order of risk.**
   - **Access your primary email account first to change the password and end all other active sessions.**
   - **Contact your bank via the number on the back of your card to report a potential SIM swap.**
   - **Check your Apple ID or Google Account for new "trusted devices" you do not recognise.**
   - **Switch your security settings from "SMS/Text" codes to an "Authenticator App" or "Passkey" where possible.**

6. **Preserve evidence of the service failure.**
   - Take a screenshot of the "No SIM" or "SOS" message on your screen.
   - Save or screenshot any texts from "7726" or your provider that mention SIM changes or PAC requests.

7. **Report the incident to the appropriate authorities if fraud is suspected.**
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** Report to Report Fraud (formerly Action Fraud) at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.
   - **Scotland:** Report to Police Scotland via 101.
   - **Suspicious Texts:** Forward any suspicious messages to 7726 (the free UK-wide reporting service).

## What can wait
- You do not need to buy a new handset or replacement SIM until your provider confirms the old one is permanently deactivated or faulty.
- You do not need to notify every contact in your phone book unless you have evidence that they are being targeted by scammers using your number.
- You can delay a full factory reset of the phone until you have finished securing your financial accounts.

## Important reassurance
A "No SIM" alert is often caused by a loose card or a minor network glitch. However, because your phone number is a key security tool for your bank and email, treating it as a priority ensures that even a rare fraud attempt is stopped before financial loss occurs. Identifying the status of your account with your provider is the standard protective action.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a sudden loss of mobile service. It does not cover long-term identity theft recovery or technical repair of damaged hardware.

## Important note
This information is for practical guidance and does not constitute legal, financial, or professional technical advice. If you are experiencing active financial theft or feel physically unsafe, contact the emergency services or your bank's emergency fraud line immediately.
