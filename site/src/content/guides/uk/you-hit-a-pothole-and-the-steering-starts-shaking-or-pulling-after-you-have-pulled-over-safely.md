# What to do if… you hit a pothole and the steering starts shaking or pulling after you have pulled over safely (UK)

## Short answer
Do not rejoin fast-moving traffic. Arrange professional recovery or a mobile technician if the steering vibrates, pulls significantly to one side, or if there is visible damage to the tyres or wheels.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "test" the vehicle by accelerating to motorway speeds to see if the shaking stops.
- Do not use a warning triangle if you are stopped on a motorway or a high-speed dual carriageway.
- Do not stand between your vehicle and oncoming traffic or behind the vehicle.
- Do not attempt to repair or inspect the vehicle while your body is exposed to live traffic.
- Do not ignore "light" shaking or pulling; even minor vibration can indicate internal tyre structural failure (delamination) that leads to sudden blowouts.

## What to do now
1. **Ensure the vehicle is in a position of relative safety.**
   - Turn your wheels to the left (away from the road) if you are on a hard shoulder or verge.
   - Keep hazard lights on; use sidelights if it is dark or visibility is poor.
   - If you are stopped in a live lane or a smart motorway section without a hard shoulder, stay in the vehicle with seatbelts on and call **999** immediately.
2. **Exit the vehicle safely if you are off the running lanes.**
   - Exit via the passenger side (left-hand doors) only.
   - Move behind the safety barrier or up the embankment and stay well away from the carriageway.
   - Ensure all passengers, especially children and pets, are kept away from the road surface.
3. **Contact the relevant road authority or emergency services.**
   - On motorways or major A-roads in England: call National Highways on **0300 123 5000** if you are an obstruction or need safety advice.
   - In Scotland: contact Traffic Scotland (Amey or BEAR Scotland depending on region).
   - In Wales: call Traffic Wales on **0300 123 1213**.
   - Use the nearest orange emergency roadside telephone if available, as it provides your exact location automatically.
4. **Identify your exact location using roadside markers.**
   - Look for the small blue "Driver Location Signs" (A or B signs) or marker posts located every 100 metres on the verge.
   - Note the road number (e.g., M1), the direction of travel (A or B), and the number (e.g., 56.5).
5. **Conduct a visual check of the wheels and tyres from a safe distance.**
   - Look for "egg" bulges on the tyre sidewall, deep cuts, or exposed metal cords.
   - Check the metal wheel rim for fresh dents, cracks, or flattened edges.
   - Check for fluid leaking from the suspension or steering components behind the front wheels.
6. **Arrange recovery to a garage or tyre specialist.**
   - Call your breakdown provider or a local recovery service.
   - State clearly that you have had a "heavy pothole impact" and the "steering is shaking/pulling," as this prioritises the call as a safety-critical mechanical failure.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the exact depth of the pothole or take photos of the road surface right now; trespassing on a motorway to photograph a pothole is a criminal offence.
- You do not need to decide which garage will perform permanent repairs while you are still at the roadside.
- You do not need to contact the local council or National Highways regarding a compensation claim until you and the vehicle are in a permanent place of safety.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel startled or shaky after a heavy impact at speed. Steering vibration or pulling often indicates that the tracking (alignment) has been knocked out or a wheel weight has been lost, but it can also hide serious internal tyre damage. Choosing to call for recovery rather than driving an unstable vehicle is the standard safety protocol for UK road users.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety instructions only. A qualified technician must inspect the vehicle's suspension, steering rack, wheel balance, and internal tyre structure before the vehicle is considered roadworthy.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and does not replace the Highway Code or professional mechanical assessment. Always prioritize the instructions of National Highways Traffic Officers or the Police. If you feel your life is in immediate danger due to your location on the road, call 999.
