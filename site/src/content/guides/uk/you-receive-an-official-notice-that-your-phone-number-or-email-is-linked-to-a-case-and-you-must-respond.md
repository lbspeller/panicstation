# What to do if… you receive an official notice that your phone number or email is linked to a case and you must respond (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the message as unverified and do not use any contact details provided within it. Contact the relevant agency directly using an official number or website you have found yourself to confirm if the notice is genuine.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links, open attachments, or scan QR codes included in the message.
- Do not call the number provided in the notice or trust the caller ID, as these can be spoofed.
- Do not provide personal data, bank details, or one-time passcodes (OTPs) to the sender.
- Do not pay any "fine," "bail," or "clearance fee" demanded in the message to resolve the matter.
- Do not engage in a conversation or "interview" over the phone or text to prove your innocence.
- Do not delete the message immediately, as it is evidence required for reporting.

## What to do now
1. **Document the communication exactly as received.** Take screenshots of the message, the sender’s phone number or email address, and any links or reference numbers provided.
2. **Verify the notice through a trusted official channel.**
   - If the sender claims to be the **Police**: call **101** (the non-emergency number) or visit the official website of the specific force named to find their genuine contact details.
   - If the sender claims to be a **Court**: use the **Find a Court or Tribunal** tool on GOV.UK to find the correct administrative office and call them directly.
   - If the sender claims to be a **Solicitor or Law Firm**: check the **Law Society’s Find a Solicitor** database to confirm the firm is real and use the phone number listed there.
3. **Move the interaction to a formal, recorded environment.** If the contact is genuine, inform the officer or official that you wish to communicate through formal channels and require all requests in writing to your verified address.
4. **Request free legal advice if you are asked to attend an interview.** If the police confirm they wish to question you, you have a right to free, independent legal advice from a **duty solicitor** at the station.
5. **Report the suspicious contact to the appropriate UK authorities.**
   - Forward suspicious **texts** to **7726** (free on all UK networks).
   - Report suspicious **calls** to **7726** by texting the word "Call" followed by the suspicious phone number.
   - Forward suspicious **emails** to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.
   - For residents of **England, Wales, or Northern Ireland**: report to **Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) online or at **0300 123 2040**.
   - For residents of **Scotland**: report to **Police Scotland** by calling **101** or using their online reporting form.
6. **Secure your digital accounts immediately.** If you clicked a link or entered a password, change the password for that account and any others that share it, and enable two-factor authentication (2FA).
7. **Contact your bank via the 159 hotline if money is involved.** If you have provided financial details or lost money, call **159** (the secure banking hotline) or use the number on the back of your bank card immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare a witness statement or defense right now.
- You do not need to decide if you are a suspect or a victim before speaking with a solicitor.
- You do not need to provide access to your devices or social media accounts for "investigation."
- You do not need to respond to deadlines set within a suspicious message.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel stressed or panicked when receiving a message that mentions legal trouble or the police. Scammers intentionally use urgent and threatening language to stop you from thinking clearly. Taking the time to verify the sender through a separate, official route is the safest action and will not be held against you if the matter is genuine.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate stabilization steps to prevent fraud and ensure proper legal process. It does not replace professional legal advice if a case is confirmed as genuine.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and applies to UK procedures as of 2026. If you believe there is an immediate threat to your safety or a crime is currently in progress, call 999.
