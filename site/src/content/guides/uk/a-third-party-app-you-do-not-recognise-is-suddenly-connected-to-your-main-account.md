# What to do if… a third-party app you do not recognise is suddenly connected to your main account (UK)

## Short answer
Immediately revoke the unknown app's access within your account security settings, then force a sign-out of all active sessions and update your password and 2-step verification. Treat any unexpected app connection as a potential account takeover attempt.

## Do not do these things
- Don't click any "review" or "secure account" links within the notification email or SMS—access your account by typing the official URL directly into your browser.
- Don't assume the threat is over because you can still log in; third-party apps can maintain "offline access" even after you change your password.
- Don't delete the alert notifications or emails immediately; you may need them for reporting or as evidence.
- Don't use the account for sensitive transactions (banking or password resets for other services) until you have completed all security steps.
- Don't reuse a previous password or a simple variation of your current one.

## What to do now
1. **Navigate to the account provider's official website directly.**  
   **Open a fresh browser window and type the address (e.g., accounts.google.com, appleid.apple.com, or outlook.live.com) manually.**  
   - Avoid using any search engine results or links from the alert message.  
   - Sign in using your existing credentials.

2. **Revoke the unknown app's permissions immediately.**  
   **Locate the "Security," "Privacy," or "Third-party apps with account access" section in your settings.**  
   - Select the unfamiliar app and choose "Remove Access," "Revoke," or "Disconnect."  
   - Look specifically for apps with "Full Account Access," "Read/Write Mail," or "Manage Account" permissions.

3. **Terminate all active sessions and devices.**  
   **Find the "Recent activity," "Devices," or "Where you're signed in" page and select "Sign out of all other sessions."**  
   - This ensures that any active session held by an attacker is invalidated.

4. **Update your password using a unique combination.**  
   **Change your password to one that you have never used on any other service.**  
   - Use at least three random, unconnected words (e.g., "Table-Forest-Anchor").  
   - If you have a password manager, use it to generate and store a high-entropy string.

5. **Enable or reset 2-step verification (2SV).**  
   **Turn on 2-step verification if it is not active, or reset it if it was already enabled.**  
   - Use an authenticator app (e.g., Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator) or a hardware security key.  
   - Download new "Backup Codes" and store them offline.

6. **Audit your account recovery information.**  
   **Check that the recovery email address and phone number are still yours.**  
   - Remove any secondary emails or phone numbers that you do not recognise.

7. **Scan for unauthorised email rules or forwarding.**  
   **If the account is an email service, check "Rules," "Filters," or "Forwarding" settings.**  
   - Delete any rules that forward your mail to an external address or move messages to "Trash" automatically.

8. **Report the incident to the appropriate UK authorities.**  
   **Forward any suspicious emails or texts to the national reporting centres.**  
   - Forward suspicious emails to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.  
   - Forward suspicious text messages to **7726** (free on UK mobile networks).  
   - If you have lost money or been the victim of a crime, report it to **Report Fraud** at **reportfraud.police.uk** or call **0300 123 2040**.

9. **Notify your bank if the account is linked to payments.**  
   **If the compromised account contains stored payment cards or is your primary email for banking, contact your bank using the number on your card.**  
   - Inform them of a potential digital account compromise.

## What can wait
- You do not need to perform a factory reset of your computer or phone immediately unless you have evidence of a local malware infection.
- You do not need to delete the account entirely right now; securing it is the priority.
- You can wait to notify your personal contacts until after you have regained full control and verified your recovery settings.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a notification about an unknown app connection is an effective early warning. By revoking access and updating your security credentials promptly, you can often stop an account takeover before any significant data or financial loss occurs. It is common for these connections to happen via a single "consent" click on a misleading website; removing the app usually severs the link immediately.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a suspected third-party app breach. It does not cover long-term forensic investigation or complex recovery for accounts where you have been completely locked out.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional cybersecurity advice. If you are experiencing an active financial loss or believe you are at immediate physical risk, contact your bank or the emergency services (999) immediately. For non-emergency cyber crime reporting in Scotland, call 101.
