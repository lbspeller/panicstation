# What to do if… a biller says your payment was reversed and late fees will start immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay again in a rush. First check your bank or building society account for what actually happened, then contact the biller through an official route and ask them to pause late fees while the payment is traced.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay a second time until you have checked whether the first payment failed, reversed, returned, or is still pending.
- Do not click links or call numbers from a surprising text or email about “reversal”, “failed payment”, or “late fees”.
- Do not share full online banking logins, one-time passcodes, card security details, or remote access to your phone or computer.
- Do not cancel a Direct Debit in a panic unless you know how the corrected payment will be made and what will happen to future payments.
- Do not spend time arguing about blame right now. Focus on getting the account marked as being checked or on hold.

## What to do now
1. **Use an official contact route.**  
   Open the biller’s app, use a number printed on a recent bill, or type the company’s website address yourself. Do not use contact details from the message that alarmed you.

2. **Check your bank or building society account for the exact payment status.**  
   Look for whether the payment:
   - completed and then shows as reversed, returned, refunded, or unpaid;
   - stayed pending and then disappeared;
   - never left your account;
   - left your account but may have the wrong reference.

3. **Save quick evidence before you contact anyone.**  
   Save a screenshot or download of the transaction page showing the date, amount, payee, reference, and current status. Do not include passwords, full card numbers, or security codes in anything you send.

4. **Identify the payment type.**  
   This helps you ask the right question:
   - **Card payment:** an authorisation may have dropped off, or the payment may have been voided or refunded.
   - **Direct Debit:** if there was an error in the payment of a Direct Debit, you can ask your bank or building society about the Direct Debit Guarantee.
   - **Bank transfer or standing order:** the money may have left, but the biller may not have matched it because of a missing or wrong reference.

5. **Ask your bank or building society one direct question.**  
   Use secure in-app chat, telephone banking, or a branch. Ask: “Was this payment actually sent and then reversed or returned, and what reason or reference can I give the biller?”

6. **Ask the biller to pause late fees while the payment is traced.**  
   Say: “I am checking the payment with my bank. Please put a short hold on late fees and mark the account as payment being traced.” Ask them to confirm:
   - the date and amount they expected;
   - the payment reference they needed;
   - the return or reversal reason they can see;
   - how long the hold will last.

7. **Only make a replacement payment through a controlled official route.**  
   If your bank confirms the original payment failed or was returned, pay through the biller’s official portal, app, or phone line. If the first payment might still arrive, ask the biller to confirm in writing how they will handle any duplicate payment and whether late fees will be reviewed.

8. **If it was a Direct Debit error, ask about the Direct Debit Guarantee.**  
   If an error was made in the payment of a Direct Debit, the bank or building society should usually provide a full and immediate refund. A refund does not automatically cancel what you owe for the bill, so ask the biller how the correct amount should be paid.

9. **Keep a simple paper trail.**  
   Write down dates, times, names, reference numbers, and what each person said. Save any confirmation that fees are paused, waived, or being reviewed.

10. **Escalate only after the urgent pause is handled.**  
   If the biller or bank will not correct an obvious mistake, ask them to log a formal complaint. For payment-service complaints, response deadlines can be shorter than ordinary complaints, so check the Financial Ombudsman time-limits page before escalating. If the complaint is about your bank, building society, payment provider, or another financial business, you may be able to take an eligible complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service after the business has responded or the relevant response period has passed. If the problem is mainly with a non-financial biller, use that biller’s complaint route and check whether a sector-specific route applies.

## What can wait
- You do not need to switch providers today.
- You do not need to cancel the service today unless there is a separate urgent reason.
- You do not need to write a long complaint letter before you know whether the payment failed, reversed, or was misapplied.
- You do not need to accept late fees as final while the payment status is unclear and you are actively tracing it.

## Important reassurance
A payment reversal notice can feel urgent, but many of these situations come from ordinary processing problems, missing references, failed authorisations, or mismatched records. The useful first move is to slow the situation down, verify the payment status, and get the biller to record that the issue is being checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce the risk of double-payment, late fees, or scam pressure. If the biller is threatening disconnection, debt collection, court action, or credit-file reporting, you may need specialist help for that type of bill.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Policies and complaint routes can vary by biller, bank, payment method, and account type. If something feels rushed or suspicious, stop and verify through official channels before sending money.
