# What to do if… you receive a jury summons but the name or address details are wrong (UK)

## Short answer
Do not ignore the paperwork; you must use the official response route to correct the error and avoid a potential fine of up to £1,000 for non-response. If the letter is for you but contains errors, reply via the official online service or post and provide the correct details immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a clerical error invalidates the summons or allows you to discard the letter.
- Do not pay any fee requested via text or phone to "postpone" or "cancel" jury service; this is a common scam often requesting £34.99.
- Do not provide bank details or National Insurance numbers to anyone calling you about a "missed summons" fine.
- Do not sign the "Form of Return" or online response using someone else's name if the letter was misdelivered.
- Do not delay your response while waiting for other documents (like a new passport or driving licence) to prove your correct name.

## What to do now
1. **Identify which UK jurisdiction issued the paperwork to ensure you follow the correct legal response timeline.**
   - **England & Wales:** Paperwork is from the Jury Central Summoning Bureau (HMCTS). You must respond within 7 days of getting the letter.
   - **Scotland:** Paperwork is a "jury citation" from the Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service (SCTS).
   - **Northern Ireland:** Paperwork is usually a "jury notice" from the Northern Ireland Courts and Tribunals Service (NICTS). You must return the "Form of Return" within 14 days.
2. **Correct your own details using the official reply method if the summons is intended for you but contains errors.**
   - **England & Wales:** Use the "Reply to a jury summons" service on GOV.UK. Log in using the juror number and the name/address exactly as printed, then use the "change your details" section to provide corrections.
   - **Scotland:** Use the "Respond to a jury citation" service on the SCTS website or contact the specific court named on the citation by telephone.
   - **Northern Ireland:** Complete the Form of Return online or by post. If your address has changed, attach a letter explaining the move to the Juries Officer.
3. **Handle misdelivered mail by returning it to the sender without opening it if the name does not belong to anyone at your address.**
   - Write "Not known at this address" or "No longer at this address" on the front of the unopened envelope.
   - Place the item back in a Royal Mail post box to ensure it is returned to the court bureau.
   - If you have already opened the letter, do not fill it in; instead, call the bureau number on the letterhead to inform them the recipient has moved.
4. **State clearly if you received the summons late due to an address error to avoid being penalised for a "late" response.**
   - Provide the date you actually received the letter.
   - Use a factual sentence: "This letter was forwarded from my previous address at [Old Address] and was received on [Date]."
5. **Verify any contact from the court by using the official contact numbers found on GOV.UK, SCTS, or NICTS websites.**
   - If you receive a suspicious text or call, hang up and call the Jury Central Summoning Bureau (England/Wales) or the local court (Scotland/NI) directly.
   - Note that providing false information on a jury return is an offence punishable by a fine.

## What can wait
- You do not need to update your details on the electoral register or with the DVLA before you reply to the summons.
- You do not need to provide formal proof of a name change (like a marriage certificate) in the initial online response, though you should keep it ready.
- You do not need to decide on a deferral or excusal request in the same hour you receive the letter; prioritize correcting the name/address first.

## Important reassurance
Clerical errors on jury paperwork are common due to lag in electoral register updates or recent house moves. Correcting these details through the official route is a standard administrative process and does not mean you have done anything wrong or are in immediate legal trouble.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for administrative errors on jury paperwork only. It does not provide legal advice on eligibility, disqualification, or the trial process itself.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and reflects 2026 UK procedural standards. Always follow the specific instructions printed on the physical summons or notice you received, as requirements can vary by court or specific case.
