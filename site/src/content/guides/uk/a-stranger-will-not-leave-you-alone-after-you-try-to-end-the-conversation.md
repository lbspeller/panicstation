# What to do if… a stranger will not leave you alone after you try to end the conversation (UK)

## Short answer
Create distance and put other people between you and them. Move to a staffed or busy place, and ask for help directly if they keep pushing, follow you, block you, or make you feel unsafe.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t stay to be polite if you feel uneasy.
- Don’t explain, debate, or keep answering questions to end it nicely.
- Don’t share personal details, such as where you live, where you are going, your phone number, or social media.
- Don’t go with them to a quieter spot, even if they frame it as nearby or quick.
- Don’t let yourself get boxed in by corners, empty stairwells, lifts, narrow doorways, or parked cars.
- Don’t accept food, drinks, cigarettes, vapes, or anything that keeps the interaction going.
- Don’t hand over your phone or unlock it for them.
- Don’t lead them to your home, workplace, hotel, or parked car if you can avoid it.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause immediately.** Step into a shop, café, station area with staff, staffed venue, busier platform area, well-lit place, or near a group. Keep an exit route in sight.
2. **Use one clear boundary once, then stop engaging.** Say calmly: “I’m not available to talk. Please stop.” Do not apologise or give reasons.
3. **Bring in another person early.** Walk up to staff, security, a transport worker, or a nearby group and say: “This person won’t leave me alone. Can I stand here, and can you help me?” A direct request is easier for others to act on.
4. **Make it harder for them to follow without confronting them.** Change direction toward staff or crowds, cross to a busier side of the street if it is safe, pause inside a staffed place, or ask staff not to let the person follow you into a back area.
5. **If you are on the rail network, use railway safety routes.**
   - Go to staff first, or use a platform Help Point if one is available.
   - For non-emergency incidents on the railway, you can text British Transport Police on 61016. Include what is happening, where you are, and when; for example, the line, direction, station, platform, or carriage if you know it.
   - BTP says you will receive a confirmation text when your message is received.
   - If there is no phone signal and it is an emergency, use the train emergency handle, a platform Help Point, staff, or a police officer if available.
   - If there is no phone signal and it is not an emergency, move toward staff or other passengers and wait until you have signal before texting.
   - In an emergency, call 999 rather than relying on 61016.
6. **Use your phone as a safety tool, not a distraction.** Call someone and say out loud where you are and that you are moving toward staff or a busy place. Keep looking around while you move.
7. **Escalate quickly if there is immediate risk.** If they follow closely, block your path, touch you, threaten you, try to make you leave with them, or you think violence could happen soon, call 999. If it is not an emergency but you want to report what happened, call 101 or report online when you are safe.
8. **When you are safe, make brief notes if you can.** Write the time, place, what they said or did, which way they went, and a simple description such as clothing, height, bag, shoes, or distinguishing features.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether it counts as harassment, stalking, antisocial behaviour, or another offence.
- Deciding whether to make a formal report.
- Writing a full account; brief notes are enough for now.
- Contacting venues, transport operators, or making complaints.
- Buying equipment, changing routines, or making long-term safety plans.

## Important reassurance
You do not owe a stranger your time, personal details, or politeness. Feeling rattled or second-guessing yourself is common; your job in the moment is to create space, get into company, and make it harder for them to continue.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes to hours. If this becomes repeated, targeted, or escalates, later decisions may need specialist support, police advice, or another trusted service.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel in danger or a crime is happening, contact emergency services.
