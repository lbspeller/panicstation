# What to do if… a merchant threatens to send a charge to collections unless you pay immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay on the spot. Pause the conversation, ask for the full details in writing, and verify the charge using contact details you find yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay immediately just to stop the threat; pressure can be a warning sign, even when a bill is real.
- Don’t share bank details, card details, passwords, one-time codes, or copies of ID until you have verified who you are dealing with.
- Don’t click payment links in texts or emails you were not expecting.
- Don’t call a number from a suspicious message; use the merchant’s official website, a recent statement, or paperwork you already have.
- Don’t say “I owe this” while you are still checking; use neutral wording such as “I am checking this.”
- Don’t pay by gift card, crypto, or a new bank transfer payee because someone is rushing you.
- Don’t ignore real court paperwork if it arrives later; a phone call, text, or email threat is not the same as court paperwork.

## What to do now
1. **End the pressure.**  
   Say: “I am not paying on this call. Send the full details in writing. I will respond after I have reviewed them.” Then end the call or chat.

2. **Check the merchant through your own route.**  
   Find the merchant’s billing or customer service details from a recent statement, an official website you navigate to yourself, or paperwork you already have. Ask whether there is an outstanding balance, what it is for, when it was incurred, and whether they have passed it to a collection agency.

3. **Ask for a written breakdown that can be checked.**  
   Ask for the dates, what was supplied, the contract or terms they rely on, the amount, how it was calculated, payments already credited, and what they say will happen next if you dispute it.

4. **If you do not recognise the charge, dispute it in writing.**  
   Write: “I do not agree that I owe this amount. Please provide evidence and investigate this as a dispute.” If the creditor or collector is FCA-regulated and this is a consumer credit debt, they generally must pause recovery steps while they investigate a dispute made on valid grounds, or what may be valid grounds. If you are unsure whether that applies, still ask them to pause collection action while they check.

5. **If it relates to a card payment, contact your card provider before paying under pressure.**  
   Ask your bank or card provider whether chargeback is possible for the disputed payment. If it was a credit card purchase, ask whether Section 75 might apply; this usually depends on the type of purchase, the amount, and how it was paid, and commonly involves a purchase price of more than £100 and not more than £30,000.

6. **If the charge may be real but you cannot pay now, keep it in writing.**  
   Ask for time to review the documents and ask what written payment or hardship options exist. Do not agree to a payment plan you cannot keep just to end the pressure.

7. **If it feels like fraud, secure the account first.**  
   If you gave card or bank details, contact your bank or payment provider immediately. If you live in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report suspected fraud through Report Fraud. If you live in Scotland, Police Scotland says fraud and other financial crime should be reported to Police via 101.

8. **Make a short evidence note.**  
   Record the date and time, the number or email used, names given, reference numbers, the amount demanded, and the exact threat. Save messages, screenshots, letters, voicemails, and payment links without clicking them again.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to pay the full amount.
- You do not need to agree to a long-term repayment plan while you are still verifying the charge.
- You do not need to keep taking calls once you have asked for everything in writing.
- You do not need to make a formal complaint today unless the immediate issue is stable.
- You do not need to do a full financial clean-up right now; first stop the pressure, verify the charge, and protect your accounts.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to ask for written details before paying a large charge. A legitimate business or collector should be able to explain what the money is for and give you time to check the facts.

## Scope note
These are first steps to slow the situation down, verify what is happening, and avoid irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about disputes, debt advice, complaints, court papers, or payment arrangements may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Debt and dispute processes can vary by company, debt type, and where you live in the UK.
