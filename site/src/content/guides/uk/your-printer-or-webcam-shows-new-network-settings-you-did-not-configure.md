# What to do if… your printer or webcam shows new network settings you did not configure (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the device from all networks immediately by enabling Airplane Mode to prevent further unauthorized approvals or data transmission.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t tap **“Allow”**, **“Approve”**, or **“Yes”** simply to clear the screen or stop the noise.
- Don’t enter any passwords, PINs, or verification codes into a prompt that appeared unexpectedly.
- Don’t click links or download "security scanners" suggested by the prompt itself.
- Don’t use the suspected device to change sensitive passwords for banking or primary email accounts.
- Don’t assume the prompts are a harmless system glitch if they occur repeatedly.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the device from the internet immediately.** 
   - Swipe to access the control centre and enable **Airplane Mode**. 
   - Manually verify that **Wi-Fi and Bluetooth** are also turned off.
2. **Force a restart if the screen is unresponsive.** 
   - If prompts prevent you from using the menus, hold the **Power and Volume Up** buttons (iPhone) or the **Power button** for 10 seconds (Android) until the screen goes black.
3. **Document the specific details of the prompts.** 
   - Take a **screenshot** or use another device to photograph the screen, ensuring the **app name**, **service name**, or **web address** in the prompt is visible.
4. **Secure your primary accounts from a different, trusted device.** 
   - Log in to your **Apple Account** or **Google Account** on a separate computer or tablet. 
   - Change your **password** and select the option to **“Sign out of all other sessions”** or "Remove trusted devices" you do not recognise.
5. **Check for malicious configuration profiles on iPhone or iPad.** 
   - Go to **Settings → General → VPN & Device Management**. 
   - If any **Configuration Profile** or **Mobile Device Management (MDM)** entry exists that you did not personally install (or that is not from your known employer/school), tap it and select **Remove Profile**.
6. **Remove suspicious apps using Safe Mode on Android.** 
   - Hold the **Power Off** icon in the power menu until the **Safe Mode** prompt appears, then tap to restart. 
   - If the phone is off, power it on and hold the **Volume Down** button as soon as the manufacturer logo appears until the home screen loads with "Safe Mode" in the corner. 
   - Go to **Settings → Apps** and **Uninstall** any recently added or unknown applications.
7. **Revoke site permissions in your mobile browser.** 
   - Open your browser (e.g., Chrome or Safari) settings and navigate to **Site Settings → Notifications**. 
   - Clear and reset permissions for any websites you do not recognise to stop "notification-based" prompts.
8. **Report the incident to the appropriate UK authority.** 
   - If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report the cyber incident to **Report Fraud** at **reportfraud.police.uk** or call **0300 123 2040**. 
   - If you are in Scotland, report via the **Police Scotland** website or call **101**.
9. **Notify your workplace if the device is used for business.** 
   - Contact your **IT department or Security Officer** immediately, as these prompts can be a sign of a targeted "MFA Fatigue" attack against corporate networks.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to factory reset the device in the first few minutes.
- You do not need to respond to "support" calls or texts that arrive shortly after the prompts start, as these are often part of a coordinated scam.
- You can delay a full audit of your entire digital history until the primary account (Apple/Google/Email) is confirmed secure.

## Important reassurance
Receiving repeated prompts (often called "push bombing" or "MFA fatigue") is a known tactic used by attackers to wear down a user's resistance. By disconnecting the device and not tapping "Allow," you have successfully interrupted the attack.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization for unexpected digital access requests. It does not provide full malware removal for advanced persistent threats or legal representation for data breaches.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional technical or legal advice. If you suspect your financial details have been compromised, contact your bank using the number on the back of your card or via the official "159" stop-scam line immediately.
