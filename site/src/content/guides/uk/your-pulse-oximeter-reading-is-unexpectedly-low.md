# What to do if… your pulse oximeter reading is unexpectedly low (UK)

## Short answer
Do one careful recheck now, but act on severe symptoms immediately. If you are very breathless, have chest pain or pressure, feel confused or faint, or your lips or skin look very pale, blue or grey, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore severe symptoms because the number “might be wrong”.
- Don’t keep rechecking every few seconds until you panic; do one careful recheck, then act.
- Don’t drive yourself to A&E if you feel faint, very breathless, confused, or your symptoms are worsening quickly.
- Don’t assume a generic “normal” range applies to you if you have a lung condition, heart condition, home oxygen, or a clinician has given you a personal target.
- Don’t use a home oximeter reading to talk yourself out of help if you feel seriously unwell.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency danger signs first.**  
   Call **999** now if you have severe or rapidly worsening breathlessness, you cannot finish a short sentence at rest, your chest feels tight or heavy, you have chest pain or pressure, you collapse or faint, you become very drowsy or confused, or your lips or skin look very pale, blue or grey. On brown or black skin, this may be easier to see on the palms of the hands.

2. **Do one careful recheck to reduce the chance of a misleading reading.**  
   Sit upright and rest quietly for about **5 minutes**. Warm your hand. Remove nail varnish or a false nail from the finger you are using. Put the oximeter on a different finger, keep your hand still at chest or heart level, and wait until the number has stopped changing.

3. **If the reading stays at 92% or lower, treat it as urgent.**  
   If your SpO₂ is **92% or below** after an immediate recheck, call **999** or go to A&E. Do not delay if you also feel unwell.

4. **If the reading is 93% or 94%, or it is lower than normal for you, get urgent NHS advice.**  
   Contact your local urgent NHS route, such as **111** where available, **111 online** where available, or your GP urgent line. If you become more breathless, cannot get through, or feel seriously unwell while waiting, call **999**.

5. **If you are pregnant or have recently given birth, use a lower threshold for urgent help.**  
   If your oxygen reading is dropping or is **94% or lower**, contact your maternity unit, midwife, GP, or urgent NHS route promptly. Call **999** if you have severe symptoms or cannot get urgent advice quickly.

6. **If you have a known lung or heart condition, home oxygen, or a personal oxygen target, follow your plan.**  
   Your usual safe range may be different. If you do not have a plan and this reading is unexpectedly low for you, contact your urgent NHS route or GP urgently; call **999** for severe symptoms.

7. **Write down the details before you call.**  
   Note your best stable SpO₂ reading, your pulse rate if shown, your main symptoms, any lung or heart conditions, pregnancy or recent birth, home oxygen use, and whether the reading is dropping.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to keep taking readings repeatedly once you have a stable recheck and a plan to get help.
- You do not need to decide about tests, medicines, devices, or long-term monitoring today.
- You do not need to search online for every possible explanation before getting urgent help.

## Important reassurance
A low pulse oximeter reading can sometimes be caused by cold hands, movement, nail varnish, false nails, poor fit, or device limitations. Pulse oximeters can also be less reliable for some people, including people with darker skin, so symptoms and changes from your usual reading matter. You are not overreacting by treating a persistent low reading or worrying symptoms as urgent.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the next few minutes to hour. Later decisions about diagnosis, treatment, monitoring, or oxygen targets may need help from NHS 111, your GP, your maternity team, emergency services, or another clinician.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not medical diagnosis, personal medical advice, or a substitute for urgent clinical assessment. If you feel seriously unwell, your breathing is worsening, or your reading stays low after a careful recheck, seek urgent NHS help.
