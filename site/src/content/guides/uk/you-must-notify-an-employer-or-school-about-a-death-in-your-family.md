# What to do if… you must notify an employer or school about a death in your family (UK)

## Short answer
Send a brief, factual message to your line manager or the school attendance office stating that there has been a death in your family and that you require immediate emergency leave. Request that they confirm the correct absence code and any policy requirements in writing once you have stabilized.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel obligated to share the cause of death or specific family details to justify your absence.
- Do not send long, emotional explanations while in the immediate shock of loss; keep the initial notification to essential facts only.
- Do not make permanent decisions about your job or education, such as resigning or withdrawing, in the first 48 hours.
- Do not attempt to negotiate leave pay or complex contract terms over the phone without a written record of the conversation.

## What to do now
1. **Send a single, short message to one designated contact at your workplace or school.**  
   - For work: Message your line manager or HR department via email or their preferred emergency contact method.
   - For school: Email the school’s attendance officer and copy the Head of Year or Pastoral Lead.
   - Template: "I am writing to notify you of a death in my immediate family. I am unable to attend [work/school] starting today. I will provide an update on my expected return by [Date/Time]. Please confirm how this absence is recorded."

2. **Identify if your situation qualifies for specific statutory rights.**  
   - If a child has died or there was a stillbirth after 24 weeks: You have a day-one right to two weeks of Statutory Parental Bereavement Leave.
   - If a spouse, partner, parent, or child has died: You have a statutory right to "Time off for Dependants" to take "reasonable" unpaid action (e.g., arranging a funeral or dealing with immediate logistics).
   - If a mother or primary adopter dies (from 6 April 2026): Eligible partners have a day-one right to "Bereaved Partner's Paternity Leave" of up to 52 weeks.

3. **Request that the absence be recorded under the most appropriate category immediately.**  
   - Ask your employer if the time will be recorded as Compassionate Leave, Time off for Dependants, or Annual Leave.
   - Ask the school to record the absence as "Authorised" to ensure it does not count toward the 10-day unauthorised absence threshold that triggers Local Authority reporting.

4. **Ask for the workplace or school bereavement policy to be sent to you in writing.**  
   - Do not rely on verbal promises about pay or leave duration. 
   - Request the "Bereavement Policy" or "Attendance Policy" via email so you can review your entitlements when you are less overwhelmed.

5. **Set a clear "no-contact" boundary for the next 24 to 48 hours.**  
   - State clearly: "I will check for messages again at [Time] on [Date]. Please do not call me before then unless it is an emergency regarding my pay or enrollment."

6. **Save a copy of every message you send and receive.**  
   - Screenshot text messages or save emails to a personal folder. 
   - If you have a phone call, write down the name of the person you spoke to and the time the call ended.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a death certificate or funeral order of service today.
- You do not need to commit to a return date if the situation is still evolving.
- You do not need to explain the nature of your relationship with the deceased beyond stating they were a "dependant" or "immediate family."
- You do not need to catch up on missed emails, assignments, or work projects during the initial shock period.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel unable to find the "right" words or to feel panicked about workplace or school reactions. A short, formal notification is a standard procedural step that managers and school staff are trained to handle. You are not failing by taking this time; you are exercising your right to manage a family emergency.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and notification only. It does not cover long-term grief support, probate, or complex employment tribunal procedures.

## Important note
This information is based on UK statutory rights as of 2026. Rights vary depending on whether you are an employee, a worker, or a student. If your employer or school refuses leave or threatens disciplinary action, contact ACAS (for work) or your local Citizens Advice Bureau (for school/work) for specific legal guidance.
