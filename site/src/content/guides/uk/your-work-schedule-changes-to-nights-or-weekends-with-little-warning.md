# What to do if… your work schedule changes to nights or weekends with little warning (UK)

## Short answer
Do not agree to a permanent change on the spot. Get the new rota or shift pattern confirmed in writing, check your contract and rota policy, and reply in writing with what you can and cannot safely cover while you ask for a quick discussion.

## Do not do these things
- Do not no-show a shift without telling anyone, because that can quickly become a conduct issue.
- Do not quit in the heat of the moment or hand in notice before you have checked your options.
- Do not rely on verbal promises like “it’s only for a bit”; ask for the new pattern, start date, and whether it is temporary in writing.
- Do not sign a new contract, variation letter, or rota agreement immediately if you are uncomfortable or unsure.
- Do not share personal medical details widely; keep health information to HR or occupational health if needed.
- Do not post about the dispute on social media or in group chats where it can be forwarded.
- Do not ignore a safety problem with night travel, fatigue, childcare cover, or health; put the specific problem in writing.

## What to do now
1. **Save proof of the change.** Screenshot the old rota, new rota, app notifications, texts, emails, and any message saying you must work the new nights or weekends. Note the date, time, who told you, and when the new pattern is supposed to start.

2. **Check the documents that control your shifts.** Look at your contract, written statement, staff handbook, rota policy, and any union or collective agreement. Check especially for normal hours, days of work, night work, Sunday work, overtime, flexibility clauses, how rotas are issued, and how much notice your workplace says it will normally give.

3. **Ask for the change in writing.** If you were told verbally, send a short message to your manager or HR: “Please confirm the new shift pattern, start date, whether it is temporary, how long it is expected to last, and how rotas will be issued or changed.” If the change affects details in your written statement of employment particulars, ask for the updated written particulars; employers must tell employees or workers about changes to the written statement within one month of making the change.

4. **Reply with practical constraints, not a long argument.** If nights or weekends cause a real problem, write down the specific issue: childcare, caring duties, transport, personal safety, health, study, or another job. Say what you can cover, what you cannot cover, and one workable alternative, such as a shorter temporary cover period, different days, a shift swap, or a phased start. Ask for a short meeting.

5. **If the new shifts include nights, check the night-work rules.** Staff who regularly work at least 3 hours during the night period are night workers. The night period is usually 11pm to 6am, unless a different 7-hour period including midnight to 5am has been agreed in writing. Night workers must not work more than an average of 8 hours in each 24-hour period; the average is usually calculated over 17 weeks, but can sometimes be longer by agreement. Your employer must offer a free health assessment before you become a night worker and regular assessments after that; you do not have to accept.

6. **Check rest between shifts before saying yes.** Workers over 18 are usually entitled to 11 hours’ rest between working days, weekly rest, and one uninterrupted 20-minute rest break during a working day of more than 6 hours. Some exceptions can apply, so write down the exact finish time and next start time if the new rota leaves you too tired or unsafe.

7. **If the weekend shift is a Sunday, check whether Sunday rules apply.** In general, Sunday working depends on your contract or written statement, and a worker cannot be made to work Sundays unless they agreed this with their employer and put it in writing. If you work in a shop or betting shop, there are specific Sunday opt-out rules, so check those before signing or agreeing to a new Sunday pattern.

8. **If you decide to cover some shifts for now, protect the paper trail.** If you work the new shifts to reduce immediate disciplinary risk, write that you are doing so temporarily and working under protest while the issue is discussed. Say clearly that you do not agree to a permanent change to your contracted hours or shift pattern. Repeat the objection in writing while the disagreement continues.

9. **Bring in support early if pressure is increasing.** If you have a union or staff representative, contact them and share the screenshots. If you do not, contact Acas for practical next steps, especially if you are being pressured to sign, threatened with discipline, or told the change has already happened without your agreement.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign.
- You do not need to write a long grievance letter immediately; first get the facts and the change confirmed in writing.
- You do not need to prove your whole personal situation; start with the practical constraints and a request to discuss alternatives.
- You do not need to decide now whether this is permanent, temporary, or something you may challenge later.
- If the change looks longer-term, you can consider a flexible working request later; you do not have to do that in the first 24 to 48 hours.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel blindsided when nights or weekends appear with little warning, especially if childcare, health, travel, or safety is affected. Getting the change written down and responding calmly buys time and reduces the risk of an irreversible mistake.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the situation, create a clear paper trail, and reduce immediate harm. If the employer insists on a permanent change, cuts pay, threatens discipline, or the situation may involve discrimination or health and safety risk, later decisions may need specialist employment advice.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employment rights depend on your contract, workplace policies, employment status, and circumstances. If you feel unsafe travelling or working at night, treat that as a safety issue and raise it promptly in writing.
