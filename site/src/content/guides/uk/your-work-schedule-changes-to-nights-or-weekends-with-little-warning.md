# What to do if… your work schedule changes to nights or weekends with little warning (UK)

## Short answer
Immediately comply with the "no contact" instruction to avoid allegations of witness tampering or insubordination, then request written clarification regarding the specific boundaries and a "workable route" to fulfill your remaining job duties.

## Do not do these things
- Do not contact the restricted colleagues to "clear the air" or explain the situation.
- Do not use personal messaging apps (WhatsApp, Messenger) or social media to bypass work systems.
- Do not ask third-party colleagues to pass messages or gather information on your behalf.
- Do not delete any evidence, including emails, chat logs, or calendar invites related to the matter under review.
- Do not post vague or "blind" status updates on social media that could be interpreted as referencing the workplace situation.

## What to do now
1. **Document the exact instruction as soon as you are alone.** 
   - Note the date, time, and location of the conversation. 
   - Record the specific names mentioned and the exact wording of the restriction. 
   - List any reasons given by management for the instruction.
2. **Request the "no contact" instruction in writing via a neutral email.** 
   - Ask for confirmation of which specific colleagues are included. 
   - Ask if the ban applies to work-related tasks, social interactions, or both. 
   - Ask if the restriction extends outside of working hours and off-site.
3. **Formalize a "workable route" for essential business communication.** 
   - Identify tasks that require input from the restricted colleagues. 
   - Propose a single point of contact (such as a manager or HR representative) to act as a relay for these tasks. 
   - Request that any necessary meetings involving restricted parties be re-routed or handled via written updates.
4. **Clarify your right to access evidence and witnesses for your defense.** 
   - If the matter is a formal investigation, ask the investigator how you can safely identify and request statements from witnesses without breaching the no-contact order. 
   - Confirm that you are permitted to discuss the matter with a trade union representative or a legal advisor.
5. **Request clarification on accompaniment for future meetings.** 
   - **Ask the employer to allow a companion for any investigation meetings**, even though the statutory right (S10 ERelA 1999) primarily applies to formal disciplinary hearings. 
   - Cite the 2026 ACAS guidance which recommends allowing accompaniment as a best practice to ensure procedural fairness.
6. **Maintain a private log of all work completed during the review period.** 
   - Record how the contact restriction affects your output to prevent later "performance" allegations. 
   - Keep all communications with management regarding the restriction strictly factual and professional.
7. **Verify the current status of the review process.** 
   - Ask for an estimated timeline for the review to be completed. 
   - Ask for the name of the designated person you should contact if you have questions about your employment status or the process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare a full rebuttal or defense statement until the specific allegations or "matters under review" are disclosed to you in writing.
- You do not need to decide whether to file a counter-grievance or a claim for constructive dismissal today.
- You do not need to inform your wider professional network or other colleagues about the restriction.
- You can wait to engage a solicitor until you have received formal notice of a disciplinary hearing.

## Important reassurance
A "no contact" instruction is a common administrative step used to ensure the integrity of a fact-finding process. It is frequently used to prevent "collusion" or "witness coaching" and does not constitute a finding of guilt. Following the instruction precisely is the most effective way to protect your professional reputation while the review is ongoing.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for employees in the UK facing temporary workplace restrictions. It focuses on harm reduction and procedural compliance and does not cover the long-term legal strategy for employment tribunals or complex whistleblowing cases.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment law in the UK is subject to change, including the 2026 transition toward a 6-month qualifying period for unfair dismissal. If you are a member of a trade union, contact your representative immediately. If the restriction causes significant distress or appears discriminatory, consider consulting a qualified employment solicitor or contacting the ACAS helpline.
