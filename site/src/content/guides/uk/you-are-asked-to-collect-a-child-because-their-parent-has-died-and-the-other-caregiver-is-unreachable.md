# What to do if… you are asked to collect a child because their parent has died and the other caregiver is unreachable (UK)

## Short answer
Do not remove the child from their current location until a professional with safeguarding authority (such as a school leader, police officer, or social worker) formally authorises the handover. Verify if you have legal Parental Responsibility (PR) or are on the pre-approved emergency collection list.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pressure staff to bypass safeguarding checks or "make an exception" because of the tragedy.
- Do not take the child to an unrecorded location or "disappear" to a relative's house without official notification.
- Do not promise the child specific long-term outcomes, such as who they will live with permanently.
- Do not post details of the death or the child's location on social media or in group messaging apps.
- Do not attempt to enter the deceased parent's home to collect belongings unless authorised by the police.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the child's current location and the specific person requesting the collection.**
   - Note the name and phone number of the person who called you.
   - Confirm if the child is at a school, Early Years setting, or with a neighbour.
2. **Request to speak with the Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) or the most senior manager on-site.**
   - **Explain your relationship to the child and whether you are on the authorised pickup list.**
   - **Ask the DSL to verify your identity using photo ID (passport or driving licence).**
3. **Establish if anyone currently reachable has Parental Responsibility (PR).**
   - **Ask the school or setting to check their records for a second parent, guardian, or emergency contact.**
   - **Provide any contact details you have for the other caregiver, even if they are currently unresponsive.**
4. **Follow the setting’s formal safeguarding procedure if PR cannot be established immediately.**
   - **Request that the school contact the Local Authority Multi-Agency Safeguarding Hub (MASH) or Children’s Social Care for a "Section 17" or emergency advice.**
   - **If it is outside normal office hours (after 5:00 PM), ask the school to contact the council’s Emergency Duty Team (EDT).**
5. **Call 101 (or 999 if the child is in immediate physical danger) if the child is not in a formal setting.**
   - **Explain clearly: "I am with a child whose primary caregiver has died, and the other parent is unreachable. I need a safe handover plan."**
   - **Wait for a police officer or social worker to arrive to formalise the temporary care arrangement.**
6. **Identify any immediate medical needs the child may have.**
   - **Ask the child or school staff if the child requires essential medication (e.g., inhalers, epipens) that may be inside the deceased parent's home.**
   - **Request that authorities (Police or Social Care) facilitate the retrieval of these items if they are inaccessible.**
7. **Ensure the handover is documented before you leave with the child.**
   - **Ask for the name and role of the professional authorising the release.**
   - **Ensure they have recorded where you are taking the child and your contact number.**
8. **Provide a stabilising environment during the transition.**
   - **Use clear, factual language: "You are safe. We are going to [Location] while the other grown-ups finish making the plans."**
   - **Avoid investigating the child for information about the death.**
9. **Clarify the "authority plan" for the next 12 to 24 hours.**
   - **Confirm who is the lead professional for the case (Social Worker or Police Officer).**
   - **Ask specifically what you are authorised to consent to (e.g., emergency medical care) while the child is with you.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on permanent custody or guardianship today.
- You do not need to begin funeral arrangements or notify extended family members immediately.
- You do not need to collect the child’s birth certificate or passport unless requested by Children’s Social Care.
- You do not need to clear out the deceased parent's home or manage their estate.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel an intense pressure to "just get the child home," but slowing down to follow safeguarding protocols protects both the child and you. Professional settings (schools and social care) are trained for these crises; following their lead ensures the child's legal and physical safety is documented.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate handover of a child in the UK. It does not cover long-term fostering, probate, or legal guardianship applications.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. In a crisis involving the death of a parent, the Local Authority and the Police hold the ultimate legal responsibility for the child's immediate welfare and placement decisions.
