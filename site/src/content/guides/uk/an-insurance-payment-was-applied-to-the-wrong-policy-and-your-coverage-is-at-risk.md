# What to do if… an insurance payment was applied to the wrong policy and your coverage is at risk (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as time-critical: contact your insurer or broker, ask them to urgently allocate the payment to the correct policy, and ask for written confirmation of your current cover status before the lapse or cancellation point.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the payment will “catch up” without confirmation; automated non-payment processes can still run.
- Don’t cancel a Direct Debit, reverse a card payment, or reclaim a payment in panic before checking how that affects the policy.
- Don’t rely only on a verbal “it’s fine”; ask for written confirmation or a clear note on the policy record.
- Don’t drive if this is motor insurance and you have been told cover has ended, or you cannot confirm that you are insured.
- Don’t cancel the “wrong” policy until the insurer or broker has explained what that would do to the payment and any live cover.

## What to do now
1. **Get the essentials in front of you.**  
   Find the policy number that should have been paid, the policy number that wrongly received the payment if you know it, the payment date, the amount, the payment method, the payment reference, and proof such as a bank statement line, card receipt, Direct Debit reference, or broker receipt.

2. **Call the insurer’s payments or collections team, or your broker if you arranged it through one.**  
   Say: “A premium payment appears to have been allocated to the wrong policy. My cover may lapse soon. Please urgently allocate it to the correct policy and confirm my cover status in writing.”  
   Ask them to confirm:
   - whether the correct policy is active right now;
   - whether they can place a hold or note on any lapse or cancellation process while they check the payment;
   - what evidence they need from you today;
   - exactly what reference to use if any further payment is needed.

3. **Send the proof while you are still dealing with them.**  
   Use the insurer’s secure message system, email address, or broker portal if available. Include the correct policy number in the subject line and attach or paste the payment date, amount, reference, bank or card evidence, and the policy the payment was meant for.

4. **Ask for a written status message before relying on the cover.**  
   Ask for an email, secure message, or letter confirming one of these: the policy remains active, cancellation is on hold while the payment is corrected, or the exact steps needed to keep or restore cover.

5. **If lapse is very close, ask whether a protective payment is needed.**  
   Do not guess the amount or reference. Ask: “If I pay the correct policy again now, will that stop cancellation, and how will the duplicate or misapplied payment be refunded or transferred?” Only pay using the route and reference they give you.

6. **If you already have a lapse or cancellation notice, ask for continuation or reinstatement based on the allocation error.**  
   Say: “Please consider keeping the policy in force or reinstating it because the non-payment appears to come from a payment allocation error.” Ask what they need today to make that decision.

7. **Avoid an accidental uninsured gap while this is unresolved.**  
   If it is motor insurance and you cannot confirm cover is live, do not drive until you have confirmation. If it is home, landlord, business, pet, travel, or another policy, avoid making cancellations or changes that could make reinstatement harder until the payment position is clear.

8. **Create a short paper trail.**  
   After the call, send a brief message with the payment date, amount, correct policy number, who you spoke to, what they said about cover status, and what you are asking for: correct allocation, a cancellation hold if available, and written confirmation.

9. **If it is not fixed quickly, submit a formal complaint and keep it factual.**  
   Ask the insurer or broker for its complaints process and title the complaint “payment misallocated and cover at risk of lapse”. If you receive a final response you are unhappy with, or usually if the business has not responded within 8 weeks, you may be able to take an eligible complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service.

10. **If a Direct Debit was taken in error, speak to your bank or building society carefully.**  
   The Direct Debit Guarantee can give a refund route for a Direct Debit payment error, but a refund may also leave the insurance premium showing as unpaid. Before reclaiming the money, try to get written confirmation from the insurer or broker about whether cover will remain in place and how the premium will be corrected.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to switch insurers, cancel another policy, or argue about compensation.
- You do not need to reconstruct the entire history right now; focus on preventing lapse and getting the payment correctly allocated.
- You do not need to write a long complaint letter immediately; a short, dated, factual complaint is enough to start the record.
- You do not need to decide whether to use the Financial Ombudsman Service until the insurer or broker has had a chance to respond, unless the situation is still urgent and you need to check the route.

## Important reassurance
This is often a records or allocation problem, and it may be fixable if raised quickly with clear payment evidence. The most useful thing right now is to get the insurer or broker to confirm the cover position in writing while the payment is traced.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce the risk of an uninsured gap and create a clear record. Later decisions, such as complaints, refunds, compensation, reinstatement disputes, or legal action, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information for urgent first steps. It is not financial, legal, insurance, or professional advice, and it cannot guarantee that cover will continue or be reinstated. Policy terms, payment methods, cancellation rules, and complaint routes vary, so prioritise written confirmation from the insurer or broker and keep copies of everything you send and receive.
