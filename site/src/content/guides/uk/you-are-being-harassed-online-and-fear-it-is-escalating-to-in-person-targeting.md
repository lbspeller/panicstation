# What to do if… you are being harassed online and fear it is escalating to in-person targeting (UK)

## Short answer
If you think someone may come to you, knows where you are right now, or has made an immediate threat, get to a safer place with other people and call 999 or 112. If the risk is not immediate, reduce what they can learn about you, save evidence where it is safe to do so, and report threats or stalking behaviour.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t reply to argue, negotiate, threaten back, or “set boundaries” with the harasser.
- Don’t post your location, routine, workplace, school, travel plans, or proof that you are safe.
- Don’t click links they send, including “proof”, “dox”, login, or file links.
- Don’t agree to meet them or let anyone persuade you to “talk it out” in person.
- Don’t delete posts, messages, emails, voicemails, or account details before saving evidence, unless keeping them visible creates a safety risk.
- Don’t delay calling 999, 112, 101, or reporting online just because your evidence is not perfectly organised.
- Don’t assume it is “just online” if they mention your real name, area, address, workplace, school, family, or routine.

## What to do now
1. Move into a safer pause first. If they know where you are right now, someone is looking for you nearby, you are being watched or followed, or there is a credible “I’m coming to you” threat, go indoors if you can, stay near other people, and call 999 or 112.

2. Tell one real-world person now. Send a simple message such as: “I’m being harassed online and I’m worried it could become in-person. Please stay reachable and check in with me.” If you are at work, school, university, or a building with reception or security, tell them today and ask them not to confirm whether you are there or let unknown visitors through.

3. Start a simple incident log. Write down the date and time, platform, account name or profile URL, what happened, and what made it feel threatening, such as “mentioned my street”, “posted my workplace”, or “said they were coming over”.

4. Save evidence where it is safe to do so. Take screenshots that show usernames, dates, times, profile details, and surrounding context. Copy URLs, keep original emails, direct messages, voicemails, and call logs, and save them somewhere you can find again.

5. Reduce what they can learn about you. Make accounts private or restrict who can message, comment, tag, or view your posts. Remove or hide your workplace, school, hometown, relationship links, check-ins, regular routes, and routine information.

6. Shut down location leaks. Turn off location sharing in phone settings and apps, stop automatic photo geotagging where you can, and ask friends or family not to tag you, post your location, or engage with the harasser.

7. Secure the accounts that could expose you. Start with your email account, then key social accounts. Change passwords, turn on 2-step verification, check recovery email addresses and phone numbers, remove anything you do not control, and sign out of other sessions if the service allows it.

8. Report the specific harmful content after evidence is saved, unless urgent safety means you need help first. Report threats, doxxing, impersonation, and posts sharing personal details through the platform’s reporting tools. Use mute, restrict, or block tools in the safest order for your situation.

9. If your personal details are being shared, ask for removal. Report the doxxing to the platform and contact the site hosting your address, phone number, workplace, or other personal data. If an organisation is holding or publishing your personal data, you may be able to ask for deletion under the UK right to erasure; this is not automatic and does not apply in every case, so keep the wording cautious and keep a copy of what you asked for.

10. Contact the police if there are threats, stalking behaviour, doxxing, impersonation, or escalation signs. In an emergency, call 999 or 112. If it is not an emergency, call 101 or report online through your local police force. Say plainly: “I’m being harassed online and I’m worried it could become in-person targeting.” Use your incident log to explain the pattern and ask for a reference number.

11. Contact specialist stalking support if the behaviour is persistent, fixated, or frightening. The National Stalking Helpline can give practical safety advice on 0808 802 0300 or through its website.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to delete all accounts, change your phone number, move home, or take legal action.
- You do not need to prove anything to the harasser or to strangers online.
- You can leave deep digital clean-up, old posts, old accounts, and wider data removal until you are safer, calmer, and supported.
- You can decide later whether to tell more people, make formal complaints, or get legal advice.

## Important reassurance
Feeling panicked, exposed, or hyper-alert is a normal response to targeted harassment. Taking practical steps early is reasonable, especially when someone is trying to make you feel reachable in real life.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: reach a safer pause, reduce exposure, preserve useful evidence where safe, and connect with appropriate support. Later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information only and is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 999 or 112.
