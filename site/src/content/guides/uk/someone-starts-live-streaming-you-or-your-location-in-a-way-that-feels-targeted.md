# What to do if… someone starts live-streaming you or your location in a way that feels targeted (UK)

## Short answer
Create immediate distance by moving to a staffed, public location and treat the situation as a personal safety incident rather than a social media interaction.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attempt to grab the person's phone or physically block the camera (this often escalates violence and creates "viral" conflict footage).
- Don’t engage in an argument or try to "explain" why they should stop, as this provides content for the stream.
- Don’t lead the person toward your home, your car, or a quiet side street.
- Don’t use your own phone with the flash on or in a high-profile way that might provoke the streamer further.

## What to do now
1. **Move immediately to a "safer pause" location with witnesses or security.** **Walk toward a shop, café, hotel lobby, or public building where there are staff or significant numbers of people present.** If you are in a vehicle, stay inside with the doors locked and drive to a well-lit, busy area or a police station.
2. **Call 999 if you feel you are in immediate danger or the person is following you.** **If you cannot speak safely, use the "Silent Solution" by dialing 999 and then pressing 55 when prompted by the automated message.** This signals to the operator that you are in a genuine emergency but cannot talk; on a mobile, the call will be terminated if you do not press 55.
3. **Deactivate live location sharing on your own device.** **Go into your phone settings and turn off "Find My" or "Share My Location" and disable location permissions for all social media apps.** This ensures that even if you are being "doxxed" on their stream, your phone is not broadcasting your precise movements to others who might be watching.
4. **Note key details of the incident only once you are in a secure spot.** **Briefly record the platform being used (e.g., TikTok, Instagram, X), the account name if visible, the time, and your exact location.** Look for identifying features of the person, such as clothing or a vehicle registration, but do not look back or stop moving to do this.
5. **Contact the National Stalking Helpline if this is part of a pattern of behavior.** **Call 0808 802 0300 for specialist advice on personal safety and legal rights regarding targeted harassment.** Note their operating hours: 09:30–20:00 on Monday and Wednesday, and 09:30–16:00 on Tuesday, Thursday, and Friday.
6. **Report the stream to the hosting platform via their in-app reporting tool.** **Select options for "Harassment," "Privacy Violation," or "Doxxing" to trigger an automated review of the live content.** If you have a friend you can contact safely, ask them to find the stream and report it from their device so you can focus on your physical surroundings.
7. **Report the incident to the police via 101 or online if there is no immediate threat.** **State clearly that you have been the victim of "targeted harassment and non-consensual filming in public" and provide the account details you noted earlier.** Under the Protection from Sex-based Harassment in Public Act 2023 and the Protection from Harassment Act 1997, police can investigate if the conduct causes alarm or distress.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if this qualifies as a specific crime like "stalking" before seeking help.
- You do not need to capture your own video or screenshots of the streamer if doing so puts you at risk.
- You do not need to contact the streamer’s followers or the platform’s support team for a long-term resolution in this moment.

## Important reassurance
Feeling vulnerable or panicked when your privacy is suddenly breached in public is a standard physiological response. Your priority is your physical safety and creating distance; professional services and platforms are responsible for managing the digital evidence later.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active incident. It does not cover long-term legal claims for privacy breaches or the removal of recorded footage after a stream has ended.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or security advice. Public filming is generally permitted in the UK, but becomes a police matter when it involves harassment, threats, or a course of conduct intended to cause distress.
