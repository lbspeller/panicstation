# What to do if… you return home and find a window screen removed or a window left slightly open when you know you closed it (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the situation as a potential active break-in: do not enter the property, move to a safe distance, and call 999 if you suspect an intruder is still inside. If you are certain the property is empty and the event happened in the past, call 101 or report online.

## Do not do these things
- Don't enter the property to "check if anyone is there" or call out to see if someone answers.
- Don't touch the window, the window frame, the removed screen, or any door handles, as this can destroy forensic evidence like fingerprints or DNA.
- Don't tidy up, move furniture, or rummage through belongings to see what is missing before police arrive.
- Don't attempt to block exits or confront anyone you see leaving the property.
- Don't walk around the back or side of the house if it requires entering narrow, unlit, or confined spaces where you could be cornered.
- Don't share your location or the fact that you are standing outside an insecure home on public social media.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a position of safety immediately.** **Leave the immediate vicinity of the open window or door and go to a location where you can observe from a distance or wait securely, such as a neighbour’s house, a nearby shop, or your locked vehicle.**
2. **Determine whether to call 999 or 101.** **Call 999 if you believe the crime is in progress, you have seen or heard someone inside, or you suspect an offender has only just left the scene.**
   - Call **101** only if you are completely certain the intruder has gone and you are in no immediate danger.
3. **Use the Silent Solution if you are in danger and cannot speak.** **If you call 999 from a mobile but must remain silent, listen for the automated prompt and press 55 when instructed to be transferred to the police.**
   - Be aware that pressing 55 does not automatically provide your precise location to the operator from a mobile; try to provide a location if it becomes safe to whisper.
4. **Note specific details about the scene without approaching.** **Record which window is affected, the position of the screen, and any descriptions of people or vehicles you saw near the property upon your arrival.**
   - Include direction of travel for any fleeing suspects or vehicles.
5. **Contact other residents or expected visitors from your safe location.** **Call or text housemates, family members, or tradespeople who have a key to confirm if they opened the window or removed the screen.**
   - Do not assume a "yes" until you have spoken to them directly; do not enter the house to find them if they don't answer.
6. **Check remote security feeds if available.** **Use your phone to view doorbell cameras or internal security monitors from your safe position to see if an intruder is visible.**
7. **Wait for the police to arrive before entering.** **Once officers are on site, follow their instructions exactly and point out the specific areas you believe were tampered with.**
8. **Request a crime reference number once the report is taken.** **Keep this number for your records as you will need it for any future insurance claims or security follow-ups.**

## What can wait
- You don’t need to determine exactly what was stolen or the value of items right now.
- You don’t need to fix the window or reattach the screen until the police have finished their initial scene assessment.
- You don’t need to call your insurance company or landlord until the immediate safety risk has been cleared by the police.
- You don’t need to decide on long-term security upgrades like new alarms or reinforced glass in this moment.

## Important reassurance
It is completely normal to feel a sudden rush of adrenaline, fear, or confusion when you find your home has been disturbed. Taking these precautions is a logical safety measure, not an overreaction. Even if it turns out a housemate simply left the window open, choosing to stay outside until you are sure is the safest choice you can make.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the minutes immediately following the discovery of a potential entry point. It does not cover forensic procedures, insurance claim management, or long-term psychological support.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional safety advice. Always prioritise your personal safety over property and follow the direct commands of emergency dispatchers and attending police officers.
