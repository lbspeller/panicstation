# What to do if… you receive an alert that a trusted device or backup email was removed from your account (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your mobile network's fraud or porting team immediately using a trusted number from their official website to cancel the unauthorised transfer and lock your account.

## Do not do these things
- Don't click any links, "cancel" buttons, or attachments within the suspicious email itself.
- Don't use any phone numbers provided in the email; they may lead to the fraudster.
- Don't wait to see if it is a mistake, as porting requests often complete within one working day.
- Don't use SMS-based password resets for any account until you have confirmed your phone line is secure.
- Don't trust "security" calls from people claiming to be your bank or network following the email; hang up and call back on a known number.

## What to do now
1. **Call your mobile network provider immediately and ask to speak with the fraud or porting department.**  
   - State clearly that you have received an unauthorised porting notification and did not request a PAC (Porting Authorisation Code).
   - Request that they cancel any pending port-out or SIM-swap requests.
   - Ask them to place a "port-out lock" or additional security PIN on your account.
   - Verify if any contact details or recovery emails on your mobile account have been changed.

2. **Check your mobile handset for a "No Service" or "SIM Failure" message.**  
   - If your phone has suddenly lost signal while others have service, the port or SIM-swap may have already occurred.
   - Tell the network provider immediately if you have lost service, as this confirms the takeover is active.

3. **Change your primary email password using a secure, separate device.**  
   - Hackers often access mobile accounts via a compromised email address.
   - Set up two-factor authentication (2FA) using an authenticator app rather than SMS.
   - Check your email settings for any "Forwarding Rules" or "Auto-delete" filters you did not set up.

4. **Contact your bank's fraud department to alert them that your phone number is compromised.**  
   - Tell them that any SMS-based one-time passcodes (OTPs) sent to your number may be intercepted.
   - Ask them to monitor for unusual activity or temporarily suspend high-risk transactions.
   - You can call **159** from your mobile or landline to be connected to the fraud department of most major UK banks.

5. **Secure other high-value accounts that use your mobile number for recovery.**  
   - Prioritise banking, cryptocurrency wallets, Apple ID, Google Account, and social media.
   - Update your security settings to remove your phone number as a recovery method where possible.

6. **Report the incident to the appropriate authorities based on your location.**  
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report to **Action Fraud** online or by calling **0300 123 2040** (Monday to Friday, 8am-8pm).
   - In Scotland, report the fraud to **Police Scotland** by calling **101**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify exactly how the fraudsters obtained your data right now.
- You do not need to notify every contact in your phone book immediately; focus on financial and primary identity accounts first.
- You do not need to buy a new handset or change your permanent number today.

## Important reassurance
Unauthorised porting is a known fraud tactic, and mobile networks have specific protocols to reverse these actions if caught early. Losing signal or receiving these emails is stressful, but taking these steps systematically will help you regain control of your digital identity.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an unauthorised number transfer. It does not cover long-term identity theft recovery or legal proceedings against providers.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Mobile network security procedures and the availability of "port locks" vary by provider. Always use official contact details found on a provider's verified website or the back of your bank card.
