# What to do if… your child tells you an adult made a sexual comment or request and you feel unsure what to do next (UK)

## Short answer
Ensure your child is physically safe and separated from the adult immediately, then contact a professional safeguarding service for advice before taking further action. Do not confront the adult or investigate the claims yourself, as this can increase risk or compromise evidence.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the adult, call them, or send "trap" messages to get a confession.
- Do not ask your child to repeat the story multiple times or "prove" it happened.
- Do not promise your child that you will keep it a secret; promise instead to keep them safe.
- Do not delete any messages, call logs, or digital evidence you have already seen.
- Do not search for more evidence on your child’s device if you haven't found it yet.
- Do not share details on social media, community groups, or school WhatsApp chats.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize your child's immediate physical safety by removing them from any potential contact with the adult.** This includes stopping all planned visits, lifts, clubs, or digital messaging immediately without providing a detailed explanation to the adult.
2. **Respond to your child with calm, neutral validation without asking leading questions.** Say something like: "Thank you for telling me. I believe you. You are not in trouble and I am going to make sure you are safe."
3. **Record exactly what was said in a factual written note as soon as you have a private moment.** Include the date, time, who was present, and your child’s specific words rather than your interpretation of them.
4. **Preserve existing digital evidence without forwarding or further investigating it.** If there are messages or images on a phone or computer, leave the device as it is; if you must, take a single screenshot of the most relevant item and keep it on the original device.
5. **Contact a specialist safeguarding professional today to discuss the next steps.** You can use the following services:
   - **NSPCC Helpline:** Call **0808 800 5000** (available 10:00–16:00 Monday to Friday) or email **help@nspcc.org.uk** (available 24 / 7).
   - **Local Authority Children's Social Care:** Search your local council website for "report a safeguarding concern" to find their 24-hour duty team.
6. **Call 999 immediately if you believe a child is in imminent danger or the adult is currently present.** If there is no immediate danger but you suspect a crime has occurred, call **101** to speak with the police.
7. **Report online sexual requests or grooming directly to CEOP if the contact happened via the internet.** The **Child Exploitation and Online Protection Command (CEOP)** provides a dedicated reporting tool for parents concerned about online sexual behavior.
8. **Notify the Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) if the adult is connected to a school or regulated club.** Ask to speak specifically to the **Designated Safeguarding Lead**; give them a factual summary and ask for their immediate support in ensuring the adult has no further access to your child.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether you want to "press charges" or go to court right now.
- You do not need to be 100 percent certain that a crime was committed before asking for help.
- You do not need to explain the situation to the adult’s family or mutual friends today.
- You do not need to figure out a long-term counseling plan in the next few hours.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel panicked, sick, or fearful that you are making a "big deal" out of nothing. Taking safeguarding steps is a protective action, not a final judgment; professionals are trained to help you navigate these uncertainties without you having to be an expert.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Safeguarding processes vary by local authority, and formal investigations are the responsibility of the police and social care services.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or clinical advice. Always prioritize the advice of local safeguarding professionals and emergency services.
