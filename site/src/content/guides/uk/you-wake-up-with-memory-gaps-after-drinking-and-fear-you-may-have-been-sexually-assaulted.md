# What to do if… you wake up with memory gaps after drinking and fear you may have been sexually assaulted (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safe place first. Then contact a sexual assault service, urgent healthcare, or 999 if you are in immediate danger or seriously unwell.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force yourself to reconstruct the night, replay memories, or fill gaps right now.
- Do not confront, meet, or message anyone who makes you feel unsafe.
- Do not post details publicly while you are shocked or unsure.
- Do not assume it was your fault because you drank, froze, cannot remember, or did not react in a certain way.
- If you may want the option of a forensic medical examination, try not to wash, shower, brush your teeth, or change clothes until you have spoken to a sexual assault service or clinician. If you have already done any of these, you can still get help.
- If you have already changed clothes, put the clothes you were wearing in a clean paper bag or clean pillowcase if you can do that without distress.

## What to do now
1. **Get yourself safe first.** If you are in immediate danger, call **999**. If you are near someone who worries you, move to a safer place such as a trusted friend’s home, a public place, hotel reception, a staffed venue, or A&E.
2. **Bring in one safe person if you can.** Call or message someone you trust and ask them to come to you, stay on the phone, or help you reach care. You do not have to explain everything.
3. **Contact a sexual assault service.** These services can offer medical care, emotional support, and options around forensic examination without forcing you to report to police. Use the official links below for England, Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland.
4. **Get urgent medical help if your body feels wrong.** Go to **A&E** or call **999** if you have pain, bleeding, breathing problems, trouble swallowing, neck pain, a hoarse voice, confusion, repeated vomiting, severe headache, loss of consciousness, or any injury that worries you.
5. **If you suspect spiking, say that clearly when you seek help.** Tell the clinician or sexual assault service that you have memory gaps after drinking and are worried about spiking, so they can advise what health checks, support, and documentation may still be possible.
6. **Use NHS 111 or your local urgent health route if you are unsure and it is not an emergency.** Say: “I have memory gaps after drinking and I’m worried I may have been sexually assaulted or spiked. I need the fastest route to appropriate care.”
7. **Write a short note only if it helps.** In your phone or on paper, write what you already know: where you were, who you were with, when you last felt okay, where you woke up, and any pain, injuries, missing items, or changed clothing. Do not pressure yourself to remember more.
8. **For confidential support now, use a specialist line.** In England and Wales, the 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line supports people aged 16 and over on **0808 500 2222** or by online chat. Elsewhere in the UK, use the official local sexual assault service links below or urgent healthcare.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to report to police.
- You do not need to know whether the right word is assault, rape, spiking, blackout, or something else before getting care.
- You do not need a full timeline or proof before contacting a sexual assault service.
- You do not need to reply to messages, see anyone from last night, or explain yourself to people who are not helping you feel safer.
- You do not need to make big decisions about work, relationships, social media, or the rest of your life while you are in shock.

## Important reassurance
Memory gaps after drinking can feel frightening and unreal, especially when you wake up feeling that something is wrong. Uncertainty does not mean you are overreacting. You are allowed to seek help, ask questions, and take this one step at a time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: get safe, get medical or specialist support, and keep immediate options open. Later decisions about reporting, healthcare, evidence, or ongoing support may need help from a sexual assault service, clinician, advocate, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, forensic, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe or seriously unwell, use emergency services. You can ask for help even if you are unsure what happened.
