# What to do if… you cannot access the home of a person who died and need to retrieve essential items (UK)

## Short answer
Do not force entry unless there is an immediate safety emergency. Treat the home as part of the estate, identify the person with authority or likely authority to deal with it, and arrange the narrowest possible witnessed access for essentials only.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t break in, climb in, force a window, or ask someone to “just pop the lock”.
- Don’t remove valuables for safekeeping unless you clearly have authority to deal with estate property, and even then keep an inventory.
- Don’t hand keys, documents, jewellery, cash, cards, phones, or laptops to relatives under pressure at the door.
- Don’t argue at the property with relatives, neighbours, a landlord, or building staff.
- Don’t post the address, key location, alarm code, or contents in group chats or on social media.
- Don’t cancel utilities, change locks, clear the home, or dispose of belongings in a hurry.
- Don’t enter alone if there is a family dispute or any risk that someone may later accuse you of taking things.
- Don’t enter to check a suspected gas smell yourself, and don’t use flames, switches, or electrical devices near a suspected gas leak.

## What to do now
1. **Separate “urgent essentials” from “estate belongings”.**  
   Write a very short list of what needs access now, such as:
   - a pet that may need food, water, or removal to safety
   - medication, medical equipment, or mobility aids needed by someone else
   - documents needed immediately to register the death, identify the property, or contact the landlord, building manager, insurer, or utility supplier
   - a safety check for an appliance, water leak, unsecured door, or similar immediate risk

2. **Find the person with authority, or likely authority, to deal with the estate.**  
   If there is a will, the named executor is usually the starting point. If there is no will, someone may need to apply for authority to deal with the estate. The paperwork and names vary within the UK; in England and Wales this may involve probate or letters of administration, and in Scotland it may involve confirmation.  
   Send one calm message: “I cannot access the home and essential items are inside. Please confirm who is dealing with the estate and what proof is needed before anyone enters.”

3. **Identify the gatekeeper for the property.**  
   - **Rented home:** contact the landlord, letting agent, council, housing association, or housing officer. Ask for supervised access for named urgent essentials only.
   - **Flat or managed building:** contact the building manager, concierge, managing agent, factor, or housing officer about keys, fobs, entry procedures, and any rule about locksmiths.
   - **Owner-occupied home:** check whether another executor, close relative, neighbour, keyholder, solicitor, or trusted friend has a key before considering any forced-entry option.

4. **Ask for a short, witnessed access arrangement.**  
   Keep the request narrow: “We need 20 minutes to feed or remove the pet, collect the named medication, check the cooker and taps, and take the document folder from the hall table.” Ask for the landlord, building staff, a neighbour, another family member, or a trusted friend to witness the entry.

5. **If a pet or immediate hazard may be inside, say that first.**  
   For a pet, ask the keyholder, landlord, building manager, or housing officer for urgent supervised access. If you smell gas or suspect carbon monoxide, do not enter to check; move away and call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999. For fire, a crime in progress, or someone in immediate danger, call 999.

6. **If you use a locksmith, keep it limited and documented.**  
   Only do this if you have permission from the person or organisation controlling access, clear authority to act for the estate, or a genuine urgent safety reason. Bring ID and any proof you have, such as the death certificate, will, tenancy details, letters from the landlord, or messages from the person dealing with the estate. Have a witness present, take a few photos of the door and lock before and after, and record who attended.

7. **Take only the essentials you named.**  
   Make a note in your phone before leaving: date, time, address, who was present, what was taken, where each item was found, and where it is now being kept. Photograph the items together before you leave if that feels safe and proportionate.

8. **Secure the property before leaving.**  
   Re-lock doors and windows, reset any alarm if you know how, and do not change locks on a rented home without the landlord or agent’s agreement. Leave a brief note inside saying who entered, when, why, and how to contact the person holding the items.

9. **If there is conflict or you fear confrontation, use the police non-emergency route first.**  
   Call 101 to ask about preventing a breach of the peace or making a non-emergency enquiry. Police may be able to help with immediate safety, but they usually cannot decide who owns estate property or who should receive belongings. If someone is in immediate danger or a crime is in progress, call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who gets belongings, whether to sell anything, or how to clear the home.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements at the property.
- You do not need to hand over keys, valuables, documents, phones, laptops, or cards just because someone is pressuring you.
- You can ask landlords, building staff, banks, insurers, and official bodies what proof they need before deciding the next step.
- Full probate, confirmation, tenancy ending, succession, sale, storage, and distribution decisions usually do not need to be solved in the same moment as feeding a pet, collecting urgent medication, or reporting a hazard.

## Important reassurance
This situation often feels urgent because grief, keys, housing, and family roles collide at once. A slow, witnessed, well-recorded entry for essentials only is usually safer than trying to solve the whole estate or tenancy problem immediately.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: get to a safe pause, protect the property, retrieve genuinely urgent essentials, and reduce the risk of later accusations. Later decisions about probate, confirmation, tenancy, ownership, insurance, inheritance, and distributing belongings may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, housing, emergency, or professional advice. Rules and paperwork vary within the UK and by whether the home is rented, owned, jointly owned, managed, or connected to an investigation, so keep action minimal, witnessed, and focused on urgent essentials until authority is clear.
