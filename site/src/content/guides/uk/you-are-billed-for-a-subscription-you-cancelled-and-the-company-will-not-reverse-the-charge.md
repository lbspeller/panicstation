# What to do if… you are billed for a subscription you cancelled and the company will not reverse the charge (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the next payment first, then start the refund or dispute route for how you paid. Keep the proof that you cancelled, but do not spend the day arguing with the company before protecting the next payment.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume replacing your card will automatically stop a recurring card payment.
- Don’t keep waiting if another payment is due soon.
- Don’t close your bank account in a panic; it can make refunds and disputes harder.
- Don’t send original documents or unnecessary ID scans to the company.
- Don’t ignore overdraft fees, missed bills, or hardship caused by the charge; tell your bank promptly.

## What to do now
1. **Identify how the payment was taken.**
   - Open your banking app, card account, or PayPal account.
   - Note the date, amount, merchant name, and whether it appears to be a Direct Debit, a card recurring payment, or a PayPal automatic payment.

2. **Stop the next payment from leaving.**
   - **Direct Debit:** cancel the Direct Debit with your bank or building society.
   - **Card recurring payment:** tell your card provider that you withdraw consent for any further recurring payment to that merchant and want it stopped.
   - **PayPal:** cancel or stop the automatic payment, subscription, billing agreement, or saved business for that merchant in PayPal.

3. **Save the proof, then stop gathering evidence.**
   - Save the cancellation email, cancellation screen, reference number, or account page showing cancelled or not subscribed.
   - Save a screenshot or PDF of the charge on your statement.
   - Save any message where the company refuses, delays, or says it cannot reverse the payment quickly.

4. **Send one short written message to the company.**
   - Say: “I cancelled on [date]. You took £[amount] on [date]. Please refund this payment and confirm the subscription is cancelled. I have attached the cancellation proof. Please give me a case reference.”
   - Use email, chat transcript, or the company’s complaint form so you have a timestamp.

5. **Start the refund or dispute route that matches the payment.**
   - **Direct Debit taken in error:** ask your bank for a refund under the Direct Debit Guarantee.
   - **Card payment taken after cancellation:** ask your card provider to dispute the payment, treat any further payment after withdrawal of consent as unauthorised, and try chargeback if appropriate.
   - **Credit card payment:** also ask whether Section 75 may apply if the service price was over £100 and up to £30,000. Do not rely on this alone, especially if PayPal, a marketplace, instalments, or a subscription structure was involved.
   - **PayPal payment:** open the relevant PayPal transaction or automatic payment record and use PayPal’s dispute or support route if the merchant will not resolve it.

6. **If the bank or card provider refuses to help, make it a complaint.**
   - Say: “I’m making a formal complaint. I withdrew consent for this recurring payment and I’m disputing a charge taken after cancellation.”
   - Ask for the complaint reference and the date by which they must respond.
   - Write down the date, time, person or team, and what they said.

7. **Escalate only when the complaint route is ready.**
   - If you get a final response and you remain unhappy, check whether you can take the complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service.
   - For many financial complaints, the business has up to 8 weeks to respond, but some payment-service complaints can have shorter 15 or 35 day response times. Use the deadline the firm gives you, or check with the Financial Ombudsman Service if you are unsure.
   - You usually have 6 months from the date of a final response to refer a complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a long complaint today; stop the next payment and start the correct dispute route first.
- You do not need to decide today whether to take legal action.
- You do not need to argue every term and condition now; focus on the cancellation date, the charge date, and the payment method.
- You do not need to post publicly or threaten the company to begin the bank, card, PayPal, or complaint route.

## Important reassurance
This is a common payment problem, and it does not mean you have failed to cancel properly. A calm timeline, cancellation proof, and the right payment route are usually more useful than repeated calls to the company.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stop further loss and start the refund process. If the company says you still owe money, says you agreed to new terms, or the disputed amount is large, later decisions may need specialist consumer or debt advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Banks, card providers, PayPal, card networks, and firms can apply different processes, so ask them to confirm the route and deadline for your specific payment.
