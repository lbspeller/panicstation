# What to do if… you discover your timesheet was changed after you submitted it (UK)

## Short answer
Save evidence of the original and changed timesheet before anything else changes. Then raise it calmly in writing with payroll or your manager, asking who changed it, why it changed, and how it will be corrected before pay is processed if possible.

## Do not do these things
- Do not accuse someone of fraud, theft, or misconduct in a meeting or group chat while you are still gathering facts.
- Do not change the timesheet back yourself if that could make the record harder to understand.
- Do not approve an amended timesheet or confirm hours until you have checked the dates, start and finish times, breaks, overtime, and any notes.
- Do not leave it unraised until after payday if payroll can still correct it before the run closes.
- Do not resign, post publicly, or send an angry message while you are shocked; save the evidence and use written facts first.

## What to do now
1. **Save what you can see right now.**
   - Download or export the timesheet if the system allows it.
   - Take screenshots showing the hours, dates, totals, and any "last edited by", "edited at", "history", or approval area.
   - Save submission emails, approval notifications, rota messages, shift confirmations, clock-in records, and any texts about the affected shifts.

2. **Write one factual message today.**
   Send payroll and your manager a short email or HR-system message saying:
   - you submitted your timesheet for the affected pay period;
   - you have noticed specific entries were changed after submission;
   - which dates, hours, breaks, or overtime entries changed;
   - you are asking for the edit reason, the audit trail if available, and confirmation of what hours will be used for pay.

3. **Ask for the edit history and matching records.**
   Ask to see or receive the timesheet history for the affected dates, plus any clock-in and clock-out logs, supervisor adjustment notes, rota records, or approval records that payroll is relying on.

4. **Check whether pay has already been affected.**
   - If you have already been paid, compare your payslip with your own record of the shifts and write down the difference in hours and pay.
   - If you have not been paid yet, ask payroll what cut-off applies and whether the disputed entries can still be corrected before the payroll run.

5. **Make a clean timeline for yourself.**
   Write down when you submitted the timesheet, when you noticed the change, what changed, who you contacted, and what they said. Keep it factual and save it somewhere you control.

6. **If the first reply does not fix it, use support inside work.**
   - If you are in a union, contact your union rep and share the saved records.
   - If informal contact does not resolve it, check your employer’s written grievance procedure and consider raising a formal grievance in writing.

7. **If you need records about you and they are not shared informally, consider a Subject Access Request.**
   You can generally ask your employer for copies of your personal information. Keep the request narrow: the dates, systems, timesheet history, payroll records, and manager or payroll notes about the changed entries. Some information may be withheld or redacted, especially if it includes other people’s information.

8. **If pay is shorted and the issue is not being fixed, get outside help early.**
   Contact Acas for advice before the calendar runs on. Employment tribunal time limits can be strict; going through a grievance does not change the time limit, although notifying Acas in time for early conciliation can pause the limit while early conciliation is taking place.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to take legal action, resign, or go public.
- You do not need a perfect calculation of every minute before raising the issue; identify the changed entries and ask payroll what will be paid.
- You can wait to write a longer statement until after the first response, unless the payroll cut-off is close.
- You do not need to label the change as deliberate. Start with the record, the edit, and the correction needed.

## Important reassurance
Finding a changed timesheet can feel alarming because it affects both trust and pay. A calm written record, screenshots, and a clear request for the audit trail often make the issue easier to correct without escalating immediately.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation, protect your pay record, and create a clear trail. Later decisions about grievances, deductions, tribunal claims, or data requests may need specialist help based on your contract, worker status, pay arrangements, and what your employer says.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, employment, data protection, or professional advice. Employment rights and the best route depend on your specific circumstances, so consider getting tailored advice from Acas, a union representative, an advice organisation, or a qualified adviser if the issue is not quickly resolved.
