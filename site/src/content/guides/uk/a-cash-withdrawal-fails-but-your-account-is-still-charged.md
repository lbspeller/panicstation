# What to do if… a cash withdrawal fails but your account is still charged (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank or building society to raise a "non-receipt of cash" dispute immediately, unless you can wait 24 to 48 hours for a potential automatic reversal. Record the ATM location, time, and terminal ID before leaving the site.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attempt the same withdrawal again at the same machine, as this may lead to multiple debits.
- Don’t leave the area without noting the ATM’s terminal ID number and exact location.
- Don’t discard any "transaction failed" or "out of service" receipts printed by the machine.
- Don’t share your PIN or card with anyone offering "help" at the machine.
- Don’t report the incident as "fraud" if you initiated the transaction; use the specific "ATM dispute" or "non-receipt of cash" claim route.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the ATM terminal ID and operator immediately.**  
   **Look for a sticker or screen message that provides the machine’s unique terminal ID and the name of the operator (e.g., NatWest, Cardtronics, or NoteMachine).**
   - Take a photograph of the ATM screen if it is displaying an error message.
   - Note the exact time and the specific shop or street name where the machine is located.
   - Check if the machine is part of the Link network by looking for the Link logo on the machine or nearby signage.

2. **Check your banking app to see if the transaction is "Pending" or "Completed."**  
   **Log into your mobile banking app to confirm the status of the withdrawal.**
   - If the transaction is "Pending," many UK banks (including Barclays and HSBC) may require you to wait up to 48 working hours for the system to automatically reverse the charge.
   - If the transaction is "Completed" (posted) and you did not receive the money, you must contact your bank to start a formal investigation.

3. **Call your bank's customer service line or use the in-app "Dispute a Transaction" tool.**  
   **Inform the bank that you are raising a dispute for "non-receipt of cash" at an ATM.**
   - Provide the ATM terminal ID, location, time, and the exact amount attempted.
   - Ask for a "Dispute Reference Number" and the expected timeframe for the investigation (usually 15 to 40 days).
   - Clarify if the bank will provide a "temporary credit" while they investigate the machine's internal logs.

4. **Alert the bank to any immediate financial hardship caused by the missing funds.**  
   **If this withdrawal failure leaves you unable to pay for essentials or risks causing bank fees, tell the advisor you are in "financial difficulty."**
   - Ask the bank to waive any overdraft fees or "returned item" charges that might occur due to the missing balance.
   - Request an urgent review of your account if you require immediate access to funds for food or bills.

5. **Wait for the ATM "reconciliation" process to complete.**  
   **Understand that the operator must physically count the cash remaining in the machine to verify the failure.**
   - The ATM operator (even if different from your bank) will compare the electronic logs with the physical cash balance.
   - If a "surplus" of cash is found matching your missing amount, the funds are typically returned to your bank and then to your account.

## What can wait
- You do not need to contact the Financial Ombudsman Service yet; you must first complete the bank's internal dispute and formal complaint process.
- You do not need to file a police report, as a technical ATM failure is a civil/contractual matter between you and the bank, not a crime.
- You do not need to replace your debit card unless the machine physically retained (swallowed) the card.

## Important reassurance
ATM "desyncs" are common technical errors, and UK banks have established "interchange" rules to handle these disputes. Because every ATM keeps a physical and electronic log of every note dispensed, there is a clear "paper trail" to prove you did not receive the cash. Most failures are resolved once the machine is balanced by the operator.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for a failed cash withdrawal in the UK. It does not cover long-term debt management or legal action against banks.

## Important note
This information is based on standard UK banking procedures as of 2026. Different banks and independent ATM operators may have varying internal timelines for reversing pending transactions. If you are in significant financial distress, contact your bank’s dedicated hardship team.
