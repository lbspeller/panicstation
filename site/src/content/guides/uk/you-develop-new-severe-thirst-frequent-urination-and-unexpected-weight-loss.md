# What to do if… you develop new severe thirst, frequent urination, and unexpected weight loss (UK)

## Short answer
New severe thirst, peeing much more than usual, and unexpected weight loss need urgent same-day medical advice because they can be signs of high blood sugar or diabetes. If you feel very unwell, are vomiting, are unusually sleepy or confused, have stomach pain, or are breathing faster or deeper than usual, call 999 or go to A&E.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait a few days to see if severe new thirst, frequent urination, and weight loss settle by themselves.
- Do not stop drinking fluids to reduce how often you pee.
- Do not use someone else’s diabetes medicine, leftover insulin, diuretics, or weight-loss medicines.
- Do not start intense exercise if you feel unwell, very thirsty, dizzy, dehydrated, or weak.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E or urgent care if you feel faint, confused, unusually sleepy, or unsafe to drive.
- Do not let a home blood sugar or ketone check delay getting urgent advice.

## What to do now
1. Check for emergency warning signs now. Call 999 or go to A&E if you have any of these:
   - vomiting or you cannot keep fluids down
   - stomach pain
   - breathing faster or deeper than usual
   - breath that smells fruity, like pear drops or nail polish remover
   - confusion, unusual sleepiness, or being hard to wake
   - severe dizziness, faintness, very dry mouth, or very little urine
   - symptoms are getting worse quickly

2. If you need A&E, do not drive yourself. Ask someone else to take you, or call 999 and ask for an ambulance.

3. If there are no emergency warning signs, get same-day help. Call your GP practice and ask for an urgent same-day appointment, or call NHS 111 and say: “I have new severe thirst, I’m peeing a lot, and I’m losing weight without trying. I’m worried about high blood sugar or diabetes.”

4. If the person with symptoms is a child, pregnant, frail, or becoming more unwell, treat this as more urgent and use NHS 111, an urgent GP appointment, or 999 if emergency warning signs appear.

5. If you have your own clean, working blood glucose meter immediately available, you can check a blood sugar now. Do not delay calling your GP, NHS 111, or 999 while trying to find or repeat a test.

6. If you already have ketone testing supplies, check ketones. Blood ketones over 3 mmol/L, urine ketones over 2+, or diabetic ketoacidosis warning signs when you cannot check ketones mean you should call 999 or go to A&E.

7. While arranging help, sip water regularly unless a clinician has told you to restrict fluids. Avoid alcohol and avoid strenuous activity.

8. Write down or be ready to say:
   - when the thirst, urination, and weight loss started
   - roughly how much weight you have lost and over what time
   - how often you are peeing, especially at night
   - any vomiting, stomach pain, fever, infection, blurred vision, fruity breath, sleepiness, or confusion
   - current medicines, especially steroids, diuretics, or diabetes medicines
   - pregnancy status, if relevant

9. Take your medicines list, allergies, NHS number if known, and any home blood sugar or ketone readings to the assessment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out what type of diabetes it might be right now.
- You do not need to start a special diet, stop eating, fast, cleanse, or make a long-term treatment plan today.
- You do not need to buy diabetes equipment before being assessed.
- Non-urgent work, school, travel, exercise, and social plans can wait until you have had medical advice.

## Important reassurance
These symptoms can feel alarming, but getting checked promptly is the safest next step. You are not expected to diagnose yourself; your job right now is to get urgent advice and avoid anything that could make dehydration or illness worse.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, for the next few hours and the immediate safe pause. Diagnosis, treatment, monitoring, and longer-term decisions may need help from a GP, urgent care service, hospital team, diabetes specialist, or other appropriate clinician.

## Important note
This guide is general information and is not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel severely unwell, rapidly worse, confused, unusually sleepy, unable to keep fluids down, or unsafe to travel without help, call 999 or go to A&E.
