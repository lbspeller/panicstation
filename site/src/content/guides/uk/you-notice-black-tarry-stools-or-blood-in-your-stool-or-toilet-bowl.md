# What to do if… you notice black, tarry stools or blood in your stool or toilet bowl (UK)

## Short answer
Treat black, tarry stools, dark red poo, bloody diarrhoea, or visible blood in the toilet as something that needs medical advice urgently. Call 999 or go to A&E now if there is a lot of blood, bleeding is not stopping, you feel faint or very unwell, your poo is black, sticky and very smelly, or you are vomiting blood or coffee-ground-like material.

## Do not do these things
- Do not dismiss new black, tarry, sticky, very smelly, dark red, or bloody poo as “just food” or “just tablets” without getting advice.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you feel weak, dizzy, faint, short of breath, confused, or very unwell; call 999 or ask someone else to take you.
- Do not stop prescribed anticoagulants or antiplatelets, such as warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban, dabigatran, aspirin, or clopidogrel, unless a clinician tells you to.
- Do not take aspirin, ibuprofen, or naproxen for pain unless a clinician has told you to use them.
- Do not use laxatives, enemas, or bowel-cleansing products to try to “clear it out.”
- Do not delay getting help because you feel embarrassed; clinicians deal with bleeding symptoms routinely.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency signs now. Call 999 or go to A&E if any apply:**
   - Bleeding is non-stop.
   - There is a lot of blood, the toilet water turns red, or you see large clots.
   - Your poo is black, sticky, very smelly, or looks tarry.
   - You are vomiting blood or vomit that looks like coffee grounds.
   - You feel faint, dizzy, confused, very weak, short of breath, cold and clammy, or generally very unwell.
   - You have severe tummy pain, sudden chest pain, or pain when your tummy is touched.
2. **If it is not a 999 emergency but your poo is black, tarry, dark red, or you have bloody diarrhoea, get urgent advice today.** Ask for an urgent GP appointment, call 111 where available, or use your local NHS urgent care route. If emergency signs appear while you are waiting, call 999.
3. **If you take blood thinners, antiplatelets, or have a bleeding disorder, make that clear straight away.** Tell 111, your GP, A&E, or the ambulance crew the exact medicine name, dose, and when you last took it. Ask urgent advice before making any medicine changes yourself.
4. **Write down or photograph the key details if it is safe and not distressing.** Note when it started, how many times it has happened, whether it looked bright red, dark red, black, sticky, or tarry, and whether there was diarrhoea, vomiting, fever, tummy pain, dizziness, weakness, or breathlessness.
5. **Gather medicines and recent relevant details.** Take your medicine packets or a list with you, including blood thinners, aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen, steroids, iron tablets, and bismuth-containing indigestion medicines. Mention recent constipation, straining, diarrhoea, or a known stomach ulcer if relevant.
6. **While waiting for help or transport, make it easier to escalate.** Stay near a toilet, sit or lie down if you feel faint, keep your medicines or medicine list ready, and call 999 if you become more unwell, the bleeding increases, or new emergency signs appear.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause right now.
- You do not need to decide whether it is piles, an ulcer, food colouring, iron tablets, or something else.
- You do not need to start a new diet, supplement, laxative, or home treatment.
- You do not need to decide about tests, referrals, or longer-term treatment before you have been assessed.

## Important reassurance
Seeing black, tarry stool or blood in the toilet can feel frightening, but the useful next step is simple: get the right level of medical advice and avoid handling it alone. Prompt assessment helps clinicians decide whether this is a minor cause, a medicine-related problem, or bleeding that needs urgent treatment.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance for the next few hours or day. Tests, treatment choices, medicine changes, and follow-up plans may need a clinician or specialist.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not a diagnosis and is not a substitute for medical or other professional advice.
