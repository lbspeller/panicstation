# What to do if… your work calendar entries are disappearing and you suspect interference (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a workplace IT or security issue first: record what you can see, avoid changing the calendar further, and report it through your employer’s IT or security route so available logs and recoverable items can be checked.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront or accuse a colleague, or send an angry message. It can escalate the situation and make fact-finding harder.
- Do not delete more items, empty Deleted Items, reinstall apps, or reset the calendar before IT has looked at it.
- Do not change sharing, delegate, or access permissions unless you need an urgent account-protection step or IT/security instructs you to.
- Do not forward sensitive calendar content to your personal email or store it in a personal cloud account.
- Do not post about it in team chats or speculate about motives or people.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the basic facts.** Note which entries disappeared, the approximate dates and times, which calendar was affected, and whether you noticed it on desktop, mobile, web, or more than one device.
2. **Capture quick evidence without changing anything.**
   - Take screenshots showing the calendar name or account, the date range, and the missing entries or gaps.
   - Screenshot any changed, cancelled, or updated invitation notices.
   - Write down the exact time you first noticed the problem.
3. **Do only low-risk checks for simple causes.**
   - Check that you are viewing the correct calendar and date range.
   - Check whether a filter, hidden calendar, mobile sync delay, or different Outlook view could explain it.
   - Look in Deleted Items if your system shows calendar items there, but do not empty anything.
4. **If your organisation uses Outlook or Microsoft 365, be cautious with recovery.**
   - Deleted calendar appointments can sometimes be restored from Deleted Items or Recoverable Items, depending on the account, Outlook version, and administrator policy.
   - Use recovery only after screenshots if you need urgent schedule continuity; write down exactly what you restored and when.
   - If you are unsure, leave recovery to IT or the Microsoft 365 administrator.
5. **If you see signs of account compromise, report it as urgent.**
   - Mention unexpected sign-in prompts, unusual MFA requests, password reset emails you did not start, “new device” notices, or other account alerts.
   - Follow your employer’s security instructions for password, MFA, and session checks rather than trying to investigate alone.
6. **Document access without changing it if you can.**
   - If this is a shared calendar, note who appears to have edit rights or delegate access.
   - If you can view calendar permissions, take a screenshot of the current permissions list.
7. **Create an IT or security ticket promptly.**
   - Say that calendar items appear to have been deleted, changed, or cancelled.
   - Ask IT or the administrator to check available audit logs, mailbox audit records, delegate access, calendar permissions, and recoverable items.
   - Ask them to preserve relevant logs and recoverable items if available.
8. **Tell your manager briefly and factually in writing.**
   - Say what appears to be missing, when you noticed it, and the IT or security ticket number.
   - If this affects customers, patients, safety, meetings, rota cover, or key deadlines, state the operational risk without naming a suspected person.
9. **If it repeats, feels targeted, or is not handled, use the workplace people-process.**
   - Keep a simple dated log of what disappeared, the impact, ticket numbers, and any patterns.
   - If needed, raise the concern through your employer’s grievance procedure.
   - For a grievance meeting, UK employees and workers usually have the right to be accompanied by a relevant companion, such as a colleague or trade union representative. Acas says the person should make a reasonable request to their employer.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out who did it right now; focus on preserving the record and getting IT to verify what happened.
- You do not need to decide today whether to raise a formal grievance.
- You do not need to rebuild the whole calendar immediately unless there is an urgent operational reason.
- You do not need to explain motives or prove intent before reporting the technical issue.

## Important reassurance
Calendar entries can disappear because of sync issues, accidental deletion, shared-calendar permissions, delegate access, app views, or automated policies. Recording what you see and routing it through IT/security is a calm first step that helps preserve evidence and reduce guesswork.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation, preserve the record, and trigger the right internal route. Later decisions about a grievance, data protection concern, disciplinary issue, or wider reporting may need specialist workplace, union, legal, or technical help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, employment, cyber security, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Workplace systems and rights can vary by employer policy, contract, facts, and technical setup; if you feel at risk or are being treated unfairly, consider speaking with your union if you have one or getting independent advice.
