# What to do if… you receive a threatening blackmail message demanding immediate action and you fear it could escalate in person (UK)

## Short answer
Do not reply and do not pay. Put real-world safety around you first, preserve the message, and report it through police or official fraud routes.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t reply “to calm them down” or negotiate.
- Don’t pay, send gift cards, or send cryptocurrency.
- Don’t agree to meet them, open your door to a stranger, or go outside to “check”.
- Don’t click links, open attachments, or install any app they send.
- Don’t use any “reporting link” or “helpline” the blackmailer gives you.
- Don’t delete the account or chat thread in a rush if you can preserve it first.
- Don’t post publicly about it in a way that reveals your location, routine, workplace, or school.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.**  
   Move away from the door and windows. If you’re alone, go near other people if you can, such as family, housemates, a trusted neighbour, or staff in a public place. If you believe someone is outside, nearby, violent, or about to harm you, call **999**.

2. **Treat immediate threats as urgent without engaging.**  
   Do not answer the sender. Call **999** if they threaten immediate violence, say they are coming now, appear to be nearby, or the crime feels in progress. If it is threatening but not immediate, report it to police via **101** or your local police force’s online reporting route.

3. **Preserve the message before blocking if you can.**  
   Take screenshots showing the sender details, usernames, phone number or email address, dates, times, and the exact threat or demand. If it is an email, keep the original email. Do not investigate the sender yourself.

4. **Stop contact and reduce what they can see.**  
   After preserving what you can, block the sender on the platform. Tighten privacy settings for who can message you and who can see your profile. Remove or hide public details that show your address, routine, workplace, school, or live location. If the message includes a password you still use, change that password through the real site or app and turn on two-factor authentication if available.

5. **Report through the right UK route.**  
   For threats, blackmail, personal safety concerns, harassment, or someone possibly coming in person, contact police by **999** if immediate or **101** if not immediate. For cyber crime or fraud, use **Report Fraud** if you live in England, Wales or Northern Ireland. If you live in Scotland, report to **Police Scotland**, usually by **101** if it is not an emergency.

6. **Report scam messages through official channels.**  
   Forward suspicious emails to **report@phishing.gov.uk**. Forward suspicious texts to **7726**, then follow any prompt to share the sender’s number. Use only official reporting routes, not links or numbers sent by the blackmailer.

7. **Add a simple in-person safety buffer for now.**  
   Tell one trusted person what is happening. Keep doors and windows locked. If you have building security, reception, a concierge, or a trusted housemate, tell them not to let in anyone asking for you.

8. **If they claim they have intimate images or video, treat it as extortion.**  
   Do not pay, do not send more material, and do not negotiate. Report through the police or Report Fraud routes above, and use the platform’s in-app reporting tools where the contact happened.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out who they are right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to change phone numbers, move, or delete accounts.
- You do not need to send a “final message” explaining yourself.
- You do not need perfect evidence before asking for help or making a report.

## Important reassurance
These messages are designed to create panic, urgency, and shame. Feeling frozen or scared is a normal response to coercion. The safest first move is to slow the situation down, stop engagement, and put real-world safety around you.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the next minutes and hours. If the threat continues, becomes repeated harassment, or starts to look like stalking, later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 999. If you are unsure whether it is serious enough, you can still report it and ask police or official reporting services to help assess next steps.
