# What to do if… you receive an unexpected eSIM activation message or QR code (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible SIM-swap or number-takeover attempt. Do not scan the QR code, do not follow links in the message, and contact your mobile network immediately using its official app, website, bill, or a trusted phone number.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t scan the QR code or follow links in the message to “cancel”, “verify”, or “secure” anything.
- Don’t reply to the message, and don’t share one-time passcodes, security codes, account PINs, or passwords with anyone who contacted you.
- Don’t call back a number provided in the message. Use the number from your bill, your network’s official app, or your network’s official website.
- Don’t assume it is harmless just because your phone still has signal. Act before any service change completes.
- Don’t rush to factory-reset your phone. This usually will not stop a SIM or eSIM transfer and can make recovery harder.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the activation path.**  
   Do not scan the QR code, do not tap any “set up eSIM” prompt, and do not enter codes into any website or app linked from the message.

2. **Contact your mobile network immediately.**  
   Use another phone if you can. Say: “I received an eSIM activation or number-transfer message I did not request. Please block any unauthorised SIM, eSIM, or number-transfer change and secure my account.”  
   Ask them to:
   - Check whether any eSIM has been issued, activated, or queued.
   - Check whether any SIM swap, PAC, STAC, porting, or number-transfer request is pending.
   - Add a new account PIN, password, or other extra verification before any SIM, eSIM, or number-transfer change.
   - Tell you how the request was made, if they can: online, by phone, in store, or another route.
   - Add a note to the account: “suspected unauthorised SIM or eSIM change”.
   - Give you a reference number for the contact.

3. **Check whether your number is already being affected.**  
   Warning signs include sudden loss of signal, “no service”, calls or texts failing, or one-time passcodes no longer arriving. If this has happened, tell the network it may already be an active SIM swap and that you need the number restored and the mobile account locked down.

4. **Secure accounts that rely on SMS codes, starting with your email.**  
   From a device and account you still control:
   - Change the password on your primary email account first.
   - Then change passwords for banking, payment apps, Apple ID, Google account, and any account that can reset other passwords.
   - Where you can do it quickly, add an authenticator app or passkey and remove SMS as the recovery route.

5. **Contact banks and payment providers if anything looks wrong.**  
   If you see unfamiliar login alerts, password resets you did not start, new payees, unusual transactions, or missing one-time passcodes, contact the bank or payment provider through its official app, card number, or website. Ask what extra checks or temporary protections they can place on the account.

6. **Report the message after the urgent account lock-down steps.**  
   If it is a suspicious text, you can usually forward it to 7726 for free so your mobile provider can investigate it. If you believe fraud has happened, report it to Report Fraud if you are in England, Wales or Northern Ireland. In Scotland, report fraud or financial crime to Police Scotland by calling 101.

7. **Keep a simple timeline.**  
   Write down the time the message arrived, what it said, whether you scanned anything, when you contacted the network, any loss of service, and any account alerts. Keep screenshots if it is safe and easy to do so, but do not open links to get them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to change mobile networks, change your phone, or change your number.
- You do not need to do a full device wipe or reinstall every app right now.
- You do not need to confront the sender or reply to the message.
- You do not need to solve every online account at once. Lock the mobile account, secure email, then check banking and payment access.

## Important reassurance
These messages are designed to rush you into scanning, clicking, or “verifying”. If you pause, avoid the QR code and links, and get the provider to lock down the mobile account, you may still be able to stop the takeover or limit the damage.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and reduce immediate risk. If money was taken, accounts were accessed, or your provider will not secure the account, later decisions may need specialist help from your bank, your mobile provider’s fraud team, Report Fraud, Police Scotland, or a consumer advice service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, technical, or other professional advice. Network processes and the names for SIM, eSIM, porting, and transfer protections vary by provider. If you feel overwhelmed, use a trusted person’s phone to contact your network and bank, and take it one call at a time.
