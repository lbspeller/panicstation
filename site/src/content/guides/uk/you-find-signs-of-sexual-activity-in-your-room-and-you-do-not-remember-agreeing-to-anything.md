# What to do if… you find signs of sexual activity in your room and you do not remember agreeing to anything (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer location immediately and contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) or urgent medical care for a confidential assessment. You do not have to report the incident to the police to receive medical help or forensic evidence preservation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront anyone you suspect or invite them to your location to "explain" what happened.
- Do not shower, bathe, brush your teeth, or wash your clothes if you want to keep the option of forensic evidence open.
- Do not clean or tidy the room or dispose of items like bedding, cups, or used protection.
- Do not post details on social media or send group messages while in a state of shock.
- Do not blame yourself for memory gaps or a lack of physical resistance.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause immediately.**
   - If you believe the person is still in the building or you feel at risk, leave the premises.
   - Call **999** if you are in immediate danger.
   - If you are safe but distressed, move to a different room or a trusted neighbour's home.

2. **Contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) for specialist care.**
   - **In England and Wales:** Find your nearest SARC by searching "NHS SARCs" online or visiting nhs.uk/sarcs.
   - **In Scotland:** Call the dedicated SARCS self-referral line on **0800 148 88 88**.
   - **In Northern Ireland:** Contact The Rowan (SARC) on **0800 389 4424**.
   - Ask for a "self-referral" appointment to discuss medical care and forensic options without police involvement.

3. **Seek urgent medical help for physical symptoms or suspected drugging.**
   - Call **111** or go to A&E if you feel physically unwell, have injuries, or suspect you were spiked.
   - Tell the staff you have a **memory gap** and are concerned about a possible assault so they can prioritise appropriate toxicology tests.
   - If you need emergency contraception, it is most effective when taken as soon as possible (usually within 3 to 5 days depending on the type).

4. **Preserve physical evidence if it feels manageable.**
   - If you have changed clothes, place the items you were wearing in a clean, dry paper bag (not plastic).
   - Avoid eating, drinking, or smoking until you have spoken to a medical professional if you suspect oral contact occurred.
   - Forensic evidence is most effectively collected within **72 hours**, though some evidence can be gathered up to 7 days later.

5. **Access confidential emotional support.**
   - **In England and Wales:** Call the Rape Crisis 24/7 Support Line on **0808 500 2222** or use their online Live Chat.
   - **In Scotland:** Call Rape Crisis Scotland on **08088 01 03 02** (daily 5pm–midnight).
   - **In Northern Ireland:** Call the 24-hour Domestic and Sexual Violence Helpline on **0808 802 1414**.

6. **Record observable facts without speculating.**
   - Write down the time you woke up and the specific signs you noticed (e.g., physical soreness, disarray in the room, specific items found).
   - Note the last thing you clearly remember and who you were with, but do not feel pressured to "fill in" the gaps.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to file a formal police report.
- Determining exactly "what happened" or why there is a memory gap.
- Telling family, employers, or housemates.
- Returning to the room or cleaning the space.

## Important reassurance
Memory loss after a traumatic event is a common biological response. Whether it was caused by shock, alcohol, or medication/drugs, a lack of memory does not mean you consented. You are entitled to medical care and specialist support regardless of how much you can recall.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for safety and time-sensitive medical/forensic needs. It does not cover long-term legal processes or psychological recovery.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not replace professional medical or legal advice. Forensic windows are time-limited; contact a SARC as soon as possible to understand your options. If you are in immediate danger or have a medical emergency, call 999.
