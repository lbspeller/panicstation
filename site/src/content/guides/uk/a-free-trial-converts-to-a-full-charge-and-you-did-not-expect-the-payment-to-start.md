# What to do if… a free trial converts to a full charge and you did not expect the payment to start (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the payment source to stop future charges immediately, then request a refund through the merchant's official portal or your bank's dispute mechanism. Use the Direct Debit Guarantee or card chargeback rules if the merchant refuses to cooperate.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume that simply deleting an app or cancelling your physical card will stop recurring payments; a Continuous Payment Authority (CPA) often persists across new card details.
- Don’t delay your request, as many automated refund systems (like Apple or Google) have strict time windows (usually 14 days or less).
- Don’t provide bank login details or one-time passcodes to anyone claiming to be "refund support" from the merchant.
- Don’t initiate a bank chargeback until you have first attempted to contact the merchant, as banks usually require evidence of a failed resolution attempt.
- Don’t believe that an auto-renewal is always irreversible; UK consumer law provides protections against lack of notice or transparency.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact payment method used by checking your bank statement or app.** **You must determine if the money was taken via Direct Debit, a recurring card payment (CPA), PayPal, or an app store (Apple/Google).** 
   - Note the merchant name, the exact date, and the transaction ID.
   - Take a screenshot of the charge for evidence.

2. **Stop the next billing cycle at the source to prevent further loss.** **Log in to the specific platform that manages the payment to withdraw your consent for future charges.**
   - **Apple App Store:** Use `reportaproblem.apple.com` to view active subscriptions and cancel immediately.
   - **Google Play:** Open the Play Store app, tap your profile, and select "Payments & subscriptions" to cancel.
   - **Direct Debit:** Log in to your banking app and cancel the Direct Debit instruction; this prevents the merchant from pulling more funds.
   - **Recurring Card Payment (CPA):** Contact your bank and tell them you are "withdrawing consent" for this merchant; under FCA rules, the bank must stop these payments when asked.

3. **Request a refund through the merchant’s official automated system if applicable.** **Use the dedicated refund portals for digital stores as these are often faster than manual emails.**
   - For Apple, use the "Request a refund" option at `reportaproblem.apple.com`.
   - For Google, use the "Request a refund" tool on the Google Play website within 48 hours of the charge.
   - For other merchants, find their "Help" or "Support" page and submit a written request citing that the trial converted without sufficient notice.

4. **Invoke the Direct Debit Guarantee if the payment was taken via a bank account and lacked notice.** **Contact your bank and inform them that the payment was "taken in error" because you did not receive the mandatory 10-day advance notice of the amount or date.**
   - The bank is required to provide a full and immediate refund under the Guarantee while they investigate.
   - Note that if the merchant proves the payment was valid, the bank may later reclaim the refund.

5. **Initiate a card chargeback if the merchant is unresponsive or refuses a legitimate refund.** **Contact your debit or credit card provider and ask to start a "chargeback" dispute for "service not as described" or "unauthorised transaction" if you cancelled before the deadline.**
   - Provide the bank with a screenshot showing you attempted to contact the merchant first.
   - Be aware that chargeback is a card scheme rule (Visa/Mastercard) rather than a legal right, but it is highly effective for subscription disputes.

6. **Check for Section 75 eligibility only if the total transaction value exceeded £100.** **If the subscription was a high-value annual plan paid on a UK credit card, you can hold the card provider "jointly and severally liable" for a breach of contract or misrepresentation.**
   - This rarely applies to monthly trials but is a powerful tool for expensive memberships or professional software suites.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to keep the service; focus on securing the refund for the unwanted charge first.
- Investigating your email history for the original sign-up terms; get the cancellation done now and look for the "fine print" later if the merchant disputes your claim.
- Changing your bank account or card numbers.
- Leaving public reviews or reporting the business to Trading Standards.

## Important reassurance
Unintended trial conversions are one of the most common financial issues in the UK. Banks and major platforms have established, high-speed protocols to handle these "subscription traps." Hesitation or a sense of "it's my fault for forgetting" is a common reaction, but it does not void your right to stop future payments or dispute a charge taken without clear notice.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and the initiation of refund/dispute processes in the UK. It does not cover long-term legal claims or ombudsman escalations which may be required if the initial refund request is denied.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute financial or legal advice. Rights under the Direct Debit Guarantee and card chargeback schemes are subject to specific criteria and time limits set by the Bacs scheme and card providers.
