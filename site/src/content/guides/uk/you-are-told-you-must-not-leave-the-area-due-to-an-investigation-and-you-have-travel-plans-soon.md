# What to do if… you are told you must not leave the area due to an investigation and you have travel plans soon (UK)

## Short answer
Do not travel until you have verified your formal legal status in writing, as departing while under specific bail conditions can result in immediate arrest. Check your release paperwork to see if you are on "Pre-charge Bail" with conditions or "Released Under Investigation" (RUI).

## Do not do these things
- Don't assume that a verbal instruction from an officer is a legally binding bail condition without checking your formal paperwork.
- Don't attempt to travel "just for the day" if your bail conditions include a requirement to reside at a specific address or a curfew.
- Don't ignore the situation or hope the police won't notice your departure; border systems may flag travel restrictions.
- Don't cancel non-refundable bookings until you have confirmed whether a variation of conditions is possible.
- Don't rely on advice from family or social media; only official paperwork or a qualified solicitor can confirm your legal obligations.

## What to do now
1. **Identify your exact legal status immediately.** **Determine if you were released on Pre-charge Bail (with or without conditions), Released Under Investigation (RUI), or following a Voluntary Attendance (Caution Plus 3) interview.** 
   - Check your paperwork for a "Bail Notice" or "Notice of Release Under Investigation."
   - If you have no paperwork, contact the custody suite where you were held or the investigating officer to request a written copy of your status.
2. **Review your bail conditions for specific travel-related wording.** **Look for clauses that explicitly restrict your movement or require you to be at a certain location at specific times.** 
   - Check for terms like "Must reside at [Address]," "Curfew between [Hours]," "Do not travel outside of England and Wales," or "Surrender passport."
   - Identify if there is a "Reporting Condition" that requires you to attend a police station on a date that overlaps with your travel.
3. **Establish the physical boundaries of any "local area" restriction.** **Request the investigating officer provide the exact geographical limits if you have been told to "remain local" or "stay in the area."** 
   - Ask if the restriction applies to the police force area, the county, or the country (UK).
   - Document the officer's name and the date/time of this clarification.
4. **Instruct a criminal defence solicitor to review your itinerary and paperwork.** **Provide your solicitor with your flight details, accommodation booking, and the reason for your travel.** 
   - Ask the solicitor to assess if the proposed travel would constitute a breach of your current conditions.
   - Request that the solicitor contact the police to seek a formal variation for the duration of the trip.
5. **Apply for a formal variation of conditions if the police refuse.** **If the police deny your request to travel, your solicitor can apply to the Magistrates’ Court for a reconsideration of your bail conditions.** 
   - Be prepared to provide evidence of the trip, such as proof it was booked before the investigation began.
   - Note that this process usually takes several business days to reach a hearing.
6. **Obtain any agreed changes or permissions in writing.** **Do not travel based on a verbal "okay" from an officer over the phone.** 
   - Ensure you have a revised bail notice or a letter from the police/court confirming the temporary variation.
   - Carry this documentation with you when you travel.
7. **Contact your travel providers to discuss flexible options.** **Inquire about moving your travel dates or obtaining a credit note while you wait for legal clarity.** 
   - Use neutral phrasing such as "legal restrictions beyond my control" rather than disclosing details of the investigation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to cancel the trip permanently until the variation process has been exhausted.
- You do not need to provide the police with a detailed "reason" for your holiday unless your solicitor advises it is necessary to secure a variation.
- You do not need to worry about the long-term outcome of the investigation while focusing on these immediate travel restrictions.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel restricted or anxious when an investigation overlaps with personal plans. The legal system provides specific routes to challenge or change conditions that are disproportionate. Taking the time to get clarity in writing prevents a misunderstanding from turning into a serious legal issue.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for travel plan conflicts during a UK police investigation. It does not cover legal strategy for the investigation itself or international extradition law.

## Important note
Breaching police bail is not a standalone criminal offence in England and Wales (except for specific terrorism-related travel restrictions), but it gives the police a power of arrest and may lead to you being remanded in custody or facing stricter conditions. Always consult a solicitor before making travel decisions while under investigation.
