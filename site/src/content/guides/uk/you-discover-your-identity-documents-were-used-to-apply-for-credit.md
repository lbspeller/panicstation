# What to do if… you discover your identity documents were used to apply for credit (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the lender’s fraud department immediately to freeze the application, then report the incident to the national Report Fraud service (England / Wales / NI) or Police Scotland (101) to obtain a crime reference number. Check your statutory credit reports across all major agencies to identify and dispute any fraudulent entries.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay any part of the debt or agree to a "settlement" plan, as this can be interpreted as an admission of liability for the account.
- Don’t use contact details provided in suspicious emails or texts; always find the lender's official fraud number via their verified website.
- Don’t ignore "soft search" notifications or letters about applications you didn't make; these are often the first sign of escalating fraud.
- Don’t apply for new, genuine credit products until you have secured your file, as the fraudulent activity may cause immediate rejections.
- Don’t dispose of any physical evidence, such as envelopes with postmarks or letters, as these may be needed for the police report.

## What to do now
1. **Log the incident details immediately.**  
   **Record the name of the lender, the date you discovered the issue, and any specific account or application reference numbers mentioned in correspondence.**
2. **Contact the lender’s fraud department to stop the application.**  
   **Call the lender using a verified number from their official website and state clearly: "I am a victim of identity fraud and did not authorize this application."**
   - Request an immediate freeze or closure of the account.
   - Ask for a written confirmation that you have reported the fraud.
   - Obtain their internal fraud case reference number.
   - Ask which credit reference agencies they have sent data to.
3. **Report the crime to the national authorities.**  
   **In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use the "Report Fraud" service (formerly Action Fraud) online at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040; in Scotland, call 101.**
   - Ensure you receive a police/crime reference number.
   - Provide this number to the lender’s fraud team to support your dispute.
4. **Access your statutory credit reports from all four agencies.**  
   **Request your free statutory report from Experian, Equifax, TransUnion, and Crediva to check for any other unauthorized activity.**
   - Look for "hard searches" you do not recognize.
   - Check for "linked addresses" or "financial associates" that are not yours.
   - Use the "Dispute" function on each agency's website for any fraudulent entries found.
5. **Add a protective layer to your credit file.**  
   **Apply for Cifas Protective Registration (£30 for 2 years) via the cifas.org.uk website to ensure lenders perform extra verification for future applications.**
   - Note that this may slightly delay your own genuine future applications while they verify your identity.
6. **Verify your post and address security.**  
   **Contact Royal Mail if you suspect your mail is being redirected or intercepted, and check that your address is listed correctly on the Electoral Register.**
7. **Replace physical documents only if they are missing.**  
   **If your passport or driving licence was physically lost or stolen, report it to the Passport Office or DVLA immediately; if you still have the documents, focus on the digital fraud markers instead.**

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to pay for premium credit monitoring "plus" services right now; the statutory (free) reports provide the necessary data.
- You do **not** need to hire a legal representative to clear your credit file; the dispute process is designed for individuals.
- You do **not** need to contact the person you suspect of the fraud yourself; let the police and lender investigations handle identification.

## Important reassurance
Identity fraud is a common occurrence, and UK lenders have established protocols for resolving these cases. While the process of cleaning your credit file requires persistence, you are generally not held liable for debts incurred through proven identity theft. It is normal to feel vulnerable after your personal details are misused; taking these systematic steps shifts the burden back to the institutional fraud teams.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and reporting. Fully removing markers from a credit file can take several weeks or months depending on the lender's investigation speed.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Statutory rights and reporting procedures (such as the transition from Action Fraud to Report Fraud) are subject to change. If you are contacted by debt collectors regarding a fraudulent account, refer them to your police report number and the lender's fraud department.
