# What to do if… you suddenly lose your balance and cannot walk steadily (UK)

## Short answer
If your balance changed suddenly, or you cannot stand or walk safely, call 999 now and say you have sudden loss of balance or trouble walking and are worried about a possible stroke. Sit or lie down while you get help.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive yourself anywhere.
- Do not try to walk it off, climb stairs, shower alone, or move around without support while you are unsteady.
- Do not drink alcohol or take sedating drugs to try to steady yourself.
- Do not take someone else’s medicine, or extra doses of your own medicine, to try to stop the symptoms.
- Do not sleep it off or wait to see if it passes if this is sudden, new, severe, worsening, linked to a fall or head injury, or comes with any possible stroke signs.
- Do not dismiss it because you are young, embarrassed, or worried about making a fuss.

## What to do now
1. Get to a safer pause immediately. Sit or lie down away from stairs, sharp edges, traffic, water, or anything hot. If you might vomit, lie on your side and keep your phone within reach.
2. If this came on suddenly, you are falling, you cannot stand safely, or your walking has suddenly changed, call 999 and say: “Sudden loss of balance or trouble walking — possible stroke.”
3. Check for stroke signs without repeatedly standing up: face drooping, arm weakness or numbness, speech problems, sudden confusion, sudden vision change, sudden severe headache, feeling or being sick, dizziness, falling over, or weakness on one side. If any are present, call 999 now.
4. If you fell, collapsed, hit your head, or cannot remember exactly what happened, treat it as higher risk. Call 999 if you have trouble staying awake, a seizure, weakness or numbness, problems with vision, hearing, understanding, speaking, writing, walking or balance, bleeding from the ears, clear fluid from the ears or nose, or you cannot get to A&E safely.
5. If you hit your head and have vomited, have a headache that does not go away, memory problems, an open wound, a large bruise or swelling, a blood clotting disorder, or take blood thinners such as warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban or dabigatran, get urgent medical advice. Use 999 if symptoms are serious, sudden, worsening, or you are unsafe to move; otherwise use NHS 111 where available or the local urgent-care route.
6. If symptoms are not sudden, are mild, and you can sit safely without worsening, use NHS 111 where available, NHS 111 online where available, or your local urgent-care route. If symptoms become sudden, severe, or unsafe, switch to 999.
7. Make it easy for help to reach you if you can do so without risking a fall: unlock the door, turn on a light, secure pets, and have your address ready.
8. Note the exact time you were last normal and the first thing you noticed. Say this clearly to 999, 111, or the clinician who assesses you.
9. Gather only essential information without walking around: your medicines, allergies, major conditions, and whether you take blood thinners. Keep them with you if an ambulance arrives or you are told to go to A&E.

## What can wait
- Working out whether this is vertigo, migraine, an inner ear problem, anxiety, a medication effect, or something else.
- Calling your GP practice, arranging work cover, or explaining everything to multiple people.
- Trying online vertigo manoeuvres, balance exercises, or home remedies.
- Deciding how the rest of the day will work beyond following urgent medical advice.
- Cleaning up, packing a full bag, or proving to yourself that you can walk normally.

## Important reassurance
Sudden loss of balance can feel frightening, confusing, and easy to minimise. Getting urgent help is a sensible safety step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, for the first unsafe period. Later decisions, diagnosis, treatment, travel, work, and follow-up may need help from clinicians or other specialist services.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, or a substitute for urgent assessment. If symptoms are sudden, severe, worsening, linked to a fall or head injury, or could be stroke-related, seek urgent medical help.
