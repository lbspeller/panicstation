# What to do if… you spot a recurring charge to a company you do not recognise (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank or card issuer immediately to withdraw your consent for the payment and request a full refund for any unauthorised transactions.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore small amounts; unauthorised "test" charges are often followed by larger recurring withdrawals.
- Don't assume that simply cancelling or replacing your physical card will stop a Continuous Payment Authority (CPA) / recurring payment.
- Don't click any links in emails or text messages that claim to offer a refund or "subscription management" for the unknown charge.
- Don't contact the merchant first if you suspect fraud; go directly to your bank to secure the account.
- Don't wait for the next payment to "see if it happens again" before taking action.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific type of payment on your statement.**
   - **Check if it is a Direct Debit, a standing order, or a recurring card payment (Continuous Payment Authority).**
   - **Note the exact merchant name (descriptor), the amount, and the frequency of the payments.**

2. **Instruct your bank or card issuer to stop all future payments.**
   - **Tell your card issuer that you are "withdrawing consent" for any further payments to that specific merchant.**
   - **Confirm that they have blocked the merchant from taking future funds, as card issuers are legally required to do this under Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) rules.**
   - **If the payment is a Direct Debit, cancel it immediately through your banking app or via telephone banking.**

3. **Request a full and immediate refund for unauthorised amounts.**
   - **Ask for an "Indemnity Claim" if it is a Direct Debit, which entitles you to a full and immediate refund from your bank under the Direct Debit Guarantee.**
   - **Request a "Chargeback" if it is a recurring card payment that you did not authorise or that continued after you withdrew consent.**
   - **Avoid accepting a "partial refund" if the entire transaction was not permitted by you.**

4. **Secure your account and card details.**
   - **Report the card as compromised if you believe your details were stolen, and request a new card with a new 16-digit number.**
   - **Change your online banking password and enable two-factor authentication (2FA) if you suspect your digital login has been accessed.**

5. **Report the incident to the appropriate national authority.**
   - **If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report the incident to Report Fraud (the national reporting service) at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.**
   - **If you are in Scotland, report the matter directly to Police Scotland by calling 101.**
   - **Keep any reference numbers provided by the police as your bank may require them for their investigation.**

6. **Check your credit report for other signs of identity theft.**
   - **Review your reports from Experian, Equifax, or TransUnion to ensure no new accounts or loans have been opened in your name.**
   - **Consider applying for CIFAS Protective Registration (£30 for 2 years) which places a flag on your file requiring lenders to perform extra identity checks.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to wait for a police investigation to be completed before your bank issues a Direct Debit refund.
- You do not need to determine the physical location or the "real name" of the company behind a confusing statement descriptor.
- You do not need to decide whether to close your entire bank account right now; isolating the specific payment is the priority.

## Important reassurance
It is common for companies to use "merchant descriptors" on bank statements that do not match their brand name. However, banks are well-equipped to handle these disputes, and UK consumer law provides strong protections for unauthorised recurring payments. Taking action now prevents further losses and does not mean you have failed to manage your finances correctly.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step guidance only for UK bank and card customers. Later decisions regarding long-term fraud recovery or credit score repair may require specialist help from your bank's fraud department or a credit reference agency.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance regarding UK financial processes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Banking protocols and your specific rights may vary depending on the terms of your account and the nature of the transaction. Always verify instructions with your bank's official fraud team.
