# What to do if… you notice a sudden swarm of flying insects indoors near a window or light (UK)

## Short answer
Move people and pets away, close the door to contain the insects, and turn off the indoor light they are gathering around. If you can do it without leaning into the insects, give them a clear outside exit and then leave the room closed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not spray aerosols, foggers, or strong chemicals while you are unsure what the insects are.
- Do not swat at a dense cluster, especially if they might be bees, wasps, or hornets.
- Do not chase the insects through the house or leave internal doors open.
- Do not seal the suspected crack, vent, or hole while insects are actively coming through.
- Do not put your face or hands close to the cluster to identify them.
- Do not try to treat a suspected wasp nest in a wall, loft, roof space, or chimney yourself.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.**  
   Move children, pets, and anyone with a known sting allergy away from the room. Close the room door. Put on shoes and long sleeves if you need to go back in.

2. **Reduce the attraction.**  
   Turn off the light the insects are gathering around. If it is dark outside, close curtains or blinds where you can do that safely. Use a torch or phone light from outside the room rather than lighting the swarm room.

3. **Let them leave only if you can do it safely.**  
   If most insects are at a window and you can reach the handle without leaning into the cluster, open the window enough to create an outside exit. Then leave the room and close the door again. Wait a short while before checking from the doorway.

4. **If they look like honey bees, contain and ask for bee-specific help.**  
   If they are clustered in a ball or mass and may be honey bees, do not spray them. Keep people and pets away, take a photo from a safe distance if possible, and contact a local honey bee swarm collector through the British Beekeepers Association.

5. **If wasps, hornets, or repeated entry from one point are likely, use pest control.**  
   Keep the room contained and avoid the entry point. For England and Wales, use GOV.UK to check whether your council provides pest control services. For Scotland and Northern Ireland, check your local council website, or use a qualified pest controller.

6. **Check for urgent sting symptoms.**  
   Call 999 if someone has sudden swelling of the lips, mouth, throat, tongue, or face, is struggling to breathe, feels faint, collapses, or seems severely unwell after a sting. If the person has an adrenaline auto-injector, follow the instructions on it.

7. **Once activity has reduced, look for the entry point without dismantling anything.**  
   From the doorway or outside the room, check around the window frame, trickle vents, extractor fan grille, fireplace, loft hatch, pipe gaps, cable holes, ceiling edges, and wall cracks. Take a photo or short video from a safe distance for a swarm collector, council service, or pest controller.

8. **Clean up only when you are confident they are not stinging insects.**  
   If the main surge has stopped and they are clearly not bees, wasps, or hornets, vacuuming dead or settled insects may help. Empty the vacuum outside straight away.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact species immediately.
- You do not need to seal gaps, pull off trim, open wall panels, or inspect the loft tonight.
- You do not need to choose sprays, traps, treatments, or long-term repairs while insects are still active.
- You do not need to decide whether this is a one-off event until you have seen whether insects keep returning from the same point.

## Important reassurance
A sudden indoor swarm can feel alarming, but the immediate job is simple: keep people away, contain the room, reduce light attraction, and avoid provoking or trapping insects. Many incidents can be stabilised before any bigger decision is needed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to make the situation safer and reduce panic. If insects keep appearing, if you suspect bees in a building structure, or if wasps or hornets are repeatedly entering from one place, later decisions may need a swarm collector, council pest service, or qualified pest controller.

## Important note
This is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, pest-control, or other professional advice. For suspected anaphylaxis or severe illness after a sting, call 999.
