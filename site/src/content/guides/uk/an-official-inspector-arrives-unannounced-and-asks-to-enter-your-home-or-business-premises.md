# What to do if… an official inspector arrives unannounced and asks to enter your home or business premises (UK)

## Short answer
Stay at the threshold or reception, verify who they are, and ask whether they are relying on a warrant, a statutory power of entry, or your consent before anyone comes inside.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t let someone in just because they look official, sound urgent, or say it will be quick.
- Don’t physically block them, grab paperwork, argue at close range, or do anything that could become a safety incident.
- Don’t sign consent, entry, search, interview, or note paperwork unless you understand what it allows.
- Don’t volunteer extra access to private rooms, unrelated files, personal devices, or passwords without asking for the legal basis.
- Don’t destroy, hide, delete, alter, or tidy away records once they have arrived.
- Don’t use a phone number they give you as the only check on who they are.

## What to do now
1. Create a calm pause point: keep the conversation at the doorway, reception desk, intercom, or another safe boundary while you think.
2. Ask to see identification before any entry:
   - photo ID;
   - name, role, organisation, and ID number;
   - any warrant card, instrument of appointment, written authorisation, or warrant they say applies;
   - the time they arrived and the reason they give for the visit.
3. Ask the key question: “Are you asking for my consent, or do you say you have a legal power to enter now?”
   - If they say they have a warrant, ask to see it and check that it names or describes the correct premises and the general thing it authorises.
   - If they say they have a statutory power of entry, ask which law or power they are relying on and ask to see the appointment, authorisation, warrant, or written explanation they say gives them that power.
   - If they say it is by consent, you can say: “I am not consenting right now. Please explain the legal basis and what happens next.”
4. Confirm legitimacy using a safe call-back. Use the organisation’s official public website or switchboard number, not only a number the visitor gives you. While you check, do not consent to entry just because you feel pressured; ask them to explain what power they say allows entry without consent.
5. If it is a business premises, bring another responsible person in:
   - ask the owner, manager, duty lead, or compliance contact to attend;
   - have one colleague act as a witness and note-taker;
   - tell the visitor you will accompany them where safe and will keep a record of what is inspected, copied, photographed, taken, or requested.
6. If it is your home, be especially careful about consent. You can say: “I am not refusing to cooperate. I am asking you to show me the warrant or the specific power that applies to my home. Otherwise, I am not consenting to entry.”
7. If entry goes ahead because they show a power, a warrant, or you choose to allow it, keep the visit focused:
   - ask them to state which areas they need to enter and why;
   - avoid offering access to unrelated rooms, private spaces, devices, or records;
   - if they ask for documents, ask for the exact documents requested and whether copies will do;
   - ask for a receipt, list, or written note for anything copied, removed, photographed, or seized.
8. Get immediate support while the visit is happening. Call a solicitor, compliance adviser, landlord or premises owner, senior manager, or trusted business lead. Keep the call calm and factual.
9. If you feel unsafe or suspect impersonation, step back, move to a safer place, and call 999 if there is an immediate threat. If police are executing a warrant, do not interfere physically; stay calm, say if you do not consent when asked, and document what happens.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the whole legal position at the door.
- You usually do not need to rush into a detailed interview without first asking whether it is voluntary and getting advice.
- Do not rush to hand over passwords, personal devices, or unrelated records; ask for the legal basis, the exact request, and time to get advice.
- Follow-up documents, explanations, complaints, or challenges can usually be organised after the immediate visit is stable.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to take a few minutes to verify identity and authority. A calm pause helps protect you, the premises, and any later decision-making.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Powers of entry differ between police, local authorities, HSE, HMRC, immigration officers, and other regulators, and the position can be different for homes and business premises. Later decisions may need specialist legal or compliance help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, medical, or professional advice. If you are unsure about an official’s authority, ask for the legal basis in writing where possible and get legal advice as soon as you can.
