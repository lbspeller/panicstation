# What to do if… you need to file a baggage claim but your bag tag receipt is missing (UK)

## Short answer
Report the missing baggage at the airline desk immediately to obtain a Property Irregularity Report (PIR) and ask staff to retrieve your tag numbers from the flight manifest using your passport or booking reference.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave the airport baggage reclaim hall without attempting to speak to a ground handler or airline representative.
- Do not assume a claim is impossible without the physical receipt; the airline holds digital records of all checked items.
- Do not guess or invent a tag number on formal documents; leave the field blank or write "unknown" if the number cannot be retrieved.
- Do not delay reporting because you want to "check at home first" for the receipt.
- Do not dispose of your boarding pass, as it often contains the secondary portion of the tag sticker or sticky residue where it was attached.

## What to do now
1. **Go immediately to the airline or ground handling baggage desk in the arrivals hall.**
   - If the desk is unstaffed, look for a "self-service" kiosk or a posted phone number specifically for baggage services at that airport.
2. **Provide your booking reference (PNR) and passport to the representative.**
   - **Request that the agent look up your "bag tag numbers" in the Departure Control System (DCS).** 
   - They can search your check-in history to find the unique barcode numbers associated with your name.
3. **Complete the Property Irregularity Report (PIR) form.**
   - **Ensure you receive a printed copy of the PIR with a 10-character reference code (e.g., LHRBA12345).**
   - If you cannot get a PIR at the airport, report the loss online through the airline’s official portal within the first 24 hours.
4. **Identify the bag using physical characteristics rather than the tag.**
   - **Provide specific details including the brand, shell material (hard or soft), number of wheels, and any external identifiers.**
   - Mention unique features:
     - Colour and size
     - Distinctive ribbons or straps
     - Stickers or luggage tags from previous trips
     - Visible scuffs or damage
5. **Check your digital check-in records for a virtual receipt.**
   - **Open the airline app or your email check-in confirmation to see if the bag tag numbers were sent digitally.**
   - Some airlines automatically include baggage counts and tag numbers in "Your baggage is loaded" app notifications.
6. **Retain all receipts for essential emergency purchases.**
   - **If you are away from home, keep itemised, dated receipts for basic toiletries and essential clothing.**
   - Do not claim for "new for old" replacements; airlines typically pay for the depreciated value or specific essential costs.
7. **Submit a formal written claim for expenses within 21 days.**
   - **Write to the airline’s customer service department once the bag is delivered or declared lost.**
   - Mention the Montreal Convention in your correspondence to indicate you are claiming under statutory rights.

## What can wait
- You do not need the physical tag receipt to start the tracing process if the airline can find your check-in record.
- You do not need to provide a full inventory of the bag's contents in the first 24 hours unless specifically requested.
- You do not need to decide on a final compensation amount until the bag is either returned or officially declared "lost" (after 21 days).

## Important reassurance
Baggage desks frequently handle reports from passengers who have lost their small thermal receipts. The airline's computer system records exactly how many bags were checked under your name and their specific barcode numbers at the time of check-in. The PIR reference number you receive at the airport is more important for tracking than the original tag stub.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for reporting missing baggage when paperwork is lost. It does not cover long-term legal litigation or complex insurance subrogation.

## Important note
This information is based on UK Civil Aviation Authority (CAA) guidelines and the Montreal Convention as of 2026. Specific airline policies regarding "proof of check-in" may vary. Always keep copies of your PIR and all communication with the airline.
