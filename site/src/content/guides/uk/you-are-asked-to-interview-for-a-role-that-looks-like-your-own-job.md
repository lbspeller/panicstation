# What to do if… you are asked to interview for a role that looks like your own job (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and get clarity in writing about what the interview is for before you treat it like a normal recruitment process. Ask whether it is a new vacancy, part of a restructuring or selection process, or connected to redundancy consultation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign “to get ahead of it” or send an angry message you cannot take back.
- Do not assume it is definitely redundancy, or definitely harmless, without asking what process you are in.
- Do not sign anything under time pressure, including a new contract, settlement terms, voluntary redundancy, or a resignation letter.
- Do not share speculation widely at work or on social media while facts are unclear.
- Do not ignore the invite and hope it goes away; silence can be misread as disengagement.

## What to do now
1. **Reply once, calmly, and ask what process this is.** You can write: “To prepare properly, can you confirm whether this interview is for a new vacancy, part of a restructuring or selection process, or connected to redundancy consultation?”
2. **Ask for the documents that let you compare the roles.** Request the job description, reporting line, location or hybrid expectation, pay band or confirmation that pay is unchanged, and the assessment criteria.
3. **Ask how decisions will be made.** Ask who is being considered, whether there is a selection pool, what scoring method will be used, and whether the criteria will be applied consistently to everyone in the pool.
4. **Ask directly whether your current role is at risk.** If redundancy is being proposed, ask when consultation starts, who your HR contact is, and whether any collective consultation rules are being considered. If 20 or more employees may be made redundant within 90 days at one establishment, collective consultation rules can apply.
5. **Start a simple private record.** Keep a timeline with dates, names, meeting titles, and what was said or sent. Save the invite, job description, org charts, criteria, and your replies. If you keep copies outside work systems, do it in a way that follows confidentiality and data policies, such as saving your own correspondence rather than exporting sensitive company data.
6. **Prepare for the interview without conceding that the process is fair or clear.** Bring a short written summary of what you do now: your responsibilities, outcomes, systems you own, specialist knowledge, and work that matches the advertised role.
7. **If the role looks like suitable alternative employment, ask that question plainly.** Write: “Is this role being treated as suitable alternative employment or redeployment? If so, please confirm what has changed from my current role, including duties, seniority, pay, hours, location, and contract terms.”
8. **Use internal support routes early, but keep the ask narrow.** If the process is unclear, ask HR or your manager for a short clarification meeting about the process, documents, and timetable. If you are in a union, consider contacting your representative before the interview or meeting.
9. **If key records are missing later, consider a subject access request.** A UK GDPR subject access request can let you ask for your personal data, such as assessment notes about you or scoring linked to you. Responses can be redacted and may not include everything you expect, especially where other people’s data or exemptions apply.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you would accept a new role, take redundancy, raise a grievance, or challenge the process.
- You do not need to threaten legal action to ask for clarity and documents.
- You do not need to perfect your CV or start job-hunting in the next hour.
- You do not need to confront colleagues or collect allies before you have the basic facts in writing.
- You do not need to make a subject access request today unless there is a specific reason to do it now.

## Important reassurance
It is common for restructures to be communicated clumsily, and it is understandable to feel blindsided when an “interview” looks like you are being asked to compete for your own work. Getting the process clarified in writing is a steady, protective first move.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation, reduce panic decisions, and preserve your options. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if redundancy, redeployment, grievance, settlement terms, employment status, or tribunal deadlines become relevant.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Employment situations depend on the facts, including your contract, workplace policies, numbers affected, consultation steps, and what your employer is actually proposing.
