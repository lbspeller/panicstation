# What to do if… you are asked to interview for a role that looks like your own job (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign any document that requires you to certify you personally witnessed or performed an action that you did not. Ask for the request in writing and offer a truthful alternative, such as signing only for a records review or requesting a re-inspection.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any field labeled "witnessed by," "inspected by," or "completed by" if you were not present.
- Do not backdate, alter, or "tidy up" existing records to imply historical compliance.
- Do not delete or hide electronic communications regarding the request to sign off.
- Do not share your digital credentials, stamps, or physical signature for others to apply on your behalf.
- Do not make public accusations or engage in verbal arguments in communal work areas.
- Do not assume that "standard practice" or verbal assurances from management provide legal protection for a false signature.

## What to do now
1. **State your position clearly and neutrally.** Say: "I cannot sign to state I witnessed this specific check as I was not present; I need to verify what statement I am physically able to certify."
2. **Identify the exact wording of the certification.** Review the specific form or digital field to see if it says "witnessed," "verified," "approved," or "inspected" to understand the legal weight of the signature.
3. **Request the instruction in writing or create a contemporaneous record.** If the request is verbal, send a follow-up email: "Confirming our conversation at [time]; you have requested I sign off the [inspection name] dated [date] which I did not personally witness."
4. **Offer a truthful alternative that reflects the actual work performed.** Only if your internal process and competence allow, propose one of the following:
   - Signing to confirm you have "reviewed the submitted paperwork" rather than "witnessed the task."
   - Signing as a "managerial acknowledgment of receipt" of the data.
   - Performing a new, personal inspection to allow for a truthful signature.
5. **Verify the underlying evidence before considering any administrative sign-off.** Ask to see raw data, time-stamped photos, instrument calibration logs, or the original job cards from the person who did perform the work.
6. **Escalate the issue through internal compliance or safety channels.** Contact your Health and Safety (H&S) lead, Quality Assurance manager, or use the formal whistleblowing/disclosure line as updated under the 2026 regulatory framework.
7. **Halt the activity if the lack of inspection poses an immediate physical risk.** If the check involves fire safety, high-voltage equipment, or hazardous chemicals, insist that the process stops until a competent person performs a verifiable inspection.
8. **Keep a private, dated log of the encounter outside of company systems.** Record the date, time, names of those involved, the specific documents mentioned, and your exact response.
9. **Contact Protect or your Trade Union representative for confidential advice.** If you are threatened with disciplinary action for refusing to sign, seek external support immediately to understand protections under the Employment Rights Act 1996 (as amended 2025/2026).

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to resign or "go legal" in the first few hours of the request.
- You do not need to prove that the person asking you to sign has "bad intentions" or is acting maliciously.
- You can wait to file a formal grievance until you have secured a copy of the disputed paperwork and your written refusal.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant pressure when a manager treats sign-offs as "just paperwork," but a signature is a legal statement of fact. Under UK health and safety law, individuals can be held personally liable for falsifying safety records. Maintaining a truthful record is the standard professional requirement and protects you from future liability if an incident occurs.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for employees facing pressure to falsify records. It does not cover specific industry regulations for highly specialized fields like aviation, nuclear, or medicine, which may have additional mandatory reporting requirements.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Falsifying records may constitute a criminal offense under Section 33 of the Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974. If you believe there is a risk to public safety or a breach of law, consult the official "List of Prescribed People and Bodies" for external reporting options in the UK.
