# What to do if… you suspect someone is impersonating law enforcement or an official authority to intimidate you (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all interaction, retreat to a secure location, and verify the person's identity using a phone line you know is clear by waiting five minutes or using a different device. If you are in immediate danger or being pressured to move/pay right now, call **999**.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t open your door, get into a vehicle, or accompany the person to a secondary location until identity is 100% verified.
- Don’t use a phone number or "verification extension" provided by the person themselves.
- Don’t hand over cash, bank cards, electronic devices, or buy vouchers / gift cards / cryptocurrency for an "official fine."
- Don’t share identity documents through an open door or provide passwords/PINs over the phone.
- Don’t assume a caller ID or a uniform makes the encounter legitimate; these are easily falsified.

## What to do now
1. **Create physical distance and secure your environment.** If the person is at your door, keep it locked and speak through a window or letterbox; if you are in public, move to a well-lit area with other people.
2. **If you feel threatened or a crime is in progress, call 999 immediately.** Inform the operator that you are being intimidated by someone claiming to be an official and that you suspect they are an impersonator.
3. **Wait five minutes or use a different phone before calling 101 to verify a police officer.** Scammers can keep a telephone line open even after you hang up, making you think you have reached the police when you are still talking to them.
   - If using a landline, wait at least five minutes for the line to clear.
   - Ideally, use a mobile phone to call 101 while the person is still present (if safe).
4. **Ask for a warrant card and note specific identification details.** Legitimate officers carry a warrant card with a photo, name, and shoulder/warrant number.
   - For plain-clothes officers: they must show this card when exercising any police power.
   - For uniformed officers: they must show it upon request unless the situation makes it impossible.
5. **Verify National Crime Agency (NCA) staff via their 24/7 control centre.** If the person claims to be from the NCA, call **0370 496 7622** to check their identity.
6. **Report fraud-based impersonation to the national reporting service.** If the interaction involved a demand for money or sensitive data, report it to **Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) via their website or by calling 0300 123 2040.
7. **Contact Police Scotland directly for any incidents occurring in Scotland.** Use **101** for non-emergencies or **999** if you are currently being intimidated or followed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide an immediate statement or "evidence" to the person at your door/on the phone.
- You do not need to confirm the person is definitely a scammer before calling 101; "checking for peace of mind" is a standard and accepted reason for a verification call.
- You can delay responding to "urgent" emails or texts from authorities until you have logged into an official government portal independently.

## Important reassurance
Official authorities expect and encourage the public to verify their identity. A genuine officer or agent will not be offended, aggressive, or impatient if you ask to check their credentials through official channels. Feeling startled or pressured is exactly what impersonators want; taking a moment to pause is your safest and most effective tool.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate safety and verification. It does not cover the legal specifics of arrest warrants or long-term fraud recovery.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. In any situation involving immediate physical threat or attempted forced entry, prioritize calling 999 and finding a secure location.
