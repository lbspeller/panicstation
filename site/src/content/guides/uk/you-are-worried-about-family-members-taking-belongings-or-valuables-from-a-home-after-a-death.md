# What to do if… you are worried about family members taking belongings or valuables from a home after a death (UK)

## Short answer
Pause sorting, removals, and dividing items. Secure the home, record what is there, and keep access transparent until the person with authority to deal with the estate is clear.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t remove items “for safekeeping” without clear authority or agreement, unless there is an immediate risk of loss and you document exactly what happened.
- Don’t start dividing possessions, making promises, or letting people choose items before authority and a process are clear.
- Don’t argue room-by-room in the home or make accusations in the moment.
- Don’t throw away, donate, sell, or give away anything to clear space.
- Don’t change locks or restrict access unilaterally unless there is an immediate security risk; if that has to happen, document why, who agreed, and who now holds keys.
- Don’t send emotional messages about blame, theft, or entitlement while you are still trying to secure and record the contents.

## What to do now
1. **Set a calm freeze rule for today.** Say once: “No items leave the house until we have recorded what is here and the person dealing with the estate confirms next steps.” Keep repeating the same line without debating it.

2. **Work out who has, or may have, authority over the estate.**
   - If there is a will, identify the named executor and note where the will is being kept.
   - Do not circulate the will widely in a family chat; share only what is needed to identify who should deal with the estate.
   - If no will is found yet, do not assume that the loudest or closest person can start distributing things.
   - The authority needed may be called probate, letters of administration, confirmation, or a grant, depending on where in the UK the estate is being dealt with.
   - The person legally dealing with the estate is responsible for protecting estate assets, and formal authority may be needed before some assets can be dealt with. Keep urgent safeguarding separate from distributing or selling anything.

3. **Secure the home in a low-drama way.**
   - Gather keys and write down who currently holds each set.
   - Lock doors and windows.
   - Close off non-essential rooms if that reduces casual access.
   - If the property will be empty, contact the home insurer and ask what unoccupied-property conditions apply, including inspections, locks, heating, water, and time limits. Write down the date, time, person spoken to, and what they said.

4. **Make a baseline record before anyone sorts anything.**
   - Do a slow walk-through and take clear photos or video of each room.
   - Photograph obvious valuables, cupboards, wardrobes, drawers, safes, cabinets, and boxes from the outside first.
   - Do not force locks, open safes, empty drawers, search private containers, or rummage unless it is agreed, necessary, and witnessed.
   - Start a simple inventory: item, brief description, where found, date and time, and who was present.

5. **If cash, jewellery, keys, documents, or small valuables are visibly at risk, use a transparency method.**
   - Move only the minimum needed to prevent loss.
   - Put the items in a container or envelope.
   - Write a contents note.
   - Take a photo of the items and the sealed container.
   - Have two people present where possible.
   - Tell the executor, likely administrator, executor dative, or other person dealing with the estate where the items are stored and share the photo and list.

6. **Avoid one-person access while the risk is high.**
   - If anyone must enter the house before things are formalised, use two people where possible.
   - Keep a short entry log: date, time, who entered, why they entered, and what was moved or checked.
   - Send factual updates in one group message or email thread so there is a single record.

7. **Separate safeguarding from ownership.**
   - Use words like “recorded”, “secured”, and “paused”.
   - Avoid words like “mine”, “stolen”, “promised”, or “owed” while you are still recording the contents.
   - If someone says an item was promised to them, ask them to put that in writing for later. Do not decide it in the house.

8. **If you suspect theft, intimidation, or immediate risk:**
   - If someone is in immediate danger, violence is threatened, or a theft is happening now, call 999.
   - If it is not an emergency but you believe a crime has happened or is likely, contact the police on 101 or through the relevant police online contact route.
   - Keep the report factual: what item, where it was last seen, who had access, and why you are concerned.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who gets what.
- You do not need to clear the house today.
- You do not need to value every item today.
- You do not need to arrange sales, donations, removals, or house clearance today.
- You do not need to resolve older family grievances before the contents are secured and recorded.

## Important reassurance
A pause can feel awkward, but it is often the least inflammatory option. A clear record and controlled access protect everyone, including the person doing the practical work.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce loss and conflict in the first hours or days. Probate, confirmation, distribution, insurance disputes, and disagreements about entitlement may need a solicitor, mediation, court steps, or another specialist route later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, insurance, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If there is serious conflict, threats, suspected theft, uncertainty about authority, or a valuable estate, get advice from a probate solicitor, insurer, or police contact route, depending on the issue.
