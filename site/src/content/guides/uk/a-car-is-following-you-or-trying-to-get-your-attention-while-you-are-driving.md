# What to do if… a car is following you or trying to get your attention while you are driving (UK)

## Short answer
Keep driving safely to a busy, well-lit place with people around, keep your doors locked and windows up, and call 999 or 112 if you think you may be in immediate danger. Do not stop or open the window to find out what they want.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pull over just because they point, wave, shout, honk, or motion for you to stop.
- Do not open your window, unlock your doors, or accept anything handed to you.
- Do not gesture back, argue, stare, or try to intimidate them.
- Do not drive home, to work, or to a quiet place where you could be boxed in.
- Do not try to lose them by speeding, running lights, swerving, or making risky turns.
- Do not pick up and use a hand-held phone while driving unless you need to call 999 or 112 in a genuine emergency and it is unsafe or impractical to stop.

## What to do now
1. **Make the car less easy to approach.** Lock the doors, keep windows up, keep bags and valuables out of sight, and keep enough safe space ahead so you are not trapped behind another vehicle.
2. **Keep driving normally and legally.** Hold a steady, safe speed, use main roads where possible, and avoid sudden lane changes unless they are needed for safety.
3. **Change your destination to somewhere public.** Head for a busy, well-lit petrol station, supermarket car park, staffed drive-through, a public-facing police station if one is nearby and clearly open, or another place with people, lights, and exits.
4. **Call 999 or 112 if you feel in immediate danger.** Use hands-free if you can do that without losing control. If you cannot call safely, keep going to the nearest busy place and call as soon as you can safely stop.
5. **Give the operator practical details.** Say where you are, your direction of travel, your vehicle description, what the other car is doing, and the other car’s colour, make, model, or registration if you can notice it safely.
6. **Do not test or investigate them.** If they keep mirroring normal, legal route choices you were already making, treat it as a safety concern and continue towards a public place while contacting police.
7. **If traffic forces you to stop, stay ready to move.** Keep doors locked, leave space between you and the vehicle in front, and do not lower the window more than a small gap unless police or emergency services tell you to.
8. **When you reach a public place, park for visibility.** Choose lights, entrances, staff, CCTV, or other people nearby. Keep the car locked and stay inside if that feels safer while you call for help.
9. **If it is no longer an emergency, report it when safely parked.** Use 101 or your local police online reporting route, especially if you have the registration number, dashcam footage, or a clear description.
10. **Save details while they are fresh.** Write down the time, road names, direction of travel, vehicle description, what the occupants did, and whether you have dashcam footage.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether it was definitely dangerous or probably nothing.
- You do not need to confront them, identify them, or prove their motive.
- You do not need to drive to your original destination until you feel safe.
- You do not need to call several people, post online, or rewatch footage until you are safely stopped.

## Important reassurance
Repeated attempts to get your attention from another moving car can feel alarming, and it is reasonable to treat it as a safety concern. Moving towards people, light, and police contact keeps your options open without escalating the situation.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes only. Once you are safe, later decisions about reporting, insurance, dashcam footage, or further support may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999 or 112.
