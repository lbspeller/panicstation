# What to do if… your car door is frozen shut and you need to get inside (UK)

## Short answer
Do not force the handle; instead, use a commercial de-icer on the door seals and apply firm inward pressure to the door panel to break the ice bond.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pull forcefully on the door handle as plastic components can snap in freezing temperatures.
- Do not pour boiling, hot, or lukewarm water on the windows, locks, or seals due to the risk of glass cracking and immediate refreezing.
- Do not use a naked flame (lighter or match) near the car door, paintwork, or seals.
- Do not use metal tools like screwdrivers or knives to pry the door open.
- Do not force a key into a frozen lock or turn it with excessive pressure.
- Do not leave the car engine running unattended while you are outside the vehicle.

## What to do now
1. **Check every potential entry point to the vehicle.** Attempt to open the passenger doors, rear doors, and the boot or tailgate, as one side may be less affected by wind or ice than the others.
2. **Ensure the windscreen wiper stalk is in the "off" position.** If you manage to start the car while the wipers are frozen to the glass, you risk burning out the wiper motor or tearing the rubber blades.
3. **Apply firm, even pressure to the door panel.** Instead of pulling the handle, place your hands flat on the door and **push the door inward** against the frame several times to crack the ice bond along the rubber seals.
4. **Target the perimeter of the door with commercial de-icer.** Spray the product along the entire length of the door seal where the rubber meets the metal bodywork and wait at least 60 seconds for the alcohol to dissolve the ice.
5. **Warm the lock or key safely if the cylinder is frozen.** 
   - Use a specific lock de-icer spray if available.
   - Hold the metal part of the key in your gloved hands for several minutes to transfer body heat before attempting to insert it.
   - Do not use hand sanitiser inside the lock as the gel residues can attract grit and cause mechanical failure over time.
6. **Use a hairdryer on a low heat setting if you are at a domestic property.** Direct the airflow along the door seal and handle from a distance of at least 15cm, keeping the dryer moving constantly to avoid overheating the paint or plastic.
7. **Monitor yourself for signs of cold exposure.** Stop immediately and move to a warm indoor area if you experience:
   - Persistent shivering or numbness in fingers and toes.
   - Slurred speech or confusion.
   - Pale, cold, or dry skin.
8. **Prioritise Highway Code safety if the vehicle is on a public road or motorway.** 
   - If you are stopped on a motorway hard shoulder, exit via the left-hand door and wait behind the safety barrier.
   - If you are stopped in a live lane on a "Smart Motorway" and cannot exit, stay in the vehicle with your seatbelt on and hazard lights active, and call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to fix the door seals or alignment right now.
- You do not need to clean any de-icer residue off the paintwork until the temperature rises above freezing.
- You do not need to decide on long-term winter storage solutions today.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for car doors to bond to rubber seals during UK cold snaps, and the resistance you feel is the ice, not necessarily a mechanical failure. Taking a few extra minutes to melt the ice rather than yanking the handle will prevent expensive bodywork or handle repairs.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for accessing a frozen vehicle. It does not cover mechanical repairs to door actuators, central locking electronics, or long-term vehicle maintenance.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace professional mechanical or breakdown assistance. If you are in a remote area and cannot gain access, contact a breakdown provider. In any situation where cold exposure poses a threat to life, call 999 or 112.
