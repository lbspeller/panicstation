# What to do if… a loved one has died in another city and you need to travel urgently (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before travelling, confirm the key facts with the official point of contact, and choose the safest travel option you can while someone you trust helps with calls and messages.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t drive a long distance if you are shaking, numb, exhausted, or unable to focus; use rail, coach, flight, taxi, or ask someone else to drive if you can.
- Don’t agree to costs, collections, funeral arrangements, or transport of the deceased because a stranger says it is urgent.
- Don’t give bank details, identity documents, or payment details to anyone who contacts you unexpectedly.
- Don’t post details publicly before close family have been told and the official facts are confirmed.
- Don’t start cancelling accounts, tenancies, benefits, or contracts while you are still in shock and unsure who has authority to deal with them.
- Don’t assume everything has to be decided today.

## What to do now
1. **Get one official named contact before you set off.**  
   Ask the hospital, police, coroner’s office, mortuary, or other official caller for their name, role, direct number or switchboard route, the address you may need to attend, and any case or reference number. If you are unsure the call is genuine, hang up and call the organisation back through its main switchboard.

2. **Confirm where your loved one is and what happens next.**  
   Ask: “Where are they now?”, “Who is the lead contact?”, “Is a coroner or procurator fiscal involved?”, “Can I attend today?”, and “When will someone contact me again?” Write the answers down, even if they feel obvious.

3. **Ask what to bring before travelling.**  
   Usually useful items are your photo ID, your phone and charger, a payment card, any medication, your loved one’s full name, date of birth, address, and any reference number you have been given. Ask whether you need an appointment, whether there are viewing arrangements, and whether security rules apply.

4. **Choose a travel plan that assumes you are in shock.**  
   Ask someone to come with you if possible. If you travel alone, share your route and arrival time with one trusted person. Pack only the essentials: ID, charger, water, snack, medication, warm layer, and the written details from step 1.

5. **If you have to drive, reduce the risk before leaving.**  
   Delay setting off until you can focus, ask someone else to drive if possible, put the destination into the satnav before moving, avoid making calls while driving, and plan stops. If your concentration drops, stop somewhere safe and ask for help.

6. **Delegate family messages to one trusted person.**  
   Send one short message such as: “I have had official news that [name] has died. I am travelling now and will share confirmed details when I have them.” Ask that person to keep a list of who has been told so you do not have to repeat the news over and over.

7. **Ask about belongings, viewing, and identification without committing to decisions.**  
   Ask where personal belongings are held, what ID is needed to collect them, whether viewing is possible, and whether anyone needs to attend for identification. If a coroner or procurator fiscal is involved, some steps may be delayed or handled through that office.

8. **Tell work only what is necessary today.**  
   A simple message is enough: “A close family bereavement has happened. I need emergency time away and I am travelling now. I will update you when I can.” In the UK, paid bereavement leave is generally not a legal right unless a specific entitlement applies, but many employers offer compassionate leave. Employees can usually take reasonable emergency time off for a dependant, including when a dependant dies. If a child under 18 has died, or there has been a stillbirth after 24 weeks, statutory parental bereavement leave may apply.

9. **When you arrive, verify before paying or signing anything.**  
   Use the official number you wrote down or the organisation’s main switchboard. If anyone pressures you for money, bank details, funeral decisions, or urgent collection arrangements, pause and verify with the official contact first.

## What can wait
- Choosing a funeral director, funeral date, service details, or transport of the deceased.
- Deciding what to do with property, belongings, debts, wills, probate, benefits, or accounts.
- Notifying every organisation immediately; government notifications are usually handled later through Tell Us Once or the relevant local route, depending on where the person lived.
- Writing public announcements or replying to every message.
- Explaining everything clearly to everyone. A short holding message is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
Shock can feel like numbness, shaking, confusion, calmness, or moving on autopilot. That does not mean you are reacting wrongly. For now, the aim is to get safe, confirm the official contact, avoid irreversible decisions, and travel only in a way you can manage.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the first hours and the urgent travel period. Later decisions about registration, the coroner or procurator fiscal, funeral arrangements, employment rights, money, property, and the estate may need help from the relevant official service, employer, adviser, or solicitor.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, employment, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes vary across the UK and depend on the circumstances of the death, where the person lived, where they died, and who the official service can speak to or who has legal authority for later decisions.
