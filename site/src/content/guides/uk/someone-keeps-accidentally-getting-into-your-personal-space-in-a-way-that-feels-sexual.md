# What to do if… someone keeps “accidentally” getting into your personal space in a way that feels sexual (UK)

## Short answer
Physically move to a high-visibility area (near staff or a group), set one short verbal boundary, and alert authority figures if the behavior continues. In any situation involving physical contact or immediate threat, contact the police.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel obligated to be polite if your physical boundaries are being crossed.
- Do not isolate yourself in quiet carriages, lifts, or stairwells with the person.
- Do not attempt to negotiate or explain your feelings; use short, directive phrases only.
- Do not ignore your instinct that the contact is intentional rather than accidental.
- Do not put yourself in physical reach to record the person on a mobile device.

## What to do now
1. **Move immediately to a high-visibility location.** Step away, change seats, or move toward staff, families, or larger groups to create an external buffer.
2. **Observe concrete sensory cues to confirm the need for action.**
   - Feeling breath on your neck or hair.
   - Repeated "brushing" that follows your movement.
   - The person standing significantly closer than the queue or crowd requires.
   - Seeing the person looking at your body while closing the gap.
3. **State one firm, audible boundary.** Use a short phrase and repeat it if necessary without engaging in further conversation:
   - "Step back."
   - "Don't touch me."
   - "You are too close; move away."
4. **Identify and approach an authority figure.**
   - In venues: Speak to bar staff, security, or "Ask for Angela" if the program is active.
   - On trains/trams: Move to the conductor's area or use the emergency help point.
   - At work: Go to a manager's office or a HR representative immediately.
5. **Use the British Transport Police (BTP) discreet reporting service.** If on a train or at a station, text **61016** with your location, carriage number, and a description of what is happening (non-emergency). Use the slogan "See it. Say it. Sorted." as a guide for what to include.
6. **Request a witness or temporary companion.** Stand next to a group or individual and say: "Someone is making me feel unsafe; may I stand with you until my stop / until security arrives?"
7. **Contact the police for immediate danger or reporting.**
   - Call **999** if the person is following you, touching you, or if you feel at immediate risk.
   - Call **101** or use your local force's online reporting tool for non-emergency reports.
8. **Access confidential support without the requirement to report.**
   - England and Wales: Call the Rape Crisis 24/7 Support Line on **0808 500 2222**.
   - Scotland: Call Rape Crisis Scotland on **08088 01 03 02**.
   - Northern Ireland: Contact Nexus NI on **028 9032 6803**.
9. **Log specific details for your own records.** Note the time, date, specific location (CCTV coverage area), and a physical description of the person.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a formal police report right now.
- You do not need to determine if the behavior meets a specific legal definition of harassment or assault to seek help.
- You do not need to gather evidence or confront the person again.
- You do not need to justify your reaction to others or "prove" it wasn't an accident.

## Important reassurance
Freezing or feeling confused when your space is invaded is a common and natural response. Moving to safety and setting a boundary are practical ways to manage the situation, regardless of how "accidental" the other person claims their behavior is.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance for immediate stabilization and safety. Long-term legal options, workplace grievance procedures (including April 2026 whistleblowing protections), and therapeutic support require specialist consultation.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. Employers have a legal duty under the Employment Rights Act 2025 to take all reasonable steps to prevent sexual harassment, including from third parties.
