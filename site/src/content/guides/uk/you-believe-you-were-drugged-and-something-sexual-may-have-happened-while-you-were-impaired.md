# What to do if… you believe you were drugged and something sexual may have happened while you were impaired (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safe place immediately and contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) or A&E for medical and forensic support, as evidence of drugging disappears from the system very quickly.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to mentally reconstruct the event or "fill in the gaps" of your memory while you are still in shock.
- Do not contact or confront the person you suspect of involvement, as this can escalate risk and create complex digital records.
- Do not feel pressured to report to the police immediately if you are not ready; medical care and forensic collection are available without a police report.
- Do not drive any vehicle if you still feel drowsy, dizzy, or uncoordinated.
- Do not shower, change clothes, or brush your teeth until you have spoken with a SARC or medical professional, if you wish to keep forensic options open.

## What to do now
1. **Find a safer pause immediately by moving to a well-lit public area or the home of a trusted friend.** If you feel you are in immediate physical danger or the person involved is still nearby, call **999**.
2. **Seek urgent medical attention at A&E if you are experiencing severe symptoms like difficulty breathing, repeated vomiting, or seizures.** Mention specifically that you suspect you have been spiked, as clinical staff need this context to prioritise certain tests.
3. **Contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) as soon as possible to discuss your options for a forensic medical examination.** You can self-refer to these centres without involving the police; they provide specialist care and can store evidence while you decide what to do next.
   - In Scotland, call the **National Self-Referral Phone Service** on **0800 148 8888** (24/7).
   - In Northern Ireland, contact **The Rowan** on **0800 389 4424**.
   - In England and Wales, find your local centre at **nhs.uk/sarcs**.
4. **Call a 24-hour helpline if you need immediate guidance on where to go or how to access support in your area.** 
   - England, Scotland, and Wales: Call **NHS 111**.
   - Northern Ireland: Call the **Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline** on **0808 802 1414**.
5. **Preserve physical evidence by placing any clothing worn at the time into a clean paper bag if you decide to change.** Avoid washing any part of your body until you have been advised by a SARC, though urgent medical needs must always come first.
6. **Note down the basic facts you remember in a secure digital note or on paper.** Include the location, approximate times, names of people you were with, and any physical symptoms you noticed (such as sudden "heavy" limbs or blurred vision).
7. **Ask a trusted person to stay with you for the next few hours.** Having someone present can help monitor your physical recovery and provide support during medical appointments or phone calls.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you want to pursue a criminal prosecution.
- You do not need to have a clear or complete memory of the event to be eligible for support or medical care.
- You do not need to notify your employer, university, or the venue where the incident may have occurred immediately.
- You do not need to justify why you were drinking or out late; the focus is entirely on your current safety and health.

## Important reassurance
Memory loss and confusion are physiological responses to certain substances and are also common symptoms of psychological shock. Not being able to remember what happened does not make your experience any less significant, nor does it mean you are responsible for the situation. Your primary concern right now is your physical health and safety.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction advice only. Long-term medical follow-up, legal decisions, and psychological support will require coordination with specialist services like SARCs or Rape Crisis.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute medical or legal advice. If you are experiencing a medical emergency or are in immediate danger, call 999. Forensic evidence of spiking is time-sensitive and often undetectable after 12 to 24 hours.
