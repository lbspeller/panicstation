# What to do if… a radiator or heating pipe starts leaking water onto the floor (UK)

## Short answer
Turn the heating and boiler off immediately to reduce pressure, then isolate the leaking radiator by closing both valves at its base. If water is near electrical sockets or switches, do not touch them; switch off power at the consumer unit if safe to do so.

## Do not do these things
- Do not touch the leaking water or the radiator with bare hands, as it may be hot enough to cause scalds.
- Do not touch light switches, sockets, or the boiler switch if they are wet or if you are standing in water.
- Do not keep the heating running to "see if it clears"; heat increases pressure and may worsen the leak.
- Do not attempt to tighten pipe joints or valves with force, as this can cause old fittings to crack or fail completely.
- Do not use the filling loop to top up the boiler pressure while the system is actively leaking.
- Do not use towels or absorbent materials near live electrical equipment or extension leads.

## What to do now
1. **Clear the immediate area.** Move people and pets away from the puddle, especially if water is moving toward electrical items. Wear shoes to prevent slips or burns.
2. **Switch off the heating and boiler.** 
   - Use the thermostat or programmer to turn the heating off. 
   - **Only if your hands are dry and you are not standing in water**, switch the boiler off at its local wall switch (fused spur).
3. **Address electrical hazards.**
   - If water is near sockets, cables, or the consumer unit, and you can reach the main switch safely (without stepping in water), turn off the electricity.
   - If you cannot safely reach the power switch, call **105** (UK national electrical emergency number) for advice.
4. **Isolate the leaking radiator.**
   - **Close the control valve:** This is usually the Thermostatic Radiator Valve (TRV) with numbers. Turn it clockwise until it reaches "0" or the "frost" symbol.
   - **Close the lockshield valve:** This is on the opposite end and usually has a plastic cap. Remove the cap and use a spanner or pliers to turn the metal spindle clockwise until it stops. 
   - If the leak is from a pipe before the valves and won't stop, turn off the **mains water** at the internal stopcock (usually under the kitchen sink).
5. **Manage the water flow.** Place a bucket, tray, or bowl directly under the drip. Use towels to create a "dam" around the puddle to prevent it from reaching other rooms or skirting boards.
6. **Check for injuries.**
   - If someone has been scalded by hot water, run the burn under cool running water for 20 minutes and call **111**. 
   - If someone has received an electrical shock, call **999** immediately.
7. **Contact your landlord or a professional.**
   - **If you rent:** Report the leak to your landlord or housing association’s emergency repair line immediately. 
   - **If you own:** Call a Gas Safe registered heating engineer or a plumber. 
8. **Document the situation briefly.** Take 2–3 photos of the leak source and any visible floor/wall damage for insurance or repair records once the area is stabilized.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific technical cause (e.g., pinhole corrosion vs. failed O-ring) right now.
- You do not need to file an insurance claim in the first hour; focus on stopping the water first.
- You do not need to attempt a permanent repair of the pipework yourself.
- You do not need to mop the floor perfectly until the flow has completely stopped.

## Important reassurance
Radiator leaks often look worse than they are because water spreads quickly across hard floors. Once the boiler is off and the valves are closed, the pressure will drop and the flow will eventually stop. Taking these steps prevents long-term structural damage.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization only. Permanent repairs to the heating system must be carried out by a qualified professional to ensure the system is correctly refilled and balanced.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization. Always treat water near electricity as a high-risk hazard. If you are unsure of your ability to safely turn off the water or power, evacuate the room and wait for professional help.
