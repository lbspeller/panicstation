# What to do if… your HVAC unit is leaking water and pooling on the floor (UK)

## Short answer
Turn the HVAC or air conditioning off at the controller and keep people away from any water near electrics. If there is smoke, burning, fire, or immediate danger, move away and call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep the HVAC or air conditioning running to see if it stops.
- Don’t touch switches, plugs, fused spurs, the consumer unit, or the unit’s electrical parts if your hands are wet or you are standing in water.
- Don’t open electrical covers or panels on the unit.
- Don’t pour chemicals down the condensate drain line or force objects into pipework.
- Don’t use mains-powered appliances in the wet area.
- Don’t ignore water tracking into ceilings, walls, sockets, switches, or light fittings.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the system.** Turn the HVAC or air conditioning off at the thermostat, remote, or wall controller.

2. **Make a dry, safe pause before touching anything else.**
   - Keep children, pets, and other people away from the wet area.
   - Do not step into water to reach a switch or plug.
   - If water is near the consumer unit, meter, service head, incoming supply, or you cannot safely isolate power, keep away and call 105 for your local electricity network operator’s emergency route.
   - For internal wiring, sockets, switches, lights, or the HVAC unit itself, use a qualified electrician or HVAC engineer.

3. **Isolate only if you can do it dry and safely.**
   - If the local isolator or fused spur for the unit is dry, reachable, and away from the water, switch it off.
   - If the route to it is wet, leave it alone and wait for qualified help.
   - Do not restore power to affected electrical fittings or the HVAC unit until a qualified person says it is safe.

4. **Contain the water without going near electrics.**
   - Put a bucket, bowl, or tray under the drip point if you can do this safely.
   - Lay towels on the floor to stop water spreading.
   - Move rugs, boxes, furniture, and valuables away from the water.
   - Use towels as a small barrier to steer water away from sockets, doorways, and electrical equipment.

5. **Check only what is visible and safe.**
   - If you can see the condensate pipe without opening covers, check whether it is kinked, loose, or obviously disconnected.
   - Gently straighten or re-seat only pipework that is dry, visible, and easy to reach.
   - If the condensate pipe discharges outside or into a visible drain, check only the visible end for a clear blockage.
   - If there is a condensate pump, do not unplug it or handle its cables if anything is damp; note whether the pump or tray seems to be overflowing.

6. **Use the right repair route.**
   - If you rent privately, report it to your landlord or agent using their emergency or urgent repair route, especially if water is near electrics or causing damage. Put a short message in writing after the immediate risk is controlled.
   - If you rent from a council or housing association, use their emergency repairs line for leaks, electrical risk, or water entering ceilings or walls.
   - If you own the property, contact an HVAC or air conditioning engineer. If water may have reached wiring, sockets, switches, lights, the consumer unit, or the local isolator, contact an electrician before power is restored.

7. **Record the situation once it is safe.**
   - Take photos of the leak point, water pooling, affected floor, walls, ceilings, and any nearby electrics.
   - Note when you turned the system off and who you contacted.
   - Keep damaged items aside if you may need them for a landlord, engineer, or insurer.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to diagnose the exact cause now.
- You don’t need to decide whether it is a blocked drain, full drip tray, pump fault, frozen coil, or disconnected pipe.
- You don’t need to open the unit, remove ceiling panels, flush pipework, or buy parts now.
- You don’t need to decide about insurance until the leak is stopped, the area is safe, and any urgent repair route has been started.

## Important reassurance
A leaking condensate line is a common air conditioning fault and is often repairable. The important first steps are to stop the system, keep water away from electrics, contain the water, and get the right help without taking the unit apart.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need a qualified HVAC or air conditioning engineer, electrician, landlord repair team, insurer, or housing adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, electrical, building, tenancy, or other professional advice. If you suspect an electrical hazard or you cannot safely isolate equipment, keep away from the area and use qualified help.
