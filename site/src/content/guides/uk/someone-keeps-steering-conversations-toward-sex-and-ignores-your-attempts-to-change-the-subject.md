# What to do if… someone keeps steering conversations toward sex and ignores your attempts to change the subject (UK)

## Short answer
State clearly that you are not discussing the topic, immediately end the interaction by leaving or stopping replies, and move to a public or staffed area.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use "soft" or apologetic language to decline the topic if your first attempt to change it was ignored.
- Don’t explain or justify why the topic makes you uncomfortable, as this can be treated as an invitation to debate.
- Don’t stay in the interaction out of a sense of social obligation or politeness.
- Don’t share any personal details in an attempt to "distract" them or "bridge" the conversation.
- Don’t agree to meet in a private place to "clear the air" or explain your boundaries further.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the sensory cues that signal a boundary breach.** Recognize if you are experiencing:
   - A "stomach sinking" or "cold" physical sensation.
   - The same sexual topic being reintroduced despite you changing the subject twice.
   - The other person ignoring direct verbal cues like "I don't want to talk about that."
2. **Issue one final, unambiguous stop statement.** Use a neutral but firm tone without apologizing.
   - "I have tried to change the subject; I am not discussing sex with you."
   - "Stop. That topic is inappropriate."
3. **End the interaction immediately without waiting for a response.** Choose the fastest exit strategy:
   - In person: Walk away toward a group of people, a staffed desk, or an exit.
   - On the phone: State "I am hanging up now," and disconnect.
   - Digital/Online: Close the app, mute the notification, and do not read further messages.
4. **Move to a "safe pause" location.** Go to a place where you are not alone with the individual:
   - A public cafe, a busy office area, or your own locked home.
   - If at an event, locate a member of staff or security and stay near them.
5. **Contact a trusted third party for an "exit bridge."** Send a short message to a friend: "I am in an uncomfortable situation, please call me now and stay on the line until I am home."
6. **Record the details while they are fresh.** Document the time, location, specific phrases used, and how you attempted to change the subject. Keep screenshots of digital messages but do not engage with them.
7. **Access jurisdictional support if needed.**
   - **England & Wales (age 16+):** Call the National Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line on **0808 500 2222** (24/7).
   - **Scotland (age 13+):** Call the Rape Crisis Scotland helpline on **08088 01 03 02** (daily 5pm–midnight).
   - **Northern Ireland (age 16+):** Call the Rape Crisis NI Support Line on **0800 0246 991** (Mon–Thu 10am–12pm and 6pm–8pm).
   - **Under 19s (UK-wide):** Call Childline on **0800 1111** (24/7).
8. **Utilize 2026 workplace protections if relevant.** Under the Employment Rights Act 2025 (effective April 2026), reporting sexual harassment is a "qualifying disclosure" for whistleblowing protection. Contact Acas for guidance on how to report this to your employer safely.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide if you want to report this to the police or HR immediately.
- You do **not** need to "forgive" the person or decide if they "meant it that way."
- You do **not** need to find the "perfect" words to explain your feelings to them later.
- You do **not** need to delete or organize evidence immediately; just ensure it is saved.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel startled or "frozen" when someone repeatedly ignores your boundaries. Using "social politeness" to manage the situation is a common protective response and not a sign that you consented to the conversation. You are entitled to end any interaction that makes you feel unsafe or pressured.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction for unwanted sexualized conversation. It does not cover legal proceedings, long-term workplace grievance processes, or therapeutic recovery.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or clinical advice. In the UK, you can access specialist support anonymously without being required to report to the police. If you are in immediate physical danger, call **999**.
