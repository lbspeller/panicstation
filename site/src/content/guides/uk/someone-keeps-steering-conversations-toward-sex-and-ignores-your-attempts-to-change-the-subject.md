# What to do if… someone keeps steering conversations toward sex and ignores your attempts to change the subject (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a boundary and safety problem, not something you need to smooth over. Make one clear stop statement, then end the interaction and move toward a safer context.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep softening your boundary if they have already ignored you.
- Don’t debate, justify, or explain your discomfort if that would keep you trapped in the conversation.
- Don’t share sexual or personal details to try to redirect them.
- Don’t meet them alone to clear the air if they have shown they will not respect a no.
- Don’t feel you have to stay polite if you feel uneasy, cornered, or pressured.

## What to do now
1. **Say one clear stop statement.** Use one sentence, then stop explaining:
   - “Stop. I’m not talking about sex.”
   - “I said no. Change the subject, or I’m ending this conversation.”
   - “That’s inappropriate. I’m leaving now.”
2. **End the interaction in the simplest safe way.**
   - In person: step back, move toward other people, go to a staffed or public area, or leave.
   - On the phone: say “I’m hanging up now,” then hang up.
   - By message: stop replying, mute or block, and do not reopen the thread to argue.
3. **Use a neutral exit line if a direct stop feels unsafe.** For example: “I need the toilet,” “I’ve got to take this call,” or “I’m late,” then go.
4. **Bring in another person.** Message or call someone you trust: “Can you call me now? I need an exit.” If you are at work, school, college, a venue, or an event, move toward a supervisor, reception, security, a tutor, or a trusted colleague.
5. **Keep control of any existing messages if it is safe.** If you already have messages or screenshots, consider keeping them unchanged somewhere safe, but do not put yourself at risk to collect anything.
6. **If this is happening at work, use a workplace route if it feels safe.** You can speak to a manager, HR, a union rep, or Acas about practical options. You do not have to work out the whole complaint process right now.
7. **Get specialist support if you want it.** This is not a commitment to report.
   - England and Wales, age 16 or over: 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line, 0808 500 2222, phone and online chat.
   - Scotland: Rape Crisis Scotland helpline, 08088 01 03 02, every day from 5pm to midnight.
   - Northern Ireland: Rape Crisis NI Information and Support Line, 0800 0246 991, Monday to Thursday from 6pm to 8pm.
   - If you are a child or young person in the UK: Childline, 0800 1111, phone and online support.
8. **If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.** If you want police help but it is not an emergency, call 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to make a formal complaint or report to police.
- You do not need to write the perfect message explaining yourself.
- You do not need to confront them again to prove your point.
- You do not need to decide what label fits before getting support.
- You do not need to make a long-term decision about the relationship, job, group chat, friendship, or workplace today.

## Important reassurance
It is common to freeze, laugh, go quiet, or try to steer the topic away when someone makes things sexual. Ignoring your attempts to change the subject is information: you are allowed to prioritise your safety and comfort over keeping the mood smooth.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate stabilisation and harm prevention. If the person is in your workplace, school, friendship group, family, or home life, later decisions may need specialist help or a safer reporting route.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If anything sexual happened without your consent, or you are not sure what counts, you can still get confidential specialist support without having to report.
