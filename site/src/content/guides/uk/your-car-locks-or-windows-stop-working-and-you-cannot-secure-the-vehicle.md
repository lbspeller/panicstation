# What to do if… your car locks or windows stop working and you cannot secure the vehicle (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself and the car to the safest practical place, remove valuables and vehicle documents, keep the keys with you, and arrange breakdown, garage, or auto-locksmith help so the vehicle can be secured or recovered.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t leave the car unattended in an isolated spot if it cannot be locked.
- Don’t leave keys, the V5C/logbook, or documents showing your address inside the vehicle.
- Don’t force windows, doors, or locks with improvised tools if it could break glass or damage the mechanism.
- Don’t leave the engine running unattended while trying to work out the fault.
- Don’t confront anyone you suspect may be tampering with the car; move away and get help.
- Don’t use 999 for a non-emergency; use 999 only if someone is in immediate danger or a crime is happening now.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer place if you can do so safely.** Aim for a busy, well-lit place, a service area, a staffed car park, a garage, or somewhere monitored. If driving is unsafe because the door will not latch, the window blocks visibility, or another fault appears, stop in the safest available place and call breakdown help.
2. **Remove theft targets from the car.** Take the keys, bags, coats, electronics, tools, cash, bank cards, and any documents showing your address. Remove the V5C/logbook if it is in the vehicle.
3. **Check the simple lock and window basics without forcing anything.** Try the spare key if you have it, check whether the key-fob battery may be low, check whether any child-lock or window-lock switch has been pressed, and check whether another door or the boot still locks.
4. **Make the vehicle less attractive while you arrange help.** Close any windows that will close, shut and test each door gently, fit a visible steering wheel lock or other physical deterrent if you have one, and keep the keys away from the car if it has keyless entry.
5. **Stay near the car only if it is safe.** If the location feels unsafe, there are suspicious people nearby, or the car is on a dangerous road, move to a safe public place and keep the vehicle in sight only if you can do that without risk.
6. **If you suspect tampering, move away first.** If someone is trying to get into the car now, or anyone is in immediate danger, call 999. If it is not happening now, report suspicious activity or attempted theft through 101 or the police online route.
7. **Arrange rapid help to secure or move the vehicle.** Call your breakdown provider, a reputable garage, or an auto locksmith. Tell them whether the fault is a door lock, boot lock, window, key fob, dead battery, or door latch, and whether the car can be driven safely.
8. **If the car cannot be secured where it is, prioritise recovery or secure storage.** Ask whether it can be taken to a locked garage, compound, repairer, or monitored parking location rather than left unsecured on the street.
9. **Record the situation after immediate safety steps.** Take quick photos of the lock, window, door, any damage or tool marks, and where the car is parked. Note the time, location, and who you contacted for repair, recovery, police, or insurance records.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the electrical or mechanical cause right now.
- You do not need to decide repair costs, upgrades, or replacement parts on the spot.
- You do not need to make an insurance decision immediately unless the vehicle has been stolen, damaged, or you need to notify your insurer promptly under your policy.
- You do not need to prove whether it was a fault or attempted theft before making the car safer.

## Important reassurance
A sudden lock or window failure can feel exposing, but the useful first steps are simple: get to a safer place, remove what would make theft easier, keep the keys with you, and arrange help to secure or recover the car.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce harm and buy time. Repairs, insurance questions, police follow-up, and longer-term security choices may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, mechanical, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe or believe a crime is happening now, prioritise personal safety and contact emergency services.
