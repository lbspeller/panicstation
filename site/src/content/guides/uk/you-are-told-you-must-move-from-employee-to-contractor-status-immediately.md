# What to do if… you are told you must move from employee to contractor status immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Request the specific allegations and the identity of the intended regulator in writing immediately. Do not resign or sign any documents until you have consulted your trade union or professional defence organisation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign immediately in an attempt to stop the referral; most UK regulators require employers to complete the referral regardless of employment status.
- Do not attend any meeting, including "informal chats," without requesting a written agenda and the right to be accompanied.
- Do not send defensive or emotional responses to HR or management via email or messaging apps.
- Do not ignore your regulator’s self-reporting requirements; failing to report a "notifiable event" can be a separate grounds for a fitness to practise investigation.
- Do not discuss the case details with colleagues, on social media, or in workplace group chats.
- Do not delete or alter any work records, even if you believe they are being used unfairly against you.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the specific allegations in writing.** Send a neutral email to the person who made the threat or to HR.
   - Request a clear summary of the alleged misconduct or lack of competence.
   - Ask for the specific dates, times, and policies involved.
   - Ask for the name of the regulator they intend to notify and the specific timeline for that notification.
2. **Review your professional self-reporting duties.** Access your regulator’s website (e.g., NMC, GMC, HCPC, GPhC, SRA) and look for the "Fitness to Practise" or "Reporting Concerns" section.
   - Identify the "threshold" for self-reporting (e.g., being placed under formal investigation).
   - Check the time limit for reporting (often "as soon as reasonably practicable" or within 14–30 days).
   - Do not submit the report yet, but mark the deadline in your calendar.
3. **Contact your professional support body.** Call your trade union representative or your professional defence / indemnity provider (e.g., MDU, MPS, RCN, UNISON).
   - Inform them that a referral has been threatened.
   - Share the written allegations once you have them.
   - Request that a representative attend any upcoming meetings with you.
4. **Formally request accompaniment for all meetings.** Even if the employer claims a meeting is an "investigative" stage where no statutory right exists, make the request in writing.
   - State: "I request the presence of [Name/Union Rep] to assist with note-taking and support during this process."
   - If the request is refused, ask for the refusal to be recorded in the meeting minutes.
5. **Gather and isolate your personal evidence.** Do not forward sensitive client or patient data to personal accounts, as this may violate GDPR or confidentiality policies.
   - Print or save copies of your contract, job description, and the employer’s disciplinary / capability policies.
   - Save copies of performance reviews, "thank you" emails from clients / patients, and training certificates.
   - Write a factual, chronological timeline of events while details are fresh.
6. **Submit a Subject Access Request (SAR) if information is being withheld.** If you believe the employer is withholding evidence that supports your position, you have a legal right to request your personal data.
   - Email the Data Protection Officer (DPO) or HR.
   - State: "This is a Subject Access Request under the UK GDPR. I require all personal data held about me, including internal emails, meeting notes, and witness statements relating to [specific incident/dates]."
   - Note that employers have one month to comply but may redact information that "prejudices management planning" or identifies others.
7. **Ensure all meeting notes are accurate.** If you attend a meeting and notes are taken:
   - Ask for a copy of the notes immediately after the meeting.
   - If you disagree with the notes, do not sign them; instead, provide a "marked-up" version or a separate "statement of corrections" to be appended to the file.

## What can wait
- Drafting a detailed rebuttal or "defence statement" (this should only be done once you have seen the full evidence file).
- Deciding whether to file a counter-grievance (this requires strategic advice from your union or lawyer).
- Notifying your regulator (unless you have confirmed that a mandatory reporting threshold has already been met).
- Applying for new roles (wait until you have clarity on whether an interim suspension or "conditions of practice" will be applied).

## Important reassurance
A threat to notify a board is a procedural step, not a final judgement. Many referrals result in no action being taken after an initial assessment by the regulator. Regulatory bodies are focused on current "fitness to practise" rather than punishing past mistakes. Your employer has a duty to follow the ACAS Code of Practice, and any failure to do so can be used in your defence.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for professionals in the UK. It does not cover the long-term legal strategy required for a full Fitness to Practise hearing or an Employment Tribunal claim.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or regulatory advice. Professional regulations vary significantly between sectors (health, law, finance, etc.). You must consult your specific regulator’s handbook and seek advice from a qualified legal professional or trade union officer.
