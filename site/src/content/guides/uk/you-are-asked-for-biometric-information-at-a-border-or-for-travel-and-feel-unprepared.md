# What to do if… you are asked for biometric information at a border or for travel and feel unprepared (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and check who is asking for your biometric information and what process it belongs to. If the request is not clearly from official border staff, a recognised visa application centre, or an official government route, do not hand over sensitive information until you have verified it independently.

## Do not do these things
- Do not give fingerprints or face scans to a person, kiosk, app, or website you cannot confirm is part of an official border or visa process.
- Do not hand your passport, phone, or payment card to an unofficial “helper” offering to sort it out for a fee.
- Do not sign a form you do not understand; ask for an interpreter, a written explanation, or a moment to read it.
- Do not delete emails, appointment notices, boarding passes, booking confirmations, or screenshots in a rush.
- Do not escalate into an argument with border or visa staff; keep your words calm and focused on what you need to do next.
- Do not use a payment link, booking link, or upload link from an unexpected message until you have checked the official source yourself.

## What to do now
1. Work out what kind of biometric request this is.
   - At a border, airport, port, rail terminal, or ferry terminal, ask: “Which authority is collecting this, and is it required for entry today?”
   - At a visa application centre, ask which country the enrolment is for and what appointment notice, passport, or identity document they need to see.
   - In an email, text, call, or social media message before travel, treat the request as unverified until you have checked it on an official government or embassy source.

2. Ask for the minimum clear facts and write them down.
   - What is being collected: fingerprints, a photo, a face scan, or something else?
   - What is it for: entry, visa enrolment, residence permission, or identity verification?
   - Is it required now, and what happens if you cannot complete it today?
   - Is there a fee, and if so, where is the official fee shown?

3. If this involves Schengen travel, check whether it sounds like EES.
   - UK passport holders travelling for a short stay to the Schengen area may be asked to register biometric details, such as fingerprints and a photo.
   - On your first visit to the Schengen area after EES started, you may be asked to create a digital record at the port or airport on arrival.
   - If you enter the Schengen area through the Port of Dover, Eurotunnel Le Shuttle at Folkestone, or Eurostar at St Pancras International, any EES checks are completed at the border before you leave the UK.
   - There is no cost for EES registration, and you do not need to pay a third party or provide information through an unofficial link before travel.

4. Verify the request using official sources, not just links you were sent.
   - Check GOV.UK foreign travel advice for your destination and look at the entry requirements section.
   - If it is a visa or permit enrolment, check the destination country’s official government, embassy, or consulate site by typing the address yourself or starting from a trusted official page.
   - If the request came from a message that pressures you to act immediately, asks for secrecy, asks for unusual payment, or asks for confidential information, stop and verify before sharing anything.
   - If the message, website, or call looks suspicious, use NCSC guidance on phishing and suspicious messages rather than replying to the sender.

5. If you are at the border and feel overwhelmed, ask for a safer pause.
   - Say: “I am not refusing. I am not prepared and I need clarification, an interpreter, or a moment to check my documents.”
   - Ask whether you can step aside to a waiting area while you read the instructions, gather documents, or contact your airline, ferry operator, rail operator, or travel organiser.
   - Keep your passport, ticket, visa, booking confirmation, and appointment notice together and visible.

6. If you cannot comply right now, ask for realistic next options.
   - Ask whether you can wait, use the correct kiosk or desk, attend a proper enrolment point, rebook, or return with the required paperwork.
   - If you are told you may be refused entry, ask what record they can provide, if any.
   - If no paperwork is provided, note the time, location, authority, staff name or badge number if visible, and the reason you were given.

7. Protect your identity and accounts.
   - Only provide biometric information at an official border point, official visa application centre, or clearly verified official route.
   - If you used a suspicious link, change the password on the email account connected to your travel documents.
   - If you paid a suspicious third party, contact your bank or card provider promptly and keep receipts, messages, and screenshots.

8. If you are detained, threatened, or cannot safely leave the immigration area, ask for consular help.
   - Ask to contact the nearest British embassy, high commission, consulate, or FCDO consular support.
   - Consular staff may be able to help you understand local processes, contact family, or raise welfare concerns.
   - They cannot force another country to admit you, stop deportation, or give legal advice.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, claim compensation, make a data request, or challenge the process.
- You do not need to understand the destination’s whole immigration system right now.
- You do not need to decide whether you will ever provide biometric information again.
- Focus only on whether this request is official, what is required today, and what options keep you safest.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to freeze when someone asks for biometric information at a border or before travel. Slowing down, checking the source, and asking for the next safe step can protect your data without turning the moment into a confrontation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Entry, visa, and biometric rules vary by destination, nationality, route, passport, visa status, and the authority dealing with you, so later decisions may need specialist immigration, legal, consular, travel, or data-protection help.

## Important note
This is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice, and it cannot replace instructions from official border or visa authorities in the place where you are.
