# What to do if… your blood sugar reading is suddenly very high and you feel sick (UK)

## Short answer
Treat a very high blood sugar reading with feeling sick as urgent. Check ketones if you can, follow your own sick-day plan if you have one, and get same-day medical advice now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to “exercise it down” when you feel sick or ketones may be present.
- Do not take a guessed extra insulin dose if you do not have a correction or sick-day plan you have already been given.
- Do not stop insulin, especially long-acting or basal insulin, unless a clinician tells you to.
- Do not ignore vomiting, severe sleepiness, confusion, fruity-smelling breath, or deep or rapid breathing.
- Do not drink sugary drinks for a high reading unless you are treating low blood sugar or following a clinician-given sick-day plan.
- Do not keep taking an SGLT2 inhibitor if you are vomiting, dehydrated, or have possible DKA symptoms without urgent medical advice.
- Do not drive yourself to urgent care if you feel faint, confused, very sleepy, or your vision is blurred.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency signs first.** Call **999** or go to **A&E** now if any of these apply:
   - Your blood ketones are **over 3 mmol/L**, or your urine ketones are **over 2+**.
   - You do not know your ketone level and you have DKA-type symptoms, such as being very thirsty, peeing a lot, feeling or being sick, fruity-smelling breath, deep or rapid breathing, unusual sleepiness, confusion, or blurred vision.
   - You cannot keep fluids down, you are getting more drowsy, or you are getting confused.
2. **Re-check the blood sugar reading if it is safe to do so.** Wash and dry your hands, use a fresh strip, and test again. If you use a CGM or flash monitor and the number does not match how you feel, confirm with a finger-prick test if you can.
3. **Check ketones as soon as possible.** This is especially important if you have type 1 diabetes, are pregnant, use insulin, or take an SGLT2 inhibitor. If you have type 1 diabetes and your glucose is above your target range, often around or above **14 mmol/L**, follow your own plan if it gives a different level.
4. **If ketones are present, rising, or you cannot check them, get urgent advice.** Contact your diabetes team, NHS 111, or your local urgent-care route. Use **999** or **A&E** if you have the emergency signs in Step 1.
5. **Follow your personal sick-day plan exactly if you have one.** Take insulin and diabetes medicines as prescribed, including any written correction guidance you have been given. If you do not have clear instructions, ask for urgent clinical advice rather than improvising.
6. **Sip fluids steadily.** Use sugar-free fluids such as water unless your own sick-day plan says otherwise. If you are vomiting, try small frequent sips; if you cannot keep fluids down, seek urgent medical help.
7. **Use the right urgent-care route for where you are.** In England, use NHS 111 online or call 111. In Scotland, call NHS 24 on 111. In Wales, use NHS 111 Wales. In Northern Ireland, use your GP out-of-hours service when your GP practice is closed. If you are worsening or have emergency signs, call 999 or go to A&E.
8. **If you take an SGLT2 inhibitor, mention it clearly.** If you have nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, deep or rapid breathing, unusual sleepiness, dehydration, or ketones, seek urgent assessment and tell the clinician you take an SGLT2 inhibitor, because ketones can be a concern even when glucose is not extremely high.
9. **Keep a simple note for clinicians.** Write down the times and results of blood sugar and ketone checks, vomiting or fever, missed doses, correction doses, diabetes medicines, and whether you use a pump.
10. **Ask someone to stay with you if possible.** If you need hospital care, bring your diabetes medicines, insulin, pump or CGM supplies, meter, strips, and ketone-testing kit if you can do so safely.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now, such as food, stress, infection, a missed dose, or a pump problem.
- You do not need to decide long-term medication changes today.
- You do not need to keep doing normal activities while you feel unwell.
- You do not need to make a perfect record; a short note of readings, ketones, symptoms, and medicines is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling sick with a very high reading is frightening, and taking it seriously is the right response. Checking ketones, avoiding guessed corrections, and getting help early can reduce the chance of things becoming more serious.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours only. Later decisions about doses, illness, pump settings, infection checks, or medication changes may need help from your diabetes team, GP, urgent-care clinician, or hospital team.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical advice. High blood sugar with illness can become an emergency. If you are worsening, pregnant, on an SGLT2 inhibitor, unable to check ketones, unable to keep fluids down, or unsure what to do next, seek urgent medical assessment.
