# What to do if… you are asked about organ or tissue donation and feel unprepared to decide (UK)

## Short answer
Ask for the Specialist Nurse for Organ Donation or donation team, say you feel unprepared, and ask them to explain what decision is needed now and what can wait.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree just to end the conversation if you do not understand what you are agreeing to.
- Don’t assume “organ donation” and “tissue donation” mean the same thing; ask which organs or tissues are being discussed.
- Don’t assume donation will automatically stop viewing, saying goodbye, or funeral arrangements; ask what applies in this case.
- Don’t let a family argument become the decision; focus on what your loved one recorded, said, believed, or would probably have wanted.
- Don’t give agreement you are too distressed to process; ask for a pause and a plain-language summary.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the right person and a calmer moment.**  
   Say: “I am not ready to decide yet. Please can the Specialist Nurse for Organ Donation or donation team speak with us, and can we have a few minutes to take this in first?”

2. **Ask which UK process applies.**  
   Organ donation law and wording differ across UK nations. Ask the nurse to explain which consent or authorisation wording applies in this situation and what role the family has in practice.

3. **Ask about any recorded decision or clear prior wishes.**  
   Ask whether the specialist nurse has checked the NHS Organ Donor Register for a recorded “yes”, “no”, nominated representative, or limits on donation. Tell the nurse about any clear conversations, written notes, faith or belief wishes, or strong views your loved one expressed.

4. **Separate the choices into plain words.**  
   Ask the nurse to write down or repeat slowly:
   - whether organ donation, tissue donation, or both are being considered
   - which organs or tissues are medically possible in this situation
   - whether cornea or eye tissue donation is being discussed, and what that means
   - whether your loved one recorded any limits
   - what supporting donation, not supporting donation, or not being able to support donation now would mean

5. **Ask the practical “will this change today?” questions.**  
   Ask directly:
   - when you can spend time with your loved one
   - whether viewing is still possible
   - whether donation affects the timing of release to the family
   - whether washing, dressing, handling, faith, cultural, or family wishes can be accommodated

6. **Ask early if a coroner or Procurator Fiscal may be involved.**  
   Say: “Is the death being reported to a coroner or Procurator Fiscal, and could that affect donation or timing?” Ask the hospital team who is responsible for that contact.

7. **If time feels pressured, ask for the actual time limit.**  
   Say: “I need a short pause to think clearly and make one call. What time limit is clinical, what time limit is practical, and what options remain if we cannot support donation right now?”

8. **If the family is split, return to your loved one’s likely wishes.**  
   Ask each person to share only what they know about your loved one’s views, values, beliefs, or recorded decision. Then ask the specialist nurse to help turn that information into the smallest necessary decision.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide funeral details, death registration, wills, accounts, notifications, or belongings as part of this donation conversation.
- You do not need to decide how you feel about donation in general.
- You do not need to contact recipients, plan follow-up support, or settle wider family disagreements right now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling unprepared is common. Being asked about donation can feel like another shock. It is acceptable to ask for time, ask basic questions, and ask for the same information more than once.

## Scope note
This is first steps only: slowing the conversation, finding any recorded decision, understanding the donation options, and making the smallest necessary choice without panic. Later questions about bereavement support, paperwork, faith needs, funeral planning, or family conflict can be handled after the immediate decision window.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. Donation decisions depend on the circumstances of the death, medical suitability, recorded wishes, family information, and the rules that apply in the relevant UK nation. Hospital staff and the specialist donation team can explain what applies in your situation.
