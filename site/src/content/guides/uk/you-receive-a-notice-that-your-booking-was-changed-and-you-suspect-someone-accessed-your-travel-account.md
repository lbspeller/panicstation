# What to do if… you receive a notice that your booking was changed and you suspect someone accessed your travel account (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the message as possible unauthorised access until you have verified it: use the official travel company app or website, secure the travel account and the email behind it, then contact the provider to freeze or protect the booking.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click “confirm change”, “verify”, or “log in” links in the message; go through the official app or website you normally use.
- Don’t assume it is only a glitch if names, dates, airports, rooms, seats, contact details, or payment details have changed.
- Don’t keep retrying passwords repeatedly if you are locked out; use the provider’s account recovery route instead.
- Don’t share booking references, passport details, screenshots, barcodes, QR codes, or PNRs in public posts or with unknown support accounts.
- Don’t wait until the day of travel if check-in, tickets, entry documents, or accommodation access could be affected.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the alert safely**
   - Open the airline, hotel, rail operator, ferry company, or online travel agent through its official app or by typing the website address yourself.
   - Check passenger names, dates, times, departure and arrival points, room or seat details, contact email, contact phone number, saved travellers, and payment method shown.
   - If the message says you must act urgently, treat that as a reason to verify through the official channel, not through the message link.

2. **Secure the travel account, if you can still access it**
   - Change the password to a new, long password you have not used anywhere else.
   - Turn on 2-step verification if the account offers it.
   - Sign out of all devices or sessions if the account has that option.
   - Remove any linked accounts, saved travellers, phone numbers, email addresses, or devices you do not recognise.
   - If you cannot sign in, use the provider’s official account recovery or fraud support route.

3. **Secure the email account used for the booking**
   - Change your email password and turn on 2-step verification.
   - Check mail forwarding, filters, rules, auto-archive settings, and “mark as read” rules; delete anything you did not set.
   - Review recent sign-ins and devices, then sign out of anything unfamiliar.

4. **Contact the travel provider through an official route**
   - Say: “I think there may be unauthorised access to my travel account or booking. Please help protect the booking and stop further online changes.”
   - Ask them to confirm the current itinerary, passenger names, contact details, payment method used, vouchers, credits, ticket numbers, and any recent changes.
   - Ask whether they can lock online changes, add a security note to the booking, or require extra checks before further changes.
   - If you booked through an online travel agent, contact both the agent and the airline, hotel, rail operator, or ferry company where possible.

5. **Check money and payment details**
   - Check the card, bank account, PayPal account, or wallet used for the booking for new or unfamiliar transactions.
   - If you see anything unauthorised, contact your bank, card provider, or payment provider immediately using known details from your card, banking app, official website, or 159.
   - Save screenshots of the changed itinerary, account alerts, receipts, and timestamps, but keep them private because they may contain usable travel or identity details.

6. **Protect the trip if travel is soon or check-in is open**
   - Prioritise correcting passenger names, dates, ticket status, check-in access, and any passport or ID details stored in the booking.
   - Add or restore a phone number and email address you control, and remove any you do not recognise.
   - If you are worried someone else could try to use the booking, ask the provider what identity checks apply and whether they can add an extra security note.

7. **Report fraud or cyber crime in the UK if it has happened**
   - If money was lost, personal details were misused, or a fraud or cyber crime took place, report it to Report Fraud if you live in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland.
   - If you live in Scotland, or the crime happened there, report it to Police Scotland on 101 for a non-emergency.
   - Keep any report reference number with your screenshots, bank notes, and provider case numbers.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to cancel the whole trip; first secure the account and confirm what the booking currently says.
- You do not need to change every password immediately; start with the travel account, the email account, and any account using the same password.
- You do not need to argue about refunds or liability in the first call; first ask the provider to stop further changes and confirm the live booking.
- You do not need to post online for help; keep booking details private while you stabilise the situation.

## Important reassurance
This is unsettling, especially when travel is involved. Working in a steady order — verify safely, secure access, contact the provider, then check payments — can limit further harm and give you clearer next steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise a suspected travel-account takeover or unauthorised booking change. Later decisions about insurance, complaints, refund disputes, identity protection, or legal issues may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, law-enforcement, cyber-security, or professional advice. Processes vary by provider and situation; use official support channels, your bank’s fraud team, and the relevant UK reporting route.
