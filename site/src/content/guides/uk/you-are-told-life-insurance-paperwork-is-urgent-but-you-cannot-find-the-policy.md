# What to do if… you are told life insurance paperwork is urgent but you cannot find the policy (UK)

## Short answer
Do not rush because someone says it is urgent. Ask for the deadline in writing, call back through a trusted route, and start the claim or policy-tracing process with the person’s details rather than waiting until you find the physical policy.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t give bank details, passwords, one-time passcodes, or full ID scans to someone who contacted you unexpectedly or is pressuring you.
- Don’t assume you must find the original policy before anyone can help.
- Don’t send a death certificate, ID, or financial documents until you have independently checked the insurer, postal address, email address, or upload route.
- Don’t pay a fee to “release” a payout or speed up paperwork unless you have independently confirmed the request with the insurer.
- Don’t throw away envelopes, letters, emails, bank statements, or direct debit information.

## What to do now
1. **Get the urgency put in writing.** Ask for the exact document they want, the stated deadline, what happens if it is late, the insurer or plan name, and any claim or case reference.

2. **End any pressured contact and call back safely.** If the person refuses to confirm details in writing, asks for money, or pushes you to act during the same call, end the contact. Use the insurer’s official website, a recent statement, or a known policy letter to find the number yourself.

3. **Ask the insurer to start with the details you have.** Say: “I cannot find the policy document. Can you record the claim or trace the policy using the person’s name, date of birth, last address, date of death, and my contact details?” Ask what documents are actually needed and whether there is any real deadline.

4. **Do a short paper-trail search for the insurer name.** Spend no more than 30–60 minutes checking:
   - bank and credit card statements for premiums, direct debits, or standing orders;
   - email searches for “policy”, “life”, “life cover”, “insurance”, “premium”, “renewal”, “beneficiary”, and “direct debit”;
   - physical files marked insurance, mortgage, pension, HR, benefits, will, or tax;
   - saved phone notes or contacts for a financial adviser, broker, or employer benefits provider.

5. **Check workplace or group life cover.** If the person was employed, recently employed, or in a workplace pension, contact HR, payroll, or the pension administrator and ask whether there was death in service benefit, group life assurance, or an employee benefits portal.

6. **Use a recognised tracing route if you still do not know the insurer.** The Association of British Insurers gives tracing guidance and says bank or credit card statements can help identify an insurer. Its FAQ for relatives also points to Gretel as a free-to-use online tracing option.

7. **Get the death certificate route moving, but do not turn this into a full estate task today.** Follow the rules for where the death happened and where the person lived. Tell Us Once can be used for someone who lived in England, Scotland or Wales, but not someone who lived in Northern Ireland. Ask the registrar how to get certified copies if insurers or other organisations ask for originals.

8. **Keep a simple record.** Write down who contacted you, the date and time, the number or email used, what they asked for, and what you sent. Keep copies or photos of anything posted or uploaded.

9. **If you may have been targeted by a scam, pause and protect the accounts first.** Contact the bank using the number on the card or statement if you gave payment details, made a payment, or shared sensitive information. You can also use the FCA’s scam guidance and reporting route for suspected financial services scams.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to use a solicitor, broker, or paid tracing service.
- You do not need to finish probate or organise the whole estate before asking an insurer to trace or record a claim.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements immediately.
- You do not need to send every document at once; first confirm exactly what is needed and where it should go.

## Important reassurance
Missing life insurance paperwork after a death is common. The safest first move is not to rush; it is to verify the request, use trusted contact routes, and begin with the details you already have.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce pressure, check whether the urgency is real, and start tracing or opening a claim. Later decisions about beneficiaries, trusts, probate, complaints, or disputed claims may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, professional, or bereavement advice. If the situation feels complex or unsafe, slow down, use trusted contact routes, and get specialist support before sending documents or money.
