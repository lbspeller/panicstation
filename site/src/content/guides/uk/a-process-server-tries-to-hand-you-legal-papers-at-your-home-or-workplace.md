# What to do if… a process server tries to hand you legal papers at your home or workplace (UK)

## Short answer
Take or keep the papers if it is safe, note the date and time, and check the first deadline using the court or tribunal details from an official source. Refusing or avoiding the papers may not stop the case or the deadline.

## Do not do these things
- Do not throw the papers away, hide them, or wait to see what happens.
- Do not pay money, give bank details, or agree to anything on the spot.
- Do not argue about the case with the person handing over the papers.
- Do not give a detailed explanation at the door, in reception, or in front of colleagues.
- Do not sign anything unless you understand exactly what the signature is for.
- Do not follow the person outside, into a car, or to a second location.
- Do not post photos of the papers online or send them to an unknown number.

## What to do now
1. **Keep the contact brief and safe.**  
   At home, speak through the door if that feels safer. At work, ask for a private room or a quiet area away from colleagues.

2. **Ask only what you need to identify the papers.**  
   Say: “What are these documents called, and which court or tribunal are they from?” Do not discuss whether the claim is right or wrong.

3. **Take the papers if it is safe to do so.**  
   Do not assume that refusing to touch them will stop service, the case, or any deadline. If the person leaves the papers nearby, keep them rather than leaving them behind.

4. **Record what happened.**  
   Write down the date, time, place, the name of the person if they gave one, who received the papers, and anything said about what the papers are.

5. **Preserve the papers exactly as received.**  
   Keep the envelope, cover sheet, staples, and every page. Take photos for your own record or for an adviser, especially the court or tribunal name, claim or case number, parties’ names, and any response deadline.

6. **Do a quick legitimacy check.**  
   Look for a court or tribunal name, a claim or case number, the names of the parties, a response pack or form, a hearing date, a solicitor’s details, and a clear deadline.

7. **Verify through an independent official route.**  
   If the papers name HMCTS, an England and Wales court, or an online money claim, use GOV.UK or the official Money Claims service rather than only the phone number on a cover sheet. If the papers name a Scottish, Northern Irish, tribunal, family, insolvency, or other court route, use the court or tribunal named on the papers and check its official contact details.

8. **Find the first thing that needs a response.**  
   You do not need to write a full defence now. Your immediate job is to identify what the document is, what the first deadline is, and where any response must be sent.

9. **Get help matched to the document.**  
   If it is about money or debt, contact a free debt advice service. If it is about family, housing, employment, an injunction, insolvency, or anything with a very short deadline, contact a solicitor, legal aid adviser, advice charity, or the court office promptly.

10. **If this happened at work, protect your privacy.**  
   Say only what is needed: “I have been handed legal documents and need a private few minutes to check a deadline.” You do not owe colleagues the details.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether you agree, defend, settle, counterclaim, or complain.
- You do not need to phone the claimant or their solicitor immediately.
- You do not need to explain the papers to family, colleagues, or your employer beyond what is needed for privacy and time.
- You do not need to read every page in detail before finding the court name, case number, and first deadline.
- You do not need to decide today what the final outcome should be.

## Important reassurance
Being handed legal papers can feel frightening and embarrassing, especially at home or work. It is usually a formal notification step, not a demand to solve everything immediately. The safest first move is to keep the papers, verify them, and protect the first deadline.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The right response depends on the exact papers, the court or tribunal named, the UK legal system involved, and the deadline shown, so later decisions may need specialist legal advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Court procedures, service rules, forms, and deadlines vary by document type and across the UK, so check the named court or tribunal and get advice quickly if you are unsure or the deadline is close.
