# What to do if… a person you care about is transferred to another detention facility with little notice (UK)

## Short answer
Write down the essential details, then use official prison or support routes to confirm where they are before you travel, send money, send property, or post documents.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t travel to a new prison or detention facility until you have checked they are there and whether visits are possible.
- Don’t send cash, property, urgent documents, or medication to an address you have not verified.
- Don’t rely on social media, rumours, or another family’s experience as proof of their location.
- Don’t post their full name, date of birth, prisoner number, case details, or location guesses online.
- Don’t keep calling random establishments without a record; use a simple call log with who you contacted, when, and what they said.
- Don’t treat a delay in a phone call as proof something has gone wrong; intake and phone access can take time after a move.

## What to do now
1. **Write a minimum-facts note.**  
   Include their full name, any other name used, date of birth or age, prisoner number if you have it, last known prison or detention facility, who told you about the transfer, the exact words used, any possible destination, and the time you heard it.

2. **Work out which UK prison system you are dealing with.**
   - **England and Wales:** if you do not know which prison they are in, use the official Find a Prisoner service. The person must give permission before their location can be shared. If you know the last prison, contact that prison and ask what they can confirm and what official route you should use next.
   - **Scotland:** use the Scottish Prison Service prison contact route. If your concern is about their mental health or wellbeing, call the prison where they are believed to be and use the dedicated concern route when prompted.
   - **Northern Ireland:** use Northern Ireland Prison Service or nidirect contact details to reach the correct prison service contact point.

3. **Use a family support route if you are stuck.**
   - For England and Wales, call the Prisoners’ Families Helpline on 0808 808 2003.
   - For Scotland or Northern Ireland, use the UK family-support information page to find the relevant support route for that part of the UK.

4. **If you have an immediate safety, self-harm, or wellbeing worry, say that clearly.**  
   Call the last known prison or the prison you believe they are being moved to. In England and Wales, ask for Safer Custody, the Safer Prisons team, the Safety team, or the Duty Governor or Orderly Officer if the risk feels immediate. In Scotland, use the Scottish Prison Service concern line for the prison and follow the prompt to raise the concern as urgent. In Northern Ireland, contact the prison or Northern Ireland Prison Service and say you are raising an urgent welfare concern.

5. **Keep the welfare message short and factual.**  
   Say who you are worried about, their prisoner number if known, where they were last held, what you have been told about the transfer, what risk you are worried about, and how prison staff can contact you. Do not add rumours or long background unless staff ask for it.

6. **Pause visits, money, property, and documents until the destination is confirmed.**  
   Once you know the receiving establishment, ask how mail should be addressed, whether any booked visit has been cancelled, how to re-book, what identification you need, and how money or property should be handled there.

7. **Expect contact to take time after arrival.**  
   In England and Wales, a first call after arrival can take up to 72 hours. After a transfer, family information services say contact is usually within 72 hours but can sometimes take up to 1 week. Ask the prison what is usual for that establishment rather than guessing.

8. **Tell their solicitor if they have one.**  
   Send the solicitor the minimum-facts note and say you have been told a transfer is happening or has happened. Ask whether they can confirm the new location or make sure court papers and urgent legal messages follow the person.

9. **Use a cautious letter fallback if you cannot confirm the location.**  
   Send a short, neutral letter to the last known prison with their full name, date of birth, and prisoner number if known, asking for it to be forwarded. This may help, but do not rely on it for urgent welfare information.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out why the transfer happened right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to complain right now unless there is a specific urgent welfare concern or you are completely blocked from raising one.
- You do not need to send money, property, documents, or new visit requests until the receiving establishment confirms the correct process.
- You do not need to tell everyone at once; one clear contact log is more useful than repeated calls and messages.

## Important reassurance
A sudden transfer can feel frightening, especially when information is partial. Transfers can be routine and fast-moving, and families often hear late. The most useful first steps are to confirm the location through official routes, keep a clear record, and raise any welfare concern plainly.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours or day after you hear about a transfer. Later decisions about visits, property, complaints, legal challenges, immigration detention, or custody arrangements may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Prison processes vary by establishment and by whether the person is held in England and Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland. If you cannot confirm something through an official route, assume the situation may still be changing and prioritise verified location and urgent welfare contact.
