# What to do if… a government agency contacts you demanding information and you are unsure what you must provide (UK)

## Short answer
Break the immediate contact and verify the request using an official number found independently on GOV.UK. Do not provide any sensitive data until you have confirmed the agency's identity and the specific legal power they are using to compel the information.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide passwords, PINs, or full bank details over the phone or via an emailed link.
- Do not click links in "urgent" emails or texts claiming to be from HMRC, the DWP, or the Home Office.
- Do not send original physical documents like passports or birth certificates without verified written instructions and a secure postal address.
- Do not feel pressured to answer questions immediately during an unexpected "voluntary" police or regulator interview.
- Do not ignore the contact entirely once you suspect it might be real; missing a statutory deadline can lead to penalties.

## What to do now
1. **End the live interaction immediately and safely.** If you are on a call, inform the caller you will call them back through their official switchboard and hang up. If you are at your door, ask for a staff ID and a formal letter of request, then close the door to verify.
2. **Verify the agency contact details independently.**
   - Search GOV.UK for the specific department (e.g., "HMRC contact" or "Companies House") and use the number listed there.
   - For Companies House identity verification, only use the official "Verify your identity" service via GOV.UK One Login.
3. **Report suspicious contact to the correct channels.**
   - Forward suspicious emails to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.
   - Forward suspicious HMRC-specific texts to **60599** (network charges may apply).
   - Forward all other suspicious texts to **7726** (free).
4. **Request the full details of the demand in writing.** Ask the agency to provide:
   - a specific list of the documents or data required,
   - the exact section of the Act or Law (legal basis) that requires you to provide it,
   - the deadline for compliance and the consequences of non-compliance.
5. **Exercise your right to silence and legal advice if contacted by police.**
   - If the police ask for a statement or an interview (including "voluntary" ones), you are not required to answer questions immediately.
   - Say: "I require legal advice before answering any questions."
   - If at a police station, ask for the **duty solicitor**; this is free and independent of the police.
6. **Follow the mandatory 2026 data complaint procedure if your data is mishandled.**
   - Under the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, you must first lodge a formal complaint with the organisation itself.
   - The organisation has 30 days to acknowledge your complaint.
   - You can only escalate to the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO) after you have completed the organisation's internal process.
7. **Keep a precise log of the interaction.** Record the time of contact, the name of the person you spoke to, the department they claimed to represent, and any reference numbers provided.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide any information during the first minute of contact.
- You do not need to decide if the request is "fair" or "legal" right now; that assessment happens after you have the written legal basis.
- You do not need to gather documents until the scope of the request is confirmed in writing.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard protective measure to verify government contact. Official departments are aware that impersonation scams are common and will expect legitimate citizens to verify their identity. Taking time to check does not make you look "guilty" or "uncooperative."

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for unexpected contact. It does not provide legal defense strategies or technical guidance for complex regulatory audits.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. If you are served with a formal warrant, a statutory notice with criminal penalties, or are under arrest, you should seek professional legal representation immediately.
