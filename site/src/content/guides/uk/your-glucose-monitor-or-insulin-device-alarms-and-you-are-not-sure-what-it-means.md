# What to do if… your glucose monitor or insulin device alarms and you are not sure what it means (UK)

## Short answer
Assume your blood glucose may be at an unsafe level or your insulin delivery has been interrupted until you can confirm your status with a finger-prick blood test. Treat any suspected low blood glucose (hypoglycaemia) immediately with 15g of fast-acting carbohydrate.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore or repeatedly silence an alarm without checking your physical symptoms and blood glucose.
- Don't take a correction dose of insulin based on a sensor reading you suspect is inaccurate.
- Don't drive, use tools, or perform hazardous tasks until you have confirmed your glucose is stable.
- Don't give food or drink to someone who is unconscious, having a seizure, or unable to swallow.
- Don't wait for "signal lost" or "sensor error" messages to resolve themselves if you feel unwell.

## What to do now
1. **Assess your physical symptoms immediately.** If you feel shaky, sweaty, hungry, dizzy, or confused, treat for a "hypo" (low blood glucose) now, even if you are unsure what the alarm means.
2. **Perform a finger-prick blood glucose check using a standard meter.** Use this result to make treatment decisions rather than the continuous glucose monitor (CGM) if the two readings conflict or if the device is alarming.
3. **Treat a reading below 4.0 mmol/L with 15g of fast-acting carbohydrate.**
   - Use 5–7 glucose tablets, 150ml of regular (non-diet) fizzy drink, or 4 large jelly babies.
   - Children may require smaller amounts (5g–10g) based on their specific weight-based plan.
4. **Wait 15 minutes and re-check your blood glucose with a finger-prick.** If it is still below 4.0 mmol/L, repeat the 15g of fast-acting carbohydrate and wait another 15 minutes.
5. **Call 999 immediately if someone is unconscious or having a seizure.**
   - **Place the person in the recovery position.**
   - **Administer emergency glucagon** if a kit is available and you have been trained to use it.
6. **Check for insulin delivery failure if using an insulin pump.** If the alarm indicates "Occlusion," "No Delivery," or "Pump Stopped," assume you are not receiving insulin.
   - Check the tubing for kinks, bubbles, or disconnections.
   - Check the infusion site for redness, wetness (leaking insulin), or the smell of insulin.
   - Follow your "back-up plan" (such as using an insulin pen) if the pump cannot be restarted immediately.
7. **Test for ketones if your glucose is above 11.0 mmol/L or you feel unwell.**
   - **Call 999 or go to A&E** if blood ketones are 3.0 mmol/L or higher, or if you are vomiting and cannot keep fluids down.
   - **Contact your diabetes team or NHS 111** if ketones are between 0.6 and 1.5 mmol/L and you feel unwell.
8. **Verify phone and app settings if the alarm is coming from a mobile device.** Check that Bluetooth is on, "Do Not Disturb" is off (or has exceptions), and the app has permission to send "Critical Alerts."
9. **Inform someone else that your device is alarming.** Stay with another person or call a contact to explain the situation until your glucose levels are confirmed as stable.

## What can wait
- Identifying the specific technical error code or "reason" for the alarm.
- Replacing a failing sensor or depleted reservoir (do this only after you are medically stable).
- Contacting the device manufacturer's technical support line for troubleshooting.
- Uploading data or reviewing long-term glucose trends.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel startled or overwhelmed when a medical device alarms, especially at night. You do not need to be a technical expert to stay safe; the priority is always checking your actual blood glucose and ensuring insulin is being delivered.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for unexpected diabetes technology alarms. It does not replace the specific clinical advice, sick-day rules, or pump settings provided by your NHS diabetes specialist team.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance during a device alert. If you are experiencing symptoms of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) or severe hypoglycaemia, or if you cannot manage your glucose levels safely, seek immediate medical assessment via NHS 111 or 999.
