# What to do if… a venue or funeral provider has paused your service due to conflicting instructions from relatives (UK)

## Short answer
Ask the venue to pause changes and put in writing what proof it needs before it will accept one person as the single contact. The single contact will usually be an executor named in a will, or the person generally entitled to deal with the estate if there is no will, but ask the venue what it will accept for your situation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confirm the date, cremation, burial, music, catering, payments, or wording just to calm the argument; some decisions may be hard to reverse.
- Do not let several relatives keep calling or emailing the venue with different instructions.
- Do not sign cremation, burial, venue, or service authorisation paperwork if you are unsure you are the person the venue can accept.
- Do not argue with staff or threaten them in the moment; ask for their requirements in writing instead.
- Do not post arrangements in group chats or on social media while the single contact is still disputed.
- Do not destroy, hide, or alter a will, funeral plan, written wishes, or venue paperwork.

## What to do now
1. **Ask the venue for a written pause and its single-contact rule.**  
   Email or call and ask: “Please pause any changes while we confirm who you can accept instructions from. Please email what documents or confirmation you need before you proceed.”

2. **Separate authority from preferences.**  
   Do not start with music, readings, seating, or who is upset. First identify who the venue can safely accept as the single contact. This is usually an executor named in the will, or, if there is no will, the person generally entitled to act as administrator, personal representative, or the equivalent person recognised for the estate in Scotland or Northern Ireland. The answer can depend on the facts and where in the UK the arrangements are being made.

3. **Do a focused check for the will, funeral plan, and named contacts.**  
   Check the person’s home paperwork, emails, safe place, bank papers, solicitor or will-writer details, and any prepaid funeral plan documents. If a will names more than one executor, ask the executors to agree one named person to deal with the venue, with the others copied into updates.

4. **Send the venue one written single-contact instruction.**  
   Include the deceased person’s full name, date of death, venue reference if known, the proposed single contact’s name, relationship, phone number and email address, and any document the venue has asked for. Use wording such as: “To prevent conflicting instructions, please treat [NAME] as the single contact for this service pending any written change the venue accepts.”

5. **Send one calm boundary message to relatives.**  
   Keep it short: “The venue has asked for one contact only so the service is not delayed. Please stop contacting the venue directly and send requests to [NAME]. Updates will be shared in one place.”

6. **Ask what can be held without becoming final.**  
   Ask the venue whether it can hold a provisional date, time, room, celebrant, or basic booking while authority is checked. Ask them to confirm clearly what is provisional and what would create a payment or cancellation obligation.

7. **Keep a simple record from this point.**  
   Save the venue’s written requirements, emails from relatives, any will or funeral plan evidence, and a short note of who contacted the venue and when. Do not turn this into a family argument file; keep only what helps the venue or a professional understand the immediate dispute.

8. **If the dispute is blocking the service, move it out of the venue quickly.**  
   Ask the venue what it will accept to proceed, such as written confirmation from the single contact, agreement from all executors, or a solicitor’s urgent note setting out who appears entitled to instruct. If the body, burial, cremation, or ashes may be dealt with against the disputed position, get urgent probate, confirmation, or estate-dispute advice before anything irreversible happens.

9. **If nobody is willing or able to arrange the funeral, ask the relevant local authority what happens next.**  
   This is a last-resort safety net and may reduce family choice. Use the council or local authority finder for the place where the person died or where the funeral is being arranged, and ask what their process is when no one is willing or able to arrange a funeral.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle readings, flowers, catering, seating plans, transport, obituary wording, or memorial details now.
- You do not need to resolve the wider family conflict today.
- You do not need to decide who was morally right; the urgent question is who the venue can accept instructions from.
- You do not need to make final spending choices for optional extras while the single contact and payment responsibility are unclear.
- You do not need to answer every relative immediately. One short holding message is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
This situation is stressful, but it is also common for venues to pause when they receive conflicting instructions. Creating one written route for decisions is not taking sides; it is a way to stop confusion, reduce pressure on staff, and protect the service from avoidable mistakes.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise communication and prevent irreversible decisions. Later decisions may need specialist probate, confirmation, estate, funeral, or dispute-resolution help, especially if relatives still disagree about burial, cremation, ashes, payment, or who is entitled to instruct.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. UK funeral, probate, confirmation, cremation, burial, local authority, and venue processes can vary by circumstances and location, so ask the venue or a suitable professional before signing anything if authority is unclear.
