# What to do if… you suspect a partner is tampering with your contraception or birth control (UK)

## Short answer
Prioritise your safety, privacy, and health. Use a safer phone or device if you can, then contact a sexual health clinic, local NHS sexual health service, GP, pharmacy, or specialist abuse helpline for confidential support.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront them while you are alone, trapped, dependent on them, or worried they may escalate.
- Do not leave contraception, condoms, pregnancy tests, clinic letters, or pharmacy items where they can reach, hide, damage, photograph, or monitor them.
- Do not assume you need proof before asking for medical care or specialist support.
- Do not use a phone, account, browser, cloud backup, location-sharing app, or shared tablet they may be able to see if you can avoid it.
- Do not make sudden phone, password, tracking, or account changes if that could alert them and put you at greater risk.
- Do not delay urgent sexual health care because you feel unsure about what happened.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause first.** If you are in immediate danger, call **999**. If calling is not safe, move towards a safer place if you can, such as a trusted person, a public place, a clinic, a pharmacy, or a room where you have a clear way out.
2. **Use safer communication.** If possible, use a phone or device they do not control, such as a trusted friend’s phone, work phone, library device, or clinic phone. If you must use your own device, assume it may be monitored and avoid searches, messages, or changes that could increase risk.
3. **Ask for confidential sexual health care.** Contact a **sexual health clinic**, **local NHS sexual health service**, **contraception clinic**, **GP**, or **pharmacy** and say: “I’m worried someone may have interfered with my contraception. I need confidential help.” You do not have to explain everything at once.
4. **Ask about emergency contraception if there is any recent pregnancy risk.** A clinician or pharmacist can talk through emergency contraception options and what is suitable for you. Ask where you can get care without information being sent somewhere unsafe for you.
5. **Ask about STI testing and urgent prevention options.** If condoms may have been removed, damaged, or interfered with, a sexual health clinic can advise on STI testing, pregnancy testing, and whether any urgent HIV prevention medicine may be relevant.
6. **Ask about a contraception method that is harder for a partner to interfere with.** You can ask about methods that do not rely on pills, condoms, packaging, or supplies kept at home. The clinic can help you think about privacy, safety, and follow-up.
7. **Reduce their access only if it is safe.** Keep contraception, condoms, medication, pregnancy tests, and paperwork somewhere they cannot reach, such as with a trusted person, at work, in a clinic-arranged place, or in a bag they do not inspect. Do not move items if that would obviously alert them.
8. **Contact specialist support without needing certainty.** For domestic abuse support, the National Domestic Abuse Helpline is **0808 2000 247**. For sexual violence support, use the service for where you live:
   - England and Wales: Rape Crisis England & Wales, **0808 500 2222**, 24 hours.
   - Scotland: Rape Crisis Scotland, **08088 01 03 02**, daily 5pm to midnight.
   - Northern Ireland: Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline, **0808 802 1414**, 24 hours.
9. **Make a brief private note only if it is safe.** If you may want a record later, write down what you noticed and when, somewhere they cannot access. Do not investigate, set traps, or put yourself at risk to collect evidence.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to leave the relationship.
- You do not need to decide now whether to report to police.
- You do not need to tell friends, family, work, or your partner before getting confidential help.
- You do not need to prove tampering before asking for emergency contraception, STI testing, pregnancy testing, or abuse support.
- You do not need a complete plan today; one safer contact or one clinic appointment is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling unsure, frightened, frozen, or hyper-alert can happen when someone may be interfering with your body, privacy, or choices. Acting quietly to protect your health and safety is reasonable, even if you are not certain.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation, protect your health, and help you reach specialist support. Later decisions about the relationship, housing, reporting, or legal options may need tailored help when you are safer.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice.
