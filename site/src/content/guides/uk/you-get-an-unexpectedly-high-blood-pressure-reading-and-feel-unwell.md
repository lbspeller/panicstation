# What to do if… you get an unexpectedly high blood pressure reading and feel unwell (UK)

## Short answer
Check for emergency symptoms like chest pain, sudden confusion, or stroke signs and call 999 if they occur. If you feel unwell and your reading is 180/120 mmHg or higher, or if you are pregnant/postpartum and the reading is 160/110 mmHg or higher, contact a clinician or NHS 111 for same-day assessment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take repeated readings every few seconds, as the stress of the number can cause a temporary rise.
- Do not take additional doses of blood pressure medication unless a doctor has given you a specific "rescue" dose plan.
- Do not engage in physical activity or exercise to "burn off" the blood pressure.
- Do not drive yourself to a hospital if you are experiencing chest pain, dizziness, or vision changes.
- Do not ignore symptoms like a sudden severe headache or vision loss even if the blood pressure number decreases on a second check.

## What to do now
1. **Immediately check for emergency signs and call 999 if any of the following occur:**
   - Sudden pain or heavy pressure in the chest.
   - Sudden weakness or numbness in the face, arm, or leg (especially on one side of the body).
   - Difficulty speaking or sudden confusion.
   - Sudden severe headache, unlike any previous headache.
   - Sudden loss of vision or severe blurring.
   - Severe shortness of breath or seizure.
2. **Sit in a quiet space and prepare for a re-check.** Sit upright with your back supported and both feet flat on the floor. Do not cross your legs. Rest for at least 5 minutes without talking or using a phone.
3. **Re-measure your blood pressure using a correct technique.**
   - Ensure the cuff is the correct size and placed on a bare upper arm, approximately 2cm above the elbow.
   - Support your arm on a table so the cuff is at heart level.
   - Take 2 readings at least 1 minute apart. Write down both the top (systolic) and bottom (diastolic) numbers.
4. **Contact NHS 111 or your GP immediately if the numbers remain very high.**
   - If you are not in immediate danger but your reading is consistently 180/120 mmHg or higher, you require a same-day medical assessment.
   - If you feel unwell (nausea, mild headache, or anxiety) with a reading significantly higher than your normal range, seek professional advice.
5. **Follow specific protocols if you are pregnant or have given birth in the last 6 weeks.**
   - Call your maternity unit, midwife, or 111 immediately if your blood pressure is 160/110 mmHg or higher.
   - Report symptoms such as a severe headache that does not go away with paracetamol, flickering lights in your vision, or sudden swelling of the face and hands.
6. **Take only your prescribed medications as scheduled.** If you have missed a dose, refer to the patient information leaflet or ask a pharmacist. Do not double the dose to try and force the number down quickly.
7. **Document your current status for the clinician.**
   - Note the exact readings, the times they were taken, and the symptoms you are feeling.
   - List any new medications, including over-the-counter cold remedies or supplements.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the underlying cause of the spike today.
- You do not need to decide on permanent medication changes or lifestyle overhauls immediately.
- You do not need to monitor your blood pressure every hour once you have been assessed or given a plan by a professional.

## Important reassurance
Blood pressure is dynamic and can spike due to pain, temporary stress, caffeine, or an incorrectly fitted cuff. A single high reading is a prompt for assessment, not a guarantee of a long-term problem. Medical professionals have established protocols to manage these spikes safely.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions for acute blood pressure spikes. It does not replace clinical diagnosis or long-term management of hypertension.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance. If you feel seriously unwell or are worried about your symptoms, your physical state is more important than the specific numbers on the monitor; seek medical help immediately.
