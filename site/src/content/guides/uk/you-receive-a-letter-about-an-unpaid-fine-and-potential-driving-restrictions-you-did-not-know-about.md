# What to do if… you receive a letter about an unpaid fine and potential driving restrictions you did not know about (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay through the letter, click its links, scan its QR code, or call its number until you have checked it through an official route. Check your driving record today, and if the notice is real, contact the court, enforcement office, council, DVLA, or DVA using contact details you found independently.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click payment links, scan QR codes, or call the number on the letter until you have verified it independently.
- Do not assume it is definitely a scam or definitely real because it looks official.
- Do not keep driving if your official record shows a disqualification, or if you cannot yet confirm whether you are allowed to drive.
- Do not admit the offence or agree to a repayment plan on the spot if you do not know what the fine relates to.
- Do not ignore the act-by date while you verify the letter.

## What to do now
1. **Put the letter somewhere safe and record the key details.** Photograph or scan it. Note the sender name, reference numbers, alleged offence or fine, dates, act-by date, payment instructions, and whether it mentions DVLA, DVA, HMCTS, a magistrates’ court, a Scottish court, a Northern Ireland court, a council, or an enforcement office.

2. **Verify the letter using official routes you find yourself.** Do not use the letter’s links or phone number until it has been checked. Use GOV.UK, nidirect, the Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service, or the council’s official website as appropriate for the part of the UK named on the letter. Quote the reference number and ask what the notice relates to.

3. **Check your official driving record today.** If your licence was issued in England, Wales or Scotland, use the GOV.UK driving record service to check penalty points and disqualifications. If your licence was issued in Northern Ireland, use the nidirect NI driving record service.

4. **If the letter says you already have a driving ban or disqualification, make a no-driving plan now.** Do not drive unless the official record or the official authority confirms you are allowed to. Arrange a lift, public transport, taxi, or delivery for anything urgent.

5. **If it is a magistrates’ court or HMCTS case you did not know about, ask about reopening it.** Say clearly: “I did not know about this case until I received this letter.” Ask whether you need to apply to reopen the case, make a statutory declaration, attend a hearing, or use another route. Ask about timing immediately, because these routes can be time-sensitive.

6. **If the letter is from Scotland or Northern Ireland, ask the named court or enforcement office what the correct process is there.** Say that you did not know about the fine or case until this letter arrived. Ask whether enforcement can be paused while they check the record and tell you the correct next step.

7. **If it is a court fine, ask whether enforcement can be put on hold while you verify and use the correct process.** Ask the court or enforcement office what they can do now. Write down the date and time, the name of the person you spoke to, the number you called, and exactly what they said.

8. **If you think your details were used by someone else, say this plainly and factually.** Tell the court or authority that you believe your details may have been used falsely or without your knowledge. Ask what evidence they need to check identity, address history, vehicle ownership, or keeper details.

9. **Fix any address problem through official services.** If you moved or your post is unreliable, update your driving licence address and, if relevant, your vehicle log book address through the official service so future notices reach you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you will contest the underlying allegation; first confirm who issued the notice, what it is for, and whether it is linked to you.
- You do not need to write a long explanation immediately; short, factual verification comes first.
- You do not need to pay just to stop the panic until you have checked the notice is genuine and correctly linked to you.

## Important reassurance
A letter like this can feel frightening, especially if it is the first you have heard of the fine. The safest first move is to slow the situation down: verify it independently, check whether you are currently allowed to drive, and get onto the official process.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: verify the notice, check your driving status, reduce the risk of escalation, and find the correct route. If it is a real court matter or you may be disqualified, later decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal advice. Processes can vary depending on where in the UK the fine or case came from, what type of fine it is, and whether a court, council, DVLA, DVA, or enforcement office is involved.
