# What to do if… you have a fever after starting an immune-suppressing medication (UK)

## Short answer
Call your prescribing or specialist team now, using any urgent or out-of-hours number you were given. If you cannot reach them quickly, use the urgent medical service where you are: 111 in England, Scotland or Wales, or GP out-of-hours in Northern Ireland.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait overnight to see if it settles without getting urgent medical advice.
- Do not keep treating the temperature at home instead of contacting your clinical team or an urgent medical service.
- Do not suddenly stop an immune-suppressing medicine unless a clinician tells you to; some medicines, including steroids, can be unsafe to stop abruptly.
- Do not rely on a pharmacy or routine appointment as your main plan for fever while immunosuppressed.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E or an emergency department if you feel faint, confused, very weak, or too unwell to drive safely.

## What to do now
1. Check and record your temperature.
   Use a thermometer if you have one. Write down the reading, the time, and whether it was taken by mouth, ear, or underarm.

2. Make the urgent call.
   Call the urgent advice number for the team that prescribed the medicine, such as rheumatology, dermatology, gastroenterology, oncology, haematology, respiratory, renal, or transplant. If you do not have a number or cannot get through quickly, use the urgent medical service where you are.

3. Use clear words at the start of the call.
   Say: “I started an immune-suppressing medication on [date]. I now have a fever of [temperature] and I feel [brief symptoms].” If you have been told you are at risk of neutropenia or neutropenic sepsis, say that immediately.

4. Escalate to 999, A&E, or an emergency department now if danger signs are present.
   Get emergency help now if you are confused, very drowsy or hard to wake, breathless, breathing very fast, have chest pain, have blue, grey, pale, blotchy, or mottled skin or lips, have a rash that does not fade when pressed with a glass, have collapsed or fainted, are passing very little urine, or are getting rapidly worse.

5. Put your medicine information in one place.
   Have ready the medicine name, dose, start date, any recent dose changes, any steroid use, allergies, other regular medicines, recent blood test results if you have them, and your temperature readings. Do not delay urgent help to find paperwork.

6. Reduce avoidable infection spread while you arrange care.
   Stay away from crowded places if you can. Avoid close contact with people who are at higher risk from infections. Wash or sanitise your hands before leaving for urgent care.

7. If you are told to attend A&E, an emergency department, or an urgent assessment unit, go promptly.
   At reception, say: “I am on immune-suppressing medication and I have a fever. I need urgent assessment for possible serious infection.” If you are on cancer treatment, have a transplant history, or have been warned about neutropenic sepsis, say that at once.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause of the fever right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to continue or stop the medicine long term.
- You do not need to research every possible infection or side effect before calling.
- Work messages, routine admin, and non-urgent plans can wait until you know whether you need assessment.

## Important reassurance
You are not overreacting by asking for urgent advice. Fever soon after starting an immune-suppressing medicine is exactly the kind of situation where early triage is safer than trying to judge it alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about tests, antibiotics, hospital assessment, or changing the medicine may need advice from your prescribing team, GP, an urgent medical service, urgent care, or emergency services.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. It is not a diagnosis and it does not replace urgent clinical assessment.
