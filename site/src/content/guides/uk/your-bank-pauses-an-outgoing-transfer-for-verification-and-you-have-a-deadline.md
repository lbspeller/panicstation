# What to do if… your bank pauses an outgoing transfer for verification and you have a deadline (UK)
## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately using a trusted route, ask what verification is needed, and contact the recipient straight away to warn them that the payment is delayed by bank checks.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay anyone who says they can “unlock”, “release”, or “speed up” the transfer.
- Do not share one-time passcodes, card PINs, full passwords, or anything that authorises a payment.
- Do not let anyone take remote control of your phone or computer.
- Do not keep sending the same transfer again and again; it can create duplicates or trigger more checks.
- Do not switch to crypto, gift cards, cash to a stranger, or another risky workaround because you feel rushed.
- Do not accept last-minute new bank details unless you independently confirm them through a contact route you already trust.

## What to do now
1. Write down the essentials before you call: amount, recipient name, sort code and account number or IBAN, payment reference, exact deadline, and the wording shown in your banking app.
2. Contact your bank through the number on your card, secure in-app chat, or the official website. Do not use a number or link from a text, email, or unexpected call.
3. Ask to speak to the team handling the payment hold, verification check, or fraud review. Say clearly: “I have an outgoing transfer paused for verification and a deadline. What do you need from me to review it safely?”
4. Ask specific questions: whether the hold is a fraud check, a payee-detail mismatch, a compliance check, or something else; what evidence or confirmation they need; whether verification can be completed while you are on the call; and what reference number you should quote if you call back.
5. Ask the bank not to release the payment until you are satisfied the recipient details are correct. If the bank asks you to confirm a warning or payee mismatch, pause and independently re-check the recipient’s details first.
6. Ask for written confirmation in the secure banking channel if available. If they cannot provide it immediately, record the date, time, contact route, adviser name or ID, case reference, and the next step they gave you.
7. Contact the recipient using a known, trusted route. Say: “My bank has paused the transfer for verification. I have contacted them and I’m working through the checks now.” Ask whether they can note the delay, extend the deadline, accept a partial payment, or use an alternative payment method they already officially accept.
8. If the deadline is with an official body, use its published contact route now. Ask what reference they need, whether they can note that payment was attempted, and what evidence they can accept if the bank delay causes lateness.
9. If you suspect a scam, wrong bank details, impersonation, or pressure tactics, tell the bank you want the transfer cancelled or kept on hold while you verify. Re-check the recipient’s details using a number, email, portal, or paperwork you already know is genuine.
10. If the bank cannot resolve it and the delay may cause loss, make clear that you are making a formal complaint about a payment service. Ask for a complaint reference. For many payment-service complaints, firms normally have 15 business days to send a final response, or up to 35 business days in exceptional circumstances if they explain the delay.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to switch banks.
- You do not need to make a detailed legal argument while the payment status is still unclear.
- You do not need to argue about compensation before you have stabilised the deadline and recorded what happened.
- You do not need to work out exactly why the bank’s systems flagged the transfer; focus on safe verification, the recipient, and records.

## Important reassurance
A paused transfer does not automatically mean you did anything wrong. Banks can hold some payments for fraud, payee-detail, or financial-crime checks, including before a Faster Payment is sent, so the safest next move is to verify through official channels and buy time with the recipient.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation, reduce the risk of a scam or duplicate payment, and protect your deadline. Later decisions, including a formal complaint, Ombudsman referral, compensation request, or legal step, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, professional, or regulated advice. Bank processes, cut-off times, transfer types, and complaint routes vary. If you think your account is compromised, prioritise account security through your bank’s official fraud route.
