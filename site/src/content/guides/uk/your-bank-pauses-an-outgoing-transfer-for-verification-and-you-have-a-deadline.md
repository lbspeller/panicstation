# What to do if… your bank pauses an outgoing transfer for verification and you have a deadline (UK)

## Short answer
Call your bank’s fraud or payments team immediately using the number on the back of your card to complete the verification, then notify the recipient that the payment is in progress but delayed by banking security protocols.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay any fee or "release charge" to an individual claiming they can speed up the process.
- Don’t share your full PIN, full password, or any one-time passcode (OTP) with anyone, even if they claim to be from the bank.
- Don’t attempt to send the same payment a second time, as this frequently triggers additional automated fraud flags and may result in a double debit.
- Don’t move to a different, unverified payment method (such as cryptocurrency or gift cards) to bypass the hold.
- Don’t follow links in text messages or emails that claim your transfer is blocked; these are often phishing attempts.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact status of the payment in your banking app or online portal.** Look for specific labels such as "Pending," "Under Review," "Awaiting Authorization," or "Paused."
2. **Call your bank using only the official number found on the back of your physical debit/credit card.** If you do not have the card, use the "Contact Us" section within your authenticated mobile app. 
3. **Ask specifically for the "Payments Verification" or "Fraud Operations" department.** Front-line customer service agents often cannot release held funds and may only be able to see that a hold exists.
4. **Provide the specific transaction details to the agent.** 
   - The exact amount and currency.
   - The recipient's name and the reference used.
   - The purpose of the payment (e.g., house deposit, invoice, or family transfer).
5. **Ask the bank for a timeframe for the release or a "case reference number."** If the agent confirms the check is complete, ask them to stay on the line until the status changes to "Sent" or "Completed" in your app.
6. **Notify the recipient or the organization expecting the funds immediately.** Provide them with the bank’s case reference number if available. 
   - Ask for a "grace period" of 24 hours.
   - Ask if they can accept a screenshot of the "Pending" status as evidence of your attempt to pay.
7. **Request a "Payment Services Complaint" be logged if the bank cannot provide a release time.** Under FCA rules (DISP 1.6.2A), firms must generally respond to payment service complaints within 15 business days. Formally naming your contact as a "complaint" triggers stricter internal monitoring.
8. **Check your "Secure Messages" or "In-app Notifications" for a missed verification request.** Some banks require you to "Accept" a warning or confirm a "Payee Match" (Confirmation of Payee) before the transfer moves from a paused state.

## What can wait
- You do not need to research new bank accounts or switching services right now.
- You do not need to calculate potential interest losses or compensation claims until the funds have actually moved.
- You do not need to determine why the automated system flagged this specific payment.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for banks to pause payments that deviate from your usual spending patterns, especially for new payees or large amounts. This is a standard security protocol designed to prevent APP (Authorised Push Payment) fraud. A hold is a procedural check and does not mean your account is being closed or that you have committed an error.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a delayed outgoing transfer. It does not cover situations where your account has been fully frozen (no access to any funds) or where you have already fallen victim to a scam.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute financial or legal advice. Under UK law, banks have a duty to monitor transactions for fraud and money laundering. In some cases, banks are legally prohibited from telling you exactly why a transfer is held if it relates to specific regulatory "tipping-off" rules. If you suspect you are being scammed, stop the payment immediately and report it to Action Fraud.
