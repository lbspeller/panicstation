# What to do if… a stranger stands too close while you enter a PIN or passcode (UK)

## Short answer
Stop entering the passcode or PIN. Cancel the transaction if you can, keep hold of your card or phone, and move to a safer, staffed, or busier place.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep typing just to get it over with while someone is crowding you.
- Do not argue, chase, or physically push them if you can step away safely.
- Do not accept help from a stranger with a cash machine, card reader, keypad, or phone screen.
- Do not let questions, tapping, or a story distract you while your card or phone is out.
- Do not rely on rumours or tricks instead of stopping the transaction and moving away.
- Do not follow the person somewhere quieter to sort it out.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the entry immediately.** Take your hands off the keypad or screen. If you are at a cash machine, card reader, or payment terminal, press **Cancel** or end the transaction if you can do that without staying exposed.
2. **Keep hold of your card or phone.** Put it away before you move if you can do so quickly. Do not hand it to anyone who says they are helping.
3. **Break the position behind you.** Step to the side or away from the machine or terminal so the person cannot stay directly behind you. Move toward staff, security, a counter, a queue with space, or a busier well-lit area.
4. **Use a short, clear sentence with staff.** Say: “Someone is standing too close while I enter my PIN. Can you help me finish this with space?”
5. **Do not restart in the same setup.** If you still need to pay or withdraw cash, use a different till, terminal, or cash machine if possible. Stand close to the keypad and shield the keypad or screen with your body and free hand.
6. **If the person follows or crowds you again, stop again and leave that spot.** Treat repeated crowding as enough reason to abandon the transaction for now.
7. **If you are at a cash machine, only check it from a safe pause.** If anything around the card slot, cash slot, keypad, or screen looks loose, added-on, damaged, or unusual, do not use it. Move away and tell the bank or police when safe.
8. **If you think the PIN or passcode was seen, treat it as compromised.** Once you are somewhere safer, use your bank’s app or the phone number on your card or statement to freeze or lock the card and ask how to reset the PIN or replace the card.
9. **If your phone passcode may have been seen, change it once you are safe.** Use a new code the person could not guess, and check that your phone, wallet, and card are still with you.
10. **If you feel threatened, go to a staffed place and contact police.** Call **999** if someone is in immediate danger, violence is being threatened, or a crime is happening now. Use **101** for non-emergency police contact.
11. **If money is taken or you see an unauthorised transaction, contact your bank immediately.** If you live in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, you can report fraud through Report Fraud. If you are in Scotland, report to Police Scotland via 101 or their official reporting routes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this was definitely criminal or probably nothing.
- You do not need to confront the person, follow them, or prove what they were doing.
- You do not need to solve every account setting immediately. First get to safety, freeze or lock the affected card if needed, and contact your bank.
- You do not need to complete that payment or cash withdrawal at that exact machine or terminal.

## Important reassurance
Stopping is a reasonable response. People do sometimes watch PINs or use distraction around cash machines and card payments, so creating space and pausing the transaction is sensible, not rude or paranoid.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes. Later decisions, such as fraud reporting, bank complaints, or replacing cards, may need help from your bank, police, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel at risk right now, prioritise moving to a safer place and contacting emergency services.
