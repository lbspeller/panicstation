# What to do if… someone you do not trust asks you to meet privately and you feel pressured to agree (UK)

## Short answer
You are never required to agree to a private meeting if you feel unsafe or pressured. Create immediate distance by declining the request in writing, informing a trusted contact of the situation, and setting clear boundaries that you will only communicate through traceable channels.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree to a meeting simply to stop the person from asking or to avoid a confrontation.
- Don’t meet the person in any private setting, including a car, their home, your home, or a quiet area of a workplace.
- Don’t allow the other person to provide your transport or choose a location that you cannot easily leave independently.
- Don’t provide your current location, home address, or travel plans as a way to "prove" you are busy.
- Don’t engage in long phone calls where you feel cornered; do not feel obligated to justify your refusal.
- Don’t delete any evidence of the pressure, such as persistent messages, call logs, or implied threats.

## What to do now
1. **Send a single, clear message that declines the private meeting and ends the negotiation.**  
   Use a direct statement such as: "I am not available to meet privately. If you have something to discuss, please put it in an email or text."
2. **Move all further communication to written formats and stop answering calls from them.**  
   If they continue to call, let it go to voicemail and only respond via text or email to reiterate your boundary.
3. **Notify a trusted friend, family member, or colleague about the pressure you are experiencing.**  
   **Tell them exactly who is asking, what has been said, and why you feel uncomfortable. Send them screenshots of the requests so they have a record of the pattern.**
4. **Remove yourself from the immediate environment if the person is physically nearby.**  
   Move to a populated area, stay with a colleague, or go to a public place where you are not alone while you manage the situation.
5. **If you choose to communicate at all, insist on a professional or supervised channel.**  
   - **Workplace:** Request that a manager, HR representative, or safeguarding lead is present.  
   - **Education:** Contact student services or a tutor to act as a mediator.  
   - **General:** Use a public service or formal office where there are witnesses.
6. **Use a "check-in" system if the person knows where you are.**  
   Tell a trusted person your location and set a time for a "safety call." If you do not check in by that time, they should take pre-agreed action.
7. **Contact the police if you feel you are in immediate or escalating danger.**  
   - **Emergency:** Call **999**. If you cannot speak, use the "Silent Solution" by pressing **55** when prompted by the operator.  
   - **Non-emergency:** Call **101** (this service is free) or report the harassment through your local police force’s website.
8. **Start a factual log of every interaction and request.**  
   Note the date, time, platform used (e.g., WhatsApp, email), and exactly what was said or asked. Save all digital copies securely.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today if you want to report this person formally to an employer or the police, unless you feel at immediate risk.
- You do not need to find the "perfect" way to explain yourself; "No" is a complete and sufficient answer.
- You do not need to worry about being "polite" or "rude" when your personal safety is the priority.
- You do not need to plan for how you will handle this person next week; focus only on staying safe and distant right now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressured or "gut feelings" about a meeting are valid indicators of risk. You have the absolute right to change your mind, revoke a previous agreement, or refuse to meet anyone without having to provide a "good enough" reason to them.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for avoiding a pressured private meeting. It does not cover long-term legal injunctions, stalking protection orders, or formal grievance procedures, which should be explored with specialists once you are safe.

## Important note
This guide is for general information and does not constitute legal or professional safety advice. If you are being harassed, threatened, or followed, contact the police or a specialist support agency like the National Stalking Helpline.
