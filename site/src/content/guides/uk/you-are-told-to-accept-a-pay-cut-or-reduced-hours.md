# What to do if… you are told to accept a pay cut or reduced hours (UK)

## Short answer
Do not agree on the spot. Ask for the proposal in writing, including when it starts and whether it is temporary, then take time to check your contract and get advice before you respond.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a new contract or variation letter if you do not understand it or do not agree.
- Do not rely on a verbal promise like “we’ll put it back later” unless it is written down with dates and conditions.
- Do not stop turning up for shifts or walk out in anger; that can create bigger problems fast.
- Do not assume they can cut pay or hours immediately because the business is struggling; you usually need clarity on what your contract allows and what you have agreed.
- Do not do unpaid work to prove yourself if your hours are cut; keep boundaries clear and track what you do.
- Do not keep working under changed terms without putting your objection in writing if the change has already been imposed.

## What to do now
1. **Get the request in writing today.** Ask for the new pay rate or hours, the start date, whether it is temporary, the review date, how rotas will work, and what happens to overtime, bonuses, commission, pension contributions, and any benefits.
2. **Check your contract and any staff handbook for the exact wording.** Look specifically for:
   - any clause about changing terms, sometimes called a variation clause
   - any clause about lay-off or short-time working
   - your normal or guaranteed hours
   - how changes must be agreed or confirmed
3. **Reply in writing with a holding response.** For example: “I’ve received your proposal. I’m not agreeing to any change yet. I’m reviewing it and will respond after I’ve checked the details.”
4. **Ask what the employer says happens if you do not agree.** You are not arguing; you are getting clarity. Ask whether they plan to consult, reduce shifts, start redundancy consultation, or consider dismissal and re-engagement.
5. **If they have already reduced your pay or hours without agreement, object in writing before simply carrying on under the changed terms.** You can say: “I do not agree to this change and I am working under protest while I take advice.” If the disagreement continues, repeat the objection regularly in writing, such as when a reduced payslip arrives.
6. **Document the change immediately.** Save payslips, rotas, timesheets, messages, letters, and the date you first noticed the change. Keep a simple timeline of meetings, calls, and what was said.
7. **If the change is being imposed, consider using your employer’s grievance route.** Keep the wording calm and specific: what changed, when it changed, that you do not agree, and what you want clarified or corrected.
8. **Check your pay still meets the legal minimum for the hours you work.** This means the National Minimum Wage or National Living Wage rules, depending on your age and apprenticeship status. If you think you may fall below the minimum, flag it in writing.
9. **Get quick, specialist help.** If you are in a union, contact your rep. If not, contact Acas for guidance on contract changes and your options. If money is urgent, also check whether you may be able to claim support if your hours drop, such as Universal Credit.
10. **If the employer is calling it short-time working or lay-off, ask what pay you will get and how long it is expected to last.** What is allowed and what you are entitled to can depend on your contract, your employment status, and the situation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to accept permanently; you can ask for a short deadline extension to review the proposal and get advice.
- You do not need to threaten resignation, a formal legal claim, or going public right now; first get the facts in writing and protect your record.
- You do not need to negotiate the perfect deal today; the priority is avoiding accidental agreement and preventing a silent, undocumented pay cut.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel cornered and panicky when your income is threatened. Taking a short pause to get the proposal in writing and respond carefully is a protective step, not being difficult.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance to help you avoid irreversible mistakes and stabilise the situation. The right next move can depend on your contract wording, your employment status, and what your employer does if you refuse.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Employment situations can change quickly and time limits may apply for some actions, so getting tailored advice early is usually safer.
