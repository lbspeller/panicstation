# What to do if… you are told to accept a pay cut or reduced hours (UK)

## Short answer
Request the new payment date and net amount in writing immediately, and ask for an emergency Faster Payment today if the delay puts you at risk of missing essential bills.

## Do not do these things
- Do not accept a verbal promise that it will be "sorted" without a specific date and time.
- Do not stop attending work in protest without checking how it affects your contract or right to be paid.
- Do not agree to be paid via unofficial methods that bypass your tax record or payslip.
- Do not assume your employer will automatically cover your bank's late fees or overdraft charges without a written request.
- Do not provide your bank login details to anyone claiming they need them to "push through" a failed payment.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact amount you are owed and the original agreed payment date.** Refer to your written statement of employment or contract to confirm when the money was legally due. Note down any missing overtime, bonuses, or shifts that were expected in this period.
2. **Obtain written confirmation of the error and the new payment schedule from HR or payroll.** Send an email or message asking for:
   - the specific reason for the failure
   - the exact date and time the payment will be re-issued
   - the payment method being used (e.g., BACS or Faster Payments)
3. **Ask for an immediate emergency payment via Faster Payments if you cannot cover essentials.** Explain clearly that you require a specific sum today to cover rent, food, or travel. Most UK banks support Faster Payments (FPS) which allow employers to send up to £1 million near-instantly, bypassing the standard 3-day BACS cycle.
4. **Notify your bank and any companies with upcoming direct debits about the delay.** Inform your landlord or utility providers before a payment fails to avoid late-payment markers on your credit file. Keep a record of these communications to support any future claims for reimbursement of fees.
5. **Request that the employer covers any financial penalties caused by their error.** Document any overdraft fees, late payment charges, or interest incurred because your wages were not available. Ask in writing for the company to reimburse these specific out-of-pocket losses.
6. **Verify your itemised payslip as soon as it is issued.** In the UK, you must receive a payslip at or before the time you are paid. Ensure the "pay date" matches the actual date received and check that any "emergency advances" have been recorded correctly so they are not deducted twice later.
7. **Escalate to the Fair Work Agency if the delay results in you being paid below the National Minimum Wage.** From April 2026, the Fair Work Agency (FWA) is the unified regulator for minimum wage enforcement. If the failure means your average hourly rate for the pay period drops below the legal minimum, you can report this to the FWA.
8. **Raise a formal grievance if the payment is still not received by the second promised date.** Follow your company's internal grievance procedure in writing. This creates a formal evidence trail required for any potential legal escalation or tribunal claim.
9. **Observe the strict 3-month minus 1 day time limit for unpaid wage claims.** If you need to take legal action for "unlawful deduction from wages," you must usually start Acas Early Conciliation within this window. 
   - Note: This limit is scheduled to increase to 6 months in October 2026.
10. **Check for signs of company insolvency if the payroll failure is widespread.** If the employer cannot provide a clear date or mentions "cash flow," gather copies of your contract, recent payslips, and P60. If the company enters administration, you may need to claim through the Redundancy Payments Service.

## What can wait
- You are not required to decide today whether to resign or take the matter to a tribunal.
- You do not need to perform a full audit of your previous year's pay tonight; focus only on the current missing payment.
- You do not need to identify the technical cause of the payroll software failure; that is the employer's responsibility.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel significant stress when a steady income is interrupted. Requesting an emergency payment or written confirmation of a new date is a standard professional response to a breach of contract and does not make you a "difficult" employee.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate payroll failure. If the delay persists, if you are being pressured to work for free, or if the employer is entering insolvency, you will require specialist legal or union advice.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. Employment rights vary based on your specific contract and worker status. If the payroll delay causes you to fall into urgent debt or risk eviction, contact Citizens Advice or your union representative immediately. Ensure all promises of payment are kept in a personal record outside of your work email.
