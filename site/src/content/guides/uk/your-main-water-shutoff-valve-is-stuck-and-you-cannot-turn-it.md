# What to do if… your main water shutoff valve is stuck and you cannot turn it (UK)

## Short answer
If water is spreading, avoid wet electrics first, contain the leak, and try to shut the water off by another safe route instead of forcing the stuck valve.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t force the valve with extreme leverage or tools. If it will not move with normal hand pressure and a small gentle back-and-forth, stop.
- Don’t keep turning harder and harder; a snapped valve or pipe can turn a leak into a bigger flood.
- Don’t touch switches, sockets, plugs, appliances, or your consumer unit if you are standing in water or anything electrical is wet.
- Don’t use mains-powered appliances in the wet area.
- Don’t try to operate an outside stop tap if it is in the road or pavement and you cannot do it safely.
- Don’t leave an outside stop tap cover open where someone could trip or a vehicle could hit it.
- Don’t restart a boiler, appliance, or electrical circuit that may have got wet until it has been checked.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause and reduce electrical risk.**  
   Keep people and pets away from the water path. If you can reach the consumer unit without stepping in water and it is dry and safe, switch off the main switch. If you cannot do that safely, keep away from electrics and focus on stopping or containing the water.

2. **Contain and divert the water.**  
   Put buckets, bowls, towels, trays, or plastic sheeting under the leak. Move rugs, valuables, documents, and anything electrical up and away from the water.

3. **Try the stuck stopcock or stop tap gently once.**  
   Use a dry cloth for grip. Turn slowly clockwise. If it will not move, try only a small gentle back-and-forth. If it still will not move, stop.

4. **Look for a local isolation valve near the leaking item.**  
   If the leak is from a toilet, basin, sink, washing machine, dishwasher, or appliance feed, look on the small supply pipe going into that item. If there is a small slot-head or lever valve, turn it slowly to isolate that item only.

5. **Use another stop tap only if it is safe and appropriate.**  
   An outside stop tap or meter stop tap is often near the property boundary, under a small cover, or near the water meter. It may belong to your water supplier and may also shut off neighbours on a shared supply, so use it only for an emergency or when you are sure it is safe. Turn it slowly clockwise and never force it.

6. **Get help that can actually isolate the water.**  
   If you are a tenant, contact your landlord, letting agent, housing association, or council repairs emergency line. If the outside stop tap cannot be found, is stuck, is in the highway, or may be shared, contact your water supplier. If water still cannot be isolated, call an emergency plumber.

7. **If the leak is near a boiler or heating pipework.**  
   If you can do so safely without touching wet electrics, switch the boiler off. Do not remove covers or dismantle parts. Arrange a plumber, or a Gas Safe registered engineer if gas boiler parts may be involved.

8. **Check whether the flow has reduced.**  
   Once you think the water is off or isolated, briefly open a cold tap to see whether the flow has stopped or reduced, then close it again.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to replace the stopcock, upgrade plumbing, or add new isolation valves.
- You do not need to work out who is responsible while water is still spreading.
- You do not need to start a full clean-up until the water source is controlled and the area is safe.
- You do not need to restart appliances, heating, or electrics immediately.

## Important reassurance
A stuck stopcock or stop tap is common, especially if it has not been moved for years. Stopping before you force it is the safer choice and can prevent a worse leak.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and buy time. Later decisions about repair, replacement, insurance, tenancy responsibilities, or supplier responsibility may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, electrical, plumbing, gas, medical, or professional advice. If there is immediate danger from electrics, unsafe water, or rapidly spreading water that you cannot control, move to a safer place and get urgent help.
