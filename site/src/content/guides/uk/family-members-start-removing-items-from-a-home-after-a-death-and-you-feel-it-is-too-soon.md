# What to do if… family members start removing items from a home after a death and you feel it is too soon (UK)

## Short answer
Request an immediate pause on the removal of all items to allow for a formal inventory and to identify the executor. Only the executor or administrator has the legal authority to distribute or dispose of the deceased's possessions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not engage in physical confrontation or attempt to forcibly reclaim items from family members.
- Do not remove items yourself for "safekeeping" without the executor's knowledge, as this can be legally classed as intermeddling.
- Do not change the locks on the property unless you are the named executor or have obtained legal advice.
- Do not make accusations of theft unless there is evidence of criminal intent and you are prepared to involve the police.
- Do not dispose of, donate, or sell any items until a full valuation for Inheritance Tax has been completed.

## What to do now
1. **Request a temporary pause to all activity.** **Clearly state that you wish to ensure all items are accounted for before anything leaves the premises.**
   - Suggest a "nothing leaves today" rule to allow emotions to settle.
2. **Identify the named executor or administrator.** **Ask if a will has been located and who is appointed to manage the estate.**
   - In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, this is the executor; in Scotland, they seek "Confirmation."
   - If no will exists, the person with the legal right to manage the estate (administrator) is determined by the rules of intestacy.
3. **Notify those present about the risks of intermeddling.** **Explain that taking items before probate is granted can lead to personal legal liability for any losses to the estate.**
   - Remind everyone that all "chattels" (possessions) form part of the estate's value for tax purposes.
4. **Create a visual record of the current state of the home.** **Use a phone to take a walk-through video and detailed photos of every room, including the contents of cupboards and drawers.**
5. **Secure vital documents and small valuables.** **Gather the will, passport, bank cards, and property deeds into a single, secure location on the premises.**
   - Ensure these are kept together and only accessed by the executor.
6. **Maintain a log of any items that are removed despite your request.** **Note the description of the item, who took it, the time it was removed, and the vehicle registration if applicable.**
   - Do not attempt to block their path; focus on accurate documentation.
7. **Contact the executor or the solicitor handling the estate immediately.** **Report that items are being removed and ask them to issue a formal instruction to all family members to stop.**
8. **Contact the police on 101 or via their online reporting tool if you anticipate a breach of the peace.** **Police will not usually intervene in the division of property but may attend to prevent violence or threats.**
   - Use 999 only if there is an immediate danger to life or a crime is currently in progress.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide who inherits specific sentimental items today.
- You do not need to begin the final house clearance or book removal services.
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family arguments or grievances about fairness.
- You do not need to verify the exact market value of every item immediately.

## Important reassurance
Conflict over possessions is a very common reaction to the shock and grief of a death. Wanting to protect the home and follow a legal process is a protective action, not an act of hostility toward your family. Taking a pause is often the best way to prevent permanent damage to family relationships.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate disputes over property. It does not replace the legal process of probate, Confirmation, or estate administration, which may require professional legal assistance.

## Important note
The legal rights of executors and beneficiaries vary across the UK. Unauthorized removal of estate assets (intermeddling) can result in the person being held personally liable for debts or taxes. If items have already been removed or the dispute is escalating, consult a solicitor specializing in probate and estate law.
