# What to do if… a job or work opportunity communication turns sexual or inappropriate (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the real-time exchange, get to a safer pause, and save the messages if you can do that safely. You do not have to play along, send anything sexual, or meet them to keep yourself safe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not meet them alone to “talk it through” or “prove you’re serious” if the communication has turned sexual.
- Do not send intimate photos, videos, identity documents, payment details, or extra personal information to keep the offer alive.
- Do not delete messages, call logs, emails, screenshots, or platform chats if you can keep them safely.
- Do not negotiate with threats about losing the job, role, or opportunity while you feel pressured.
- Do not click new links, open attachments, or move to a more private channel because they ask you to.
- Do not blame yourself for being polite, hopeful, or professional.

## What to do now
1. **End the live exchange.** Use one short line if it is safe to do so: “I’m not comfortable with this. Please keep communication professional.” Then stop responding for now.
2. **Create a safer pause.** Move away from the chat, call, meeting place, or app. If you are due to meet them, do not go alone; cancel, delay, or move to a public and professional setting.
3. **Save what happened if it is safe.** Keep the full thread, including names, profile links, phone numbers, dates, times, job advert details, and any threats or promises linked to the role. Store copies somewhere the other person cannot access.
4. **Protect your contact details.** Do not give more personal information. If possible, move any necessary future contact to email or a formal recruitment platform, or stop contact entirely.
5. **Check the organisation independently.** Do not use links or numbers they sent. Search for the employer, agency, production company, client, or platform yourself, and contact HR, safeguarding, compliance, or a main switchboard if there is a real organisation behind the role.
6. **Report the profile or advert on the platform.** Use the platform’s report function for sexual harassment, impersonation, recruitment abuse, or unsafe conduct. Keep your saved copies before blocking if you can.
7. **If you are unsafe now, prioritise safety.** Get to a public place, a trusted person, or a staffed location. If there is immediate danger, violence, stalking, or pressure to meet right now, call 999. For non-emergency police contact in the UK, call 101 or use the official online police reporting route.
8. **Use specialist support if you feel shaken, threatened, or unsure what counts.** In England and Wales, the 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line supports people aged 16 and over affected by sexual harassment or sexual violence. In Scotland, Rape Crisis Scotland can help you find specialist support. In Northern Ireland, nidirect lists sexual violence support routes.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide now whether to report to the police, make a formal complaint, leave the opportunity, or take legal action.
- You do not have to write a perfect account tonight.
- You do not have to confront the person or persuade them that it was wrong.
- You do not have to decide whether this was “serious enough” before getting support.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, trying to stay polite, or feeling confused is common when someone with power over a job, role, or opportunity crosses a sexual boundary. The responsibility is on the person sexualising the opportunity, not on you.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the moment, reduce immediate risk, and preserve options. Later decisions about complaints, police, employment rights, immigration concerns, contracts, or ongoing contact may need specialist support.

## Important note
This guide provides general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, employment, or professional advice. Processes and support options can vary by location and circumstances; if you feel unsafe or at risk, prioritise immediate safety and urgent help.
