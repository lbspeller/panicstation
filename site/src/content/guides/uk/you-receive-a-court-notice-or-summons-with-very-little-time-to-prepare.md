# What to do if… you receive a court notice or summons with very little time to prepare (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the notice as real until you have checked it through an official court route. Confirm the court, case number, date, time, and what you are required to do, then get same-day legal advice if the case is about you or if missing the hearing could have serious consequences.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore it because the notice arrived late or looks confusing.
- Don’t rely only on a phone number or email printed on the notice if you are unsure it is genuine.
- Don’t assume the court will postpone the hearing because you have work, travel, childcare, illness, or short notice.
- Don’t contact the other party in anger or panic, especially in family, harassment, injunction, or criminal matters.
- Don’t post photos of the notice online; it may contain personal data, addresses, case numbers, or private allegations.
- Don’t assume that remote attendance, late arrival, or non-attendance is allowed unless the court confirms it.

## What to do now
1. **Read only the essential details first.**  
   Find the court or tribunal name, date and time, address or remote hearing details, case or reference number, your name, and the wording used on the notice, such as summons, notice of hearing, order, witness summons, or citation.

2. **Check the notice using official contact details.**  
   Use an independent official contact route, not just the details printed on the notice if you are unsure:
   - In England and Wales, use the official “Find a court or tribunal” service.
   - In Scotland, use Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service court, tribunal, and office contact details.
   - In Northern Ireland, use Northern Ireland Courts and Tribunals Service contact details.

   Ask: “Can you confirm this case number, the hearing date and time, whether I must attend, and whether this is in person or remote?”

3. **Work out what the notice says your role is.**  
   - If you are named as a defendant, respondent, applicant, claimant, petitioner, party, or similar, treat it as urgent because the court may still deal with the case if you do nothing.
   - If you are a witness and the notice says witness summons, citation, or similar, treat it as urgent because you may be required to attend.
   - If you cannot tell what your role is, ask the court office to explain what the notice requires from you, without asking them for legal advice.

4. **If the case is about you, seek legal help today.**  
   Contact a solicitor or legal advice service in the relevant part of the UK. Say: “I have received a court notice on short notice. The hearing is on [date/time]. The case number is [number]. I need urgent advice on attendance and what to do before the hearing.”

5. **If you are a witness, contact the right witness contact after verifying the notice.**  
   If the notice or the court confirms a witness care officer, prosecutor, defence solicitor, party, or court contact, ask them what time to arrive, whether you must attend in person, and who to tell if there is a serious problem with attending.

6. **If you genuinely cannot attend, contact the court immediately.**  
   Keep it brief and factual: “I received the notice on [date]. The hearing is on [date/time]. I cannot attend because [short reason]. What is the correct way to ask for an adjournment, postponement, or remote attendance if that is possible?” Do not assume anything has changed unless the court confirms it.

7. **Make attending realistic if the court says you must attend.**  
   Arrange transport, time off work, childcare, and enough time for security and finding the right room or desk. Bring the notice, related papers, any ID requested or available, a phone charger, and any documents the notice specifically tells you to bring.

8. **Keep a simple record.**  
   Write down who you contacted, the date and time, the number or email used, and what they said. Save copies of emails, forms, delivery receipts, screenshots of remote hearing details, and any proof you sent to the court.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your whole legal strategy right now.
- You do not need to prepare a perfect statement today unless the court or your solicitor has told you to do that immediately.
- You do not need to gather every possible document before you have confirmed what the notice is and whether you must attend.
- You do not need to contact the other side unless the court, your solicitor, or a proper official process tells you to.

## Important reassurance
A short-notice court letter can feel alarming, but the first safe move is practical: verify it, contact the court through an official route, and avoid going silent. Early contact is usually much safer than guessing or hoping it will go away.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The right next actions depend on the UK nation, court or tribunal type, the wording of the notice, and whether you are a party, defendant, respondent, witness, or someone else.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Court procedures and consequences vary, and missing a required court appearance can sometimes lead to serious outcomes, including decisions being made without you or a warrant in some cases. Get urgent advice from a qualified UK legal professional if you are unsure what the notice requires.
