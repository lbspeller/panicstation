# What to do if… you are placed on paid leave during your notice period with no clear explanation (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all direct communication with the client immediately and obtain an approved "holding statement" from your manager. Do not speculate, apologise, or explain the internal change until you have written instructions from your employer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess the reason for the change (such as "it is an HR matter" or "there is an internal review") when speaking to the client.
- Do not blame your employer, colleagues, or the client for the removal.
- Do not use personal email or messaging apps to bypass revoked system access.
- Do not share confidential internal staffing decisions or performance data.
- Do not post about the situation on LinkedIn or other professional social networks.
- Do not delete client files or correspondence from work systems "in a panic".

## What to do now
1. **Check for an immediate 'No Contact' order.** Review your most recent emails or chat messages to see if a manager has explicitly told you to cease contact with the client.
   - If a 'No Contact' order exists, forward any client inquiries to your manager without replying to the client.
2. **Draft a neutral holding line if a reply is required.** If you must respond to maintain professional standards, use a brief, non-committal statement.
   - Example: "Thank you for reaching out. The account management team is currently restructuring our support for your project. I have copied [Name] here, who will be your primary contact moving forward."
3. **Request the official "Reason for Change" wording.** Send a formal email to your line manager or the designated client lead asking for the specific narrative you should use if asked again.
4. **Clarify your status regarding the 6-month rule.** If you have more than 6 months of service, you have increased protections under the Employment Rights Act 2025. 
   - Ask if this removal is part of a formal capability or disciplinary process.
5. **Log all revoked access points (10 minutes).** Write down which systems you can no longer enter (CRM, email, shared drives) and at what time the access was cut.
6. **Request an informal "fact-finding" meeting.** If no reason has been given, ask your manager for a brief meeting to understand if the removal is due to routine restructuring or a specific concern.
7. **Verify your right to be accompanied.** If you are called to a formal meeting regarding the removal, you have a statutory right under the Acas Code of Practice to be accompanied by a colleague or trade union representative.
8. **Secure your own evidence legally.** Keep a log of your successful client outcomes and positive feedback in a private note. 
   - Do not download or forward proprietary client data to personal devices, as this may constitute a gross misconduct offence.
9. **Contact the Fair Work Agency (FWA) if you suspect a breach.** If you believe the removal is an act of retaliation or discrimination, the FWA (operational as of April 2026) can provide guidance on enforcement.

## What can wait
- You do not need to defend your professional reputation to the client today.
- You do not need to decide if you want to raise a formal grievance until you have the "fact-finding" results.
- You do not need to update your CV or LinkedIn profile immediately.
- You do not need to assume this will lead to dismissal; client removals are often handled as "Some Other Substantial Reason" (SOSR) and may simply result in a new internal assignment.

## Important reassurance
Being removed from a client account is a common operational occurrence in UK professional services. It often relates to internal resource planning, contract renewals, or routine rotation of staff. Following the approved communication chain protects your professional standing and your employment rights while the situation is clarified.

## Scope note
This guide covers initial stabilization and communication management during the first 24 hours of an account removal. It does not replace legal advice for complex redundancy, disciplinary, or whistleblowing cases.

## Important note
This information is based on UK employment law as of 2026, including the Employment Rights Act 2025 and Acas Code of Practice. Individual rights can vary based on your specific contract (e.g., worker vs. employee status) and your length of service. If you are threatened with dismissal, consult a qualified legal professional or a trade union representative immediately.
