# What to do if… your spare key has been moved or accessed without your permission (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the key as compromised until you know otherwise. Get to a safer pause first; if someone could still be inside or you feel in immediate danger, do not go in and call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go inside to “check quickly” if you feel unsafe, hear movement, or notice another sign of entry such as a door ajar, a window open, fresh lock marks, or lights changed.
- Do not confront, accuse, or message someone you suspect while you are shaken.
- Do not leave the same spare-key, lockbox, or key-safe setup in place longer than necessary if it may now be known to someone else.
- Do not post identifying details in a public or building group chat.
- Do not tidy up, wipe surfaces, or handle disturbed items if police may need to look at the scene.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.**  
   Step away from the door if you are outside. Go to a neighbour, your car, a staffed public place, or another locked space where you can make calls without being overheard.

2. **Choose emergency or non-emergency help.**  
   Call **999** if you think someone could still be inside, you hear movement, you see clear signs of a break-in, or you feel in immediate danger. If there is no immediate danger but you want the incident logged, you can contact **101** or use your local police force’s online reporting route.

3. **Check from outside only.**  
   From a safe place, look for visible signs: the spare key not where you left it, a lockbox open or reset, fresh scratches around the lock, a door not sitting correctly, an open window, moved curtains, or lights that are unexpectedly on or off. If you see signs of entry, stay out.

4. **Stop the spare key being useful.**  
   If it is safe, remove any remaining spare key from its hiding place, key safe, or lockbox. If a key safe or lockbox may have been opened, change its code before using it again, and consider moving it or stopping use of it.

5. **Secure the door access.**  
   Arrange for the relevant external lock cylinders or locks to be changed as soon as practical. If you cannot change them immediately, stay elsewhere if you can, ask a trusted person to be with you, keep doors and windows locked, and avoid being alone near the entry point while you organise the change.

6. **If you rent, put the access concern in writing.**  
   Tell your landlord or letting agent that a spare key may have been accessed or moved without your permission. Ask who currently holds keys, whether any recent access was made, who authorised it, and what can be done about changing locks or key control. Tenant access rules vary across the UK by tenancy type and nation, so check your tenancy agreement and get housing advice before relying on a precise notice period.

7. **Reduce other forgotten access routes.**  
   Think about communal entrance fobs, garage keys, back gate keys, shed keys, alarm codes, smart-lock users, cleaner keys, contractor keys, and trusted-person spare keys. Change codes, remove unknown users, and ask the relevant building manager or key holder what can be replaced or reissued.

8. **Make a simple record.**  
   Take photos of the spare-key location, key safe, door, lock, windows, and anything disturbed. Write down the date and time you noticed it, the last time you are sure it was normal, who knew about the spare key, and any messages you send or receive.

9. **If anything is missing or damaged, keep records for police and insurance.**  
   Make a short list of missing items or damage. If you report a crime, ask how to get or keep the reference details before contacting your insurer.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out who did it right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to move home, install CCTV, buy a full alarm system, or change every security habit.
- You do not need to confront a neighbour, housemate, contractor, landlord, or agent immediately.
- You can do a full inventory later if there is no immediate danger and no obvious damage.
- You can compare longer-term security products later; the first job is making the current key access unusable.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel unsettled when a spare key may have been touched or moved. You do not have to prove what happened before acting; treating access as compromised and calmly securing the entry points is a sensible first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: immediate safety, key control, lock security, and a clear record. Later decisions about landlord disputes, insurance claims, police follow-up, or long-term security may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, security, locksmith, insurance, or other professional advice. If you feel at risk or unsafe, prioritise getting to a safer place and contacting the police.
