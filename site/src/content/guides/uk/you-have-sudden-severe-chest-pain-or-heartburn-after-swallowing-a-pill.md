# What to do if… you have sudden severe chest pain or heartburn after swallowing a pill (UK)

## Short answer
Treat sudden chest pain seriously. If the pain is severe, not easing, or comes with breathlessness, sweating, nausea, faintness, or pain spreading to your arm, neck, jaw, back, or upper stomach, call 999 or 112 now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take another dose of the same pill to see if it passes or to cancel it out.
- Do not lie down flat, unless you feel faint; if you do, lie on your side and call 999 or 112.
- Do not force down more tablets, dry bread, or large gulps to push it through.
- Do not induce vomiting.
- Do not drive yourself to hospital if you feel faint, very unwell, or think it could be heart-related.

## What to do now
1. Check for emergency warning signs.

   Call 999 or 112 now if you have any of these:
   - chest pain, pressure, tightness, squeezing, burning, or indigestion-like pain that is severe or not going away
   - shortness of breath, sweating, feeling sick, light-headedness, collapse, or unusual paleness
   - pain spreading to one or both arms, the neck, jaw, back, or upper stomach
   - severe difficulty breathing, choking, wheezing, or swelling of the lips, face, or tongue
   - you cannot swallow your saliva, are drooling, or feel that food, liquid, or the pill is blocking your throat
   - vomiting blood, vomit that looks like coffee grounds, or black, tarry stools

2. Get into the safest position while you get help.

   Sit upright and rest. Loosen tight clothing. Keep your phone with you. If you are alone and can do it safely, unlock your door so help can reach you.

3. Stop further irritation.

   Do not take any more of the suspected pill for now. If you can swallow comfortably, take tiny sips of water only. Stop immediately if swallowing is painful, you cannot swallow saliva, you start drooling, or you feel you might choke.

4. Use urgent NHS help if it is not clearly an emergency but symptoms are significant or persisting.

   Call 111 where available, use 111 online in England, or use your local NHS urgent-care route. Do this today if you have persistent chest burning, pain when swallowing, or a strong stuck-pill feeling. Follow the advice you are given about an urgent treatment centre or A&E. If you worsen at any point, call 999 or 112.

5. Keep the medicine information ready.

   Put the packet, bottle, or blister strip where you can reach it. Note the medicine name, dose, time swallowed, whether you took it with water or food, and what you felt first, such as burning, pressure, pain on swallowing, or a stuck feeling.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this is reflux, a pill irritating your gullet, or something else. The priority is safe triage.
- You do not need to search for which pill causes this while symptoms are happening. Keep the packet and focus on getting help if symptoms are severe or persisting.
- You do not need to make a permanent decision about your medication plan today. Pause the next dose of the suspected medicine until you have medical advice.

## Important reassurance
Chest pain can feel frightening because heartburn, indigestion, pill irritation, and heart symptoms can overlap. Taking chest pain seriously is not overreacting; it is the safer first step.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance for the next minutes and hours. Later decisions, such as changing medicines or needing tests, depend on what a clinician finds.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not a diagnosis, medical advice, or professional advice. Chest pain can be serious even when it feels like heartburn, so seek urgent help if you are unsure or symptoms are severe.
