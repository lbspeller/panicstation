# What to do if… you receive an official hearing notice with very little time to respond (UK)

## Short answer
Act today: contact the office named on the notice, ask exactly how to request a postponement, adjournment, change of hearing date, or extension, and send a short urgent written request using the route they tell you. Prepare as if the hearing is still going ahead unless you receive written confirmation that it has changed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice because it feels unfair or too fast; missing a deadline may make it harder to undo.
- Do not assume a phone call alone is enough; follow up using the filing route stated on the notice, such as a form, portal, email or post, and keep proof.
- Do not send a long emotional explanation as your first response; send a short, structured request first.
- Do not miss the hearing just because you asked for it to be moved, unless you receive clear written confirmation that it has changed.
- Do not send original documents unless the notice or office says originals are required; send copies or photos and keep originals safe.
- Do not rely on a form you found online unless it matches your exact court, tribunal, agency and case type.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the essentials from the notice.** Record the hearing date and time, how it will happen, the case or reference number, any response deadline, who sent the notice, and the exact contact and filing route listed on it.

2. **Contact the listed office today.** Ask what you must file, what counts as filed, whether there is a required form, whether you must copy anyone else, and whether the hearing will still go ahead while your request is being considered.

3. **Send an urgent written request the same day if you need more time.** Put “URGENT” in the subject line or heading. Include the case number, hearing date, when you received the notice, what you are asking for, and the short reason, such as late notice, missing papers, illness, caring emergency, lack of interpreter, access needs, or not having key documents.

4. **If another party is involved, ask about copying them in.** Many processes expect you to tell the office whether the other party agrees, objects, or has not replied. If the office says you must copy them, do that and keep proof.

5. **Send a holding response if something is due before you can fully prepare.** Keep it brief: say you intend to take part, say you are asking for more time or a new date, and list what you still need, such as the decision letter, case file, hearing pack, evidence list, translation, interpreter, or representation.

6. **Ask for missing papers in clear terms.** Use the wording from the notice where possible. Ask for the decision being reviewed, the case file or record, the evidence or exhibits list, and any hearing pack or bundle. List exactly what you have not received.

7. **Ask for adjustments now if you need them.** If you need a language interpreter, BSL interpreter, captions, breaks, accessible formats, help with reading material, or another adjustment, ask in writing and explain what would help you take part. Ask for written confirmation of what will be provided.

8. **Make a quick proof pack.** Save the notice, screenshots, emails, portal confirmations, delivery receipts and copies of anything you send. If you post anything, use post only if it is an accepted route for your case and keep proof of posting.

9. **Prepare for the hearing still happening.** Put together the notice, the key decision or letter, the few documents that matter most, and a one-page bullet summary of what you want and why. If the hearing goes ahead, repeat at the start that you had short notice and have asked for more time.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a perfect legal argument today; the first job is to protect your ability to take part.
- You do not need to gather every possible document; start with what the notice says is required and the few documents that matter most.
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain about the process; focus first on deadlines, attendance, and getting the right papers.
- You do not need to choose a long-term strategy before you have confirmed whether the hearing date or response deadline can be changed.

## Important reassurance
Short-notice official letters often make people freeze. The useful move is small and immediate: contact the right office, send a clear written request, keep proof, and prepare for the hearing unless you are told in writing that it has changed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stop avoidable damage and buy time. The correct forms, deadlines and wording vary by court, tribunal, agency, location and case type, and later decisions may need specialist legal or advice-sector help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, immigration, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If the notice suggests urgent high-stakes consequences, such as losing housing, benefits, a licence, immigration status, detention, prison, or bail, seek urgent specialist help if you can and tell the hearing office you are doing so.
