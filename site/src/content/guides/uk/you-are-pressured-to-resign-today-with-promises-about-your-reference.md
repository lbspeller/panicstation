# What to do if… you are pressured to resign today with promises about your reference (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the suspension as a neutral, temporary step rather than a final verdict: obtain all suspension terms in writing (pay, restrictions, and contact points) and avoid discussing the allegations with anyone other than a union representative or legal advisor.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign immediately or send "bridge-burning" messages to management or colleagues while in a state of shock.
- Do not attempt to contact witnesses, colleagues, or clients to "clear your name" or gather evidence if you have been told not to.
- Do not access work systems, files, email accounts, or premises unless you have explicit written permission.
- Do not delete, move, or download company data or "confidential" files to your personal devices for safekeeping.
- Do not hand over personal phones, laptops, or social media passwords to your employer without formal written scope and legal advice.
- Do not post details about the suspension or the allegations on social media, even in private groups.

## What to do now
1. **Request written confirmation of the suspension terms within one working day.** Ask HR or your manager to provide a letter or email that confirms:
   - the specific allegations or the nature of the investigation
   - that the suspension is a "neutral act" and not a disciplinary sanction
   - that you remain on full pay and retain all contractual benefits (unless your contract explicitly allows for unpaid suspension)
   - the specific rules regarding contact with colleagues, clients, and access to work property.
2. **Ask why alternatives to suspension were not used.** ACAS 2026 guidelines require employers to consider if the investigation can proceed while you work from home, move to a different department, or change your shift; ask for the reasoning why these were ruled out.
3. **Identify your single point of contact (SPOC) for the duration of the suspension.** Ensure you have one person (usually an HR representative or a neutral manager) to whom you can send all queries, sickness reports, or holiday requests.
4. **Confirm how and when the suspension will be reviewed.** Suspension must be as short as possible; ask in writing for the date of the first review and how you will be updated on the investigation’s progress.
5. **Request reasonable adjustments for any meetings if you have a disability.** If you have a physical or mental health condition that affects your ability to participate in an investigation meeting, you have a legal right to request a companion or advocate as a reasonable adjustment, even if a "right to be accompanied" does not normally apply to investigations.
6. **Save a copy of your contract and the company handbook (if you already have access).** Check the sections on "Suspension," "Disciplinary Procedures," and "Confidentiality" to ensure your employer is following their own stated rules.
7. **Contact your trade union representative or a legal advisor immediately.** If you are a union member, they can help you communicate with HR and ensure the process is fair; if you are not, check your home insurance or bank account for "Legal Expenses Insurance" which may cover employment advice.
8. **Create a factual timeline of events while they are fresh in your mind.** Write down dates, times, what was said by whom, and the names of potential witnesses, but do not contact these witnesses yourself.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare a detailed "defence statement" or formal response to the allegations today.
- You do not need to decide if you are going to sue for unfair dismissal; those decisions require the full investigation report.
- You do not need to explain your absence to friends, family, or colleagues today.
- You do not need to apply for new jobs immediately unless the suspension is part of a wider redundancy or closure.

## Important reassurance
Being suspended is often a procedural "pause" used to ensure an investigation is impartial; it is common to feel a sense of panic, injustice, or isolation. These reactions are normal, but they do not mean the outcome is pre-determined. Most suspensions are resolved through a structured process that includes your right to respond.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for the initial 24–48 hours of a UK workplace suspension. It does not cover the technicalities of disciplinary hearings, tribunal claims, or criminal investigations.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional legal or union advice. Employment law is subject to individual contract terms and the specific circumstances of your case. If you are threatened with unpaid suspension or believe the suspension is discriminatory, seek advice from ACAS or an employment solicitor immediately.
