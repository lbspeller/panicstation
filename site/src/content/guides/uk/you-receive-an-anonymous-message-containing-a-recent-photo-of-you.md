# What to do if… you receive an anonymous message containing a recent photo of you (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible safety, stalking, harassment, blackmail, or scam situation. Do not reply, move to a safer place if you feel at risk, and save the message if you can do so without delaying urgent help.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply to ask who they are.
- Do not click links, open files, scan QR codes, or install anything they send.
- Do not send more photos, location details, passwords, money, or personal information.
- Do not agree to meet them or prove anything to them.
- Do not post the photo publicly or ask strangers online to identify the sender.
- Do not keep the conversation going to collect more evidence.
- Do not delay calling 999 if you think you or someone else is in immediate danger.

## What to do now
1. **Check immediate safety first.**  
   If you think the sender may be nearby, the photo was taken outside your home, workplace, school, or regular route, or you feel in danger now, move towards other people, lock doors if relevant, and call 999.

2. **Do not engage with the sender.**  
   Leave the message unanswered. Mute the chat if that helps you avoid reacting. Block only after you have saved the key details, unless blocking is needed immediately to stop you feeling pressured.

3. **Save the message in a simple way.**  
   If you can do this safely, take screenshots showing the sender’s number, handle, profile, message text, photo, date, time, and platform. Save the photo as received, without cropping or editing it. Write down where you were when the photo may have been taken and anything visible in the background.

4. **Report it to police if there is fear, threat, repeated contact, following, surveillance, or blackmail.**  
   If it is not an emergency, use 101 or your local police online reporting route. In Scotland, use Police Scotland’s reporting routes. In Northern Ireland, use PSNI reporting routes. Do not wait to gather perfect evidence before reporting if you feel unsafe.

5. **Report it on the app or platform where it arrived.**  
   Use the in-app report option for harassment, stalking, threats, impersonation, blackmail, or unwanted contact. Include the sender’s account and the message with the photo.

6. **If there is a demand for money, payment, or login details, treat it as possible fraud or extortion.**  
   Do not pay. Do not negotiate. In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report cyber crime or fraud to Report Fraud. In Scotland, report fraud or cyber crime to Police Scotland on 101 unless it is an emergency.

7. **If it looks like phishing or a scam, use the UK scam-reporting routes.**  
   Forward suspicious emails to report@phishing.gov.uk. Forward suspicious text messages to 7726. If the message also contains threats or stalking behaviour, preserve it as evidence and report the threat or stalking concern to police.

8. **Reduce easy access today.**  
   Turn off live location sharing and remove recent public posts or story details that show your current location, routine, home, workplace, school, gym, commute, or where you are going next. If the account shows signs of unauthorised access, change its password, review logged-in sessions, and turn on two-step verification.

9. **Tell one trusted person what happened.**  
   Send them the screenshots, tell them where you are, and arrange a check-in for today. If you need to go out soon, avoid being alone if possible and choose a route with other people around.

10. **Use specialist stalking support if this feels targeted or repeated.**  
   The National Stalking Helpline can help you think through practical safety steps and how to record incidents without pushing you to make decisions before you are ready.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the sender right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to change your number, move, delete accounts, or confront anyone.
- You do not need to make a public post about it.
- You do not need to keep talking to the sender to prove what is happening.
- You do not need to solve every account, privacy, or security setting at once.

## Important reassurance
A recent photo from an unknown sender is unsettling, and it makes sense to take it seriously. Slowing down, not replying, saving key details, and using official reporting routes is a strong first response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If contact continues, threats escalate, or you think someone is physically following or monitoring you, later decisions may need help from police, specialist stalking services, cyber-safety support, or legal advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you or someone else is in immediate danger, call 999.
