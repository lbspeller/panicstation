# What to do if… people are arriving at your home after a death and you need privacy and boundaries (UK)

## Short answer
You do not have to receive visitors right now. Ask one trusted person to handle the door, calls, and messages, and use one simple line: “We’re not having visitors today. We’ll be in touch.”

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door just because someone is there.
- Do not try to host, feed, or comfort everyone while you are in shock.
- Do not argue on the doorstep; repeat one boundary line and end the interaction.
- Do not hand over documents, bank cards, keys, phones, or medication to anyone who is “helping” in the moment.
- Do not make big decisions about the funeral, money, belongings, or family disputes while people are crowding you.
- Do not let anyone pressure you into drinking alcohol or taking anything to cope if you feel unsteady.

## What to do now
1. **Create a quick pause inside your home.** Lock the door if it is safe to do so. Move away from the front door and front windows. Sit somewhere steadier, such as a sofa, bed, or the floor with a wall behind you. Drink some water.
2. **Keep one route open for urgent contact.** Silence non-urgent notifications, but allow calls or messages from your gatekeeper, emergency services, or one key contact.
3. **Pick one person to be your gatekeeper right now.** Text or call one trusted friend, relative, or neighbour: “I need you to handle the door and messages for the next few hours. Can you come over, or stay on the phone with me?” If they can come, ask them to speak to visitors so you do not have to.
4. **Use one boundary line and keep it boring.** Say through the door, by phone, or by message: “Thank you for coming. We’re not able to have visitors today. Please leave a message and we’ll be in touch.” Repeat once if needed, then stop replying.
5. **Stop the chain of people arriving.** Ask your gatekeeper to send one group message: “Please don’t come round. The house is closed to visitors today. We’ll share updates when we can.” This reduces misunderstandings and second-hand messages.
6. **Put a visible boundary at the door.** If it feels helpful, put up a note saying: “No visitors today. Please do not knock. Leave a message.” You do not need to explain more than that.
7. **Protect your space and essentials.** Put keys, wallet, phone, medication, and important papers together in one bag, drawer, or room. If anyone starts tidying, sorting, or moving things, say: “Please don’t move anything. We are leaving the house as it is today.”
8. **Ask for specific help only if you want it.** Use small, clear requests such as: “Please bring milk and bread and leave it at the door,” “Please sit quietly with me for 20 minutes,” or “Please phone X and tell them not to come round today.”
9. **Treat pressure or refusal to leave as a safety issue.** If someone is threatening you, forcing entry, using violence, or the situation could get heated or violent very soon, call 999. If it is not an emergency but you need police help or advice, call 101 or report online through Police.uk or your local police force.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who can attend the funeral, what happens to belongings, or how to handle family conflict.
- You do not need to reply to every message, answer every call, or give everyone updates.
- You do not need to complete all official steps in the next hour; they can be handled in sequence.
- You do not need to comfort everyone else. The immediate task is to make the next few hours quieter and safer.

## Important reassurance
Feeling numb, irritated, protective, or overwhelmed by people arriving can happen after a death. Wanting the house closed is a reasonable boundary while you get through the first shock.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise your space, reduce pressure, and get immediate support. Later decisions about notifications, paperwork, funeral arrangements, money, property, or family disputes may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe, prioritise immediate safety and contact emergency services. If your distress feels unmanageable, you can contact NHS 111 and select the mental health option; if someone’s life is at risk or you do not feel able to keep yourself or someone else safe, call 999 or go to A&E.
