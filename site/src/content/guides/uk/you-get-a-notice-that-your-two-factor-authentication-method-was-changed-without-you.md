# What to do if… you get a notice that your two-factor authentication method was changed without you (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the notice as a high-priority account takeover attempt; navigate directly to the official service website to secure your account and reset all recovery methods immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don't click any links, buttons, or "Review Activity" prompts within the alert email or text message.
- Don't use the phone number provided in the alert to contact "support."
- Don't ignore the alert even if you still have access to the account, as attackers may be setting up a permanent lockout.
- Don't use the same password for the affected account on any other service.
- Don't assume a "SIM change" or "No Service" message on your phone is a technical glitch; treat it as a SIM swap attack.

## What to do now
1. **Navigate to the official login page manually:** Open a new browser window and type the service's address directly (e.g., google.com, icloud.com) or use the official mobile app.
2. **Attempt an immediate password reset:** 
   - If you still have access, **change your password** to a unique, 12+ character string.
   - If you are locked out, **initiate the "Account Recovery" or "I've been hacked" flow** on the official site.
3. **Terminate all active sessions:** Locate the "Security" or "Login Activity" section and **select "Sign out of all other devices"** or "Terminate all sessions."
4. **Audit and reset 2FA methods:**
   - **Remove any unknown authenticator apps**, phone numbers, or security keys.
   - **Re-enable your own 2FA method** (prioritise an Authenticator App over SMS).
   - **Generate new backup/recovery codes** and save them in a physical location or an encrypted vault.
5. **Check for persistent access rules:** Review your account settings for **automatic email forwarding**, **new inbox rules/filters**, or **third-party app permissions** you did not authorise.
6. **Secure your primary email account:** Ensure your **email login and 2FA** are secure, as attackers use email access to bypass 2FA on other linked accounts.
7. **Forward the suspicious alert:** If the alert was a phishing attempt via SMS, **forward the message to 7726** (the free UK reporting service).
8. **Contact your mobile provider if you lose signal:** If you suspect a SIM swap, **call your network provider immediately** to verify if a new SIM has been issued and request a port-out lock.
9. **Report the incident officially:**
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland: **Report to the Report Fraud service** (formerly Action Fraud) via reportfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040.
   - In Scotland: **Report to Police Scotland** via their online "Reporting Cybercrime" form or by calling 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific IP address or location of the attacker right now.
- You do not need to notify all your contacts until your primary email and the affected account are fully secured.
- You do not need to replace your hardware (phone/computer) unless you have evidence of local malware.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an unauthorized change notice is an automated security feature working as intended to give you a window to react. By following the official recovery steps directly on the provider's site rather than through the alert link, you can effectively halt most takeover attempts.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for individual account security. Complex corporate breaches or incidents involving significant financial loss may require specialist technical support or legal advice.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or technical professional advice. Security procedures and reporting links for specific providers (like Google, Microsoft, or Apple) are subject to change. Always verify you are on a `.gov.uk` or official service domain before entering credentials or personal details.
