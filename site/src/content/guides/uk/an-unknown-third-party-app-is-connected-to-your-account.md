# What to do if… an unknown third-party app is connected to your account (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as possible account compromise. Go to the account by typing the website address yourself, remove the unknown app, then secure the account before using it for anything sensitive.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click “review”, “confirm”, or “secure your account” links in the alert email or pop-up; go to the account settings by typing the site address yourself.
- Don’t assume nothing happened just because you can still log in; connected apps may keep some access until you remove them.
- Don’t keep using the account for banking, password resets, or private messages until you have removed the app and checked access.
- Don’t reuse an old password or a small variation of one you have used before.
- Don’t delete emails, alerts, or account notifications yet; keep them until you have stabilised access.

## What to do now
1. **Use a trusted route into the account.**  
   Close the alert message. Open a new browser window or the official app, then sign in by typing the service’s website address yourself or using a saved bookmark you already trust.

2. **Remove the unknown app immediately.**  
   Go to the account’s **Security**, **Privacy**, **Connected apps**, **Third-party access**, or **Apps with access** area. Remove the unfamiliar app or integration. If you see permissions such as **read mail**, **offline access**, **manage account**, **full access**, or similar broad permissions, treat this as urgent.

3. **Remove other stale connected apps.**  
   While you are on the connected-apps page, remove apps you no longer use or do not recognise. Do not spend time researching every old app right now; remove the obviously unknown or unnecessary ones first.

4. **Check email forwarding, filters, and mailbox access.**  
   If this is your email account, or if this email is used to recover other accounts, check **Rules**, **Filters**, **Forwarding**, **Delegates**, and **Mailbox access**. Remove anything you did not add. This matters because a compromised mailbox can be used to receive password resets or hide security alerts.

5. **Change reused or exposed passwords.**  
   Change the password for this account. Also change the password for any other account that used the same or a similar password. Use a long, unique password for each account.

6. **Sign out sessions and devices you do not recognise.**  
   Look for **Sign out of all devices**, **Log out of all sessions**, **Manage devices**, or **Recent activity**. Sign out anything unfamiliar. If there is a “sign out everywhere” option, use it after changing the password.

7. **Turn on 2-step verification.**  
   Enable 2-step verification, 2FA, or MFA if it is available. Use the strongest option you can manage, such as a passkey, security key, or authenticator app. SMS is still usually better than having no 2-step verification.

8. **Check recovery details and security settings.**  
   Confirm your recovery email addresses, phone numbers, backup codes, trusted devices, app passwords, and security questions where the service uses them. Remove anything you did not add.

9. **Look for what may have changed.**  
   Check recent activity, security logs, payment settings, profile details, API keys, tokens, app passwords, and messages sent from the account. Take screenshots of important pages if you may need to show the provider, your bank, or a reporting service later.

10. **Report phishing messages through UK routes if a message led you here.**  
   Forward suspicious emails to **report@phishing.gov.uk**. Forward suspicious text messages to **7726**. Do not click links in the suspicious message before reporting it.

11. **Escalate if money, fraud, or immediate danger is involved.**  
   Contact your bank or card provider using the number on your card, in your banking app, or on the bank’s official website. In England, Wales and Northern Ireland, report fraud or cyber crime to **Report Fraud**. In Scotland, contact **Police Scotland** on **101** for fraud or financial crime. If there is immediate danger or a crime is in progress, call **999**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to delete the account, post a public warning, or confront anyone.
- You do not need to do a full device wipe unless there are strong signs of malware or a support professional tells you it is needed.
- You can review every connected app slowly later; for now, remove the unknown app, secure sign-in, and check the recovery route.

## Important reassurance
This can happen after one convincing login or permissions prompt. Removing the app, checking email rules, changing reused passwords, and turning on 2-step verification are practical steps that usually reduce the immediate risk.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise access and reduce harm. Later decisions may need help from the account provider, your bank, your workplace IT team, or a cyber security professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, forensic, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you are at immediate risk of harm or active fraud is happening now, prioritise urgent help through emergency, police, or banking channels.
