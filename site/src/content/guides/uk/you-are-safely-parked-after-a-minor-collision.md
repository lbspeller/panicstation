# What to do if… you are safely parked after a minor collision (UK)

## Short answer
You must remain at the scene to exchange your name, address, and vehicle registration with anyone affected; if you cannot do this, you must report the incident to the police within 24 hours.

## Do not do these things
- Don't leave the scene without providing your details to other involved parties or leaving them on a parked vehicle.
- Don't admit liability or apologize for the accident as this can be used against you in insurance or legal proceedings.
- Don't offer or accept cash payments to settle the damage privately at the roadside.
- Don't stand between vehicles or in the flow of traffic to inspect damage or take photographs.
- Don't neglect to record the other vehicle's registration number immediately, even if the other driver seems cooperative.

## What to do now
1. **Ensure the scene is stabilized and your hazard lights are active.** **Keep your hazard lights on to warn other road users and do not exit the vehicle until you have confirmed it is safe to step onto the pavement or a verge.**
   - Switch off your engine.
   - If you have a warning triangle and it is safe to place it (never on a motorway), set it at least 45 meters behind your vehicle.
2. **Perform a quick welfare check for yourself and any passengers.** **Identify if anyone is experiencing pain, confusion, or shock, and call 999 immediately if there are injuries or if the road is blocked.**
   - Note any physical symptoms like dizziness or nausea.
   - Look for external hazards such as leaking fluid or smoke.
3. **Exchange the legally required information with the other parties involved.** **You must provide your name, address, and vehicle registration number to anyone who has reasonable grounds to ask for them.**
   - If you do not own the vehicle, you must also provide the owner's name and address.
   - If any person (other than yourself) was injured, you must also provide your insurance details if requested.
4. **Collect essential evidence of the scene and the vehicles.** **Use your phone to photograph the positions of all vehicles, the specific damage, and any relevant road signs or markings.**
   - Capture the make, model, and registration of all vehicles involved.
   - Note the exact time, date, and weather conditions.
   - Request contact details from any independent witnesses who saw the incident.
5. **Report the collision to the police if details were not exchanged.** **If you were unable to provide your details at the scene for any reason, you must report the accident to a police station or a police constable as soon as possible and within 24 hours.**
   - You can also report non-injury collisions online via the local police website.
   - Call 101 if the other driver refuses to share their details or leaves the scene without stopping.
6. **Notify your insurance provider about the incident as soon as possible.** **Most insurance policies require you to report any collision within a specific timeframe, even if you do not intend to make a claim or if there is no visible damage.**
   - Use the notes and photos you took to provide an accurate account.
   - Avoid discussing fault or "off the record" agreements with the other driver's insurer if they contact you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine who was at fault or how the accident happened right now.
- You do not need to get repair quotes or contact a mechanic immediately.
- You do not need to decide whether you will pursue a personal injury claim today.
- You do not need to respond to any aggressive communication from the other party after you have left the scene.

## Important reassurance
It is entirely normal to feel a surge of adrenaline, shakiness, or "brain fog" after even a very minor bump. These physiological reactions do not mean you are failing to handle the situation; they are standard responses to a sudden event. By following these basic steps for exchanging information, you are fulfilling your legal obligations and protecting your interests.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate aftermath of a minor road traffic collision in the UK. It does not cover legal defense, complex insurance litigation, or long-term vehicle recovery.

## Important note
The information provided is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional advice. Requirements for reporting and exchanging details are governed by Section 170 of the Road Traffic Act 1988. Always consult your insurance policy documents for specific reporting obligations.
