# What to do if… your porch, balcony, or deck feels like it is pulling away from the building (UK)

## Short answer
Evacuate the structure immediately, block all access points to it, and keep everyone away from the area underneath. If you see active movement or falling debris, move to a safe distance and call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stand on the structure or use it "one last time" to retrieve items.
- Do not attempt to test the stability by jumping, pushing, or adding weight.
- Do not attempt to use props, jacks, or DIY fixings to "hold it up" yourself.
- Do not allow anyone to walk or park vehicles in the area directly beneath the structure.
- Do not stand near windows or glass doors that are directly below or adjacent to the connection point.
- Do not ignore sudden loud cracks, widening gaps, or a "spongy" feeling underfoot.
- Do not use electrical switches or naked flames if you also smell gas near the separation point.

## What to do now
1. **Move everyone off the structure and away from the immediate area.** Calmly direct all people and pets indoors or to a safe distance where they are not at risk from a sudden collapse or falling debris.
2. **Lock and block the door leading to the porch, balcony, or deck.** Use a physical barrier such as a heavy chair or tape a large "DANGER: DO NOT USE" sign over the handle and at eye level to prevent accidental access.
3. **Establish a exclusion zone on the ground level below the structure.** Cordon off the area with tape, chairs, or bins to ensure no one walks underneath or near the "drop zone" where materials could fall.
4. **Identify immediate danger signals from a safe, distant vantage point.** Look for indicators such as a gap wider than 10mm, fresh masonry cracks at the wall connection, leaning support posts, or visible tilting of the platform.
   - If the structure is actively shifting or pieces are falling, **call 999 immediately**.
5. **Report the hazard to the relevant local building authority.**
   - In England and Wales: Contact your **Local Authority Building Control (LABC)** and ask for the "Dangerous Structures" department.
   - In Scotland: Contact the **Local Authority Building Standards** "Dangerous Buildings" service.
   - In Northern Ireland: Contact your **Local District Council Building Control** department.
6. **Alert your landlord or property manager if you do not own the building.** Notify them via phone immediately to report that the structure is unsafe for use, then follow up with a brief written confirmation once the situation is stable.
7. **Document the damage from a safe distance only.** Take clear photographs or video through a window or from the ground, ensuring you do not enter the exclusion zone or stand under the structure.
8. **Contact emergency utility lines if you suspect pipe or cable damage.**
   - If you smell gas in Great Britain: Call the **National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999**.
   - If you smell gas in Northern Ireland: Call the **Northern Ireland Gas Emergency Service on 0800 002 001**.
   - For damaged electrical lines or equipment: Call **105** to reach your local network operator.
9. **Request a professional assessment from a structural engineer.** Once the immediate area is secured, arrange for a specialist to inspect the ledger board, fixings, and structural integrity of the building’s connection.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause of the failure or who is liable right now.
- You do not need to obtain repair quotes or hire a builder until a structural engineer has assessed the damage.
- You do not need to file an insurance claim in the first few minutes; safety and site securing are the priorities.
- You do not need to worry about permanent repairs or "making it look right" while the risk of collapse exists.

## Important reassurance
If you have noticed a change in how the structure feels or looks, treating it as a failure-in-progress is the safest response. Many structural collapses occur after warning signs were dismissed as "minor" or "settling." Closing the area and creating distance effectively removes the immediate risk to life.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and risk reduction for property-related structural concerns. It does not provide engineering solutions or definitive safety clearances.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional engineering, legal, or medical advice. If there is an immediate risk of collapse, fire, or gas leak, contact emergency services and evacuate the area.
