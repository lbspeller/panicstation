# What to do if… you are asked for a local address for entry and you do not have one you can document (UK)

## Short answer
Do not provide a false or invented address. Identify a single, legitimate first-night address you can document, such as a confirmed hotel booking, and ensure this address is used consistently across all digital and physical forms.

## Do not do these things
- Do not invent a random local address or use one found online that you cannot verify.
- Do not provide the address of a business or person you are not actually visiting.
- Do not pay unofficial third parties who offer to provide a fake address or "fix" your paperwork.
- Do not cancel a booking you are using as your entry documentation until you have cleared immigration.
- Do not offer conflicting addresses on different forms, such as your airline check-in and your arrival card.

## What to do now
1. **Identify exactly which checkpoint is requesting the information.**  
   Determine if the request is for airline Advance Passenger Information (API), a mandatory digital authorization like the UK Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA), or a verbal query from a border officer.
2. **Secure a legitimate first-night booking immediately.**  
   If you have no arrangements, use a mobile device to book a hotel or hostel that provides an instant digital confirmation.
   - Ensure the confirmation shows the full street address and contact number.
   - Choose a booking that allows cancellation later if your plans are flexible, but keep the confirmation active for the duration of your transit.
3. **Download a copy of your documentation for offline use.**  
   Save the booking confirmation as a PDF or take a clear screenshot.
   - Do not rely on airport Wi-Fi or cellular roaming to access your email at the immigration desk.
4. **Synchronize all digital and physical forms.**  
   Enter the exact property name, street address, and postal code from your confirmation into all required fields.
   - This includes the airline’s check-in app and any digital entry permits required for your destination.
5. **Prepare a concise verbal explanation for stay-with-friends scenarios.**  
   If you are staying at a private residence, ensure you have the host's full name, full address, and a contact phone number.
   - If you cannot produce a message or letter from the host, a commercial hotel booking for the first night is generally easier to document at a high-pressure checkpoint.
6. **Check for mandatory digital permissions (ETA).**  
   If you are a non-visa national traveling to or through the UK in 2026, verify that you have an approved Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA).
   - Airlines are legally required to deny boarding if the ETA is missing or if the address provided in the API is flagged as invalid.
7. **Contact the FCDO if you are a British national in a crisis.**  
   If you are being held or denied entry in a foreign jurisdiction and feel unsafe, contact the nearest British embassy or call the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office (FCDO) 24/7.
   - Phone: **+44 (0)20 7008 5000**.
   - Use the "Register Your Presence" service if you are in a region currently affected by a designated crisis.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide an address for every night of your trip if only a first-night address is requested.
- You do not need to finalize your full travel itinerary or onward transportation beyond the first night.
- You do not need to resolve long-term accommodation questions while standing at the check-in desk.

## Important reassurance
Administrative delays regarding address documentation are common, especially for travelers with flexible itineraries. Establishing one verifiable point of contact for your first 24 hours is a standard procedure that usually resolves the immediate block and allows you to proceed with your journey.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for travelers facing immediate documentation requests at checkpoints. It does not replace specific visa requirements or the legal entry criteria of individual nations.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or immigration advice. Entry requirements are subject to change by national governments and individual carrier policies. Border officials have the final authority to grant or refuse entry based on local laws.
