# What to do if… airport Wi-Fi requires a local phone number to connect and you need access to travel documents (UK)

## Short answer
Prioritize airline desk assistance to retrieve or print documents while using localized hardware fallbacks like passport-scanning Wi-Fi kiosks or offline app caches.

## Do not do these things
- Do not enter passport numbers, credit card details, or login credentials on a network until you have verified the exact SSID name with official airport staff.
- Do not accept "hotspot" help or SMS code relaying from unauthorized individuals or strangers in the terminal.
- Do not pay for third-party "Wi-Fi unlocker" apps or services that appear in search results or pop-ups.
- Do not repeatedly attempt to log in to locked accounts if you lack a stable, secure connection, as this may trigger a security lockout.
- Do not assume that all airport kiosks are free; some budget carriers now mandate digital-only boarding and charge for airport reprints.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific document required for the next checkpoint.**
   - Determine if you need a Booking Reference (PNR), Boarding Pass QR code, Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA), or Visa PDF.
2. **Search for physical Wi-Fi kiosks in the terminal.**
   - Look for "Internet Kiosks" or "Wi-Fi Vouchers" where you can scan your passport page to receive a printed access code, bypassing the need for a local SMS.
3. **Check the airline app for an offline cache.**
   - Open your airline app; many (including British Airways and EasyJet) are designed to display the most recently viewed boarding pass even without an active data connection.
   - Check the "Apple Wallet" or "Google Wallet" apps, as documents saved there do not require internet for retrieval.
4. **Proceed directly to the airline check-in or service desk.**
   - Inform the staff that you cannot access the local Wi-Fi and require a printed boarding pass or itinerary.
   - Be aware that some carriers (e.g., Ryanair) may charge a fee for this service unless your phone is technically non-functional.
5. **Verify the network identity before connecting any device.**
   - Locate official airport signage or ask information desk staff for the exact, official Wi-Fi SSID to avoid "Evil Twin" hotspots.
   - If using a VPN, you must typically disable it to pass the initial "Captive Portal" sign-in page, then immediately re-enable it before opening email or airline apps.
6. **Utilize a 2026 connectivity fallback.**
   - If your device supports it, download a travel eSIM using a brief roaming connection or a friend's hotspot to gain independent data access.
   - If traveling to the UK, use the "UK ETA" app if you have already applied; it may have status information stored locally.
7. **Create a manual "backup note" if you gain brief access.**
   - Once connected, take screenshots of all QR codes and references.
   - Write down the PNR, flight number, and gate information on paper or in a local "Notes" app that does not require the cloud.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve your long-term roaming or SIM card issues while standing in the check-in line.
- You do not need to troubleshoot the airport's specific network configuration once you have your physical or cached documents.
- You do not need to update your travel apps or device software until you reach a secure, private network.

## Important reassurance
It is a documented issue that many international transit hubs use local-only SMS verification for Wi-Fi. Airline and airport staff deal with this daily and have established protocols to verify your identity and travel status using just your physical passport at the desk.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for accessing digital travel documents in an airport setting. It does not cover long-term cybersecurity recovery or legal disputes regarding airline reprint fees.

## Important note
This information reflects standard airport and airline procedures as of early 2026. Security protocols regarding public Wi-Fi and digital travel credentials (like ETAs) are subject to change. Always prioritize instructions from uniformed airport staff or official government border officials.
