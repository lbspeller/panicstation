# What to do if… you are trapped in a car park because the exit barrier, gate, or bollard will not open (UK)

## Short answer
Stop somewhere safe, do not force the equipment, and use the car park’s help point, intercom, or posted phone number to ask the operator to release the exit. If there is immediate danger, such as fire, a medical emergency, violence, or a threat to life, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive into the barrier, gate, or bollard, or try to push it with your car.
- Do not lift, force, dismantle, or wedge the equipment yourself.
- Do not tailgate another vehicle through a small gap.
- Do not reverse suddenly or unpredictably; pedestrians or cars may be behind you.
- Do not stand in the exit lane or walk around moving traffic to look for help.
- Do not keep the engine idling for long in an enclosed car park if you are safely stopped.
- Do not call 999 only because you are delayed; use it for immediate danger.

## What to do now
1. **Make the exit area safe.** If you can, move out of the exit lane into a marked bay or safe waiting area, put your hazard lights on, and leave space for other vehicles and pedestrians.
2. **Use the official help point.** Press the intercom or help button on the barrier, bollard, pay station, or exit machine. If there is a phone number on the sign or machine, call it.
3. **Give the operator the exact problem.** Say which exit you are at, the level or zone if shown, and whether the barrier or gate will not open, the bollard is blocking the exit, your ticket will not scan, your payment has not registered, or the machine has kept your ticket or card.
4. **Ask for a safe release route.** Ask whether they can release the exit remotely, send an attendant, open a different exit, or tell you where to wait safely until staff arrive.
5. **Look for staffed help only if it is safe.** If signs point to an attendant, security desk, reception, or control room, go there only if you can do so without walking through moving traffic or leaving your vehicle in a dangerous place.
6. **Use police routes only when they fit the situation.** If there is fire, smoke, a medical emergency, violence, or an immediate threat to life, move to a safer place if you can and call 999. If you need non-emergency police help, call 101.
7. **Record the fault while you wait, only if safe.** Take photos or video of the stuck equipment, error message, help button, posted number, ticket, receipt, payment screen, or app screen. Note the time you reached the exit and keep your call log.
8. **Leave normally once released.** Keep your ticket, receipt, app record, photos, and call log. If you later receive an overstay notice or parking charge, that evidence can help explain that the delay was caused by exit equipment failure.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, request a refund, or challenge a charge.
- You do not need to argue with other drivers or prove fault at the barrier.
- You do not need to write an appeal while you are still stuck; collect safe evidence first and deal with paperwork later.
- You do not need to decide who is legally responsible for the delay at the exit.

## Important reassurance
A stuck exit is stressful, but the safest next step is simple: stay visible, avoid forcing anything, and contact the car park operator through the official help route. Turning a delay into damage, injury, or a dispute is the main thing to avoid.

## Scope note
These are first steps to help you get out safely and reduce avoidable problems. Later questions about a refund, complaint, parking charge, or penalty notice may need the car park operator, the issuer of the notice, an appeal service, or specialist advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you later receive a parking notice, follow the instructions and deadlines on that notice and use your evidence. For private parking charges, you usually appeal to the operator first; if rejected, the independent appeal route depends on the operator’s trade association, such as POPLA for British Parking Association Approved Operator Scheme members or the Independent Appeals Service for International Parking Community members. Council-issued penalties follow a different process and the notice should explain how to challenge it.
