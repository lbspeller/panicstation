# What to do if… a lawyer or claims company contacts you right after a death and you are unsure whether to respond (UK)

## Short answer
End the communication immediately without providing any personal information. Record the caller's details and verify their legitimacy through official regulatory registers before deciding whether to engage.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confirm or provide personal data, including your National Insurance number, date of birth, bank details, or copies of the death certificate.
- Do not sign any digital or physical "authority forms," "letters of instruction," or fee agreements during the initial contact.
- Do not click links in unsolicited emails or text messages claiming to lead to "probate portals" or "inheritance documents."
- Do not pay "release fees," "tax deposits," or "admin charges" to unlock a supposed inheritance or life insurance payout.
- Do not allow a caller to pressure you into keeping the contact secret from other family members or executors.
- Do not assume a firm is legitimate because they know the name of the deceased or the date of death; this information is often available via public notices.

## What to do now
1. **Create an immediate safe pause by ending the contact.** Say: "I am not discussing this now. Send your details and the nature of your inquiry in writing by post."
   - Hang up the phone, close the door, or leave the email unanswered.
2. **Document the contact details for later verification.** Write down the following information if available:
   - The name of the individual and the full legal name of the firm.
   - The telephone number or email address used.
   - The specific "hook" used (e.g., a "lost" insurance policy, a motor finance claim, or "undisclosed assets").
3. **Verify the firm through the official Solicitors Register if they claim to be a lawyer.** Search the **Solicitors Regulation Authority (SRA) Register** to confirm the firm and individual are regulated in England and Wales.
   - For firms in Scotland, use the **Law Society of Scotland** "Find a Solicitor" tool.
   - For firms in Northern Ireland, use the **Law Society of Northern Ireland** directory.
4. **Check the FCA Firm Checker if they claim to be a claims management company.** Use the **Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) Firm Checker** tool to see if the business is authorised to conduct "claims management" activity.
5. **Verify any "official" claims by contacting institutions directly.** If the caller claims to be from "the probate office," "HMCTS," or "the court," find the official number for the **Probate Call Centre** via GOV.UK and call them yourself.
6. **Report suspicious or fraudulent contact to Report Fraud.** If you believe you have been targeted by a scam or have already shared sensitive data, contact **Report Fraud** (the UK's national reporting centre) online at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.
7. **Contact Police Scotland on 101 if you are in Scotland.** Fraud reporting in Scotland is handled directly by the police rather than the central Report Fraud service.
8. **Register with the Telephone Preference Service (TPS) to reduce legitimate nuisance calls.** While this does not stop illegal scammers, it helps filter out unsolicited marketing from legitimate UK businesses.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on legal representation or a claims representative today.
- You do not need to respond to "urgent" deadlines mentioned by unsolicited callers; probate and inheritance processes generally take months, not hours.
- You do not need to justify your silence or your decision to hang up.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed or "frozen" when contacted by professionals immediately after a loss. These firms often rely on this vulnerability to secure instructions. Taking days or weeks to verify a firm's identity is a standard and protective measure, not a sign of being difficult.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and verification only. It does not provide legal advice on the validity of any specific claim or the necessity of probate.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Regulatory statuses can change; always use official .gov.uk or regulator-hosted search tools for the most current information.
