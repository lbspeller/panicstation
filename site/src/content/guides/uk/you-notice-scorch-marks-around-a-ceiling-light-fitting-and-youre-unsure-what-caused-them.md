# What to do if… you notice scorch marks around a ceiling light fitting and you’re unsure what caused them (UK)

## Short answer
Stop using the light immediately and switch off the power to that circuit at the fuse box / consumer unit. If you see smoke, smell strong burning, or hear crackling, evacuate everyone and call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don't turn the light on again to "test" it or see if it still works.
- Don't touch the fitting, the bulb, or the scorched area of the ceiling with your bare hands.
- Don't attempt to unscrew the fitting or investigate the internal wiring yourself.
- Don't ignore the marks even if the light appears to be functioning normally.
- Don't cover the area with tape, paint, or furniture to hide the damage.
- Don't climb a ladder to get a closer look if you smell burning or see smoke.

## What to do now
1. **Alert everyone in the property immediately that there is a potential electrical hazard.**
2. **Perform a sensory check from a distance without touching the fitting:** look for **active smoke**, **visible sparks**, or **glowing**; listen for **buzzing or crackling**; and check for a **strong smell of fish or burning plastic**.
3. **If there is active smoke, fire, or a loud crackling sound:**
   - **Evacuate the building immediately** and ensure everyone is accounted for.
   - **Call 999** once you are outside and request the **Fire Service**.
   - **Do not re-enter** the building until a fire officer tells you it is safe.
4. **If there is no immediate fire or smoke, isolate the power:**
   - **Turn off the wall switch** for the affected light and leave it off.
   - **Go to your fuse box / consumer unit** and switch off the circuit labeled "Lights" or the specific circuit for that room.
   - **If you are unsure which switch to use**, turn off the **Main Switch** to isolate power to the entire property, provided this does not disable life-critical medical equipment.
5. **Clear the area surrounding the light fitting:** move any flammable materials such as curtains, clothing, or paper away from the area directly below or adjacent to the light.
6. **Arrange a professional inspection by a qualified electrician:**
   - **If you rent your home:** contact your landlord or letting agent immediately. State that you have "visible scorch marks and a potential fire hazard at a light fitting." In the UK, landlords have a legal duty to ensure electrical installations are safe and must act on "potentially dangerous" (C2) or "immediately dangerous" (C1) hazards.
   - **If you own your home:** find a registered electrician via the **Registered Competent Person Electrical** search or a trade body like **NICEIC**, **NAPIT**, or **SELECT** (in Scotland).
7. **Document the damage from the floor:** take photos of the scorch marks from a safe distance to show the electrician or for insurance purposes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the technical cause (such as a loose neutral, over-wattage bulb, or failed transformer) before calling for help.
- You do not need to clean the soot or repair the ceiling until an electrician has certified the circuit as safe.
- You do not need to contact your buildings insurance provider until the immediate hazard is stabilized and an electrician has provided a report.

## Important reassurance
Noticing these marks early is a successful safety catch. Scorch marks are a warning sign that the system is successfully indicating a fault before a full ignition occurs. Isolating the power is the most effective way to stop the heat source immediately.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying and isolating a potential electrical fire hazard. It does not replace a professional Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR) or a physical inspection by a qualified professional.

## Important note
Electrical faults are a leading cause of domestic fires in the UK. If you have any doubt about the safety of your electrical system, treat it as an emergency. All electrical remedial work must be carried out by a person competent to do so, typically a registered electrician.
