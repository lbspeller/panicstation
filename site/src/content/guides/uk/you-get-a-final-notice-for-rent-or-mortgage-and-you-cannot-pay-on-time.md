# What to do if… you get a final notice for rent or mortgage and you cannot pay on time (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your landlord or mortgage lender in writing immediately to state what you can pay and request a payment plan while seeking urgent advice from a specialist housing or debt service.

## Do not do these things
- Do not move out before receiving legal advice, as voluntary departure can reduce your eligibility for local authority housing assistance.
- Do not assume the "vacate by" date on a landlord's notice is the date you must leave; a court order and bailiff warrant are usually required for legal eviction.
- Do not ignore court forms or hearing dates, as this allows the landlord or lender to obtain a possession order in your absence.
- Do not agree to a repayment schedule that exceeds your realistic budget; an unsustainable plan leads to immediate further default.
- Do not use high-cost, short-term credit or unregulated lenders to pay arrears unless you have a verified way to repay without further debt.

## What to do now
1. **Identify your specific jurisdiction and the exact name of the notice received.**  
   **The legal process depends entirely on which part of the UK you are in.**
   - England: Look for a "Section 8" notice (Section 21 was abolished for new notices on 1 May 2026).
   - Wales: Look for a "Section 173" (no-fault) or "Section 157" (breach) notice under an Occupation Contract.
   - Scotland: Look for a "Notice to Leave" for Private Residential Tenancies.
   - Northern Ireland: Look for a "Notice to Quit" (notice periods vary from 8 weeks to 7 months as of 2026).

2. **Send a written response to the landlord or lender today and retain a dated copy.**  
   **State clearly that you have received the notice, confirm your intention to pay what you can, and ask for a temporary pause in escalation.**
   - Include the specific amount you can pay today, even if it is only a partial payment.
   - Request that all future communication regarding the debt be in writing.

3. **Request a 'Breathing Space' if you are in England or Wales.**  
   **Contact a debt adviser to apply for the Debt Respite Scheme (Breathing Space).**
   - This provides up to 60 days of legal protection from creditor action and freezes interest and charges.
   - You must still prioritize ongoing rent or mortgage payments during this period to prevent new arrears.

4. **Access means-free legal help if you have received a possession notice.**  
   **Use the Housing Loss Prevention Advice Service (HLPAS) in England and Wales.**
   - This service provides free legal advice and representation at court regardless of your income or savings.
   - You qualify as soon as you have written notice that someone is seeking possession of your home.

5. **Apply for a Discretionary Housing Payment (DHP) if you receive benefits.**  
   **Contact your local council to request an application for DHP to cover rent shortfalls.**
   - You must already be receiving Housing Benefit or the housing element of Universal Credit to be eligible.
   - Provide evidence of the final notice and your current financial hardship.

6. **Exercise your rights under the Mortgage Charter if you have a home loan.**  
   **Ask your lender for Charter-specific support options if they are a signatory.**
   - Options may include switching to interest-only payments for 6 months or extending your mortgage term.
   - These specific Charter options do not require a new affordability assessment and should not impact your credit score.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to sell the property or find a new rental today.
- You do not need to provide a full long-term financial audit in your first message to the landlord or lender.
- You do not need to respond to informal threats or verbal pressure to leave if the formal legal process has not been completed.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a final notice is a procedural step in a legal timeline, not an immediate eviction. There are established support systems designed to stabilize housing during financial crises. Communicating in writing and involving a professional adviser early frequently prevents the loss of a home.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for housing payment crises in the UK. It does not cover specific legal defenses for court hearings or long-term debt insolvency procedures.

## Important note
This information is not legal or financial advice. Housing laws differ significantly between England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. If you are facing immediate eviction by bailiffs or have an upcoming court hearing, contact Shelter, Citizens Advice, or a solicitor immediately.
