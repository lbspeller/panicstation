# What to do if… you experience repeated power surges or flickering lights and worry about electrical damage (UK)

## Short answer
Treat repeated brief surges or flickering lights as a safety warning: unplug sensitive electronics, reduce electrical load, and be ready to isolate the supply if there is heat, smoke, sparking, crackling, buzzing, scorching, or a burning smell.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep testing appliances by turning them on and off during ongoing surges.
- Do not open your consumer unit, remove covers, or touch exposed wiring.
- Do not ignore burning or plastic smells, buzzing, crackling, hot plugs, hot sockets, scorch marks, smoke, or sparks.
- Do not use damaged extension leads, damaged power strips, or surge protectors showing a failed protection indicator.
- Do not run high-draw appliances such as a kettle, tumble dryer, oven, or electric heater while the power is fluctuating.
- Do not assume it is definitely the network supply or definitely your wiring before it has been checked.
- Do not use water on an electrical fire.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger first.** If you notice smoke, sparks, a burning smell, crackling, buzzing, heat at a socket, or visible scorching, switch off power at the main switch in the consumer unit if it is safe to reach. If there is fire, smoke spreading, or you cannot safely isolate power, leave, stay out, and call 999.
2. **Unplug sensitive and valuable electronics.** Switch off at the wall first if you can do that safely, then unplug computers, TVs, consoles, routers, chargers, smart devices, and other non-essential electronics. Leave essential items such as a fridge or freezer only if they are not behaving dangerously.
3. **Reduce electrical load.** Turn off non-essential appliances and avoid high-power items until the surges stop or the supply has been checked. This reduces stress on the installation if there is a loose connection, voltage problem, or overheating accessory.
4. **Check whether it is only your home.** Look for obvious signs without touching anything unsafe: are neighbours’ lights flickering too, are communal lights affected, or is it only your sockets and lights? This helps you report the problem clearly.
5. **Report a possible supply problem to the electricity network operator.** In England, Scotland, or Wales, call 105 if the situation feels unsafe, urgent, or you are unsure. In Northern Ireland, contact NIE Networks on 03457 643 643 for power supply faults or hazards.
6. **If it seems limited to your home, arrange a registered electrician.** Use a registration route that applies to your UK nation. Ask them to check for loose connections, overheating sockets or switches, consumer unit issues, earthing or bonding problems, and whether surge protection is appropriate for your installation.
7. **If you rent, tell the landlord or agent promptly in writing.** Say there are repeated voltage fluctuations, flickering lights, or possible surges, and include any warning signs such as heat, smell, scorch marks, or tripping. Ask for an urgent electrical safety check rather than an informal patch.
8. **Make a brief incident log.** Note the date and time, what happened, which lights or appliances were affected, what was running, whether neighbours were affected, and any visible damage. Photograph scorch marks or damaged equipment only if it is safe and you do not need to touch it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether the property needs rewiring.
- You do not need to replace major appliances until the supply or wiring issue has been checked and the surges have stopped.
- You do not need to buy new surge protectors immediately unless one is damaged or showing a failed protection indicator.
- Insurance questions can wait until immediate safety is handled and you know whether anything is actually damaged.

## Important reassurance
You are not overreacting by treating repeated surges, flickering lights, or hot electrical fittings as a safety issue. The useful first steps are simple: reduce risk, protect equipment, record what happened, and get the right network operator or electrician involved.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety and preventing avoidable damage. Finding the exact cause may need the electricity network operator, a registered electrician, your landlord, or your insurer later.

## Important note
This is general information for a stressful situation, not legal, electrical, insurance, fire-safety, or other professional advice. If there are signs of fire or immediate danger, leave if needed and call 999.
