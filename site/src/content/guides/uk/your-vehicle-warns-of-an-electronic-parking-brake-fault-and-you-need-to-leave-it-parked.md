# What to do if… your vehicle warns of an electronic parking brake fault and you need to leave it parked (UK)

## Short answer
Get to firm, level ground if you can do that safely and normal braking feels normal. Before you get out, make sure there is more than one thing helping to hold the vehicle: Park or a gear, wheels turned, and chocks if available.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the vehicle is safely held just because the EPB light is on or you heard it try to apply.
- Don’t leave it on a slope if you are not confident it cannot roll.
- Don’t keep repeatedly toggling the EPB while the vehicle is not secured.
- Don’t crawl underneath the vehicle or attempt a roadside mechanical fix.
- Don’t do any test that could let the vehicle roll towards people, traffic, or property.
- Don’t ask someone to stand in front of or behind the vehicle to “check” it.

## What to do now
1. **Keep your foot on the brake and choose the safest place to stop.**  
   If the warning is only about the parking brake, normal braking feels normal, and it is safe to move, go to firm, level ground such as a flat car park bay or flat side road before you switch off. If the main brake warning is red, the brake pedal feels wrong, or you are unsure, stop as safely as you can and call recovery.

2. **Set a transmission backup before you release the footbrake.**  
   - **Automatic:** keep the footbrake pressed and select **P**.
   - **Manual:** keep the footbrake pressed and select **1st gear** if facing uphill, or **reverse** if facing downhill.

3. **Try the parking brake once, if the dashboard or manual does not say otherwise.**  
   If it appears to apply, do not rely on it alone. If the vehicle says the EPB has a fault, treat Park or gear, wheel position, and chocks as the things buying you time.

4. **If you are on a hill, turn the wheels the Highway Code way.**  
   - **Facing uphill:** turn the wheels **away from the kerb**.
   - **Facing downhill:** turn the wheels **towards the kerb**.  
   This reduces the chance of the vehicle rolling into the road if it moves.

5. **Chock a wheel if you can do it safely.**  
   Proper wheel chocks are best. Put the chock firmly against a tyre on the downhill side of the wheel. If you do not have chocks, only use a solid, stable object if it will not slide or break, and only if you can place it without putting any part of yourself in the vehicle’s possible path.

6. **Check stillness only while seated and able to stop immediately.**  
   With Park or gear selected, wheels turned, and any chock placed, gently ease pressure off the footbrake just enough to see whether the vehicle stays still. If it moves at all, press the footbrake again and do not leave it unattended.

7. **If you cannot make it secure, stay with it and get help.**  
   Call your breakdown provider or manufacturer assistance and say: “Electronic parking brake fault; I’m not confident the vehicle will hold when parked.” If it could roll into traffic, block the road, or create immediate danger, call **999**. For a non-emergency road safety concern, contact the police on **101** or online.

8. **Only after it is not at risk of rolling, reduce the chance someone else unknowingly moves it.**  
   If you have to step away briefly to meet recovery or speak to help, leave a clear visible note: “Electronic parking brake fault. Do not move unless secured first.”

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to diagnose the fault now.
- You do **not** need to choose a garage, repair route, or complaint route now.
- You do **not** need to search menus, reset systems, or keep cycling the ignition while the vehicle is unsecured.
- Costs, warranty questions, and longer-term repair decisions can wait until the vehicle is safely secured.

## Important reassurance
This warning is alarming because it affects a basic safety expectation: the vehicle staying put. Taking a minute to add simple backups is a sensible response, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent a roll-away and buy time. Model-specific emergency guidance varies, so later decisions may need the owner’s manual, breakdown assistance, or a qualified mechanic.

## Important note
This is general information, not mechanical, legal, financial, or professional advice. Vehicle systems vary by make, model, and year; follow your owner’s manual and use professional roadside assistance if you cannot secure the vehicle confidently.
