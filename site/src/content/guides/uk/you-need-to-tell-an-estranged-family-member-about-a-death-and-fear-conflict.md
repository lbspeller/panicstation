# What to do if… you need to tell an estranged family member about a death and fear conflict (UK)

## Short answer
Tell them in the safest, lowest-contact way first, usually a short written message. Do not put yourself in a situation where you can be pressured, trapped, or drawn into an argument.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t meet them in person “just to get it over with” if you’re worried it could escalate.
- Don’t make the first contact while you’re exhausted, angry, driving, at work, or in the middle of funeral, hospital, or registrar tasks.
- Don’t include blame, family history, or explanations about who did what.
- Don’t share your address, where you’re staying, or your exact movements if you feel unsafe.
- Don’t commit to funeral arrangements, money, possessions, or inheritance conversations in the first contact.
- Don’t answer accusations, demands, or long messages just because they arrive immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Pick the safest channel.**  
   Use a short text message or email if you can. If direct contact does not feel safe, use a letter, or ask a trusted person to pass on only the basic message. Avoid a phone call if calls with them usually become arguments.

2. **Write a one-screen message and stop there.**  
   Keep it factual and contained. For example:
   - “I’m letting you know that [Name] died on [date].”
   - “I can’t talk in depth right now.”
   - “If you want practical updates, reply by text or email.”

   Do not include the funeral location or timing unless you have decided it is safe and appropriate to share.

3. **Put one clear boundary in the same message.**  
   For example: “Please don’t call. I’ll read messages and reply when I can.”  
   Or: “If messages become abusive, I’ll stop responding.”

4. **Put a buffer between you and the reaction.**  
   After sending, mute the conversation, put your phone on Do Not Disturb, or ask someone you trust to sit with you or hold your phone for a short while. The aim is to stop you replying under pressure.

5. **If you think they may come to your home, make a simple safety plan.**  
   Don’t be alone if you are worried about a visit. Keep doors locked, don’t open the door to anyone you are not expecting, and stay with someone else if that feels safer tonight.

6. **Choose one contact person if possible.**  
   Decide whether you, or someone you trust, will handle any replies. This reduces the chance of several relatives being pulled into separate arguments.

7. **Save hostile messages and act on threats.**  
   Keep screenshots, texts, emails, and voicemails if things turn hostile. If you need to block them, block them. If someone is in immediate danger or a crime is happening, call 999. For non-emergency police contact, use 101 or an online police reporting route.

8. **Keep death admin separate from the conflict.**  
   You do not need to settle official tasks in the same message to the estranged family member. If you are also handling UK death admin, use official guidance separately. Tell Us Once can usually be used if the person who died was living in England, Scotland, or Wales. It cannot be used if the person was living in Northern Ireland when they died; use Northern Ireland guidance instead.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who attends the funeral, who speaks, or who is “allowed.”
- You do not need to respond immediately to accusations, demands, or long messages.
- You do not need to discuss the will, possessions, money, or “who gets what” now.
- You do not need to justify your relationship with the person who died.
- You do not need to make one message solve the whole family history.

## Important reassurance
Worrying about conflict does not mean you are doing anything wrong. A brief, bounded notification can be a careful and responsible choice during a fragile moment.

## Scope note
These are first steps to notify someone while reducing risk and avoiding irreversible blow-ups. Later decisions about funeral arrangements, estate questions, harassment, or ongoing contact may need help from a solicitor, police, bereavement service, mediator, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel in danger or are being threatened, prioritise immediate safety and contact emergency services.
