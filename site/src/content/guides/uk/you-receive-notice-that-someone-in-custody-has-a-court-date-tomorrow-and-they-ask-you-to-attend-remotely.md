# What to do if… you receive notice that someone in custody has a court date tomorrow and they ask you to attend remotely (UK)

## Short answer
Independently verify the hearing details via the official Find a Court or Tribunal service and email the court office immediately to request remote access as an observer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links or download apps sent via unofficial message threads or third-party texts.
- Do not record, photograph, or screenshot any part of the hearing, as this is a criminal offence and contempt of court.
- Do not share the joining link, meeting ID, or passcode with anyone else.
- Do not attempt to speak or interrupt during the proceedings unless specifically addressed by the judge.
- Do not send money or credit to any individual claiming it is required to "activate" a court video link.
- Do not assume access is guaranteed: the judge may refuse remote observation if it is not in the interests of justice or if the hearing is private.

## What to do now
1. **Gather the specific hearing details from the person in custody or their solicitor.**
   - Full legal name and date of birth of the person in custody.
   - The specific court name and location (e.g., Manchester Magistrates' Court).
   - The scheduled time and the type of hearing (e.g., first appearance or remand).
   - The name of the solicitor's firm representing them, if applicable.

2. **Verify the hearing through official HMCTS channels.**
   - Use the **Find a Court or Tribunal** service on GOV.UK to find the verified email address and phone number for that specific court.
   - Cross-reference the person's name with the **public court lists** available online to confirm the case is listed for tomorrow.

3. **Email the court office immediately to request remote access.**
   - Subject line: "Urgent: Remote Observation Request - [Name of Person] - [Date/Time of Hearing]".
   - Include your **full name, email address, home address**, and your relationship to the person in custody.
   - State clearly that you wish to **observe the hearing remotely** and explain why you cannot attend in person.

4. **Confirm which platform the court is using for the hearing.**
   - Most UK courts now use the **Video Hearings (VH) service** or **Cloud Video Platform (CVP)**.
   - Check if you need to create a **VH service account** or perform a mandatory **pre-hearing tech check** via the link the court provides.

5. **Prepare your equipment and environment for the hearing.**
   - **Use a reliable device with a working camera and microphone.**
   - **Complete the platform's self-test or pre-hearing check** to ensure your internet speed and hardware are compatible.
   - **Find a quiet, private space** where you will not be interrupted or overheard, as if you were sitting in a physical courtroom.

6. **Join the hearing early and follow the formal rules of conduct.**
   - **Log in at least 15 minutes before the scheduled start time.**
   - **Keep your microphone muted and your camera off** unless the court staff or judge instructs otherwise.
   - **Have a valid form of photo ID ready** to show the camera if the court clerk needs to verify your identity.

7. **Take alternative action if remote access is denied or fails.**
   - **Contact the court office by phone** if you have not received a link 30 minutes before the start time.
   - **Attend the court building in person** if remote access is refused and the hearing is open to the public.

## What can wait
- You do not need to obtain a full case history or copies of the evidence tonight.
- You do not need to decide on long-term legal representation or funding before the initial hearing.
- You do not need to contact the prison about visiting or property until the custody status is confirmed at the hearing.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel overwhelmed when a hearing is scheduled at short notice. Courts have established protocols for remote access, and while the process is formal, court staff are accustomed to assisting family members and observers. Following the official verification steps ensures you are accessing a legitimate hearing.

## Scope note
This guide is for initial stabilization and remote attendance procedures only. It does not provide legal advice, representation, or instructions on how to participate as a witness or defendant.

## Important note
Remote observation is permitted at the discretion of the judge and is subject to the Courts Act 2003 and the Remote Observation and Recording Regulations. Unauthorised recording or transmission of court proceedings can result in imprisonment for up to two years.
