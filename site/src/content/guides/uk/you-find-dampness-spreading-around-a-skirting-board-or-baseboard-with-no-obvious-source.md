# What to do if… you find dampness spreading around a skirting board or baseboard with no obvious source (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the water supply at the internal stopcock and check for movement on your water meter to determine if there is an active pipe leak. Avoid all electrical sockets in the damp area and notify your landlord or a plumber immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t touch or use electrical sockets, switches, or appliances located on or near the damp wall.
- Don’t attempt to "seal" the dampness with waterproof paint or covers, as this can force moisture further into the wall structure.
- Don’t use high heat to dry the area if the source is not yet stopped, as this can encourage mould growth.
- Don’t pull away skirting boards or lift flooring if you are unsure of the location of hidden electrical cables or gas pipes.
- Don’t ignore a small patch if it is visibly growing or the wall feels soft to the touch.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate hazards and isolate power to the affected area.** If the dampness is near sockets, cabling, or the consumer unit, **turn off the relevant circuit at the consumer unit immediately.**
   - Do not reset a tripped RCD (Residual Current Device) if you suspect moisture has entered the circuit.
   - Keep children and pets away from the wet area.

2. **Conduct an active leak test using your water meter if available.** Stop using all water in the property (taps, showers, toilets, and appliances) and **check if the small dial or digits on your water meter are moving.**
   - Take a reading, wait 30 minutes without using any water, and check the reading again; any change indicates a leak.

3. **Close the internal stopcock to stop the flow of water.** Turn the valve **firmly clockwise** until it stops to shut off the mains water supply.
   - Internal stopcocks are typically found under the kitchen sink, under the stairs, in a downstairs cloakroom, or near the front door.
   - If the stopcock is seized, do not force it with tools; locate the external shut-off valve in the street or call an emergency plumber.

4. **Observe the area for specific sensory cues that indicate the source.** Look for **bubbling paint, a tide mark of salt (efflorescence), or a dark "wet" stain** that changes when the stopcock is off.
   - Check the other side of the wall and the floor above for leaking radiator valves, shower trays, or waste pipes.
   - If the wall is external, check for blocked gutters or downpipes directly outside the damp spot.

5. **Contact your landlord or a qualified plumber to report the emergency.** If you are a tenant, **notify your landlord in writing immediately** and mention the damp is spreading or near electrics to trigger urgent repair duties.
   - Under the Renters’ Rights Act 2026 and Awaab's Law, landlords must investigate significant damp and mould hazards within fixed, urgent timeframes.
   - If you are an owner-occupier, contact an emergency plumber or your buildings insurance 24-hour helpline.

6. **Document the spread with photos and a timeline.** Take clear photos of the **damp patch with a ruler or coin for scale** and note the time you turned off the water.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify if the issue is "rising damp" or "penetrating damp" before calling for professional help.
- You do not need to purchase or use a dehumidifier until the primary leak has been identified and stopped.
- You do not need to decide on permanent redecoration or plaster replacement in the first 24 hours.

## Important reassurance
It is common for water to travel horizontally along joists or behind plasterboard before appearing at a skirting board. Finding moisture at the floor line does not automatically mean a total floor failure; identifying the source and stopping the flow is the standard first step to preventing structural damage.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for mystery dampness at floor level. It does not provide long-term mould remediation or structural damp-proofing advice.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not replace a professional inspection by a plumber or electrician. If moisture has entered electrical fittings, do not attempt to dry or test them yourself.
