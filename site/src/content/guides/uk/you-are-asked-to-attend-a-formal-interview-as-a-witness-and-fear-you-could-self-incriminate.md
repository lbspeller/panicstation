# What to do if… you are asked to attend a formal interview as a witness and fear you could self-incriminate (UK)

## Short answer
Do not attend, answer questions, or give a statement until you have spoken to a criminal defence solicitor. Ask for the request in writing and ask whether you are being treated as a witness, a suspect, or someone who may be cautioned.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go in alone to “clear it up” if you think your answers could incriminate you.
- Do not give an informal account, “off the record” chat, phone account, or written statement before legal advice.
- Do not guess, fill gaps, or agree with suggestions just to be helpful.
- Do not agree on the spot to searches, device access, passwords, or handing over documents without asking for a solicitor first.
- Do not delete messages, call logs, photos, notes, or files after official contact.
- Do not argue about the facts with the interviewer, officer, or investigator. Keep contact about interview details and legal advice.

## What to do now
1. **Pause before you answer anything about the facts.**  
   You can say: “I want legal advice before I answer questions or give a statement. Please send the interview details in writing.”

2. **Ask for the basic details in writing.**  
   Ask for:
   - the interviewer’s name, role, organisation, and contact details,
   - where and when they want you to attend,
   - whether the interview is voluntary,
   - the general topic or suspected offence,
   - whether you are being treated as a witness, a suspect, or someone who may be cautioned.

3. **Ask the key question plainly.**  
   Say: “Am I being treated as a suspect, and will I be cautioned?”  
   If the answer is unclear, treat it as higher risk and do not discuss the facts until a solicitor has advised you.

4. **Get a solicitor who deals with criminal interviews in the place where the interview will happen.**  
   Police and official interview rules are not identical across the UK or across all interviewing bodies. Tell the solicitor who contacted you, where the interview will be, and whether it may be under caution.

5. **Ask how legal advice will be arranged before the interview.**  
   If it is at a police station or under caution, ask whether free independent legal advice is available and how to request it. Do not attend or answer questions until the solicitor has explained the safest route.

6. **If an interview is already booked, ask for it to be delayed until legal advice is in place.**  
   Use this line: “I am not refusing to cooperate, but I will not attend or answer questions until I have had legal advice and know whether I am being cautioned.”

7. **If you are already at the interview location, ask before it starts:**  
   “Am I under arrest, or am I free to leave?”  
   Then ask: “Can I speak privately to a solicitor before any questions?”  
   If the answer is unclear, stop discussing the facts and keep asking for legal advice.

8. **Make a short record for your solicitor only.**  
   Write down:
   - who contacted you, when, and how,
   - what they said the interview was about,
   - any date, time, location, reference number, or deadline,
   - what they asked you to bring.  
   Keep it factual. Do not add guesses or explanations.

9. **Keep support messages very limited.**  
   If you need to tell someone, say only: “I have been asked to attend a formal interview. I am getting legal advice.” Do not discuss the facts in texts, group chats, or social media messages.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to explain everything.
- You do not need to prepare a full timeline or statement before speaking to a solicitor.
- You do not need to decide whether you have done something wrong.
- You do not need to hand over devices, documents, or passwords on the spot without legal advice.
- You do not need to rush because an interviewer asks for a quick appointment.

## Important reassurance
It can happen that someone is approached as a witness but still has personal legal risk. Pausing, asking clear questions, and getting a solicitor before answering is a normal way to protect yourself and keep the process calm.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. What happens next depends on where in the UK the interview is taking place, who is interviewing you, whether it is voluntary, whether you are cautioned, and whether you are treated as a witness or suspect; those decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not legal advice or professional advice. If there is any chance your answers could incriminate you, get legal advice before attending, answering questions, or giving a statement.
