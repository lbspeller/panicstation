# What to do if… you are contacted by media or strangers about a family member’s death and want privacy (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and do not engage in real time. Put one person in charge of replies, send one clear “do not contact us” message where needed, then block, report, or complain if contact continues.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t answer questions “just to get it over with”; details can spread quickly and may be hard to pull back.
- Don’t argue, threaten, or negotiate in DMs or comments; it can increase attention and can be screenshot.
- Don’t share documents, addresses, funeral details, private messages, or official letters with anyone who contacted you first.
- Don’t post public updates that add names, timings, locations, schools, workplaces, or family links.
- Don’t assume someone is a journalist because they say they are.
- Don’t hand over photos or a “family statement” unless you truly want it to be published or shared.

## What to do now
1. **Choose one person to handle contact for now.**  
   Pick one trusted person to receive screenshots, voicemails, emails, and letters. Everyone else can mute unknown contacts and stop checking messages for a while.

2. **Use one short boundary message, then stop replying.**  
   Send this once if you need to respond:  
   “Our family is grieving and we do not want publicity. Please do not contact us again, come to our home, or ask other relatives for information.”  
   After that, do not debate or explain.

3. **If someone comes to your home, do not open the door unless you want to.**  
   You can speak through the door, use a doorbell message, or say nothing. If you do speak, repeat the boundary once. If they will not leave, you feel intimidated, or threats are made, consider reporting it as harassment or threatening behaviour.

4. **Make the easiest privacy changes next.**  
   - Set your social media accounts to private.
   - Ask close relatives to make public posts private or remove names, addresses, schools, workplaces, funeral details, and tagged locations.
   - Ask friends not to tag you or post tribute details that identify private family information.
   - Remove public photos that you would not want reused by a news outlet or shared by strangers.

5. **Save evidence without engaging.**  
   Screenshot messages, profiles, posts, timestamps, caller numbers, and voicemails. Keep a simple note of dates, times, names used, and what happened. Do not delay calling 999 if someone is in immediate danger.

6. **For press contact, use the right complaint route only when you have capacity.**  
   - For an IPSO-regulated newspaper, magazine, or digital news publisher, IPSO can advise on intrusive approaches, harassment concerns, and privacy notices to editors.
   - For an IMPRESS-regulated publisher, you will usually raise the complaint with the publisher first, unless there is urgent risk of harm.
   - For TV, radio, or on-demand services, use Ofcom’s complaint page to find the correct route.
   - If you are unsure who regulates the outlet, send your boundary message and keep the evidence.

7. **If contact becomes persistent, threatening, or coordinated, report it.**  
   Call 999 if there is immediate danger, violence is threatened, or a crime is happening now. For non-emergency reporting, call 101 or use the relevant online police reporting route for where you live. Avoid responding to the person while you report it.

8. **Reduce the routes people can use to reach you.**  
   Silence unknown callers, let calls go to voicemail, filter messages from unknown accounts, and block accounts that keep contacting you. Consider using a separate email address for practical matters such as work, school, funeral arrangements, or official appointments.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to tell your story, correct rumours, or issue a detailed statement.
- You do not need to argue about what is public record right now; focus on stopping direct contact and limiting new details.
- You do not need to chase every post. Prioritise posts that reveal addresses, children’s information, funeral location, funeral time, or private family photos.
- Longer-term takedown requests, formal complaints, legal advice, or wider family decisions can wait until the immediate contact is calmer.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel invaded, angry, frozen, or unsure what to say when strangers ask questions after a death. Wanting privacy is reasonable. You can set a firm boundary without explaining your grief or defending your decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce contact, protect private details, and buy time. If harassment continues, private information is being widely shared, or a regulated outlet has acted intrusively, later decisions may need specialist help from police, a press regulator, or a solicitor.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice, media advice, professional safety advice, or therapeutic advice. Complaint routes and options depend on who is contacting you, where you live in the UK, and what has happened. If you feel unsafe or threatened, contact emergency services.
