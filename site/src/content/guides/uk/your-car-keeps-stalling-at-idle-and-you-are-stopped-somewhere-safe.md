# What to do if… your car keeps stalling at idle and you are stopped somewhere safe (UK)

## Short answer
Ensure the vehicle is secured with the handbrake and hazard lights, then perform a single restart attempt only if there are no signs of fire or serious mechanical failure. If it will not idle reliably, remain stopped and contact a breakdown recovery service.

## Do not do these things
- Do not make repeated restart attempts if the first one fails, as this can drain the battery and mask underlying mechanical damage.
- Do not keep the engine running if you see smoke, smell fuel, or see a red oil pressure warning light on the dashboard.
- Do not attempt to investigate the engine bay or perform roadside repairs if you are near moving traffic.
- Do not place a warning triangle if you are on a motorway or a high-speed dual carriageway.
- Do not attempt to "limp" the car through heavy traffic if it is cutting out every time you stop.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the vehicle and increase your visibility immediately.** **Apply the handbrake firmly, put the gearbox in neutral or Park, and switch on your hazard warning lights.** 
   - If visibility is poor or it is dark, also turn on your sidelights.
2. **Check for immediate sensory signs of danger.** **If you smell smoke, see flames, or smell a strong scent of unburnt fuel, switch off the engine and exit the vehicle away from traffic.** 
   - Move all passengers behind a safety barrier or well away from the road.
   - Call 999 and ask for the Fire Service if there is a fire or significant fluid leak.
3. **Perform a single, calm restart attempt if it is safe to do so.** **Turn off all electrical consumers such as the radio, air conditioning, and heated windows before attempting to start the engine.**
   - If the car starts, check the dashboard for any red warning lights. 
   - If an oil pressure or temperature light remains on, turn the engine off immediately.
4. **Move to a location of greater safety if the car is currently obstructing traffic.** **If the engine is running, move the vehicle to the nearest car park, side road, or deep lay-by and stop.**
   - Do not attempt to drive a long distance; the goal is only to reach a "place of relative safety."
5. **Request professional recovery if the engine will not maintain a steady idle.** **Contact your breakdown provider or a local garage and provide your exact location using a map app or road markers.**
   - Inform them of any specific warning lights or unusual noises.
   - If you are obstructing a live lane and cannot move, call 999 immediately before calling recovery.
6. **Follow specific motorway safety protocols if you are on a high-speed road.** **If you are on a motorway, follow the "Go Left" rule by pulling into an emergency area, motorway service station, or onto the hard shoulder.**
   - Switch your wheels to the left so the car would roll away from traffic if hit.
   - Exit via the left-hand doors only and wait behind the safety barrier.
   - If you are stuck in a live lane and cannot exit, stay in the car with your seatbelt on and call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify which sensor or mechanical part caused the stall right now.
- You do not need to check the oil or coolant levels while stopped in a hazardous roadside position.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of repairs or which garage to use until the vehicle is recovered to a secure location.

## Important reassurance
Engine stalling at idle is a common mechanical issue often related to fuel sensors, air intake, or battery health. While it is frustrating, you have already taken the most important step by stopping somewhere safe. Focus on remaining visible and arranging a safe tow rather than fixing the vehicle yourself.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. It is intended to help you manage the immediate minutes after a breakdown and does not cover mechanical diagnosis or long-term repair strategies.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace the instructions provided by emergency services or professional mechanics. Roadside environments are inherently dangerous; if you feel at risk from traffic or other factors, prioritize your physical safety over the vehicle.
