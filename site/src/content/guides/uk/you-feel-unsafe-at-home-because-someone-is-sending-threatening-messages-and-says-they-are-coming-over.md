# What to do if… you feel unsafe at home because someone is sending threatening messages and says they are coming over (UK)

## Short answer
If you think they may arrive soon, are nearby, or may harm you, call 999 now; you do not have to wait until they arrive. Lock the door, do not open it to them, keep your phone with you, and move to the safest place you can inside the home.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door to calm things down or argue it out on the doorstep.
- Do not go outside to check whether it is really them.
- Do not get pulled into repeated messaging; it can distract you and may escalate things.
- Do not tell them exactly where you are in the home or what you are doing.
- Do not feel you must prove the risk before calling for help.
- Do not delete messages, call logs, or voicemails in the moment.
- Do not search for paperwork, screenshots, or old messages if that slows down getting safe or calling 999.

## What to do now
1. Call 999 if there is any immediate danger.
   Call 999 if they are on the way, nearby, outside, threatening violence, trying to get in, likely to force entry, or if the situation could get heated or violent very soon. Say: “Someone I know is threatening to come to my home and I feel unsafe.” Give your address, their name if you know it, whether they may have a key, and whether anyone else is with you.

2. If you cannot speak safely, still call 999.
   From a mobile, dial 999, listen to the operator, and cough, tap, or make a small noise if you can. If you need police and are prompted, press 55 to confirm it is a real emergency and to be put through to police. From a landline, stay on the line if you can; if the operator hears background noise, the call may be transferred to police, and landlines usually provide location information.

3. Lock and secure what you can in the next minute.
   Lock doors and accessible windows. Use a chain, door bar, or deadlock if you have one. Keep your phone with you, put it on charge if possible, and take keys, shoes, and a coat to the place where you will wait.

4. Bring children, pets, or vulnerable people with you.
   Do not leave people split across the home if you can safely gather them. Keep everyone away from the front door, letterbox, and ground-floor windows.

5. Move to a safer room while you call or wait.
   Choose a room you can lock or close, away from the front door if possible. If there is a safer alternative, avoid waiting in a kitchen, garage, utility room, or anywhere with knives, tools, or an easy route in from outside.

6. Put another person on notice.
   Call someone you trust and say: “I feel unsafe. Please stay on the phone. My address is [address]. If we disconnect and you cannot reach me, call 999.” If you live in a block or managed building, alert a neighbour, concierge, or building security that you are not expecting this person and do not want them let in.

7. Send one boundary message only if it feels safe.
   If a short message is unlikely to inflame the situation, send: “Do not come to my home. I will not open the door. If you come here, I will call the police.” Then stop replying. If messaging them feels risky, skip this step.

8. If they arrive, do not engage through the door.
   Do not open the door, even briefly. Call 999 or stay on the line and say: “They are outside my home now. I feel unsafe.” If there is a court order, police condition, or police reference number you can remember, tell the call handler; do not delay the call to search for it.

9. Preserve only what is quick and safe.
   If you are safe enough to do it, screenshot the most threatening messages and the message saying they are coming over. Note the rough time they said it and whether they have arrived. Do not spend time building a full timeline right now.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to make a formal statement, pursue a court order, move home, or take any long-term action.
- You do not need to decide whether this counts as abuse, stalking, harassment, or a crime before seeking help.
- You do not need to explain, negotiate, justify your no, or answer every message.
- You do not need a perfect record of what happened before calling 999.

## Important reassurance
It is common to freeze, doubt yourself, or worry that you are overreacting when someone you know escalates. Feeling unsafe in your home is enough reason to take protective steps and call for help early.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance for the next minutes and hours. Later decisions about reporting, safety planning, housing, relationship contact, or legal protections may need specialist help when you are in a safer, calmer place.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you believe you are at immediate risk, call 999. If you are not in immediate danger but want to report what is happening, contact police on 101 or through an official online police reporting route.
