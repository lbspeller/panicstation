# What to do if… your pulse oximeter reading is unexpectedly low for you (UK)

## Short answer
Recheck your reading immediately using a warm, clean finger while sitting still; if it remains at 92% or less, or if you have severe difficulty breathing, call 999 or go to A&E immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore physical symptoms like chest pain or confusion just because the oximeter number seems "okay".
- Don't repeatedly test yourself every few seconds as anxiety can alter your breathing and heart rate.
- Don't drive yourself to the hospital if you feel faint, severely breathless, or confused.
- Don't assume a "normal" range of 95-100% applies to you if a doctor has given you a different target range for a condition like COPD.
- Don't rely solely on the oximeter if you have brown or black skin, as these devices can sometimes overestimate oxygen levels in darker skin tones.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency symptoms that require an immediate 999 call regardless of the oximeter number.**
   - Call 999 if you have blue/grey/pale lips or face, can't finish a short sentence at rest, have sudden chest pain, or feel severely confused or drowsy.

2. **Perform a "clean" recheck to rule out a device error or user issue.**
   - Remove any nail varnish or false nails from the finger you are using.
   - Warm your hand if it is cold, as poor circulation in cold fingers causes false low readings.
   - Sit upright and rest quietly for 5 minutes before taking the measurement.
   - Place the device on your middle or index finger, keep your hand still at the level of your heart, and wait 60 seconds for the reading to stabilise.

3. **Take emergency action if the reading is 92% or less after the recheck.**
   - If your SpO₂ is 92% or less, call 999 or attend A&E immediately.

4. **Seek urgent medical advice via NHS 111 or your GP if the reading is 93% or 94%.**
   - If your reading is consistently 93-94% and this is lower than your usual level, contact NHS 111 or your GP surgery as soon as possible for an urgent assessment.

5. **Follow your personal clinical action plan if you have a pre-existing lung condition.**
   - Use the specific target ranges (often 88-92%) and escalation steps provided by your respiratory team or GP.
   - If you do not have a personal plan but have a diagnosed condition and the reading is lower than your stable baseline, contact your specialist team or 111.

6. **Note down your findings to tell the healthcare operator.**
   - Record the SpO₂ percentage, your pulse rate (BPM), how you feel (e.g., "more breathless than usual"), and whether the number has been dropping over the last hour.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the cause of the low reading (such as pneumonia or a blood clot) yourself.
- You do not need to buy a different brand of oximeter to "double check" the first one.
- You do not need to decide on long-term lifestyle changes or treatments while in an acute situation.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for pulse oximeters to give an initial low reading due to cold hands, movement, or poor positioning. If a careful recheck shows a normal number and you feel well, the first reading was likely a "false low". However, if you feel unwell, trust your symptoms over the device—medical professionals expect you to seek help if you are worried.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and escalation only. It does not replace a clinical examination by a healthcare professional.

## Important note
Pulse oximeters are screening tools and have limitations, including reduced accuracy in people with darker skin tones or poor circulation. Always seek medical help if your condition is deteriorating, regardless of the device reading.
