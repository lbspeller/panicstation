# What to do if… you suspect someone is taking advantage of grief to push you into signing documents quickly (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the interaction and refuse to sign any document until you have a physical or digital copy in your possession for independent review. Assert that no signatures will be provided on the same day as the request.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any document to stop the pressure or end a difficult conversation.
- Do not rely on verbal summaries that describe a document as "standard," "routine," or "a formality."
- Do not hand over original documents such as passports, death certificates, or title deeds to individuals you do not already have an established professional relationship with.
- Do not share bank login details, PINs, or one-time passcodes (OTPs) under any circumstances.
- Do not use contact details provided within a suspicious message or document to "verify" the sender.
- Do not allow yourself to be isolated from other family members or your own legal advisors.

## What to do now
1. **State a firm refusal to sign immediately.** Say: "I am not signing any documents today; I require time to review this independently." 
   - Repeat this statement without providing further justification if the pressure continues.
2. **Secure a full copy of the document.** Obtain all pages, including any small-print terms and conditions or schedules. 
   - Note the name and professional credentials of the person presenting the document. 
   - Request a written explanation of any "urgent" deadlines claimed by the other party.
3. **Identify the document type through observation.** Check for specific headings or keywords such as:
   - Funeral Service Agreement or Authorization
   - Grant of Probate or Letters of Administration
   - Deed of Variation or Disclaimer of Interest
   - Release and Indemnity Agreement
   - Bank Account Closure or Transfer Form
4. **Transition to a controlled communication channel.** Request that all further correspondence be sent via email from an official company domain or via recorded post to your address. 
   - Do not click links in unsolicited emails; type the official website address directly into your browser.
5. **Involve an independent third party for review.** Contact one of the following before signing:
   - Your own solicitor (independent of the person or firm pushing the document)
   - Citizens Advice for guidance on contracts or consumer rights
   - A trusted friend or family member who is not a beneficiary of the estate
6. **Verify financial requests through secure channels.** If the pressure involves bank transfers or account changes:
   - Call your bank using the number on the back of your card or via the "159" hotline. 
   - Inform the bank that you are bereaved and are experiencing high-pressure requests.
7. **Report suspected fraud to the correct regional authority.** 
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** Report the incident to **Report Fraud** at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.
   - **Scotland:** Contact **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
8. **Verify the status of any Power of Attorney.** Note that in England and Wales, a Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) ends immediately upon the death of the donor. 
   - If someone claims they are still "acting as attorney" after the death, do not sign their documents and seek legal advice.
9. **Record the details of the encounter.** Write down the date, time, contact methods used, and exactly what was requested. 
   - Save copies of any "urgent" or "final notice" language used in communications.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today how the estate will be distributed.
- You do not need to respond to repeated phone calls or messages once you have stated your boundary.
- You do not need to worry about "offending" the person who is pressuring you.
- You do not need to resolve family disputes or determine the validity of a will immediately.

## Important reassurance
Pressure to sign legal documents during bereavement is a common tactic used to bypass normal scrutiny. Taking 24 to 48 hours to step away and consult an independent source is a standard and protective procedure. This reaction is a rational response to a high-stress situation and does not indicate a lack of trust in legitimate professionals.

## Scope note
This guidance is for immediate stabilization and the prevention of rushed legal commitments. It does not replace professional legal advice regarding inheritance, probate, or contract law.

## Important note
This information is for general use and does not constitute legal advice. If you feel physically unsafe or are being threatened with violence, call 999 immediately.
