# What to do if… someone repeatedly tries to get you to remove clothing for photos and frames it as harmless fun (UK)

## Short answer
End the interaction immediately and move to a space where you are not alone with the person. Treat repeated pressure as a serious boundary violation regardless of how they frame it, and contact a 24/7 support line for guidance on safety and reporting.

## Do not do these things
- Do not engage in a debate about whether the request is "harmless" or a "joke" if it makes you uncomfortable.
- Do not provide a "compromise" photo (such as underwear only) in the hope that it will satisfy them and make them stop.
- Do not delete messages or call logs yet, as these are evidence of repeated harassment.
- Do not meet the person alone to explain why their behaviour was wrong.
- Do not blame yourself for not being more forceful or for trying to "laugh it off" to keep the peace.
- Do not share the details with anyone you do not fundamentally trust to keep the information private.

## What to do now
1. **Remove yourself from the physical or digital presence of the person immediately.** You do not owe them a detailed explanation; a simple "I am leaving now" or ending the call is sufficient.
2. **Set a clear, non-negotiable boundary in writing if it is safe to do so.** Use direct language that cannot be misinterpreted as a joke.
   - "I have said no. Do not ask me to remove my clothes or take photos again."
3. **Capture evidence of the repeated requests before blocking or muting.** Take screenshots of messages, record dates and times of verbal requests, and note any witnesses who were present.
4. **Call the Rape Crisis England & Wales 24/7 support line on 0808 500 2222 for confidential advice.** They can help you process what happened and explain your options for safety and support without involving the police unless you choose to.
5. **Contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) if any physical contact or non-consensual photos were taken.** They provide medical and emotional support and can store forensic evidence securely if you are unsure about reporting.
6. **Report the behaviour to a Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) if you are in a school or college setting.** Under 2026 KCSIE guidelines, staff must take these reports seriously and follow specific "all reasonable steps" protocols to protect you from further harassment.
7. **Submit a "qualifying disclosure" to HR or a manager if this is occurring at work.** As of April 2026, sexual harassment is protected under whistleblowing law, and your employer has a legal duty to prevent further contact.
8. **Utilize 48-hour takedown rules if images have been shared online.** Contact the platform’s reporting tool immediately; UK law now requires major platforms to remove non-consensual intimate content within 48 hours of a report.
9. **Call 999 if the person is physically blocking your exit or you feel an immediate threat of violence.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to press criminal charges.
- You do not need to figure out the "true intentions" of the person before taking steps to protect yourself.
- You do not need to tell your family or friends if you are not ready to do so.
- You do not need to provide a formal statement to your employer or school in the first few hours.

## Important reassurance
Repeated pressure to undress is a form of harassment, even when disguised as "banter" or "fun." It is normal to feel confused, frozen, or like you are "overreacting" when a friend, partner, or colleague ignores your "no." Your discomfort is a valid signal that a boundary has been crossed, and you have every right to seek distance and support.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm reduction only. Later decisions regarding legal action, workplace grievances, or long-term therapeutic support should be made with the assistance of a qualified specialist or advocate.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or medical advice. The legal landscape regarding image-based abuse and workplace harassment in the UK is subject to specific 2026 updates under the Employment Rights Act 2025 and the Online Safety Act; consult a solicitor or professional advocate for advice on your specific case.
