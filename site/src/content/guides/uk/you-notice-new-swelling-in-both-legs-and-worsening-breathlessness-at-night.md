# What to do if… you notice new swelling in both legs and worsening breathlessness at night (UK)

## Short answer
Use NHS 111 now unless any emergency signs are present. Call 999 immediately if breathing is severe, you have chest pain or tightness, you faint, you become confused, your lips or skin look blue or grey, or you are coughing up blood.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “sleep it off” if breathlessness is worsening at night or when lying down.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you feel very breathless, faint, confused, clammy, or have chest pain; call 999 instead.
- Do not take extra “water tablets” or other prescription medicines unless you have a written plan from a clinician for exactly this situation.
- Do not drastically restrict fluids or take over-the-counter “water loss” products.
- Do not keep repeatedly lying flat “to test it” if that makes breathing worse.
- Do not wait for a routine GP appointment if the swelling and breathlessness are new or clearly worsening.

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 immediately if any emergency signs apply:**
   - severe difficulty breathing, gasping, choking, or being unable to get words out
   - chest tightness, heaviness, or pain, especially if it spreads to your arm, back, neck, or jaw
   - lips or skin looking very pale, blue, or grey
   - new confusion, fainting, collapsing, or feeling very clammy
   - coughing up blood
   If someone is with you, ask them to stay nearby and unlock the door if it is safe to do so. Do not drive yourself.

2. **If it is not 999-level but it is new or worsening, contact NHS 111 now.**
   - Use 111 online if you can, or call 111 if online is not suitable for you.
   - Call 111 rather than relying on online only if you have complex problems from an existing medical condition or a care plan for this health problem.
   - Say clearly: “New swelling in both legs” and “Breathlessness worse when lying down or waking up at night short of breath.”

3. **Sit upright while you arrange help.**
   - Sit supported upright, or prop yourself up with pillows.
   - Avoid unnecessary exertion while you are arranging help.
   - If lying flat makes your breathing worse, do not keep trying to lie flat.

4. **Get the most useful information ready, but do not delay calling 111 or 999 to collect it.**
   - Your medicines, or photos of the boxes.
   - Any allergies.
   - Any known heart, lung, kidney, liver, or clotting problems.
   - When the swelling started, whether it is getting worse daily, and whether shoes or socks suddenly feel tight.
   - If you can check without exertion: today’s weight, any recent rapid weight change, and your heart rate.

5. **Mention possible clot warning signs clearly.**
   - DVT symptoms are often in 1 leg, rarely both.
   - Tell 111 or 999 if one leg is much more swollen, painful, red, darkened, hot, or tender than the other.
   - If leg pain or swelling comes with breathlessness or chest pain, treat it as an emergency and call 999.

6. **If you already have a heart failure nurse, heart team, or written action plan, use it without delaying urgent help.**
   - Follow the plan for who to contact.
   - If breathlessness is worsening at night, you cannot lie flat, or you cannot reach the team quickly, use 111 or 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause tonight.
- You do not need to decide about long-term treatment changes now.
- You do not need to start a strict fluid restriction, new diet, supplement, or exercise plan right now.
- You can wait to sort work, family, or transport logistics until you have contacted 111 or been assessed.
- You can wait to research possible diagnoses until urgent safety decisions have been made.

## Important reassurance
New swelling in both legs with breathlessness that is worse lying down or waking you at night is a recognised reason to seek urgent medical advice. It does not prove one particular diagnosis, and many causes can be treated, but it is worth being checked promptly.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stay safer and reach the right urgent service. Diagnosis, tests, medication changes, and longer-term decisions need a clinician who can assess you directly.

## Important note
This is general information, not a diagnosis, and not medical or professional advice. If symptoms are severe, rapidly worsening, or you are unsure, choose the safer option: call 999 or contact NHS 111 now.
