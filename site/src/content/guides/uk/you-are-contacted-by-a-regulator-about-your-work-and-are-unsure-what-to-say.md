# What to do if… you are contacted by a regulator about your work and are unsure what to say (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before giving substantive answers off the cuff. Verify who contacted you, ask for the request and any deadline in writing, and get advice from the appropriate internal team, union, or an independent solicitor before you speak in detail.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t “just explain it quickly” on the phone to make it go away.
- Don’t guess, speculate, or fill silences with extra detail.
- Don’t click links, open attachments, or use call-back numbers from an unexpected message until you’ve verified them independently.
- Don’t delete, edit, backdate, or “tidy up” records, including emails, messages, notes, logs, or drafts.
- Don’t sign a statement, timeline, witness account, or “summary of what happened” on the spot.
- Don’t share details widely at work or online to check your memory.
- Don’t contact witnesses, complainants, customers, clients, or colleagues to “fix” the issue.
- Don’t hand over personal devices, passwords, or private accounts unless you have verified that it is formally required and have taken advice where possible.
- Don’t ignore written notices, deadlines, inspection appointments, or requests just because you feel overwhelmed.

## What to do now
1. **Preserve the contact exactly as received.** Save the email, letter, text, voicemail, envelope, reference number, attachments, and caller details. If it is an email, keep the original message rather than only forwarding a screenshot.

2. **Verify the contact independently.** Use the regulator’s official website or GOV.UK page to find a switchboard, contact form, or published email address. Ask them to confirm the person’s name, role, team, reference number, and whether the request is genuine.

3. **Ask for the request in writing.** If they call, say you want to cooperate but need the request confirmed in writing so you can answer accurately. Ask what they want, why they want it, the legal power or process they are using, the deadline, and the safest channel for sending any response.

4. **Use a holding line if you are pressured to answer immediately.** You can say: “I want to cooperate, but I’m not confident I can answer accurately without checking the records. Please put the request in writing and I’ll respond after I’ve checked what is needed.”

5. **Tell the right support route, but check for conflicts.** If you are employed, contact your manager and your employer’s compliance, legal, data protection, safety, finance, or risk team as appropriate, and forward the exact message rather than summarising from memory. If the message says it is confidential, names you personally, or you think you may be blamed, disciplined, or prosecuted, get independent advice before relying only on your employer’s lawyers.

6. **Preserve relevant records now.** Do not delete or amend emails, chats, drafts, notes, photographs, logs, calendars, files, or device records. If a work system auto-deletes messages or overwrites logs, tell the relevant IT, compliance, or legal contact so preservation can be considered.

7. **Make a simple record map.** Write down where relevant material might be held, such as work email, shared drives, case systems, finance systems, inspection records, phone messages, notebooks, personal devices used for work, or archived folders. Do not start editing or reorganising the material.

8. **Clarify what type of contact this is.** Ask whether it is an informal enquiry, an inspection, a request for documents, written questions, a formal information notice, a request for a witness statement, or an interview.

9. **If a formal notice has a deadline, protect the deadline.** Put the deadline in your calendar. If the request is unclear, very broad, or impossible to meet in time, ask promptly in writing for clarification or an extension instead of leaving it until the last moment.

10. **If an interview under caution is mentioned, slow down and get legal advice.** Treat it as formal. Ask what legal or interview framework applies, whether it will be recorded, what allegation or issue is being considered, and whether you can have legal representation before attending or answering.

11. **Start a communications log.** Record dates, times, names, job titles, reference numbers, what was requested, what was sent, what is still outstanding, and every deadline. Keep copies of anything you send.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the full story right now.
- You do not need to resign, confess, accuse anyone, or defend everyone today.
- You do not need to have a long phone call to clear things up.
- You do not need to work out every legal consequence before taking the first safe steps.
- You do not need to contact clients, customers, witnesses, complainants, or former colleagues right now.

## Important reassurance
Regulatory contact can feel intimidating even when the issue is routine, factual, or directed at the organisation rather than you personally. Verifying identity, preserving records, asking for clarity, and taking advice before answering in detail are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to prevent irreversible mistakes and buy time. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if there is a formal notice, an inspection, an interview under caution, a possible offence, or a conflict between you and your employer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, employment, financial, regulatory, therapeutic, medical, or other professional advice. If you receive a formal notice, are invited to an interview under caution, or think you may have personal exposure, consider seeking independent legal advice promptly.
