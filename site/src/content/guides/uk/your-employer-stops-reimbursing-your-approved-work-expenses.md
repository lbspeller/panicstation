# What to do if… your employer stops reimbursing your approved work expenses (UK)

## Short answer
Pause any further out-of-pocket work spending and send one clear written request today setting out what was approved, what you paid, what you are owed, and when you need a payment date.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep paying personally for work costs while repayment is uncertain.
- Do not assume it is only a timing issue without asking for a written explanation and payment date.
- Do not rely only on verbal updates or chat messages that are hard to find later.
- Do not threaten, accuse, resign, or post publicly while you are still trying to get the payment fixed.
- Do not assume an internal grievance pauses any external deadline if you later need outside help.

## What to do now
1. **Pause new spending in writing.**  
   If you are being asked to book travel, buy equipment, entertain clients, pay mileage, or cover any other work cost, reply calmly that you cannot incur further personal costs until the approved reimbursements are paid. Ask for a company card, central booking, direct supplier payment, or an advance instead.

2. **Make a simple evidence pack.**  
   Put these in one folder or one PDF:
   - the approval, such as an email, message, or system record showing who approved it and when
   - the expenses policy or guidance you followed
   - receipts, invoices, mileage records, or booking references
   - the submitted expense claim and any claim reference numbers
   - any responses from your manager, finance, payroll, or the expenses system

3. **Send one clear payment request today.**  
   Email your manager and the expenses, finance, or payroll contact. Include:
   - the total amount owed
   - an itemised list with date, supplier, amount, reason, and where the approval is shown
   - when you submitted each claim
   - the question: “When will this be paid, and what has changed?”
   - a clear boundary that you are pausing further out-of-pocket spending unless a company-funded method is provided

4. **Ask for a short-term fix if this is causing financial harm.**  
   If the delay is causing overdraft fees, missed bills, or immediate money pressure, say that plainly and ask whether they can arrange an off-cycle payment, a temporary advance, or direct booking so you are not fronting work costs.

5. **If the answer is vague, use the grievance route.**  
   If they will not give a payment date, will not explain the delay, or keep moving the answer, raise it through your employer’s formal grievance process in writing. Keep it factual: approved expenses not reimbursed, amounts, dates, impact, and the outcome you want, such as payment by a specific date and written confirmation of the expenses process going forward.

6. **Know the companion point if there is a formal meeting.**  
   For a grievance meeting about a possible legal or contractual duty, employees and workers can usually ask to bring an eligible companion. This is normally a work colleague, a certified workplace trade union representative, or an official employed by a trade union.

7. **Protect your options without deciding the whole dispute today.**  
   If the issue does not resolve, the correct route may depend on your contract, expenses policy, employment status, whether you are still employed, and what kind of claim it would be. For many employment tribunal claims, the first step is to notify Acas within the time limit and go through early conciliation, unless an exemption applies. In most tribunal claims the limit is 3 months minus 1 day from the relevant event, but some claims have different limits and the exact deadline can be complicated.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, threaten legal action, or make a claim.
- You do not need to prove why the employer stopped paying before you ask for payment and pause new spending.
- You do not need a perfect spreadsheet before acting; gather the approvals, receipts, amounts, and dates, then get the request logged.
- You do not need to agree to more personal spending while the existing approved expenses are unpaid.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel alarmed when money you paid for work is not returned, especially if the expense was approved and you relied on reimbursement. The first useful move is not a fight; it is to stop the money leak, put the facts in writing, and ask for a clear payment route.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and reduce financial harm. Later decisions about grievances, claims, resignation, or settlement may need specialist advice based on your contract, policy, employment status, and the exact history.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Employment rights and deadlines can vary with the facts, so get independent advice before taking irreversible action.
