# What to do if… you receive a message from someone you know that feels “off” and asks you to meet urgently in a private place (UK)

## Short answer
Do not go to a private or secluded location. Verify the sender's identity using a different communication method, such as a direct phone call to their known number, before taking any further action.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go to a private home, a car, or a secluded outdoor spot "just to see" what is happening.
- Don’t go alone or allow the sender to arrange your transport or pick you up.
- Don’t share your live location, home address, or travel details in response to the message.
- Don’t provide security codes, passcodes, or banking details even if they claim it is to "prove" who you are.
- Don’t engage in an argument or reveal your suspicions to the sender while you are still unverified and potentially vulnerable.

## What to do now
1. **Initiate a safety pause.** Put the phone down for at least 60 seconds to break the cycle of artificial urgency. Recognize that high-pressure "emergencies" are a primary tactic for both scams and physical entrapment.
2. **Verify the sender "out-of-band."** 
   - Call the person’s known mobile number directly (do not use any link or number provided in the new message).
   - If they do not answer, try a video call or a different messaging platform you have used with them previously.
   - Ask a specific question that only the real person would know the answer to, such as a detail from your last face-to-face conversation.
3. **Handle "emergency" claims with safer alternatives.**
   - If the sender claims to be in crisis, offer to call emergency services or a mutual trusted contact on their behalf rather than meeting in private.
   - **Call 999** if you have a genuine belief that a person is at immediate risk of serious harm or death.
   - Note that under current "Right Care, Right Person" protocols, police may not attend non-emergency welfare checks unless there is an immediate, real, and substantial risk to life.
4. **Change the meeting conditions if you decide to proceed.**
   - Insist on a busy, staffed public location such as a supermarket cafe, a train station concourse, or a high-street coffee shop.
   - Arrange your own transport and ensure you have a clear, independent exit route. 
   - Bring a third party with you or have a friend wait in the same venue at a nearby table.
5. **Establish a secondary safety net.**
   - Tell a trusted third party exactly who you are meeting, where, and what time you will check in with them.
   - Share your live location with *that* trusted person, not the person you are meeting.
   - Ensure your mobile phone is fully charged and keep one ear free of headphones to maintain situational awareness.
6. **Use "Ask for Angela" in participating venues.**
   - If you are at a venue and feel unsafe, approach a member of staff and ask for "Angela."
   - Staff at venues participating in the "Official Pledge" are trained to discreetly help you leave safely or contact security.
7. **Use the Silent Solution in a high-risk emergency.**
   - **Call 999.** If you cannot speak without being heard, listen for the automated prompt.
   - **Press 55** when prompted on a mobile. This tells the operator your emergency is genuine and transfers you to the police. Note that 55 does not automatically track your location.
8. **Report the message through official channels.**
   - Forward suspicious text messages to **7726** (it is free).
   - Forward suspicious emails to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.
   - If you believe an account has been taken over, report it to the platform's security team immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to figure out why the message felt "off" before choosing not to go.
- You do not need to reply to the message or explain your absence while you are verifying.
- You do not need to decide whether to block the person or report the account until you have confirmed your own safety.

## Important reassurance
Trusting your intuition is a valid safety measure. Taking time to verify a strange request does not make you a bad friend; if the person is truly in need, they will understand your preference for a safer, public meeting or a phone call.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate period following a suspicious or high-pressure request. It does not cover long-term cyber-security recovery or legal reporting following a confirmed crime.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and is not a substitute for professional legal or police advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. Police responses to non-emergency welfare concerns are subject to local jurisdictional policies and the "Right Care, Right Person" framework.
