# What to do if… your phone touchscreen stops responding while the device is still on (UK)

## Short answer
Do not factory-reset the phone or keep guessing at the lock screen. First, use another device to protect access to the accounts and codes you need, then try only low-risk restart and non-touch workarounds.

## Do not do these things
- Do not factory reset the phone while you still need data, tickets, codes, photos, contacts, or authenticator access from it.
- Do not keep tapping randomly or repeatedly trying the passcode; this can cause unwanted actions or lockout delays.
- Do not sign out of Apple, Google, email, banking, work, or password-manager accounts on other devices unless you know you can sign back in without this phone.
- Do not erase the phone remotely unless protecting the data is more important than anything still stored only on the phone.
- Do not press hard on the screen, heat it, or keep charging it if it is swollen, smells unusual, is very hot, or has obvious liquid damage.
- Do not hand the phone over for repair before you have thought about the accounts, SIM, banking apps, authenticator apps, photos, and documents tied to it.

## What to do now
1. **Name the one thing you need first.**
   Decide the immediate access problem: a one-time code, a banking approval, a boarding pass, a work login, a contact, a ticket, a photo, or an authenticator app. Handle that before trying to solve the whole phone.

2. **Move the urgent task to another device if possible.**
   On a laptop, tablet, or borrowed phone, try the web version of the service you need. Check email, cloud storage, airline or train accounts, your password manager, and any device where you are already signed in.

3. **Keep control of email and password access.**
   Make sure you can open your main email and password manager on another device. If you can, save any urgent ticket, code, address, booking reference, or document somewhere you can reach without the broken touchscreen. Change passwords only from a working device and only if you are confident it will not strand you behind the broken phone as the only second factor.

4. **Check second-factor options before changing account settings.**
   Look for backup codes, a trusted computer, a second phone or tablet already signed in, a security key, SMS to a SIM you can move, or a workplace administrator who can reset access. For banking, travel, work, or government services, use the official support route for that service rather than guessing inside the locked phone.

5. **If the phone might be lost, stolen, or accessible to someone else, secure it from another device.**
   For an iPhone, use iCloud Find Devices or the Find My app if already enabled. For Android, use Google Find Hub if the device is eligible and online enough for the command. Lock or secure the device first where possible; erase only if the risk of someone accessing the data outweighs losing what is on the phone.

6. **Try the safest physical checks.**
   Remove the case and screen protector. Disconnect accessories. Dry the outside with a soft cloth if it is damp. Plug it into power and leave it for a short while; if the battery is very low, follow the maker’s charging guidance.

7. **Try a hardware-button restart, not a reset.**
   On newer iPhones without a Home button, quickly press Volume Up, quickly press Volume Down, then hold the Side button until the Apple logo appears. Older iPhones use different button combinations, so check Apple’s official model steps from another device. On Android, force-restart buttons vary by maker and model; many use a sustained press of Power, or Power and Volume Down, but check the official steps for your exact model.

8. **Try non-touch control only if your phone supports it.**
   Some Android phones can be controlled with a USB mouse through a USB OTG adapter. If it works, a pointer appears and you may be able to unlock, copy files, approve a sign-in, or back up essentials. It may not work on every model, at every lock screen, or after serious damage.

9. **If you regain access even briefly, extract essentials first.**
   Send the urgent item to yourself, save it to a cloud account you can open elsewhere, export or photograph backup codes if the service allows it, confirm contacts and photos are syncing, and add another sign-in method where the account supports it.

10. **Protect your phone number if SMS codes matter.**
   Contact your mobile network from another phone or web chat about replacement SIM or eSIM options. Ask how they protect your account from unauthorised SIM swaps or number-porting.

11. **Plan repair without losing account access.**
   Before repair, write down what is still trapped on the device and which accounts depend on it. Ask the repairer whether the repair may erase data. If the phone was sold faulty or develops a fault not caused by accidental damage, your UK consumer-rights route is generally with the retailer, not only the manufacturer; rights depend on timing and circumstances.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to upgrade, switch from iPhone to Android, change bank, move every account, or fully diagnose whether the fault is hardware or software. The urgent job is to keep access to your accounts and avoid wiping or locking yourself out.

## Important reassurance
A phone can be powered on while the touch layer is not working. This is disruptive, but it does not automatically mean everything is gone. Slowing down, using another device, and avoiding resets gives you the best chance of getting the essential access back.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Repair, replacement, account recovery, consumer-rights decisions, and security hardening may need help from the phone maker, your mobile network, the retailer, your bank, your workplace, or another relevant specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, professional, or technical diagnostic advice. Phone models, account settings, backups, repair risks, and consumer-rights outcomes vary. Use only steps that match your device and situation.
