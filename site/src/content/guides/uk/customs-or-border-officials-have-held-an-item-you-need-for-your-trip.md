# What to do if… customs or border officials have held an item you need for your trip (UK)

## Short answer

Get written confirmation of what happened, why the item is being held, and the reference number. Then make a travel plan that does not depend on getting the item back today.

## Do not do these things

- Do not argue, raise your voice, obstruct officers, or try to take the item back.
- Do not leave without paperwork, or at least the reference number, the office involved, and how to follow up.
- Do not sign anything you do not understand; ask what it means in plain language first.
- Do not hand over extra passwords or account access unless you are told it is legally required; ask what is being requested and what will be recorded.
- Do not miss your departure while waiting for a “maybe” outcome; set a clear cut-off time for boarding, rebooking, or leaving the area.

## What to do now

1. **Ask exactly what status the item has.** Calmly ask:
   - “Is the item being detained temporarily, seized, or held for checks?”
   - “What is the reason or category?”
   - “What reference number should I use when I follow up?”
   - “Which office is dealing with it?”

2. **Get the item into writing before you walk away.** Ask for the seizure notice, detention paperwork, receipt, or written reference showing:
   - what the item is
   - the date, time, and location
   - your name and contact details
   - the reference number
   - whether HMRC, Border Force, or another office is handling it
   - what the notice says you can do next

3. **Ask one practical release question, then stop negotiating if the answer is no.** Ask:
   - “Is there anything I can show now, such as proof of ownership, a prescription, a licence, an import document, or duty payment, that could allow release today?”
   - If they say no, move to your travel decision and follow-up plan.

4. **If it is medication or a medical device, say so clearly.**
   - Show the pharmacy label, prescription, clinician letter, device documentation, or travel letter if you have it.
   - Ask what document they need for review or release.
   - Start a replacement plan straight away, such as contacting your prescriber, pharmacy, travel insurer, airline assistance desk, or medical support at your destination.

5. **Separate the trip from the item recovery.**
   - If you can travel safely without the item, continue with check-in, security, boarding, or onward travel using the reference number for follow-up.
   - If you cannot travel without it, step aside, get the paperwork or reference first, then contact the airline, travel company, hotel, insurer, or destination contact about rebooking or replacement.

6. **If the item was in checked baggage or a shipment, contact the carrier now.**
   - For baggage, ask the airline baggage desk to add a note that the item or bag is being held by border or customs officials, and get the airline’s reference number.
   - For a courier or shipment, ask the carrier what document they need from you, such as an invoice, proof of ownership, licence, permit, or customs declaration.

7. **If the item has been seized, do not mix up the two main routes.**
   - **If you think customs was wrong to seize it:** send a notice of claim. HMRC or Border Force must receive it within one month of the seizure. Use the reference number and follow the address or contact route on your notice.
   - **If you want the item returned even if you accept the seizure:** ask for restoration. Send the request as soon as possible, ideally within 45 days, because some items may be destroyed, sold, or dealt with sooner if they are perishable.
   - Check the exact date and instructions on your own notice before sending anything.

8. **Make a replacement plan in parallel.**
   - Write down what the item was needed for: dose, charger, work access, baby feeding, mobility, paperwork, clothing, or equipment.
   - Choose the fastest substitute: airport purchase, local pharmacy, replacement prescription, rental, buy on arrival, borrowed equipment, or courier to your destination.
   - Keep receipts if you may later need them for insurance, reimbursement, or a complaint.

9. **Write a short incident note for yourself.**
   - Record the date, time, airport or port, desk or area, what was held, what you were told, the reference number, and any officer or office name offered.
   - Photograph the paperwork if you are allowed to keep it.

## What can wait

- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, get legal advice, or make a formal claim.
- You do not need to win the argument at the counter; your priority is paperwork, a reference number, and a workable travel decision.
- You do not need perfect wording now; a clear follow-up is easier once you are calm and have the notice in front of you.

## Important reassurance

It is normal to feel embarrassed, angry, or panicked when an item you rely on is suddenly held. Getting the situation into writing is usually the fastest way to regain control and avoid losing your travel options.

## Scope note

These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if the item is restricted, prohibited, a medicine, a medical device, work equipment, cash, animal or plant material, food, or part of a shipment.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Follow the specific instructions, contact details, and deadlines written on your official notice or receipt.
