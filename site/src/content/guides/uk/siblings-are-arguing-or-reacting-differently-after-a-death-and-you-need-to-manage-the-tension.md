# What to do if… siblings are arguing or reacting differently after a death and you need to manage the tension (UK)

## Short answer
Make a short, “today only” plan that keeps everyone physically safe, lowers the temperature, and delays big decisions. If anyone may harm themselves, harm someone else, or cannot be kept safe, call 999 now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not demand that everyone grieves in the same way or shows the same emotion.
- Do not try to settle old family arguments today.
- Do not make irreversible decisions during an argument, including decisions about money, possessions, public announcements, or funeral details beyond immediate essentials.
- Do not continue a group chat, phone call, or room discussion once it has become shouting, insults, threats, or repeated blame.
- Do not drive, travel, drink heavily, use drugs, or take extra medication while emotions are out of control.
- Do not leave someone alone if they are talking about not wanting to live, self-harm, or doing something risky.

## What to do now
1. **Create a calmer pause point for 10 to 20 minutes.** Move to separate rooms, step outside with a safe person, or stop replying in the group chat. The aim is not to solve anything yet; it is to stop the argument from getting worse.

2. **Do a quick safety check, not a debate.** Ask each sibling plainly:
   - “Are you safe right now?”
   - “Have you had thoughts of harming yourself or not wanting to be here?”
   - “Are you about to do anything risky, such as driving while very upset, drinking heavily, or taking extra medication?”
   If you think there is immediate danger, call 999.

3. **Use the right urgent support route if it is urgent but not an immediate 999 emergency.**
   - In England, call 111 and select the mental health option.
   - In Wales, call 111 and press option 2 for urgent mental health support.
   - In Scotland, call 111 and choose the mental health option through NHS 24.
   - In Northern Ireland, call Lifeline on 0808 808 8000 for crisis support.
   If someone just needs to talk right now, Samaritans can be called free on 116 123.

4. **Set three “today only” rules in writing.** Keep them short:
   - No shouting, insults, threats, or blame.
   - No big decisions today unless they are truly time-critical.
   - One person handles urgent calls or messages for the next few hours, and nobody else has to explain themselves.

5. **Name the difference without judging it.** Use one neutral sentence, such as: “We are all reacting differently, and that is making us collide.” This can reduce the pressure to prove who is grieving correctly.

6. **Make a six-hour plan.** Choose only the next few practical things:
   - who is eating or drinking something now;
   - who is staying with whom tonight, especially if someone does not feel safe being alone;
   - who will handle any necessary contact today, such as a funeral director, close relative, hospital, care home, or GP;
   - which conversation is paused until tomorrow.

7. **Remove the easiest triggers for 24 hours.** Put alcohol away, pause the group chat, stop sorting belongings, and delay sensitive messages to extended family unless they are essential. If a message must be sent, ask one calm person to send one short version.

8. **If children or vulnerable adults are present, give them steadiness first.** Keep voices low, use simple explanations, and make sure one calm adult is looking after food, sleep, medication, and basic routine.

9. **Get bereavement support without making it a family vote.** One sibling can contact support even if the others are not ready. A GP, NHS grief information, local bereavement services, Samaritans, or a trusted faith or community leader can be used for support without forcing everyone to agree on the same help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today what the “right” grief reaction is.
- You do not need to settle inheritance, possessions, old resentments, or family roles today.
- You do not need to agree every funeral detail today unless something is genuinely time-critical.
- You do not need to write the perfect announcement or update everyone immediately.
- You do not need to choose longer-term counselling, mediation, or family meetings today unless an urgent service advises a next step.

## Important reassurance
Numbness, anger, crying, practical task mode, silence, guilt, and withdrawal can all happen after a death. Different reactions can look like not caring, blaming, or taking over, even when people are shocked and trying to cope.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours to 1 or 2 days. Ongoing conflict, worsening mental health, complicated grief, estate issues, or safeguarding concerns may need help from a GP, bereavement service, solicitor, social care, or specialist mental health service.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If someone is at immediate risk of harm, call 999. If urgent mental health help is needed but it is not a 999 emergency, use the relevant UK route: 111 mental health option in England, 111 press option 2 in Wales, NHS 24 111 in Scotland, or Lifeline in Northern Ireland.
