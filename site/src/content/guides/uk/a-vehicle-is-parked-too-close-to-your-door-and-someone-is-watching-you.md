# What to do if… a vehicle is parked too close to your door and someone is watching you (UK)

## Short answer
Do not go close to the driver’s door. Move to a safer, busier place with staff or witnesses, and call 999 if you are in immediate danger, being threatened, or a crime is in progress.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the person, argue through a window, or test them by getting closer.
- Do not squeeze into the gap at the driver’s door if it puts you within arm’s reach.
- Do not unlock your car and stand with the door open while you decide what to do.
- Do not move to a quieter corner of the car park for space.
- Do not stand next to the vehicles while searching your phone.
- Do not accept help from the person in the other vehicle if it keeps you close to them.
- Do not drive home if you think you are being followed.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance immediately.** Turn away from your car and walk to a busier, staffed, well-lit place, such as a shop entrance, reception desk, petrol station counter, café counter, or car park office.
2. **Ask for help from a real person nearby.** Tell staff, security, or another safe-looking person: “A vehicle has blocked my driver’s door and someone inside is watching me. Please stay with me and help me get somewhere safe.”
3. **Call for help early.**
   - If you feel in immediate danger, someone is threatening you, or a crime is in progress, call 999.
   - If you are now safe but want to report suspicious behaviour, call 101 or use police online reporting.
4. **If you cannot speak safely on a 999 call, still call 999.** Try to speak, whisper, cough, or tap. If you are calling from a mobile and are prompted, press 55 to be connected to police. Pressing 55 only works on mobiles and does not tell police your exact location by itself, so give any location details you safely can.
5. **Ask staff or security to deal with the car, not you.** Ask them to check CCTV, announce for the other driver if appropriate, walk you to a safe place, or wait with you while police or someone trusted arrives.
6. **If you are already inside your car, lock the doors and keep the windows up.** If you are going to drive, put your seatbelt on. If the exit route is clear and you can leave without moving closer to the person, drive to a busy public place. If you cannot move safely and you feel at risk, stay locked in and call 999.
7. **If you need to reach your car and the driver’s side is blocked, do not wedge yourself into the driver’s gap.** Only consider using the passenger side if it is clearly safer, keeps distance from the person, and lets you leave quickly.
8. **Note details only from a safe place.** Record the number plate, make, colour, exact location, direction of travel, and a brief description. If it is safe, take one quick photo from where you already are; do not move closer for evidence.
9. **If you think you are being followed, do not drive home.** Drive to a busy public place, keep the doors locked, and call 999. If you already know a nearby staffed police station is open, you can drive there instead; do not search unfamiliar streets for one.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it was definitely a crime or just odd parking.
- You do not need to confront them, leave a note, or gather more evidence than basic details.
- You do not need to sort out parking complaints, insurance questions, or a longer report until you are safe.
- You do not need to return to the car alone just because you feel embarrassed or unsure.

## Important reassurance
Your instincts are enough reason to step back. Feeling shaky, angry, or unsure is normal. Choosing distance, witnesses, and help is a sensible response.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes only. If this happens repeatedly, especially with the same person, vehicle, place, or pattern, keep a simple record from a safe place and report the pattern when you can; police or specialist support can advise what category it may fall under.

## Important note
This guide is general information for immediate safety decisions. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice.
