# What to do if… two relatives both claim authority to make decisions after a death (UK)

## Short answer
Pause anything irreversible and ask both people for proof in writing. Authority usually depends on the will, the intestacy position, and any court-issued grant or confirmation for the part of the UK where the estate is being dealt with.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hand over keys, valuables, paperwork, passwords, or account access to either person just because they are family.
- Don’t sign forms or give instructions to banks, insurers, landlords, utilities, or estate agents unless your own authority is clear.
- Don’t rely on screenshots, photos, or informal messages as proof of authority; ask for originals or certified copies where documents matter.
- Don’t destroy, amend, staple, write on, or mark any will, codicil, letter of wishes, or probate papers.
- Don’t distribute belongings, clear the home, sell items, or close accounts while authority is disputed.
- Don’t argue the whole dispute in group chats or public posts; keep messages short, factual, and save copies.

## What to do now
1. **Identify which UK route applies before acting.**
   - If the deceased’s last permanent home was in England or Wales, the process is usually probate or letters of administration through the Probate Registry.
   - If the deceased’s last permanent home was in Scotland, the process is usually confirmation through the sheriff court.
   - If the deceased’s last permanent home was in Northern Ireland, the process is usually through the Northern Ireland probate system.
   - If the deceased lived in one UK nation but had property, accounts, or organisations involved in another, ask the relevant organisation or a probate professional which process they require before you act.

2. **Use one neutral sentence with both relatives.** Say: “Until authority is verified, I will only take practical safeguarding steps and will not follow instructions from either side without proper proof.”

3. **Ask both relatives for the same proof in writing.** Ask for:
   - the deceased’s full name, date of death, and last permanent address
   - the part of the will or codicil naming any executor, if there is a will
   - who holds the original will and any later will or codicil
   - whether any probate, letters of administration, confirmation, or caveat application has started
   - copies of any court-issued grant, letters of administration, confirmation, or sealed court papers already obtained

4. **Protect the original will and key papers without awarding control.**
   - If you know where papers are, do not search alone if the family is already in conflict.
   - Make a dated note of what is found, where it was found, and who was present.
   - Handle documents as little as possible, keep them flat and dry, and do not remove them except for agreed safekeeping or professional advice.
   - If there appears to be more than one will or codicil, treat it as a “do not act” situation until qualified advice confirms what should be used.

5. **Secure estate property only to prevent loss or damage.**
   - Lock doors and windows, check alarms, and arrange basic insurance-related safety checks if needed.
   - Take dated photos of rooms, valuables, vehicles, keys, and important documents before anything is moved.
   - Start a simple inventory of items you hold or can see.
   - Keep post together in one safe place and do not redirect it to one side of the family.

6. **Tell organisations that authority is disputed.** If banks, insurers, landlords, care providers, pension providers, utilities, or funeral providers contact you, say: “There is a dispute about who is authorised. Please require the appropriate proof before acting on instructions.”

7. **Use the correct pause route if someone is rushing an application.**
   - In England and Wales, a caveat can stop a grant of probate being issued while a dispute about who can apply or whether a will exists is addressed.
   - In Northern Ireland, a caveat can also be used where there is a dispute about who can apply for probate or whether a valid will exists.
   - In Scotland, confirmation and executry disputes follow Scottish procedure; ask a Scottish solicitor or the sheriff court for the correct urgent route before assuming the England and Wales caveat process applies.
   - A caveat or formal challenge can escalate costs and conflict, so keep communications factual and get specialist probate advice if assets may be at risk.

8. **For funeral arrangements, slow down final commitments where possible.** Tell the funeral director that authority is disputed, ask what proof they require, and avoid signing contracts or choosing irreversible arrangements unless the family has agreed or the proper authority is clear.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today which relative is right.
- You do not need to settle inheritance questions, distribute belongings, or clear the property now.
- You do not need to close bank accounts, sell assets, or redirect all post immediately.
- You do not need to answer threats or emotional pressure in the moment; record what was said and return to proof, safety, and written communication.

## Important reassurance
Slowing things down is not taking sides. When authority is unclear after a death, asking for documents and preventing irreversible decisions is a normal way to protect everyone, including the person who eventually proves they can act.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Deciding who can act, which will is valid, how executors should work together, or how to stop misuse of estate assets may need specialist probate advice in the relevant UK jurisdiction.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. If assets are being taken, someone is being intimidated, documents may be destroyed, or anyone is in immediate danger, seek urgent help from an appropriate professional or emergency service.
