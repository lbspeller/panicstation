# What to do if… your low-beam headlights stop working and you realise before setting off in the dark (UK)

## Short answer
Do not set off in the dark with failed dipped headlights. Pause the journey and use a safer plan: delay until daylight, use another vehicle, arrange transport, or get assistance where the car is.

## Do not do these things
- Do not treat main beam as a replacement for dipped headlights; it can dazzle other road users.
- Do not assume daytime running lights, sidelights, or fog lights make night driving safe or acceptable.
- Do not start the trip hoping the lights will come back on.
- Do not keep cycling the switch repeatedly if the lights are not responding.
- Do not attempt repairs in the dark, near moving traffic, or without the correct manual, fuse, or bulb information.
- Do not drive in darkness or poor visibility with failed dipped headlights just to reach a garage.

## What to do now
1. **Decide not to drive this car in the dark.**  
   If the journey would involve darkness or poor visibility, delay it, use public transport, book a taxi, get a lift, or use a different roadworthy vehicle.

2. **Check that it is really the dipped headlights.**  
   Turn the lighting control to manual headlights-on rather than auto. Check whether the rear lights and dashboard lighting respond. Try main beam briefly while stationary, then switch it off.

3. **Check for an obvious simple cause while parked.**  
   Make sure the engine or ignition mode is the one normally needed for your lights. Check that the light stalk or dial is not between settings. Look for any warning message on the dashboard.

4. **If both dipped beams are out, treat it as more than a single bulb until checked.**  
   Two bulbs can fail, but both dipped beams not working may involve a shared fuse, relay, switch, wiring, or control issue. Do not rely on guesswork for a night journey.

5. **Do one quick, reversible check only if you are somewhere safe.**  
   If you are at home or in a safe driveway or car park, use the owner’s manual to find the correct headlight fuse. Replace a fuse only if it is clearly accessible and you have the same rating. If anything is unclear, stop.

6. **Arrange help without driving at night.**  
   If you have breakdown cover, contact them and say: “My dipped headlights are not working and I have not started the journey.” Otherwise, book a daytime garage appointment or a mobile mechanic.

7. **If the car itself is creating a risk where it is parked, reduce exposure.**  
   Prefer moving it only within private property, such as a driveway or car park, at very low speed. If you are already in a place where the vehicle is an immediate obstruction, prioritise getting people away from danger and ask for assistance rather than making a night journey.

8. **Travel tonight only by changing the transport.**  
   Use a different vehicle with working lights, a taxi, public transport, or a lift. Do not build the plan around a lighting workaround.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact electrical fault right now.
- You do not need to choose the final repair tonight.
- Relay testing, wiring checks, switch testing, bulb replacement, and garage comparison can wait until daylight or professional help.
- You do not need to decide whether the whole trip is cancelled; you only need to avoid driving this car in the dark while the dipped headlights are not working.

## Important reassurance
Noticing the fault before setting off is a good catch. The problem is usually fixable, and the safest immediate decision is simply not to turn it into a night-driving risk.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Repairing the fault and confirming the vehicle is roadworthy may need a mechanic, auto-electrician, breakdown service, or garage.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice, mechanical advice, or a substitute for professional assessment. If you are not confident your vehicle lighting is working properly for the conditions, do not drive until it has been checked and fixed.
