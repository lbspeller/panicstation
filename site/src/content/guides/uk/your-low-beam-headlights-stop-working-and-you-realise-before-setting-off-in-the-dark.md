# What to do if… your low-beam headlights stop working and you realise before setting off in the dark (UK)

## Short answer
Do not drive the vehicle on public roads during the hours of darkness or in poor visibility. Abandon the journey, use an alternative vehicle or transport method, and seek professional mechanical repair.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use main beam (high beam) as a substitute for dipped headlights, as this dazzles other drivers and is illegal in built-up areas.
- Don’t assume daytime running lights (DRLs) or fog lights provide sufficient or legal illumination for night driving.
- Don’t attempt to "limp" the car to a garage after sunset; a vehicle without working dipped beams is unroadworthy at night.
- Don’t attempt to replace bulbs or fuses if you are unfamiliar with the vehicle's electrical system or if the car uses high-voltage HID/Xenon systems.
- Don’t repeatedly cycle the light switch if you smell burning or see smoke from the steering column.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the failure is occurring on all dipped-beam settings.**  
   **Turn the headlight switch manually to the "On" position rather than relying on "Auto" modes.**  
   - Exit the vehicle and check the front of the car to confirm both left and right units are dark.  
   - Check if the rear position lamps (tail lights) and number plate lights are still functioning.

2. **Verify that the issue is not a software or setting error.**  
   **Check the vehicle's "Check Lights" or "Bulb Failure" warning on the dashboard if equipped.**  
   - Ensure the brightness or "dimmer" switch for the dashboard hasn't accidentally been turned down, making it appear as though the electrical system has failed.

3. **Check for a simple, accessible fuse failure if you are at home or in a safe location.**  
   **Consult the owner's manual to locate the fuse box and identify the specific fuse for the dipped beams.**  
   - Only attempt to inspect or replace a fuse if you have the correct spare of the **exact same amp rating**.  
   - Note that many modern vehicles (post-2020) use electronic modules (BCMs) that do not have traditional user-serviceable fuses for headlights.

4. **Isolate the vehicle if you detect signs of an electrical short.**  
   **Switch off the engine and remove the key (or move the fob away) if you smell burning plastic or see smoke.**  
   - Do not attempt further troubleshooting if a fire risk is suspected.

5. **Deploy a warning device if the vehicle is in a hazardous position.**  
   **Place a 2026-compliant warning triangle or approved LED beacon at least 45 metres (147 feet) behind the vehicle if it is obstructing a road or in a poorly lit area.**  
   - Do not use a warning triangle on motorways.

6. **Arrange alternative transportation immediately.**  
   **Secure a taxi, use public transport, or use a different vehicle for your journey.**  
   - If you have breakdown cover, call them and specify that the vehicle has "total dipped-beam failure" and cannot be driven safely or legally in the dark.

7. **Plan for a daytime repair.**  
   **Leave the vehicle parked in a safe, legal spot until it can be inspected in daylight.**  
   - Mobile mechanics or local garages can typically address bulb or fuse issues during business hours.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if it is a bulb, a relay, or a switch failure right now.
- You do not need to buy spare parts immediately if it is dark and shops are closed.
- You do not need to report the fault to the DVSA unless it is part of a known safety recall.

## Important reassurance
Discovering a lighting fault before you set off is the best possible timing. While frustrating, it prevents a high-risk situation on the road where you could be dazzled, cause an accident, or be stopped by the police. This is a common maintenance issue that is usually resolved with a simple bulb or fuse change in daylight.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm prevention for a pre-journey lighting failure. It does not cover roadside repairs, wiring diagnostics, or motorway breakdown procedures.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional mechanical advice. Under the Road Vehicles Lighting Regulations 1989 and the Highway Code, driving without functional headlights during the hours of darkness (half an hour after sunset to half an hour before sunrise) is an offence. If you are unsure of your vehicle's safety or legal compliance, do not drive.
