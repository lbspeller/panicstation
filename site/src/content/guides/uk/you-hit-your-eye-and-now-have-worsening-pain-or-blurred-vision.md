# What to do if… you hit your eye and now have worsening pain or blurred vision (UK)

## Short answer
Worsening eye pain or blurred vision after an eye hit needs urgent assessment. Arrange A&E assessment now, and call 999 if you cannot travel safely or you think the injury may be severe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sleep on it or wait until tomorrow if your vision has changed or the pain is severe or worsening.
- Do not rub, press, or massage the eye.
- Do not try to remove anything stuck in or piercing the eye.
- Do not wear contact lenses until you have been assessed.
- Do not drive yourself if your vision is blurred, doubled, reduced, or you feel unsafe to travel.
- Do not rinse the eye after a hard impact unless a chemical, dust, grit, or loose material is in it.

## What to do now
1. **Choose A&E now if any urgent sign is present.** Go to A&E or call 999 if you have blurred, doubled, reduced, or changed vision; severe or worsening pain; headache or light sensitivity; nausea or vomiting after the injury; blood, pus, or fluid coming from the eye; obvious blood inside the eye after the injury; an oddly shaped or unequal pupil; you cannot open or move the eye; something is stuck in or has pierced the eye; or the hit was high-speed, such as from a ball, tool, mowing, gardening equipment, or a fast-moving object.
2. **Call 999 if getting there is not safe.** This includes if you cannot see well enough to travel, feel faint or very unwell, are alone without safe transport, or suspect a penetrating or serious eye injury.
3. **Ask someone else to take you.** Do not drive. If you can, ask another adult to come with you and bring any medicines you take.
4. **Protect the eye while you arrange help.** Keep it closed if that is more comfortable. If you are worried about another knock, loosely shield it with something clean and rigid, such as a paper cup taped to the bone around the eye, not pressed onto the eyelid.
5. **Leave anything stuck in the eye alone.** Do not pull it out. Keep the eye still, avoid pressure, and get emergency help.
6. **Remove contact lenses only if it is easy.** If a lens feels stuck, removal increases pain, or your vision is badly affected, leave it and tell the clinician.
7. **Use simple pain relief only if you normally can.** Paracetamol can be used if you take it safely. Follow the packet instructions and tell the clinician what you took and when.
8. **Say clearly what changed.** When you speak to A&E, 999, or NHS 111 where available, say: “I hit my eye earlier and now the pain is worsening or my vision is blurred.” Note the time of injury, what hit the eye, whether it was high-speed, and whether you have double vision, light sensitivity, new flashes or floaters, headache, nausea, or vomiting.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether it is definitely serious before seeking help; worsening pain or blurred vision after an eye hit is enough.
- You do not need to search for a diagnosis or try several home treatments first.
- You do not need a routine GP appointment before urgent assessment if your vision has changed or pain is worsening.
- You do not need to sort out work, sport, screens, or contact lenses now; the immediate task is getting the eye checked safely.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaken or unsure is understandable, especially when symptoms worsen after the first shock has passed. Urgent assessment is a protective step because different eye injuries can feel similar early on, and checking promptly is safer than guessing at home.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the hours after an eye injury with worsening pain or blurred vision. Later decisions about tests, treatment, follow-up, work, driving, sport, or contact lenses may need help from an eye specialist or another clinician.

## Important note
This guide is general information only, not a diagnosis, medical advice, or professional advice. If you have worsening pain, blurred vision, or any vision change after an eye injury, seek urgent medical care.
