# What to do if… your device warns that a certificate or profile was installed unexpectedly (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible device security risk: stop using the device for sign-ins, disconnect it from the internet, then check and remove any certificate, VPN, profile, or device management setting you do not recognise.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t tap “allow”, “trust”, or “install” just to make the warning go away.
- Don’t sign in to banking, email, password managers, or work systems from that device until you have checked what changed.
- Don’t delete random apps, photos, or messages in panic; focus first on the certificate, profile, VPN, or management setting.
- Don’t assume it is safe because it uses a familiar name; fake profiles and certificates can copy real-looking names.
- Don’t factory reset immediately unless removal fails, the setting keeps returning, or trusted support tells you to do it.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and isolate the device.**  
   Turn on Airplane Mode, or switch off Wi-Fi and mobile data. If the warning appeared on public Wi-Fi, leave that network and do not re-join it for now.

2. **Work out what kind of warning it is.**
   - **“Profile”, “Device Management”, “MDM”, “VPN”, or “Work/School”** usually points to a configuration, VPN, or management profile.
   - **“Certificate”, “CA”, “Root certificate”, or “Certificate authority”** usually points to a trust certificate.
   - If you are not sure, take a screenshot of the warning before changing anything.

3. **On iPhone or iPad, check for unknown profiles.**  
   Go to **Settings → General → VPN & Device Management**.
   - If you see a profile you do not recognise, open it, take a screenshot of its name and organisation, then choose **Remove Profile** or **Delete Profile** if that option is available.
   - Restart the device after removal.
   - If there is no profile listed, move to the certificate trust check below.

4. **On iPhone or iPad, check certificate trust if the warning mentions trust, root, CA, or SSL.**  
   Go to **Settings → General → About → Certificate Trust Settings**.
   - If anything is enabled under full trust that you did not intentionally install for a known reason, treat it as suspicious.
   - The fix is usually to remove the profile that installed it, then restart the device.
   - If the device says it is managed by work, school, or another organisation, do not try to bypass management; use step 6.

5. **On Android, remove unknown user-installed certificates.**  
   Menu names vary, but on many Android devices look for **Settings → Security and privacy → More security settings → Encryption and credentials**.
   - Check **User credentials** or **User certificates** and remove anything you do not recognise.
   - If you cannot tell what is safe, **Clear credentials** can remove user-installed certificates without removing the permanent system certificates the phone needs.
   - Clearing credentials may stop some work Wi-Fi, school Wi-Fi, or VPN services working until they are set up again.

6. **If this is a work, school, or managed device, contact the right IT support.**  
   Say: **“I got an unexpected certificate or profile warning. I put the device offline and have not logged in. Can you confirm whether an MDM, VPN, profile, or certificate was pushed to my device, and tell me how to remove it if not?”**

7. **From a different, known-clean device, secure your most important accounts.**  
   Start with your email account, because it can be used to reset other passwords. Then check your Apple Account or Google Account, banking, and any work account.
   - Change passwords for accounts that may have been exposed, and any other accounts using the same password.
   - Turn on or re-check two-step verification.
   - Review recent sign-ins, connected devices, forwarding rules, and recovery details.

8. **If the warning appeared after a link, app install, or Wi-Fi connection, undo that trigger.**
   - Uninstall any app you installed just before the warning, unless you are certain it is legitimate.
   - Forget the Wi-Fi network that appeared to trigger the warning.
   - Update the operating system and restart the device.

9. **If money, identity details, or work systems may be affected, report through the right route.**
   - If you live in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland and this involves fraud or cyber crime, use Report Fraud.
   - If you live in Scotland, report to Police Scotland via 101.
   - If this involves an organisation, a workplace device, or a live cyber security incident, follow your organisation’s incident process and use the NCSC reporting route where appropriate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you have been “hacked”; first make the device safer and protect the accounts that matter most.
- You do not need to contact every service you use today; start with email, your Apple Account or Google Account, banking, and work accounts.
- You do not need to factory reset unless the profile or certificate cannot be removed, keeps coming back, or trusted device or IT support recommends it.

## Important reassurance
An unexpected certificate or profile warning is a valid reason to pause. Disconnecting the device, checking what was installed, and protecting your email account are practical first steps that reduce the chance of further harm.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate risk and buy time. If the device is managed by an employer or school, the profile or certificate will not stay removed, or you suspect account access or financial loss, later decisions may need specialist IT, device maker, bank, or reporting support.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. If you are unsure whether a profile or certificate is legitimate, keep the device offline and ask the official organisation that would have installed it, such as your employer, school, device maker, or mobile provider.
