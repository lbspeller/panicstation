# What to do if… you are asked to collect belongings of a person who died from a hospital or facility (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the hospital’s Bereavement Office or Patient Affairs department to book a property collection appointment and confirm what identification you must bring. You will typically need to sign an indemnity form to acknowledge receipt of valuables and clothing.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not** arrive at the hospital or care home without a confirmed appointment or verbal confirmation from the specific team holding the items.
- **Do not** expect ward staff to have all items (valuables like jewellery, cash, and phones are often moved to a central safe or the hospital cashier’s office).
- **Do not** attempt to collect items if the death has been referred to the Coroner and staff state that specific items (such as clothing or medication) are being held as evidence.
- **Do not** sign the property release or indemnity form until you have visually checked that high-value items (purses, wallets, wedding rings, or hearing aids) are present in the bag.
- **Do not** take home prescription medications unless explicitly told to do so for disposal; hospitals usually incinerate these as clinical waste.

## What to do now
1. **Identify which department is currently holding the property.**  
   **Call the main hospital switchboard and ask for the Bereavement Office or Patient Affairs.**
   - Ask if the property is still on the ward or has been moved to a central office.
   - Ask if an appointment is required (most NHS trusts require a scheduled time).
   - Ask for the specific room location and the nearest hospital entrance/car park.

2. **Confirm who is legally authorised to sign for the release.**  
   **Clarify with the bereavement officer whether the "Next of Kin" or the "Executor" must attend.**
   - If you are not the primary contact listed in the hospital records, ask if you need a signed letter of authority from that person.
   - Note that for significant cash amounts or high-value jewellery, hospitals may require proof of Executor status.

3. **Prepare your identification and any required documentation.**  
   **Gather your photo ID and proof of address to bring to the facility.**
   - Standard requirements include a passport or UK driving licence.
   - Some facilities also require a utility bill or bank statement from within the last three months.

4. **Verify the inventory against the items provided.**  
   **Request a copy of the hospital’s property record (Patient Property Book) and check the contents of the bags.**
   - Check for "hidden" items: dentures, glasses, hearing aids, and mobile phone chargers.
   - Ensure you receive any "purple bags" (standard NHS bereavement property bags) containing clothing.

5. **Sign the indemnity or property release form.**  
   **Read the form to ensure it accurately lists the items you are taking.**
   - By signing, you are usually confirming that the facility is no longer liable for the items.
   - If items are missing, do not sign until the bereavement officer has noted the discrepancy and given you a contact number for the Patient Advice and Liaison Service (PALS).

6. **Establish the clearance deadline for care homes or hospices.**  
   **Ask the facility manager for the specific date by which the room must be cleared.**
   - Care home contracts often specify a 7-day to 10-day window for room clearance.
   - Confirm if there are storage fees if items are not collected by the deadline.

7. **Secure high-priority items immediately.**  
   **Identify house keys, car keys, and mobile phones as soon as you have the bags.**
   - If the person lived alone, house keys are a priority for securing the property.
   - Secure the mobile phone to prevent the battery from dying, as it may contain essential contact information.

## What can wait
- **You do not need to sort or laundry clothing immediately;** it can be kept in the sealed bags until you are ready.
- **You do not need to notify banks or utility companies in the first hour;** simply securing the cards and ID is enough for the immediate pause.
- **You do not need to decide which items to keep, donate, or discard today;** the goal is safe transit from the facility to a secure location.

## Important reassurance
The process of collecting a loved one's belongings can feel administrative and cold, but it is a standard safeguarding procedure. It is common to feel overwhelmed by the sight of hospital property bags; taking a friend or relative with you to handle the inventory check is a frequent and helpful choice.

## Scope note
This guide covers the first-step physical collection of personal effects from a healthcare or residential facility. It does not cover the legal distribution of the estate, probate requirements, or resolving disputes between family members.

## Important note
Hospital and care home policies vary by trust and provider. If a death is referred to the Coroner, some items may be withheld until the investigation or post-mortem is complete. This guide does not constitute legal advice regarding the ownership of the deceased's property.
