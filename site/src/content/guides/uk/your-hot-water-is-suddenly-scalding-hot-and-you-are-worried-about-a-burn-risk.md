# What to do if… your hot water is suddenly scalding hot and you are worried about a burn risk (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as an immediate scald risk: stop using hot taps, showers, and baths, keep children and vulnerable people away from them, and turn off the hot-water heat source if you can do that safely. If you smell gas, suspect carbon monoxide, or a CO alarm sounds, leave the property and call the National Gas Emergency Service.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using the shower or bath as normal.
- Don’t let children, older adults, or anyone with reduced mobility or reduced sensation use the hot water right now.
- Don’t keep resetting the boiler or turning the hot water back on to see whether it settles.
- Don’t remove boiler or immersion-heater covers, touch wiring, or adjust internal parts.
- Don’t operate electrical switches if you smell gas or suspect a gas leak.
- Don’t permanently lower stored hot-water temperatures as a quick fix without competent advice, because hot-water systems also have hygiene risks.

## What to do now
1. **Warn everyone in the home.**  
   Say clearly: “The hot water is unsafe right now.” Put a note on taps and shower controls if that helps. Keep children and vulnerable adults away from hot taps and showers.

2. **Stop using hot water.**  
   Use cold water only. Do not test the hot water with your hand, and postpone baths, showers, washing-up, and cleaning that would involve hot water.

3. **Turn off the hot-water heat source if it is clearly safe.**  
   - **If you have an immersion heater:** switch it off at its clearly labelled wall switch or timer control if you can do that without touching anything damaged or wet.  
   - **If you have a boiler:** use the normal boiler controls to turn hot water off or to the lowest setting.  
   - **If you are unsure:** stop using the hot water and arrange a repair rather than guessing at controls.

4. **If you smell gas, suspect carbon monoxide, or a CO alarm has sounded, switch priorities.**  
   Get everyone into fresh air. Open doors and windows if you can do that quickly and safely. Do not smoke, use naked flames, or turn electrical switches on or off. Call the National Gas Emergency Service on **0800 111 999**. Do not go back inside until you have been told it is safe. If anyone is very unwell, confused, collapsing, struggling to breathe, or has lost consciousness, call **999**.

5. **Arrange a competent check before using hot water again.**  
   - **If you rent:** report it straight away to your landlord or letting agent as a safety repair involving hot water and scald risk.  
   - **If you own:** contact a qualified plumber. If the fault may involve a gas boiler, use a Gas Safe registered engineer and check they are qualified for the work.  
   Say: “The hot water has suddenly become much hotter than normal. I have stopped using it because of scald risk.”

6. **If someone has already been scalded, give first aid promptly.**  
   Hold the burn or scald under cool running water for at least 15 minutes, or until the pain feels better. Remove clothing or jewellery near the burn if it is not stuck. Call **111** if you are not sure what to do, and call **999** or go to A&E for a very large or deep burn, or a burn on the face, genitals, or bottom.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out whether the fault is the boiler thermostat, cylinder thermostat, immersion heater, or mixing valve right now.
- You do not need to choose upgrades or replacement parts today.
- You do not need to keep retesting the water temperature.
- You do not need to decide anything about long-term hot-water settings until the immediate scald risk has been made safe.

## Important reassurance
A sudden increase in hot-water temperature is a reasonable thing to treat seriously. Stopping hot-water use, warning others, and getting the system checked are sensible protective steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce scald risk and prevent the situation getting worse. Later decisions about repairs, thermostats, stored-water temperature, or temperature-limiting valves may need help from a competent plumber, a Gas Safe registered engineer, your landlord, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. For gas, carbon monoxide, serious burns, or an unsafe home situation, use the relevant emergency or professional route.
