# What to do if… a stranger repeatedly appears near your workplace entrance at the same times (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause inside, or with other people, and tell your workplace immediately so you are not handling this alone. Call 999 if there is immediate danger; otherwise use 101 or your local police force’s online reporting route if you feel unsafe, targeted, followed, or the pattern continues.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the person alone or try to test them by approaching.
- Do not keep using the same entrance, route, or timing if you feel unsafe.
- Do not post publicly about the incident in a way that reveals your workplace, times, or routine.
- Do not try to gather proof by getting close, taking risky photos, or following them.
- Do not wait until you can label it as stalking or a crime before telling your workplace.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause first.** If you are outside, go inside your workplace, into reception, or into a staffed nearby business. Stay where there are other people, lighting, and cameras if possible.
2. **Tell your workplace immediately and name the pattern.** Report it to your manager, HR, security, reception, or building management: “A stranger has repeatedly appeared at our entrance around the same times.” Ask who is coordinating the response today.
3. **Ask for immediate help with your next entry or exit.** Ask for a colleague or security escort, someone to watch the entrance at your arrival or departure time, or permission to use a different entrance if one is available.
4. **Ask your workplace to log it as a safety concern or incident today.** Ask them to note the date, time, location, what happened, who was told, and what temporary protection is being put in place.
5. **Start a brief incident log without putting yourself at risk.** Record the date, time, exact location, what the person did, how long they stayed, a simple description, any vehicle details that were safely visible, and any witnesses at work.
6. **Ask promptly for relevant CCTV or entry records to be preserved.** Ask your workplace, security team, or building manager to retain footage and reception or security notes for the relevant dates and times, because retention periods vary.
7. **Reduce predictability for the next few days without isolating yourself.** Arrive or leave with someone where possible, avoid being alone in car parks or bike stores, use staffed or well-lit areas, and move any taxi or lift pickup to reception or another staffed point.
8. **Contact the police if you feel unsafe, targeted, followed, blocked, threatened, or the pattern continues.** Call 999 if you believe you are in immediate danger, the person is following you now, blocking your path, threatening violence, or a serious incident is happening. For a non-emergency report, call 101 or use your local police force’s online reporting route, describe the repeated pattern, and ask for a reference number.
9. **Widen the safety net for today.** Tell one trusted person how you are getting home and arrange a check-in once you are safely away from work.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it is stalking, harassment, coincidence, or a crime.
- You do not need to identify the person, learn their name, or confront them.
- You do not need to make big decisions today, such as quitting, moving, changing every routine, or handling this alone.

## Important reassurance
Noticing a repeated pattern and taking it seriously is a reasonable safety response. You are allowed to ask your workplace for practical support and to contact the police before the situation becomes clearer or more serious.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, recording the pattern, and getting the right people involved. If it continues, later decisions may need help from your workplace, the police, a stalking support service, a union representative, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, security, or other professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.
