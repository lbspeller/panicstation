# What to do if… you are banned from a place you need to access for work, school, or family (UK)

## Short answer
Do not go back to the place, even briefly, until you have the restriction in writing and know exactly what it bans. Ask the issuer for written clarification and a safe, written route for urgent access, such as work, education, child arrangements, medication, keys, or essential belongings.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “just pop in quickly” to explain; if the wording bans entry, even a short visit may be treated as a breach.
- Do not rely on informal permission from a receptionist, manager, friend, relative, security guard, or protected person.
- Do not contact a protected person, or ask someone else to contact them, if the document restricts direct or indirect contact.
- Do not go near an unclear boundary, entrance, campus, workplace, home, or exclusion zone while you are still trying to clarify it.
- Do not delete, edit, or tidy up messages, emails, letters, screenshots, or call logs about the ban.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe pause away from the restricted place.**  
   Stay outside the building, site, campus, home, street, or exclusion zone while you read the document and make calls. If you are already nearby, leave calmly and do not argue at the door.

2. **Identify what kind of restriction it is.**  
   Look for who issued it: a court, police, employer, school, university, landlord, council, or private venue. Note the date, case number or reference number, the exact place covered, any map or distance wording, and any “no contact” wording.

3. **Get a clean written copy before acting.**  
   If you only have a verbal warning, photo, text message, or unclear screenshot, ask the issuer for the full written notice or order. Ask for the boundary in writing, including any map, entrance rules, road names, campus areas, floors, reception points, or named people you must not contact.

4. **If it is a court order, treat it as strict unless the court changes it.**  
   Use the court name and case number on the document. Contact a solicitor urgently and ask what route applies to vary, discharge, appeal, or review the order so you can deal with essential access. Do not assume a protected person, employer, school, or venue can give permission if the court order says otherwise.

5. **If it is police bail or another police-imposed condition, ask for formal variation in writing.**  
   Contact the named officer, custody contact, or case team on the paperwork. Say what you need, such as a work shift, exam, child handover, medication, keys, or belongings, and ask what formal route applies to request a temporary or limited change. Keep following the current condition unless you receive written variation or professional advice that it has changed.

6. **If it is a workplace, school, university, or venue notice, ask for the policy and review route.**  
   Write to HR, the school office, student services, safeguarding contact, venue management, or the named decision-maker. Ask for the decision letter, the review or appeal route, any deadline, and an interim plan that does not require you to enter the restricted place.

7. **Ask for a controlled handover for essentials.**  
   For medication, passport, keys, work equipment, child items, school materials, or personal belongings, ask for a pre-agreed method: items left at reception, posted, collected by a nominated person, brought to a neutral point, or collected at an escorted appointment. Do not turn up unannounced.

8. **Protect work, education, or family arrangements without breaching the restriction.**  
   Send a short factual message: “I have received a notice restricting me from attending [place]. I want to comply and I am seeking clarification. Can we arrange a temporary alternative, such as remote work, online attendance, an alternative site, or a controlled handover of essential items?” Keep the message calm and save a copy.

9. **Make a simple compliance log.**  
   Write down when you received the order or notice, who gave it to you, what it says, where you have stayed away from, and each message or call you make to clarify it. Save copies in one folder.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether the ban is fair or whether to challenge it on principle.
- You do not need to write a long statement, gather witness accounts, or explain the whole history immediately.
- You do not need to post about it online or persuade people at the place to take your side.
- The urgent tasks are to avoid an accidental breach, get the exact wording, and arrange safe access for essentials.

## Important reassurance
Feeling trapped is understandable when a place you rely on suddenly becomes off-limits. The safest first move is not to negotiate at the door, but to create a written trail, keep away from the restricted area, and use the proper route to ask for limited access.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. What happens next depends on the exact document, the issuing body, the place, and the legal route that applies to your document and where you are in the UK, so later decisions may need help from a solicitor, adviser, union, school, university, employer, or specialist support service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Court or police restrictions may lead to arrest or criminal proceedings if breached, and private or institutional bans can still have serious work, education, housing, or family consequences. When in doubt, treat the restriction as strict until you have written clarification or qualified advice.
