# What to do if… you sent an online bill payment to the wrong payee and your due date is near (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as two jobs: contact your bank now to stop or recover the wrong payment if possible, and contact the correct biller now to protect the due date. Do not wait for recovery before dealing with the bill that is due.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait for the recovery process before contacting the correct biller.
- Don’t repeat the same payment to the same saved payee.
- Don’t assume a bank transfer will reverse itself automatically.
- Don’t send a replacement payment until you have checked the correct biller details and you can safely cover it.
- Don’t share one-time passcodes, card PINs, or remote-access permissions with anyone who contacts you.
- Don’t delete confirmations, screenshots, payment references, chat messages, or emails.

## What to do now
1. **Check what kind of payment it was.**  
   In your banking app or online banking, check whether it was:
   - a Faster Payment or bank transfer to a sort code and account number,
   - a saved bill-payment payee inside your banking app,
   - a card payment,
   - a standing order,
   - a scheduled future payment, or
   - a Direct Debit.

2. **If it is still pending or scheduled, try to cancel it now.**  
   Look for “cancel”, “stop”, or “amend” in the app or online banking. Save the cancellation confirmation or take a screenshot.

3. **Contact your bank using the app, the number on your card, or the number on its official website.**  
   Say: “I sent a bill payment to the wrong payee and the due date is near. I need urgent help to stop it or start recovery.” Ask:
   - whether the payment has left your account,
   - whether it can still be cancelled,
   - what recovery process applies to that payment type,
   - if it was a Faster Payment or bank transfer, whether they can start Credit Payment Recovery or their equivalent wrong-account recovery process,
   - whether there is a case number you should give if you call again.

4. **Keep any Confirmation of Payee warning or mismatch evidence.**  
   If you saw a “no match”, “partial match”, “unable to check”, or similar warning before sending, save a screenshot or write down exactly what it said.

5. **Contact the correct biller now and ask how to protect the due date.**  
   Give them the account or customer reference for the bill, not your banking passwords or passcodes. Ask:
   - the fastest safe way to pay today,
   - the exact payment reference they need,
   - whether a card payment, phone payment, or portal payment will show on your account fastest,
   - whether they can place a temporary hold on late fees, collections, service restriction, or reporting while the payment is corrected,
   - whether they can send confirmation by email or secure message.

6. **Make a replacement payment only after the biller confirms the correct route and you can safely cover it.**  
   Use the exact payee details and reference the biller gives you. Save the receipt, confirmation screen, transaction ID, date, and time.

7. **If the payment type was actually a Direct Debit, use the Direct Debit route.**  
   If a Direct Debit was taken in error, contact your bank or building society and ask about a refund under the Direct Debit Guarantee. Also contact the biller so they know the bill may still need settling.

8. **If it was a card payment, ask your card provider what options apply.**  
   Card payment routes are different from bank transfers. Keep working with the correct biller on the due date while your card provider checks what can be done about the wrong payment.

9. **Make a short record before details blur.**  
   Write down the amount, date, time, wrong payee name, wrong account details if visible, correct biller name, bill reference, bank case number, and who you spoke to.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide today whether to make a formal complaint.
- You don’t need to work out whether anyone was legally at fault before you contact the bank and biller.
- You don’t need to contact the wrong payee directly unless your bank advises that this is appropriate.
- You don’t need to write a long explanation; dates, amounts, references, and receipts are enough for now.

## Important reassurance
This is a stressful mistake, but it has a clear first sequence: stop or recover what you can, protect the bill due date, and keep proof. Acting quickly gives your bank and the biller more options.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from your bank, the biller, the Financial Ombudsman Service, a debt adviser, or legal advice depending on what happened and whether the money is recovered.

## Important note
This is general information, not financial, legal, professional, or debt advice. Banks and billers can have different cut-offs and processes. If you feel pressured, rushed, or suspicious, pause and contact your bank through your official banking app, the number on your card, or another trusted route.
