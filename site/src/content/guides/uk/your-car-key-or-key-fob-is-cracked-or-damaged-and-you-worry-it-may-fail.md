# What to do if… your car key or key fob is cracked or damaged and you worry it may fail (UK)

## Short answer
Use a spare key now if you have one. If this is your only working key, protect it from further damage and arrange a dealer, manufacturer, breakdown-cover, or reputable auto-locksmith route before it fails completely.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep repeatedly testing lock, unlock, or start buttons; repeated use may worsen an already loose casing, battery contact, or button fault.
- Don’t force the casing open or glue it shut unless you are following the vehicle or key maker’s instructions, or a suitable professional has told you to do it.
- Don’t leave the cracked smart key in rain, loose pockets with coins, or anywhere it can be bent, crushed, or pulled apart.
- Don’t assume a newly cut blade alone will solve it; many modern keys also rely on a chip, transponder, remote, or programmed smart-key function.
- Don’t post photos of your key online, especially clear photos of the blade, cuts, labels, or serial markings.

## What to do now
1. **Switch to a spare key if you have one.** Put the damaged key somewhere dry and safe, away from your daily keyring, so it is not flexed or dropped again.

2. **If this is your only key, reduce avoidable starts and lock cycles today.**
   - Combine essential trips so you are not repeatedly switching off and restarting.
   - Avoid optional stops in places where a no-start would be hard to handle, such as remote car parks, tight ramps, or queues where you cannot easily move aside.
   - Do not leave the engine running unnecessarily while stationary on a public road.
   - If you need fuel, still switch the engine off before refuelling.

3. **Check the exact backup entry and start method for your vehicle.** Look in the owner’s manual, vehicle app, or manufacturer guidance for your model. Some fobs have a hidden mechanical key blade for the door, and some keyless-start vehicles have a specific place to hold a weak or damaged fob so the car can detect it.

4. **Stabilise the damaged key gently.**
   - Put it in a snug protective cover or small pouch if you have one.
   - Use a small amount of tape only to stop the cracked casing spreading; do not tape over buttons, the battery door, emergency key release, or any part that needs to move.
   - Keep it dry and avoid pressure on the cracked section.
   - If the battery cover is loose, secure it gently so the battery is less likely to lift or lose contact.

5. **Arrange a proper replacement route today.** Choose one of these:
   - Your dealer or manufacturer service, especially for newer smart keys.
   - A reputable auto locksmith who can cut and programme the correct key, fob, or smart key for your vehicle.
   - Your breakdown provider or insurer, if your policy includes key assistance or key cover.

6. **Prepare the details they may ask for.** Have your photo ID, vehicle location, registration number, make, model, year, and VIN ready if you can. The VIN is often visible at the base of the windscreen. If you have the V5C logbook or policy details available, keep them nearby, but do not leave them in the car.

7. **If the key is already intermittent, plan the next safe pause.** Move the car only if it is sensible to do so, park somewhere legal and accessible for help, and save the number for your chosen dealer, locksmith, breakdown provider, or insurer before you need it under pressure.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to buy a third key, change locks, or upgrade security hardware.
- You do not need to diagnose every possible cause, such as water damage, battery contacts, casing damage, or programming faults.
- You do not need to find the cheapest long-term option before making the car usable and reducing the risk of being stranded.

## Important reassurance
A damaged key can sometimes keep working for a while, but it is sensible to act before it becomes your lockout or no-start problem. Using a spare, protecting the damaged key, and booking help are enough for the first safe step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Vehicle-specific entry, starting, cutting, programming, and security decisions may need help from your vehicle manual, dealer, manufacturer, breakdown provider, insurer, or a suitably skilled auto locksmith.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, mechanical, insurance, locksmith, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe where you are, prioritise getting to a safer place and contacting breakdown support or emergency services if needed.
