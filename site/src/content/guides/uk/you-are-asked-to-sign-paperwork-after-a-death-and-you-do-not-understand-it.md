# What to do if… you are asked to sign paperwork after a death and you do not understand it (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before signing. Ask for a copy, ask what the document authorises, and ask whether signing makes you personally responsible for any costs.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign while you feel too rushed, exhausted, or pressured to understand what the document means.
- Do not sign blank or partly completed forms, or anything with spaces to be filled in later.
- Do not sign in any role or capacity, such as executor, next of kin, informant, applicant, or attorney, unless you understand what that role means on that document.
- Do not agree to cremation, burial, embalming, donation, ashes instructions, or other irreversible decisions if you are uncertain or there is a family dispute.
- Do not assume you have to sign before you can ask questions or take a copy away.
- Do not share passwords, PINs, banking logins, or security codes with anyone for death paperwork.

## What to do now
1. **Use one sentence to slow things down.**  
   Say: “I cannot sign this until I understand it. Please give me a copy and explain what it commits me to.”

2. **Write down the exact name of the document.**  
   Ask: “What is this form called?” Common types include:
   - a funeral arrangements contract, estimate, or order form
   - a cremation application or cremation authorisation form
   - burial paperwork
   - hospital, hospice, care-home, or mortuary release paperwork
   - death-registration papers or register-office appointment details
   - coroner, procurator fiscal, or other death-investigation paperwork, depending on where in the UK the death is being dealt with
   - forms about the person’s estate, bank, insurance, pension, or benefits

3. **Ask the two risk questions before anything else.**
   - “Does signing this make me personally responsible for paying anything?”
   - “Does signing this authorise cremation, burial, embalming, donation, ashes instructions, release of the body, or anything else that cannot easily be undone?”

4. **Ask them to mark the exact signing box and the exact capacity you are signing in.**  
   Say: “Please show me where it says who I am signing as, and why I am the right person to sign.” If the answer is unclear, do not sign yet.

5. **Get a copy before signing.**  
   Ask for a photocopy, email scan, or clear photo of every page, including the terms and conditions, price estimate, declaration, and signature page. Keep the copy somewhere you can find it.

6. **If money is involved, ask for the cost position in writing.**  
   Say: “Please show me the total cost, what is optional, what has already been agreed, and who will be billed.” If it is a funeral director’s contract, ask whether payment can wait while you check the estate, bank, insurance, funeral plan, or family payment position.

7. **If the form mentions power of attorney, pause.**  
   In general, do not sign as an attorney after the person has died unless the relevant office, bank, registrar, funeral provider, or a solicitor confirms in writing that this is the right authority for this specific document.

8. **If a coroner, procurator fiscal, or other death-investigation office is involved, ask who is allowed to authorise the next step.**  
   Say: “Is the death investigation affecting registration, release, burial, or cremation paperwork?” Then ask the funeral director, registrar, hospital bereavement office, or investigation office what document is needed before anything proceeds.

9. **If there is disagreement or doubt, request a temporary hold on irreversible steps.**  
   Say: “There is uncertainty about authority. Please pause any irreversible steps, especially cremation, burial, embalming, donation, or ashes decisions, until this is clarified.”

10. **Get quick support before signing if the answer still is not clear.**  
   Contact the register office or local death-registration service for registration questions, the funeral director or crematorium for funeral and cremation-form questions, the hospital or mortuary bereavement office for release paperwork, an independent advice service for practical guidance, or a solicitor if the document affects costs, authority, property, the estate, or a dispute.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide every funeral detail today.
- You do not need to accept personal payment responsibility just to move things forward.
- You do not need to resolve the whole estate, executor role, inheritance position, or family dispute before you understand the document in front of you.
- You do not need to notify every organisation right now; first, avoid signing something unclear, costly, or irreversible.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to find paperwork difficult immediately after a death. Asking basic questions, taking a copy away, and pausing before signing are reasonable protective steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help prevent an irreversible or costly mistake while you are overwhelmed. The right paperwork and the right person to sign can depend on where in the UK the death is being dealt with, whether there is a death investigation, and what the document is for, so later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If anyone is pressuring you to sign, or the document could authorise something irreversible or make you personally liable for costs, it is appropriate to delay signing until you have a clear explanation and independent advice if needed.
