# What to do if… someone tries to steer you toward a quieter area and you feel pressured (UK)

## Short answer
Stop moving with them and move back toward people, light, and staff. If they will not back off or you feel in immediate danger, call 999 and say someone is trying to isolate you.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep walking to be polite or to avoid making a scene.
- Don’t let yourself be moved to a quieter place just to talk, find a taxi, or be shown something.
- Don’t go to your home, your car, a side street, or a secluded pickup point with them.
- Don’t argue about whether their reason is reasonable; you are allowed to say no without explaining.
- Don’t unlock your phone, open your wallet, or search your bag while they are close enough to grab or distract you.
- Don’t assume you have to handle it alone if staff, door staff, transport staff, or bystanders are nearby.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the walking pattern.** Plant your feet, turn so you can see what is around you, and take one or two steps away if you can. Use a short line: **“No. I’m staying here.”**
2. **Move toward the safest visible place, not the quietest or nearest place.** Head for a staffed, well-lit place such as a shop counter, hotel reception, venue entrance, busy bus stop, open café, or a group of people.
3. **Ask one specific person for help.** Pick a staff member, door staff member, transport worker, driver, or someone in a group and say: **“Can you help me? I don’t know this person. Please stay with me.”**
4. **If you are in a bar, club, or venue, use direct language.** Some licensed venues use **Ask for Angela** as a discreet way to ask staff for help. If staff do not recognise it, say plainly: **“I feel unsafe. Please help me get away from this person.”**
5. **Call police if it feels urgent.** If you feel in immediate danger, call **999**. Say your exact location, that someone is trying to take you somewhere quieter, and give a description if you can do that safely.
6. **Use non-emergency police routes only once you are safe.** If it is not an emergency but you want to report what happened or ask for advice, call **101** or use your local police force’s online reporting route.
7. **If you are on the rail network, go to staff or a help point.** Say you feel unsafe and need help staying in public. For a non-emergency rail incident, you can text British Transport Police on **61016** with what happened, where you are, and when it happened. Do not use 61016 for emergency help; call **999** in an emergency.
8. **Make it harder to follow you without escalating risk.** Change direction toward a brighter or busier area, step inside a staffed place, and stay there. If you think they are following you, do not head home; stay public and ask for help again.
9. **When you are safer, note the details.** Write down the time, place, what was said, a description, direction of travel, and any vehicle details. If venue or transport staff are involved, ask them to note the incident and whether any CCTV may be retained.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it counts as a crime or whether you overreacted.
- You do not need to confront them, educate them, or prove anything in the moment.
- You do not need to post about it or warn others publicly while you are still unsettled.
- You do not need to make a full report immediately if you are shaking; getting to safety comes first.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressured, frozen, or confused can happen when someone is pushing your boundaries in public. You are allowed to trust the discomfort, create distance, and ask for help without proving that the situation was dangerous.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance for the next few minutes. If this person knows you, keeps contacting you, or you think you are being targeted repeatedly, later decisions may need specialist support or police advice once you are calm and safe.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
