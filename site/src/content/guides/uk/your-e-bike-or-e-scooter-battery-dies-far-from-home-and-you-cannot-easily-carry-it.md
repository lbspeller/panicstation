# What to do if… your e-bike or e-scooter battery dies far from home and you cannot easily carry it (UK)

## Short answer
Get out of traffic to a safer pause point, power the device fully off, and switch to a safe “get home” plan rather than trying to keep riding.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t try to charge it with a random charger, adapter, power bank, or improvised cable.
- Don’t keep handling or transporting it if the battery or casing is unusually hot, swollen, leaking, smoking, hissing, cracked, smells chemical, or looks damaged.
- Don’t walk a long way to a station or stop before checking whether that operator allows your device.
- Don’t ride a private e-scooter on roads, cycle lanes, pavements, or other public places.
- Don’t walk the device in the carriageway if there is a safer walking route where pedestrians can still pass.
- Don’t abandon it unlocked or blocking a pavement, ramp, doorway, crossing, bus stop, or station entrance.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause point.** Get out of the road or cycle lane and away from junctions. Aim for a wide pavement, a lit area, or a staffed place such as a petrol station, supermarket, café, or station forecourt.

2. **Power it fully off and check for battery danger signs.** Turn it off completely and remove the key if it has one. If you notice unusual heat, swelling, smoke, hissing, leaking, damage, or a strong chemical or burning smell, step away, keep others back, and call 999 if there is smoke, fire, or an immediate fire risk. Do not try to tackle a battery fire yourself.

3. **Separate rental from personally owned.**
   - **Rental e-scooter or rental e-bike:** use the app to end the trip or report that the battery has died and you cannot move it. Follow the operator’s instructions. If the app tells you to leave it, leave it upright, not blocking access, and take a photo of the location.
   - **Personally owned e-bike:** plan around pushing, pickup, taxi, or a rail service that confirms it will accept your device.
   - **Personally owned e-scooter:** do not ride it in public. Treat it as something you need to walk, collect, taxi, or secure.

4. **Choose the least-effort safe way home.** Use one of these before committing to a long push:
   - **Friend or family pickup:** send your live location and a clear landmark, such as the front entrance of a named supermarket or petrol station.
   - **Taxi or minicab:** ask for a larger vehicle and say you have an e-bike or e-scooter before they arrive. Keep the device powered off. If there are any battery danger signs, do not put it in an enclosed vehicle.
   - **Rail, only if allowed:** check National Rail or the train company’s current rules before walking there. Many operators allow road-legal pedal-assist e-bikes, but restrictions, reservations, peak-time limits, and storage rules vary. E-scooters may be refused by transport operators.

5. **If you are in London, avoid a wasted trip onto the wrong network.**
   - TfL does not allow e-scooters or e-unicycles on its services or in its stations, even when folded.
   - TfL does not allow non-folding e-bikes on most TfL services, including the Tube, Overground, Elizabeth line and DLR.
   - Folded e-bikes are generally allowed on almost all TfL services, but staff may still refuse travel if the service is too busy or if the device is unsafe.

6. **If you must leave it briefly, lock it where it is visible and not obstructing anyone.**
   - Lock the frame, not just a wheel, to something immovable.
   - If the e-bike battery is removable and looks normal, cool, and undamaged, take it with you.
   - Photograph the lock-up point and note the exact location.

7. **Make the walk safer if you have to push it.**
   - Use a quieter, better-lit route rather than a fast road.
   - Walk a private e-scooter; do not ride it in public. For an e-bike, do not coast or ride it if it feels unstable.
   - Stop at the nearest staffed landmark and switch to pickup or taxi if you feel exhausted, unsafe, or unable to control the weight.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the fault, open the casing, or attempt a repair now.
- You do not need to decide now whether the battery or device needs replacing.
- You do not need to solve every transport rule now; just choose the safest workable way to get yourself and the device to a stable place.
- You do not need to contact the seller, insurer, or repair shop until you are somewhere safe.

## Important reassurance
This can happen suddenly, and the stress is mostly because the device is heavy and your plan has collapsed. Powering off, stepping away from battery warning signs, and arranging pickup or permitted transport is a steady enough first plan.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to get you and the device out of the immediate situation. Later decisions about repairs, battery checks, transport rules, insurance, or replacement may need help from the operator, manufacturer, retailer, repairer, transport provider, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, mechanical, fire-safety, transport, financial, medical, or professional advice. If the battery may be failing or there is any sign of smoke or fire, prioritise distance, warning others, and emergency help over moving or saving the device.
