# What to do if… an upstairs floor is bowing or sagging and you suspect water damage (UK)

## Short answer
Stop using the room and keep people, pets, and weight off the bowed section of floor. If you suspect an active leak, turn off the water only if you can reach the stop tap safely, then arrange urgent assessment.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep walking on the bowed area to test it.
- Don’t let anyone stand, sleep, play, or store items on the bowed part of the floor.
- Don’t move heavy furniture across the area.
- Don’t drill, cut, lift boards, or open up the floor while you are unsure what is underneath.
- Don’t use switches, sockets, or ceiling lights where water may have reached electrics.
- Don’t touch a wet consumer unit, wet socket, wet switch, exposed wiring, or anything showing arcing or overheating.
- Don’t try to prop the ceiling below with makeshift supports.
- Don’t delay getting help if the bowing is worsening, the floor is creaking loudly, cracks are appearing, water is dripping, or the ceiling below is bulging.

## What to do now
1. **Make a no-go zone immediately.** Keep everyone, including pets, out of the room and off the bowed section. Close the door if you can, and put a clear note on it.

2. **Check from a safer position, not from on top of the bowed area.** From the doorway or another safe spot, look for:
   - water dripping,
   - a damp patch or stain spreading,
   - a ceiling below that is bulging or sagging,
   - new or widening cracks,
   - a door suddenly sticking near the area,
   - loud creaking, cracking, or movement.

3. **If any warning sign is present, treat the area as unsafe.** Move people away from the room and the room below it. If there is immediate danger to people, contact emergency services. If a part of the building may be dangerous, contact your local council or local authority and ask for the dangerous structures or building control emergency route.

4. **If you suspect an active leak, turn off the water only if it is safe to reach the control.** Use the internal stop tap if you know where it is and can get to it without stepping into the risky area. Stop taps usually turn clockwise to close. Do not force a stuck valve.

5. **If the leak is clearly from one fixture, isolate that fixture if you can do it safely.** For example, use a local isolation valve for a toilet, basin, bath, shower, or appliance if it is accessible and dry. If you are unsure, leave it and wait for a plumber.

6. **Reduce electrical risk.** Do not use electrical items in or below the affected area. If the consumer unit is dry, safely reachable, and there are no signs of water, arcing, overheating, or exposed wiring, you may switch off the affected circuit or main switch. If there is any doubt, do not touch it; keep away and contact a registered electrician.

7. **Record what you can without entering the danger area.** Take photos or video from a doorway or safe place. Capture the bowed floor, any damp or bulging ceiling below, any visible leak, and the time you first noticed the problem.

8. **Call urgent practical help.** Contact an emergency plumber if a leak is suspected. Contact a chartered structural engineer, or a suitable building professional with structural experience, if the bowing is new, worsening, near a bathroom, or linked to suspected joist damage.

9. **Notify whoever is responsible for the building.** If you rent, contact your landlord or agent urgently and follow up in writing. If you are in a flat, contact the managing agent, freeholder, factor, or building manager as appropriate, especially if the leak or structure may affect another home.

10. **Tell anyone below the affected area to keep clear.** If there is a room underneath, keep people away from the area below the bowed floor until the leak and structure have been checked.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether it is definitely structural damage or only water damage.
- You do not need to lift floors, open ceilings, dry out hidden spaces, or start strip-out before the area is safe.
- You do not need to move heavy furniture out across the bowed section right now.
- You do not need to settle responsibility, insurance, landlord, neighbour, or lease questions in the first few minutes.
- You do not need to choose a permanent repair before the leak risk and load risk have been checked.

## Important reassurance
A bowed floor does not automatically mean the floor is about to collapse, but it is a sign to stop loading it and get the cause checked promptly. Keeping weight off the area, reducing water flow, and avoiding electrics near damp areas are useful first steps while help is arranged.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce risk and stop the situation worsening. Leak tracing, structural assessment, electrical safety checks, repairs, insurance decisions, and responsibility disputes may need qualified tradespeople or specialist advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional building, plumbing, electrical, insurance, legal, or emergency advice. If you believe there is immediate danger to people, use the appropriate emergency route.
