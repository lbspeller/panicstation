# What to do if… your surviving parent is overwhelmed after a death and you need urgent support for them (UK)

## Short answer
Make sure your parent is safe and not alone right now. If there is immediate danger, call 999; otherwise use urgent mental health support today: 111 in England, Wales or Scotland, or Lifeline in Northern Ireland.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave them alone if they are saying they cannot go on, seem severely confused, may hurt themselves, or may collapse.
- Do not argue with their grief, demand they “be strong”, or force decisions about the funeral, home, belongings, money, or family arrangements today.
- Do not try to calm them with alcohol, sedatives not prescribed to them, or extra doses of medication.
- Do not promise things you cannot know, such as “you’ll feel better tomorrow”.
- Do not drive them anywhere yourself if they are too distressed to travel safely, fainting, heavily sedated, dissociated, or physically unwell.

## What to do now
1. **Check immediate safety in plain words.** Ask: “Are you feeling like you might hurt yourself, or that you can’t stay safe right now?” If they say yes, you cannot get a clear answer, they have taken an overdose, they have seriously harmed themselves, or you do not feel you can keep them safe, call 999 or go to A&E.

2. **Make sure an adult stays with them.** Sit nearby, keep your voice low, and reduce noise and visitors. If you cannot be there, call a trusted relative, neighbour, close friend, faith leader, or family friend and say: “Please sit with them now. I’m worried they are not safe alone.”

3. **Use the urgent mental health route for where they are.**
   - In England, call 111 and choose the mental health option if offered.
   - In Wales, call 111 and press option 2 for urgent mental health support.
   - In Scotland, call 111 for NHS 24 mental health support.
   - In Northern Ireland, call Lifeline on 0808 808 8000.
   Say: “My parent has had a recent bereavement, is overwhelmed and not coping, and I’m worried about their safety and functioning at home.”

4. **Call their GP practice if it is open.** Ask for an urgent same-day call-back. Say it is an urgent deterioration after a bereavement and ask what local crisis, bereavement, or medical support is available today.

5. **Use a listening line while you wait.** Your parent, or you, can call Samaritans on 116 123. You do not need the perfect words; it is enough to say that there has been a death and they are overwhelmed.

6. **Put bereavement-specific support in motion.** Contact Cruse Bereavement Support in England, Wales or Northern Ireland, or Cruse Scotland in Scotland. If the person who died was known to a local hospice, ask the hospice whether bereavement support is available for family members.

7. **Reduce the next few hours to basics.** Put water, simple food, tissues, glasses, regular medication, and a phone charger within reach. Offer to answer calls and messages for them. Cancel non-essential visitors and appointments.

8. **Get practical adult support if home is not manageable.** Search the local council website for adult social care, emergency duty team, or out-of-hours social work. In Northern Ireland, search the local Health and Social Care Trust for emergency social work. Say: “A vulnerable adult is not coping at home after a bereavement; what urgent support is available?” If there is immediate danger, use 999 instead.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today about funeral details, probate, selling property, clearing belongings, finances, or where everyone will live long term. You also do not need to work out whether this is “normal grief” or something else right now. The task for now is safety, company, hydration if possible, and urgent support.

## Important reassurance
Overwhelm after a death can look frightening: panic, numbness, anger, confusion, crying, not eating, and not sleeping can all happen. This does not mean your parent is failing or that you have to solve everything. Today’s aim is to keep them safe, supported, and not alone while the right help connects.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours or days when a surviving parent is overwhelmed after a death. Later decisions may need specialist bereavement, medical, mental health, social care, financial, or legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you think there is immediate danger, call 999. If you are unsure, it is still appropriate to seek urgent help.
