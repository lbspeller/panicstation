# What to do if… your surviving parent is overwhelmed after a death and you need urgent support for them (UK)

## Short answer
Ensure your parent is in a safe environment and not alone, then call NHS 111 and select the mental health option (Option 2) for immediate professional triage. If there is an imminent risk of self-harm or physical collapse, call 999 immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave a severely distressed or confused parent alone, especially if they are expressing thoughts of self-harm or "not wanting to be here."
- Do not force them to make administrative or financial decisions, such as choosing a funeral director or discussing probate, in the first 24 hours.
- Do not provide alcohol or any medication that has not been specifically prescribed to them for this current situation.
- Do not use dismissive phrasing like "be strong for the family" or "everything happens for a reason."
- Do not drive them to a hospital yourself if they are physically shaking, hyperventilating, or experiencing chest pains; call an ambulance instead.

## What to do now
1. **Assess their immediate safety and physical state by asking direct questions.** **Ask if they feel they can keep themselves safe right now or if they are experiencing any physical pain, such as chest tightness or severe dizziness.**
2. **Arrange for a constant presence in the home for the next several hours.** **If you cannot stay with them, call a trusted relative, close friend, or neighbour to sit with the parent until professional help arrives or the situation stabilizes.**
3. **Call NHS 111 and select the mental health option (usually Option 2) for 24/7 support.** **Explain to the advisor that there has been a recent bereavement and that the surviving parent is overwhelmed, unable to function, or unsafe.**
   - Request an urgent clinician call-back or advice on the nearest "Crisis Cafe" or "Safe Haven."
   - If you are in Northern Ireland, you can also call Lifeline on 0808 808 8000 for 24/7 crisis support.
4. **Contact the parent's GP practice and request an emergency same-day appointment or phone consultation.** **State clearly that this is an "urgent mental health deterioration following bereavement" to ensure the request is prioritized by the duty doctor.**
5. **Use the Samaritans 24/7 listening line for immediate emotional grounding while waiting for medical callbacks.** **Call 116 123 from any phone to talk to a trained volunteer who can provide a non-judgmental space for the parent or for you as a supporter.**
6. **Reach out to Cruse Bereavement Support for specialist grief guidance.** **Call the Cruse National Helpline on 0808 808 1677 during their opening hours (Monday/Wednesday/Thursday/Friday 9:30 am – 5:00 pm; Tuesday 1:00 pm – 8:00 pm).**
   - If the deceased was under hospice care, call that hospice's family support or bereavement team directly, as they often provide rapid-response support for families.
7. **Simplify the parent's immediate environment to reduce sensory overload.** **Turn off the television, dim bright lights, and limit the number of people in the room to one or two calm individuals.**
8. **Manage incoming communications on their behalf.** **Offer to take their mobile phone and tell them: "I will handle the messages and calls so you don't have to think about them right now."**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on the type of funeral service or burial/cremation today.
- You do not need to notify the "Tell Us Once" service or government departments in the first few hours.
- You do not need to sort through the deceased's clothing, paperwork, or personal effects.
- You do not need to provide a medical "diagnosis" for the parent's distress; focusing on immediate safety is the priority.

## Important reassurance
Seeing a parent in a state of total overwhelm is frightening, but these intense reactions—including numbness, extreme weeping, or disorientation—are common responses to significant loss. You are not failing them by seeking professional help; you are ensuring they have the safety net required to process this shock.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hours following a bereavement crisis. It does not replace long-term mental health care, legal probate advice, or formal grief counselling.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute medical or legal advice. If you believe a life is in danger or a medical emergency is occurring, dial 999 immediately. NHS 111 and GPs are the appropriate primary contacts for urgent but non-life-threatening mental health crises in the UK.
