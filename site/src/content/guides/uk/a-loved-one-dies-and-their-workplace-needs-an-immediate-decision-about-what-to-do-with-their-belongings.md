# What to do if… a loved one dies and their workplace needs an immediate decision about what to do with their belongings (UK)

## Short answer
Instruct the workplace to **secure all items in a locked location** and confirm they will **not dispose of anything** until the legal Personal Representative is identified. If pressed for a choice, state: **"Please hold everything securely; I will provide formal collection instructions once the estate representative is confirmed."**

## Do not do these things
- Do not authorise the workplace to **dispose of any items**, including documents, papers, or perishables, without a written inventory first.
- Do not permit **informal collection** by colleagues or multiple family members, as this can lead to disputes or lost items.
- Do not provide a **shipping address** until you have received and verified a full list of what is being sent and the tracking method.
- Do not attempt to **bypass IT security** by asking staff for passwords or access to the deceased’s work accounts.
- Do not assume **next of kin status** automatically grants the right to remove items; legally, the workplace may require the Executor or Administrator to provide authority.

## What to do now
1. **Send a formal "hold" instruction via email:**  
   **Direct the employer to place all personal items in secure, locked storage and to halt any disposal or "clean-up" of the workspace.**
2. **Request a single point of contact for practicalities:**  
   **Ask for the name and direct contact details of one HR representative or manager to handle all further communications regarding belongings.**
3. **Require a written inventory and photographic record:**  
   **Ask the employer to document everything in the workspace, including items in desks, lockers, pedestals, coat cupboards, and tool stores.**
   - Request that they include a list of any company-owned property found (e.g., laptops, ID cards).
4. **Clarify the distinction between personal and company property:**  
   **Confirm in writing that you acknowledge company property (e.g., security passes, equipment) must be returned/retained, while personal effects must be held for the estate.**
5. **Establish the requirements for release:**  
   **Ask exactly what documentation they require to release the items (e.g., a certified copy of the death certificate or a letter from the Executor).**
6. **Set a specific timeframe for the next contact:**  
   **Inform the employer that you will provide a decision on the method of collection (courier vs. in-person) by a specific date, such as "within three working days."**
7. **Organise a controlled handover if collecting in person:**  
   **Request a scheduled appointment in a private room and ensure a staff member is present to sign an inventory receipt as items are handed over.**
8. **Document every interaction:**  
   **Keep a log of who you spoke to, what was promised, and the location where items are currently stored.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide which specific family member gets which item right now.
- You do not need to settle final salary, pension, or death-in-service benefit queries before securing the belongings.
- You do not need to have the Grant of Probate or Letters of Administration in hand just to ask them to **hold** the items safely.
- You do not need to provide a final destination for the items today.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed by administrative requests immediately after a death. Employers often ask for "immediate decisions" to clear space or for security reasons, but you have the right to ask for a pause. Requesting a secure hold is a standard and protective first step.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate stabilization of personal property at a workplace. It does not cover long-term estate administration, tax implications, or employment law regarding final payments.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Requirements for releasing property can vary between organizations. If there are disputes over who is authorized to collect items, consult the solicitor handling the estate or the Bereavement Advice Centre.
