# What to do if… a merchant confirms a refund but the money has not arrived in your account (UK)

## Short answer
Get the refund trace details from the merchant, especially the ARN for a card refund, then ask your bank or card provider to trace the refund. Do not rely on waiting alone if the refund has not appeared.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume “refunded” always means “already credited to your account”; it may only mean the merchant has started the refund.
- Don’t leave it so long that your card provider says a chargeback route is out of time.
- Don’t ask the merchant to send a second refund unless you are tracking exactly what has already been sent and when.
- Don’t close the account, card, or banking app access while you are trying to trace the refund, unless you have no choice.
- Don’t stop paying a credit card bill just because you expect a refund; keep the account safe while the refund is being traced.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the basic facts:** note the merchant name, amount, original payment date, refund confirmation date, and how you paid, such as debit card, credit card, bank transfer, Direct Debit, PayPal, or a wallet.

2. **Save proof of the refund:** screenshot or download the refund email, chat message, order page, receipt, or app screen showing the merchant says the refund was made.

3. **Check the places where a refund can be missed:**
   - Check pending transactions, completed transactions, and your latest statement.
   - Check whether the refund went to a card that has since been replaced.
   - Check whether the refund went to an account or card that is now closed.
   - Check whether the refund appears as a credit with a different merchant name or payment processor name.

4. **Ask the merchant for the refund trace details:** for a card refund, ask for the ARN, which means Acquirer Reference Number. Also ask for the refund date, refund amount, last four digits of the card or payment method refunded, and any processor reference or refund receipt.

5. **Ask your bank or card provider to trace the refund:** say, “The merchant says the refund has been sent. I have the ARN or refund reference. Please trace whether the refund has been received, rejected, or sent to a replaced or closed card.” If the first person cannot help, ask for the card transaction disputes or chargeback team.

6. **If you paid by card and the refund still does not appear, ask about chargeback:** chargeback is a card-scheme process and is not guaranteed, but it can help with debit, credit, or prepaid card payments. Card providers usually need to start the process within about 120 days from the transaction or from when the goods or services were due, so ask your provider what deadline applies to your case.

7. **If you paid by credit card, ask whether Section 75 may apply:** this is separate from chargeback. It may apply if the purchase qualifies, usually where the item or service cost more than £100 and up to £30,000 and there is a breach of contract or misrepresentation.

8. **If this was a Direct Debit problem, use the Direct Debit route:** if the wrong amount was taken, the payment was taken on the wrong date, or a Direct Debit should not have been taken, ask your bank about a refund under the Direct Debit Guarantee. If you receive a refund you were not entitled to, you may later have to repay it.

9. **If the bank or card provider will not help, make a formal complaint:** ask for a complaint reference number and keep a copy of what you sent. If you do not get a final response within the relevant complaint time limit, or you are unhappy with the final response, you can usually take the complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service. Some payment service complaints, such as bank transfers or Direct Debits, normally have a 15-business-day response period, which can extend to 35 business days in exceptional circumstances; most other financial complaints have an 8-week response period. You usually have six months from the date of the final response to complain to the ombudsman.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to leave reviews, threaten legal action, or change banks.
- You do not need to prove who is at fault before you ask for a trace.
- You do not need a perfect evidence pack before starting; the refund confirmation, amount, date, payment method, and ARN or reference are enough to begin.
- You do not need to understand card networks or banking systems to ask your bank to trace a missing refund.

## Important reassurance
A missing refund does not always mean the money is gone. Refunds can be delayed, routed to a replaced card, shown under a different name, or need manual tracing, and asking for an ARN trace is a normal next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps to locate the refund and protect any time-sensitive card or complaint options. Later decisions, such as a formal legal claim or specialist complaint support, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Exact processes and timelines can vary by bank, card scheme, merchant, payment processor, and payment method.
