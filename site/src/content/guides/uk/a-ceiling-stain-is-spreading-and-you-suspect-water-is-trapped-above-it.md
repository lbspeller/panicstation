# What to do if… a ceiling stain is spreading and you suspect water is trapped above it (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as an active leak. Keep people out from under it, avoid wet electrics, stop the water if you safely can, and contact the person or trade who can reach the source.

## Do not do these things
- Do not turn on lights, fans, or switches in the affected room, especially if the stain is near a light fitting.
- Do not touch wet switches, wet plugs, exposed wiring, or a consumer unit that may have water on or inside it.
- Do not stand under a bulging or sagging patch of ceiling.
- Do not put a ladder, chair, or bucket directly under a bulge.
- Do not poke, drill, cut, or try to drain the ceiling yourself.
- Do not assume it is only condensation if the stain is spreading quickly.
- Do not delay urgent calls while you tidy up.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Keep everyone, including pets, out of the room beneath the spreading stain. Move phones, laptops, chargers, and valuables away from the wet area if you can do this without standing under the stain.

2. **Avoid the affected electrics.** If water is near a light fitting, ceiling rose, smoke alarm, extractor fan, socket, switch, or appliance, do not use that room’s electrics. If you can reach the consumer unit from a dry, safe position, switch off the affected circuit. If you are not sure which circuit it is, switch off the main switch only if the consumer unit is dry and safe to touch.

3. **Step back if the consumer unit may be wet.** If you can see or suspect water on or inside the consumer unit, do not touch it. Keep clear and contact a qualified electrician or emergency help.

4. **Look for the likely source only from a safe place.** Check what is directly above the stain: a bathroom, toilet, airing cupboard, radiator, washing machine, dishwasher, roof space, or upstairs flat. If you see an overflowing bath, running tap, leaking appliance, or radiator leak, turn that fixture or appliance off if it is safe.

5. **Turn off the water if the source is not quickly controlled.** Use the inside stop valve, also called a stop tap or stopcock, to stop most internal mains-water leaks. It is often under the kitchen sink, in a downstairs cupboard, near the front door, or where the water pipe enters the home.

6. **If you are in a flat, contact the likely source immediately.** Ask the upstairs neighbour to stop using water and check their bathroom, kitchen, boiler, appliances, and radiators. Contact the landlord, letting agent, managing agent, caretaker, or building emergency number if access or shared pipework may be involved.

7. **Contain only what is already dripping.** Put a bucket under drips that are away from the bulge. Put towels at the base of a wall if water is running down it. Keep the floor as dry as you safely can to reduce slipping.

8. **Call the right help urgently.** If water continues, you cannot find the source, you cannot shut the water off, or the ceiling is sagging, contact an emergency plumber or your landlord’s emergency repair route. If water has been near lights, wiring, sockets, or the consumer unit, arrange a qualified electrician before using that circuit again.

9. **Contact your water company if the supply pipe or outside stop tap may be involved.** If your inside stop valve will not turn, you cannot find it, or you suspect a leak on the supply pipe into the property, use your water company’s emergency contact route or find your supplier by postcode.

10. **Record the basics once people are safe.** Take a few photos or a short video showing the stain, drips, ceiling shape, and any obvious source. Note the time you first noticed it, who you contacted, and what was turned off.

11. **Start drying only after the leak and electrics are safe.** Open windows if safe. Use a dehumidifier or fan only when the affected electrics and the area are safe for mains-powered equipment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make an insurance claim; basic photos, times, and contact notes are enough for the first pause.
- You do not need to strip, repaint, or patch the ceiling today.
- You do not need to decide who is legally responsible while water is still active.
- You do not need to start mould treatment immediately; stopping the leak and safe drying come first.
- You do not need to move every item in the room if doing so puts you under the bulge or near wet electrics.

## Important reassurance
A spreading ceiling stain is frightening, but there are useful first steps. Keeping clear of the ceiling, avoiding wet electrics, stopping water where possible, and contacting the right person can reduce danger and limit damage.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Finding the exact source, checking ceiling stability, drying the structure, repairing damage, and deciding responsibility may need a plumber, electrician, landlord, managing agent, insurer, surveyor, or other specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, electrical, building, insurance, tenancy, or other professional advice. If there is immediate danger, such as fire, electric shock, serious structural collapse, or someone is injured, move away and call emergency services.
