# What to do if… your child may have swallowed a medicine or unknown substance but seems fine so far (UK)

## Short answer
Get urgent medical advice immediately, as many poisons do not cause symptoms for several hours. **Call 999 or go to A&E now if you suspect the substance is dangerous or if symptoms appear; call 111 for immediate triage if the child is currently well and you are unsure of the risk.**

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for symptoms to appear before seeking help.
- Do not attempt to induce vomiting.
- Do not give the child food or drink unless specifically instructed by a clinician.
- Do not give other medicines, such as paracetamol, to "treat" the situation.
- Do not rely on internet search results to determine a "safe" dose.

## What to do now
1. **Assess the child for life-threatening symptoms immediately.** If they are unconscious, having a seizure, struggling to breathe, or have collapsed, call 999 or go to the nearest A&E department right away.
2. **Isolate the substance and stop further exposure.** Take the container or product away from the child and move them to a different room if necessary. Gently remove any visible pills or debris from their mouth and wipe their face with a damp cloth.
3. **Administer immediate first aid for skin or eye contact.** If the substance is on their skin or in their eyes, rinse the affected area with running tap water for at least 15 minutes.
   - For corrosive substances (like bleach or drain cleaner) that have been swallowed, give the child small sips of water or milk to ease burning if they are conscious and able to swallow.
4. **Gather vital information without delaying your call for help.** Locate the packaging and identify:
   - The exact name of the medicine or product.
   - The strength (e.g., 500mg tablets or 250mg / 5ml liquid).
   - How much is missing or may have been taken.
   - The approximate time the ingestion occurred.
   - The child's approximate weight and age.
5. **Contact the correct urgent service for your location.** 
   - **England, Scotland, and Wales:** Call **111** immediately if the child is currently well and has no symptoms.
   - **Northern Ireland:** Call your **GP or GP out-of-hours service** immediately; if you cannot reach them instantly, go to the nearest **A&E department**.
   - **Any location:** If you know the substance is hazardous (such as heart medication, iron supplements, or antidepressants) or if the child develops vomiting, drowsiness, or breathing changes, call **999** or go to **A&E**.
6. **Keep all packaging and evidence for the medical team.** Bring the bottle, packet, or any remaining substance with you to the hospital. If the child vomits, bring a sample of the vomit in a clean container if possible.
7. **Monitor the child continuously until they are cleared by a professional.** Watch for delayed symptoms such as unusual sleepiness, agitation, stomach pain, or changes in skin colour (bluish or pale). If any change occurs, upgrade your call to 999 immediately.

## What can wait
- Determining how the child accessed the substance can wait until they are medically stable.
- Cleaning up the area or searching for more missing items can wait until after the initial medical call.
- Calculating the exact toxic dose yourself is not necessary; the clinical triage team will do this.

## Important reassurance
It is common for children to act normally and look healthy immediately after ingesting a harmful substance. Seeking help now while they are asymptomatic is a proactive safety step, not an overreaction. Most accidental ingestions that are caught early are successfully managed.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions only. It does not replace the need for professional clinical assessment or the specific treatment protocols provided by NHS 111 or emergency services.
