# What to do if… you find an unknown device listed as trusted on your account (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the account as possibly compromised. From a device you control, secure the account, remove the unknown device or session, and check recovery details before you relax.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links in emails, texts, or pop-ups about the device; type the official address into your browser or use the account settings app.
- Don’t remove the unknown device and stop there; the person may still have a password, recovery method, app access, or session.
- Don’t change passwords from a shared, work-managed, or suspicious device if you have a safer device available.
- Don’t delete recovery methods, passkeys, or security keys until you know you still have at least one sign-in and recovery method you control.
- Don’t factory reset your phone or laptop as your first move if you rely on it for sign-in codes; you could lock yourself out.

## What to do now
1. **Use a safer sign-in route.**  
   Use your own phone or computer if you can. Open the account settings by typing the official address yourself or by using the Apple, Google, or Microsoft account settings already on your device.

2. **Change the password and check two-step verification.**  
   Change the affected account password to a long, unique password you have not used anywhere else. Check that two-step verification is on. Before removing any sign-in method, make sure you still control at least one working way to sign in and recover the account.

3. **Remove the unknown device or session.**  
   - **Apple:** check your Apple Account device list. If you do not recognise a listed device, remove it from the account.
   - **Google:** open Google Account security settings, review **Your devices**, and sign out of any device or session you are not sure is yours.
   - **Microsoft:** review the Microsoft account devices page and remove or unlink devices you do not own or use. If you suspect unauthorised access, use Microsoft’s **Sign out everywhere** option as well; it may take up to 24 hours and may not sign out an Xbox console.

4. **Check recovery and sign-in methods.**  
   Look for recovery emails, phone numbers, backup codes, passkeys, security keys, app passwords, and trusted browsers that you did not add. Remove anything suspicious only after you confirm you still have a recovery method you control.

5. **Check for silent access.**  
   In the email account linked to Apple, Google, or Microsoft, check for forwarding addresses, filters, rules, and connected apps you did not create. Pay particular attention to Gmail forwarding and filters, Outlook forwarding and rules, and any third-party app access you do not recognise.

6. **Check recent activity and payment impact.**  
   Review recent sign-ins, security events, purchases, subscriptions, and saved payment methods. If a bank card or account may have been used or exposed, contact your bank or card provider using the number on your card or the official banking app.

7. **Keep simple evidence if something looks wrong.**  
   Take screenshots of the unknown device, recent sign-ins, suspicious recovery changes, purchase history, and any alerts. Write down dates and times. Do not spend long investigating if the account is still unsecured.

8. **Report fraud or clear criminal activity if needed.**  
   If you lost money, were tricked into giving details, or see clear fraud, report it through Report Fraud if you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland. In Scotland, contact Police Scotland on 101 for non-emergencies. If a crime is happening now or someone is in immediate danger, call 999.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to wipe devices, change every account you own, or explain the situation publicly. First secure the affected Apple, Google, or Microsoft account and any email account that can reset it. Wider clean-up can wait until the main account is stable.

## Important reassurance
An unfamiliar trusted device can be a sign of account takeover, but it can also come from an old device, a recent reset, a browser session, or a location label that is not exact. Taking the steps above can often stop further access and give you a clearer picture of what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and reduce immediate harm. If the account controls work systems, family accounts, large payments, or sensitive data, later decisions may need specialist IT support, your organisation’s security team, or another appropriate professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. Platform screens and names can change, so use the official account settings and support pages for the most current wording.
