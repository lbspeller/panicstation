# What to do if… you are asked to verify a travel document and do not understand the process (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and confirm who is asking, what document they mean, and whether they mean an identity check, a certified copy, a translation, or legalisation with an apostille. Do not upload, post, or pay for anything until you have checked the request through an official contact route you found yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not upload passport scans or “selfie with passport” images to a link you received unexpectedly or cannot verify independently.
- Do not send an original passport or other hard-to-replace document unless you have independently confirmed the instructions, the address, and the return method.
- Do not pay a “verification fee” until you have checked the legal name of the organisation, why it is involved, and whether the requester actually requires it.
- Do not assume “verification” means legalisation or an apostille; it may only mean an identity check or a certified copy.
- Do not provide extra documents “to help” unless they are specifically required and you understand why.

## What to do now
1. **Get the request in writing and make it specific.** Ask for:
   - the exact document they need, such as a passport biodata page, visa, birth certificate, or certificate
   - what “verified” means in their process
   - whether they need an original, a certified copy, a translation, a digital upload, or a legalised document
   - the deadline and what happens if you miss it
   - the full legal name of the third party and why they are authorised to handle your document.

2. **Check the request through an independent route.**
   - For an airline, university, bank, employer, travel agent, or government body, go to the official website by typing the address yourself and use the contact details published there.
   - For a visa or immigration application, check the official application portal or the official communication route you used to apply.
   - Do not rely only on links, phone numbers, QR codes, or email addresses inside the unexpected message.

3. **Identify which process they mean before spending money.**
   - **Identity or eligibility check:** a provider checks your passport and may ask for a face match in an app or portal.
   - **Certified copy:** someone signs and dates a photocopy to confirm it is a true copy of the original. The requester may have rules about who they accept.
   - **Certified translation:** a translator confirms the translation is accurate and gives their details.
   - **Legalisation or apostille:** the UK Legalisation Office checks eligible UK documents, signatures, stamps, or seals and attaches an apostille for use abroad.

4. **If they mean a certified copy, confirm the accepted certifier first.**
   - Ask the requester: “Which professions do you accept to certify this copy, and do you require specific wording?”
   - GOV.UK gives wording for certifying a true copy, but the organisation asking for the document may have its own rules.
   - Take the original document and the photocopy to the certifier; do not send an original to a person or service you have not checked.

5. **If they mean legalisation or an apostille, use the official UK route.**
   - Check that the receiving country or organisation has asked for a UK document to be legalised, not just certified.
   - Apply through the UK Legalisation Office route on GOV.UK and keep the application confirmation and delivery details.
   - If a recipient wants to check an apostille, use the GOV.UK apostille verification service.

6. **If you are abroad, check official consular guidance without assuming the embassy can do it.**
   - British embassies, high commissions, and consulates may be able to direct you to relevant support or local services.
   - Certification by a British embassy or consulate is limited. In non-Commonwealth countries, they may certify a copy of an original UK passport only in specific circumstances. In Commonwealth countries, they cannot certify a copy of any document.
   - If the requester says only a British embassy or consulate can verify the document, ask them to put that requirement in writing before you pay or travel.

7. **If you already sent documents and now feel unsure, limit further harm.**
   - Stop sending more documents or money until you have checked the request.
   - Save emails, texts, payment requests, web addresses, tracking details, and upload confirmations.
   - If you suspect fraud and you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report it through Report Fraud. If you are in Scotland, contact Police Scotland through its non-emergency reporting routes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to challenge the requirement; first identify what “verification” means in this specific request.
- You do not need to pay for expedited services until you have confirmed the request is legitimate and necessary.
- You do not need to send extra documents “just in case”. Provide only the minimum required once the request has been checked.

## Important reassurance
“Verification” is used for several different processes. Slowing down to confirm the exact meaning is a normal safety step and can prevent both scams and wasted time on the wrong procedure.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Once you know whether the request is for an identity check, a certified copy, a translation, or legalisation with an apostille, later decisions may need help from the requesting organisation, an official document service, a solicitor, a notary, or consular guidance.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, financial, or professional advice. Requirements vary by country, organisation, and document type, and can change. If anything feels inconsistent, high-pressure, or payment-focused, treat it as suspicious until independently confirmed.
