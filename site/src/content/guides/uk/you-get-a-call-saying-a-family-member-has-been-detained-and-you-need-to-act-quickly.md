# What to do if… you get a call saying a family member has been detained and you need to act quickly (UK)

## Short answer
End the call immediately and verify the claim through official channels. Contact the police on 101 or via their official website to confirm if the person is in custody; never use contact details provided by the caller.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money, bank transfers, gift cards, or cryptocurrency to "secure release" or pay for "legal fees."
- Do not stay on the line or follow instructions to keep the call secret from other family members.
- Do not provide personal information, including your address, date of birth, or banking passwords.
- Do not call any "verification number" provided by the caller; scammers can spoof these or use accomplices.
- Do not assume a "police" caller ID is genuine, as phone numbers can be easily spoofed.

## What to do now
1. **End the call immediately to break the sense of urgency.** Scammers rely on panic to prevent you from thinking clearly. Simply state you will call back through official channels and hang up.
2. **Note down every detail you can remember.**
   - The time of the call and the number displayed.
   - The name, rank, or station the caller claimed to represent.
   - Any "reference numbers" or specific details provided about the relative.
3. **Verify the detention through an official police route.**
   - **Call 101** (the non-emergency number) and explain you received a call about a relative in custody. 
   - Ask to speak to the **Custody Suite** of the station mentioned.
   - Be aware that due to data protection, 101 operators may not confirm detention unless you are the person the detainee nominated to be informed. 
4. **If the relative is under 18 or a vulnerable adult, request an "Appropriate Adult."**
   - The police are legally required to have an Appropriate Adult present for these individuals.
   - Ask if you can fulfill this role or if a professional Appropriate Adult has been called.
5. **Ensure the detainee has legal representation.**
   - If custody is confirmed, ask if they have requested the **Duty Solicitor** (which is free and independent).
   - Do not pay for private solicitors over the phone; legal aid for police station attendance is a right in the UK regardless of income.
6. **Report the incident if you suspect a scam.**
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** Report to **Report Fraud** (the national fraud reporting center, formerly Action Fraud) at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.
   - **Scotland:** Report directly to **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
7. **Contact your bank immediately if you have already shared financial details.** Ask for the fraud or "Stop Payments" department to secure your account.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the guilt or innocence of your relative right now.
- You do not need to hire a private lawyer immediately; the duty solicitor can handle the first interview.
- You do not need to pay "bail" or "fines" over the phone; the UK police do not collect bail money in this manner.
- You do not need to alert your entire social circle until the situation is verified.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sudden spike in adrenaline or fear during these calls. Scammers use these emotions to bypass your logic. Taking five minutes to hang up and call 101 is the most effective way to protect your relative and yourself. If they are truly in custody, a five-minute delay for verification will not change their legal standing or safety.

## Scope note
This guide is for the immediate stabilization and verification of a reported detention. It does not cover long-term criminal defense, court procedures, or prison visitation rules.

## Important note
Police procedures and disclosure rules vary between England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. Officers may be restricted in what they can tell you over the phone for privacy reasons. This guide does not constitute legal advice.
