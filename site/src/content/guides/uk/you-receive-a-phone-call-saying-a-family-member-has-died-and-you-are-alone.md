# What to do if… you receive a phone call saying a family member has died and you are alone (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself safe and steady first, then verify the call through an official route you control, such as the hospital switchboard, the hospital’s official website, police 101, or the official website of the named organisation, before you tell lots of people or make decisions.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t drive anywhere right now if you feel shaky, tearful, numb, faint, or on autopilot.
- Don’t send money, bank details, one-time passcodes, login codes, or copies of ID to anyone on the phone, even if they say it is for release, fees, paperwork, or urgent travel.
- Don’t post on social media or message lots of people before you have verified the news.
- Don’t agree to anything immediate, such as funeral arrangements, contracts, travel, payments, or collecting belongings, while you are still in shock.
- Don’t stay on the line if the caller is pressuring you, hostile, asking for secrecy, or refusing to let you call back through an official number.

## What to do now
1. **Make your body safer for the next few minutes.** Sit down, put both feet on the floor, and take slow breaths. Keep your phone charged and near you. If you feel faint, sit or lie down somewhere safe; if you might collapse or cannot stay safe, call 999.
2. **Write down exactly what you were told.** Note the caller’s name, role, organisation, phone number shown, reference or incident number, time of call, where they say your family member is, and what they say has happened.
3. **Treat the call as unverified until you confirm it yourself.** If they ask for money, secrecy, bank details, passcodes, urgent transfers, or copies of ID, end the call and verify through an official route.
4. **Verify using a call-back you control.**
   - If they said **hospital or NHS**: ask for the hospital name and ward or department. Hang up, find the hospital’s official website, trust or health board page, or a known directory entry, call the main switchboard, and ask to be put through to the ward, bereavement office, or on-call team that can confirm what they are able to confirm.
   - If they said **police**: ask for the police force area and any reference or incident number. Hang up and call 101, or use the force’s official website contact route. Ask them to verify the contact and reference.
   - Use **999** only if there is immediate danger to life, violence, a crime happening now, or another emergency happening right now.
5. **Bring one other person into the moment.** Call or message one trusted person: “I’ve had a call about [name]. I’m alone. Can you stay on the phone while I verify it?” If nobody answers and you feel unsafe alone, consider knocking for a neighbour or going to a nearby staffed place.
6. **If the death is confirmed and you are the contact person, ask only what you need for today.**
   - “Where is [name] now?”
   - “Who is the best point of contact?”
   - “What are the opening hours?”
   - “What official number should I call back on?”
   - “Is this being handled by a coroner, the Procurator Fiscal, or another death investigation service?” if they mention this.
7. **Make one calm, factual call to the closest other family decision-maker.** Keep it short: whether the news is confirmed or still unverified, where the person is said to be, and the official route you used or will use to check.
8. **If you cannot stay safe alone, treat that as urgent.** Call 999 if you are in immediate danger or need emergency help. For free, 24-hour listening support, call Samaritans on 116 123.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide about funerals, belongings, probate, money, travel, or what happens next tonight.
- You do not need to contact every relative, friend, employer, school, or neighbour immediately; one or two key people is enough.
- You can wait to deal with registering the death and government notifications until you have verified facts and support around you. In England, Wales and Scotland this may later include Tell Us Once; Northern Ireland has separate guidance and services.

## Important reassurance
Shock can feel like numbness, confusion, shaking, crying, disbelief, or being weirdly calm. None of that means you are handling this wrongly. Your only job right now is to steady yourself, verify the information, and avoid irreversible decisions while you are alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the first hours after the call. Later decisions may depend on where the death occurred, whether a hospital, police force, coroner, Procurator Fiscal, registrar, or Northern Ireland service is involved, and whether you need specialist legal, medical, bereavement, or practical help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are unsure the call is genuine, or you feel unsafe, prioritise immediate safety and verification through official numbers.
