# What to do if… you have lost your phone and cannot receive verification codes for your accounts (UK)

## Short answer
Use a trusted device to lock or mark the phone as lost, then contact your mobile network provider to bar the SIM and get a replacement SIM or eSIM for the same number.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep guessing passwords or repeatedly requesting codes if the service warns you about lockouts.
- Don’t go to an unfamiliar location or confront anyone because a phone-finding map shows the device there.
- Don’t use a public or shared device for email or password-manager sign-ins if you can avoid it.
- If you must use a shared device, use a private window, don’t save passwords, sign out fully, and later remove that session from the account’s security settings.
- Don’t post your phone number, home address, or “text this number” messages on social media.
- Don’t remove a missing iPhone from Find My just to tidy your device list; removing it can weaken Activation Lock in some cases.
- Don’t assume it is harmless because it may be “just lost”; secure the phone and SIM first.

## What to do now
1. **Lock or mark the phone as lost from a trusted device.**
   - For iPhone, go to `https://www.icloud.com/find` and use **Mark as Lost** or **Lost Mode**. Apple says you do not need a verification code just to sign in to iCloud.com/find and mark a device as lost.
   - For Android, go to `https://www.android.com/find` or use Google Find Hub. Use **Mark as lost**, **Play sound**, or other available options if the phone is connected and Find Hub was enabled.
   - If the phone appears at an address you do not recognise, do not try to recover it yourself.

2. **Contact your mobile network provider straight away.**
   - Ask them to **bar the SIM** so the number cannot be used from the missing phone.
   - Ask for a **replacement SIM or eSIM with the same number**, so you can receive verification codes again.
   - Ask whether they can block the handset using its **IMEI**, and what details they need from you.
   - Ask whether there could be charges from unauthorised use before you reported it missing.

3. **Protect the account that unlocks the rest of your accounts.**
   - Start with your main email account.
   - Use the provider’s official recovery page, recovery email, backup code, recovery prompt, or trusted contact option if available.
   - Follow the provider’s recovery flow carefully; avoid repeated guesses if it warns you about delays or lockouts.
   - Once you get in, check for unfamiliar forwarding rules, recovery emails, recovery phone numbers, and signed-in devices.

4. **Prioritise the accounts that can cause the most harm.**
   - Secure your email, password manager, mobile network account, Apple or Google account, banking, and main messaging apps first.
   - Change passwords for accounts that show unfamiliar access, password reset messages, or recovery-detail changes.
   - Sign out of unfamiliar sessions where the account gives you that option.

5. **When the replacement SIM or eSIM is active, reset verification methods.**
   - Confirm that your number works again before changing multiple accounts.
   - Update recovery phone numbers, backup codes, trusted devices, and authenticator settings for the accounts you use most.
   - Remove the missing phone as a trusted device only when you are sure it is the right step for that account and it will not weaken device recovery or an insurance claim.

6. **If you think the phone was stolen, report it to police.**
   - For a non-emergency stolen phone report, use your local police force’s online reporting route or call **101**.
   - Call **999** only if someone is in immediate danger, a serious offence is in progress, or the situation is otherwise an emergency.
   - Keep the crime reference number and the IMEI if you have it or can get it from your network provider.

7. **If you see signs of fraud or account takeover, report that too.**
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud for cyber crime or fraud.
   - In Scotland, report fraud or cyber crime through Police Scotland by calling **101**.
   - Contact your bank immediately if banking apps, cards, payments, or account alerts were on the phone or anything looks suspicious.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether the phone is definitely lost or stolen.
- You do not need to change every password at once unless there are signs of account access.
- You do not need to erase the phone immediately if locking it, barring the SIM, and moving your number are still possible.
- You do not need to choose a new long-term security setup today; backup codes, passkeys, extra recovery methods, and a spare authenticator route can be reviewed after access is stable.

## Important reassurance
This situation feels trapping because the missing phone is also the key to your accounts. The first goal is not to solve everything at once; it is to lock the device, protect the number, restore code access, and secure the accounts that control the rest.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise access and reduce harm. Later decisions about insurance, replacement devices, account recovery disputes, or suspected fraud may need help from the relevant provider, bank, police route, or specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, technical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you see signs of fraud, unexpected number-transfer messages, new logins, password resets you did not request, or payments you did not make, treat it as urgent and contact your bank and relevant providers immediately.
