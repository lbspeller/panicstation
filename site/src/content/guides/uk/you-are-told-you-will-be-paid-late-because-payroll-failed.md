# What to do if… you are told you will be paid late because payroll failed (UK)

## Short answer
Request an immediate short-hold or supervised export window from your IT department to retrieve specific personal items before the wipe occurs. Do not attempt to bypass security software or copy work-related material, as this may be treated as a security breach or gross misconduct.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to bypass Mobile Device Management (MDM) or security software to stop the wipe.
- Do not copy, email, or upload work product or confidential company data alongside personal items.
- Do not use personal USB drives or unapproved cloud storage to move data without explicit IT permission.
- Do not manually delete system logs, browser history, or messages in an attempt to hide activity before the wipe.
- Do not repeatedly enter incorrect passwords if you are locked out, as this can trigger an immediate automated wipe.
- Do not assume that data can be recovered after the wipe has been initiated.

## What to do now
1. **Document the wipe notice and the current status of the device immediately.** Use an external camera (such as your personal phone) to take a photo of the wipe notification or deadline on the screen.
2. **Identify the specific personal items you need to preserve.** Create a written list of non-work items, such as personal photos, contact lists stored locally, browser bookmarks for personal accounts, and professional certifications.
3. **Request a supervised export window from IT or HR in writing.** Send an email or support ticket stating that you have specific personal data on the device and requesting a 60-minute window or a supervised transfer to an approved encrypted drive or secure link.
4. **Secure and de-authorise your personal accounts from the device.** Log out of personal email, social media, and banking accounts through the device settings, and then use a separate trusted device to "remove" or "sign out" the work device from those accounts' trusted session lists.
5. **Download essential employment documents from the HR portal rather than the device drive.** Access your employer’s self-service system to save copies of your latest payslips, P45 (if you have already left), or P60 (if you are currently employed on April 5) directly to a personal location.
6. **Ensure you have recovery access for any two-factor authentication (2FA) linked to the device.** If the work phone is your primary 2FA device for personal accounts, generate and save backup codes or move the authentication to a personal device **before** the wipe begins.
7. **Submit a formal Subject Access Request (SAR) if IT refuses access to personal data.** Under the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, you have the right to request a copy of your personal data held by the organisation.
   - Be aware that under 2026 rules, employers can "stop the clock" on the response deadline if they require clarification of your request.
   - Be specific that you are seeking personal files located on the specific device ID or serial number.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the legality of the wipe or your termination status in this moment.
- You do not need to organise or file the items you retrieve; focus entirely on the transfer process.
- You do not need to respond to the substance of any exit or disciplinary interview while managing the data transfer.

## Important reassurance
It is common for personal and professional data to become commingled on modern work devices. Most IT departments have standard procedures for managed offboarding. Acting transparently and requesting assistance through official channels is the most effective way to prevent permanent data loss without triggering security alerts.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate steps for data preservation and risk mitigation during a remote wipe scenario. It does not provide legal strategy for employment disputes or technical instructions for data recovery.

## Important note
This information is provided for first-step guidance and does not constitute legal or technical advice. Workplace policies and the Computer Misuse Act 1990 govern the use of company equipment; unauthorised access or data removal can lead to criminal or civil penalties. Under the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, employers have specific obligations regarding personal data, but these do not typically grant you an absolute right to delay a security-mandated wipe.
