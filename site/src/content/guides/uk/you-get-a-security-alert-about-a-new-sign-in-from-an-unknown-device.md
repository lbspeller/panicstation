# What to do if… you get a security alert about a new sign-in from an unknown device (UK)

## Short answer
Access the account directly through a trusted app or by typing the website address manually to verify the alert; if the activity is unrecognised, secure the account immediately by changing the password and terminating all active sessions.

## Do not do these things
- Don't click any links, "Yes/No" buttons, or "Secure Account" prompts within the email or SMS alert itself.
- Don't provide a verification code or One-Time Password (OTP) to anyone who contacts you, even if they claim to be from the service's security team.
- Don't assume the alert is false just because the listed location is in a different city (IP-based locations are often imprecise).
- Don't use a variation of your current password when resetting.
- Don't ignore the alert if you recently used a VPN, but do check if the VPN location matches the alert before taking drastic action.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the alert through an independent channel.**  
   **Open the official app on your device or type the service's website address directly into your browser.**
   - Navigate to the **Security**, **Privacy**, or **Recent Activity** section.
   - Compare the time, device type, and browser listed in the alert with the activity log in the account.

2. **Secure the account using the service's internal tools.**  
   **If the sign-in is not yours, select the "This wasn't me" or "Secure account" option found within the official settings page.**
   - This typically triggers a guided security flow provided by the service (e.g., Google's "Security Checkup" or Microsoft's "Sign-in activity" review).

3. **Change your password immediately from a known-safe device.**  
   **Create a new, unique password consisting of three random words or a long, complex string that you have never used elsewhere.**
   - If you use a password manager, update the entry there first.

4. **Terminate all active sessions and remove unrecognised devices.**  
   **Use the "Sign out of all devices" or "Log out everywhere" feature in the security settings.**
   - Manually remove any "Trusted Devices," "Linked Devices," or "Passkeys" that you do not recognise.

5. **Enable or update Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).**  
   **Switch to an authenticator app or hardware security key if you are currently using SMS-based codes.**
   - Download a new set of "Backup Codes" or "Recovery Codes" and store them offline in a secure location.

6. **Audit recovery and communication settings.**  
   **Check that the recovery email address, phone number, and security questions have not been altered.**
   - Ensure "Two-Step Verification" is active on the recovery email account itself.

7. **Inspect email rules and forwarding settings (for email accounts).**  
   **Check for any "Rules," "Filters," or "Auto-forwarding" instructions that may have been created to intercept your mail.**
   - Look for rules that delete or move emails from banks or other services to the "Trash" or "Archive" folders.

8. **Forward the suspicious alert to national reporting services.**  
   **Send suspicious emails to `report@phishing.gov.uk` or forward suspicious text messages to 7726.**
   - This helps the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) identify and block malicious infrastructure.

9. **Report financial loss or cyber crime to the appropriate authorities.**  
   **If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report the incident to Report Fraud (the national reporting service) at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.**
   - If you are in Scotland, contact Police Scotland via **101** or use their online reporting form for cyber crime.

## What can wait
- You don't need to identify the exact IP address or person responsible for the sign-in immediately.
- You don't need to replace your hardware or factory reset your phone unless the security alerts continue after you have secured the account and MFA.
- You don't need to notify your entire contact list unless you have evidence that the account was used to send messages to others.

## Important reassurance
Security alerts are common and are a sign that the service's automated protections are working. Receiving an alert does not always mean your data has been stolen; often, it is an early warning that allows you to block access before any harm occurs. Taking systematic steps to change your password and sign out of other sessions is the standard and effective way to resolve this.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an unrecognised sign-in alert. It does not cover deep forensic recovery, device-level malware removal, or long-term identity theft protection.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional security or legal advice. If you suspect your banking details have been compromised, contact your bank directly using the number on the back of your card. For incidents involving work or school accounts, follow your organization's internal IT security reporting procedures.
