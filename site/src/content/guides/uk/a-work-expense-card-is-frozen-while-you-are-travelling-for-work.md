# What to do if… a work expense card is frozen while you are travelling for work (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign or backdate any document that contains a date or information you know to be inaccurate. Request the instruction in writing and escalate the matter through your organisation's formal compliance or whistleblowing (protected disclosure) channels.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign or approve a document with a date or statement you believe is untrue, regardless of the perceived "minor" nature of the fix.
- Do not alter electronic timestamps, version histories, server logs, or email metadata to support a false date.
- Do not delete any messages, emails, or drafts related to the request to backdate.
- Do not transfer company documents, audit trails, or internal communications to your personal email or personal cloud storage.
- Do not engage in heated verbal arguments; maintain a professional, written record of all responses.
- Do not allow "audit deadlines" or "remediation windows" to bypass your standard verification procedures.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and request a clear clarification of the specific document and the reason for the date change.** Ask specifically which document is involved, what the original execution date was, and why a different date is being requested for the audit record.
2. **Obtain the instruction in a written format or document the verbal request immediately after it occurs.** If the request was verbal, send a neutral email to the requester stating: "To confirm our conversation, you have requested that I [sign/date] the [document name] as of [date]. I am writing to clarify that this date does not reflect the current day."
3. **Propose a truthful alternative that maintains the integrity of the audit trail.** Offer to sign with today's date and include a contemporaneous note (a "memo to file" or "late entry" note) explaining when the underlying event occurred and why the document is being finalised now.
4. **Identify the relevant internal policies for records management and whistleblowing.** Use the company intranet to locate the "Document Control Policy," "Code of Conduct," or "Speak Up / Whistleblowing Policy" to determine the mandated route for reporting irregularities.
5. **Escalate the request through the organisation's independent oversight functions.** Contact **Compliance**, **Internal Audit**, **Legal**, or the **Risk Department** directly, or use the designated whistleblowing hotline if you believe the request violates the Companies Act 2006 or internal integrity standards.
6. **Maintain a private, factual log of the interaction within approved company systems.** Record the date and time of the request, the name of the person making it, any witnesses present, and the specific wording used, without removing confidential data from company control.
7. **Verify if the request constitutes a 'protected disclosure' under the Employment Rights Act 1996.** If you reasonably believe the request involves a criminal offence (such as fraud or forgery) or a failure to comply with a legal obligation, you may be eligible for legal protection against detriment or dismissal.
8. **Consult a Prescribed Person if internal escalation is not viable or has failed.** For audit and accounting matters in the UK, the **Financial Reporting Council (FRC)** is the relevant prescribed body for making an external disclosure.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to resign or initiate an employment tribunal claim.
- You do not need to prove fraudulent intent on the part of your manager; your responsibility is limited to ensuring your own actions remain compliant.
- You do not need to communicate with external regulators or law enforcement in the first instance unless there is an immediate risk of significant public harm or destruction of evidence.

## Important reassurance
Startled or anxious reactions are common when faced with pressure to "tidy up" paperwork for an auditor. Following established compliance protocols and insisting on factual accuracy is a standard professional requirement, not an act of obstruction. Taking a moment to verify the correct procedure is a protected part of your role.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for document integrity crises in the workplace. It does not cover specific regulatory requirements for highly specialized sectors (such as pharmaceutical clinical trials or nuclear safety) which may have additional mandatory reporting triggers.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal or professional advice. UK employment and criminal laws, including the Fraud Act 2006 and the Employment Rights Act 1996, carry significant implications for document falsification. If you fear retaliation or are being pressured to commit a crime, seek advice from ACAS, your trade union, or a qualified solicitor specializing in UK employment law.
