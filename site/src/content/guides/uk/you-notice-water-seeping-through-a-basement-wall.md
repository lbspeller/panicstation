# What to do if… you notice water seeping through a basement wall (UK)

## Short answer
Treat seepage as a possible electrical and contamination risk first. Keep people and pets out of the affected area, and only isolate electricity or water if you can do it from a dry, safe place.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t touch sockets, extension leads, appliances, switches, or the consumer unit if you would have to stand in a damp or wet area to reach them.
- Don’t run mains-powered equipment, including a dehumidifier, heater, fan, pump, or wet vacuum, in the affected basement area until the electricity there is safely isolated and a registered electrician says it is safe.
- Don’t drill, chisel, or open up the wall while water is actively seeping through it.
- Don’t ignore foul smells, gurgling drains, toilets not draining, or water coming up through a drain, as this may mean sewage backflow.
- Don’t put valuables, textiles, cardboard boxes, or electrical items against the wet wall.
- Don’t throw away damaged items unless they are unsafe to keep or your insurer tells you to.

## What to do now
1. **Make a safer pause.** Keep children and pets out of the basement. Assume there may be slip risk, electric shock risk, and contaminated water if the seepage is near wiring, sockets, appliances, extension leads, drains, or stored chemicals.

2. **Step back if electricity could be involved.** If you can isolate electricity to the basement or affected circuit from a dry place, do that. If you cannot reach the consumer unit or main switch without entering a damp area, do not try. Call a registered electrician, or call 105 for your local electricity network operator if there is an electrical hazard or power emergency.

3. **If you smell gas, leave the area.** Do not turn electrical switches on or off, do not use flames, and call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999 from outside or from a safe place.

4. **Check for an internal water source only if it is safe to get close enough.** Look for dripping or spraying pipes, a boiler or pressurised system leak, a water softener leak, a washing machine supply leak, or a blocked condensate or overflow pipe. If you suspect an internal plumbing leak and can reach the stop tap, stopcock, or nearest isolation valve safely, turn it off. If you cannot reach it safely, call an emergency plumber.

5. **If it looks like rainwater or groundwater seepage, limit spread without working on the wall.** Move stored items and anything electrical up off the floor and away from the wet wall. Use towels or absorbent pads only to slow water running across the floor and reduce slipping. Do not try to seal the wall from the inside while water is actively coming through.

6. **Check the drain signs.** If there is a foul smell, gurgling, slow toilets, or water rising from a floor drain, avoid contact with the water and contact your water or sewerage provider promptly.

7. **Take a simple record now.** Take photos or video of where the water is coming through, any cracks, the size of the damp patch, the floor area affected, and anything already damaged. Note the time and whether there has been heavy rain.

8. **Contact your home insurer or landlord early.** Report water ingress or possible flooding and ask what emergency steps they want you to take before repairs. Keep notes of calls, names, dates, and what was agreed.

9. **If you have a sump pump, be cautious.** Only check or operate it if you can do so without entering the wet area and you are confident the power supply is safe. Do not pump water into drains, sewers, ditches, or watercourses unless a professional or the relevant authority says it is acceptable.

10. **Escalate if the situation changes.** If the wet area expands quickly, water starts pooling, the wall bulges or cracks, sewage is suspected, electrics may be wet, or anyone is in immediate danger, move away and seek urgent professional or emergency help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today about tanking, drainage systems, waterproofing, excavation, or major building work.
- You do not need to strip plaster, remove finishes, or open up the wall unless a professional says it is needed for safety or urgent damage control.
- You do not need to run drying equipment until the water source is controlled and the electrics are confirmed safe.
- You do not need to diagnose the exact cause before making the area safer, recording what you can see, and contacting the right help.

## Important reassurance
Noticing seepage before it becomes standing water gives you a useful early warning. The aim right now is not to solve the whole basement problem; it is to reduce shock, slip, contamination, and damage risks while creating a clear record for the people who can help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from an insurer, landlord, plumber, registered electrician, flood-restoration contractor, structural surveyor, or damp and waterproofing specialist.

This is general information for immediate harm-prevention only. It is not legal, insurance, electrical, plumbing, structural, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice.
