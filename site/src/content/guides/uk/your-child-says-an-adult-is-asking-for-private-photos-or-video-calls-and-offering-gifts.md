# What to do if… your child says an adult is asking for private photos or video calls and offering gifts (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a child-safety issue, not a discipline issue. Stay calm, stop your child replying if you can do that safely, avoid creating or sharing any new copies of sexual images or videos, and get specialist help from CEOP, police, or the NSPCC.

## Do not do these things
- Do not blame, shame, or punish your child for telling you.
- Do not tell your child to keep talking to the adult to “get proof.”
- Do not forward, upload, share, resend, or create new copies of any sexual image or video of a child.
- Do not ask your child to send the image or video to you again.
- Do not delete chats, accounts, usernames, payment records, gift details, or device information if they still exist and can be left safely.
- Do not contact or threaten the adult yourself.
- Do not force your child to repeat everything over and over to different people if it can be avoided.
- Do not promise absolute secrecy; explain that you may need to involve adults whose job is to help keep children safe.

## What to do now
1. Move to a calmer, private space with your child and thank them for telling you. Use simple language such as: “You are not in trouble. I’m glad you told me. This is not your fault.”
2. Help your child stop replying. If you can, keep the chat or account available without deleting it, then mute, block, or log out in the safest way available.
3. Preserve basic details without spreading harmful content. Write down or save non-image information such as usernames, profile links, app names, message dates, gift promises, payment offers, meeting suggestions, phone numbers, and account handles.
4. If sexual images or videos may exist, do not copy, forward, screenshot, or upload them. Ask CEOP or police how to handle them, and use Report Remove if your child needs help trying to remove sexual images or videos of themselves from the internet.
5. Check the immediate risk: whether a meeting is planned, whether the adult knows your child’s school, address, phone number, route, routine, or live location, whether money or gifts changed hands, and whether threats are being made.
6. Report it through CEOP if you are concerned your child is being sexually abused or groomed online. Use 999 if there is immediate danger, if an in-person meeting is happening now or very soon, or if someone may be harmed. For non-emergency police help, use 101 or your local police online reporting route.
7. Contact the NSPCC Helpline for support as a parent or carer if you need help deciding what to say or do next. Your child can also contact Childline directly for support.
8. Tell only the people who need to know right now. If the adult may be connected to school, sport, tuition, religion, transport, or another organised setting, tell the school’s designated safeguarding lead or the relevant safeguarding lead as soon as you can.
9. Stay physically and digitally close for a while. Check privacy settings together, look for other accounts the adult used, and make sure your child is not dealing with new messages, threats, money, or gift offers alone tonight.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today how to handle every app, every friendship, every device rule, or every long-term online boundary. You also do not need to force a full written timeline from your child right now. The urgent part is safety, stopping contact, preserving what can safely be preserved, avoiding new copies of sexual images or videos, and getting the right support involved.

## Important reassurance
Children are often targeted through secrecy, praise, pressure, gifts, attention, isolation, or threats. Freezing, replying, hiding it, feeling embarrassed, or going along with it can happen when a child is being pressured. Telling you was an important protective step.

## Scope note
This is first steps only for a highly sensitive situation. Later decisions about school, devices, reporting, safeguarding, legal questions, and emotional support may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, police, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you think there is any immediate risk of contact in person, immediate harm, or ongoing coercion, contact emergency services now.
