# What to do if… a stranger claims you damaged their car and insists you move to a quieter location (UK)

## Short answer
Do not go to a quieter or isolated place. Stay in, or move only as far as needed to, a busy well-lit public place, keep the doors locked, and call 999 if you feel threatened, blocked in, or followed.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t follow them to a side street, car park corner, industrial estate, or “round the back” area.
- Don’t get out of your car if you feel unsafe.
- Don’t hand over your driving licence, phone, wallet, insurance certificate, or let them photograph documents.
- Don’t argue about fault, apologise, or agree to pay cash on the spot under pressure.
- Don’t drive home if you think they may follow you there.
- Don’t wait until later to report it if you could not safely exchange details after a possible collision.

## What to do now
1. **Make your position safer without going with them.** If the place you have stopped feels unsafe, put your hazard lights on if needed and move only as far as necessary to the nearest busy, well-lit public place, such as a petrol station forecourt, supermarket front, or main road. Keep the doors locked and the windows mostly up.
2. **Call for help based on risk.**
   - If you feel in danger, they are trying to get into your car, blocking you in, threatening you, or following you, call **999** and say you feel unsafe after a claimed car damage incident.
   - If it is not an emergency but you need police involvement, contact **101** or use your local police force’s online reporting route once you are in a safe public place. If a legal accident report is required, follow police instructions about how to make it.
3. **Use one clear sentence and do not debate.** From inside the car, say: “I will exchange details here, in public, or with police present. I am not going somewhere quieter.”
4. **Exchange only the usual required details if it is safe.** If there may have been contact, damage, or injury, give and ask for the driver’s name and address, the vehicle registration number, and the owner’s name and address if the vehicle is not owned by the driver. If insurance details are requested, give the relevant insurance information without handing over documents for photographs.
5. **Protect your ID.** If they demand photos of your driving licence, insurance certificate, or other documents, say: “I’m not sharing document photos. Police or insurers can deal with the paperwork.”
6. **Record what you can without escalating.** Note their registration number, vehicle make, model and colour, what they said, where it happened, and what made you feel unsafe. Take photos or video only if you can do it without making the situation worse. Preserve dashcam footage if you have it.
7. **If it is not safe to exchange details, leave for safety and report promptly.** Go to a safer public place and report the incident to police as soon as you can. If there may have been an accident causing damage or injury and you could not give details at the time, report it as soon as reasonably practicable and, in any case, within 24 hours; this does not mean you should wait.
8. **Tell your insurer once you are safe.** Report what happened to your insurer even if you do not plan to make a claim. Keep it factual and mention any safety concerns, pressure to move location, or suspected staged incident.
9. **If you suspect a staged incident or insurance fraud, report it once safe.** Tell police or the Insurance Fraud Bureau CheatLine what happened, including pressure to isolate you, demands for document photos, intimidation, or pressure to pay cash.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide who was at fault right now.
- You do not need to negotiate costs, get quotes, or agree to any payment today.
- You do not need to give extra personal information, such as extra address context, workplace, route, or photos of documents.
- You can deal with any claim details once you are safe and calm.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel shaken when someone is confrontational at the roadside. Staying in public, keeping your identity documents protected, and involving police if you feel unsafe are sensible first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stay safe and avoid irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about any collision, insurance claim, police report, or dispute may need help from your insurer, police, or a qualified adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, insurance, or professional advice. If you feel threatened or unsafe, call 999.
