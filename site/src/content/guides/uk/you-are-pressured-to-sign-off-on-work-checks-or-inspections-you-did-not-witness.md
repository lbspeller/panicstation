# What to do if… you are pressured to sign off on work, checks, or inspections you did not witness (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign anything that says, or could reasonably imply, that you personally witnessed, carried out, or verified checks you did not witness. Pause, ask for the request in writing, and offer wording that only states what you actually did.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign as “completed”, “inspected”, “checked”, “verified”, or “witnessed” if that is not true.
- Do not backdate, alter, delete, or tidy up records to make them look compliant.
- Do not let someone else use your login, password, stamp, initials, or signature.
- Do not rely on “everyone does it” or “it is only paperwork” as a reason to sign.
- Do not accuse people in a public channel; keep your response factual and contained.
- Do not destroy messages, notes, photos, forms, or audit records about the request.

## What to do now
1. **Buy a short pause.** Say: “I cannot sign to say I witnessed that. I need to check what I can truthfully sign.”
2. **Look at the exact wording before you respond.** Check whether the form says “inspected by”, “witnessed by”, “verified by”, “approved by”, “reviewed by”, or something else.
3. **Ask what your signature is meant to confirm.** Keep it simple: “Can you confirm what you need my signature to mean on this record?”
4. **Put the key point in writing.** Send a neutral message such as: “Confirming I have been asked to sign the [check or inspection] dated [date/time]. I did not witness or carry out that check, so I can only sign wording that reflects what I actually did.”
5. **Offer a truthful alternative, if it is accurate.** For example:
   - “I can sign that I reviewed the records provided to me, not that I witnessed the inspection.”
   - “I can sign to acknowledge receipt of the report, not as the person who inspected it.”
   - “I can sign after I personally re-check the item, if I am authorised and competent to do that.”
6. **Ask for the evidence behind the check.** Depending on the workplace, this might include the inspection log, photos, readings, job card, timestamp, name of the person who did it, defect record, calibration record, or follow-up action.
7. **If the check is safety-critical, treat it as a safety issue.** If the item could harm people, ask for the equipment, process, area, or activity to be paused, isolated, or escalated under workplace safety procedures until someone suitably authorised checks it.
8. **Escalate calmly through the right internal route.** Use your line manager’s manager, duty manager, health and safety lead, quality or compliance lead, or whistleblowing channel. Ask for a decision that does not require you to make an inaccurate statement.
9. **Make a private factual note.** Record what you were asked to sign, who asked, when it happened, what the form said, what you replied, and what alternative you offered. Keep copies only where your workplace rules allow it.
10. **Get support early if pressure continues.** If you have a union, contact your rep. You can also check Acas guidance. If the issue involves safety, legal compliance, fraud, falsified records, or risk to others, whistleblowing guidance may be relevant, but the exact protection depends on the facts.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, make a formal grievance, or contact an external regulator.
- You do not need to prove anyone’s motive right now.
- You do not need to solve the whole workplace process today.
- You can wait to write a fuller statement until you have stabilised the immediate request and made a clear note of what happened.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel pressured when a signature is treated as routine. You are not being difficult by limiting your signature to what you actually know, saw, checked, or reviewed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a union, Acas, a whistleblowing adviser, a workplace safety specialist, or an employment professional, depending on your role, sector, and the risk involved.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, employment, health and safety, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Workplace processes and legal protections can depend on the facts, your role, your employment status, and how concerns are raised.
