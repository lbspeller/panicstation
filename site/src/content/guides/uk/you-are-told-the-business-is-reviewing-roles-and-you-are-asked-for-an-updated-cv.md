# What to do if… you are told the business is reviewing roles and you are asked for an updated CV (UK)

## Short answer
Do not agree to the change verbally or in writing; request the full proposal in a formal letter and check your contract for a "mobility clause" before responding.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any "variation of contract" or acceptance form during the initial meeting.
- Do not resign or stop attending your current workplace until the notice period for the change is confirmed.
- Do not assume that a "mobility clause" gives the employer unlimited power; the move must still be "reasonable" under 2026 standards.
- Do not rely on verbal promises of travel expenses or relocation packages; if it is not in the written proposal, it does not exist.
- Do not agree to "fire and rehire" terms without professional advice, as these are now subject to strict automatic unfair dismissal rules under the Employment Rights Act 2025.

## What to do now
1. **Request the full relocation proposal in writing immediately.** Ensure the document specifies the new address, the proposed start date, any changes to hours, and whether the move is permanent.
   - If they refuse to provide this, send an email stating: "I am waiting for the written details of the relocation proposal to review against my contract."
2. **Locate your current employment contract and staff handbook.** Search specifically for the "Place of Work" section and any mention of a "mobility" or "flexibility" clause.
3. **Verify the specific wording of any mobility clause found.** Note if it limits moves to a certain radius (e.g., "within 25 miles") or requires a specific notice period.
4. **Identify concrete barriers to the new location.** List specific factors such as increased commute time, lack of public transport, childcare/caring conflicts, or disability-related travel limitations.
5. **Send a formal "holding response" to your manager or HR.** **State clearly that you are reviewing the proposal and have not yet accepted the change.**
6. **Request a formal consultation meeting if one has not been offered.** **Ask the employer to explain in writing why they believe the move is "reasonable" in your specific circumstances.**
7. **Submit a statutory flexible working request if the move is impossible.** Under 2026 rules, this is a "day one" right, and employers must provide a written, reasonable justification if they refuse.
8. **Check if a "redundancy" situation has been triggered.** If there is no mobility clause and the move is significant, the employer may be legally required to treat this as a redundancy, entitling you to statutory pay.
9. **Inquire about collective consultation if 20 or more staff are affected.** Under the Employment Rights Act 2025, the "protective award" for failing to consult has doubled to 180 days of pay.
10. **Record every interaction regarding the move.** Save all emails to a personal device and take dated notes of every meeting, including who was present and exactly what was said.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to sell your home or move your family today.
- You do not need to choose between resigning or relocating until the formal consultation period is complete.
- You do not need to finalize travel arrangements or childcare changes until a final start date is mutually agreed.
- You do not need to start a formal grievance until you have seen the employer's written justification for the move.

## Important reassurance
Being told your place of work is moving is a significant disruption that often triggers immediate stress. It is normal to feel overwhelmed by the logistics of a longer commute or relocation. You are entitled to take the time necessary to check your legal position; an employer cannot legally force an instant agreement to a fundamental change in your contract.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. Employment law regarding relocation is complex and depends heavily on specific contract wording and the "reasonableness" of the employer's conduct.

## Important note
This guide provides general information based on UK employment law updates for 2026, including the Employment Rights Act 2025. It is not legal advice. If your employer threatens dismissal or if the move involves more than 20 people, contact Acas or a qualified employment solicitor immediately to protect your rights.
