# What to do if… you receive urgent mail about legal proceedings involving a person who died and deadlines are close (UK)

## Short answer
Notify the court and the claimant’s solicitor in writing immediately to confirm the person has died and provide the date of death. This action prevents a default judgment while the legal representative (executor or administrator) is identified and substituted in the proceedings.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the correspondence even if it is not addressed to you.
- Do not pay any requested sums or sign any admissions of liability on behalf of the deceased.
- Do not send original documents like the death certificate or the Will through regular post; use scanned copies or tracked delivery.
- Do not miss the 14-day response deadline thinking the death automatically cancels the case.
- Do not attempt to file a full legal defense yourself if you are not the appointed personal representative.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the case details and the jurisdiction.**  
   **Locate the claim number, the name of the court (e.g., County Court at Central London or a Scottish Sheriff Court), and the date the papers were issued.**
   - If the papers are from England or Wales, the Civil Procedure Rules (CPR) apply.
   - If the papers are from Scotland, the system involves "Confirmation" and distinct Sheriff Court rules.

2. **Calculate the response deadline immediately.**  
   **Determine the 'date of service,' which is usually the second business day after the postmark or date on the letter.**
   - In England and Wales, you generally have 14 days from the date of service to file an Acknowledgment of Service.
   - Missing this deadline allows the claimant to apply for a "Default Judgment" against the estate.

3. **Contact the court office by telephone and follow up in writing.**  
   **Tell the court staff that the defendant is deceased and provide the date of death.**
   - Ask the court to note the death on the electronic case file.
   - Ask for the specific email address or postal address where you should send a copy of the death certificate.
   - Request information on the procedure for "staying" (pausing) the case until a personal representative is appointed.

4. **Inform the claimant’s solicitor in writing.**  
   **Send a brief letter or email to the address listed under 'Address for Payment/Service' on the claim form.**
   - State clearly: "[Name of Defendant] died on [Date]."
   - State whether a Grant of Probate or Letters of Administration has been applied for or issued.
   - Request that they "stay" the proceedings or agree to an extension of time under CPR 15.5 to allow the estate to be properly represented.

5. **Verify the status of the estate representation.**  
   **Check if a legal representative (executor or administrator) has already been formally appointed.**
   - If a Will exists, the person named as executor has the immediate authority to act.
   - If no Will exists, a close relative may need to apply for Letters of Administration before they can formally defend the claim.
   - Use the official GOV.UK probate search tool to see if a Grant of Representation has already been issued.

6. **Seek emergency legal advice if the claim involves property or large sums.**  
   **Contact a solicitor or a free advice service like Citizens Advice if the deadline is less than 48 hours away.**
   - Professional advice is required to handle the "substitution of parties" under CPR 19.8, which is the formal process of replacing the deceased with their estate representative.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether the deceased actually owed the money right now.
- You do not need to pay the court fee for a probate application today.
- You do not need to notify all beneficiaries about the specific details of the claim until the deadline is secured.
- You do not need to provide a full "Statement of Case" or evidence for a defense in the first 48 hours.

## Important reassurance
Receiving court papers for a deceased loved one is common and does not mean you are personally liable for the debt. Courts have established procedures (such as CPR 19.8 in England/Wales) to deal with the death of a party. By notifying the court and the claimant promptly, you fulfill the immediate procedural requirements to stop the case from escalating to a judgment.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization only to prevent default judgments. It does not provide legal strategy for defending the claim on its merits or the long-term administration of the estate.

## Important note
Legal procedures and terminology vary significantly between England/Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. Deadlines are strict and court staff cannot provide legal advice. If you are not the executor or administrator, you may have limited standing to act; prioritize identifying the correct legal representative.
