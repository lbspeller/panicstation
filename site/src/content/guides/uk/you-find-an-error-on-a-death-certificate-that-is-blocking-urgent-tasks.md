# What to do if… you find an error on a death certificate that is blocking urgent tasks (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the registrar, registration office, or national records body responsible for the place where the death was registered and ask how to start the official correction route today.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to edit, annotate, or overwrite the certificate yourself.
- Do not keep sending the same certificate to urgent organisations without saying a correction is being requested.
- Do not pay anyone promising a quick private fix before checking the official registration route.
- Do not throw away emails, receipts, envelopes, certificate copies, or registration paperwork.

## What to do now
1. **Make one short list of what is wrong.**  
   Write the exact field that is wrong, what it says now, and what it should say. Include details such as name spelling, date, place, address, occupation, marital status, or any other incorrect entry. Take a clear photo or scan of the certificate.

2. **Gather proof for each wrong detail.**  
   Use documents that show the correct information, dated or valid around the time of the death where possible. This might include a passport, photocard driving licence, birth or marriage certificate, bank statement, utility bill, letter from a doctor or hospital, letter from a government department, or occupation evidence. Keep originals safe unless the official route specifically asks for them.

3. **Find the right official route for where the death was registered.**  
   Contact the registration office, registrar, or national records body for the place of registration. Ask:
   - whether they can handle this correction or whether it must be referred elsewhere
   - who is allowed to request the correction
   - what evidence they need for this specific error
   - whether copies must be certified
   - what form, email, post, appointment, or online route to use
   - what fee applies, if any
   - how corrected extracts or updated certified copies are issued after the correction is recorded

4. **Start the correction request and ask for proof it is underway.**  
   Complete the form or written request they tell you to use. Send only the evidence they ask for. Ask for a reference number, receipt, or email confirming they have received it, if they can provide one.

5. **Tell only the urgent organisations for now.**  
   Make a short blocking list, such as bank access, insurance, housing, care home fees, pension, utilities, solicitor, or probate work. Contact each urgent organisation and say:
   - the death certificate has an error
   - you have started, or are starting, the official correction request
   - you can provide supporting documents showing the correct detail
   - you need to know whether they can place a note, pause a deadline, or accept other evidence while the correction is pending

6. **If estate or probate work is already moving, stop the error spreading.**  
   Tell the solicitor, executor, administrator, or probate contact that the death certificate detail is being corrected. Ask what should be paused and what can safely continue without relying on the wrong detail.

7. **Plan certificate copies carefully.**  
   Do not assume an issued certificate itself can be altered. Ask the registration authority how the correction will appear on later certificates, extracts, or certified copies, and order further copies only when you know which version urgent organisations need.

## What can wait
- You do not need to fix every downstream task today.
- You do not need to contact every bank, insurer, utility, and pension provider at once.
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, escalate, or challenge how the error happened.
- You do not need perfect paperwork before making the first call; start with the certificate, your list of errors, and the best proof you can quickly find.

## Important reassurance
Official correction routes exist for death-registration errors. The most stabilising first step is to get the correct registration authority engaged and then tell urgent organisations that the correction is in progress.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce the immediate blockage and start the official correction route. Later decisions about probate, disputes, identity problems, or losses caused by delay may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Processes, evidence, fees, timescales, and certificate wording can vary depending on where in the UK the death was registered and what kind of correction is needed.
