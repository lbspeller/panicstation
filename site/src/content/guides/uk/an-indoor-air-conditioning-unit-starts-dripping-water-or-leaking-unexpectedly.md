# What to do if… an indoor air-conditioning unit starts dripping water or leaking unexpectedly (UK)

## Short answer
**Turn off the air-conditioning system immediately and isolate the power supply if it is safe to do so.** Contain the water using buckets or towels and ensure no moisture reaches electrical sockets, plugs, or the consumer unit.

## Do not do these things
- Don't keep the unit running to "test" it — this will continue the production of condensate and worsen the leak.
- Don't use a household vacuum cleaner to try and suck water out of the unit or the drainage line.
- Don't touch the consumer unit (fuse box) if you have to step in water or if the board itself appears damp.
- Don't attempt to open the plastic casing or poke objects into the internal fan/coil area while the system has power.
- Don't ignore water that has travelled behind wallpaper or into floorboards, as this can lead to structural rot or mould.

## What to do now
1. **Power down the unit using the remote or wall controller immediately.** This stops the cooling process and the further creation of water (condensate).
2. **Isolate the electrical supply at the local isolator switch if one is visible near the indoor or outdoor unit.** If there is no local switch and you can safely reach your consumer unit without stepping in water, switch off the circuit marked "Air Conditioning" or "AC".
   - If water is actively entering a socket, light fitting, or the consumer unit, do not touch any switches; evacuate the immediate area and call 999 if there is smoke, sparking, or fire.
3. **Protect all nearby electrical equipment and sockets from moisture.** Unplug devices in the "drip zone" only if they are currently dry. If a socket is already wet, do not touch it or anything plugged into it.
4. **Contain the leak using buckets, trays, or heavy-duty towels.** If the water is running down the wall, you can use masking tape to secure a piece of plastic sheeting (like a bin bag) to the wall to funnel the water into a container.
5. **Check the external drainage point from ground level only.** If the condensate pipe exits through a wall to the outside, check if the end of the pipe is submerged in a full water butt or blocked by debris; do not use ladders or climb to height.
6. **Identify and book a Refcom-registered air-conditioning company.** In the UK, anyone working on systems containing refrigerant must be F-gas certified. Verify the company's credentials on the Refcom or Quidos register before they attend.
7. **Document the leak and any resulting damage for insurance or landlord records.** Take clear photos of where the water is exiting the unit and any damp patches on the ceiling, walls, or carpets.
8. **Contact your landlord or managing agent immediately if you are a tenant.** State clearly that there is an "active water leak from the HVAC system" and that you have isolated the power for safety.

## What can wait
- You don't need to determine the technical cause (e.g., a failed condensate pump or frozen coil) right now.
- You don't need to start permanent repairs to plasterwork or paint until the leak is fully resolved and the area is dry.
- You don't need to decide on a full system replacement until a certified engineer has performed a diagnostic.
- You don't need to file a formal insurance claim in the first hour; focus on containment first.

## Important reassurance
Air-conditioning units produce significant amounts of water as part of their normal operation. A leak usually means a simple blockage or a minor pump failure rather than a burst pipe or a "broken" system. Once the power is off, the water production stops almost immediately.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for domestic and small commercial indoor units only. It does not provide instructions for technical repairs, refrigerant handling, or electrical testing, which require professional certification.

## Important note
Water and electricity are a lethal combination. If you cannot safely reach the power switches or if water is dripping directly into electrical components, keep away and contact a qualified electrician or emergency services. All AC repairs involving refrigerant must be performed by an F-gas certified engineer under UK law.
