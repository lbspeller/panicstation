# What to do if… a funeral provider says a body must be moved today due to capacity limits (UK)

## Short answer
Do not permit a transfer until you have the destination, total costs, and reason in writing. Confirm if the Medical Examiner or Coroner (England/Wales) or Procurator Fiscal (Scotland) has officially released the body before agreeing to any movement.

## Do not do these things
- Do not authorise a move until you know the specific address of the facility where the body will be held.
- Do not agree to embalming or "hygienic treatment" as a condition of storage—standard refrigeration is usually sufficient and reversible.
- Do not sign any "full funeral contract" just to resolve a storage issue; keep the storage arrangement separate from the final funeral plan.
- Do not accept verbal assurances about "partner facilities" without a named provider and an itemised transport fee.
- Do not assume the funeral director has the final legal say; the executor named in the will (or the nearest relative if there is no will) is responsible for the body’s custody.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the legal status of the body with the Medical Examiner or Coroner’s Office.**  
   **Ask the funeral director if the body has been released by the Medical Examiner (England/Wales) or the Procurator Fiscal (Scotland).**
   - If the death is still being investigated, the body may not be moved without official authority.
   - Contact the Coroner’s Officer or the local Medical Examiner’s office directly to confirm if a "Form 100A" or similar release has been issued.

2. **Request an immediate 'Transfer of Deceased' summary in writing (email or text).**  
   **Demand the following details before giving any authorisation:**
   - The full address of the proposed storage location.
   - The name of the company performing the transport.
   - An itemised list of charges, specifically for collection, transport, and daily storage.
   - Confirmation that the facility meets the standards of the Funeral Director Code of Practice (e.g., individual refrigerated compartments).

3. **Use a scripted response to slow the process down.**  
   **Say: “I understand there is a capacity issue. I am not refusing to assist, but I require the destination address and a written breakdown of all costs before I can provide the signed authorisation required by the Code of Practice.”**

4. **Check for "Out of England and Wales" requirements if the move is cross-border.**  
   **If the provider suggests moving the body from England/Wales to Scotland (or vice-versa), ask for proof of "Form 103" (Coroner’s Permission to Move a Body out of England and Wales).**
   - Moving a body across these borders generally requires specific legal forms even for natural deaths.

5. **Identify a "back-up" provider if you feel pressured.**  
   **Call a different funeral director and ask if they have refrigeration capacity and what their "urgent collection and storage only" fee is.**
   - If the second provider is available, you can instruct the first provider to release the body to them.
   - Tell the current provider: "We are moving the deceased to [New Provider]. Please confirm the body is ready for collection and provide your final invoice for services rendered to date only."

6. **Provide a "Limited Authority" statement in writing.**  
   **If you agree to the move, send a message stating: "Authorisation is for transfer and refrigerated storage only. No preparation, embalming, or coffin choice is authorised at this time."**

7. **Report concerns of "Predatory Capacity Claims."**  
   **If you believe the capacity limit is being used to force a quick sale or higher fees, contact the National Association of Funeral Directors (NAFD) or the Society of Allied and Independent Funeral Directors (SAIF) if the provider is a member.**
   - You can also report misleading business practices to Citizens Advice, who can escalate to Trading Standards.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a coffin, flowers, or a service date today.
- You do not need to pay for the full funeral upfront to secure storage.
- You do not need to decide which family members will attend a viewing before the body is moved to a stable location.

## Important reassurance
It is common for funeral providers to experience temporary capacity fluctuations, but this does not override your right to clear information and dignified care. Taking ten minutes to get details in writing will not cause a failure in care; it ensures the deceased is tracked and the estate is protected from unauthorised fees.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization of custody and costs during a capacity crisis only. It does not cover long-term funeral planning, legal disputes over wills, or the technical details of post-mortem examinations.

## Important note
This information is based on UK funeral industry codes and death certification reforms as of 2026. Legal requirements for moving bodies differ between England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. Always prioritize instructions from the Coroner or Procurator Fiscal over those of a private contractor if an investigation is active.
