# What to do if… your engine temperature warning light comes on and you have pulled over safely (UK)

## Short answer
Switch the engine off, keep people away from traffic, and let the engine cool before checking anything. Do not open the coolant or expansion tank cap while the engine is hot, steaming, or hissing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the radiator cap or coolant/expansion tank cap while the engine is hot, steaming, hissing, or likely to be pressurised.
- Do not restart the engine “just to see if it’s fine” while the warning is still on or the gauge is high.
- Do not add water or coolant while the engine is hot.
- Do not stand on the traffic side of the vehicle or put passengers at the roadside edge.
- Do not place a warning triangle on a motorway.
- Do not attempt roadside repairs on a motorway or in an emergency area.
- Do not keep driving if the warning returns or the gauge climbs again; stop and get help.

## What to do now
1. **Lock in roadside safety first, especially on motorways.** Keep hazard lights on. If you can leave the vehicle safely, exit by the side furthest from traffic, usually the left-hand side, and wait well away from the car, ideally behind a safety barrier. If it is not safe to exit, for example you are stopped in a live lane or an unsafe position, stay belted in with hazards on and call 999.
2. **Switch the engine off and wait for it to cool.** A common minimum is at least 30 minutes; longer is safer if it was very hot, steaming, or hissing.
3. **Only when things are calm and cool, open the bonnet carefully if it is safe.** Do not open it if there is fire, heavy smoke, strong steam, or strong hissing. Keep your face and hands away from any remaining steam.
4. **Check the coolant level in the expansion tank.** Look for the MIN/MAX marks. Check your vehicle handbook if you are unsure which tank is coolant. Look for obvious signs of a leak, such as a puddle, wetness, or white staining near hoses or under the front of the car.
5. **If coolant is low and the engine is fully cool, top up only if you can do it safely and confidently.** Use the correct coolant if you have it. If you have no coolant, plain water may be used as a temporary emergency top-up only with a cold engine. If the reservoir was empty, you suspect a leak, or you are unsure, skip this and get professional help.
6. **Use UK help channels rather than risking another overheat.** If you are on a motorway and a free emergency roadside telephone or SOS phone is safely reachable, use it for advice and assistance. Otherwise contact your breakdown or recovery provider. If you are in an emergency area, follow the operator’s advice before trying to leave. If the warning returns after any top-up, do not drive; request recovery.
7. **Write down the key details for the call-out.** Note the warning light or message, gauge position, any steam or smell, how long you waited, and whether coolant was low.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the cause, such as the fan, thermostat, leak, water pump, or sensor, at the roadside.
- You do not need to pick a garage, approve repairs, or discuss costs while you are still in a vulnerable place.
- You do not need to decide whether it is “serious” right now. Treat it as serious until the car has cooled and been checked.

## Important reassurance
This warning can feel frightening, but pulling over safely and switching off the engine are the right first moves. Cooling the engine, keeping away from traffic, and not opening a hot coolant cap are the main things that reduce immediate risk.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and avoid common mistakes. Whether it is safe to drive again may need specialist help from a breakdown provider, recovery service, or qualified mechanic.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, mechanical, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe where you are, if you cannot stop in a place of relative safety, or if you suspect fire, prioritise personal safety and call emergency services.
