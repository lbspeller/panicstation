# What to do if… you need to make a funeral or memorial service private due to safety or privacy concerns (UK)

## Short answer
If you are unsure, choose private or invitation-only for now and share details only with a small need-to-know list. It is usually easier to widen access later than to regain privacy once the time, date, or location has spread.

## Do not do these things
- Do not publish the time, date, or location widely while you are still unsure.
- Do not put sensitive details in a public notice, such as a home address, children’s names, travel plans, or anything that suggests a home will be empty.
- Do not try to manage a risky attendee alone on the day.
- Do not treat threats, stalking, harassment, or a history of violence as a social problem to smooth over.
- Do not pay money or share personal or financial information because of an unexpected urgent call, text, email, or social media message.

## What to do now
1. Make a temporary decision for now: “Private for now. Invitation-only.” You can widen the list later if that feels safe.

2. Pick one trusted gatekeeper immediately. Ask them to handle invitations and replies using one line: “The family is keeping details private; invited attendees will get details directly.”

3. Tell the funeral director, venue, celebrant, faith leader, or organiser that the service is invitation-only. Ask what they can realistically do, such as a guest list at the door, fewer entry points, a staff briefing, a private waiting area, or a quieter entrance and exit.

4. Share details one-to-one rather than in large group chats or public posts. If forwarding is a concern, share the general plan first and the exact place later, and say: “Please do not forward details. Ask me if someone needs to come.”

5. If the risk is a specific person, agree a simple arrival plan with the organiser and gatekeeper. Decide who will speak to staff, what name or description staff should know, where you can wait away from the person, and what phrase or text means “get staff now.”

6. If anyone may be in immediate danger, call 999. If it is not an emergency but there are threats, stalking, or harassment, call 101, report online through the police, or ask the police for advice. Keep messages, screenshots, voicemails, and dates if you already have them, but do not delay urgent help to gather evidence.

7. If the risk is domestic abuse-related, including an ex-partner, partner, or family member, keep details limited by default. A protective court order may be an option in some situations, but you do not have to solve that today unless risk is urgent; start by contacting a domestic abuse support service, police, or a legal adviser.

8. If there will be a livestream, make it private by default. Use a password or invite-only link, avoid public posting, turn off recording or sharing if the platform allows it, and ask someone else to manage access and remove disruptive attendees.

9. Reduce scam pressure. If someone contacts you about costs, accounts, documents, or urgent payments, ask for it in writing and contact the organisation using a number or website you already trust, not the contact details in the message.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who is right in any family conflict.
- You do not need to publish a full notice or obituary now.
- You do not need to explain the safety concern to everyone.
- You do not need to choose a legal route immediately unless there is urgent risk.
- You do not need to finalise livestream access, seating, wording, flowers, readings, or long-term family boundaries right now.

## Important reassurance
Choosing private for now is not rude or dramatic. It is a protective pause that reduces exposure while you are grieving and gives the organiser time to put simple controls in place.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for making a rapid privacy and safety decision about a service. Later decisions may need help from the funeral director, venue, police, domestic abuse support, a solicitor, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe or threatened, use emergency services or ask the police, venue, funeral director, or a specialist support organisation for practical guidance.
