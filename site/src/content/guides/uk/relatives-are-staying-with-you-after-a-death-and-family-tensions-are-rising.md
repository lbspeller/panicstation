# What to do if… relatives are staying with you after a death and family tensions are rising (UK)

## Short answer
Create a pause: stop arguments, stop big decisions, and set simple house rules for tonight. If you feel in immediate danger, or someone is using violence or threatening violence, get away from them if you can and call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree to funeral, money, property, or belongings decisions while people are angry, exhausted, or drinking.
- Don’t let anyone take paperwork, keys, phones, wallets, bank cards, ID, or sentimental items just because they insist.
- Don’t try to referee every dispute yourself.
- Don’t carry on a long group-chat argument while emotions are high.
- Don’t stay alone in a room with someone who is cornering, threatening, or intimidating you.
- Don’t feel you must keep hosting if your home no longer feels safe.

## What to do now
1. **Make a safety pause.** Say one calm line, such as: “I need everyone to pause. We are not doing this right now.” Move away from the argument, keep your phone with you, and put a door or another person between you and anyone who is threatening you.

2. **Call a no-big-decisions pause.** Say: “For now, no funeral decisions, no money talk, and no sorting possessions. Write down what you want raised and we will come back to it.” This is a practical boundary, not a final decision.

3. **Set house rules for tonight.** Keep them short:
   - quiet hours so people can sleep;
   - no alcohol, or a clear alcohol pause, if it is making things worse;
   - no cornering people for private arguments;
   - stop the conversation when voices rise.

4. **Separate people before the next flare-up.** Put people in different rooms, suggest a short walk, ask someone to make tea, or send one person on a small errand. Separation is prevention, not punishment.

5. **Protect key items without making a scene.** Put the will if there is one, death paperwork, letters, keys, wallets, bank cards, phones, passports, driving licences, and small sentimental items in one bag or box. Keep it with you or in a safe place. If someone is sorting possessions, say: “Nothing leaves the house today. We can make a list later.”

6. **Choose one calm person to handle messages.** Their job is to answer calls, take notes, and say “we will come back to that.” This does not make them legally in charge and does not mean disputed decisions must be made now.

7. **Use one outside anchor.** Choose the most useful next contact:
   - a funeral director, to explain what genuinely needs deciding now and what can wait;
   - the registrar or GOV.UK death-aftercare guidance, to check the official next step;
   - Tell Us Once, if available after registration or with a registrar reference; it is generally for England, Scotland and Wales, not Northern Ireland;
   - Cruse Bereavement Support, if you need a neutral bereavement-support route;
   - urgent health support, if panic or distress is becoming unmanageable.

8. **Use the right urgent help route.** In England, use NHS 111 online or call 111 and select the mental health option. In Scotland, NHS 24 on 111 can help with mental health distress. In Wales, call NHS 111 Wales and press option 2. In Northern Ireland, use your GP, GP out-of-hours service, or local crisis route. If anyone is in immediate danger, call 999.

9. **End the visit if you need to.** Say: “I cannot host this tonight. You need to stay somewhere else.” Ask a trusted friend, neighbour, or calmer relative to come over or help someone leave safely if that would lower the tension.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve old family grievances now.
- You do not need to decide who was right or who cared most.
- You do not need to divide belongings, clear rooms, or settle inheritance questions today.
- You do not need to host everyone indefinitely.
- You do not need to answer every call, message, or demand for updates tonight.

## Important reassurance
Tension after a death is common. Grief can come out as anger, control, blaming, or frantic activity. Setting a pause is not cold or selfish; it is a way to stop the next few hours causing damage that is harder to undo.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation at home and prevent irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about funerals, estates, property, mediation, or ongoing family conflict may need help from a suitable specialist or support service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe, call 999. If you are struggling to cope, contact your GP, the appropriate urgent mental health route for where you are, or a bereavement support service.
