# What to do if… you get alerts that someone is trying to recover your account (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible account takeover attempt. Do not reply to the alert or use its links, numbers, or chat routes; open the real service yourself and secure the account recovery details first.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click “secure your account” links in the alert email or text; open the service through the official app or by typing the address you already use.
- Do not share one-time codes, verification numbers, backup codes, passwords, or screenshots of security pages with anyone.
- Do not phone, WhatsApp, or live-chat using details provided in the alert message.
- Do not approve a 2-step verification prompt you did not start.
- Do not disable 2-step verification to stop prompts; that can make takeover easier.
- Do not reuse an old password or a password you use anywhere else.

## What to do now
1. **Open the real service safely:** use the official app, a saved bookmark you trust, or type the service’s address yourself. Do not use anything from the alert.
2. **Check account recovery details first:**
   - Check recovery email addresses and phone numbers.
   - Remove anything you do not recognise.
   - Check backup email, backup phone, recovery codes, trusted devices, and any account delegates or family access settings if the service has them.
3. **Check your email account because it is often the recovery key:**
   - Look for unfamiliar forwarding rules, filters, auto-delete rules, archive rules, or delegated access.
   - Remove anything you did not set.
   - Change your email password if anything looks wrong, or if the same password has been reused elsewhere.
4. **Change the targeted account password:**
   - Use a strong, unique password you have not used anywhere else.
   - If you reused that password on other sites, change those passwords too, starting with email, banking, card, shopping, and payment accounts.
5. **Kick out other access:**
   - Review devices and active sessions.
   - Use “sign out everywhere” or the nearest equivalent if it is available.
   - Review connected apps, linked services, browser extensions, and apps with account access; remove anything unfamiliar.
6. **Turn on or reset 2-step verification:**
   - Use an authenticator app if the provider offers one and you can set it up safely.
   - Generate fresh backup codes if offered.
   - Store backup codes somewhere safer than your email inbox.
7. **Use official support only, initiated by you:**
   - Go to the provider’s help centre from the app or site you opened yourself.
   - Tell them you are receiving account recovery attempts through support channels.
   - Ask whether any extra verification, account-change hold, or temporary protection is available.
8. **Check for money risk:**
   - If the account has saved cards, bank details, store credit, gift cards, subscriptions, or payment access, check recent activity.
   - Contact your bank or card provider using the number on the back of your card or inside the official banking app if anything looks wrong.
9. **If money has been lost or you cannot regain control:**
   - Report fraud or cyber crime through the official UK Report Fraud route once you have stabilised access as far as you can.
   - If you are in Scotland and need police help, contact Police Scotland through the usual non-emergency route unless there is immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out who is doing it now.
- You do not need to confront anyone or reply to the person who contacted you.
- You do not need to post publicly about it.
- You do not need to change every password at once; prioritise email, the targeted account, financial accounts, and anywhere you reused the same password.
- Device updates and wider security checks can usually wait until the account is stable, unless the provider shows an unknown device, active session, or app with access.

## Important reassurance
These alerts can happen when someone is testing recovery or support routes. Acting through the real service, securing recovery details, checking email forwarding, signing out other sessions, and protecting email can often stop the takeover before more damage is done.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise a possible support-channel account recovery takeover. If you have lost access, lost money, or suspect identity misuse, you may need help from the provider, your bank, Report Fraud, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This guide gives general information, not legal, financial, technical, cyber security, or professional advice. Follow the official recovery process for the account provider, and use official UK reporting routes if fraud or cyber crime may have happened.
