# What to do if… people start arriving at your home after a death and you need boundaries and support immediately (UK)

## Short answer
You are not required to admit anyone into your home. Designate one trusted person to manage the door and phone, and use a single, neutral script: “We are not receiving visitors today; we will contact you when we are ready.”

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door simply because someone is knocking or ringing the bell.
- Do not attempt to host, serve refreshments, or provide emotional support to others while you are in shock.
- Do not move the deceased person or tidy the immediate area until a medical professional or the police have attended.
- Do not hand over documents, keys, bank cards, or medications to anyone "to help" until you have had time to process the situation.
- Do not feel obligated to explain your reasons for wanting privacy or to argue with people on your doorstep.
- Do not make any significant decisions regarding the funeral, estate, or family disputes while your space is crowded.

## What to do now
1. **Ensure the death has been formally reported and verified.** If you have not already done so, you must call the appropriate service immediately to confirm the death.
   - Call **999** if the death was sudden, accidental, or unexpected.
   - Call the person’s GP or **NHS 111** if the death was expected (e.g., terminal illness).
2. **Secure your physical space and your phone.** Lock your external doors and set your phone to Do Not Disturb or Airplane Mode to stop the influx of notifications.
3. **Appoint a "gatekeeper" to manage all interactions.** Contact one trusted person (friend, relative, or neighbour) and ask them to handle the door and all messages for the next few hours. 
   - Ask them to physically stand at the entrance or manage your phone so you do not have to.
4. **Use a standard, repeatable boundary script.** Through the door or via text, state clearly:
   - “Thank you for your support. We are not having visitors today. We will reach out with updates when we are able.”
   - Repeat this exactly if people persist, then end the interaction.
5. **Issue a single group message to stop further arrivals.** If you are part of a family or community group chat, have your gatekeeper send one message: 
   - “The house is now closed to visitors to allow for privacy and rest. Please do not come round until further notice.”
6. **Place a physical sign on your front door.** Tape a clear note to the door:
   - “No visitors today. Please do not knock or ring the bell. Leave a message via [name of gatekeeper].”
7. **Identify and protect essential items.** Collect the deceased person’s wallet, phone, keys, and any legal papers (like a Will or ID) and place them in a secure bag or locked room. 
   - Clearly tell any people inside the house: “Please do not move, tidy, or sort any belongings right now.”
8. **Contact specialist support if you are overwhelmed.** If the shock feels unmanageable, you can access immediate help.
   - Call **NHS 111 and select the mental health option** (available 24/7) for urgent emotional crisis support.
   - Call the **Cruse Bereavement Support** helpline on **0808 808 1677** (check current hours) for grief-specific guidance.
9. **Call the police if you feel threatened or unsafe.** Use **999** if someone refuses to leave and becomes aggressive or if you feel in immediate danger. Use **101** or report online if you need non-emergency advice regarding harassment or uninvited presence.

## What can wait
- You do not need to respond to messages of condolence or give updates to extended family right now.
- You do not need to start planning the funeral or discussing the estate today.
- You do not need to manage the emotions or grief of other people who arrive at your home.
- You do not need to decide who is or is not "allowed" to be involved in the long term; the current closure is temporary.

## Important reassurance
Wanting to be alone or only with a few selected people after a death is a standard and healthy protective response to shock. It is not rude or disrespectful to close your home; it is a necessary step to ensure you have the space to process what has happened without the pressure of hosting others.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for managing immediate privacy and boundaries at home. It does not cover long-term bereavement counseling, estate law, or full funeral arrangements.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or medical advice. If you are in immediate danger, or if someone is attempting to enter your home against your will, contact the emergency services on 999.
