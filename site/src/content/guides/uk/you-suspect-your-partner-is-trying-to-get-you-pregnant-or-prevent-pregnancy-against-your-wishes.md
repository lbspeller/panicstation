# What to do if… you suspect your partner is trying to get you pregnant or prevent pregnancy against your wishes (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause and contact confidential specialist support from a device or account your partner cannot monitor. If pregnancy is possible, seek confidential sexual health care as soon as you safely can.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront them in the moment if you fear it could escalate, especially if they have threatened you, blocked you from leaving, or monitor your phone.
- Do not make visible contraception changes without clinical advice if pregnancy is possible — get medical advice as soon as you safely can.
- Do not assume it is just a misunderstanding if your consent is being bypassed or pressured — treat this as a safety issue.
- Do not use a shared device or shared accounts (for example shared Apple/Google accounts, shared location sharing, or shared laptops) to search for help if monitoring is possible.
- Do not destroy messages, packaging, photos, or notes in a panic if you may want support later.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** If you can, move to a place where you can think without them present (bathroom with the door locked, a short walk, a public place, or a friend’s home). If you are in immediate danger, call **999**.
2. **Use a safer way to reach help.** If there is any chance your phone is monitored, use a trusted friend’s phone, a work phone, or a library device. Avoid shared accounts; consider turning off location sharing only if it will not escalate risk.
3. **Contact a domestic abuse helpline that fits you.** Choose the option that feels safest and most relevant:
   - **National Domestic Abuse Helpline (Refuge):** Call 0808 2000 247 (24/7 service for women).
   - **Men’s Advice Line:** Call 0808 8010 327 (Mon–Fri 10am–8pm) or use their webchat.
   - **Galop:** Call 0800 999 5428 (for LGBT+ people experiencing abuse).
   Ask for **safety planning** that includes how to communicate safely, what to do if monitoring is suspected, and what to prepare if you need to leave quickly.
4. **Get confidential contraception and sexual health support.** Contact an **NHS sexual health clinic**, contraception service, or your **GP** and say you need confidential advice because you are worried a partner may be interfering with contraception or consent. Ask about:
   - **Pregnancy testing** and what timing makes sense for you.
   - **Emergency contraception** (which can be effective up to 3 or 5 days after sex, depending on the type).
   - **Contraception methods that are harder to interfere with**, such as the implant or IUD/IUS, if you want them.
   - **STI testing** if there was condom interference, non-consensual sex, or pressure you could not safely refuse.
5. **If a condom was removed without your consent (stealthing), consider a SARC.** In the UK, "stealthing" is legally classified as rape. A **Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC)** can offer confidential medical and practical support. You do **not** have to report to the police to get help.
6. **Make a quick record somewhere safe.** If it is safe, note dates and what made you concerned (missing pills, damaged condoms, threats, or refusal to let you use contraception). Store it somewhere your partner cannot access (with a trusted person or on paper kept outside the home).
7. **Tell one safe person and set a check-in.** Keep it simple: “I’m not safe to talk freely, but I’m worried my partner is interfering with contraception.” Agree a neutral code word meaning “call me” or “I need help.”
8. **Contact Rape Crisis for specialist sexual violence support.** Call the 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line on 0808 500 2222. You can talk confidentially about what happened and what you want now without pressure to report.

## What can wait
- You do **not** have to decide today whether to report to the police, end the relationship, or move out.
- You do **not** have to decide today whether to tell your family or friends about the situation.
- You do **not** have to prove anything or provide evidence to get help from a helpline, SARC, GP, or sexual health clinic.
- You can decide later whether you want to keep documentation or speak to a legal advocate.

## Important reassurance
Feeling confused, unsure, or like you are “overreacting” is common when someone undermines your reproductive choices. Wanting control over if or when you become pregnant is basic bodily autonomy. Reproductive coercion is a form of coercive control and you deserve support, privacy, and safety.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step guidance only to help you stabilize your situation and access confidential support. Later decisions regarding your relationship, health, or legal options may require professional specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical or legal advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If pregnancy or health harm is possible, seek urgent clinical care from an NHS service as soon as you safely can.
