# What to do if… you are asked to return medical equipment or medications after a death (UK)

## Short answer
Slow the request down and verify who is asking before anything leaves the home. Keep medicines secure, especially strong pain medicines, and arrange equipment return through the original provider or local community equipment service.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hand equipment or medicines to anyone you cannot clearly identify.
- Don’t throw medicines in the household bin, pour liquids down the sink, or flush them.
- Don’t give unused medicines to anyone else.
- Don’t let equipment be removed before you have noted what is being taken.
- Don’t dismantle heavy equipment, oxygen equipment, beds, pumps, or mattresses yourself if you are not sure it is safe.
- Don’t move or dispose of items that police, the coroner, or another official has specifically told you to leave in place.

## What to do now
1. **Take the pressure off the handover.**
   - Ask for the organisation name, the person’s name and role, and exactly what they want collected or returned.
   - Say: “I can’t do an on-the-spot handover. I’ll call the main number to confirm and arrange it.”

2. **Call back using a trusted route.**
   - For NHS or community loaned equipment, look for a sticker, label, delivery note, discharge paperwork, or equipment register showing the supplier.
   - If you cannot find the supplier, ask the GP practice, district nursing team, hospice, or hospital discharge team which service supplied it.
   - For hospice equipment, call the hospice main switchboard rather than a number given by an unexpected caller.

3. **Make a quick inventory before anything leaves.**
   - Write down the item, supplier, any asset or serial number, and key accessories such as a charger, pump, mattress, tubing, sling, remote, or cushion.
   - Take photos of labels and serial numbers.
   - Ask whether the collector can give a collection note or receipt listing what was taken.

4. **Secure all medicines in one place.**
   - Gather tablets, liquids, patches, injections, “just in case” medicines, and strong pain medicines into one bag or box.
   - Keep them in their original packaging if possible.
   - Put the bag or box out of sight, ideally locked away, until you can use a verified return route.

5. **Use a community pharmacy as the usual safe return route for medicines.**
   - NHS public guidance says unwanted medicines should not go in the bin or toilet and should be taken to a pharmacy for safe disposal.
   - Call the pharmacy first if there is a large quantity, strong pain medicine, patches, injections, or anything you think may be a controlled drug, such as morphine, oxycodone or fentanyl.
   - If a pharmacy cannot take the medicines immediately because it does not have safe storage capacity, keep them secure and ask when to return or which local pharmacy can help.
   - If you cannot leave home, ask the hospice, district nurse team, GP practice, or pharmacy what the local arrangement is.

6. **Verify any medicine collection before handing medicines over.**
   - Only hand medicines to a collector if the organisation confirms through an official number that this is part of their process.
   - Do not use an informal collector, courier, neighbour, or driver unless the organisation has confirmed the arrangement.
   - Ask the collector to show identification.
   - Keep a note of the date, organisation, collector name if given, and what was collected.

7. **Arrange equipment return safely.**
   - Ask for a collection appointment or window.
   - Tell the service if the item is heavy, upstairs, connected to power, connected to oxygen equipment, or cannot be moved by you.
   - Keep accessories with the matching item so they are not lost.

8. **Handle sharps carefully.**
   - If there is a sharps bin, close or lock it if you can do so safely and keep it upright.
   - Do not put loose needles, syringes, or sharps in household rubbish or recycling.
   - Ask the pharmacy, GP practice, district nurse team, hospice, or local council what the local sharps collection or drop-off route is.
   - In England and Wales, GOV.UK can route you to local council clinical waste collection where available.

## What can wait
- You do not need to sort every medicine today; secure them first, then choose a safe verified return route.
- You do not need to deep-clean, repackage, dismantle, or move equipment yourself.
- You do not need to settle ownership, charges, or timing today.
- You do not need to respond to pressure for an immediate doorstep handover.

## Important reassurance
It can feel abrupt to be asked for returns so soon after a death. It is reasonable to pause, verify identity, secure medicines, and ask for a proper record of what leaves the home.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent unsafe disposal, loss, mix-ups, and unverified removal. Later questions about ownership, charges, complaints, or disputed timing may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Local NHS, council, hospice, pharmacy, and equipment-provider arrangements can vary, so check through an official contact route if anything is unclear.
