# What to do if… your smartwatch flags an unusually low heart rate and you feel faint (UK)

## Short answer
Lie down flat immediately with your legs raised and call 999 if you have fainted, have chest pain, are struggling to breathe, or are confused. Treat the physical symptoms as the priority over the digital alert.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stand up, walk, or "test" your balance to see if you are okay.
- Do not drive yourself to a GP surgery or A&E.
- Do not continue checking the watch or scrolling through heart rate data while upright.
- Do not consume caffeine or extra medication to try and "boost" your heart rate.
- Do not stay alone in a locked room; ensure someone knows you feel unwell.

## What to do now
1. **Get into a safe position on the floor immediately.** Lie flat on your back to prevent a fall.
   - If possible, raise your legs so they are higher than your head (using a chair or cushions) to improve blood flow to the brain.
   - If you cannot lie down, sit on the floor with your back against a wall.
2. **Call 999 immediately if any of the following emergency criteria are met.** Tell the operator you have a low heart rate alert and feel faint.
   - You or the person with you has fainted and cannot be woken within one minute.
   - You have chest pain, pressure, or tightness.
   - You have sudden shortness of breath or difficulty speaking.
   - You feel suddenly confused, disoriented, or are shaking/jerking.
   - You fainted while exercising or while already lying down.
3. **Alert someone nearby or unlock your front door.** If you are alone, call a neighbor or family member and tell them to come to your location. Leave the front door unlocked so emergency services can enter if you lose consciousness.
4. **Identify observable cues for the clinician.** Without standing up, note any of the following to report to 111 or 999:
   - Visible paleness or blue tint to lips/fingernails.
   - Cold, clammy, or sweaty skin.
   - Blurred or "tunnel" vision.
   - A pulse that feels irregular, thumping, or very weak at the wrist.
5. **Contact NHS 111 if you do not meet the 999 criteria but the symptoms are new.** Use the 111 online service or call 111 for an urgent clinical assessment. They may arrange for a GP appointment or advise you to attend an Urgent Treatment Centre.
6. **Keep your smartwatch on but stop interacting with it.** The device may continue to log data that is useful for doctors later (such as an automated ECG trace), but manual re-checking can increase physical stress.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the technical accuracy of the watch right now.
- You do not need to research the definition of "bradycardia" or normal heart rate ranges.
- You do not need to decide on long-term lifestyle changes or supplement use in this moment.
- You do not need to contact the device manufacturer.

## Important reassurance
Feeling faint or seeing an emergency alert can cause physical sensations of anxiety, such as a racing pulse or shaking. This is a common physiological response to a perceived threat. By lying down and elevating your legs, you are taking the most effective immediate step to stabilize your blood flow.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute episode of faintness combined with a low heart rate alert. It does not replace clinical diagnosis or long-term management of heart rhythm disorders.

## Important note
A smartwatch is not a diagnostic medical device, but its alerts should be taken seriously when accompanied by physical symptoms. If you experience recurrent fainting or blackouts, you are legally required to notify the DVLA, as it may affect your fitness to drive.
