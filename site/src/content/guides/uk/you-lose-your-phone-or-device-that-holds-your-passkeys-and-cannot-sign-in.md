# What to do if… you lose your phone or device that holds your passkeys and cannot sign in (UK)

## Short answer
Secure the missing device first, then protect the email or main device account that controls your recovery options. Use only the service’s official sign-in recovery route from a trusted device or computer.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click “we found your phone”, “confirm your login”, or “recover your account” links from unexpected texts, emails, or social messages.
- Don’t factory-reset or wipe your only remaining signed-in device to “start fresh”; it may be your last working route back in.
- Don’t keep repeating recovery attempts after a lockout or rate-limit message; pause, write down what happened, then use the official support route.
- Don’t create a duplicate account with the same email just to get in; it can make support checks harder.
- Don’t share one-time codes, recovery codes, security information screenshots, or passkey prompts with anyone who contacted you first.

## What to do now
1. **Work out whether the device is only misplaced or may be stolen.**
   - If it is probably nearby, search briefly, but still protect the account.
   - If it was lost in public, stolen, or you are not sure, treat the device and phone number as needing protection.

2. **Use the official lost-device tool for the missing device.**
   - For an Apple device, use Find My or iCloud Find Devices to mark it as lost if that option is available.
   - For an Android device, use Google’s Find My Device or “find, secure, or erase” flow to secure it.
   - For a Windows device, use Microsoft’s Find My Device to locate or lock it if it was enabled.
   - Avoid erasing the device unless you are comfortable that erasing may remove data and may not help you recover the passkey.

3. **If the missing passkey device is a phone, contact your mobile network provider.**
   - Ask what they can do to suspend or block the SIM if the phone may be stolen.
   - Ask about extra protection on the mobile account, because phone numbers are often used in account recovery.

4. **Find any device or browser where you are still signed in.**
   - Check a laptop, tablet, desktop browser, or another phone.
   - If you are still signed in to the locked service, go to its security settings and look for devices, sessions, passkeys, security keys, or trusted devices.
   - If the option is available, remove the missing device, sign out other sessions you do not recognise, and add a new passkey on a device you control.

5. **Protect the account that stores or syncs your passkeys.**
   - Check your Apple Account, Google Account, Microsoft account, or password manager account from a trusted device.
   - If passkeys were synced through a credential manager, they may return on a replacement device after you restore that account and complete the provider’s checks.
   - If you use an Apple recovery key, follow Apple’s recovery flow carefully, because recovery options can be stricter.

6. **Start the locked service’s official recovery route.**
   - Open a private or fresh browser window on a trusted computer.
   - Type the provider’s official address yourself.
   - Choose the provider’s “can’t sign in”, “forgotten details”, “account recovery”, or support option.
   - Write down the exact username or email used, the options offered, and any on-screen message or case number.

7. **If you see signs of account misuse, make an official report trail.**
   - If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud for cyber crime or fraud reports.
   - If you are in Scotland, report non-emergency fraud or cyber crime to Police Scotland on 101.
   - If a crime is happening now or someone is in immediate danger, call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to abandon the account, buy new hardware, or change every digital service you use.
- You do not need to audit every account at once; start with email, the main Apple, Google, or Microsoft account, banking, and the account you are locked out of.
- You do not need to perfect your setup while stressed. Aim first for safe access restored or the account secured.

## Important reassurance
Losing the device that holds a passkey is a known problem with passwordless login. It can feel urgent, but going in order helps: secure the device, protect recovery accounts, then use official recovery.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise access and reduce harm. Later decisions about account recovery, device replacement, recovery keys, or stronger account setup may need help from the provider, your bank, your mobile network, or another specialist support route.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Recovery steps vary by provider, device, and your previous settings. If you see unexpected recovery prompts, new devices, payment activity, or security changes you did not make, prioritise securing your main accounts and using official support or reporting channels.
