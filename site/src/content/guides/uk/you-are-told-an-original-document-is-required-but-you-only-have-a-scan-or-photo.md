# What to do if… you are told an original document is required but you only have a scan or photo (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and ask the organisation to confirm in writing whether they need the physical original, an original digital document issued by the authority, a certified copy, or a legalised document. Then move to the fastest version they will accept, usually an official replacement from the issuer or a properly certified copy.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a screenshot, printout, or phone photo will be accepted because it is clear.
- Do not edit the content of the image or crop away edges, stamps, seals, watermarks, signatures, page numbers, or the back of the document.
- Do not send your only physical original by post or courier without tracking, suitable cover, and a return plan.
- Do not pay an online certification, notarisation, translation, or apostille service until the requesting organisation confirms it will accept that route.
- Do not keep guessing what “original format” means; that can waste the time you need to get the right replacement.

## What to do now
1. **Ask one clarifying question and get the answer in writing.**  
   Send: “When you say ‘original format’, do you mean the physical original document, the original digital document issued by the authority, a certified copy, or a legalised apostilled version?”  
   Also ask: “Will you accept an official replacement from the issuing authority?”

2. **Separate the document type before you spend money.**
   - **Passport or travel document:** do not rely on a scan or photo unless the airline, border authority, appointment centre, or requesting organisation confirms it is acceptable. If you are abroad and cannot use your UK passport for urgent travel, check whether an emergency travel document may be available.
   - **UK immigration status evidence:** ask whether they need an official online status check, such as an eVisa or share code, rather than a scan.
   - **Birth, marriage, death, adoption, or civil partnership certificate:** order an official copy from the relevant issuing authority or register office. The route can differ for England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland.
   - **Qualification, employment, bank, or institutional document:** ask the issuing organisation whether it can issue an official replacement, official transcript, verification letter, or certified copy.

3. **Ask the original issuer for the fastest official version.**  
   Contact the body that issued the document and ask for:
   - an official replacement or official copy
   - the fastest dispatch option available
   - delivery to your current location, if you are abroad
   - a reference number or confirmation email showing you have requested it

4. **If they will accept a certified copy, get their exact certification rule first.**  
   Ask who may certify it, what wording they require, whether every page must be certified, and whether the certifier must see the physical original. Ordinary UK certified-copy rules may allow a professional person or person of good standing, but the receiving organisation can set stricter rules.

5. **If the document is for official use overseas, check whether it needs legalisation or an apostille.**  
   The UK route for legalising certain UK documents is through the FCDO Legalisation Office. Before applying, ask the requester whether they need the original document or a certified copy, whether a UK solicitor or notary must sign it first, and whether they will accept an e-Apostille or need a paper apostille.

6. **If this is blocking travel, an appointment, or access to a service, ask for a temporary route.**
   - At airline check-in, a visa centre, a university desk, a bank, or an appointment counter, say plainly: “I only have a photo or scan. What can you accept today while I obtain the official version?”
   - Ask whether they can accept proof that you have ordered the replacement, a reference number, an issuer contact, a rescheduled appointment, or a short extension.
   - If you are abroad, ask for the requirement and any temporary alternative in writing.

7. **Make a small proof pack for the next person you contact.**  
   Keep together the scan or photo, the written requirement, proof that you have requested an official replacement or certified copy, payment or dispatch confirmation, and any case or reference numbers.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, ask for compensation, or challenge the rule.
- You do not need to argue about whether the rule is fair; first get the exact acceptable document route.
- You do not need to pay for certification, legalisation, translation, courier services, or fresh appointments until you know which version they will accept.

## Important reassurance
This is a common document problem. Organisations often use “original”, “original format”, “certified”, “notarised”, and “legalised” loosely. This is not a judgement on you; the next useful step is to pin down the wording and move to the fastest official substitute.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the situation, avoid irreversible mistakes, and get the requirement in writing. Later decisions may need specialist help from the issuing body, the receiving organisation, a solicitor, a notary, a translator, or an immigration or travel specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, immigration, financial, travel, or other professional advice. Requirements vary by the receiving country and the organisation requesting the document. Follow the requesting authority’s written instructions and use official channels for replacement, certification, and legalisation.
