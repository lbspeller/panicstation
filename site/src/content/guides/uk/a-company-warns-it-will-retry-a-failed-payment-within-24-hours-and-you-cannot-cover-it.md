# What to do if… a company warns it will retry a failed payment within 24 hours and you cannot cover it (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank or building society now to cancel the Direct Debit instruction, then tell the company you cannot cover the retry and need another way or date to pay. Because the retry is within 24 hours, ask the bank whether the payment is already in flight and what can still be stopped.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume “it failed once, so nothing will happen”; a retry may still be attempted.
- Don’t guess the payment type; check whether it is a Direct Debit, standing order, or recurring card payment, because the way to stop it is different.
- Don’t move money needed for essentials, such as rent, food, travel to work, or utilities, without checking what else is due to leave your account.
- Don’t close your bank account in a rush; this can create more missed payments and make later checks harder.
- Don’t assume cancelling the Direct Debit cancels the bill or contract; it stops that payment method, not any money you may still owe.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm it is a Direct Debit.**  
   In your banking app or online banking, look at the payment details. If it says Direct Debit, use the steps below. If it is a recurring card payment, contact the card issuer and say you withdraw consent for that payment and want the next payment stopped.

2. **Check your available balance and the next 48 hours of payments.**  
   Look for rent, mortgage, food money, utilities, travel costs, childcare, insurance, or other payments that would cause more immediate harm if the Direct Debit retry goes through.

3. **Cancel the Direct Debit instruction with your bank or building society now.**  
   Use the banking app or online banking if you can, or call the bank. Ask them to confirm the Direct Debit is cancelled and ask whether the retry is already in flight. Cancelling is still sensible, but a payment very close to collection may already be processing.

4. **Tell the company straight away that you cannot cover the retry.**  
   Send a message or email if possible, so you have a record. Say that you have cancelled the Direct Debit instruction, cannot cover a retry within 24 hours, and need them not to re-present the payment by Direct Debit.

5. **Ask for one clear alternative.**  
   Ask for a later payment date, a one-off payment when you can cover it, or an invoice or bank-transfer option. Do not agree to a date or amount that would leave you unable to pay essentials.

6. **Keep proof while it is easy.**  
   Screenshot the retry warning, the Direct Debit cancellation confirmation, and any message you send to the company. Note the date and time you contacted your bank and the company.

7. **If the payment is taken anyway, contact your bank immediately.**  
   Say you want to claim under the Direct Debit Guarantee if you believe the Direct Debit was taken in error, such as after cancellation, on the wrong date, or for the wrong amount. Ask the bank what it needs from you and keep a record of the response.

8. **If fees or interest appear, ask about them quickly.**  
   Ask the bank whether it will refund or reverse any fees caused by an incorrect Direct Debit. If the bank or company does not resolve the problem, use its complaints process and keep copies of all messages.

## What can wait
- You do not need to solve every bill today.
- You do not need to agree a full repayment plan in the next few minutes.
- You do not need to make a formal complaint unless the payment is taken incorrectly, fees appear, or the bank or company refuses to deal with the problem.
- You do not need to explain everything in detail to the company right now; a short written message asking them not to retry and asking for a safe alternative is enough for this first step.

## Important reassurance
A failed Direct Debit retry warning is stressful, but it is a common banking problem with clear first steps. Cancelling the payment method and getting the company to confirm an alternative can buy time, even if the underlying bill still needs sorting out.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next 24–48 hours to reduce immediate financial harm. Later decisions about the bill, contract, arrears, affordability, or wider debts may need specialist money or debt advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, debt, or professional advice. Banks and companies have different cut-off times and processes, so ask them to confirm in writing what has been cancelled, what may still happen, and what your next payment options are.
