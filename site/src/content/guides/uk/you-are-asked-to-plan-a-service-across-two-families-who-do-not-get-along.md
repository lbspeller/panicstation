# What to do if… you are asked to plan a service across two families who do not get along (UK)

## Short answer
Identify the legal Personal Representative (the executor named in a Will, or the person with priority under Intestacy Rules) and ensure the funeral director accepts instructions only from them. Establish a single point of contact and a controlled communication channel to prevent conflicting orders.

## Do not do these things
- Don't attempt to mediate historic family grievances or resolve long-standing disputes during the planning process.
- Don't sign a funeral contract or pay a deposit unless you are the authorised Personal Representative or have their written permission.
- Don't promise specific inclusions (such as speakers or music) to either family until the authorised person has approved the final plan.
- Don't share sensitive information regarding the estate, cause of death, or private family history in an attempt to justify decisions.
- Don't allow yourself to be used as a messenger for insults or demands between hostile parties.
- Don't assume "next of kin" is a single legal status; several people may have equal standing unless a Will specifies an executor.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm who has the legal right to give instructions to the funeral director.**
   - **Check for a Will immediately.** If one exists, the named executors have the legal right to the body and the final say on funeral arrangements.
   - **Identify the Administrator if there is no Will.** Priority follows the "Rules of Intestacy" (Spouse/Civil Partner, then Children, then Parents, then Siblings).
   - **Establish who will be the "Applicant" on the funeral contract.** This person is personally liable for the costs and is the only person the funeral director is legally required to listen to.
2. **Formally notify the funeral director of the potential for conflict.**
   - **Inform the funeral director in writing** that there is a family dispute regarding the arrangements.
   - **Request that the funeral director notes the file** to only accept instructions from the named Applicant/Personal Representative.
   - **Ask the funeral director to provide a copy of their trade body code of practice** (NAFD or SAIF) regarding instruction disputes.
3. **Establish a single, transparent communication channel for all requests.**
   - **Send a neutral message to both families** stating that to ensure the service proceeds without error, all suggestions must be sent to one person (the Applicant) via a specific email or text thread.
   - **State clearly that the funeral director and officiant** will not take individual calls or requests from family members outside of this process.
4. **Utilise the funeral director or officiant as a neutral buffer for the service structure.**
   - **Ask the officiant/celebrant to set the service template.** Use a standard, neutral structure to limit the number of "choice points" that could cause conflict.
   - **Request that the officiant manages the "tributes" list.** They can set a hard limit on the number of speakers and a strict time limit for each.
5. **Draft a "Minimum Agreement" plan focused on logistics rather than sentiment.**
   - **Prioritise essential decisions first:** date, time, location, and method (burial or cremation).
   - **Use "closed lists" for non-essentials.** Offer three music choices or two reading options rather than asking for open suggestions.
6. **Arrange practical "flashpoint" controls for the day of the service.**
   - **Request reserved seating blocks** that keep hostile parties on opposite sides of the aisle or separated by a buffer row.
   - **Ask the funeral director to provide extra staff/ushers** to manage the arrival and seating of guests to prevent direct confrontations.
   - **Ensure the venue has a clear entry/exit plan** so that groups do not have to congregate in the same small foyer or waiting area.
7. **Consider a "Split-Service" or "Dual Gathering" model if agreement is impossible.**
   - **Suggest a small, private committal for immediate legal family** followed by two separate memorial gatherings at different times or locations.
   - **Remind both parties that a "funeral" is the legal disposal of the body,** while a "memorial" can be held by anyone, anywhere, at any time.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on long-term issues such as the placement of a headstone or the final scattering of ashes right now; these can be held by the funeral director for months.
- You do not need to resolve which family "meant more" to the deceased or settle arguments about the deceased's past.
- You do not need to finalise an elaborate wake or reception if the tension makes a joint event unsafe or unmanageable.

## Important reassurance
High-conflict family dynamics are common during bereavement and funeral directors are trained to handle them. Feeling overwhelmed or pressured is a normal response to this situation. Establishing clear legal boundaries early is the most effective way to reduce the emotional burden on everyone involved.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step guidance only to stabilise funeral planning and prevent escalation. It does not cover long-term estate litigation or complex legal challenges to a Will.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. In the UK, while executors have the right to decide on the disposal of the body, these decisions can be challenged in court if they are deemed unreasonable. In Scotland, different statutory regulations may apply under the Burial and Cremation (Scotland) Act. If a dispute becomes physical or involves threats, contact the police.
