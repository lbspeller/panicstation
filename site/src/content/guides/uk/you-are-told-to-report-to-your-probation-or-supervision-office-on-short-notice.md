# What to do if… you are told to report to your probation or supervision office on short notice (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as time-sensitive: verify the instruction using a trusted probation, supervision, justice social work, or licence contact route, then attend if you can. If you cannot attend on time, contact the supervising office or worker immediately, keep a record of the contact, and ask exactly what they need you to do now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the message or wait to see what happens.
- Do not assume it is genuine if it came from an unfamiliar number and the person cannot confirm basic details such as your name, the office, and who supervises you.
- Do not miss the appointment without first trying to contact the office or worker supervising you and explain why.
- Do not send angry, threatening, or long messages; keep it brief, factual, and calm where possible.
- Do not bring anything that could cause security concerns or breach your conditions.
- Do not post about the instruction, your case, or your conditions on social media while panicking.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the instruction using a trusted route.**  
   Use the number or email from your court, licence, sentence, supervision, justice social work, or probation paperwork. If you do not have it, use an official contact route for the office or service named in your paperwork, such as Probation Finder where it applies, rather than only replying to the number that contacted you. Ask: “Is this reporting instruction genuine, when and where do I need to attend, and who do I need to see?”

2. **Ask for the details in a form you can keep.**  
   Ask for a text, email, or letter confirming the date, time, address, and name of the person you must see. If they cannot send it, write down the details yourself, including the time of the call and the name of the person you spoke to.

3. **If you can attend, prepare for a simple, low-risk visit.**  
   Check the address and travel time. Take any supervision or probation paperwork you have, photo ID if you have it, and a charged phone kept on silent. Ask in advance if there is anything you need to bring or avoid bringing.

4. **If you genuinely cannot get there on time, say so immediately.**  
   Give the specific reason and ask what they want you to do now. Offer a clear alternative, such as a later time the same day, the first available time the next day, or a phone check-in now followed by an in-person appointment as soon as possible.

5. **Keep the contact traceable.**  
   If nobody answers, leave a voicemail if possible. Follow up by text or email if you have a contact method: “I tried to call at [time]. I have been told to report today. I can attend at [time] or need confirmation of what to do next.” Keep call logs and screenshots.

6. **Preserve quick proof if there is a real barrier.**  
   If work, childcare, travel disruption, illness, or a medical appointment is the issue, keep simple proof that already exists, such as rota messages, appointment confirmations, travel disruption screenshots, or employer messages. Do not spend hours gathering evidence before making contact.

7. **If breach, recall, or court is mentioned, focus on contact first.**  
   Missed meetings or appointments without a good reason may have serious consequences, and licence, parole, or court issues may need quick attention. Make contact traceable, follow the instruction if you can, and briefly alert a solicitor or support worker who already knows your case if you have one.

## What can wait
- You do not need to argue today about whether the short notice was fair.
- You do not need to write a long explanation before making contact.
- You do not need to make a complaint, or request a change of officer or area, before dealing with the immediate instruction.
- You do not need to decide wider case strategy in this moment.

## Important reassurance
A sudden instruction to report can feel threatening, but the most protective first step is small and practical: confirm it, respond quickly, and keep a record. Even if you cannot attend exactly as requested, prompt contact is usually better than silence.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance for handling a sudden instruction to report. Later decisions about disputes, repeated short-notice demands, disability or health needs, travel barriers, licence conditions, or breach action may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide provides general practical information and is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Probation, licence, and supervision requirements depend on your sentence, paperwork, location, and individual conditions, so confirm directly with the relevant office or practitioner as soon as possible.
