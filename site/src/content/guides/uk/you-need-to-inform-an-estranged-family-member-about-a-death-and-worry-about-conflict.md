# What to do if… you need to inform an estranged family member about a death and worry about conflict (UK)

## Short answer
Send a brief, factual written notification via a low-contact channel (text, email, or letter) and immediately mute further communications to prevent escalation or entrapment in an argument.

## Do not do these things
- Don't meet them in person or invite them into your home if you anticipate an aggressive or volatile reaction.
- Don't use social media or public posts for the initial notification.
- Don't include personal grievances, historical blame, or emotional explanations in the message.
- Don't feel obligated to answer the phone immediately after sending the notification.
- Don't discuss the will, money, or inheritance in the first communication.
- Don't provide your current location or address if you feel physically unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Choose a safety-first communication channel.**  
   **Use a text message, email, or a physical letter to ensure there is a written record and no immediate verbal pressure.**
   - If a direct message is too high-risk, ask a solicitor or a neutral third party to deliver the news.

2. **Draft a minimalist, factual notification.**  
   **Include only the name of the deceased, the date of death, and a statement that further details are not yet available.**
   - Do not include funeral times or locations unless you have already decided they are welcome and safe to attend.

3. **Set a clear boundary for future contact.**  
   **State explicitly how and when you will provide updates, and that you will not engage in verbal arguments.**
   - Example: "I will provide updates via email when funeral arrangements are made; please do not call my mobile number."

4. **Isolate yourself from the immediate reaction.**  
   **Mute the relative's contact notification settings or put your phone on 'Do Not Disturb' for at least two hours after sending the message.**
   - This prevents 'reactivity' where you feel pressured to defend yourself against an angry reply.

5. **Secure your physical environment.**  
   **Ensure your doors are locked and avoid being alone if you expect the person might travel to your location uninvited.**
   - If you live in a flat with a communal entrance, do not buzz in unexpected visitors.

6. **Check your 2026 employment rights for bereavement leave.**  
   **Determine if you are eligible for the 'day one' statutory bereavement leave introduced under the Employment Rights Act 2025/2026 to give yourself time away from work during the crisis.**
   - This right now applies to all employees from their first day of employment for the loss of a dependent.

7. **Use the correct 'Tell Us Once' procedure based on location.**  
   **In England, Scotland, or Wales, use the Tell Us Once service provided by the registrar to notify government departments, but note this does not notify estranged individuals.**
   - If the person died in Northern Ireland, you cannot use Tell Us Once; contact the Northern Ireland Bereavement Service on 0800 085 2463 instead.

8. **Contact the police for non-emergency harassment.**  
   **Call 101 if the relative begins a pattern of harassment, stalking, or unwanted persistent contact that makes you feel unsafe.**
   - In a situation of immediate threat or violence, call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the guest list for the funeral today.
- You do not need to read long, accusatory emails or listen to voicemails right now.
- You do not need to justify why you were the one to notify them.
- You do not need to engage with questions about the estate or belongings for several weeks.

## Important reassurance
Choosing a low-contact method of notification is a valid safety measure, not a sign of weakness or "disrespect" to the deceased. You are allowed to protect your mental and physical safety while fulfilling the social obligation of informing family.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for death notification in high-conflict scenarios. It does not cover legal disputes over probate or long-term mediation of family estrangement.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or security advice. If you are a victim of domestic abuse or fear for your life, contact the National Domestic Abuse Helpline (0808 2000 247) or emergency services immediately.
