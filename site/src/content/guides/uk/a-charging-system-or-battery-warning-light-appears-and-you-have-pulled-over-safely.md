# What to do if… a charging-system or battery warning light appears and you have pulled over safely (UK)

## Short answer
Stay safe and visible, then get help rather than trying to continue. A charging-system or battery warning may mean the car can soon lose electrical power or stop without much warning.

## Do not do these things
- Do not rejoin fast traffic just to “get a bit further” unless a clearly safer place is immediately reachable and the vehicle still feels normal.
- Do not repeatedly restart the engine to see if the warning clears.
- Do not attempt roadside repairs on a motorway, hard shoulder, emergency area, or live carriageway.
- Do not place a warning triangle on a motorway.
- Do not touch orange cables, sealed high-voltage parts, or unfamiliar wiring on a hybrid or electric vehicle.
- Do not stand between your vehicle and moving traffic.

## What to do now
1. **Keep the vehicle visible.**  
   Keep hazard lights on. If it is dark or visibility is poor, use sidelights too.

2. **If you are on a motorway or other high-speed road, get safe first.**  
   If it is safe and possible, leave the vehicle by the side furthest from traffic and wait well away from the vehicle, behind a barrier if there is one. If you cannot get out safely, are stopped in a live lane, or feel in immediate danger, stay in the vehicle with seat belts and hazards on and call 999.

3. **Preserve the remaining electrical power.**  
   Turn off non-essential electrical loads, such as heated seats, heated screens, interior lights, high fan settings, and non-essential chargers. Keep power for hazard lights and essential visibility.

4. **Do not keep testing the engine.**  
   If you smell burning, see smoke, feel unusual heat, or anything seems unsafe, switch off the engine, move people away if it is safe, and call 999. Otherwise, do not repeatedly switch the engine off and on; ask the emergency phone operator, highway contact, or recovery provider what to do while you wait.

5. **Get help for the road you are on.**  
   - On England motorways and major A-roads, use a free emergency roadside telephone where available, or call National Highways on 0300 123 5000.
   - On Scotland trunk roads and motorways, call Traffic Scotland on 0800 028 1414.
   - On Wales trunk roads and motorways, call Traffic Wales on 0300 123 1213.
   - In Northern Ireland, use a motorway emergency telephone where available, call 999 if you are in immediate danger or cannot get clear of traffic, then contact your breakdown or recovery provider.
   - If you are not on one of these roads, contact your breakdown or recovery provider once everyone is in the safest available place.

6. **Give your exact location.**  
   Use the road name, direction of travel, junction number, nearest exit, driver location sign, marker post, emergency area number, or a phone map location.

7. **Only do a look-only check if you are well away from moving traffic.**  
   In a car park, driveway, or quiet lay-by, you can look for an obviously loose battery clamp or visible damage. If it needs tools, force, touching hot parts, or you are unsure, stop and wait for recovery.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out whether the fault is the alternator, belt, battery, wiring, or charging module right now.
- You do not need to choose a garage, compare prices, or decide on repairs while you are still roadside.
- You do not need to prove the warning is serious by restarting or driving further.

## Important reassurance
Pulling over safely was the right first step. This warning is a common breakdown reason, and the safest priority is to avoid losing power in a worse place.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to keep people safe and prevent the situation escalating. Diagnosis, repair decisions, and any dispute about recovery or costs can wait until you are away from roadside risk and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not a substitute for professional, legal, mechanical, medical, financial, therapeutic, or emergency advice. If you feel unsafe where you are, call 999.
