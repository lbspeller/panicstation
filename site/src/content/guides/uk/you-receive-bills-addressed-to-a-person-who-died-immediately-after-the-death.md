# What to do if… you receive bills addressed to a person who died immediately after the death (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay any bills using your own money. Notify the sender of the death so the account can be frozen or marked "deceased" while the estate is formally processed by the executor or administrator.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay the bill from your personal bank account to "clear the air" or stop letters.
- Don’t agree to transfer a debt into your own name or set up a personal payment plan.
- Don’t ignore genuine "final demand" letters; notify the sender of the death instead to pause the process.
- Don’t send original death certificates in the post; use certified copies or digital notification services where possible.
- Don’t feel pressured by automated "late payment" language or red text on envelopes.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the person with legal authority to handle the estate.**
   - If there is a will, this is the **Executor**.
   - If there is no will (intestacy), this is the **Administrator** (usually the closest living relative).
   - If you are not this person, pass the bills to them and do not communicate with the creditors yourself.
2. **Organise all incoming mail into a single secure folder.** **For each bill, log the sender, account number, date, and amount.**
3. **Use the "Tell Us Once" service if available in your area.** **This government service notifies many departments (HMRC, DWP, DVLA, and local councils) simultaneously after a death is registered.**
   - Note: This service is available in England, Scotland, and Wales, but is not currently available in Northern Ireland.
4. **Use the "Death Notification Service" to notify multiple banks and building societies at once.** **This free online service allows you to inform most major UK financial institutions of the death in one step.**
5. **Contact utility providers and service companies individually.** **Ask them to note the account as "deceased" and request a final meter reading or balance as of the date of death.**
   - Essential services (gas, water, electricity) will not typically be disconnected immediately following a death notification.
6. **Prioritise funeral expenses if funds are needed.** **Most UK banks will allow you to pay the funeral director directly from the deceased person's bank account before probate is granted if you provide an original invoice and death certificate.**
7. **Inform credit card companies and loan providers that the estate is being settled.** **Request that they stop adding interest or late fees while the legal process (probate or letters of administration) is underway.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to pay off any unsecured debts (like credit cards) today.
- You do not need to decide how to split the remaining money among beneficiaries yet.
- You do not need to settle the final Inheritance Tax bill immediately; you usually have six months from the end of the month of death.
- You do not need to respond to every automated letter within 24 hours.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for automated billing systems to continue sending letters for several weeks after a death. Receiving these does not mean you are in trouble or that you have failed to do something. Creditors are accustomed to this process and will usually pause collection activity once they have been formally notified.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilisation and notification only. It does not cover the full probate process, insolvency rules, or the complex legal order of debt priority.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. If the estate has more debt than assets (an "insolvent estate"), seek professional legal advice before paying any creditors, as specific laws dictate the order of payment and you could become liable if you pay them in the wrong order.
