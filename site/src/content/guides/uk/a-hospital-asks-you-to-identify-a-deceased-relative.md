# What to do if… a hospital asks you to identify a deceased relative (UK)

## Short answer
Call the hospital back through the main switchboard, ask for the bereavement team, and confirm who is asking for identification and what they need from you. Ask whether a visual viewing is necessary, whether another method can be used, or whether another suitable relative or person can attend instead.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t rush to the hospital without first confirming the caller’s name, role, team, and a safe number to call back.
- Don’t go alone if you are in shock; take a calm support person, or ask staff whether someone can accompany you.
- Don’t assume “identify” always means you must view the body without warning; ask what will happen before you agree to proceed.
- Don’t sign forms or give a statement while confused unless you understand what it is for; ask for a pause and a plain-English explanation.
- Don’t force yourself to be the identifier if you feel unable; say this clearly and ask what other options are acceptable in this case.
- Don’t make funeral, probate, account-closing, or wider family decisions while you are still trying to understand this immediate request.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the call through the hospital.**  
   Call the hospital switchboard using a number you find yourself, not only a number from the message. Ask for the bereavement office, bereavement centre, ward, or mortuary team dealing with your relative. Write down the caller’s name, role, team, direct number, and any hospital or case reference they can safely share.

2. **Ask who is leading the request.**  
   Ask: “Is this being handled by the hospital, by a coroner in England, Wales or Northern Ireland, by the police, or by the Procurator Fiscal in Scotland?” Then ask: “Why is identification needed in this case, and what exactly are you asking me to do?”

3. **Ask whether a visual viewing is needed.**  
   Say: “I am not sure I can cope with seeing them. Is visual identification required, can identity be confirmed another way, or can another suitable person do this?” If police, a coroner, or the Procurator Fiscal is involved, ask who they will accept as the family contact or identifier.

4. **Ask what will happen before you attend.**  
   Ask where you should go, who will meet you, whether this is an office appointment or a mortuary visit, whether you will be asked to sign anything, and what will happen first. Ask staff to brief you privately about what you may see before you decide whether to continue.

5. **Set safe boundaries for attending.**  
   If you are too distressed to travel safely, say so and ask whether the appointment can be later, whether someone else can attend, or whether staff can explain another option. If you do attend, take water, tissues, your phone, and a support person if allowed.

6. **Ask for support on-site.**  
   Ask for a quiet room, a staff escort between departments, and chaplaincy or spiritual care if you would find that helpful. Chaplaincy support is usually for people of any faith or none.

7. **Use the hospital concerns route if you feel pressured or unclear.**  
   If you are in England, ask for PALS. Elsewhere in the UK, ask the switchboard for the hospital’s patient advice, liaison, complaints, or concerns service. Say: “I am distressed and I need someone to help me understand what is being asked before I attend.”

8. **Keep one simple note.**  
   Write down: who called, who is leading the process, what identification means in this case, whether you need to attend, and the next agreed time or contact point.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today about the funeral, probate, bank accounts, belongings, or telling everyone.
- You do not usually need to register the death before this immediate identification question is clarified; registration depends on the medical examiner, coroner, Procurator Fiscal, or local paperwork route.
- You can send one holding message to others: “I’m dealing with the hospital now and will update you when I can.”
- You can deal with belongings, certificates, Tell Us Once, benefits, and estate matters after you know who is handling the death and what the next step is.

## Important reassurance
Being asked to identify a deceased relative can feel unreal, frightening, or too much to process. You are allowed to ask for a slower explanation, a support person, and the least distressing acceptable option.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate hospital or authority request only. Later decisions about registration, coroner or Procurator Fiscal processes, funeral arrangements, documents, money, and the estate may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes vary across the UK and by the circumstances of the death, so the hospital, coroner’s office, police, Procurator Fiscal, registrar, or funeral director involved can tell you what is required in your situation.
