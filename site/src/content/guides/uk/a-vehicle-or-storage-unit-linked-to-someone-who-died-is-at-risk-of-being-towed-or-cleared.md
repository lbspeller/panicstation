# What to do if… a vehicle or storage unit linked to someone who died is at risk of being towed or cleared (UK)

## Short answer
Ask the person or organisation controlling the vehicle or storage unit to pause removal, sale, disposal, or clearance in writing while you gather proof of authority. Your immediate aim is a written hold, a named contact, and a clear list of what they need from you next.

## Do not do these things
- Do not move the vehicle, empty the unit, or remove belongings unless you are authorised and it is safe and lawful to do so.
- Do not ignore notices saying “final notice”, “sale”, “disposal”, “clearance”, “removal”, or “collection deadline”.
- Do not assume a private parking operator can lawfully clamp or tow on private land in England and Wales; ask who has lawful authority, but still treat the notice as urgent.
- Do not pay by cash, personal bank transfer, or a link in a message unless you have verified the organisation and have an invoice or receipt.
- Do not throw away keys, letters, V5C documents, insurance papers, access cards, plates, or photos.

## What to do now
1. Work out who controls the deadline.
   - For a vehicle, look for the notice issuer, council, police reference, highways contact, managing agent, landowner, parking operator, tow company, pound, or tax enforcement contact.
   - For a storage unit, contact the storage facility manager or accounts team.
   - Write down the vehicle registration, unit number, notice reference, address, deadline, and the name of the person you spoke to.

2. Ask for a written hold today.
   Say that the person linked to the vehicle or unit has died, the estate is trying to prevent loss of property, and you need a short written hold while authority is checked. Ask them to email:
   - the exact deadline;
   - what action will stop removal, sale, disposal, or clearance;
   - what documents they need;
   - whether fees are increasing daily;
   - the safest official payment route if payment is needed.

3. Get a small proof pack ready.
   Prepare:
   - your ID;
   - the death certificate, if you have it;
   - grant of probate or letters of administration, if already available;
   - a short signed note or email from the executor or administrator if you are helping them;
   - the vehicle registration, V5C details if found, storage unit number, account email, phone number, and address.
   DVLA does not need a death certificate for its bereavement notification, but councils, pounds, landowners, or storage companies may ask what evidence they can accept temporarily.

4. If the vehicle has already been removed, find where it is before arguing about the reason.
   Use the notice, the local council, police 101, or the relevant vehicle release route to find the pound or enforcement contractor. Ask what is needed to place a hold, what fees are accruing, and what documents are needed for release to an executor, administrator, or authorised helper.

5. If the vehicle is still on private land, ask for a short written extension.
   Contact the landowner, managing agent, site manager, or parking operator. Ask them not to move, dispose of, or add avoidable charges while the estate arranges lawful collection. In England and Wales, private land clamping and towing without lawful authority is generally prohibited, but rules and enforcement routes can differ, so keep the request practical and in writing.

6. If the issue is a storage unit, ask for the contract process in writing.
   Ask the facility to email:
   - the arrears or fees balance;
   - the sale, disposal, or clearance process;
   - the notice dates and how notices were sent;
   - what they need before an executor, administrator, or authorised helper can access the unit;
   - whether a partial payment, full payment, or signed undertaking would pause action.

7. If authorised access is allowed, protect irreplaceable items first.
   Before removing anything, take clear photos of the vehicle or unit as found. Prioritise ID documents, wills, legal papers, keys, access cards, insurance letters, bank letters, devices, photos, sentimental items, and paperwork proving ownership or account details. Make a short list of what was removed and where it is being kept.

8. Start the DVLA bereavement step once the immediate hold request is under way.
   If Tell Us Once is available in the local area, it can notify DVLA that the person has died. You still need to follow the separate DVLA steps if the vehicle is being kept, sold, registered off road, taxed, or dealt with when the V5C is missing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who inherits the vehicle or the unit contents.
- You do not need a full inventory now; photos and a short list are enough for the immediate pause.
- You do not need to finish probate today before asking for a temporary hold.
- You do not need to settle every estate question before asking what stops towing, sale, disposal, or clearance.

## Important reassurance
This is a common kind of urgent admin shock after a death. A written hold, a named contact, and a short proof pack are sensible first steps even if the wider estate paperwork is not ready.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent immediate towing, sale, disposal, or clearance and to buy time. Later decisions about ownership, contracts, fees, probate, disputes, or transfer may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, probate, or professional advice. Rules and release processes can vary by UK nation, council, site, contract, and enforcement route, so check the notice, the contract, and the organisation controlling the deadline.
