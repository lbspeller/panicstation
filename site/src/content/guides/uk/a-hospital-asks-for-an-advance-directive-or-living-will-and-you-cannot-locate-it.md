# What to do if… a hospital asks for an advance directive or living will and you cannot locate it (UK)

## Short answer
Ask the hospital to continue treating the patient in their best interests while you perform a focused search. Identify immediately if a Health and Welfare Power of Attorney exists, as this provides legal authority to make decisions.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t guess or summarize the contents of the missing document from memory if you are uncertain.
- Don’t assume the hospital has a central digital copy; most Advance Decisions (ADRTs) are kept as physical papers by the patient or GP.
- Don’t delay life-saving or urgent treatment while searching unless the medical team specifically advises it is safe to do so.
- Don’t present unverified or partial screenshots of documents as legally binding evidence.
- Don’t feel pressured to make immediate long-term decisions without the paperwork; focus only on the current emergency.

## What to do now
1. **Ask the clinical team for a specific deadline for the next major decision.**  
   **Boldly confirm with the doctor what will happen if the document is not found within that timeframe.**  
   - Request that the patient is treated under "best interests" (England, Wales, and NI) or "principles of the Act" (Scotland) in the interim.  
   - Ask for a written note in the medical records stating that a document is reported to exist but is currently missing.

2. **Verify which specific document the hospital is requesting.**  
   **Ask if they need an Advance Decision to Refuse Treatment (ADRT), a Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA), or a ReSPECT form.**  
   - In England and Wales, an ADRT is a legally binding refusal of specific treatments.  
   - In Scotland, this is typically called an Advance Directive.  
   - In Northern Ireland, there is no statutory Health and Welfare Power of Attorney; decisions are made by clinicians in consultation with next of kin.

3. **Check for a digital access code if the patient is in England or Wales.**  
   **If an LPA was registered on or after 1 January 2016, look for a 13-character access code starting with V.**  
   - This code allows the hospital to view a summary of the Power of Attorney online immediately.  
   - Search the patient’s email or recent letters from the Office of the Public Guardian for an "activation key" or "access code".

4. **Perform a high-speed search of the most likely physical locations.**  
   **Focus your search on the patient’s bedside, wallet, fridge (for "Message in a Bottle" kits), and the "Notes" or "Files" app on their phone.**  
   - Check the patient’s handbag or wallet for a small card indicating they have an ADRT or LPA.  
   - Look for a clearly marked folder in their home office or bedside drawer labeled "Life Documents" or "Medical".

5. **Contact the patient's GP surgery and ask for an urgent scan of their records.**  
   **Ask the surgery specifically if an ADRT, Advance Directive, or ReSPECT form has been uploaded to the patient's electronic summary care record.**  
   - If found, ask the surgery to email or fax it directly to the hospital ward using a secure NHS route.  
   - If the surgery is closed, check if the patient has a palliative care folder or "Yellow Folder" at home.

6. **Submit an urgent search request to the relevant public registry if the existence of a Power of Attorney is uncertain.**  
   **In England and Wales, use the "Urgent Enquiry" route with the Office of the Public Guardian to check if a deputy or attorney is appointed.**  
   - In Scotland, contact the Office of the Public Guardian (Scotland) and request an urgent register search.  
   - Note that these offices usually operate during standard business hours; clinicians will proceed with best-interests decisions if no response is received.

7. **Clarify who is the designated "next of kin" or "relevant person" to consult.**  
   **Provide the hospital with a single point of contact for all discussions to prevent conflicting information.**  
   - If the patient has no family or friends, ask the hospital to appoint an Independent Mental Capacity Advocate (IMCA) for serious medical decisions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find the original physical "Will" (for property/inheritance) today.
- You do not need to prove the patient's entire life history or every stated preference.
- You do not need to resolve disagreements between family members about long-term care plans right now.
- You do not need to apply for a court order in the first few hours of a medical crisis.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for these documents to be missing during an emergency admission. Medical teams are trained to act in the patient's best interests when wishes are unclear, and they will prioritize safety while you look for the paperwork. Hesitation or confusion in this moment is a normal response to a high-pressure situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. It does not replace legal advice or the professional judgment of the clinical team. Decisions involving the withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment are subject to strict legal and clinical protocols that continue even if paperwork is missing.

## Important note
Legal frameworks for medical decision-making differ significantly between England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. This information is for general guidance and may not cover every specific local trust policy or legal nuance.
