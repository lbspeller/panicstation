# What to do if… you are a friend of someone who has died with no known family and you are being pressured to take responsibility (UK)

## Short answer
Keep your role narrow: share useful information, but do not sign or agree to be financially responsible, arrange a funeral, clear a home, or deal with the estate unless you have decided to take that role.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign anything saying you are the funeral applicant, funeral arranger, executor, administrator, or “person responsible” unless you understand and accept what that means.
- Do not pay funeral costs, rent arrears, debts, storage fees, or cleaning costs from your own money “for now”.
- Do not remove valuables, documents, money, cards, keys, or belongings unless the professional handling the case explicitly asks you to do a limited task.
- Do not promise a funeral date or book a funeral director if a coroner, the Procurator Fiscal, hospital, or registrar has not confirmed the body can be released for burial or cremation.
- Do not call yourself “next of kin”, “executor”, or “administrator” unless that is formally correct.

## What to do now
1. **Find out who is handling the death right now.**  
   Ask whether the death is being handled by a coroner in England, Wales or Northern Ireland, the Procurator Fiscal in Scotland, the police, a hospital bereavement or medical examiner office, a care home manager, or a registrar. Get the name, phone number, email address, and any case or reference number.

2. **Set one clear boundary and repeat it.**  
   Say: “I’m a friend. I can share information and help contact people, but I’m not agreeing to pay, arrange the funeral, clear the home, or act as executor or administrator.”

3. **If the death has not been formally confirmed, treat it as an emergency.**  
   If you have just found the person and there is any doubt about whether they have died, call 999. If police, ambulance staff, hospital staff, a doctor, or care staff are already dealing with it, ask them who you should speak to next.

4. **Make only urgent safety checks at the home.**  
   If you have access and it is safe to do so, lock doors and windows when you leave. If there are pets needing care, food left cooking, water leaks, or obvious hazards, tell the police, coroner’s office, Procurator Fiscal contact, hospital, care home, landlord, or council and ask what they want you to do. Write down only what you did, with the date and time.

5. **Give officials a short information note.**  
   Write down what you know: full name, address, date of birth if known, GP surgery, workplace, landlord or housing provider, known friends, possible relatives, pets, and any known funeral wishes. Send it to the professional handling the case rather than taking control of documents or belongings yourself.

6. **Ask what happens if nobody can arrange the funeral.**  
   In England and Wales, a local authority can have a duty to arrange burial or cremation where a person died or was found dead in its area and it appears no one else will do it. In Scotland or Northern Ireland, ask the hospital, Procurator Fiscal, coroner, registrar, or local council what the local route is if there is no family and no one is willing or able to arrange the funeral.

7. **If you are asked to register the death, check before agreeing.**  
   Ask the registrar: “Am I eligible to register this death as a friend in this part of the UK, and does doing so make me responsible for arranging or paying for the funeral?” If you do register, keep your wording clear: you are acting only as the informant for registration, not accepting wider responsibility.

8. **If property, money, or a will comes up, do not take possession of it.**  
   Tell the official handling the case if you know where documents, keys, bank cards, a will, or valuables may be, but avoid searching, sorting, or removing items unless you are specifically asked to do one limited task. If there appears to be no will and no known family, the estate may need to be treated as unclaimed or passed to the appropriate estate process.

9. **If a funeral director, landlord, creditor, or council officer pressures you, pause.**  
   Say: “I am not the executor, administrator, next of kin, or funeral applicant. Please put in writing what you are asking me to agree to, and I will check it before responding.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether you will arrange a funeral, register the death, clear the home, contact every organisation, or deal with belongings.
- You do not need to find all relatives today; give the names and clues you know to the official handling the case.
- You do not need to negotiate with landlords, banks, utility companies, creditors, or funeral directors beyond passing on the correct official contact.

## Important reassurance
You can still help without taking over. Giving accurate information, naming possible contacts, flagging pets or property risks, and refusing unclear responsibility is a reasonable and careful way to respond.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the situation, avoid accidental responsibility, and get the case into the right official process. Later decisions about a funeral, registration, probate, intestacy, property, or an unclaimed estate may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, bereavement, funeral, or other professional advice. Processes vary across England and Wales, Scotland, Northern Ireland, local authorities, hospitals, registrars, coroners, and the Procurator Fiscal, so ask the professional handling the case to confirm in writing what you are and are not agreeing to.
