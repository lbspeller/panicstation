# What to do if… you are told identification may require a recent photo or dental information after a death (UK)

## Short answer
Provide the specific contact details of current and past dentists to the Coroner’s officer or Family Liaison Officer (FLO) so they can obtain official records for scientific comparison. Do not attempt to source or interpret clinical dental records yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Don't post a public appeal for information or photos on social media unless specifically instructed by the police, as this can compromise the investigation.
- Don't feel you have failed if you cannot find dental details immediately; the authorities have systems to trace records through the NHS and the General Dental Council.
- Don't attempt to visit a dental practice in person to "collect" records; the Coroner has a statutory right to request these directly, and original records are required for legal validity.
- Don't assume that the request for dental records implies a suspicious death; scientific identification is a standard procedure when visual identification is not considered reliable or appropriate.
- Don't send original hard-copy photos via post if you can provide high-quality digital scans, to avoid the risk of losing irreplaceable items.

## What to do now
1. **Identify your single point of contact and case reference.**
   **Ask if the person contacting you is the Coroner’s Officer or a Police Family Liaison Officer (FLO).** 
   - Write down their full name, direct telephone extension, and the "Coroner's Case Reference Number."
   - All subsequent communications should be directed to this specific individual.

2. **Locate dental and orthodontist contact information.**
   **Search for any paperwork, appointment cards, or emails from dental practices used by the deceased.**
   - Include the practice name, town, and if possible, the name of the specific dentist.
   - If they had dental implants or orthodontic work (braces), identify those specialist clinics as these provide high-quality "Primary Identification" data.

3. **Select 2 to 5 clear, recent photographs.**
   **Choose images where the person’s face is clearly visible and well-lit.**
   - Prioritise photos where the person is smiling and teeth are visible, even if the photo is less "formal."
   - Note the approximate date each photo was taken.
   - Avoid using photos with heavy digital filters or those where other people obscure the face.

4. **Ask for the "Secure Evidence Transfer" method.**
   **Request the official police or Coroner’s portal link or a secure government email address to send the files.**
   - Do not send sensitive personal data via standard SMS or unverified social media messaging apps.
   - If using email, ask them to confirm receipt of the attachments immediately.

5. **Confirm the "No-Viewing" status if applicable.**
   **Ask if the current request means that a visual identification (viewing the body) is currently not possible or not recommended.**
   - This allows you to prepare yourself and other family members for the fact that a traditional viewing may be delayed or unavailable for safety or forensic reasons.

6. **Clarify the timeline for the "Identification Commission."**
   **Ask when the Forensic Odontologist (the dental identification specialist) is expected to review the evidence.**
   - This helps manage expectations, as scientific comparison can take several days depending on the availability of the specialist and the original records.

## What can wait
- You do not need to begin funeral arrangements yet; the Coroner cannot release the body (issue "Form 6" or a "Coroner’s Burial/Cremation Order") until identification is legally confirmed.
- You do not need to provide a complete medical history or DNA samples unless the dental comparison proves inconclusive.
- You do not need to notify wider family about the "method" of identification if you find it distressing; you can simply state that formal procedures are ongoing.

## Important reassurance
In the UK, the Coroner has a legal duty to ensure identification is 100% accurate before a death is registered. Using "Primary Identification" methods like dental records or DNA is the gold standard of care. It is an act of professional diligence intended to prevent any possibility of misidentification, providing the family with absolute certainty in the long term.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for families asked to assist with forensic identification. It does not cover the legalities of the Inquest process or the technical details of the post-mortem examination.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Under the Coroners and Justice Act 2009 and the Death Certification Reforms (September 2024), the Coroner has statutory authority to investigate and identify the deceased. Procedures may vary slightly between England and Wales, Scotland (Procurator Fiscal), and Northern Ireland.
