# What to do if… a public electric vehicle charger will not start and your battery is too low to reach another station (UK)

## Short answer
Get the car and yourself into the safest available position, then call the helpline number printed on the charger and say your battery may be too low to reach another charger. Ask the operator to remotely reset or start the unit, confirm which stall is working, or tell you the nearest realistic alternative.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep re-trying the same payment or app start over and over; it may create repeated pending card holds or stuck app sessions.
- Don’t force the connector, cable, flap, or latch if it will not lock or release.
- Don’t drive off to another charger unless you have checked it is available, compatible, reachable, and safe to reach with your remaining battery.
- Don’t leave a motorway live lane, hard shoulder, or unsafe car park position untreated while you troubleshoot the charger.
- Don’t use improvised cabling, extension leads, unsafe adapters, or help from someone who is not equipped to recover an EV safely.
- Don’t spend your remaining phone battery on repeated app refreshes before calling the charger helpline or roadside assistance.

## What to do now
1. **Make the situation safe before troubleshooting.** Put on hazard lights if your position could surprise other drivers. If you are on a motorway, in a live lane, or cannot get to a safer place, call 999 and ask for the police. If you are stopped in a motorway emergency area, lay-by, or hard shoulder, use the emergency phone if provided or call National Highways on 0300 123 5000, then contact breakdown recovery.
2. **Stay in the charging bay if moving could strand you.** If you are already beside the charger and the cable can reach, stay there while you call support unless the position is unsafe. Move to a nearby parking space only if it is safe, your battery allows it, and you are not giving up the only realistic chance to charge.
3. **Check the simple car-side causes once.**
   - Make sure the connector is fully seated and the plug is not being pulled at an angle.
   - Check whether the car is set to scheduled charging, delayed charging, or a charge limit that stops an immediate charge.
   - Select “charge now” or the nearest equivalent in the car if available.
4. **Do one clean restart, then stop looping.**
   - If the charger, app, or card shows an active session, end or cancel it first.
   - Unplug only if the car and charger allow it.
   - Lock and unlock the car, wait a short moment, then try one fresh start.
5. **Try one different start method or connector if available.**
   - If the app or RFID card failed, try contactless card if the unit offers it.
   - If contactless failed, try the app or RFID card if you already have access.
   - If there are several stalls, check the screen or app status and move to a different stall only if you can do so safely.
6. **Call the operator helpline printed on the charger.** Most public charge points should display a free 24-hour staffed helpline. Give the operator:
   - the charge point ID or unit number
   - the site name and exact location
   - the error message or screen status
   - the connector type you are using
   - your battery percentage, displayed range, and whether you can safely reach another charger
7. **Ask direct, practical questions.**
   - “Can you remotely reset or start this unit?”
   - “Is this connector showing as available at your end?”
   - “Which stall at this site is working right now?”
   - “What is the nearest charger you can confirm is working and compatible with my car?”
   - “Should I stay connected while you reset it?”
8. **Reduce avoidable drain while you wait.** Keep enough phone battery for calls and location sharing. Turn off heated seats, rear demist, high cabin heat, and other obvious drains if you can do so without making yourself unsafe or unwell.
9. **Arrange roadside help early if the operator cannot get you charging.** Call your breakdown provider, vehicle roadside assistance, lease provider, or insurer’s assistance line and say: “This is an EV with insufficient range to reach a charger after a failed public charge session.” Tell them your exact location, battery percentage, whether the car can still move, and whether you are near fast traffic.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide who is at fault right now.
- You do not need to make a complaint, chase a refund, or resolve a pending card hold while you are still at risk of being stranded.
- You do not need to compare every nearby charger; you only need one confirmed, compatible, reachable option.
- You do not need to decide whether to change charging networks, apps, cars, or breakdown cover today.

## Important reassurance
A failed start does not mean you have done anything wrong. Public chargers can fail because of a stuck session, payment problem, connector issue, car setting, or network fault, and the next useful step is to involve the operator or roadside assistance before the battery gets critically low.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation, keep you safe, and help you get charging or recovered. Later decisions about refunds, complaints, card authorisations, charger reliability reports, or vehicle checks may need specialist help once you are safe and mobile.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, mechanical, safety, or professional advice. Follow emergency-service, National Highways, charger-operator, vehicle-manufacturer, and recovery-provider instructions where they apply.
