# What to do if… you are pressured to book a funeral date and key relatives cannot attend (UK)

## Short answer
You usually do not have to choose a funeral date on the spot. Ask for a clear pause, for example until tomorrow, while you check what paperwork or authorisation is needed and when the key relatives can realistically attend.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confirm a date just to end a pressured call.
- Don’t assume “it has to be this week” unless someone explains what document, authorisation, storage issue, or venue limit makes that true.
- Don’t let a group chat make the decision in real time; pause the chat and collect facts first.
- Don’t feel you must explain private family circumstances in detail; “key relatives cannot attend that date” is enough.
- Don’t pay a deposit or sign paperwork while you feel cornered; ask for the options and costs in writing.

## What to do now
1. Ask the question that slows the pressure down:
   “What exactly means the date has to be chosen today: paperwork, authorisation, storage, or venue availability? Please put the available dates, costs, and any deadline in writing.”

2. Check the earliest possible funeral date before you discuss preferences. Ask whether the death has been registered and what certificate or authorisation is still needed before a burial or cremation can take place. In England and Wales, this may include the certificate for burial or cremation, often called the green form. In Scotland or Northern Ireland, ask the registrar or funeral director what equivalent authorisation is needed.

3. If the death has been referred to a coroner, or in Scotland to the procurator fiscal, ask who will confirm when arrangements can proceed. Do not treat a suggested date as final until you know whether that confirmation is still outstanding.

4. Ask for a provisional hold, not a final booking. Say: “Can you hold one or two possible slots while I check travel for the key relatives?” If they cannot hold slots, ask them to note that you are not confirming a date today.

5. Make a short key-relatives list. Write down the 2–5 people whose attendance matters most, where they are travelling from, and the earliest date they can realistically attend. Use that list to avoid being pulled into every wider family preference at once.

6. Offer one practical option that buys time. Ask about a later funeral date, a smaller committal with a memorial later, or, if it fits the person’s wishes and the family’s needs, direct cremation with a separate memorial later.

7. Put one person in charge of calls for the next day. That person should speak to the funeral director or registrar first, and the venue, crematorium, or burial authority if needed, then update everyone else with the same written information.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide readings, music, flowers, catering, order of service, coffin details, who speaks, livestreaming, or the wake location now.
- You do not need to solve every family disagreement today.
- You do not need to plan travel for every mourner before protecting a workable date for the key relatives.
- You do not need to choose the perfect format now; committal and memorial arrangements can sometimes be separated.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to ask for time when a funeral date would exclude key relatives. A short pause to check paperwork, travel, and written options is not being difficult; it is a way to make the next decision more calmly.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for slowing pressure and buying time. Later decisions about costs, legal responsibility, disputes, religious requirements, burial rights, or unusual circumstances may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes can differ across England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, and timing can change depending on the cause of death, registration, and any required authorisations.
