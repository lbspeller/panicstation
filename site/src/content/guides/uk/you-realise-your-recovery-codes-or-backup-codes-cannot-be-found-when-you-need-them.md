# What to do if… you realise your recovery codes or backup codes cannot be found when you need them (UK)

## Short answer
Attempt to access your security settings through a device where you are already signed in to generate new codes, or use the service provider's official account recovery process without engaging with third-party "unlocking" services.

## Do not do these things
- Do not enter your credentials into any link sent via unsolicited email, text, or social media message.
- Do not pay individuals or "recovery services" on platforms like WhatsApp or Telegram who claim they can bypass 2FA.
- Do not repeatedly attempt incorrect codes as this may trigger a permanent security lockout or account freeze.
- Do not disable existing security features if you still have partial access, as this makes the account vulnerable to immediate takeover.
- Do not provide scans of your passport or ID to any site that is not the provider's verified official domain.

## What to do now
1. **Verify that you are on the official service provider website before taking further action.** If you reached the sign-in page via a link, close the browser tab and manually type the website address (e.g., accounts.google.com or login.live.com) into the address bar.
2. **Search your digital and physical storage for common file names or phrases associated with backup codes.**
   - Check for files named "Backup Codes," "Recovery," "2FA," or "Security" in your computer's Downloads and Documents folders.
   - Search your email inbox for the phrase "Two-Step Verification" or "Security Codes" from the date you originally enabled the feature.
   - Look for "Secure Notes" or "Attachments" within your password manager.
   - Check physical notebooks, safes, or printed documents where you might have stored sensitive credentials.
3. **Check every device where you might still be signed in to the account.**
   - Open the app or website on your smartphone, tablet, or a secondary computer.
   - If you have an active session, navigate immediately to the **Security** or **Sign-in** settings.
   - Look for the option to **Generate New Backup Codes** or **View Recovery Codes**.
   - If you generate new codes, the previous (missing) set will be automatically invalidated.
4. **Use alternative verification methods if they were configured during setup.** Look for links on the sign-in screen such as "Try another way," "Use a trusted device," or "Send a code to my recovery email."
5. **Begin the provider's official Account Recovery process if you are completely locked out.**
   - Follow the "Forgot Password" or "Account Recovery" prompts on the official site.
   - Provide accurate historical information such as previous passwords, account creation dates, or billing information for paid subscriptions.
   - Avoid guessing; if you are unsure of an answer, try to provide the most likely information from a network or device you frequently use.
6. **Report suspicious activity immediately if you believe your codes were stolen or your account is being accessed by others.**
   - Forward suspicious emails to the Suspicious Email Reporting Service (SERS) at **report@phishing.gov.uk**.
   - Forward suspicious text messages to **7726**.
   - If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland and have lost money, report it to **Report Fraud** via `reportfraud.police.uk` or call **0300 123 2040**.
   - If you are in Scotland, report the incident to **Police Scotland** using their online reporting form or by calling **101**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a new security hardware key or a different 2FA method until your current access is restored.
- You do not need to notify all your contacts unless you have confirmed that your account is sending out spam or phishing messages.
- You do not need to delete the affected account immediately; focus on recovery and securing the associated recovery email first.

## Important reassurance
Losing recovery codes is a frequent occurrence and does not automatically mean your account is lost forever. Most major providers have established protocols for identity verification that do not rely solely on backup codes, provided you can verify your identity through other means or trusted devices.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for users who have lost access to secondary authentication codes. It does not cover technical "cracking" of accounts or long-term cybersecurity architecture.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional cybersecurity advice. If you suspect your financial information has been compromised along with your digital account, contact your bank immediately using the number on the back of your card.
