# What to do if… a service says your account is under review and you fear losing access to stored data (UK)

## Short answer
Use one trusted device, stay calm, and immediately try the service’s official export, download, review, or appeal route while you are still logged in.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t repeatedly try passwords, codes, resets, or verification steps in a panic; repeated attempts can make access harder.
- Don’t delete the account, start a new account to replace it, or remove recovery details to “fix” the review.
- Don’t use strangers, paid “recovery” services, or people offering account help through messages.
- Don’t factory-reset devices, clear app data, or wipe local folders until you have checked whether copies of the files are stored there.
- Don’t open several conflicting support cases if one formal review or appeal route is available.

## What to do now
1. **Keep any working session open.**
   - Use one trusted device and network that you have used with the service before.
   - If you are already logged in, stay logged in and do not sign out just to test whether you can get back in.

2. **Capture what the service is telling you.**
   - Screenshot the review message, warning banner, email, notification, and any error shown when you try to export or download.
   - Save the date, time, account email, service name, and any case or reference number in one note.

3. **Try the official data export or download route.**
   - Look in account settings, privacy settings, data settings, storage settings, or help pages for words such as “download your data”, “export”, “archive”, “takeout”, or “request a copy”.
   - If a full export is not available, download the most important files, photos, documents, messages, or folders first.
   - If the export fails, screenshot the error and record the time.

4. **Check for local copies before changing devices.**
   - Look for synced folders on your computer, phone photo libraries, offline files, cached documents, email apps, or backup folders linked to the service.
   - Copy the most important local files to a safe place you control, such as an external drive or another trusted cloud account.
   - Do not rely on a sync folder as a backup if it may mirror deletion or restriction from the service.

5. **Use the service’s review or appeal route once, clearly.**
   - Submit a short message saying your account is under review, you need access to stored data, and you are willing to verify your identity through the official process.
   - Include the case number, account email, approximate dates, and screenshots if the form allows attachments.
   - Keep a copy of the message before submitting it.

6. **Avoid unnecessary security changes, but act if compromise is possible.**
   - If the service prompts you to secure the account, follow the official prompts.
   - If you suspect someone else accessed the account, change the password once to a strong, unique password if the service lets you do that safely.
   - Check that your recovery email, recovery phone, and two-step verification methods are still yours. Add or confirm details if allowed, but do not remove working recovery options during the review.

7. **If the service will not restore access, make a focused UK data-rights request.**
   - You can make a Subject Access Request for copies of your personal data held by the service.
   - If relevant, you can also ask for data portability for personal data you provided, where the right applies and the data is held electronically.
   - Be specific about what you need, such as account profile data, uploaded files connected to you, messages you sent, login history, or exportable content.
   - These rights may not cover every stored file, shared item, third-party content, or data affected by exemptions. If the organisation mishandles your request, you can complain to the ICO after giving the organisation a chance to respond.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to leave the service permanently.
- You do not need to move every account, file, or contact today.
- You do not need to write a long defence of yourself; a short, factual record is enough for first contact.
- You do not need to decide on legal action now.

## Important reassurance
An account review can feel like everything is about to disappear, but the safest first steps are practical: keep any access you still have, copy what matters most, and create one clear support trail.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce the chance of data loss and preserve a record. Later decisions may need specialist technical help, legal advice, or direct support from the service depending on what the account contains.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, technical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Services have different rules, and UK data rights usually apply to personal data rather than every file or item stored in an account.
