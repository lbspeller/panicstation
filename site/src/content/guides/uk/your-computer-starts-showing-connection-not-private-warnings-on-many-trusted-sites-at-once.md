# What to do if… your computer starts showing “connection not private” warnings on many trusted sites at once (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the repair process immediately and copy your most important files to an external device before attempting any automated fixes. Repairs often involve overwriting or "cleaning" corrupted sectors, which can permanently delete the data you are trying to save.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click “Repair,” “Fix,” or “Scan and Repair” until a backup is secured.
- Don’t restart the computer repeatedly if it fails to boot; every boot cycle stresses the drive.
- Don’t run "defragmentation" or "disk cleanup" tools.
- Don’t move or rename large batches of files.
- Don’t download or install new software, as this may overwrite the very data you need to rescue.
- Don’t ignore physical sounds like clicking, grinding, or whirring; if you hear these, turn the device off immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Minimize drive activity immediately.** Close all non-essential applications and stop any background downloads or updates.
2. **Dismiss the repair prompt safely.** Select "Cancel," "Skip," or "Ask me later" to return to the desktop.
3. **Connect a separate, healthy destination drive.** Use an external USB hard drive, a high-capacity flash drive, or cloud storage if the files are small and the internet connection is stable.
4. **Copy your most critical, irreplaceable files first.** Transfer data in small batches rather than one giant folder to reduce the risk of a total hang if a specific file is unreadable.
   - Priority 1: Documents, spreadsheets, and work projects.
   - Priority 2: Personal photos and videos.
   - Priority 3: Browser bookmarks or specific configuration files.
5. **Skip files that cause errors.** If the computer reports it cannot read a specific file, skip it and move to the next; do not let the process stall on a single dead sector.
6. **Use "Recovery Mode" for Mac users if the system won't boot.** Restart and hold Command-R (Intel) or the Power button (Silicon) to access Disk Utility, but use "Share Disk" or "Target Disk Mode" to move files to another Mac before running First Aid.
7. **Verify your data on the new device.** Open a few rescued files on a different computer to ensure the copy was successful.
8. **Execute the repair once data is safe.** Run `chkdsk /f` on Windows or "First Aid" on macOS.
   - If the tool reports "Fixed," monitor the drive closely for 48 hours.
   - If the tool fails or the errors return, the hardware is likely failing and must be replaced.
9. **Assert your UK consumer rights if the device is faulty.** If the computer or drive was purchased within the last six months, the Consumer Rights Act 2015 assumes the fault was present at the time of sale unless the retailer can prove otherwise. 
   - Within 30 days: You have a legal right to a full refund.
   - After 30 days (up to 6 months): You are entitled to one repair or replacement at the retailer's expense.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide which replacement hardware to buy right now.
- You do not need to organize or delete files on the failing drive.
- You do not need to determine if the error is "logical" or "physical" until your data is off the device.

## Important reassurance
Disk warnings are an early alert system designed to prevent total loss. If you can still see your files, you are in a strong position to save them. Feeling overwhelmed by technical prompts is a standard reaction; the most important step is simply creating a second copy of your work.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization only. Professional data recovery services may be required if the drive becomes completely unreadable or makes mechanical noises.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace professional IT support. If the data is business-critical, encrypted, or highly sensitive, seek professional recovery services before attempting DIY repairs. Under UK law, your contract is with the retailer who sold you the device, not the manufacturer; contact the retailer first for warranty or statutory claims.
