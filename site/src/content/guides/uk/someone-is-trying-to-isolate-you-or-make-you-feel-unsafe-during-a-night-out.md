# What to do if… someone is trying to isolate you or make you feel unsafe during a night out (UK)

## Short answer
Move back into a staffed, busy area and ask venue staff to help you rejoin your group or leave safely. If you think you are in immediate danger, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go outside, to a quieter spot, or to another venue, car, or taxi with them just to end the awkwardness.
- Do not let them hold your phone, bag, or coat, or take you somewhere to charge your phone.
- Do not argue it out somewhere private, such as a toilet corridor, car park, alley, or smoking area. Keep it public and staffed.
- Do not accept a lift home from them or someone they call for you.
- Do not downplay it because you worry about seeming dramatic. Act on the unsafe feeling.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance immediately without negotiating.** Step to the bar, security, a door staff member, or a busy table. If you can, put a solid barrier, such as a bar or counter, between you and them.
2. **Use venue staff as your shield.** Say clearly: “I don’t feel safe. Please help me get back to my friends or help me leave.”
   - If the venue uses a code-phrase scheme, you can try **“Ask for Angela”** to discreetly signal that you need help.
3. **Get reconnected fast.** Ask staff to help you call or message your friends or coworkers. Ask them to come to you at a specific, visible place, such as the main bar or entrance. Do not go searching alone.
4. **Send a short message if your phone is in your hand.** For example: “I feel unsafe. Come to the bar now.” Share your live location with a trusted person if that is quick and safe.
5. **If you need to leave, leave with a buffer.** Ask staff or security to:
   - keep the person away from you
   - help you wait inside until a friend or trusted person arrives
   - escort you to the toilets, exit, or official taxi pickup point
6. **Call police if danger feels immediate.**
   - Call **999** if you believe you are in immediate danger or a crime is happening now.
   - If you cannot safely speak on a mobile 999 call, stay on the line and press **55**, or tap as prompted, to confirm it is a genuine emergency.
7. **If you feel suddenly unwell or more drunk than you should be.** Tell staff and stay with trusted people.
   - If symptoms feel serious or are getting worse, call **999**.
   - If it is not an emergency but you need urgent medical advice, ask staff to help you contact urgent medical help, such as **111** where it is available.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to confront them, report it, or prove anything.
- You do not need to explain yourself in detail to friends or staff. “I don’t feel safe” is enough.
- You can decide later whether to write down a description, time, and location while it is still fresh.

## Important reassurance
Someone who is trying to separate you from your group may rely on you feeling embarrassed, confused, or pressured to be polite. Moving toward staff, crowds, and trusted people is a normal, sensible response.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the moment and get you back to safety. Later decisions, such as venue complaints, formal reporting, medical follow-up, or specialist support, can wait until you are safe and with someone you trust.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, prioritise getting to a staffed or public area and contacting emergency services.
