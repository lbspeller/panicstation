# What to do if… an interviewer or recruiter asks sexual or inappropriate personal questions (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself to a calmer, safer pause as soon as you can. You do not have to answer sexual or intimate personal questions to keep a job opportunity open.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel you must “play along” or answer sexual questions to avoid seeming difficult.
- Do not give extra personal details, such as your home address, social media accounts, relationship details, or private contact routes, to smooth things over.
- Do not keep arguing in the moment if you feel pressured or unsafe; your priority is to end the interaction safely.
- Do not delete emails, messages, call logs, meeting links, calendar invites, or screenshots right now.
- Do not post identifying details publicly while you are still shaken; it can escalate contact and may make later choices harder.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safe pause.** If you are in person, end the meeting and move to a public area or somewhere you feel safe. If it is a call or video interview, end the call.
2. **Use one short boundary line if it feels safe to do so.** For example:
   - “I’m not comfortable with that question. It does not seem relevant to the role.”
   - “I’m going to end the interview now.”
3. **Save what already exists.** Keep emails, messages, recruiter profiles, job adverts, interview invites, calendar entries, call logs, and any written sexual or personal questions. If the questions were in chat or email, take screenshots as well.
4. **Write a private note while it is fresh.** Include the date and time, company or agency, names or job titles, where it happened, what was asked, approximate wording, and whether anyone else was present.
5. **Move any further contact into writing.** If they contact you again, use email or another written channel where possible. You can say you only want communication about the role and recruitment process.
6. **Optional: ask the organisation to log it.** If it was an agency recruiter, you can ask for a manager or complaints contact and ask not to be contacted by that recruiter again. If it was a direct employer interview, you can contact HR, the hiring lead, or a general recruitment contact and ask for the concern to be recorded.
7. **If there is immediate danger, threats, stalking, or unwanted sexual touching, prioritise safety.** Call 999 now if you or someone else is in immediate danger. For non-emergency police contact, you can use 101 or the Police.uk contact route. You do not have to decide about a formal report before getting safe.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to make a formal complaint, legal claim, regulator complaint, or police report.
- You do not have to confront the person again to get an apology or prove what happened.
- You do not have to decide right now whether to continue with the application.
- You do not have to write a perfect account today; a short private note is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaken, confused, embarrassed, angry, or unsure is a normal response when someone crosses a boundary in a professional setting. You are allowed to stop the interaction, protect your privacy, and decide later what, if anything, you want to do next.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stabilise, preserve options, and reduce further contact. Later decisions about complaints, employment rights, harassment, discrimination, or safety may need specialist support.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or professional advice. Processes and rights can vary by UK nation and by the details of what happened, so tailored advice can help if you want to take it further.
