# What to do if… you are asked to attend a court hearing remotely for someone in custody tomorrow (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and verify the hearing through an official court contact route, not through the message or link you were sent. Then ask the court whether remote observation is possible, so you reduce the risk of missing a real hearing or following a scam link.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click unknown links, download unfamiliar “court apps”, or enter personal details from a message until you have checked the hearing through an official court contact route.
- Do not share any joining link, dial-in number, meeting ID, or passcode unless the court says you may.
- Do not record, livestream, screenshot, photograph, or retransmit any part of a hearing. This can be a criminal offence and contempt of court.
- Do not contact the judge directly or try to pass messages through informal channels.
- Do not send urgent money, gift cards, crypto, phone credit, or “hearing fees” because someone says it is needed for the hearing.
- Do not assume you are allowed to attend. Some hearings are private, restricted, or only open to certain people.

## What to do now
1. **Get the minimum facts from the person in custody, without promising anything:**
   - their full name, and any other name used in the case
   - their date of birth
   - where they are held, such as a prison or police station
   - their prisoner number or custody reference, if they know it
   - the court name, town or city, date, and time
   - the type of hearing, if they know it
   - whether they have a solicitor, and the solicitor’s firm name if they are comfortable sharing it

2. **Verify the hearing through an official route:**
   - Find the court’s contact details through an official court finder, the named court’s official webpage, or paperwork you already know is genuine.
   - Do not use phone numbers, email addresses, or links from the suspicious message until checked.
   - Ask: “Can you confirm whether there is a hearing tomorrow for [full name and date of birth], and whether remote observation is possible?”

3. **Be clear about the role you are asking for:**
   - If you only want to watch, say you are asking to observe remotely and will not speak.
   - If the person said they want you there for support, ask the court what role, if any, is allowed.
   - Give your full name, email address, phone number if asked, your connection to the person, and any other information the court requests.
   - Keep the request short. The court or judge may allow remote access, ask for extra checks, suggest attending in person, or say the hearing cannot be observed.

4. **Ask exactly how access will be sent:**
   - Ask whether the hearing will be by telephone, Cloud Video Platform, or another approved service.
   - Ask what you should expect to receive, such as an email invite, meeting link, dial-in number, meeting ID, passcode, or joining instructions.
   - Ask when it is likely to arrive, and what to do if it has not arrived shortly before the hearing.

5. **Do a simple tech check tonight:**
   - Use a device you trust and can charge.
   - Check your email, spam folder, microphone, speakers, camera, and internet connection.
   - Choose a quiet, private place and use headphones if you have them.
   - If the court says the hearing uses Cloud Video Platform, follow the official CVP joining guidance and test your camera, microphone, and speakers.
   - If the link or platform does not work, use the technical support details in the joining notice or contact the court office.

6. **Join tomorrow as if you are entering a courtroom:**
   - Be ready at least 20 minutes before the hearing if the joining instructions allow.
   - Use only the official joining details sent or confirmed by the court.
   - Keep your microphone muted unless you are told to speak.
   - Turn your camera off if the court asks observers to do so.
   - Have photo ID nearby in case you are asked to confirm who you are.
   - If you lose connection, try the same official link or dial-in details again, then contact the court office if needed.

7. **If remote access is not arranged in time, use the next safest route:**
   - Ask the court whether you may observe in person instead.
   - Ask whether the hearing is private, restricted, or not available to observers.
   - If you are worried about the person’s immediate welfare in custody, contact the prison, police station, or place of detention through its main published switchboard and ask for the correct welfare or safer-custody route. Keep the message factual and brief.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide legal strategy tonight.
- You do not need to interpret what the hearing means or predict the outcome.
- You do not need to chase transcripts, paperwork, or detailed case records before confirming the hearing and access route.
- You do not need to argue with the person in custody about details you cannot verify. Focus on court name, time, contact route, and access instructions.

## Important reassurance
A last-minute court message can feel urgent, but slowing down is useful. Checking the court details first is a practical way to help without handing control to a scam, a mistaken message, or a rushed decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps for checking whether a court date is real and asking whether you can attend remotely as an observer. Later decisions about the case, bail, remand, sentencing, representation, or what to say to a solicitor may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Court practices vary by court, tribunal, hearing type, and judge. Always follow the court’s instructions, and do not record or share hearing access details.
