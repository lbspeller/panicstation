# What to do if… a bank will not speak with you after a death and a bill is due today (UK)

## Short answer
Deal with the biller first: ask for a same-day bereavement hold, no late fee, and no service action while authority over the bank account is being sorted. Then contact the bank’s bereavement or estate team and ask for a written checklist of what they need before they can speak to you or consider any urgent payment option.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use the deceased person’s card, PIN, online banking, or login to make a payment.
- Do not sign the deceased person’s name on anything.
- Do not pay from your own money unless you are sure you want to and you understand whether you are personally responsible.
- Do not assume you are personally liable for a debt just because a company is chasing payment.
- Do not rely on a phone promise without asking for written confirmation.
- Do not try to solve the whole estate today.

## What to do now
1. Separate the problem into two tasks:
   - Today: get the biller to pause fees, enforcement, or service action.
   - Next: get the bank to confirm who they can speak to and what documents they need.

2. Contact the biller now and ask for a bereavement hold.
   - Say: “There has been a death. I cannot access the payer’s bank account yet. Please place a short bereavement hold so there are no late fees, enforcement steps, or service action today.”
   - Ask for the hold, the new date, and any conditions in writing.

3. If the bill is essential, ask for the biller’s bereavement or vulnerability process.
   - For energy, water, rent, mortgage, council tax, care fees, insurance, or telecoms, ask what they can do today.
   - Ask whether they can pause late fees, delay action, suspend collection contact, or accept a temporary arrangement while authority is sorted.

4. Make a short note before calling the bank again.
   - Deceased person’s full name, date of birth, last address, and date of death.
   - Your name, contact details, and relationship to the person who died.
   - The biller’s name, account number, amount due, and deadline.
   - Any bank reference you have, such as bank name, branch, or last four digits of an account.
   - Whether you have a death certificate, interim death certificate, will, grant of probate, letters of administration, or none of these yet.

5. Call the bank and ask only for the bereavement or estate team.
   - Say: “This is a bereavement case. A time-sensitive bill is due today. I need your written checklist for who you can speak to, what documents you need, and whether any urgent payment route exists.”
   - If the first person cannot help, ask them to transfer you to the bereavement or estate team rather than explaining the whole situation again.

6. State your position carefully.
   - Say whether you are named as an executor, expecting to apply as administrator, a surviving joint account holder, the spouse or civil partner, next of kin, or someone only trying to stop immediate harm.
   - Ask: “What is the minimum you need before you can discuss the next step with me?”

7. Ask whether the bank has any direct-payment option.
   - Say: “Do you have any policy for paying a provider directly from the deceased person’s account, without giving me account access, if I provide the bill or invoice and the documents you require?”
   - Do not assume this is available. If the answer is no, ask what the fastest correct route is.

8. Use the Death Notification Service if there may be more than one bank or building society.
   - It can notify participating financial institutions of the death.
   - It does not replace each institution’s own bereavement process, but it may reduce repeated first notifications.

9. If the bank still will not route you properly, log a complaint today.
   - Ask the bank to register a formal complaint and give you a reference number.
   - Keep it short: “Bereavement case; urgent bill due today; unable to reach bereavement or estate support; need document checklist and urgent-payment options.”
   - Check the Financial Ombudsman Service complaint page for when it can look at a complaint if the bank does not resolve it.

10. Keep a simple record.
   - Write down the date, time, organisation, person or team spoken to, what they said, and any reference number.
   - Save emails, screenshots, bills, and letters in one place.

## What can wait
- You do not need to complete probate today.
- You do not need to close accounts, distribute money, or settle the estate today.
- You do not need to decide today whether every debt is valid or who must pay it.
- You do not need to pay other people’s debts just to stop feeling pressured.
- You can come back to longer-term estate administration after the biller has responded and the bank has given its document checklist.

## Important reassurance
Banks usually limit what they discuss after a death until they can confirm authority. That is upsetting when a deadline is today, but you may still be able to reduce immediate harm by getting the biller to pause action and by forcing the bank request into a clear written checklist.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise a same-day bill deadline and unblock communication. Later decisions about the estate, debts, probate, joint accounts, or personal liability may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Bank and biller policies vary, and urgent payments from a deceased person’s account are not guaranteed. If you are unsure about your liability, do not use the deceased person’s banking access, focus on written confirmation of any hold, and keep a record of what each organisation says.
