# What to do if… you return to your parked vehicle and it looks like someone may have broken in or tampered with it (UK)

## Short answer
Step away from the vehicle to a safer, visible place first. Call 999 if someone may still be nearby, you feel in immediate danger, or a crime is happening now; use 101 or online reporting if it appears to have happened earlier and there is no immediate threat.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t get into the vehicle “just to check” if you feel uneasy or someone could be nearby.
- Don’t confront anyone you suspect has been in or around the vehicle.
- Don’t touch, move, or open anything unfamiliar that looks deliberately placed.
- Don’t start driving if you notice signs the vehicle may be unsafe, such as a strong fuel smell, obvious fluid leaking, damaged wheels, broken glass near pedals, or warning lights you did not have before.
- Don’t tidy up, wipe surfaces, or throw things away if you may report it or make an insurance claim.
- Don’t stay alone in an isolated car park to guard the vehicle.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance first.** Step away from the vehicle and move to somewhere visible, lit, and public, such as a shop entrance, staffed desk, petrol station counter, reception area, or a place near other people.
2. **Check for immediate danger from a distance.** Look for a door ajar, smashed glass, interior lights on, damage around the lock, someone lingering nearby, or anyone watching or following you. If you feel threatened, or you think a crime is happening now, call 999.
3. **Ask for support before going closer.** If you are at a supermarket, petrol station, hospital, venue, workplace, station, or managed car park, ask staff or security to stay with you while you assess what to do next.
4. **Use the right police route for the moment.** Call 999 for immediate danger, violence, threat of violence, or a crime in progress. Use 101 or online reporting if it looks like a break-in, theft, or tampering happened earlier and there is no immediate threat. Ask how to get a crime reference number or report reference.
5. **Record only what you can safely see.** From a safe distance, take photos of outside damage, doors, locks, windows, the interior through the glass, and the surrounding area. Note the time, exact location, car park level, bay number, and anything you noticed when you first arrived.
6. **Check only the essentials, preferably with someone present.** Confirm your keys or fob are still with you, whether the vehicle still locks, and whether obvious high-risk items are missing, such as wallet, bank cards, house keys, ID, devices, or documents.
7. **Stop if you find something unfamiliar or threatening.** If you see an unfamiliar item that looks deliberately placed, dangerous, or threatening, do not handle it. Move away and call 999.
8. **Do not drive if the vehicle may be unsafe.** If there is broken glass around the controls, a fuel smell, leaking fluid, tyre or wheel damage, steering or brake concerns, or anything that makes the vehicle feel unsafe, arrange a trusted lift, roadside assistance, or recovery instead of driving away.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to claim on insurance or pay for repairs yourself.
- You do not need to work out who did it.
- You do not need to search the area.
- You do not need a perfect list of every missing item before you move somewhere safer.
- You do not need to clean, repair, or rearrange the vehicle immediately unless it is needed to make the area safe.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaken, angry, or hyper-alert in this situation is understandable. Treating it as a safety issue first is a reasonable response, even if it later turns out to be a mistake or something harmless.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment you notice something suspicious around your vehicle. Later decisions about police reporting, insurance, repairs, replacement keys, or security may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.
