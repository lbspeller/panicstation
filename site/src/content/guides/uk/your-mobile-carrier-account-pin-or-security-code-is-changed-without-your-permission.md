# What to do if… your mobile carrier account PIN or security code is changed without your permission (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible SIM swap, eSIM, or number-transfer attempt. Use an official route to contact your mobile provider now, preferably from another phone or trusted Wi-Fi, and ask them to secure the account and stop any pending SIM, eSIM, PAC, or port activity.

## Do not do these things
- Do not call back a number from a text, email, or voicemail about a SIM change, PAC, port, or security code.
- Do not share one-time codes, PAC details, security answers, or screenshots with anyone who contacts you unexpectedly.
- Do not rely on SMS codes as your only way to secure important accounts while your number may be at risk.
- Do not keep guessing the new PIN or repeatedly trying resets if the account starts locking you out.
- Do not assume everything is safe because your phone still has signal.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer way to act.** Use trusted Wi-Fi if you can. Use another phone, a landline, or webchat from the provider’s official website so you are not relying only on the SIM that may be affected.

2. **Contact your mobile provider using an official route.** Use the number on your bill, the provider’s app, or the provider’s website. Say clearly:
   - “My account PIN or security code was changed without my permission.”
   - “This may be a SIM swap, eSIM, or number-transfer attempt.”
   - “Please secure the account and stop any pending SIM, eSIM, PAC, port, or security-detail change.”

3. **Ask for the strongest available protection on the line.** Ask the provider to:
   - reverse the unauthorised PIN or security-code change if they can,
   - block or pause pending account changes if available,
   - require stronger verification before any SIM, eSIM, PAC, port, or security-detail change,
   - add a clear account note that the PIN or security code was changed without your permission.
   Ask for a case or complaint reference number.

4. **Ask what has already happened.** Ask whether there has been:
   - a SIM replacement,
   - an eSIM activation,
   - a PAC request,
   - a number port request,
   - a change to the email address, home address, password, payment details, or contact number,
   - a successful login or support contact you did not make.
   Ask for the date and time of the PIN or security-code change.

5. **Secure the accounts that could unlock other accounts.** From a trusted device, prioritise email, banking, Apple ID or Google account, and your password manager. Change passwords to unique ones, sign out of other sessions where the account offers it, and switch important accounts from SMS codes to an authenticator app, backup codes, or security key where that option is available.

6. **Call your bank or card provider if the number is linked to money.** Say your mobile number may be at risk from SIM swap or number-transfer fraud. Ask them to check for new payees, unusual transfers, changed contact details, and any recent failed security checks.

7. **Report suspicious texts or calls to 7726 if you received them.** Forward scam texts to 7726. For a suspicious call, follow the 7726 reporting instructions from your provider or Ofcom.

8. **Make a fraud report if money, accounts, or identity details are affected.** If you live in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud. If you live in Scotland, report fraud to Police Scotland by calling 101.

9. **Start a complaint trail if the provider does not secure the account promptly.** Ask the provider to log a formal complaint and give you the reference number. If the complaint remains unresolved after six weeks, or sooner if the provider sends a deadlock letter, check Ofcom’s ADR page to find whether your provider uses Communications Ombudsman or CISAS.

## What can wait
- You do not need to secure every online account at once; mobile provider, email, and banking come first.
- You do not need to decide today whether to change your phone number permanently.
- You do not need to identify the person responsible.
- You do not need to post publicly about it.
- You do not need to wipe your phone unless there are separate signs that the device itself is compromised.

## Important reassurance
A changed mobile account PIN or security code is alarming, but it is also a recognised pattern that providers, banks, and fraud-reporting routes deal with. The useful first move is not to solve everything; it is to slow or stop changes to the number and protect the accounts that rely on it.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: secure the mobile account, protect the most important linked accounts, and create a clear record. Later decisions, complaints, financial disputes, or identity-protection steps may need specialist help.
