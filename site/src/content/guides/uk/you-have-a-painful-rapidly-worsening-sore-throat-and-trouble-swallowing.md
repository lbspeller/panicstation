# What to do if… you have a painful, rapidly worsening sore throat and trouble swallowing (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as urgent. If you have breathing difficulty, drooling, noisy or high-pitched breathing, a muffled or changed voice, severe symptoms getting worse quickly, or you cannot swallow, call 999 now and do not drive yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait overnight if symptoms are severe or getting worse quickly.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you have emergency warning signs; call 999.
- Do not lie flat if it makes breathing or swallowing harder.
- Do not force food, tablets, or large gulps of water if you are choking, gagging, or struggling to swallow.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics or someone else’s medication.
- Do not smoke or vape while your throat or breathing feels affected.
- Do not repeatedly poke at your throat or try to inspect it if it triggers gagging, panic, or distress.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency red flags. Call 999 now if any apply:**
   - You are struggling to breathe.
   - Your breathing is noisy, rasping, or high-pitched.
   - You cannot swallow, or you are drooling or spitting out saliva because you cannot swallow it.
   - Your voice has become muffled, very hoarse, or suddenly odd.
   - Your symptoms are severe and getting worse quickly.
   - You feel very unwell, faint, or unusually confused.

2. **While waiting for emergency help, stay in the safest comfortable position.**
   - Sit upright.
   - Lean slightly forward if that makes breathing or swallowing easier.
   - Do not lie flat.
   - Avoid food for now. If you can swallow safely, take only small sips of water.

3. **If there are no 999 red flags but swallowing is painful and the sore throat is rapidly worsening, get same-day urgent help.**
   - Use 111 where available, contact your GP practice for urgent same-day triage, or use your local urgent care route.
   - Say clearly: “I have a rapidly worsening sore throat with painful swallowing and trouble swallowing.”
   - Follow the advice you are given about whether to use a GP appointment, urgent treatment service, emergency department, or another local service.

4. **Use simple pain relief only if you can swallow it safely and it is usually safe for you.**
   - You may be able to use paracetamol or ibuprofen, following the label.
   - Do not take ibuprofen if you have been told not to take it.
   - If tablets are difficult to swallow, use a suitable liquid form you already have if it is safe for you.

5. **Get ready for assessment without delaying urgent help.**
   - Bring or list your current medicines, allergies, major medical conditions, and when the throat symptoms started.
   - Ask someone to stay nearby or check in by phone, especially if symptoms are changing quickly.
   - If you are already at hospital or an urgent care site and breathing or swallowing gets worse, tell staff immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to decide whether you need antibiotics before a clinician assesses you.
- You do not need to keep checking your throat or taking photos if it makes you gag or panic.
- Work messages, errands, and non-urgent plans can wait until you have been checked or directed to the right care.

## Important reassurance
Many sore throats improve on their own, but rapid worsening with trouble swallowing is a pattern that needs prompt attention. Seeking urgent help is a sensible protective step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to help you stay safe and reach the right level of care. Later decisions may need assessment and advice from a clinician or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not a diagnosis, and not medical or professional advice. If you develop breathing difficulty, drooling, inability to swallow, noisy or high-pitched breathing, a muffled voice, or rapid deterioration, treat it as an emergency and call 999.
