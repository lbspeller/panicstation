# What to do if… you receive repeated calls from an unknown number claiming they are outside your home (UK)
## Short answer
Stop engaging with the caller, stay inside with doors locked, and do not go outside to check. If you feel in immediate danger or think someone may actually be outside, call **999**.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go outside to check or open the door because of the call.
- Don’t argue with the caller, threaten them back, or try to catch them out.
- Don’t confirm your address, who is with you, whether you are alone, or where you are in the building.
- Don’t follow instructions such as coming to a window, switching lights on and off for them, or stepping outside.
- Don’t post about it on social media in the moment.
- Don’t assume caller ID proves who it is, because numbers can be spoofed.

## What to do now
1. **End the call and stop engaging.** Let further calls go to voicemail. Silence unknown callers, or use do not disturb with exceptions for trusted contacts, if available.
2. **Move to a safer position inside.** Lock doors and accessible windows. Turn on outside lights only if you can do so without going near a door or exposing yourself.
3. **Check without opening up.** Use a peephole, upstairs window, doorbell camera, CCTV, or a trusted neighbour’s view if you have those options. Do not stand in a lit doorway or shout through the door.
4. **Keep other people and pets away from entrances.** Move children, vulnerable adults, and pets away from doors, ground-floor windows, shared entrances, and letterboxes.
5. **If you feel in immediate danger or think someone may be outside, call 999.** Say you are receiving repeated calls from an unknown number claiming they are outside your location and you cannot confirm it. Follow the call handler’s instructions.
6. **If you cannot speak safely when calling 999 from a mobile, use the Silent Solution.** Stay on the line and listen. If you can, answer by coughing, tapping, or making a small sound. If prompted, press **55** so the call can be put through to police.
7. **If it does not feel immediate but the calls are abusive, threatening, or repeated, call 101.** Tell them what was said, when the calls happened, and whether there are voicemails, screenshots, or call logs.
8. **Contact your phone provider about malicious or nuisance calls.** Ask for its nuisance or malicious calls team and ask what it can do now, such as call blocking, anonymous call rejection, spam filtering, or advice on what records to keep.
9. **Bring in one calm third party.** Call or text a trusted neighbour, friend, building security, or concierge. Ask them to stay in contact or check communal areas only if they can do that safely and without confronting anyone.
10. **Keep quick records without focusing on proof.** Note the time of each call, the number shown, what was said, and whether there is voicemail. Screenshots are enough for now.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to change your number.
- Buying extra security devices.
- Writing a detailed timeline.
- Making complaints to regulators.
- Trying to identify the caller yourself.
- Contacting lots of people; one trusted person is enough right now.

## Important reassurance
A caller may be trying to scare you into opening the door, stepping outside, or confirming that you are alone. Staying inside, limiting what you reveal, and contacting emergency or non-emergency help when needed is a reasonable response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stay safe and avoid irreversible mistakes. If the calls continue, later decisions may need help from police, your phone provider, or a specialist support service.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in danger or someone is outside and you feel unsafe, call **999**.
