# What to do if… a rental car company offers a different vehicle type and you do not feel safe driving it (UK)

## Short answer
Do not accept or drive a vehicle you do not feel safe driving. Pause at the counter, clearly refuse that vehicle type, and ask for a safe equivalent or cancellation before you accept the vehicle or sign the collection agreement.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t let yourself be rushed into signing “just to get going” if you feel unsafe or unsure.
- Don’t take the keys and drive out of the car park to test whether your confidence improves.
- Don’t accept a vehicle with controls, size, visibility, height, fuel type, or transmission you cannot use safely.
- Don’t sign paperwork or tap “accept” if the vehicle group, type, or transmission shown is not what you are willing to drive.
- Don’t argue about blame at the counter. Keep repeating: “I’m not confident driving that vehicle type. I need an alternative or to cancel.”

## What to do now
1. **Stop and make the safety decision clear.** Say: “I’m not confident driving that vehicle type. I can’t accept it safely.”

2. **Ask for a like-for-like vehicle you can drive.** Be specific about what matters:
   - the size or class you booked, such as a small car rather than an SUV or van
   - the transmission you booked, such as automatic rather than manual
   - visibility or features you rely on, such as parking sensors or a reversing camera
   - any unfamiliar operating model you cannot safely handle, such as an electric vehicle if you are not comfortable using one

3. **Ask them to check practical alternatives while you stay at the desk.** Ask whether they can provide:
   - a different car on-site
   - a car from another nearby branch
   - a returned vehicle, with a realistic waiting time
   - a smaller or more familiar vehicle group at no extra cost if the booked group is unavailable

4. **If they cannot provide a vehicle you can safely drive, ask to cancel and avoid new charges.** Say: “If you can’t provide a vehicle I can safely drive, I need to cancel and I do not agree to any extra charge for this substitute vehicle.”

5. **Check the collection agreement before accepting anything.** Do not accept the keys or sign the collection agreement unless the vehicle shown is one you can safely drive, including the class and transmission where those details are shown.

6. **Record what happened before you leave the counter.** Keep or photograph your own booking confirmation, emails, agreement screens, receipts, and any visible vehicle details if staff allow. Note the time, branch, staff name if available, the vehicle offered, and the alternative you asked for.

7. **If you booked through a broker or comparison website, contact them from the counter.** Ask them to confirm what you booked, contact the branch, and log in writing that the offered vehicle type is not one you feel safe driving.

8. **Ask what will happen to any payment or pre-authorisation.** If a pre-authorisation has been taken, ask the branch to confirm whether it will be voided or reversed if you cancel. The time it takes to disappear from your account can depend on your bank or card issuer.

9. **Leave without driving if the pressure continues.** Ask for a copy of anything you have signed or been asked to sign, then step away and make the complaint once you are no longer at the counter.

10. **Use the complaint route later if needed.** Complain to the hire company first. If the company is a BVRLA member, the transaction took place in the UK, and you have exhausted the company’s complaints process and received its final written decision, you can usually ask BVRLA’s ADR service to look at the dispute within 12 months of that final response.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a formal complaint.
- You do not need to calculate compensation at the counter.
- You do not need to quote laws or argue about legal rights while you are feeling pressured.
- You do not need to post reviews today.
- You can sort out refunds, chargeback, Section 75, ADR, or a written complaint after you are safely away from the counter and have saved your documents.

## Important reassurance
Feeling unable to drive an unfamiliar, larger, manual, automatic, electric, or otherwise different vehicle is a safety judgement, not a personal failure. It is reasonable to refuse a vehicle you do not feel confident controlling, especially when you are under time pressure or in an unfamiliar place.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: they are meant to help you avoid driving unsafely and avoid accepting paperwork under pressure. Later decisions about complaints, refunds, ADR, chargeback, or Section 75 may need help from Citizens Advice, your card provider, the hire company, BVRLA if relevant, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Car hire terms vary by company, booking channel, vehicle group, and payment method. If you feel pressured or unsafe, prioritise leaving without driving and keep a clear written record of what happened.
