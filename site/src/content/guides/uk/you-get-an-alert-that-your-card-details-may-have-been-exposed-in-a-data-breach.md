# What to do if… you get an alert that your card details may have been exposed in a data breach (UK)

## Short answer
Freeze your card immediately through your banking app and verify the alert by calling **159** or the number on the back of your physical card. Do not click links in the alert message or provide security codes to anyone who contacts you.

## Do not do these things
- Don't click any buttons or links in the notification email or text message.
- Don't call phone numbers provided within the alert itself; use trusted sources only.
- Don't share your PIN, full password, or any One-Time Passcode (OTP) with anyone, even if they claim to be from your bank's fraud team.
- Don't approve "add card to wallet" or "verify device" prompts on your phone unless you specifically triggered them.
- Don't assume your account is safe just because there are no large missing transactions yet.

## What to do now
1. **Freeze your payment card immediately using your bank’s official mobile app or website.** Most UK banks provide a "freeze" or "lock" toggle that stops new transactions while you verify the situation.
2. **Verify the alert by calling your bank directly using the 159 hotline or the number on the back of your card.** If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, you can also report the breach to **Report Fraud** at 0300 123 2040; if you are in Scotland, call **Police Scotland via 101**.
3. **Review your transaction history line-by-line for small, unfamiliar amounts.** Look specifically for "test" payments of £0.00, £0.01, or small amounts under £5.00, as these often precede larger fraudulent charges.
4. **Order a replacement card and confirm the old card number is permanently cancelled.** A simple freeze is temporary; if a breach is confirmed, you require a new card with a different 16-digit number and CVV code.
5. **Check your "Continuous Payment Authorities" (CPAs) and recurring card subscriptions.** Unlike Direct Debits, these are linked to your card number and will stop working once the card is replaced; you will need to update these manually once your new card arrives.
6. **Adjust your contactless spending limits and international payment settings in your banking app.** Use 2026 app features to lower your contactless ceiling or disable "card not present" (online) transactions until your new card is secured.
7. **Remove the compromised card from all digital wallets and saved merchant accounts.** 
   - Delete the card from Apple Pay, Google Wallet, or Samsung Pay.
   - Remove saved card details from high-frequency apps like Amazon, Uber, Deliveroo, and supermarket websites.
8. **Update your email account password and enable two-factor authentication (2FA).** If your card was leaked, your login credentials for the site that was breached might also be at risk; securing your primary email is the priority.
9. **Apply for Cifas Protective Registration if your personal identity details were also leaked.** If the breach included your name, address, or National Insurance number, pay the £30 fee (valid for 2 years) to place a warning flag on your credit file that requires lenders to perform extra identity checks.

## What can wait
- Changing passwords for accounts that do not have your card details or personal data saved.
- Filing a formal complaint against the breached company; focus on securing your funds first.
- Cancelling Direct Debits or Standing Orders, as these use your account number and sort code, not your card details.
- Requesting a full credit report, though you should do this within the next 30 days to check for new accounts.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a breach alert is a standard security procedure and does not mean your money has already been stolen. By freezing the card and requesting a replacement, you effectively "kill" the stolen data, making it useless to criminals. UK banks have robust protections for unauthorised card payments, and you are generally not liable for losses if you act promptly.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate financial stabilisation following a card data breach. It does not cover long-term identity theft recovery or legal action against breached entities.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute financial or legal advice. Regulations regarding fraud reimbursement, such as the mandatory APP fraud rules, apply differently depending on the transaction type. Always follow the specific security instructions provided by your bank's official fraud department.
