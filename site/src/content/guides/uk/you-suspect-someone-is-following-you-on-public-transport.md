# What to do if… you suspect someone is following you on public transport (UK)

## Short answer
Move to a busier, staffed place and ask transport staff or police for help. Do not go home, leave at an isolated stop, or try to prove what is happening. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the person, argue, or try to prove they are following you.
- Do not move into an empty carriage, a quiet platform end, a deserted bus stop, or a secluded station exit.
- Do not get off at a quiet stop just to lose them.
- Do not lead them to your home, workplace, hotel, or usual walking route if you can avoid it.
- Do not go out of public view with someone you do not know; stay near staff, a counter, gates, or other passengers.
- Do not film, photograph, or stare at them if that could make the situation less safe.

## What to do now
1. **Move toward people, visibility, and staff.**  
   In a station, go toward ticket gates, a staffed area, a help point, a shop, or a busy concourse. On a platform, move away from the quiet end and stay where other passengers and staff are more likely to see you.

2. **Change position without isolating yourself.**  
   On a train, underground, or tram, move to a busier part of the carriage or change to a busier carriage at the next stop. On a bus, move closer to the driver and stay where other passengers can see you.

3. **Tell staff early and plainly.**  
   Say: “I think someone may be following me. I am staying in public view. Can you help me get to a safe staffed area or contact police?” You do not need to prove it before asking.

4. **Use the right police route.**  
   For trains, stations, and rail networks where British Transport Police operates, text 61016 or call 0800 40 50 40 for non-emergency help. For buses or roads, local police can usually be contacted on 101 for non-emergencies. If someone is threatening you, very close to you, blocking you, or you feel in immediate danger, call 999.

5. **If you cannot safely speak on a 999 call, stay on the line and follow the operator’s instructions.**  
   Keep the phone connected if you can. Move toward staff or a public counter while you do this.

6. **If you decide to get off, choose a busy, staffed place.**  
   Get off at a busy station or stop, not a quiet one. Go straight to a staffed, well-lit public area such as a ticket hall, barrier line, help point, shop, or a place near the driver, and stay there while you ask for help.

7. **Give only safe, useful details.**  
   Tell staff or police your route, direction of travel, next stop, time, platform, bus route, train service, carriage area if known, and a simple description of the person such as clothing or bag. Do not put yourself closer to them to collect details.

8. **Message a trusted person as an anchor.**  
   Send your location, route, next stop, and the fact that you are staying near staff. If they are meeting you, ask them to meet inside a busy, staffed station or other public place, not outside in a quiet area.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether they definitely followed you.
- You do not need to finish your planned journey.
- You do not need to gather evidence, make a formal report, or explain the whole history before getting help.
- You do not need to choose what to do later until you are in a safer, staffed, public place.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to second-guess yourself or feel embarrassed in this situation. Moving toward staff and other people is a reasonable safety step even if it later turns out to be nothing.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you reach a safer pause and get support. Later decisions, including whether to make a report or change your route, can be made after the immediate situation is stable and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
