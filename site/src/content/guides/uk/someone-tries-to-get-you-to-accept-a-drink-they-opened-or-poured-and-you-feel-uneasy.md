# What to do if… someone tries to get you to accept a drink they opened or poured and you feel uneasy (UK)
## Short answer
Do not drink it. Immediately put the drink down, move to a populated area, and notify venue staff or a trusted person that you feel uncomfortable.

## Do not do these things
- Don't drink it to be polite or to avoid an awkward conversation.
- Don't carry the drink with you while you move; leave it on a flat surface and walk away.
- Don't go to a private area (toilets, car, or outside) to discuss your unease with the person who provided the drink.
- Don't try to "test" the drink by taking a small sip.
- Don't worry about being "wrong" or overreacting; your safety is the priority.

## What to do now
1. **End the interaction and create physical distance.** Use a neutral statement like "I'm okay for now" or "I've changed my mind," then **put the drink down** and **walk toward the bar or a security station**.
2. **Alert a member of staff or security immediately.** Use a direct request: "I don't feel safe with the person I am with" or "I am being pressured to drink and I need help leaving."
   - If the venue is part of the national safeguarding scheme, you can **ask for "Angela"** at the bar to signal you need discreet assistance.
3. **Contact someone you trust to stay with you.** If you are with friends, tell them clearly: "I feel uneasy, please stay with me and don't let me leave with anyone else."
4. **Arrange a safe way home that you control.** Ask staff to help you book a taxi from a reputable company or wait with you at the door until your transport arrives.
5. **Monitor yourself for sudden physical changes.** If you begin to feel unusually confused, dizzy, nauseous, or significantly more intoxicated than expected:
   - **Call 111** for urgent medical advice if you feel unwell but are stable.
   - **Call 999** immediately if you have trouble breathing, lose consciousness, or have a seizure.
6. **Preserve evidence only if it is safe and manageable.** If you suspect spiking or non-consensual contact, keep your glass (if possible) or take a photo of it.
   - If you suspect sexual assault, try to **avoid washing, eating, or brushing your teeth** until you have spoken to a professional, as this preserves forensic evidence.
7. **Contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) for specialist support.** You can access medical care, forensic testing, and emotional support through the NHS without having to report the incident to the police.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to file a formal police report.
- You do not need to explain your departure or "finish" the date or social outing.
- You do not need to prove that the drink was tampered with before seeking help.
- You do not need to check your phone or respond to messages from the person making you uneasy.

## Important reassurance
Feeling uneasy or "off" is a valid and sufficient reason to stop what you are doing and seek safety. Many people experience a "freeze" or "fawn" response when pressured, which is a natural reaction to stress. Getting to a safe, observed environment is the most important step.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for navigating immediate social pressure and potential safety risks in a nightlife or social setting. It is not a substitute for medical or legal advice.

## Important note
If you believe a crime has been committed or you are in immediate danger, call 999. For non-emergency reporting of spiking, call 101. Specialist support is available through SARCs regardless of whether you choose to involve the police.
