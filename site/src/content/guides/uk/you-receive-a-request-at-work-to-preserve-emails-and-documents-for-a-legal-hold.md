# What to do if… you receive a request at work to preserve emails and documents for a legal hold (UK)

## Short answer
Create an immediate written timeline of your initial report and the subsequent changes in your treatment, and secure copies of all non-confidential evidence off work systems. If you are facing dismissal or have already been dismissed, seek advice immediately to meet strict 7-day or 3-month legal deadlines.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign immediately to escape the situation without taking advice, as this can severely limit your legal protections and claim options.
- Do not sign any settlement agreements, "final warnings," or new contract terms during a meeting; always request a reasonable period to review the documents.
- Do not delete emails, calendar invites, or messages, even if they seem minor, as these form the core of your evidence trail.
- Do not download or remove confidential company data, trade secrets, or client lists to "prove" your case, as this may give your employer grounds for a fair dismissal.
- Do not record meetings or conversations secretly unless you have verified your workplace policy; instead, take detailed notes and send a written summary afterward.
- Do not discuss the situation on social media or with colleagues who are not involved in the formal process.

## What to do now
1. **Document every observable change in your working conditions immediately.** Note specific dates and times for actions such as a sudden increase in workload, removal from long-standing projects, changes to shift patterns, or being excluded from standard team communications.
   - Record the names of anyone present during these incidents.
   - Describe the "detriment" in factual terms (e.g., "removed from weekly 10:00 meeting" rather than "boss is being mean").
2. **Secure a copy of your original grievance or protected disclosure.** Ensure you have the exact date and method of submission (e.g., emailed to HR at 14:02 on 12 March) to establish a clear link between your "protected act" and the suspected retaliation.
3. **Save all relevant non-confidential communications to a personal device or physical folder.** This includes meeting invites, performance reviews, and emails where your tone or work was previously praised before the grievance was filed.
4. **Request all performance-related concerns or disciplinary allegations in writing.** If a manager gives verbal "feedback" that feels like retaliation, send a follow-up email stating: "Following our chat, I understand your concerns are X, Y, and Z; please let me know if I have missed anything."
5. **Exercise your legal right to be accompanied to formal meetings.** If you are called to a grievance or disciplinary hearing, notify your employer in writing that you wish to bring a trade union representative or a colleague.
   - If your companion cannot attend, you have the right to suggest an alternative time within 5 working days of the original date.
6. **Report the suspected retaliation as a new or supplementary grievance.** Follow your employer’s formal procedure to state that you believe you are being subjected to "victimisation" (under the Equality Act) or "detriment" (under whistleblowing law).
7. **Identify and monitor your tribunal deadlines immediately.** Most claims must be submitted to an Employment Tribunal within 3 months minus 1 day of the act of retaliation.
   - Note: From October 2026, most time limits are scheduled to extend to 6 months under the Employment Rights Act 2025.
   - If you have been dismissed for whistleblowing, you must apply for "interim relief" within **7 days** of your termination date to potentially keep your pay running.
8. **Contact Acas to start Early Conciliation if the situation is not resolving.** This is a mandatory step before most tribunal claims and "pauses the clock" on your 3-month deadline.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to stay with the company or resign today.
- You do not need to prove the legal "category" (victimisation vs. whistleblowing) yourself; a solicitor or advisor will do this later based on your facts.
- You do not need to have a "perfect" or complete evidence file before you first raise the issue with HR or Acas.
- You do not need to respond to every minor slight or "cold" behavior if it does not affect your pay, duties, or health.

## Important reassurance
Feeling isolated or targeted after speaking up is a common experience, and your reaction of stress or confusion is normal. By moving from memory-based arguments to a written timeline, you are taking the single most effective step to regain control of the situation.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and evidence preservation only. Employment law involving whistleblowing and victimisation is complex; you will likely need specialist advice from a union, solicitor, or Acas if the situation escalates to a hearing or dismissal.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance in the UK and does not constitute legal advice. Employment rights vary based on your specific contract, length of service, and whether your report qualifies as a "protected disclosure." Deadlines in the Employment Tribunal are extremely strict; if you are unsure of your dates, contact Acas or a legal professional immediately.
