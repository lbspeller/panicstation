# What to do if… you are removed from key duties at work without explanation and think you are being pushed out (UK)

## Short answer
Do not resign or send angry messages while you are panicked. Ask for the change and your current priorities in writing, make a factual timeline, and use your employer’s grievance route or your union if informal clarification does not work.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign “to make a point” or because you feel cornered.
- Do not send accusatory messages saying the change is illegal or that they are pushing you out.
- Do not sign a role change, variation, settlement document, or resignation letter on the spot.
- Do not remove confidential company, client, or colleague information to prove what happened.
- Do not rely only on verbal reassurance about what your role now is.
- Do not leave the change undocumented.
- Do not refuse reasonable work in the moment unless there is an immediate safety, legal, or health reason.

## What to do now
1. **Start a factual timeline today.**  
   Write down dates, times, duties removed, meetings you were dropped from, access removed, who told you, what was said, and who else was present. Keep it factual, not emotional.

2. **Ask for written clarity on your current role.**  
   Send one calm message to your manager, such as: “Please confirm which responsibilities have moved away from my role, what my priorities are now, and how my performance will be assessed going forward.” If it was said verbally, add: “My understanding from our conversation is that… please correct anything I have misunderstood.”

3. **Keep permitted personal employment records.**  
   Keep copies of your contract, offer letter, job description, objectives, appraisals, recent written feedback, and any written instructions about the change. Stay within policy; personal employment records are different from confidential business, client, or colleague materials.

4. **Ask whether this is a temporary instruction or a proposed role change.**  
   Ask for any updated job description, reporting line, objectives, and whether pay, grade, title, location, or working pattern are changing. This helps separate a short-term workload change from a possible contract or role change.

5. **If you do not agree, say so calmly in writing while continuing to work.**  
   You can write: “I am concerned these changes significantly alter my role. I will continue to work while this is discussed, but I am not agreeing to a permanent change without proper discussion.” If you need to make clear that you are not accepting the change as final, you can use the words “working under protest” and ask a union representative, Acas, or an adviser if you are unsure.

6. **Use the grievance route if you cannot get a clear answer.**  
   If informal messages do not resolve it, submit a short formal grievance asking: why your duties were removed, what your current duties and objectives are, whether there are performance concerns, whether this is temporary or permanent, and what process will be followed. Keep it specific and attach only relevant permitted records.

7. **Ask for support at formal meetings.**  
   If you are in a union, contact your representative early. For a grievance meeting about a possible breach of a legal or contractual duty, employees and workers generally have the right to be accompanied by a trade union representative or a work colleague. Ask in writing who may attend with you and give reasonable notice of your chosen companion.

8. **Protect dates without rushing into a claim.**  
   Employment tribunal time limits can be short. For most claims the limit is 3 months minus 1 day from the relevant act, while some claims have different limits, and an internal grievance does not change the time limit. The usual first step is to notify Acas within the time limit so Early Conciliation can be offered. Note key dates now and get prompt advice if dismissal, discrimination, whistleblowing, unpaid wages, or retaliation may be involved.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, negotiate a settlement, or start a legal claim.
- You do not need to prove motive right now; focus on what changed, when it changed, and what you were told.
- You do not need a perfect grievance letter before you make any record.
- You do not need to confront colleagues or campaign internally.
- You do not need to agree to a permanent role change just because you need time to think.

## Important reassurance
Having key duties removed can feel humiliating and frightening, especially when no one explains why. The safest first move is usually slow and factual: keep working where you can, ask for the position in writing, and create a record before making any big decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, aimed at stabilising the situation and preventing irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about grievances, settlement talks, resigning, tribunal claims, or legal action may need specialist help and depend on your contract, length of service, and the facts.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Employment situations vary, so consider getting independent advice promptly if you may be at risk of dismissal, discrimination, whistleblowing retaliation, unpaid wages, or a forced contract change.
