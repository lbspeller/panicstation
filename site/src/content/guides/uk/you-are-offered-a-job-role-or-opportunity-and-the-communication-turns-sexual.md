# What to do if… you are offered a job, role, or opportunity and the communication turns sexual (UK)

## Short answer
Stop communicating immediately to create a safe pause, preserve all evidence without deleting it, and do not attend any further meetings alone. You are legally protected as a job applicant from sexual harassment under the Equality Act 2010.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete any messages, photos, or call logs, even if they are distressing or embarrassing.
- Do not meet the individual alone to "clarify" the situation or to try and resolve the tension.
- Do not send any personal information, home addresses, or intimate images to appease them.
- Do not feel obligated to explain why you are uncomfortable; your lack of consent is sufficient.
- Do not assume you have lost your legal rights because you haven't signed an employment contract yet.

## What to do now
1. **End the interaction immediately without providing an excuse.** If you are in a live conversation or digital chat, stop responding once a boundary has been crossed.
   - You may send a single final message: "I am only interested in professional communication regarding this role; I am ending this conversation now."
2. **Preserve the evidence exactly as it appeared.** Take screenshots of every message, including the sender's profile details, timestamps, and any links sent.
   - Do not edit or crop the screenshots.
   - Save these to a secure personal cloud or email them to a secondary account that only you can access.
3. **Verify the legitimacy of the organisation independently.** Search for the company's official website using a search engine rather than clicking links provided by the recruiter.
   - Check the official "Contact Us" page or HR department details.
   - Call the main switchboard to confirm if the individual actually works there or if this is a recruitment scam.
4. **Contact the relevant support service for your region.** Professional staff can help you process what happened and discuss your safety options.
   - **England and Wales:** Call the 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line on 0808 500 2222.
   - **Scotland:** Call the Rape Crisis Scotland Helpline on 08088 010 302 (open 5pm–midnight).
   - **Northern Ireland:** Call the Nexus/Rape Crisis NI Support Line on 0800 0246 991.
5. **Report the conduct to the organisation’s HR department if they are a legitimate employer.** If the individual is an employee, the organisation has a legal duty to prevent harassment.
   - From April 2026, reporting sexual harassment is a "qualifying disclosure" which grants you specific whistleblowing protections.
   - State clearly that you are a job applicant and have experienced unwanted conduct of a sexual nature.
6. **Involve the police if you feel threatened or are being stalked.** If the person has your address, is following you, or is making threats, this is a criminal matter.
   - Call **999** for immediate danger.
   - Call **101** or report via your local police force website for non-emergency harassment.
7. **Contact Acas for neutral guidance on your rights as an applicant.** They can explain how the Equality Act 2010 applies to your specific recruitment situation.
   - Use the Acas helpline or website to understand the 2026 updates to employer liability for third-party harassment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to file a formal Employment Tribunal claim.
- You do not need to decide whether to tell your current employer or family.
- You do not need to "fix" the job opportunity or try to salvage the offer.
- You do not need to write a full witness statement or chronological report immediately.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel "frozen," confused, or even guilty when a professional opportunity is sexualised. These reactions are biological responses to a breach of trust and power. The responsibility for this behaviour lies entirely with the person who crossed the boundary, not with you for being professional or seeking a job.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only for stabilising your safety and preserving evidence in the UK. Later decisions regarding legal claims or police investigations may require specialist legal or advocacy support.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal or medical advice. UK laws regarding workplace harassment and whistleblowing changed significantly in 2026; always verify current procedures with a qualified solicitor or advocate if you intend to take legal action.
