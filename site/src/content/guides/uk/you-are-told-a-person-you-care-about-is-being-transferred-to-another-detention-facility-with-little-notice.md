# What to do if… you are told a person you care about is being transferred to another detention facility with little notice (UK)

## Short answer
Record all provided details immediately and verify the person's location through the relevant national prison service before attempting to travel or send items.

## Do not do these things
- Do not travel to a suspected new location until the establishment confirms the person has been processed and is permitted to receive visitors.
- Do not send money, property, or legal documents to an unverified address.
- Do not rely on unverified information from social media or other prisoners' families regarding the specific destination.
- Do not share the person's sensitive personal data or prisoner number on public forums.
- Do not call multiple prisons simultaneously; follow a structured contact order to avoid conflicting information.

## What to do now
1. **Create a record of all known facts regarding the transfer.** 
   - Note the person's full name, date of birth, and prisoner number if available. 
   - Record the name of the last known prison and the name/job title of the person who informed you of the move. 
   - Write down the exact wording used (such as "in transit," "on a late van," or a specific prison name).

2. **Follow the specific verification route for the relevant UK jurisdiction.**
   - **England & Wales:** If you know the last prison, call their main switchboard to ask if the person is "still on site," "in transit," or "confirmed arrived" elsewhere. Use the official "Find a prisoner" email service if the location remains unknown for 72 hours (note that replies can take up to 4 weeks).
   - **Scotland:** Contact the switchboard of the last known prison; most Scottish Prison Service (SPS) establishments have automated systems where you can "Press 9" to raise a priority welfare concern or request location confirmation.
   - **Northern Ireland:** Contact the Northern Ireland Prison Service (NIPS) headquarters or the specific establishment's family liaison officer to confirm the person's current status.

3. **Contact the Prisoners’ Families Helpline for procedural support in England and Wales.** 
   Call **0808 808 2003** (9am – 8pm Monday to Friday; 10am – 3pm weekends and bank holidays, excluding Christmas Day and Boxing Day). This service can provide guidance on how transfers affect visiting rights and telephone credits.

4. **Address urgent safety or medical concerns directly with the Safer Custody team.** 
   Call the switchboard of either the sending or receiving prison and explicitly request the **Safer Custody Team** or the **Duty Governor**. State clearly that you are reporting an "urgent welfare concern" if the person has known risks of self-harm, specific medical needs, or safety vulnerabilities that may be exacerbated by the move.

5. **Wait 72 hours for the person to make initial telephone contact from the new facility.** 
   New arrivals are typically permitted a "reception call" within 24 to 72 hours of arrival, though this may be delayed during weekends or if the intake volume is high. If they call, prioritize asking for their new prisoner number (if changed) and the correct address for mail.

6. **Inform the person's legal representative of the reported transfer.** 
   If they have a solicitor or barrister, provide them with the updated information so they can verify the location for official court correspondence and legal visits.

7. **Use a neutral forwarding letter if the location cannot be confirmed.** 
   Send a short letter to the **last known prison** address clearly marked "Please Forward to New Establishment" on the envelope, including the person's full name and date of birth.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the reason for the transfer (such as categorization changes or population management) immediately.
- You do not need to settle accounts for canteen or private cash until the new prison's finance office confirms their arrival.
- You do not need to lodge a formal complaint about the lack of notice unless there is a breach of a specific court order or safety protocol.

## Important reassurance
It is common for families to receive very little notice before a prison transfer occurs. This lack of information is often due to security protocols or the speed of the transport system rather than a specific issue with the individual. Feeling overwhelmed or anxious when contact is temporarily lost is a frequent reaction to the prison environment.

## Scope note
This guidance covers the immediate stabilization of information and location verification in the first 24 to 72 hours of a transfer. It does not cover long-term transfer appeals or property claims.

## Important note
This guide provides general procedural information and does not constitute legal advice. Prison systems in England & Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland operate under different regulations and management structures. Always prioritize information received directly from official prison service channels.
