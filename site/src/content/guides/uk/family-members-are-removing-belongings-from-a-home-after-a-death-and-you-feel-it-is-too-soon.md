# What to do if… family members are removing belongings from a home after a death and you feel it is too soon (UK)

## Short answer
Ask for a calm temporary pause: nothing non-urgent leaves the home today while you record what is there and identify who is dealing with the estate.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t get into a shouting match or accuse someone of stealing unless there is an immediate safety or crime concern.
- Don’t physically block doorways, grab items, or try to stop people by force.
- Don’t secretly remove items yourself for safekeeping unless the person dealing with the estate has agreed.
- Don’t throw away, donate, sell, or divide belongings while people are upset.
- Don’t rely on verbal promises for valuables, documents, keys, or sentimental items.

## What to do now
1. **Create a calm pause.** Say: “I’m not saying anyone is doing anything wrong. I need us to pause so nothing leaves today until we have recorded what is here.”
2. **Ask for one calmer person to be present.** This could be a friend, neighbour, or relative who is not driving the conflict. Their role is to keep the moment steady, not to take sides.
3. **Secure the home without making a scene.** Close windows, lock doors when people leave, and put spare keys in one visible place. Agree that no extra keys are handed out until the person dealing with the estate has agreed a temporary key plan.
4. **Make a quick record before anything else moves.** Take a walk-through video of each room if it is safe. Photograph cupboards, drawers, boxes, and any items that people are discussing. Write a short list of high-risk items such as cash, jewellery, important documents, keys, phones, laptops, and sentimental objects.
5. **Set aside documents and essentials.** Ask everyone to help locate the will, passport, driving licence, bank cards, cheque books, property papers, tenancy papers, insurance papers, keys, and medication. Put them in one labelled bag or box, keep them secure, and do not use bank cards or cheque books.
6. **Ask who is dealing with the estate.** Ask: “Who is named as executor in the will?” If nobody knows, focus on finding the most recent will or confirming that one has not been found. The executor or administrator is generally the person responsible for dealing with the estate, but what they can do immediately may depend on the asset and whether a grant is needed.
7. **Suggest a temporary process for today.** Say: “No one takes belongings today. If something genuinely has to be removed, such as perishable food or medication that needs returning, we photograph it first and write down who took it.”
8. **If someone keeps removing items, reduce confrontation.** Record what you can safely see without following, blocking, or provoking anyone. Say once: “I’m asking you to stop removing items until the estate has been recorded.” Then step back and get advice from a calm place.
9. **Call for help if there is danger or a crime in progress.** Use 999 if someone is in immediate danger, violence is threatened, forced entry is happening, or a crime is happening now. Use 101 or online police contact for a non-emergency concern.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who gets what.
- You do not need to clear the home, value items, sell belongings, or sort sentimental possessions now.
- You do not need to resolve family history, fairness arguments, or inheritance tension in this moment.

## Important reassurance
Wanting things to slow down is not being difficult. A short pause, a basic record, and a clear temporary boundary can protect everyone and make later decisions easier to handle.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and reduce the chance of belongings being lost or arguments escalating. Later decisions about the estate, the home, missing items, or disputes may need specialist legal advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Processes can vary depending on where in the UK you are and the details of the estate, the property, and who has authority to act.
