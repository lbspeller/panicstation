# What to do if… you notice new slurred speech or difficulty getting words out (UK)

## Short answer
Call **999 now** and say there is **new slurred speech or trouble getting words out — possible stroke**. Do **not** drive yourself to A&E.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if it passes, even if it seems mild or comes and goes.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E or ask someone to take you instead of calling 999.
- Do not eat or drink unless the 999 call handler or paramedics say it is safe.
- Do not self-start aspirin, take extra doses, or take “just in case” medicines while waiting for emergency advice.
- Do not go to sleep to see if it improves.
- Do not keep working, walking around, or trying to push through the speech problem.

## What to do now
1. **Dial 999 immediately.**
   - Say: “**New slurred speech or trouble getting words out — possible stroke**.”
   - If speaking is difficult, put the phone on speaker, stay on the line, and make any sound or tap if you can.
2. **Note the time symptoms started.**
   - If you are not sure, use the last time you were definitely speaking normally.
   - Tell the 999 call handler and paramedics this time.
3. **Use FAST while help is coming, but do not delay the call to check.**
   - **Face:** one side drooping, uneven smile, or new face weakness.
   - **Arms:** one arm weak, numb, or drifting down.
   - **Speech:** slurred, garbled, confused, or unable to get words out.
   - **Time:** call 999 immediately if any one of these is present.
4. **Make it easier for paramedics to reach you.**
   - Unlock the door if safe.
   - Move pets away from the entrance.
   - Turn on a light if it helps people find you.
   - Keep your phone nearby.
5. **Reduce the chance of a fall or choking.**
   - Sit down and avoid walking around.
   - If you feel very faint, very drowsy, or you vomit, lie on your side if you can do so safely.
6. **Prepare only the essentials if it does not delay help.**
   - Write down the symptom start time or last known well time.
   - Add your address, allergies, and key medicines, especially blood thinners or anticoagulants.
   - Put medicine boxes nearby if they are easy to reach.
7. **If the speech problem stops, still treat it as urgent.**
   - Stroke-like symptoms that go away can still need immediate assessment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether this is a stroke, a TIA, a “mini-stroke”, anxiety, migraine, or something else.
- You do not need to call your GP, NHS 111, a pharmacy, or a routine clinic first.
- You do not need a full medical history now; the symptom time and key medicines are enough.
- You do not need to contact work, cancel plans, or explain everything to other people before calling 999.

## Important reassurance
Sudden trouble speaking can feel frightening and unreal. Calling 999 is the right action even if you are not sure, because emergency teams are used to assessing stroke-like symptoms quickly and calmly.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: get urgent help, reduce delay, and avoid common harmful actions. Later tests, treatment, and decisions need clinicians once you are safe.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice or a diagnosis. If you suspect a stroke or sudden neurological symptoms, treat it as an emergency and call 999.
