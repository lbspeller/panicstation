# What to do if… you notice new slurred speech or difficulty getting words out (UK)

## Short answer
**Dial 999 immediately** and tell the operator you suspect a **stroke**. Do not wait to see if it improves, even if it feels minor or passes quickly.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive yourself or ask someone else to drive you to the hospital.
- Do not eat or drink anything, as a stroke can impair your ability to swallow safely.
- Do not take any medication "just in case," including aspirin, unless specifically instructed by the 999 dispatcher.
- Do not go to sleep or lie down to see if you feel better after a rest.
- Do not call your GP or NHS 111 first; these services will only tell you to call 999.
- Do not wait for a second symptom to appear before calling.

## What to do now
1. **Dial 999 immediately.**
   - **State "Stroke" clearly when asked which service you require.**
   - **If you are a BSL user, use the 999 BSL app or website; if you are a text relay user, call 18000.**
   - **Stay on the line and put your phone on speaker so you can follow instructions while moving.**
2. **Identify the "Last Known Well" time.**
   - **Pinpoint the exact time the speech change was first noticed.**
   - **If the symptoms started during sleep, note the time you last spoke normally before going to bed.**
3. **Perform a FAST check while waiting for the ambulance.**
   - **Face:** Can you smile evenly, or does one side of the mouth or eye droop?
   - **Arms:** Can you raise both arms and hold them there, or does one drift downward?
   - **Speech:** Is it slurred, garbled, or are the words completely wrong?
   - **Time:** If any one of these is present, it is an emergency.
4. **Prepare your environment for paramedics.**
   - **Unlock the front door and clear a wide path for a stretcher to move through the hallway.**
   - **Turn on outside lights if it is dark and put pets in a separate room.**
5. **Sit in a supported position.**
   - **Sit on the floor with your back against a wall or in a sturdy armchair to prevent falls.**
   - **If you feel sick, roll onto your side into the recovery position to keep your airway clear.**
6. **Gather essential handover information.**
   - **Write down the time symptoms started and a list of current medications, especially blood thinners (anticoagulants).**
   - **Place your medication boxes or a repeat prescription slip in a clear bag by the door.**
7. **Treat resolving symptoms as a priority.**
   - **Even if the slurred speech disappears completely before help arrives, you must still go to the hospital.**
   - **A Transient Ischaemic Attack (TIA) is a medical emergency and a major warning sign of an impending full stroke.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if this is a "mini-stroke" or a full stroke; that is for the hospital to decide.
- You do not need to pack a hospital bag or worry about long-term arrangements right now.
- You do not need to notify your employer or family members before the ambulance arrives.
- You do not need to find your NHS number or detailed medical files.

## Important reassurance
Difficulty speaking is a common emergency symptom that paramedics and hospital stroke teams are trained to handle every day. It is normal to feel confused or frustrated when you cannot get words out. Calling 999 is the most effective way to protect your brain and ensure the best possible recovery.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and emergency contact only. It does not provide diagnosis or long-term medical management.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and is not a substitute for professional medical assessment. If you notice sudden speech changes, always treat it as a stroke until proven otherwise by a clinician.
