# What to do if… your phone screen is broken and you cannot enter your passcode to unlock your device (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as an account-access problem first. From another trusted device, secure your email, Apple or Google account, banking access, and mobile number before you try resets or repairs.

## Do not do these things
- Do not factory reset or erase the phone as a first move; it can remove data and make app recovery harder.
- Do not keep guessing passcodes or PINs; repeated wrong attempts can trigger lockout delays.
- Do not share your phone passcode, banking PIN, one-time codes, or recovery codes with a repair shop or anyone offering help.
- Do not accept help from anyone who contacts you first by call, text, email, pop-up, or social media saying they can fix account access.
- Do not remove two-step verification in a panic unless you already have another safe sign-in method working.

## What to do now
1. **Work out whether the phone is only broken or might be lost**
   - If the phone is with you, keep it powered on, charged, and somewhere safe.
   - If the phone is lost, stolen, or no longer under your control, prioritise Step 4 and Step 6.

2. **Secure the accounts that unlock everything else**
   - On a trusted laptop, desktop, tablet, or spare phone, try to sign in to your primary email.
   - Check that your recovery email, recovery phone number, and signed-in devices are still yours.
   - Do the same for your Apple Account or Google Account.
   - If the phone is missing, or you see sign-ins you do not recognise, change the password from the trusted device and sign out of other sessions where the account offers that option.
   - Save any recovery codes only somewhere private that is not on the broken phone.

3. **Protect banking and payment access**
   - Use online banking on a computer, or call the bank using the number on the back of your card or on the bank’s official website.
   - Review recent transactions.
   - Freeze or temporarily block cards if anything looks wrong.
   - Lower payment or transfer limits if your bank offers that option.
   - Tell the bank you are temporarily locked out of app authentication.
   - If you receive a suspicious financial call and are unsure whether it is genuine, hang up and dial 159 to reach many UK banks safely. It works for participating banks and through participating phone providers; if it does not connect, use the number on the back of your card or the bank’s official website.

4. **Get control of your mobile number**
   - If your SIM card is accessible and you have a trusted spare phone, move the SIM into that phone so you can receive calls or texts.
   - If you use an eSIM, the SIM is damaged, or the phone is lost or stolen, contact your mobile network from another phone and ask about a replacement SIM or eSIM.
   - Ask the network what extra account security they can add to reduce unauthorised SIM swaps or number transfers.
   - If the phone is lost or stolen, ask the network to block the SIM.

5. **Try the least-destructive way to enter the passcode once**
   - If the display is visible but touch does not work, ask a reputable repairer whether a temporary screen, keyboard, mouse, or external display method can be used without erasing the phone.
   - Some Android phones may accept a USB or Bluetooth keyboard or mouse, depending on the model, settings, and lock-screen state.
   - On iPhone, mouse or trackpad control usually depends on accessibility settings that normally need to have been enabled already, so it may not help at the lock screen.
   - If Face ID, Touch ID, or fingerprint unlock still works, use that moment to open critical apps, back up recovery codes, and add another trusted sign-in method where the app allows it.

6. **If the phone is lost, stolen, or showing suspicious prompts, use official remote-lock tools**
   - For Android, use Google Find Hub from another device to find, lock, secure, or erase the phone if the service is available for that device.
   - For iPhone, use Find My or iCloud Find Devices to find the phone, mark it as lost, or erase it.
   - Choose erase only when you accept that local phone data may be removed and you can still sign in to the same Apple or Google account afterwards.

7. **If you cannot physically unlock the phone**
   - For Android, official guidance says that if you cannot unlock the device, you may need to erase it, set it up again, and restore what is available from backups.
   - For iPhone, Apple’s supported reset routes can include resetting from the device on newer iOS versions or restoring with a computer; these processes erase the data currently on the iPhone.
   - Before resetting, confirm from another device that you can sign in to your Apple or Google account and access the recovery email or phone number.

8. **If fraud may already have happened**
   - Contact your bank first using a trusted route.
   - For England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, report fraud or cyber crime through Report Fraud.
   - If you live in Scotland, or the incident happened in Scotland, contact Police Scotland; use 999 only if there is immediate danger or a crime is happening now.
   - Write down what happened, when you noticed, which accounts were affected, and what you changed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to upgrade your phone, change provider, replace every password, or rebuild your whole security setup.
- Non-urgent app recoveries, shopping apps, and social media can wait until your email, Apple or Google account, banking access, and mobile number are stable.
- Repair, insurance, data recovery, and longer-term security improvements can wait until the immediate access risk is calmer.

## Important reassurance
A broken screen can feel like being locked out of your life, but the safest order is simple: protect the key accounts, protect money, regain your mobile number, then deal with the device. You do not have to fix every app at once.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce loss and regain basic access. Later decisions about repairs, insurance, data recovery, fraud disputes, or account recovery may need specialist help from the relevant company, bank, network provider, repairer, or official reporting service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, security, or professional advice. Follow the instructions of your bank, mobile network, device maker, and official reporting services for your specific situation.
