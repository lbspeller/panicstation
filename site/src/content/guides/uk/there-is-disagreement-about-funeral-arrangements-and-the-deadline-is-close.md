# What to do if… there is disagreement about funeral arrangements and the deadline is close (UK)

## Short answer
Notify the funeral director and mortuary in writing immediately to place a "dispute hold" on all irreversible actions while legal entitlement is confirmed. In the UK, the final decision rests with the Executor (if there is a Will) or the person with the highest legal priority to administer the estate (if there is no Will).

## Do not do these things
- Do not proceed with cremation, burial, or water cremation "to keep the peace" if you believe the instructions are legally invalid.
- Do not assume "next of kin" has a specific legal meaning; authority depends on the Will or the strict order of priority under probate laws.
- Do not sign any "Application for Cremation" or "Notice of Interment" if you are not the legally entitled person.
- Do not remove the deceased's documents, Will, or valuables from their property without the presence or consent of all potential executors.
- Do not attempt to "self-help" by physically moving the body or obstructing a funeral service without a court order.

## What to do now
1. **Instruct the funeral director to pause all irreversible steps.**
   - **Send a written notice (email followed by a call) stating there is a formal dispute regarding the right to instruct.**
   - Request an immediate "hold" on cremation, burial, or any transportation of the body out of the local area.
   - Ask the funeral director what paperwork they currently hold and who signed the contract for services.

2. **Establish the legal decision-maker based on your location.**
   - **In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:**
     - If there is a Will, the named **Executor(s)** have the legal right to decide.
     - If there is no Will, the person(s) with the highest priority to apply for "Letters of Administration" have the right (Spouse/Civil Partner, then adult Children, then Parents, then Siblings).
   - **In Scotland:**
     - The "Burial and Cremation (Scotland) Act 2016" provides a statutory list: 1. Spouse/Civil Partner, 2. Cohabiting Partner (6+ months), 3. Children, 4. Parents, 5. Siblings.
     - If an Executor is named in a Will, they generally take precedence.

3. **Verify the existence and validity of the Will.**
   - **Search for the original Will at the deceased's home, with their solicitor, or via a national Will search service.**
   - If a Will is found, identify all named Executors; if there are multiple Executors, they must ideally act together, or one must formally "power reserve" or renounce.

4. **Identify "Must Decide" vs "Can Wait" items.**
   - **Focus only on the current location of the body and the "hold" status.**
   - Categorise disputed items like the type of service, burial vs cremation, or location of the wake as "deferred" until authority is confirmed.

5. **Engage with the Coroner or Procurator Fiscal if applicable.**
   - **Check if a Certificate of Cause of Death has been issued or if the death is under investigation.**
   - If the Coroner (E/W/NI) or Procurator Fiscal (Scotland) is involved, notify them of the dispute; they may delay releasing the body for the funeral if entitlement is unclear.

6. **Seek a temporary injunction if a funeral is imminent.**
   - **Contact a specialist contentious probate solicitor to apply for an emergency injunction to stop a burial or cremation.**
   - In England and Wales, this may involve an application under Section 116 of the Senior Courts Act 1981 to "pass over" a person's right to act if there are special circumstances.

7. **Communicate the pause to all parties.**
   - **Send a neutral message: "There is currently a disagreement regarding the legal authority to plan the funeral. For everyone's protection, the funeral director has been asked to pause all irreversible steps until authority is verified."**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on the order of service, music, or readings now.
- You do not need to decide on the scattering of ashes or the wording of a headstone now.
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family grievances or determine "who loved them more" right now.
- You do not need to pay the full funeral bill while the contract holder is in dispute.

## Important reassurance
Disagreements during bereavement are common and often intensified by shock and exhaustion. Taking a few days to verify legal authority is a standard procedure intended to prevent errors that cannot be corrected later. This pause is a logistical step, not a personal judgment on any family member.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate 24–48 hours of a funeral dispute. It does not cover long-term mediation, the permanent settling of estates, or complex international repatriation disputes.

## Important note
Funeral law is highly technical and differs significantly between Scotland and the rest of the UK. This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. If a burial or cremation is scheduled within the next 24 hours, you should contact an emergency legal service immediately.
