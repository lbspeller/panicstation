# What to do if… you are told you are being investigated for a data breach (UK)

## Short answer
Do not agree to the change immediately; request the proposal and the specific business reason in writing to check if your contract allows for "short-time working" or "lay-offs."

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign immediately or stop attending shifts, as this may be treated as a voluntary resignation and impact your right to benefits or redundancy pay.
- Do not give verbal consent to a permanent contract change if you only intend to help out for a single week.
- Do not sign any variation of contract letter without keeping an identical, dated copy for your own records.
- Do not assume that "temporary" has a legal time limit; without a written end date or review trigger, the reduction could last indefinitely.
- Do not rely on informal verbal assurances that your hours will return to normal soon.

## What to do now
1. **Request the full details of the proposed reduction in writing from your manager or HR today.** You need to know the exact number of hours or shifts being cut, the start date, and specifically what event or date will trigger the return to your original hours.
2. **Review your written employment contract and staff handbook for a "short-time working" or "lay-off" clause.** This clause determines if your employer has the legal right to reduce your work and pay without your specific consent during a business downturn.
   - Look for terms like "variation of hours," "flexibility," or "employer's right to lay off."
3. **Send a written holding response stating that you are reviewing the proposal but are not yet agreeing to a permanent contract change.** Explicitly state that you remain "ready, willing, and available" to work your full contractual hours while you seek advice.
4. **Clarify with your employer whether they are invoking a specific contractual right or asking you to voluntarily vary your contract.** Ask directly: "Is this a formal period of short-time working under my contract, or are you proposing a mutual change to my terms of employment?"
5. **Request a formal consultation meeting to discuss the impact of the reduction and bring a trade union representative or colleague if possible.** Take detailed notes during the meeting and follow up immediately with an email summarizing what was discussed and any deadlines mentioned.
6. **Determine if you are eligible for Statutory Guarantee Pay (SGP) for days where no work is provided.** If you are an employee with at least one month of service and you are provided with no work on a day you normally work, you are entitled to a statutory rate.
   - The rate from 6 April 2026 is £41 per day.
   - You can claim this for a maximum of 5 days in any 3-month period (maximum £205).
7. **Calculate if your reduced pay falls below the threshold for a statutory redundancy claim.** If you are put on short-time working (earning less than half a week's pay) for 4 consecutive weeks, or 6 weeks in a 13-week period, you may be able to resign and claim statutory redundancy pay.
   - This typically requires 2 years of continuous service.
   - You must serve a "Notice of Intention to Claim" (NIC) in writing to your employer within 4 weeks of the short-time period ending.
8. **Contact ACAS or your trade union to verify your specific rights before you take any action that could end your employment.** If the hours reduction is significant, check your eligibility for Universal Credit to top up your income through the "Work Allowance" or "Earnings Threshold" rules.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to look for a new job or resign today.
- You do not need to prove the employer's financial situation is "fake" right now; focus on the contractual authority they are claiming.
- You do not need to finalize a long-term budget until you have the written proposal and know your eligibility for top-up benefits.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed and anxious when your income is suddenly threatened without a clear end date. Taking a few days to check your contract and get everything in writing is a standard professional response, not a sign of being "difficult."

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for UK employees facing sudden hours reductions. It does not cover the specifics of Employment Tribunal claims, which involve strict 3-month (less one day) or 6-month time limits depending on the claim type and jurisdiction.

## Important note
Employment laws regarding redundancy and contract variations differ between Great Britain (England, Scotland, Wales) and Northern Ireland, particularly regarding the "week's pay" cap which is higher in Northern Ireland (£783 vs £751 from April 2026). This guide is for general information and should not be treated as legal advice. Always consult ACAS, a solicitor, or a qualified advisor before triggering a redundancy claim or signing a new contract.
