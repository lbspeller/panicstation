# What to do if… you are waiting for a body to be released and need to plan next steps (UK)

## Short answer
Get one clear point of contact and one clear release plan today: who currently has the person, what must happen before release, and which funeral director should collect them once release is authorised.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t book a fixed funeral date, or pay non-refundable costs, until the funeral director confirms the person can be collected and the paperwork route is clear.
- Don’t assume “released later” means today; ask what the release depends on.
- Don’t have several people calling different offices at once. Choose one caller and one backup.
- Don’t feel pressured by unsolicited callers or messages offering funeral, legal, or claims help. Pause and verify independently.
- Don’t discard, wash, move, or alter anything that could matter if police, coroner, or procurator fiscal enquiries are ongoing. Ask the named contact before changing anything.

## What to do now
1. **Write the essentials in one place.** Note the person’s full name, date of birth, last address, where they died, who told you release would be later, and any reference or case number.

2. **Find out who is handling release.** Ask which person or office currently controls the release decision:
   - the hospital bereavement office, mortuary, clinician, or named contact who told you about the delay, if there is no death investigation;
   - the coroner’s office, in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland;
   - the procurator fiscal or COPFS contact, in Scotland.

3. **Ask three direct questions and write down the answers.**
   - “What still needs to happen before release can be authorised?”
   - “Who will you release the person to?”
   - “What is the earliest realistic timescale, and how will we be told?”

4. **Choose a funeral director now, without choosing every funeral detail.** Tell them you are waiting for release authorisation and ask them to be ready to collect once they are permitted. Give them the office contact details and any reference number.

5. **If cremation is possible, say so early without committing.** Cremation paperwork can depend on where the death happened, where cremation will take place, and whether a coroner or procurator fiscal is involved. Ask the funeral director and the relevant office what form or authorisation is needed before booking a crematorium time.

6. **Ask whether any temporary document is available while you wait.** If registration is delayed by an investigation or inquest, ask whether an interim death certificate, or the local equivalent document, can be issued. This may help with urgent practical admin, but it is not the same as completing the final registration process.

7. **Make a soft plan only.**
   - Ask the funeral director whether they can hold 2 or 3 provisional options.
   - Choose one family spokesperson and one backup.
   - Prepare one short update to reuse: “We are waiting for release authorisation and will confirm arrangements as soon as we can.”

8. **Set a spending pause.** Use this rule for today: no decision that costs money or cannot be changed unless the funeral director confirms it is safe to commit.

## What can wait
- Choosing readings, music, flowers, catering, order of service, or a final attendance list.
- Posting a public announcement or obituary.
- Sorting belongings, probate, estate decisions, or major housing decisions.
- Deciding what the “right” funeral looks like.
- Registering or final paperwork decisions, unless the coroner, procurator fiscal, registrar, bereavement office, or funeral director asks you for something specific now.

## Important reassurance
This waiting period can feel urgent and stuck at the same time. You do not need to solve everything now. The useful job today is to make sure the right people have each other’s details and that collection can happen smoothly once release is authorised.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the waiting period before release. Later decisions about registration, funeral arrangements, the estate, complaints, legal questions, or financial matters may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes vary across the UK and by the circumstances of the death, so ask the coroner’s office, procurator fiscal contact, bereavement office, registrar, or funeral director to confirm what applies before committing to dates or costs.
