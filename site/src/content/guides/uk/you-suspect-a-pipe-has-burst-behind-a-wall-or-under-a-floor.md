# What to do if… you suspect a pipe has burst behind a wall or under a floor (UK)

## Short answer
Turn off your water at the main stop tap or stopcock, then keep people away from any wet electrics. Once the water is isolated and it is safe to do so, call the right repair contact and start limiting water damage.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore an active or growing leak to see if it dries out.
- Don’t keep the boiler, central heating, or immersion heater running if you suspect a burst or major leak.
- Don’t touch sockets, switches, light fittings, or appliances in a wet area.
- Don’t walk through standing water to reach the consumer unit, fuse box, or stop tap.
- Don’t cut into walls or floors in a panic; you could hit electrics, worsen flooding, or damage pipes further.
- Don’t force a stuck stop tap or stopcock.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and check for immediate hazards.** If water is pooling near sockets, lights, appliances, the consumer unit, or the fuse box, keep people and pets out of that area.

2. **Shut off the water at the main stop tap or stopcock.**
   - Common locations include under the kitchen sink, near where the water pipe enters the property, under the stairs, in a utility room, in a cellar, in a garage, or near the front door.
   - Turn it clockwise to close.
   - In a flat or shared building, there may be a shared stop valve where the supply enters the building, and sometimes another one where it enters your flat.

3. **Turn off the boiler, central heating, and immersion heater if you can do this safely.** Do not restart them until the leak is controlled and the system has been checked or refilled if needed.

4. **Make electrics safer only if you can do it without going near water.**
   - If you can reach the consumer unit or fuse box safely, switch off the affected circuit or the main power.
   - If water may have reached the consumer unit, fuse box, wiring, sockets, or light fittings, stay back and contact a registered electrician or emergency repair service.
   - Do not turn power back on just because the visible water has stopped.

5. **Relieve pressure cautiously.**
   - Once the main water is off, open cold taps briefly to reduce pressure and drain some water away from the leak.
   - Do not open hot taps if you suspect frozen pipes, have a hot water cylinder and are unsure, or do not understand the system.
   - If you are unsure, leave the taps alone and wait for a plumber.

6. **Limit water damage while staying safe.**
   - Put towels, bowls, or buckets under drips or where water is emerging.
   - Move rugs, documents, electronics, and furniture away from the wet area if you can do so safely.
   - If water is coming through a ceiling, place a bucket under the lowest point and keep away from any bulging, sagging, or electrically fitted area.

7. **Warn anyone who may be affected.** If you are in a flat, terraced house, semi-detached house, or any building with neighbours below or next door, tell them quickly because water can travel through floors, ceilings, and shared walls.

8. **Call the right repair contact.**
   - If you rent, contact your landlord, agent, housing association, or council repairs line and say there may be an active burst pipe or hidden leak.
   - If you own, contact a WaterSafe-approved plumber or another qualified emergency plumber.
   - If the stop tap will not turn or the water is not isolating, say clearly that the water cannot be turned off.
   - If electrics may be affected, ask for a registered electrician before anything is switched back on.

9. **Record only what is quick and safe.** Take a few photos or a short video of damp patches, ceilings, flooring, and visible water, then return to safety and containment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether walls or floors should be opened.
- You do not need to choose the final repair contractor or drying company immediately.
- You do not need to fully dry everything before the leak is isolated.
- You do not need perfect evidence for insurance now; a few clear photos, times, and notes are enough for the first moment.
- You do not need to decide who is legally responsible for the pipe until the immediate leak and electrical risk are under control.

## Important reassurance
A hidden leak feels alarming because you cannot see the source, but the first useful moves are simple: stop the water, avoid wet electrics, warn anyone affected, and get qualified help. Doing those things buys time and reduces the chance of avoidable damage or injury.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and reduce immediate risk. Repairs, drying, mould prevention, responsibility for pipework, landlord issues, and insurance decisions may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This is general information for immediate harm prevention. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, electrical, plumbing, insurance, or other professional advice.
