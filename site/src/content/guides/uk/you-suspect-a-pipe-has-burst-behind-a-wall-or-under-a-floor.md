# What to do if… you suspect a pipe has burst behind a wall or under a floor (UK)

## Short answer
Shut off the main water supply at the internal stopcock immediately and switch off the electricity at the consumer unit if water is anywhere near electrical fittings. Open the lowest cold tap in the property to drain the system and reduce pressure.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait to see if a damp patch dries out; hidden leaks can cause structural collapse or electrical fires within minutes or hours.
- Don’t touch any light switches, sockets, or appliances if the wall or floor is damp.
- Don’t attempt to "test" the leak by turning the water back on until a professional has inspected the pipe.
- Don’t use a hairdryer or heater to dry out walls while the leak is still active or the power is on.
- Don’t force a stuck stopcock with a tool; if it will not budge, move to the external shut-off point.

## What to do now
1. **Locate and close your internal stopcock immediately.** 
   - This is usually found under the kitchen sink, in a hallway cupboard, under the stairs, or in a garage.
   - **Turn the handle clockwise until it stops** to cut off the rising main.
2. **Identify and avoid electrical hazards.**
   - If water is dripping from a light fitting or pooling near sockets, **do not enter the room**.
   - **Switch off the "Main Switch" at your consumer unit (fuse box)** if you can reach it safely without stepping on wet flooring or touching wet walls.
3. **Open the lowest cold water tap in the property.**
   - This is usually the kitchen tap or an outside garden tap.
   - **Opening this tap breaks the vacuum in the pipes**, allowing water to drain out of the system and into the sink rather than through the burst.
4. **Turn off the boiler and central heating.**
   - Switch the system off at the control panel or the dedicated fused spur switch. 
   - **Do not run the heating** while the water is isolated, as it can damage the heat exchanger or pump.
5. **Check the external stopcock if the internal one fails.**
   - If your internal valve is stuck or missing, look outside near the boundary of your property for a small plastic or metal cover marked "W" or "Water".
   - **You may need a stopcock key** or a pair of pliers to turn the valve inside this chamber.
6. **Notify neighbours immediately if you live in a flat.**
   - Water travels horizontally along floor joists and vertically through service ducts. 
   - **Alert those below or adjacent** so they can check their own ceilings and electrics.
7. **Contact a WaterSafe-approved emergency plumber.**
   - Use the official WaterSafe registry to find a qualified professional who is insured and certified for UK water regulations.
   - **State clearly if you have been unable to isolate the water supply.**
8. **Contain the visible spread of water.**
   - Place buckets under drips and use towels to prevent water from reaching other rooms.
   - **If a ceiling is bulging**, place a bucket underneath and do not stand directly under the bulge.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact point of the burst yourself; a plumber will use leak detection equipment.
- You do not need to pull up carpets or cut into plasterboard right now.
- You do not need to call your insurance company until the immediate water flow has stopped and the property is safe.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when water is hidden behind walls. By turning off the stopcock and the electricity, you have already taken the two most critical steps to prevent the situation from escalating. The remaining water in the pipes will eventually stop draining once the system is empty.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a suspected hidden water leak. It does not cover permanent plumbing repairs, structural drying, or mould remediation, which require specialist equipment and certification.

## Important note
If water has entered electrical fittings, do not restore power until a qualified electrician has inspected the circuits. This guide is for emergency information only and does not replace the advice of a certified professional on-site.
