# What to do if… a cheque you deposited is returned unpaid and your balance drops (UK)

## Short answer
Stop spending from the account and contact your bank before using it again. Ask them to confirm the unpaid cheque, the amount removed, any charges or interest, and what you can do now to stop more payments being refused.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the money is safe just because it showed as available; an unpaid cheque can still lead to the credit being removed.
- Don’t keep making card payments, direct debits, or standing orders without checking the available balance first.
- Don’t send money back to anyone who is pressuring you to “refund” an overpayment, especially if you do not know them well.
- Don’t try to re-present the original paper cheque yourself if your bank uses cheque imaging; ask your bank what their process is.
- Don’t take high-cost emergency credit in a panic before asking your bank about charges, temporary options, and hardship support.

## What to do now
1. **Pause spending and check the available balance.**  
   Look for entries such as “returned unpaid”, “unpaid cheque”, “returned item”, “refer to drawer”, or an overdraft fee or interest line. Save screenshots showing the date, amount, and wording.

2. **Contact your bank through the app, phone number on your card, or another official route.**  
   Ask:
   - What reason was given for the cheque being returned unpaid?
   - What exact amount has been removed, including any fees or interest?
   - Are any direct debits, standing orders, card payments, rent, mortgage, or bills due today or tomorrow likely to fail?
   - What is the quickest way to bring the account back to £0 or within an arranged overdraft limit?

3. **Ask the bank for immediate damage-limiting help.**  
   If the unpaid cheque pushed you into an unarranged overdraft or caused charges, ask whether they can waive, refund, pause, or reduce any charges. If you are in financial difficulty, say that clearly and ask what short-term support is available.

4. **Cover the shortfall only in the safest way you have.**  
   If you can, move money from savings or another account, or ask a trusted person for enough to stabilise the balance. The aim is to reduce further charges and stop essential payments being refused.

5. **Protect essential payments due soon.**  
   Check rent, mortgage, council tax, utilities, childcare, insurance, and other essential payments due in the next couple of days. If one may fail, contact that organisation before it happens and ask for a short hold, new payment date, or different way to pay.

6. **Contact the person or business who gave you the cheque, unless it feels unsafe or scam-like.**  
   Say only that the cheque was returned unpaid and ask for replacement payment. Keep messages, emails, letters, and any cheque-return notice from the bank.

7. **Treat pressure to refund money as a possible scam.**  
   Red flags include a stranger, an overpayment, urgency, a request to send money elsewhere, or a changing story. Do not send money back until your own bank has checked what happened and told you what is safe.

8. **If the bank will not explain or you think charges are unfair, make a formal complaint.**  
   Ask for a written breakdown of the return reason, the amount removed, and any charges. If you are unhappy with the bank’s final response, or the bank has not replied within the usual complaint timescale, you can ask the Financial Ombudsman Service whether it can help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to take legal action against the cheque writer.
- You do not need to argue about blame before you know the bank’s recorded reason for the unpaid cheque.
- You do not need to write a perfect complaint today; save the evidence and stabilise the account first.
- You do not need to replace every missed payment at once; focus first on essentials and payments due soonest.

## Important reassurance
This is a common and frightening shock, especially when the balance had looked usable. Your first job is not to solve everything today; it is to stop further spending, find out the bank’s reason, and prevent avoidable fees or missed essentials.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the account, prevent knock-on charges, and keep a clear record. Later decisions about complaints, debt, fraud, or recovering money from the cheque writer may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not financial, legal, debt, fraud, or professional advice. Bank policies, fees, overdraft rules, and complaint handling can vary. If anyone is pressuring you to move money quickly, pause and verify directly with your bank through an official channel.
