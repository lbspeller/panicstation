# What to do if… a cheque you deposited is returned unpaid and your balance drops (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all outgoing payments from the account immediately and contact your bank to confirm the return reason and establish a temporary buffer to avoid further interest or refused payment fees.

## Do not do these things
- Don't assume the funds were permanent just because they appeared in your "available balance" — cheques are only fully cleared after the clearing cycle completes.
- Don't continue using your debit card or allowing Direct Debits to leave the account if the balance is now negative.
- Don't send any "refunds" or "change" to the person who gave you the cheque; this is the primary mechanism of cheque overpayment fraud.
- Don't try to scan or deposit the physical cheque again yourself if your bank uses digital imaging, as this creates a "duplicate presentment" error.
- Don't ignore the situation; unarranged overdraft interest is calculated daily and will accumulate.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact available balance and pending transactions.**  
   **Log in to your banking app or use an ATM to see your "Available Funds" rather than your "Current Balance."** Identify any upcoming Direct Debits or Standing Orders due within the next 48 hours that may now fail.
2. **Contact your bank's customer service or fraud department.**  
   **Ask the representative these four specific procedural questions to clarify your position:**
   - What is the specific "Reason Code" or wording given for the return (e.g., "Refer to Drawer" or "Represent")?
   - What is the exact total of the reversed credit and any associated fees or interest charges?
   - Will the bank provide a "grace period" (usually until 2:30 PM or 3:30 PM) to pay in funds before further interest is applied?
   - Can the bank temporarily increase your arranged overdraft limit to cover the shortfall and prevent other payments from being refused?
3. **Request a waiver of fees and interest.**  
   **Inform the bank if this is a one-off occurrence or if you have been a victim of a scam.** Under FCA "Consumer Duty" rules (2026), banks must act to prevent foreseeable harm; ask if they can waive the returned item fee or the daily interest associated with the unarranged balance.
4. **Isolate and preserve the original cheque if you still have it.**  
   **Keep the physical cheque and any paper "Return Advice" letters sent by the bank.** If the bank has provided a digital image only, take a screenshot of the notification, as this is required for any future dispute or police report.
5. **Contact the cheque issuer for an alternative payment method.**  
   **Notify the person or business that the cheque was returned and request a "Faster Payment" bank transfer instead.** Do not accept another cheque from the same source if the first was returned for "Insufficient Funds" or "Refer to Drawer."
6. **Move funds from savings to stabilise the account.**  
   **Prioritise bringing the account balance back to £0 or within your arranged limit as quickly as possible.** This stops the accrual of daily unarranged interest, which is the most significant cost in these scenarios.
7. **Report potential fraud to Action Fraud.**  
   **If the cheque was from someone you do not know well or involved an "overpayment" request, report it immediately.** Use the Action Fraud online reporting tool or call 0300 123 2040 to get a crime reference number.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to initiate a formal legal claim (Small Claims Court) against the issuer today; focus on bank stabilization first.
- You do **not** need to submit a formal complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service yet; you must allow the bank 15 days to respond to payment-related complaints first.
- You do **not** need to determine whose fault the return was (e.g., a technical error vs. lack of funds) until you have the bank's written explanation.

## Important reassurance
Cheque returns are a standard banking procedure and do not automatically mean you have done something wrong or that your credit score is permanently ruined. While the sudden drop in balance is startling, most banks have established protocols to help customers resolve these shortfalls if they are contacted immediately.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and harm reduction for UK retail bank accounts only. It does not provide legal advice for debt recovery or detailed instructions for complex business-to-business cheque disputes.

## Important note
This information reflects UK banking standards as of 2026. Different banks have different "cut-off" times for clearing and different interest rates for unarranged borrowing. Always verify specific terms with your provider’s current tariff of charges.
