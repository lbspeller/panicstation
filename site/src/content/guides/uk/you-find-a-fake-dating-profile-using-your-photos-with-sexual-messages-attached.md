# What to do if… you find a fake dating profile using your photos with sexual messages attached (UK)

## Short answer
Do not message the account; instead, report the profile directly through the app's internal tools and save a single set of screenshots as evidence before disengaging.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send a message or a "friend request" to the impersonator to confront them.
- Don’t pay any money, cryptocurrency, or gift cards to someone promising to delete the profile.
- Don’t post a public link to the fake profile on your own social media, as this often increases traffic to the harmful content.
- Don’t share your own login credentials with "recovery services" claiming they can hack and delete the account.
- Don’t repeatedly refresh or check the profile for updates once you have recorded the evidence.
- Don’t delete your own original photos from your device, as you may need them to prove your identity to the platform.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritise immediate physical safety.** If you believe the person behind the profile is nearby or if you are receiving threats of immediate physical violence, call **999**.
2. **Collect a single evidence set and then stop.** Take screenshots that clearly show:
   - the profile name, handle, and all photos used
   - any sexual or suggestive text in the "bio" or "about me" section
   - the date and the specific app or website URL where the profile is hosted  
   - **Store these in a secure folder and do not share them publicly.**
3. **Submit an official report through the app or website.** Use the **Report / Impersonation** function within the platform. 
   - Note that under the 2026 Online Safety Act updates, platforms are legally required to remove non-consensual intimate imagery within 48 hours of a valid report.
4. **Use a regulated escalation service if the profile remains active.** If the platform has not responded or removed the profile after 48 hours, contact **Report Harmful Content** for specialist assistance in escalating the takedown request.
5. **Contact a specialist helpline if intimate or deepfake images are involved.** 
   - If the profile uses real intimate images of you: contact the **Revenge Porn Helpline** (UK) for removal support.
   - If the images are AI-generated deepfakes: these are now criminalised under the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025; the Revenge Porn Helpline can also assist with these.
   - If you have the original intimate image: use **StopNCII.org** to create a "digital fingerprint" that helps participating platforms (like Bumble and Meta) block re-uploads.
6. **Follow child safety protocols if anyone in the images is under 18.** If the profile contains images of a minor, do not save or share them. Report immediately to the **Internet Watch Foundation (IWF)** and notify the police via **999** (if in danger) or **101**.
7. **Secure your existing online presence.**
   - Change your privacy settings on Instagram, Facebook, and other social media to "Private."
   - Limit who can see your profile pictures or send you direct messages.
8. **Involve the police for harassment or stalking.** If the impersonation is part of a pattern of stalking, threats, or unwanted contact from strangers at your home or workplace, report it via **101** or the online reporting tool of your local force.
9. **Report financial scams if the profile is soliciting money.** 
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland: report the profile to the **Report Fraud** service (formerly Action Fraud).
   - In Scotland: report via **101** to **Police Scotland**.
10. **Seek emotional stabilisation if needed.** If the discovery has left you unable to settle or feel safe, contact **Samaritans** by calling **116 123** for a confidential, non-judgmental space to talk.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to find out exactly who created the profile right now.
- You do **not** need to explain the situation to your entire social circle today.
- You do **not** need to decide on a long-term legal strategy or whether to press charges in this moment.

## Important reassurance
Impersonation and the attachment of sexual messages are common tactics used to cause distress or commit fraud. Feeling a sense of shock, violation, or panic is a standard physiological response to this situation. These reactions do not indicate a failure to handle the crisis; they are typical responses to a breach of privacy.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and takedown procedures only. Ongoing harassment, complex legal issues, or workplace impacts may require consultation with legal professionals or specialist support services.

## Important note
This information is for guidance in the immediate aftermath of discovering an impersonation profile. It does not constitute legal or professional advice. Contact 999 if you are in immediate danger.
