# What to do if… you start vomiting repeatedly and cannot keep fluids down (UK)

## Short answer
If you keep being sick and cannot keep fluid down, get urgent medical advice now: use 111 in England, 111 Wales in Wales, NHS 24 111 in Scotland, or your GP or GP out-of-hours service in Northern Ireland. While you arrange help, try tiny, frequent sips of fluid unless poisoning is possible or you have been told not to drink.

## Do not do these things
- Do not gulp large drinks to catch up; this can trigger more vomiting.
- Do not force yourself or anyone else to vomit.
- If poisoning is possible, do not give food, drink, or counteracting remedies unless 999, 111, or a clinician tells you to.
- If you also have diarrhoea, do not have fruit juice or fizzy drinks; they can make diarrhoea worse.
- Do not make baby formula weaker.
- Do not give children under 12 medicine to stop diarrhoea.
- Do not give aspirin to children under 16.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E or an emergency department if you feel faint, confused, very weak, or are actively vomiting; ask someone to drive you or call 999.

## What to do now
1. Check for emergency red flags. Call 999 or go to A&E or an emergency department now if you or the person who is vomiting:
   - vomits blood, or vomit that looks like coffee grounds
   - has green vomit as an adult, or yellow-green or green vomit as a child
   - may have swallowed, touched, or breathed in something harmful
   - has a stiff neck and pain when looking at bright lights
   - has a sudden, severe headache
   - has a sudden, severe tummy ache
   - has blue, grey, pale, or blotchy skin, lips, or tongue; on brown or black skin, check the palms of the hands or soles of the feet
   - has severe difficulty breathing, is taking lots of quick short breaths, is confused, is unusually sleepy, or is not responding as usual
2. Get urgent advice now if there is no emergency red flag but you cannot keep fluid down:
   - In England, call 111 or use 111 online for people aged 5 and over; call 111 for a child under 5.
   - In Wales, use 111 Wales online or call 111.
   - In Scotland, call NHS 24 on 111 if your GP practice is closed.
   - In Northern Ireland, contact your GP or GP out-of-hours service; if you cannot get help and you are worried, use urgent and emergency care services.
3. Say this clearly when you ask for help: “I keep being sick and cannot keep fluid down.” Also say if any of these apply:
   - a baby under 12 months is ill
   - a child has stopped breast or bottle feeding
   - a child under 5 has signs of dehydration, such as fewer wet nappies
   - you or a child aged 5 or over still has dehydration signs after oral rehydration sachets
   - there is bloody diarrhoea or bleeding from the bottom
   - vomiting has lasted more than 2 days
4. Start rehydrating in the way your stomach is most likely to tolerate, unless poisoning is possible:
   - Adults can try small sips of water or squash, pause, then repeat.
   - If sips trigger vomiting, try 1 teaspoon every few minutes, then increase slowly if it stays down.
   - If you have oral rehydration sachets, mix them with water exactly as directed.
   - Ask a pharmacist, 111, your GP, or a clinician which oral rehydration solution is right for you or your child.
   - For babies, keep giving breastmilk or formula in smaller amounts more often. Do not dilute formula.
5. Use dehydration signs to decide how urgent this is. Treat it as urgent and follow 111, GP, or emergency advice if there is very dark or strong-smelling pee, peeing much less than usual, dizziness when standing that does not settle, unusual tiredness, drowsiness, confusion, fast breathing, a fast heart rate, dry mouth, sunken eyes, few or no tears in a child, or fewer wet nappies in a baby.
6. Make a short note for 111, your GP, or A&E:
   - when vomiting started
   - how often it is happening
   - whether any fluid stays down
   - the last time you peed
   - any fever, severe pain, diarrhoea, blood, possible pregnancy, poisoning exposure, or recent medicines
   - a list or photo of medicines you take
7. If poisoning might be involved, call 999 or go to A&E or an emergency department now. Keep the packaging, container, plant, medicine packet, or substance nearby to show clinicians if you can do that safely.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to force food; focus on safe fluid replacement first.
- You do not need to decide alone whether this is serious enough; urgent medical advice services can triage you.
- You do not need to clean everything immediately unless vomit or diarrhoea is making the area unsafe. Move away from the mess, wash your hands, and deal with hygiene once help is arranged and you are steadier.

## Important reassurance
Repeated vomiting can feel frightening and out of control. The main immediate risk is dehydration, and asking for help early while taking tiny amounts of fluid is a sensible, protective response.

## Scope note
This guide covers first steps only: reducing immediate risk, checking for emergency signs, and getting to the right level of help. Decisions about tests, medicines, the cause, or ongoing care can come later with a clinician if needed.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are deteriorating, have red-flag symptoms, may have been poisoned, or cannot keep fluids down, use 111, 999, A&E, your GP, GP out-of-hours, or your local urgent medical advice service promptly.
