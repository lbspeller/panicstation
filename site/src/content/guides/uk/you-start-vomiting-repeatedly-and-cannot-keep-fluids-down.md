# What to do if… you start vomiting repeatedly and cannot keep fluids down (UK)

## Short answer
If you cannot keep any fluids down, **call NHS 111 now** for triage. **Call 999 immediately** if you have red-flag symptoms such as vomiting blood, green vomit, a stiff neck with light sensitivity, or signs of severe dehydration like confusion.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drink large amounts of fluid at once; gulping often triggers further vomiting.
- Do not have fruit juice or fizzy drinks, as these can worsen stomach irritation and diarrhoea.
- Do not try to make yourself (or anyone else) vomit.
- Do not take "counteracting" remedies if you suspect poisoning unless instructed by a clinician.
- Do not drive yourself to a hospital if you are feeling faint, dizzy, or are actively vomiting.
- Do not give children under 12 medicine to stop diarrhoea unless a doctor tells you to.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency red-flag symptoms immediately.** Call 999 or go to A&E if you or the person ill:
   - Vomits blood or material that looks like coffee grounds.
   - Has green or yellow-green vomit.
   - Might have swallowed something poisonous (medicines, chemicals, or unknown substances).
   - Has a stiff neck and pain when looking at bright lights.
   - Has a sudden, severe headache or sudden, severe abdominal pain.
   - Shows signs of sepsis (blue, grey, or pale skin/lips; severe breathing difficulty; confusion or unresponsiveness).
2. **Contact NHS 111 for urgent advice if you cannot keep fluids down.** Use 111 online or call 111 and state clearly that you are "vomiting repeatedly and unable to keep any fluid down." 
   - You must also call 111 if: you are worried about a baby under 12 months; a child stops feeding; a child under 5 has dehydration signs; or vomiting has lasted more than 2 days (adults).
3. **Attempt small, frequent rehydration sips while waiting for medical advice.** Use water, diluted squash, or oral rehydration solution (ORS) sachets if available.
   - Take one teaspoon or a tiny sip every 2 to 3 minutes.
   - If a sip triggers vomiting, wait 5 to 10 minutes before trying a smaller amount.
   - For babies: continue breast or bottle feeds; try giving smaller amounts more often than usual.
4. **Monitor for signs of worsening dehydration.** Seek urgent help if you notice:
   - Dark yellow, strong-smelling pee or passing significantly less urine than normal.
   - Persistent dizziness, lightheadedness, or feeling unusually tired.
   - For babies/infants: a sunken soft spot (fontanelle) on the head, crying without producing tears, or fewer wet nappies.
5. **Have critical information ready for the 111 or 999 operator.** This includes:
   - When the vomiting started and how many times it has occurred.
   - If any fluid (even a teaspoon) has stayed down in the last 4 hours.
   - The time of the last urination or wet nappy.
   - Presence of other symptoms like fever, diarrhoea, or rash.
   - A list or photos of any regular medications and any suspected poisons.
6. **Isolate to prevent spreading the infection.** Stay at home and avoid contact with others.
   - Do not return to work or school until at least 48 hours have passed since the last episode of vomiting or diarrhoea.
   - Wash hands frequently with soap and water (alcohol gels do not kill all stomach bugs like norovirus).

## What can wait
- You do not need to eat solid food right now; focus exclusively on fluid intake.
- You do not need to determine the cause of the vomiting (e.g., food poisoning vs. virus) before seeking help.
- You do not need to visit a GP surgery in person, as this risks spreading the infection.

## Important reassurance
Nausea and repeated vomiting are physically exhausting and can cause significant anxiety. Most cases are caused by common stomach bugs that resolve within 24 to 48 hours, but addressing dehydration early through tiny sips and clinical triage is the standard safety protocol.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions for acute vomiting and dehydration risk. It does not provide medical diagnosis or long-term treatment plans.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional medical advice. If your condition deteriorates or you cannot contact 111, seek emergency care.
