# What to do if… someone you rely on for care makes sexual comments and you fear retaliation (UK)

## Short answer
Prioritise immediate safety and continuity of care. Create distance from the person where you can, and tell the care provider and your local adult safeguarding or adult protection route that you need a replacement and are worried about retaliation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront them alone if you fear retaliation, escalation, or loss of essential care.
- Do not agree to sexual comments, secrecy, or contact outside the care arrangement to keep access to care, medication, keys, transport, benefits, or support.
- Do not let them isolate you during visits if you can avoid it, such as by insisting on closed doors, private conversations, or no contact with others.
- Do not assume you have to prove exactly what happened before asking for a different worker.
- Do not push yourself into detailed statements, recording, or evidence-gathering if that would make you feel less safe.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause and protect your immediate care needs.**  
   If you feel in immediate danger, move to where another person is present if you can, such as a neighbour, reception area, shared room, building staff, or nearby family member. Call **999** if you are in immediate danger or need urgent medical help. If it is safer to end the visit, use a simple phrase such as “I’m not well, please leave now,” then contact the provider as soon as you safely can.

2. **Create a no-alone arrangement for the next contact.**  
   If care has to continue before it is changed, ask someone trusted to be present, keep your phone within reach, and avoid private spaces with the person where possible. If visits are scheduled, ask the provider for a different worker, two-person visits, or a manager to attend as an urgent temporary measure.

3. **Tell the care provider and request a replacement.**  
   Contact the agency, organisation, care home manager, direct payment support service, or person who schedules the care. Use simple words, or whatever words feel safest: “I do not feel safe with this person. They made sexual comments. I am afraid of retaliation. I need them removed from my care and a replacement arranged.”  
   Ask for:
   - a different worker, and that this person is not sent again
   - a note on your file that you are worried about retaliation
   - a named manager or safeguarding lead so you do not have to repeat yourself to several people
   - confirmation of how your essential care, medication, meals, washing, transport, or access needs will be covered

4. **Contact the local adult safeguarding or adult protection route.**  
   In England, this is usually through your local council adult safeguarding team. In Wales, you can contact local social services or call 101. In Scotland, this is usually through your local council adult support and protection route. In Northern Ireland, this is usually through the Adult Protection Gateway Service in your local Health and Social Care Trust. Tell them you rely on the care, the person has made sexual comments, and you are worried about retaliation or care being withdrawn. Ask for help with safety planning and alternative care arrangements.

5. **Use confidential sexual violence support if that feels safer than starting with the provider.**  
   If you are aged 16 or over in England or Wales, Rape Crisis England & Wales runs a free 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line by phone and online chat. You do not have to report to the police to speak to them. If you are elsewhere in the UK, ask NHS 111, your GP, a sexual health clinic, a SARC, or a trusted professional to help you find local sexual violence support.

6. **Consider a Sexual Assault Referral Centre if something sexual has happened or you are worried it might.**  
   A SARC can discuss medical care, practical support, and options after rape or sexual assault. You can ask about support without deciding right now whether to report to police.

7. **Make a brief private note only if it is safe.**  
   Write down only what helps you avoid repeating yourself later: date, time, where you were, the short version of what was said or done, and why you fear retaliation, such as control over medication, keys, transport, personal care, food, money, or communication. Keep it somewhere the person cannot access.

8. **If the service is in England, you can also tell the regulator.**  
   The Care Quality Commission can receive concerns about care homes, home-care agencies, and other adult social care services in England. This is an extra step; it does not replace contacting the provider, local safeguarding or adult protection route, or 999 in an emergency.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to report to the police, make a formal complaint, change your whole care plan, or start legal action.
- You do not need to find the perfect label for what happened. It is enough to say that sexual comments were made, you rely on the person for care, and you fear retaliation.
- You do not need to give a detailed statement today. A short account and a clear request for safer care is enough for first steps.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, minimising it, or worrying that you will lose essential help is a common response when the person involved has power over your daily care. Asking for a different worker and telling a safer third party is a reasonable protective step.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise safety and protect your care. Later decisions, such as formal complaints, advocacy, police contact, care plan changes, or legal options, may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If you feel unable to stay safe with the current care arrangement, prioritise contacting the care provider and your local adult safeguarding or adult protection route urgently.
