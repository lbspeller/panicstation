# What to do if… you develop sudden, new shortness of breath (UK)

## Short answer
If breathing is severely difficult, you cannot speak normally, your chest feels tight or painful, your lips or skin look very pale, blue or grey, or you feel confused, drowsy, dizzy, or faint, call 999 or go to A&E immediately. Do not drive yourself. If it is new and worrying but not clearly an emergency, in England, Wales or Scotland, call 111; in England you can also use 111 online. In Northern Ireland, use your GP out-of-hours or local urgent-care route.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait it out if this is new, sudden, or getting worse.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you feel breathless, dizzy, faint, or unwell.
- Do not lie flat if that makes breathing harder.
- Do not breathe into a paper bag for new unexplained breathlessness.
- Do not take someone else’s inhaler or medication.
- Do not take sedatives, sleeping tablets, or alcohol to calm down.
- Do not eat or drink if you are struggling to breathe or swallow.

## What to do now
1. Check for emergency red flags now. Call 999 if any apply:
   - You are gasping, choking, or cannot get words out.
   - Your chest feels tight or heavy, or you have chest pain spreading to your arm, back, neck, or jaw.
   - Your lips or skin look very pale, blue, or grey. On brown or black skin, this may be easier to see on the palms, soles, lips, gums, tongue, or inside the eyelids.
   - You feel suddenly confused, unusually drowsy, dizzy, faint, or you collapse.
2. If you are alone and calling 999, unlock the door if you can do this safely, sit upright near the entrance, keep your phone on speaker, and follow the call handler’s instructions.
3. If it is not clearly 999-level but it is new, sudden, or worrying, in England, Wales or Scotland, call 111. In England, you can also use 111 online. In Northern Ireland, use your GP out-of-hours or local urgent-care route. Say: “I have new sudden shortness of breath.” Mention any chest pain, fever, wheeze, coughing up blood, pain or swelling in one leg, swelling of lips or tongue, rash or hives, dizziness, recent illness, recent travel, or smoke or chemical exposure.
4. Sit upright and reduce effort. Lean slightly forward with your forearms supported on your knees or a table, loosen tight clothing around your neck and chest, and keep sentences short.
5. Use only your own prescribed rescue medicines if you have them. If you have prescribed rescue medicine for a breathing condition, use it only as prescribed or as your personal action plan says. Call 999 if symptoms are getting worse, the plan says to call, you do not have your rescue medicine, or it is not helping.
6. If you have signs of a severe allergic reaction, such as rapid swelling of the lips, tongue or throat, a hoarse voice, widespread hives, or breathing trouble, use your adrenaline auto-injector if prescribed and call 999.
7. Gather essentials for clinicians without overexerting yourself: your medication list or medication boxes, allergies, and key conditions such as asthma, COPD, heart disease, or previous clots. If you can, note the time it started and what changed just before it began.
8. If symptoms ease, do not cancel help without advice. New breathlessness can fluctuate, so continue with 999, 111, local urgent-care, or clinician advice unless they tell you otherwise.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out whether this is heart, lung, allergy, infection, asthma, panic, or something else right now.
- You do not need to measure oxygen, buy equipment, or search symptoms online before getting help.
- You do not need to contact work, school, or several family members beyond one practical message such as: “I’m unwell and getting medical help.”
- You do not need to make long-term decisions about treatment today.

## Important reassurance
Sudden breathlessness can feel frightening, and getting urgent help is a reasonable safety step when it is new for you. You are not wasting anyone’s time by getting checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the initial safe pause. Tests, diagnosis, treatment choices, and follow-up plans should be discussed with a clinician once you have been assessed.

## Important note
This guide provides general safety information, not medical diagnosis or professional medical advice. If you think you may be seriously unwell, call 999 or go to A&E, or use 111 or your local urgent-care route if you are unsure.
