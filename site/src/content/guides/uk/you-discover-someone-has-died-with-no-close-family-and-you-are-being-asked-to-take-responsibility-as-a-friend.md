# What to do if… you discover someone has died with no close family and you are being asked to take responsibility as a friend (UK)

## Short answer
Provide information to officials but do not sign any documents as the "applicant" or "arranger" for the funeral. Clearly state you are a friend and are not taking financial or legal responsibility for the estate.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a contract with a funeral director unless you are prepared to pay the full bill yourself.
- Do not remove money, valuables, or furniture from the home to "keep them safe."
- Do not pay for anything from your personal funds with the expectation of being paid back.
- Do not tell the registrar you are the "next of kin" if you are a friend.
- Do not agree to clear the property or hand back keys to a landlord until the coroner or council has finished their search for a will.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific official currently holding legal responsibility for the body.**  
   - **If the death was sudden or at home, this is likely the Coroner (England, Wales, NI) or the Procurator Fiscal (Scotland).**  
   - **If the death was in a hospital, contact the Bereavement Office or Medical Examiner’s office.**  
   - **Ask for the case reference number and the name of the officer in charge.**

2. **State your legal relationship to the deceased immediately to every agency.**  
   - **Use the phrase: "I am a friend, not a relative or executor, and I am not taking responsibility for the funeral or the estate."**  
   - **Repeat this whenever you are asked to provide information or help with access.**

3. **Secure the premises and pets without taking possession of the property.**  
   - **Ensure windows and doors are locked and leave the keys with the police or the council’s environmental health team.**  
   - **If there are pets, notify the local council’s animal welfare officer or the RSPCA / SSPCA / USPCA immediately.**  
   - **Keep a written log of the date and time you secured the home and who you gave the keys to.**

4. **Provide essential biographical data to help officials find the next of kin.**  
   - **List the person's full name, date of birth, GP surgery, and any known workplaces.**  
   - **Mention any specific names of distant relatives, even if you do not have their contact details.**

5. **Direct the authorities to perform a Public Health Funeral if no family is found.**  
   - **Under Section 46 of the Public Health (Control of Disease) Act 1984, the local council has a duty to arrange burial or cremation if no other arrangements are being made.**  
   - **Ask the coroner’s officer or hospital: "Please refer this case to the local authority's public health funeral team."**

6. **Check your legal status before registering the death as the "informant."**  
   - **In England and Wales, you can register as the "person causing the disposal" (the person arranging the funeral), but this may imply financial liability.**  
   - **Wait for the council to decide if they are taking the case; they will often register the death themselves if they are arranging the public health funeral.**  
   - **In Scotland, you must register within 8 days; in England, Wales, and NI, it is usually 5 days.**

7. **Pass all financial documents and will-related information to the Treasury Solicitor if no kin exists.**  
   - **If there is no will and no family, the estate is "Bona Vacantia" (unclaimed).**  
   - **Tell the officials where you believe the person kept their paperwork, but do not search through it yourself unless requested by a police officer or council official.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a funeral date or type.
- You do not need to notify utility companies or cancel subscriptions yet; the council or "Tell Us Once" service can handle this later.
- You do not need to worry about "unclaimed" property or what happens to the person's belongings.
- You do not need to respond to pressure from landlords or neighbors to "sort things out" immediately.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for hospitals, police, or councils to look for a friend to take charge because it simplifies their process. You are not being "bad" or "unkind" by saying no. By stepping back, you ensure the person receives a dignified public health funeral and that you are protected from complex legal or financial liabilities.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only. It is designed to stabilize the situation and prevent you from accidentally assuming legal or financial burdens. Later decisions regarding the estate may require a probate specialist.

## Important note
This guide is for general information only and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding death registration and public health funerals differ between England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. Always confirm your status with the official handling the case.
