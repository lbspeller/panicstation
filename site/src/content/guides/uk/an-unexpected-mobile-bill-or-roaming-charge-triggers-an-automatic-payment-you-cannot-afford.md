# What to do if… an unexpected mobile bill or roaming charge triggers an automatic payment you cannot afford (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the charges from growing, stop any further automatic payment attempts, then contact your mobile provider the same day to ask for an itemised breakdown, a hold, and an affordable way to deal with the disputed bill.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore it and hope it fixes itself; repeat attempts may add bank charges, provider fees, or reconnection problems.
- Don’t describe your own valid use as fraud if the phone was used by you, even accidentally; use “unexpected charges”, “unclear charges”, or “billing dispute” if that is accurate.
- Don’t close your bank account or cancel your card in a panic before checking whether you can stop the specific Direct Debit or recurring card payment.
- Don’t agree to a new contract, upgrade, or longer minimum term as a quick fix before you have the bill breakdown in writing.
- Don’t keep using mobile data or roaming while you are unsure whether charges are still building.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the mobile charges from growing.**
   - If you are abroad, turn **Data Roaming OFF**, switch on **Airplane Mode**, then turn **Wi-Fi** back on only if you need internet access.
   - If you are in the UK and this is an overage, turn **Mobile Data OFF** and use Wi-Fi until you have checked your allowance and bill limit.
   - If you need the phone for banking codes or urgent calls, keep the number active but avoid mobile data until the provider confirms what is chargeable.

2. **Work out how the automatic payment was taken.**
   - Check your banking app or statement for whether it says **Direct Debit**, **recurring card payment**, **continuous payment authority**, or a one-off card payment.
   - Screenshot the transaction amount, date, merchant name, and reference.
   - Save the mobile account screen showing the bill, usage spike, or roaming charge.

3. **Stop the next automatic payment attempt.**
   - If it is a **Direct Debit**, cancel the Direct Debit in your banking app or ask your bank to cancel it. Tell the mobile provider as well, because cancelling the payment method does not cancel the bill.
   - If the Direct Debit was taken for the wrong amount, on the wrong date, or not in line with the notice you were given, ask your bank about a refund under the **Direct Debit Guarantee**.
   - If it is a **recurring card payment** or **continuous payment authority**, tell your card issuer or bank that you are withdrawing consent and want future payments to that provider stopped.
   - Keep enough records to show that you stopped future payments because the bill is unaffordable or disputed, not because you are trying to disappear.

4. **Contact the mobile provider’s billing team and ask for three things.**
   - Ask for an **itemised bill and usage breakdown** showing dates, times, countries or networks if roaming, and data amounts.
   - Ask for a **temporary hold on late fees, collections activity, or service restriction** while they check the charges and your affordability issue.
   - Ask for an **affordable payment plan or short breathing space** if the bill is partly valid but you cannot pay it all now.

5. **Ask the provider to check the roaming or overage protections on the account.**
   - Ask whether a bill limit, spend cap, roaming block, or out-of-bundle data block was active.
   - Ask whether they sent the required roaming information or alert when the phone started roaming, if roaming is involved.
   - Ask whether they can apply a goodwill credit, adjustment, or backdated bundle if the charge was unexpected, unclear, or caused by settings you did not understand.

6. **Put a limit or block in place before you use mobile data again.**
   - Ask whether your contract can have a mobile bill limit and, if available, set it to £0 above your normal monthly charge or the lowest practical amount.
   - Ask for roaming to be blocked until you deliberately turn it back on.
   - Ask for out-of-bundle data to be blocked or capped where your provider offers this.

7. **Create a small proof pack.**
   - Save screenshots of your plan allowance, bill limit or spend cap settings, roaming messages, usage page, and the bank transaction.
   - Write down the date you noticed the charge, where the phone was being used, and what you did to stop more charges.
   - Keep copies of chat transcripts, complaint references, and emails from the provider.

8. **If the provider does not deal with it clearly, make it a formal complaint.**
   - Say: “I am making a formal complaint about billing and affordability. Please confirm the complaint reference and send me your final response in writing.”
   - Ask which Ofcom-approved ADR scheme the provider belongs to.
   - If the complaint is still unresolved after at least **six weeks**, or if the provider sends a **deadlock letter** sooner, you can usually take it to the relevant ADR scheme.

9. **Protect essential payments for the next few days.**
   - If the payment has left you short for rent, mortgage, food, travel, energy, or childcare, contact your bank and ask whether they can review or waive any overdraft charges linked to this event.
   - If an essential bill is due soon, contact that biller early and ask for a short extension or temporary arrangement.
   - Keep the explanation simple: an unexpected mobile bill payment has left you short while you dispute the charges.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide today whether to switch networks, cancel the contract, upgrade, or change your phone.
- You do **not** need to write a long complaint letter before you have the itemised bill and complaint reference.
- You do **not** need to solve your whole budget today; first stop repeat charges, protect essentials, and get the provider’s position in writing.
- You do **not** need to accept an upgrade or new minimum term to get billing support.

## Important reassurance
A sudden mobile overage or roaming bill can happen quickly, especially when background data, roaming settings, or app updates are involved. The immediate job is not to prove everything at once; it is to stop more charges, stop repeat payment attempts, and move the bill into a documented check.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the money pressure, prevent repeat charges, and start a documented billing dispute. Later decisions about the contract, debt support, switching provider, or a formal escalation can come after the bill is clear.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, debt, or professional advice. Options can vary by provider, contract type, payment method, and whether the account is personal or business. Ask your bank and mobile provider to confirm important decisions in writing before taking irreversible steps.
