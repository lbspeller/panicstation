# What to do if… a basement or retaining wall starts bulging and you fear it may fail (UK)

## Short answer
Evacuate the immediate area and create a physical cordon at least 1.5 times the height of the wall. Report the situation as a "dangerous structure" to your local council Building Control or Building Standards department immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t enter the basement or stand near the base of the wall to inspect cracks or take measurements.
- Don’t attempt to shore up the wall using car jacks, timber props, or DIY bracing, as sudden pressure shifts can trigger collapse.
- Don’t turn off utility meters (gas/electric) if they are located in the basement and you suspect a leak or see structural movement nearby.
- Don’t remove soil from the base of the wall (unloading the toe) or dig behind the wall (increasing hydrostatic pressure).
- Don’t ignore new sounds such as loud bangs, persistent "popping," or the sound of running water behind the masonry.
- Don’t allow vehicles to park or heavy machinery to operate on the ground directly above or behind the bulging wall.

## What to do now
1. **Evacuate and cordon off the danger zone.** Move all people and pets away from the wall, including the area directly above it and the "fall zone" in front of it (typically 1.5 times the wall's height).
2. **Call 999 if collapse appears imminent.** Request the Fire and Rescue Service if the wall is visibly moving, if you hear loud structural cracking, or if a collapse would immediately endanger lives or public highways.
3. **Contact Local Authority Building Control or Building Standards.**
   - Search for your local council's "Dangerous Structures" emergency line; most provide a 24-hour response service.
   - Inform them of the specific location and whether the wall is a basement (supporting a house) or a retaining wall (supporting land).
4. **Isolate the area from vibrations and loads.** Stop the use of heavy appliances (washing machines, tumble dryers) and prevent anyone from walking on the floors directly above the bulging basement section.
5. **Check for utility hazards from a safe distance.**
   - **If you smell gas:** Move away immediately and call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999.
   - **If you see sparks or damaged cables:** Call 105 to reach your local electricity network operator.
6. **Notify neighbours if the wall is on a boundary.** Ensure they are aware of the risk and advise them to keep clear of the affected area on their side of the property.
7. **Document the movement safely.** Take photographs from a distance once you are outside the fall zone to show the extent of the bulge and any fresh cracking for the building surveyor.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause (such as hydrostatic pressure, root damage, or poor drainage) until the structure is stabilized.
- You do not need to contact a private structural engineer until the local authority has conducted their initial safety assessment.
- You do not need to initiate an insurance claim or seek repair quotes while the area is still considered an active collapse risk.

## Important reassurance
Structural movement, especially bulging, is a high-priority event that requires professional intervention. Taking the immediate step of creating distance and notifying the local authority is the standard safety protocol and does not mean a collapse is guaranteed, but it ensures the risk is managed.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and risk reduction. It does not replace a formal structural survey or the legal requirements of the Building Act 1984 (England/Wales) or the Building (Scotland) Act 2003.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only. Only qualified Building Control surveyors or structural engineers can determine if a structure is safe to remain near or if evacuation of the entire property is required.
