# What to do if… your bike brakes feel unreliable during a pre-ride check and you still need to travel (UK)

## Short answer
Do **not** ride on public roads if either brake feels weak, inconsistent, or touches the handlebar when squeezed. Secure the bike and use public transport, a taxi, or walk while you arrange a professional repair.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attempt to ride in traffic while relying on only one working brake.
- Don’t perform high-speed "test stops" on the road to see if the braking power returns.
- Don’t attempt to adjust internal hydraulic systems or complex cable routing if you are under time pressure.
- Don’t ignore "spongy" levers; this often indicates air in hydraulic lines or failing cables that could snap.
- Don’t assume the brakes will "clean themselves" during the ride if they are contaminated with oil or grease.

## What to do now
1. **Accept that the bike is currently unsafe and must not be ridden on the road.** Under the UK Pedal Cycles (Construction and Use) Regulations 1983, you must have two independent, efficient braking systems to ride legally and safely.
2. **Perform a stationary "pull test" on both levers separately.** **Squeeze each lever as hard as you would in an emergency stop while the bike is stationary.**
   - If the lever touches the handlebar, the brake is failed.
   - If the lever feels "mushy" or slowly sinks toward the bar, there is a leak or cable failure.
3. **Check for three simple mechanical oversights that can be fixed instantly.** **Check these only if you can see them without dismantling any parts.**
   - **Wheel security:** Ensure the quick-release levers or thru-axles are fully closed and the wheel is seated straight in the frame.
   - **Brake release lever:** On rim brakes, ensure the small "opening" lever on the caliper is flipped down (closed).
   - **Brake surface contamination:** Look for visible oil, grease, or thick mud on the wheel rims or disc rotors.
4. **Switch to an alternative transport mode immediately to reduce stress.** **Secure the bike at home or a safe location and use the bus, train, or a private hire vehicle (minicab).**
   - Notify your workplace or appointment that you are delayed due to a mechanical safety issue.
5. **Walk the bike if you must move it to a repair shop or secure storage.** **Do not sit on the saddle or coast down hills even if the road looks clear.**
   - Use your feet to stop the bike if it begins to roll while you are walking it.
6. **Identify the specific symptom to tell a mechanic later.** **Observe whether the issue is a "loose" feel, a "grinding" noise, or a total lack of stopping power.**
   - Note if the pads look worn down to the metal or if there is fluid leaking from the levers or calipers.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether you need new pads, a "brake bleed," or new cables right now.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of the repair while you are trying to reach your destination.
- You do not need to finish your planned cycling route; your only priority is reaching your destination via a safe method.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel frustrated when a mechanical failure disrupts your plans, but choosing not to ride is the correct safety decision. Most brake issues are common and can be resolved quickly by a local bike shop once you are no longer in a rush.

## Scope note
This guidance focuses on immediate stabilization and alternative travel decisions. It does not provide instructions for technical repairs, hydraulic bleeding, or cable replacement, which should be performed by a competent mechanic.

## Important note
In the UK, it is a legal requirement for bicycles to have efficient, independent front and rear brakes when used on public roads. Riding with faulty brakes increases the risk of serious injury to yourself and others, especially on E-bikes which have higher mass and average speeds. Use an alternative travel method until the bike is certified safe.
