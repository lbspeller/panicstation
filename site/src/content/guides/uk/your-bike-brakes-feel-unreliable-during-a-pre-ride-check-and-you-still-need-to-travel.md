# What to do if… your bike brakes feel unreliable during a pre-ride check and you still need to travel (UK)

## Short answer
Do not start the ride on public roads if either brake feels unreliable. Secure the bike and switch to a safer travel option, such as public transport, walking, a taxi, or a licensed private hire vehicle, while you arrange a brake check.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hope the brakes will improve once you are moving.
- Don’t rely on one brake for a normal road journey if the other brake feels weak, inconsistent, or unclear.
- Don’t do a fast test ride on the street to see if the problem improves.
- Don’t keep riding if a brake lever pulls close to the handlebar, feels inconsistent, or the bike does not stop decisively in a slow controlled check.
- Don’t attempt unfamiliar brake repairs under time pressure.
- Don’t ride downhill, in traffic, or with extra passengers or loads if the brakes are not clearly strong and consistent.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and decide not to ride unless both brakes clearly pass a slow check.** Being late is easier to manage than setting off on a bike that may not stop properly.

2. **Do a controlled brake check away from traffic and moving vehicles.** On flat ground, push or walk the bike slowly and apply each brake firmly, one at a time.
   - You are looking for a firm lever feel, predictable bite, and the wheel stopping decisively.
   - If either brake feels weak, inconsistent, or the lever comes close to the handlebar, treat the bike as not safe to ride.

3. **Check only the obvious things you can verify without guessing.**
   - Make sure each wheel looks fully seated in the frame or fork.
   - Make sure any quick-release lever you can identify is fully closed, not half-open.
   - If you have rim brakes with an opening or quick-release mechanism, make sure it is closed.
   - Check that brake pads are present, not visibly detached, and not obviously missing the rim or disc.
   - If anything looks wrong, loose, damaged, or unclear, stop checking and do not ride.

4. **Switch travel mode now.**
   - If you are expected somewhere, send a short message saying your bike has an unsafe brake issue and you are delayed.
   - Use an option available now: bus, train, tram, underground where available, walking, taxi, licensed private hire vehicle, or a lift from someone nearby.

5. **Move the bike only by walking it.** Walk it to a safer place you can lock it, such as home, work, a staffed building, or a bike shop. Do not ride it there.

6. **Secure the bike.** Lock it somewhere safer than the street if possible. Remove easy-to-take accessories such as lights, bags, and clip-on items.

7. **Arrange a brake safety check.** Call a local bike shop and describe the exact symptom, such as “brake lever pulls close to the handlebar”, “rear brake feels weak”, “disc brake squeals and will not bite”, or “brake feel changes each time”. Ask for the soonest brake safety check or repair assessment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the perfect repair, upgrade, or service package now.
- You do not need to diagnose every possible cause at the roadside.
- You do not need to finish the planned ride today.
- You only need to decide how to travel without relying on brakes you do not trust.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel torn when you need to get somewhere. Not riding on unreliable brakes is a sensible safety decision, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation. Later decisions about repairs, parts, servicing, or whether the bike is roadworthy may need help from a competent cycle mechanic or another suitably experienced person.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not professional mechanical, legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. UK road rules generally require cycles used on roads to have efficient brakes; if you are not confident the brakes are strong, consistent, and controllable, do not ride.
