# What to do if… water starts leaking from the refrigerator or freezer and you can’t tell where it’s coming from (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the electricity first if it is safe to do so, then shut off any plumbed water supply to the appliance. Contain the water immediately to prevent it tracking under kitchen units or toward electrical sockets.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t touch the appliance, plug, or socket if you are standing in water or if your hands are wet.
- Don’t ignore a "constant stream" of water; this indicates a burst supply pipe rather than a simple blockage.
- Don’t pull the appliance out by force if it is plumbed in, as you may snap the plastic water line.
- Don’t use a vacuum cleaner to suck up the water unless it is specifically a "wet and dry" model.
- Don’t tip or tilt the fridge freezer excessively to look underneath while it is still plugged in.
- Don’t poke sharp metal objects into the internal drain hole to clear it; this can puncture the internal cooling pipes.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the power safely.** If you can reach the plug **without stepping in the puddle**, switch it off and unplug it. If the plug is hidden behind the unit, look for a **switched fused spur** (a wall switch with a fuse tray) above the worktop and turn it off there.
   - If water is touching a socket or you hear a **buzzing or crackling** sound, do not touch the switch; turn off the "Sockets" circuit at your **main consumer unit (fuse box)**.
2. **Stop the water supply (for plumbed-in models).** If your fridge has a water dispenser or ice maker, locate the **small blue or white isolating valve** on the thin plastic pipe (usually under the kitchen sink). If you cannot find it and water is still flowing, turn off your **internal stopcock** (usually found under the sink or in the hallway) by turning it clockwise.
3. **Contain the spread.** Use towels or floor cloths to create a **barrier** along the base of your kitchen cabinets to stop water tracking underneath the units. Place a shallow tray or baking sheet under the drip point if it is visible.
4. **Identify the leak type.** 
   - **Constant flow:** This is almost certainly a split water supply pipe or a loose connection at the back.
   - **Slow drip or intermittent puddle:** This is likely a blocked defrost drain or a failed door seal causing excess condensation.
5. **Check the internal drain hole (low risk).** Open the fridge door and look at the back wall for a small V-shaped groove and a hole. If this is full of water, the drain is blocked. Use a **cotton bud or a flexible plastic straw** to gently clear the debris.
6. **Protect your food.** Keep the doors closed as much as possible once the power is off. 
   - **Fridge:** Food is generally safe for **4 hours**. If the internal temperature rises to **8°C or above**, high-risk foods (meat, dairy, fish) must be eaten within 4 hours or discarded.
   - **Freezer:** A full freezer will keep food frozen for **48 hours** (24 hours if half-full) if the door remains sealed.
7. **Notify the relevant parties.** 
   - **Renters:** Contact your landlord or letting agent immediately, especially if water has seeped under floorboards or units.
   - **Owners:** If you suspect a pipe leak behind built-in units, call a **plumber**. For internal appliance faults, contact an **appliance repair technician**.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to defrost the entire unit immediately unless an engineer has confirmed an internal ice blockage.
- You do **not** need to move all food to a neighbor's house in the first hour; focus on the leak containment first.
- Deep-cleaning the area under the fridge can wait until the electricity and water are confirmed as isolated and safe.

## Important reassurance
Most fridge freezer leaks are caused by a simple blocked drain hole or a door left slightly ajar. These do not usually require a replacement appliance. By turning off the power and stopping the water, you have already prevented the most serious types of damage.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization only. If your appliance is plumbed into the mains water, or if water has entered electrical sockets, professional repair or inspection is mandatory to ensure safety and prevent fire.

## Important note
This information is provided for damage limitation and general safety. It is not a substitute for professional electrical or plumbing advice. If you suspect water has affected your home's fixed wiring, do not restore power until it has been inspected by a qualified electrician.
