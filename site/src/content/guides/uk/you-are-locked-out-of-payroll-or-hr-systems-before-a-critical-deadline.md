# What to do if… you are locked out of payroll or HR systems before a critical deadline (UK)

## Short answer
Do not resign or sign any documents during the meeting. Formally request the proposal in writing and ask for a minimum of 10 days to consider the terms and seek independent advice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide a verbal or written resignation "on the spot" to end the immediate pressure.
- Do not sign any "full and final settlement" or waiver documents without independent legal review.
- Do not accept a verbal-only promise of a "good reference" as it is legally difficult to enforce.
- Do not attempt to defend your entire performance or conduct history while under high stress in the meeting.
- Do not delete evidence of the pressure, such as meeting invites sent with no notice or threatening emails.

## What to do now
1. **Request a formal pause and written proposal.** State clearly: "I am not prepared to resign today. Please put your full proposal, including the suggested reference wording, in writing so I can consider it properly."
2. **Identify the nature of the meeting.** Ask the employer to clarify if the current meeting is an informal discussion, a formal disciplinary hearing, or a "protected conversation" under Section 111A of the Employment Rights Act.
3. **Assert your statutory right to be accompanied if the meeting is formal.** If the employer clarifies this is a disciplinary or grievance hearing, state: "I am exercising my legal right to be accompanied by a trade union representative or a colleague. We must reschedule this meeting to allow them to attend."
4. **Verify if your role requires a mandatory reference.** In the UK, most employers are not legally required to provide a reference unless:
   - your employment contract explicitly states they will.
   - you work in a regulated industry, such as financial services (FCA/PRA regulated) or certain safeguarding roles.
5. **Demand specific details for any promised reference.** If a reference is offered as part of an exit deal, ask for the written draft to specify:
   - the exact wording of the reference.
   - whether it is a "factual only" reference (dates and job title) or a detailed one.
   - who at the company is authorised to provide it.
6. **Apply the 10-day consideration rule.** Refer to the ACAS Code of Practice which recommends that employees are given at least 10 calendar days to consider the terms of a settlement agreement.
7. **Note that settlement agreements require independent advice.** Remind the employer that for a settlement agreement to be legally binding, you must receive advice from a "relevant independent adviser" (usually a solicitor or certified union official).
8. **Document the interaction immediately.** After leaving the room, write a timed and dated summary of what was said, who was present, and the specific "resign or be fired" ultimatums used.
9. **Contact ACAS or the Fair Work Agency.** Use the ACAS helpline for procedural guidance or contact the Fair Work Agency if you believe the employer is using prohibited "fire and rehire" tactics or breaching core employment standards.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to accept the reference offer or fight a dismissal today.
- You do not need to have a new job lined up before refusing to resign under pressure.
- You do not need to provide a counter-offer for a settlement figure in the initial meeting.
- You do not need to decide if you will bring a "constructive dismissal" claim until you have spoken to a professional.

## Important reassurance
Feeling overwhelmed when pressured to quit is a normal response to a high-stakes workplace tactic. You are legally entitled to stop the meeting, walk out of the room to gather your thoughts, and insist that all further communication is handled in writing.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling immediate workplace pressure only. Strategic decisions regarding employment tribunals, settlement negotiations, or claims of constructive dismissal require consultation with a qualified legal professional or union representative.

## Important note
This information reflects UK employment law as of 2026. Employment rights can vary significantly based on your length of service and specific contract terms. If you are experiencing severe distress or mental health symptoms due to workplace pressure, contact a healthcare provider or a crisis support service immediately.
