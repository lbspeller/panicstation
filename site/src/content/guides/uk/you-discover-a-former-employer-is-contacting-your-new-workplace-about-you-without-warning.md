# What to do if… you discover a former employer is contacting your new workplace about you without warning (UK)

## Short answer
Report the loss immediately to your employer’s security or IT department to deactivate the credential and prevent unauthorised physical or digital access.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delay reporting to perform an extended search for the item.
- Do not post details or photos of the missing badge on social media or public forums.
- Do not use a colleague's badge or request "tailgating" entry through secure doors.
- Do not rely on PIN protection or biometric layers as a reason to delay deactivation.
- Do not attempt to confront individuals you suspect may have found or stolen the badge.

## What to do now
1. **Perform a single, brief search of the most likely locations and then stop.** Check the last point of use (such as a turnstile, printer, or reception desk), your immediate bags, and vehicle compartments.
2. **Report the loss through your organisation’s fastest official channel.** Contact workplace security, the facilities management office, or the IT service desk immediately.
   - If you are off-site or after hours, call the emergency security line or your line manager to initiate the deactivation process.
3. **Request immediate deactivation of all linked credentials and permissions.** Explicitly ask for the badge to be disabled for building access, IT login, secure printing, and any cashless catering or locker functions.
   - Ask for a reference number or email confirmation that the card has been successfully voided.
4. **Provide a specific timeline of when the badge was last in your possession.** This allows security to review access logs and CCTV for any unauthorised attempts to use the card between the time of loss and the time of deactivation.
5. **Identify any secondary items lost alongside the badge.** Disclose if the badge was in a holder with physical keys, alarm fobs, or written notes containing door codes or sensitive information.
6. **Alert your Data Protection Officer if the badge contains personal data.** If the card displays your full name, photograph, or job title, the organisation must assess if this constitutes a personal data breach under UK GDPR.
   - Note that organisations generally have 72 hours to report significant breaches to the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO).
7. **Report theft to the police via the 101 non-emergency service.** If you believe the badge was stolen, obtain a crime reference number to provide to your employer’s HR or security department.
   - Use 999 only if there is an immediate threat to life or a crime is currently in progress.

## What can wait
- Administrative paperwork for a permanent replacement badge.
- Payment of any replacement fees or deposits.
- Retracing your entire travel route once the card is already disabled.
- Disciplinary or liability discussions regarding the circumstances of the loss.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel anxious when a piece of your professional identity is missing. However, modern access control systems are designed to mitigate this exact risk; once the card is deactivated in the system, it becomes a piece of inert plastic with no functional access power. Reporting the loss quickly is the primary way to resolve the security threat.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilisation and harm reduction following the loss of a physical access credential. Individual workplace policies regarding security levels, high-clearance zones, and specific reporting software will take precedence.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and does not constitute legal, HR, or professional security advice. Always follow the specific security protocols and employee handbooks provided by your employer. If you suspect an active security breach or impersonation attempt is occurring at your workplace, contact on-site security or the police immediately.
