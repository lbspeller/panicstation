# What to do if… you are told you should resolve a sexual misconduct issue “informally” with the person involved (UK)

## Short answer
You are not required to meet or "sort it out" directly with the person involved — state clearly that you require the formal process and interim safety measures in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to meet the person alone, participate in mediation, or engage in "restorative" discussions under pressure.
- Do not sign any document, non-disclosure agreement (NDA), or "informal agreement" in the moment; ask for the digital copy to review independently.
- Do not assume that choosing a formal route is "overreacting" or that you are making things difficult for the organization.
- Do not delete any evidence, including messages from the person involved or emails from managers suggesting you handle this informally.
- Do not allow anyone to convince you that your legal rights to a formal investigation are waived by "trying" an informal talk first.

## What to do now
1. **Request the organization's formal policy and your options in writing immediately.**  
   - Use a clear statement: "I am not comfortable with an informal resolution or direct contact. Please provide the formal reporting procedure and the list of available interim safety measures in writing."

2. **Explicitly decline mediation or direct confrontation as a resolution method.**  
   - State: "I do not agree to mediation or any process that requires me to be in the same room or on the same call as the person involved."

3. **Identify and contact the correct formal oversight body for your specific setting.**  
   - **Workplace:** Contact HR or a senior director not involved in the incident and request the **formal grievance** process; remind them of their legal duty to take "all reasonable steps" to prevent further harassment.
   - **University / Higher Education:** Contact the specialized **Sexual Misconduct / Student Support** office and request immediate "interim measures" such as no-contact orders or timetable changes.
   - **School / College:** Ask for the **Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL)** or their deputy.

4. **Keep a log of who suggested an "informal" route and what specific pressure was applied.**  
   - Document the date, time, and the exact phrasing used (e.g., "it's better for everyone if we keep it quiet") to ensure you have a record of any potential failure in the organization's duty of care.

5. **Engage an independent support person or representative before any further meetings.**  
   - This may be a union representative, a specialized Sexual Violence Advocate (ISVA), or a trusted colleague.
   - Request that any future meetings regarding this issue have a written agenda and a formal note-taker provided.

6. **Prioritize your physical safety by requesting immediate "no-contact" protocols.**  
   - Ask the organization to implement a formal instruction that the person involved must not contact you via work/study channels, social media, or in person while the matter is being addressed.

7. **Access confidential medical or forensic support through a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) if needed.**  
   - You can self-refer to a SARC for healthcare and evidence preservation without involving the police or your organization; search "NHS SARC" to find your nearest unit.

8. **Note that your report is a "protected disclosure" under UK whistleblowing law.**  
   - Remind the organization, if necessary, that from April 2026, sexual harassment reports are expressly protected disclosures, meaning you have enhanced legal protection against retaliation or detriment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report the matter to the police.
- You do not need to provide a complete, final statement or "proof" during the initial request for a formal route.
- You do not need to worry about the career or academic future of the person involved; that is the organization's responsibility to manage via the formal process.
- You do not need to respond to personal messages or "apology" attempts from the person involved.

## Important reassurance
It is common for organizations to suggest informal routes because they are faster or less administratively heavy for them, but this does not mean your situation is "minor." Wanting a formal record and physical distance is a standard safety requirement. You have the right to a process that does not require you to negotiate your own safety.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling pressure to resolve sexual misconduct informally. It does not cover the specifics of police investigations, employment law litigation, or long-term therapeutic recovery.

## Important note
This information is for guidance and does not constitute legal advice. UK law regarding NDAs and "all reasonable steps" for employers is strictly enforced as of 2026. If you are in immediate physical danger, call 999.
