# What to do if… you are asked for proof of a name change for travel and cannot access the document (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and focus on one consistent travel identity: the name on your passport should match the name the airline, carrier, visa, ETA, or eVisa record is using, where relevant. Contact the airline or booking agent now and ask exactly what they can change, what evidence they will accept, and what happens if you travel under the passport name instead.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a phone photo, scan, or screenshot will be accepted if staff have asked for an original, replacement, or certified copy.
- Do not argue at the check-in desk about whether your name has changed; ask what document they can accept and what booking options are available.
- Do not pay a random online document service that promises instant official certificates.
- Do not hand over your passport or only ID to someone away from the desk unless you know who they are and why; ask for a supervisor if you feel unsure.
- Do not keep moving through the journey without checking the next travel-critical name record, especially any onward flight, visa, ETA, or eVisa.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the anchor name you can prove right now.**  
   This is usually the exact name on your passport. Write it down exactly, including middle names, hyphens, spacing, and order. Your immediate aim is to reduce mismatches between your passport, booking, boarding pass, and any travel permission.

2. **Contact the airline or booking agent before arguing with the desk.**  
   Use the airline app, live chat, phone line, or the desk supervisor and say: “I cannot access my name-change document right now. My passport says [exact passport name]. Can you change, correct, reissue, or rebook this so the travel name matches my passport?”  
   Ask them to confirm:
   - whether this is treated as a small correction or a larger name change;
   - whether the change must be made by the airline or the booking agent;
   - what evidence they will accept today;
   - whether they will accept a scan or photo while you arrange the official document.

3. **Ask what proof they mean, not just “proof of name change.”**  
   The usual supporting document may be a marriage certificate, civil partnership certificate, deed poll, statutory declaration, court order, or another official document that shows the link between the old and new names. If the issue relates to divorce, ask whether they need the divorce document, the earlier marriage certificate, or both, because one document may not show the full name link.

4. **If you cannot produce accepted proof today, ask about travelling under the passport name.**  
   This may mean correcting the existing booking to the passport name, having the ticket reissued, or buying a new ticket in the passport name. Ask the airline to say the available options clearly before you choose.

5. **Try quick document recovery in parallel.**  
   Spend a short, fixed period trying the fastest routes:
   - use another device, browser, password manager, recovery email, or backup code;
   - ask a trusted person at home to find the document and send clear photos of the whole document, including any reverse side or certificate number;
   - search email and cloud storage for terms such as certificate, marriage, civil partnership, deed poll, statutory declaration, solicitor, registrar, GRO, court, or PDF;
   - ask the airline whether they will look at the scan while you arrange an official replacement.

6. **Start the correct official replacement route if the document is not recoverable.**  
   This may not solve check-in today, but it gives you a clear next step and may help later conversations with the airline, insurer, employer, or passport office.
   - For England and Wales birth, death, marriage, adoption, or civil partnership certificates, use the General Register Office route on GOV.UK.
   - For Scotland certificates or extracts, use National Records of Scotland.
   - For Northern Ireland certificates, use the General Register Office Northern Ireland route through nidirect.
   - For a deed poll or statutory declaration, contact the solicitor, provider, court route, or person who helped prepare it, if there was one. If it was self-made, locate the signed original or saved copy and ask the airline what, if anything, they can consider today.

7. **If you are already abroad, separate the airline problem from any passport problem.**  
   If your passport is still valid and with you, the urgent issue is usually the carrier’s booking and evidence policy. If your passport is also lost, stolen, damaged, expired, full, or unavailable, check the UK emergency travel document route and contact UK consular support for practical guidance. Consular staff cannot override airline policy or instantly issue replacement UK certificates.

## What can wait
- You do not need to fix your whole name-change history today.
- You do not need to update every bank, employer, medical, or government record right now.
- You do not need to decide which name you will use for all future travel.
- You do not need to order every possible certificate immediately; focus first on the document the airline or carrier says matters for this journey.
- Complaints, insurance claims, and refund arguments can wait until you have stabilised the immediate travel problem.

## Important reassurance
This is stressful because it happens in public and under time pressure, not because you have no options. The practical choices are usually to make the booking match the passport, recover acceptable evidence, reissue or rebook if needed, and then replace the missing document through the official route.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for an urgent travel document mismatch. Later decisions about passports, immigration records, certificates, refunds, complaints, or complex name histories may need specialist, official, or professional help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Airline, carrier, border, visa, ETA, and eVisa requirements can vary, so ask the airline or relevant official body what they will accept and keep any written confirmation, such as an email or chat transcript.
