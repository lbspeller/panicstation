# What to do if… your brake pedal feels soft or unusually low while the vehicle is stopped (UK)

## Short answer
Do not drive off. Keep the vehicle stationary and treat a soft, sinking, or unusually low brake pedal as a possible brake-failure risk until a breakdown service, garage, or qualified mechanic has checked it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “see if it’s fine” by driving a short distance or driving to a garage unless a qualified person has confirmed it is safe.
- Do not keep pressing the pedal until it seems better and treat that as proof the brakes are safe.
- Do not top up brake fluid as a substitute for finding the cause; low brake fluid may mean a leak or another fault.
- Do not ignore a brake warning light, ABS warning light, burning smell, smoke, or fluid under the vehicle.
- Do not let anyone move the vehicle at speed to test the brakes.

## What to do now
1. **Keep the vehicle stopped and secure.**  
   Put an automatic in Park, or a manual in Neutral, apply the parking brake, and keep your foot ready on the brake pedal. Use hazard lights if other road users may not expect you to be stopped. If you are blocking traffic, only move a very short distance at walking pace if it is clearly safer to do so and you can stop immediately; otherwise stay put and call for help.

2. **Do one calm stationary check.**  
   With the engine running, press the brake pedal firmly and hold it for several seconds. If it feels unusually soft or low, keeps sinking, reaches near the floor, or you are unsure, treat the vehicle as unsafe to drive.

3. **Check the driver footwell.**  
   Look for a trapped floor mat, bottle, bag, or loose object that could affect brake pedal movement. Remove anything obvious, then re-check the pedal while still stationary. If the pedal still feels wrong, do not drive.

4. **Look for brake warning signs without putting yourself in danger.**  
   Check for a brake warning light, ABS warning light, fresh fluid under the vehicle, wetness near a wheel, smoke, or a burning smell. If any of these are present, do not attempt to drive the vehicle.

5. **Check brake fluid only if it is easy and safe.**  
   If you can identify the brake fluid reservoir without guessing, check whether the level is below the minimum mark. If it is low, do not drive; arrange recovery and inspection.

6. **Call for help rather than driving.**  
   Contact breakdown assistance, a local garage, or a qualified mechanic. Say: “The brake pedal feels soft or unusually low while stationary,” and mention any warning lights, fluid, smell, smoke, or pedal sinking.

7. **Check recall or defect routes later, once the vehicle is safe.**  
   You can check whether the vehicle has an outstanding safety recall. If you believe there may be a serious safety defect, report it to the manufacturer. If you are unhappy with how the manufacturer deals with it, you can tell DVSA.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the cause right now.
- You do not need to decide on repairs, parts, costs, or whether this is a recall while you are stressed.
- You do not need to attempt DIY brake work in a hurry.
- You do not need to move the vehicle just because it is inconvenient where it is; keeping it stopped is the priority.

## Important reassurance
Noticing this while stopped is a useful catch. You have already avoided the riskiest next step: driving off with brakes that may not respond normally.

## Scope note
These are first steps for a sudden soft, sinking, or unusually low brake pedal noticed before moving off. Brake systems are safety-critical, and later decisions may need help from a qualified mechanic, garage, breakdown service, manufacturer, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not mechanical, legal, financial, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe where you are parked, prioritise staying as safe as you can and getting professional help rather than trying to drive the vehicle.
