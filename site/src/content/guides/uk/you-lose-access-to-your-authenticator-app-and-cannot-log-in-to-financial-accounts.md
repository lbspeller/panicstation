# What to do if… you lose access to your authenticator app and cannot log in to financial accounts (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the account from potential fraud and use a verified contact method to initiate a formal identity reset. Call 159 or the number on your physical bank card to secure your funds and request a multi-factor authentication (MFA) reset.

## Do not do these things
- Do not approve any push notifications, sign-in requests, or "add device" prompts that appear unexpectedly.
- Do not use contact details or links provided in SMS, email, or social media messages claiming to help you "restore access."
- Do not share one-time passcodes (OTP), recovery keys, or your banking PIN with anyone, including individuals claiming to be bank staff or police.
- Do not attempt repeated failed logins, as this may trigger a permanent security block on the account.
- Do not move money to a "safe account" if instructed to do so by a caller; banks never request this.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the cause of the access loss and check for physical recovery assets.**  
   **Locate any "Backup Codes" or "Recovery Keys" generated when you first enabled the authenticator app.** These are often 8-12 character alphanumeric strings meant to be stored offline. If the loss is due to a broken or lost device:
   - Check if the authenticator app (e.g., Google, Microsoft, Authy) was backed up to a cloud account (iCloud / Google Drive) on a different device.
   - If the loss is unexpected and you still have your phone, treat this as a high-risk security event.

2. **Establish a secure communication line with your financial providers.**  
   **Call 159 if your bank is a participant in the "Stop Scams UK" scheme, or use the official number on the back of your debit/credit card.**
   - If you are currently on a call with someone claiming to "fix" your access, hang up immediately.
   - Use a different phone if you suspect your current device is compromised.

3. **Request an immediate security hold and an MFA reset from each bank.**  
   **Inform the agent that you have lost your 2FA device and cannot authenticate your login.** Explicitly ask the bank to:
   - Verify that no recent changes have been made to your contact details (email or mobile number).
   - Place a temporary note on the account to flag any attempted high-value transfers.
   - Initiate their "Account Recovery" or "MFA Reset" protocol, which may involve visiting a branch with photo ID or receiving a code via post.

4. **Secure the primary recovery routes (email and mobile network).**  
   **Check your email account on a trusted computer for "New Device Sign-in" alerts or "Password Reset" notifications.**
   - Review email "forwarding rules" to ensure your bank communications are not being redirected to an external address.
   - If your phone has lost signal entirely, contact your mobile provider immediately to check for an unauthorized "SIM Swap."

5. **Monitor for unauthorized transactions through alternative channels.**  
   **Ask the bank representative over the phone to read out the last five transactions and any pending payments.**
   - Review physical receipts or merchant emails for any purchases not made by you.
   - If unauthorized activity is found, instruct the bank to transition the call to their Fraud Department immediately.

6. **Report the incident to the national reporting centre if a scam is suspected.**  
   **Contact the appropriate authorities based on your location once your accounts are secured.**
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** Report to **Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) via reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.
   - **Scotland:** Report directly to **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
   - For crimes currently in progress or immediate danger, call 999.

## What can wait
- Choosing a new authenticator app or security hardware (e.g., YubiKey).
- Closing the bank account entirely (unless the bank recommends it due to a compromise).
- Updating your marketing preferences or non-essential account settings.
- Escalating a complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service (this can only happen after the bank provides a "Final Response" letter).

## Important reassurance
Losing an authenticator app is a common occurrence during phone upgrades or device failures. Banks have established, albeit strict, procedures to verify your identity and restore access without the app. The delay you experience is a protective measure designed to prevent unauthorized individuals from taking over your account.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only. It is intended to stabilize your financial security and prevent fraud following a loss of access. Specific recovery steps and timelines vary by institution and may require physical documentation.

## Important note
This guide provides general procedural information and does not constitute legal, financial, or cybersecurity advice. If you suspect fraud or a criminal attempt on your accounts, prioritize direct contact with your bank via trusted, official channels and follow their internal security instructions.
