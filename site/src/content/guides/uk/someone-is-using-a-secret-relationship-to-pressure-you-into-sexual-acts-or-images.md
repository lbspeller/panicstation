# What to do if… someone is using a secret relationship to pressure you into sexual acts or images (UK)

## Short answer
You do not owe anyone secrecy or sex. Get to a safer pause, stop the immediate pressure if you can, and tell one trusted person or a specialist support line today so you are not carrying this alone.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree to sex, sexual touching, photos, videos, or messages just to keep the peace or stop them sulking, getting angry, or threatening exposure.
- Don’t accept “secrecy” as proof of love, loyalty, maturity, or trust.
- Don’t meet them somewhere private to “talk it out” if you feel pressured, unsafe, or worried it may turn sexual again.
- Don’t send more intimate images or messages to reassure them or stop threats.
- Don’t forward intimate images to anyone else.
- Don’t get drawn into long arguments about whether your boundary is fair.
- Don’t delete messages in a rush if you may want support or options later, unless keeping them puts you at risk.
- Don’t blame yourself for freezing, going quiet, going along, or not knowing how to stop it in the moment.

## What to do now
1. **Create immediate breathing space.** If you are with them, move toward a public place, a locked safe room, reception, transport, a neighbour, or a trusted person. If you are not with them, do not meet them to prove trust, explain yourself, or repair the secrecy.
2. **Use one simple boundary if it is safe.** You can say or send: “I am not keeping this secret to be pressured sexually. Do not ask me for sex, sexual photos, or sexual messages.” After that, stop debating and put distance between you.
3. **Tell one real person today.** Choose someone calm who can help you stay away from the pressure: a trusted friend, family member, teacher, tutor, manager, GP surgery, sexual health clinic, or specialist support line.
4. **Use a specialist support route that fits where you are.**
   - England and Wales, age 16+: Rape Crisis 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line, 0808 500 2222, phone or online chat.
   - Scotland: Rape Crisis Scotland helpline, 08088 01 03 02, daily 5pm to midnight.
   - Northern Ireland: Rape Crisis NI Information & Support Line, 0800 0246 991, Monday to Thursday, 10am to 12 noon and 6pm to 8pm.
   - UK-wide: The Survivors Trust helpline, 0808 801 0818, set opening hours.
   - England and Wales: Victim Support Supportline, 08 08 16 89 111, 24/7.
   - Under 19 in the UK: Childline, 0800 1111.
5. **If anything sexual happened without freely given consent, or you are unsure, consider health support.** You can ask for medical and emotional support without deciding now whether to report to police. In England, the NHS SARC finder can help you locate a sexual assault referral centre. Outside England, ask a specialist support line, GP, sexual health clinic, or local urgent care service where to go. Use 999 for immediate danger or urgent medical emergency.
6. **Reduce their leverage quietly.** Change passwords they may know, especially email and messaging accounts. Turn on two-factor authentication. Check whether they can see your location through Find My, Google, Snapchat, WhatsApp, shared calendars, or other apps.
7. **Use a safer device if you may be monitored.** Contact support, change passwords, or save messages from a friend’s phone, school or college computer, work device, library computer, or another device they cannot access.
8. **Keep only what is safe to keep.** If you may want options later and it is safe, save relevant message threads, threats, screenshots of threats, usernames, dates, and call logs somewhere they cannot access. Do not put yourself at risk to collect proof, and do not forward intimate images.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to report to police. Use 999 if there is immediate danger or a crime is happening now; 101 is for non-emergency police contact.
- You do not have to decide today whether the relationship is over forever.
- You do not have to label what happened as assault, abuse, coercion, or blackmail before you ask for help.
- You do not have to explain the whole history perfectly.
- You do not have to confront them, convince them, or get them to admit anything.
- You do not have to tell everyone; one safe person or one specialist service is enough for the next step.

## Important reassurance
Secrecy used to pressure you sexually can be a control tactic. Feeling confused, frozen, ashamed, loyal, scared, or unsure does not mean you agreed freely or caused this. The next step is not to solve everything; it is to reduce the pressure around you and get support on your side.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and reduce harm. Later decisions about contact, reporting, legal protection, health care, housing, school, work, or longer-term support may need specialist help and a safer plan.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
