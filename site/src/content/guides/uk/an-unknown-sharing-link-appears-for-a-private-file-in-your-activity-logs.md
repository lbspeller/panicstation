# What to do if… an unknown sharing link appears for a private file in your activity logs (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible account compromise. Revoke the link or make the file private first, then change the account password, turn on 2-step verification, and sign out other sessions.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t open the sharing link to test it. If you need it for IT or support, record the link text without visiting it or forwarding it.
- Don’t delete the file or your whole account in panic, because this may remove useful activity history.
- Don’t message unknown recipients or support contacts you find in logs.
- Don’t assume it is only a glitch if you cannot explain it.
- Don’t create a new public link as a workaround.

## What to do now
1. **Make the file private or stop the link.**
   - Open the file’s sharing, permissions, or manage access panel.
   - Delete the unexpected sharing link if that option is available.
   - If you cannot delete the link, set access to **Restricted**, **Only people added**, or the closest private setting.
   - If the service offers **Stop sharing**, use it for that item.

2. **Check who or what still has access.**
   - Look for any people you do not recognise.
   - Look for any setting such as **Anyone with the link**, **public**, or **external sharing**.
   - Look for edit permissions that should only be view permissions.
   - Remove unknown people and reduce access to the minimum needed.

3. **Record the key details before they change.**
   - Screenshot or note the file name, folder path, timestamp, link type, and whether the link allowed view or edit access.
   - Record any visible device, session, location, IP address, app, or user information.
   - If this is a work, school, or organisation account, do not move the file to a personal account unless IT tells you to.

4. **Secure the account that owns the file.**
   - Change the password for that account.
   - Change passwords on any other accounts that reused the same password.
   - Turn on 2-step verification if it is available.
   - Sign out other devices, sessions, and apps from the account security page.

5. **Check for quiet access routes.**
   - Review connected apps, third-party access, automations, and add-ons. Remove anything you do not recognise or no longer use.
   - Review the account’s device list and sign out unfamiliar devices.
   - Check your email account for unexpected forwarding rules, mailbox rules, recovery email changes, or security alerts.

6. **If it is a work, school, or organisation account, escalate quickly.**
   - Contact IT, the helpdesk, or the admin team.
   - Say: **“A sharing link appeared in the audit or activity logs that I did not create.”**
   - Ask them to check audit logs, external sharing settings, connected apps, and recent sign-ins.

7. **If the file contained sensitive personal or financial information, reduce the immediate risk.**
   - Make a short list of what was in the file, such as ID scans, bank details, addresses, medical information, or passwords.
   - If bank or card details were included, contact your bank using its official app or a number from your card or bank statement, and follow their fraud advice.
   - If passwords, recovery codes, or identity documents were included, treat those items as exposed and replace or report them through the relevant provider where possible.

8. **If you think this is linked to fraud or cyber crime, use the UK reporting route if you decide to report.**
   - In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud online.
   - In Scotland, report fraud or cyber crime by calling 101.
   - If someone is in immediate danger, call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether there should be a formal investigation; contain access and secure the account first.
- You do not need to contact everyone who might have received the link until you have contained access and recorded what you know.
- You do not need to reinstall your device immediately unless you see separate signs of malware or your IT team tells you to.
- You can postpone reorganising cloud storage, changing provider, or rewriting all folder permissions.

## Important reassurance
Finding an unexpected sharing link is frightening, but it is also a useful early warning. Revoking the link, removing unknown access, and locking down the account can stop further exposure and give you time to work out what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps to contain an unexpected sharing link and regain control. Later decisions about incident reports, legal or regulatory duties, forensics, and notifications may need specialist help, especially for work or organisation accounts.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, IT, cybersecurity, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are using an employer, school, or organisation system, follow internal policies and involve the admin or IT team promptly.
