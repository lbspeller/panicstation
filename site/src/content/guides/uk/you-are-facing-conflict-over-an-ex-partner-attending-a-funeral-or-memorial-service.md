# What to do if… you are facing conflict over an ex-partner attending a funeral or memorial service (UK)

## Short answer
Pause the argument, name one person the funeral director or officiant will take instructions from, and choose the guest-list plan most likely to keep the service calm and safe.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t decide while texts, calls, or group chats are actively escalating.
- Don’t announce a final decision in a wide group chat.
- Don’t try to settle the whole relationship history through the funeral plan.
- Don’t use threats, blame, or bargaining over attendance.
- Don’t let several people give conflicting instructions to the funeral director, venue, or officiant.
- Don’t expect venue staff to manage a known conflict on the day without warning.
- Don’t put yourself between people who may shout, threaten, or become violent.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the live argument for now.**  
   Send one short message: “We are pausing this discussion tonight. One person will speak to the funeral director and come back with the plan.” Then stop replying to repeated arguments.

2. **Name one contact person for the service.**  
   Choose the person who is arranging the funeral, or the person the funeral director has already accepted instructions from. If there is a dispute about who should lead, ask the funeral director what paperwork or authority they need before any further decisions are made.

3. **Check whether the service is public or invitation-only.**  
   If the service has been widely announced, you may not be able to control who arrives, so focus on seating, arrival times, and behaviour. If it is private, make a simple invitation list and ask the funeral director or officiant how that can be handled at the venue.

4. **Ask the funeral director, venue, or officiant for a conflict plan.**  
   Say: “There is conflict about whether an ex-partner attends, and we need a calm plan to reduce disruption.” Ask about:
   - one named contact for instructions
   - reserved seating or separate areas
   - staggered arrival or leaving times
   - a quiet place for someone to step out
   - what staff can and cannot do if someone becomes disruptive

5. **Choose one low-drama attendance plan.**  
   Pick the option that best protects the service, not the option that proves who is right:
   - invite the ex-partner with brief conditions about calm behaviour
   - do not invite them, but offer a separate time to say goodbye if that is safe and workable
   - delay the attendance decision for a short, stated period while the funeral director checks what is possible

6. **Put the decision in one brief message.**  
   Keep it factual. For example: “The plan is a quiet service with no confrontation. The funeral director has one contact person. Anyone who cannot keep things calm will be asked to step away from the service.”

7. **Make a day-of plan without making it dramatic.**  
   Ask one or two calm people to greet arrivals, direct people to seats, and get the funeral director or venue staff if tension rises. Do not ask them to argue, restrain anyone, or decide who is morally entitled to attend.

8. **Use police routes only if safety is at risk.**  
   If there are threats, intimidation, stalking, harassment, violence, or you think someone may be harmed, contact the police. Use 999 for immediate danger and 101 or online reporting for non-emergency concerns.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether the ex-partner was right or wrong in the relationship.
- You do not need to resolve inheritance issues, old family disputes, or who is “taking sides” before the service.
- You do not need to explain the full history to every person who asks.
- You can leave wider conversations until after the funeral or memorial period.

## Important reassurance
A calm plan is not cold or unfair. It is a way to protect the service from becoming another painful event while people are grieving.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilising the immediate conflict, reducing the chance of disruption, and getting a workable plan for the service. Later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. Who can arrange a funeral and who a funeral director can take instructions from can depend on the facts and on where in the UK the arrangements are being made. If the dispute is blocking arrangements, ask the funeral director what they require and consider specialist legal advice.
