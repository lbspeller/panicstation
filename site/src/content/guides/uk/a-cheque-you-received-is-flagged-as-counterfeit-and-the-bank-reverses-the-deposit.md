# What to do if… a cheque you received is flagged as counterfeit and the bank reverses the deposit (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately via the number on your card to report the reversal and prevent further account restrictions, and do not send any "refund" money to the cheque issuer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money back to the person who gave you the cheque (via bank transfer, cash, crypto, or gift cards) even if they pressure you.
- Do not assume that "available funds" or a cleared balance means the cheque is valid; banks can reverse deposits weeks later if fraud is detected.
- Do not ignore messages from your bank regarding "returned unpaid" items or account suspensions.
- Do not attempt to "test" the cheque at a different bank or ATM.
- Do not close your account immediately, as this may hinder the bank's fraud investigation and your ability to dispute fees.

## What to do now
1. **Call your bank’s fraud or security department using the official number on your bank card.**
   - Ask for the specific reason for the reversal (e.g., counterfeit, altered, or reported stolen).
   - Confirm if your account is currently under "review" or "restricted" status.
   - Request a list of any fees or interest charges triggered by the reversal.
   - Ask for a copy or image of the returned cheque for your records.

2. **Pause all transactions related to the cheque issuer immediately.**
   - If you have not yet sent goods, provided a service, or issued a "partial refund," stop now.
   - Do not engage in further communication with the sender until you have spoken to the bank.
   - If you have already sent money, tell the bank during your initial call so they can attempt a "payment recall."

3. **Report the incident to Report Fraud (the successor to Action Fraud).**
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** Report online at reportfraud.police.uk or call **0300 123 2040**.
   - **Scotland:** Report to Police Scotland via **101**.
   - Record the crime reference number provided, as your bank may require it to waive fees.

4. **Preserve all physical and digital evidence.**
   - Keep the original cheque (if you have it), the envelope, and any deposit receipts.
   - Save all messages, emails, or adverts related to the transaction.
   - Take screenshots of the person's profile or contact details if the transaction originated online.

5. **Forward scam messages to the national reporting services.**
   - Forward suspicious **emails** to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.
   - Forward suspicious **text messages** to **7726** (this is free).

6. **Monitor your account for "de-banking" notifications.**
   - If the bank suspects you were complicit in fraud, they may attempt to close your account.
   - Under 2026 rules, banks must generally provide 90 days' notice for closures, but they can suspend accounts immediately for financial crime investigations.

7. **Apply for Cifas Protective Registration if you shared personal data.**
   - If you provided the scammer with your ID, address, or banking details, go to the **Cifas website** to apply for Protective Registration.
   - This costs £30 for 2 years and places a flag on your credit file to alert lenders that your identity is at risk.

## What can wait
- Determining the true identity of the cheque issuer (they are often using stolen or synthetic identities).
- Filing a claim in the small claims court (this is only possible if the issuer is a real, traceable entity).
- Moving your entire banking relationship to a new provider.

## Important reassurance
Cheque fraud is designed to exploit the technical delay in the UK clearing system. Banks often make funds available before the cheque has been fully verified for authenticity. Being targeted by this scam is a common experience and does not necessarily mean your account will be permanently closed, provided you report it quickly and honestly.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization following a cheque reversal in the UK. It does not cover long-term debt recovery or the legal defense of money laundering allegations if you were an active participant in a "money mule" scheme.

## Important note
This information is not legal or financial advice. Banking procedures in 2026 are subject to specific anti-money laundering (AML) regulations. If your account is frozen or a large sum is involved, you should consult a qualified legal professional or the Citizens Advice Bureau.
