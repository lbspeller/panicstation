# What to do if… your vehicle security system or immobiliser prevents the engine from starting (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause first: put hazards on, stay out of traffic, and follow motorway breakdown safety steps if you are on a fast road. Then try only low-risk checks: spare key, key fob battery, and the backup start method in your vehicle handbook.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep trying to start the engine again and again; it can drain the battery and make the situation harder.
- Do not try to bypass the immobiliser, cut wires, or pull security-related fuses.
- Do not disconnect the vehicle battery unless your handbook or a qualified professional tells you to for your exact model.
- Do not attempt repairs on a motorway carriageway, hard shoulder, or emergency area.
- Do not hand your keys or vehicle electronics to an unknown person offering roadside “reprogramming”.

## What to do now
1. **Make the place safer before troubleshooting.**
   - Put your hazard warning lights on.
   - Apply the handbrake and select park or neutral.
   - If the vehicle is still movable and it is safe, get it out of traffic into a car park, lay-by, service area, emergency area, or another place of relative safety.

2. **If you are on a motorway or fast road, stop troubleshooting until you are safe.**
   - If possible, leave the carriageway at the next exit or pull into a service area.
   - If you cannot leave, move left if you can, stop as far left as possible, leave space to exit, turn the wheels left, and keep hazards on.
   - If it is safe and possible, get everyone out on the side furthest from traffic and wait behind a safety barrier where there is one.
   - Use a free emergency telephone where provided, use your vehicle SOS button if you have one, or call your breakdown provider.
   - If you cannot safely leave the vehicle, keep seatbelts on, keep hazards on, and call 999, asking for the police.

3. **Look for a clear security clue.**
   - Check the dashboard for a key symbol, padlock symbol, “security” message, or immobiliser light.
   - Notice whether the engine does not crank at all, cranks but will not start, or shows “key not detected”. Tell this to breakdown help.

4. **Try the spare key or spare fob.**
   - If a spare key starts the vehicle, stop further roadside testing.
   - Keep the failed key separate and arrange repair, battery replacement, or replacement when you are somewhere safe.

5. **Check the key fob battery without forcing anything.**
   - If the fob buttons are weak, intermittent, or not working, the fob battery may be flat.
   - Replace the fob battery if you can do it calmly and have the correct battery.
   - If you cannot replace it now, use the vehicle’s backup start method instead.

6. **Use the manufacturer backup start method.**
   - For many push-button vehicles, a weak fob may still be read if you hold it against the start button while pressing the brake or clutch and pressing start.
   - Some vehicles have a marked backup reader, slot, tray, or steering-column position.
   - Check the handbook or in-car manual for “key not detected”, “smart key battery low”, “emergency start”, “backup start”, or “immobiliser”.

7. **Reduce simple interference and try once more.**
   - Keep the fob away from phones, chargers, laptops, metal items, and other keys.
   - Put the fob in the backup reader position if your handbook shows one.
   - Try one controlled restart. If it still fails, stop repeated attempts.

8. **Switch to professional help.**
   - Call your breakdown provider and say: “The immobiliser or security system seems to be preventing start; the key may not be recognised; there is a warning light or message.”
   - If the key, transponder, or programming has failed, you may need a reputable auto locksmith, dealer, breakdown service, or another suitable specialist.
   - Keep proof of ownership and ID available, because key and immobiliser work may require checks.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide at the roadside whether the fault is the fob battery, the key transponder, the immobiliser, the alarm, or the vehicle battery.
- You do not need to compare repair options, arrange insurance paperwork, or choose a dealer while you are still in an unsafe place.
- Fault-code scans, key programming, module checks, and replacement-key decisions can wait until the vehicle and people are safe.

## Important reassurance
An immobiliser or key-recognition failure can feel alarming because the vehicle may look normal but still refuse to start. Many cases are linked to a weak fob battery, a damaged key, or the vehicle not reading the key; take the safety steps first, then try only the small checks above.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and avoid making it worse. Later decisions may need help from a breakdown service, qualified mechanic, reputable auto locksmith, dealer, or another suitable specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not vehicle-specific mechanical, legal, financial, or professional advice. Immobiliser systems and backup start methods vary by make, model, and year, so use your vehicle handbook and qualified help where possible.
