# What to do if… a coworker repeatedly asks about your sex life and the questions become more intrusive (UK)

## Short answer
Identify the behavior as sexual harassment under the Equality Act 2010 and initiate a formal or informal report through your employer's required procedure to trigger their legal duty to protect you.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide "polite" answers or personal details to de-escalate the tension.
- Do not delete any digital evidence or throw away physical notes.
- Do not attempt to "handle it" alone if you feel the behavior is escalating or making you avoid work areas.
- Do not agree to keep the matter "confidential" or "off the record" if a manager suggests it during a reporting meeting.
- Do not apologize for your reaction or for finding the questions inappropriate.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer location and minimize immediate interaction.** If the coworker is currently present, relocate to a communal area, stay near other colleagues, or move your workstation if possible.
2. **State a single, clear boundary once if you feel safe doing so.** Use a direct statement: "I am not comfortable with you asking about my sex life; stop asking these questions."
3. **Record the specific details of every incident in a private log.** Include the date, time, location, exact words used, any witnesses present, and your immediate response.
   - Keep this log on a personal device or in a physical notebook at home.
   - Avoid keeping the log on company hardware or shared cloud drives.
4. **Preserve all digital evidence of the intrusive behavior.** Save screenshots of messages, emails, or call logs that demonstrate the pattern of questioning.
5. **Identify your employer’s sexual harassment and grievance policies.** Locate these in your staff handbook or on the company intranet to find the designated reporting contact.
6. **Submit a report naming the behavior as "unwanted conduct of a sexual nature."** Explicitly state that the behavior is creating a hostile or offensive environment.
   - Note that since October 2024, UK employers have a "proactive duty" to prevent sexual harassment.
   - Note that from 6 April 2026, reporting sexual harassment is a "qualifying disclosure," giving you enhanced whistleblowing protection against retaliation.
7. **Request specific interim measures to ensure your safety during the investigation.** Ask for changes that reduce contact with the coworker.
   - desk or team relocation
   - a different reporting line if the harasser is your manager
   - the presence of a witness or representative in all meetings
   - temporary remote working arrangements
8. **Contact 999 if there is an immediate threat of violence or physical harm.** For non-emergency reports of criminal behavior (such as stalking or sexual assault that has already occurred), call 101 or use your local police force’s online reporting tool.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove the coworker’s "intent" was to cause harm; the law focuses on the effect the behavior has on you.
- You do not need to decide if you want to take the case to an Employment Tribunal today.
- You do not need to have a perfect "formal" letter ready before notifying HR or a manager that you feel unsafe.
- You do not need to worry about the 3-month (or 6-month from October 2026) tribunal deadline in the first 24 hours.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel hesitant, embarrassed, or to doubt your own perception when someone uses "just questions" to cross boundaries. Under UK law, any unwanted behavior of a sexual nature that violates your dignity or creates an offensive environment is harassment, regardless of whether the other person claims they were "joking."

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for workplace harassment in the UK. It does not cover long-term legal strategies or the full Employment Tribunal process.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and is not legal advice. For specific legal support, contact ACAS, a trade union representative, or a solicitor specializing in employment law. If you are in immediate physical danger, contact emergency services.
