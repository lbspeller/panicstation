# What to do if… you are contacted about workplace benefits for a person who died and deadlines are approaching (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the contact through an official employer channel and request a written extension immediately if you cannot meet the date. Most workplace death benefits are paid at the discretion of trustees, meaning they often bypass probate and can be paid directly to nominated beneficiaries.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t reply to unexpected emails or texts using the links or phone numbers provided in the message until you have verified the sender independently.
- Don’t assume you must have a Grant of Probate or Letters of Administration to start the process; many workplace schemes use "Expression of Wish" forms to pay out without waiting for the estate to be settled.
- Don’t pay any "processing fees" or "release taxes" upfront to a third party to access benefits; legitimate employer schemes do not operate this way.
- Don’t ignore the deadline entirely; while often flexible, missing the "2-year rule" (HMRC requires most pension death benefits to be paid within two years of notification) can result in significant tax charges.
- Don’t guess sensitive information like National Insurance numbers or dates if you are unsure; incorrect data can trigger fraud flags.

## What to do now
1. **Identify exactly which benefit is being discussed.** Ask the contact if they are referring to:
   - Death-in-service (usually a multiple of salary).
   - A workplace pension (refund of contributions or a survivor's pension).
   - Unpaid salary, commission, or holiday pay (this usually goes to the estate/executor).
2. **Verify the caller or sender through a trusted route.** **Find the employer's official website or a previous payslip and call the main switchboard.** Ask to be put through to "HR", "Pensions Department", or the named "Scheme Administrator" to confirm the request is genuine.
3. **Request a formal extension in writing.** **Send a brief email or letter stating you need more time to gather documentation.** If you do not have the death certificate yet, inform them of the expected date. Most trustees can grant extensions, but they need the request logged.
4. **Determine if an "Expression of Wish" exists.** **Ask the administrator if the deceased person completed a nomination form.**
   - If a form exists, the trustees can often pay you directly without probate.
   - If no form exists, ask for their "Claimant Questionnaire" which allows you to explain your relationship and financial dependency.
5. **Establish who has legal authority to act.** **Confirm whether the benefit is "discretionary" (outside the estate) or "absolute" (part of the estate).**
   - Discretionary benefits can be claimed by beneficiaries directly.
   - Absolute benefits or final salary payments usually require the executor or administrator to provide a Grant of Probate.
6. **Apply for certified copies of the death certificate.** **Order at least 5 to 10 "certified copies" from the registrar.** Most schemes will not accept a photocopy or a scan for the final payout, and they may not return originals quickly.
7. **Use government notification services to reduce admin.** **Use the "Tell Us Once" service (England, Scotland, Wales) or the "NI Direct Bereavement Service" (Northern Ireland) after registering the death.** This notifies most government departments, including public sector pension schemes, in one step.
8. **Check for "Bereavement Support Payment" eligibility.** **Ensure you are not missing the 3-month window for full backdating of government support.** This is separate from workplace benefits and has its own strict time limits.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide how to invest or spend the money today; your immediate goal is simply to secure the claim.
- You do not need to have the full probate document in hand to *start* the conversation with HR.
- You do not need to resolve disputes between family members before notifying the scheme that a claim is pending.

## Important reassurance
Deadlines in bereavement letters often sound more aggressive than they are because they are generated by automated systems. The "2-year rule" is a genuine tax threshold, but it starts from the date the scheme is notified of the death, meaning you usually have more time than a single letter suggests. Most HR and pension professionals are trained to be flexible once you make initial contact.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for UK workplace benefits only. It does not cover private life insurance, inheritance tax calculations, or legal disputes over wills.

## Important note
Workplace benefits are governed by specific scheme rules and trust deeds. This information is for general guidance and does not constitute financial or legal advice. If a claim is denied or a deadline is strictly enforced to your detriment, you may need to contact the Pensions Ombudsman.
