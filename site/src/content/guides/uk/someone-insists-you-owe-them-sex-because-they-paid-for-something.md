# What to do if… someone insists you owe them sex because they paid for something (UK)

## Short answer
You do not owe anyone sex because they paid for food, drinks, travel, tickets, a room, or anything else. Move towards a safer place, end the interaction as soon as you can, and contact someone who can stay with you or help you leave.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t have sex to “pay them back” or to make the argument stop.
- Don’t move somewhere more private, such as their car, a bedroom, a hotel room, a toilet area, or a quiet side street, to talk it out.
- Don’t keep arguing about consent, money, fairness, or what they expected if they are escalating.
- Don’t accept more drinks, drugs, lifts, or accommodation from them now.
- Don’t delete messages, call logs, payment references, or location details if you may want support or options later.
- Don’t contact them again tonight to explain yourself, apologise, or negotiate repayment.

## What to do now
1. **Move towards other people first.** Go towards staff, a reception desk, security, a shop counter, a lit busy street, a transport desk, or a group of people. If you are indoors and leaving is not safe yet, move away from private rooms and towards an exit-facing, staffed, or shared area.
2. **Use one short line.** Say or text: “No. I am leaving now.” You do not need to justify it. Repeat once if needed, then focus on getting away.
3. **Bring in another person quickly.** Call someone you trust and say out loud where you are. If you cannot call, text your location and: “Please call me now. I need help leaving.”
4. **Ask staff for practical help.** If you are in a bar, restaurant, hotel, venue, taxi rank, station, campus, workplace, or shop, say: “I am being sexually pressured and I need help leaving safely.” Ask them to stay with you, call a taxi, contact security, or walk you to a safe pick-up point.
5. **Change the route home if they control it.** If they were meant to drive you, host you, pay for your room, hold your ticket, or know your planned route, ask a trusted person or staff to help you arrange a different lift, taxi, public transport route, or safe place to wait.
6. **Call emergency help if there is immediate danger.** In the UK, call 999 if a crime is happening now, someone is in immediate danger, or you need urgent medical help. For non-emergency police contact, you can call 101.
7. **Contact specialist support when you are safe enough to do so.**
   - England and Wales, age 16 or over: 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line 0808 500 2222 or online chat.
   - Scotland: Rape Crisis Scotland helpline 08088 01 03 02, every day from 5pm to midnight.
   - Northern Ireland: Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline 0808 802 1414, 24/7.
   - Children and young people: Childline 0800 1111.
8. **If anything sexual happened without your consent, or you are unsure, consider a Sexual Assault Referral Centre.** A SARC can offer medical, practical, and emotional support. You can usually contact your nearest SARC directly to ask how to get help.
9. **If you may want options later, preserve what you already have without investigating.** Screenshot messages where they demanded sex, threatened you, or linked sex to payment. Note the date, time, place, and any witnesses, then put your phone on charge and get somewhere safer.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight what to call this.
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to report it.
- You do not need to repay them before you are safe.
- You do not need to write a full account, gather extra evidence, or confront them.
- You do not need to answer their calls, messages, or demands.

## Important reassurance
Paying for something does not create consent. Consent means agreeing by choice, with the freedom and capacity to choose; pressure, guilt, threats, fear, or being trapped can take that freedom away. Freezing, appeasing, going quiet, or trying to keep them calm are common survival responses.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance to help you get safer, reduce pressure, and keep options open. Later decisions about support, reporting, medical care, money, travel, or contact with the person may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or need urgent medical help, call 999.
