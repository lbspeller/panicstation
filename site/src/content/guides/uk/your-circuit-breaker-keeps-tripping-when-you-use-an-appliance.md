# What to do if… your circuit breaker keeps tripping when you use an appliance (UK)

## Short answer
Treat repeated tripping as a safety warning. Stop using the appliance, turn it off, unplug it if you can, and leave the circuit off if it will not stay on.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep resetting the breaker, RCD or RCBO to see if it eventually holds.
- Don’t bypass an RCD or RCBO.
- Don’t fit a higher-rated fuse or breaker to stop the tripping.
- Don’t keep using the same appliance, even briefly, if it trips the circuit.
- Don’t move the appliance around the house to keep testing different sockets.
- Don’t use extension leads, multi-way adapters or a different room as a workaround.
- Don’t remove internal covers, touch wiring, or work inside the consumer unit. It is usually fine to open the outer flap to reach the switches.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger signs.** If there is smoke, flame, a spreading fire, serious overheating, sparks, scorching, a burning smell, or buzzing or crackling, keep people away. If you can do it without getting close to danger, switch off the affected circuit at the consumer unit. If there is smoke or fire, leave the area and call 999.
2. **Stop using the appliance that triggered it.** Turn it off and unplug it. If it is hard-wired, such as a fixed appliance on a fused spur, switch it off at its local isolator or fused spur if you can identify it safely.
3. **Unplug other items on the affected circuit or in the affected room.** Include chargers, lamps, extension leads, dehumidifiers, heaters and kitchen appliances.
4. **Try one cautious reset only if there are no danger signs.** Push the tripped switch fully to off if needed, then back on. If it trips immediately with everything unplugged, leave that circuit off and get help from a registered electrician or competent person.
5. **If the switch holds, reconnect items carefully.** Plug items back in one at a time. If one item makes the breaker, RCD or RCBO trip again, stop there, unplug that item, and do not keep testing it.
6. **Label the suspect appliance.** Put a clear note on it saying “Do not use” so nobody plugs it back in. If it is essential, such as a fridge or freezer, keep the doors shut and arrange prompt professional help rather than trying to run it from another socket.
7. **If you rent, report it as an electrical safety fault.** Tell your landlord, letting agent or housing provider what appliance was in use, which socket or room was affected, and whether the circuit trips even with everything unplugged. Ask what electrical safety inspection or report applies where you live.
8. **If you own, arrange an electrical check.** Ask a registered electrician or competent person to test the circuit, the protective device, the socket and the suspect appliance.
9. **Write down the pattern.** Note the appliance, socket, room, whether it tripped immediately or after a delay, whether there was damp or water nearby, and any heat, smell, scorch marks, sparks or sounds.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether it is an overload, earth fault, short circuit, faulty appliance, socket fault or protective-device fault.
- You do not need to buy a new breaker, consumer unit, socket or appliance before proper testing.
- You do not need to dismantle plugs, sockets, appliances or the consumer unit.
- You do not need to make a final decision about repair or replacement until the immediate safety issue is isolated.

## Important reassurance
A breaker, RCD or RCBO tripping is the system warning you that something may be unsafe. Unplugging the appliance, not repeatedly resetting, and leaving a circuit off when it will not hold are sensible first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce shock and fire risk, isolate the likely trigger, and buy time. Persistent or unexplained tripping needs proper testing by a registered electrician or competent person.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not electrical, legal, fire-safety or professional advice. If there is smoke, flame, serious overheating, or immediate danger, leave the area and call 999.
