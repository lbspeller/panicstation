# What to do if… you feel unsafe on public transport because someone is following you or blocking your exit (UK)

## Short answer
Create distance without confronting them and move to where other people or staff can see you. If you are on the rail network, at a station, or on a TfL service covered by British Transport Police, and it is not an emergency, you can discreetly text **61016** if your phone can send a text; if you feel in immediate danger, call **999**.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t argue with them, accuse them, or try to “win” the situation.
- Don’t move into a quieter carriage or area if it leaves you isolated.
- Don’t squeeze past someone who is deliberately blocking you if it risks a physical struggle.
- Don’t follow them off the service, or get drawn into a confrontation on the platform or street.
- Don’t feel you have to stay seated until your planned stop if you feel unsafe.
- Don’t share personal details, such as where you are getting off, where you live, or your name, to defuse things.

## What to do now
1. **Change your position to break the pattern without confronting them.**  
   Stand up, step away, and place a solid object between you, such as a pole, seat back, or your bag held in front. Move nearer a cluster of passengers. If you can, keep your back to a wall or partition so you are harder to box in.

2. **Move toward staff or high-visibility areas on that service.**  
   - **Bus or coach:** move to a seat close to the driver, or stand where the driver can see you.
   - **Train, Underground, tram, DLR, or London Overground:** move to a busier area and keep yourself near other passengers.
   - **Station or stop:** head for staffed areas, bright lighting, Help Points, intercoms, or a visible group of people.

3. **Use the next stop as your reset if you need it.**  
   When the doors open at the next safe, busy stop or station, step onto the platform or pavement to create space. Then either:
   - **stay in a visible place** near staff, a Help Point, or other people, or
   - **re-board through a different door or carriage** only if it is clearly safe to do so.  
   You do not have to get off at your planned stop.

4. **If they block your exit, avoid a physical squeeze-past.**  
   Switch to a different door or exit if you can. If you cannot, stay close to other passengers and move with a group when the doors open. If you believe you are being physically prevented from leaving or violence is about to happen, treat it as an emergency.

5. **Bring in help early, quietly if needed.**  
   - **Emergency or immediate danger:** call **999**. If you cannot speak, follow the operator’s instructions and stay on the line if you can.
   - **Rail network, station, or TfL service covered by British Transport Police, not an emergency:** text **61016** if your phone can send a text. Include where you are, the line or route, direction of travel, next stop, what is happening, and a brief description.
   - **No phone signal on a train or station, and it is urgent:** use a Help Point, emergency handle, staff member, or police officer if one is available.
   - **British Transport Police non-emergency phone contact:** call **0800 40 50 40**.
   - **Bus, coach, street, or other non-emergency police help:** call **101** or report online later.  
   If speaking aloud feels risky, use brief phrases and keep moving toward other people or staff.

6. **Make it harder to isolate you.**  
   Phone someone and speak out loud, for example: “I’m on public transport now, I’m staying near other people, and I’m getting off at the next busy stop.” Do not say your home stop, final destination, or personal details out loud if the person may hear. If you can, share your live location with someone you trust.

7. **Once you are safe, capture only the basics.**  
   Note the time, service or route, direction, stop or station, and any carriage, bus, coach, or vehicle identifiers you can see. This can help staff or police act if you report it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to make a formal statement or complaint.
- You do not need to confront them, prove intent, or work out why they did it.
- You do not need to post about it online or replay the situation in your head to justify leaving.
- You can decide later whether to change your route, travel with someone, or seek further support.

## Important reassurance
Feeling alarmed when someone repeatedly closes distance or blocks your exit is a normal protective response. You are allowed to move, leave early, and ask for help based on your safety instincts, without waiting for the situation to become more serious.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance for the next few minutes. If this involved harassment, threats, stalking, or physical contact, you may want specialist support or formal reporting later, but you do not have to decide that while you are still shaken.

## Important note
This guide provides general information for immediate safety steps. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in danger or someone is being harmed, prioritise getting to a safer place and contacting emergency services.
