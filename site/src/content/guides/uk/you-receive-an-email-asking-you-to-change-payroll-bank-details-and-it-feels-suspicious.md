# What to do if… you receive an email asking you to change payroll bank details and it feels suspicious (UK)

## Short answer
Prioritise immediate safety by distancing yourself and others from the hazard, then make a factual, written record of the incident and the instruction to suppress the report. If internal channels are blocked or compromised, escalate the concern to a "Prescribed Person" such as the Health and Safety Executive (HSE) to access whistleblowing protections.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the hazard if it poses an ongoing risk of injury or ill health to yourself or others.
- Do not engage in verbal confrontations regarding motives or blame with the person telling you not to report.
- Do not delete or alter any existing safety logs, messages, or emails related to the incident.
- Do not share details of the safety breach or the management dispute on social media.
- Do not sign any document stating a hazard is resolved or "safe" if you have not verified this through professional inspection.
- Do not attempt to fix a technical or industrial hazard yourself unless you are specifically qualified and authorised to do so.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe distance and alert those in immediate danger if it is safe to do so.** If there is an imminent threat to life or a major fire, call **999** or text **999** (if pre-registered with the emergency SMS service). 
2. **Create a contemporaneous, factual record of the safety breach and the instruction you received.** Write down the date, time, location, specific equipment or processes involved, and the exact words used by the person who told you not to report the issue. 
   - Note any witnesses who were present.
   - Describe only observable facts (e.g., "The guard was missing from the saw") rather than opinions.
3. **Submit a formal report through the established internal reporting system if one exists.** Use your company’s accident book, near-miss software, or health and safety email address to ensure there is a digital or paper trail.
   - If you are verbally told to ignore it, follow up with a brief email: "I am writing to formally record the safety concern I raised earlier regarding [hazard] to ensure it is documented for the safety of the team."
4. **Identify the correct enforcing authority for your specific workplace sector.** Most industrial, construction, and manufacturing sites are regulated by the **Health and Safety Executive (HSE)**, while retail, office, and leisure sectors are often regulated by **Local Authority Environmental Health** departments.
5. **Escalate to a "Prescribed Person" if you reasonably believe the matter is being covered up or that you will be treated unfairly for reporting it.** Reporting to a regulator like the HSE or the Environment Agency is a "protected disclosure" under the Public Interest Disclosure Act (PIDA) if you believe the report is in the public interest. 
6. **Seek advice from a trade union safety representative or a whistleblowing charity if you fear retaliation.** Contact **Protect** (the UK whistleblowing charity) or **Acas** for guidance on your specific rights regarding "detriment" (unfair treatment) following a safety report.
7. **Document any changes in your work environment or treatment following your report.** Keep a log of any sudden changes to your shifts, duties, or disciplinary status, as this evidence is critical if you need to claim whistleblowing protection later.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove that the person who told you not to report acted with "malice" or criminal intent right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to leave your job or initiate a legal tribunal claim in the immediate minutes following the incident.
- You do not need to gather exhaustive "evidence" beyond your own factual account and any existing documents; the regulator is responsible for the investigation.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressured to stay silent about a workplace hazard is a high-stress situation, and hesitation is a normal response to professional pressure. By focusing on the physical risk and creating a factual paper trail, you are taking the most effective steps to protect both your colleagues and your legal standing as a worker.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for reporting a safety breach when facing management pressure. It does not cover the long-term legal process of Employment Tribunals or complex litigation.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional health and safety advice. As of 6 April 2026, UK law explicitly includes sexual harassment as a qualifying disclosure for whistleblowing, alongside health and safety breaches. If you are a public official, be aware of the "Duty of Candour" which may legally require you to be transparent about failures. Always prioritise immediate physical safety over procedural reporting.
