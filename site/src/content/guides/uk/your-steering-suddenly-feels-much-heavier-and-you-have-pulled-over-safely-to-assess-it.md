# What to do if… your steering suddenly feels much heavier and you have pulled over safely to assess it (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the vehicle, keep it parked, and arrange professional recovery rather than attempting to drive or "test" the steering in traffic.

## Do not do these things
- Do not rejoin a fast road or motorway to see if the steering "clears" while moving.
- Do not turn the steering wheel forcefully against the "full lock" (the limit of its travel) while parked.
- Do not open the power-steering fluid reservoir cap while the engine is hot.
- Do not reach near engine belts or fans while the engine is running.
- Do not place a warning triangle on a motorway or a high-speed dual carriageway.
- Do not stand between your vehicle and oncoming traffic.

## What to do now
1. **Follow the correct safety protocol for your specific stopping location.**
   - **If stopped in a live motorway lane:** stay in the vehicle, keep your seatbelt on, switch on hazard lights, and call **999** immediately.
   - **If on a motorway hard shoulder or emergency area:** exit via the left-hand doors, move behind the safety barrier or up the embankment, and call National Highways on **0300 123 5000** or use an emergency roadside phone.
   - **If on a local road:** keep hazard lights on and move to a safe distance from the carriageway (such as a pavement or verge).
2. **Check the dashboard for warning symbols without restarting the engine first.**
   - Look for a steering wheel icon, often accompanied by "EPS" or "PS" in red or yellow.
   - Look for a battery (charging) symbol or an engine temperature (coolant) warning. 
   - Note if multiple lights appeared simultaneously, as this often indicates a snapped auxiliary belt.
3. **Perform a visual check of the exterior only if it is safe to walk around the car.**
   - Look for a flat or significantly under-inflated front tyre, which can cause sudden heavy steering.
   - Check the ground beneath the engine bay for fresh fluid leaks (typically red, clear, or brown). Do not touch the fluid.
4. **Try one brief restart check only if the vehicle is in a quiet, safe location.**
   - Switch the engine off completely.
   - Wait 60 seconds to allow the electronic control units to reset.
   - Restart the engine and check if the steering assist returns or the warning light vanishes.
   - **If the steering remains heavy or the light stays on, stop troubleshooting immediately.**
5. **Contact your breakdown provider or a mobile mechanic.**
   - Provide your exact location (using a postcode, road name, or the **What3Words** app).
   - Report the specific symptoms: "sudden loss of steering assistance," "heavy steering," and any dashboard warning lights.
6. **Move the vehicle only for immediate life-safety reasons.**
   - If you must move a few metres to clear a dangerous position, be aware that steering will require significantly higher physical effort than usual.
   - Do not attempt to drive to a garage or home; wait for a recovery truck.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific mechanical cause (e.g., pump failure, fluid leak, or electrical fault) at the roadside.
- You do not need to check fluid levels if you are on a high-speed road; wait for professional assistance.
- You do not need to decide on a permanent repair or choose a garage while in a state of panic.

## Important reassurance
Sudden steering failure is a high-stress event, but by pulling over and stopping, you have already mitigated the primary danger. It is normal to feel a physical "adrenaline dump" or shaking after such an incident; choosing to stay parked and wait for help is the safest and most responsible action.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the minutes following a steering fault. It is not a repair manual. Modern steering systems are complex; driving with a fault can lead to total loss of control or engine damage if an auxiliary belt has failed.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not replace the advice of emergency services or professional mechanics. Always follow the instructions provided by National Highways or the police when on the motorway network. Mechanical failures are unpredictable; prioritise your personal safety over the vehicle at all times.
