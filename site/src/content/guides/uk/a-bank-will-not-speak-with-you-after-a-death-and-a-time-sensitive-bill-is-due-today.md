# What to do if… a bank will not speak with you after a death and a time-sensitive bill is due today (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the biller immediately to request a payment hold, then provide the bank's bereavement team with a death certificate and invoice to request a direct payment from the frozen account.

## Do not do these things
- Don't use the deceased person’s debit cards, PINs, or online banking credentials to make payments.
- Don't sign the deceased person’s name on any cheques or legal documents.
- Don't ignore the bill or assume it will be automatically "cancelled" by the bank.
- Don't pay the bill from your personal funds unless you have confirmed you are legally liable for the debt.
- Don't attempt to "test" the account by making small withdrawals or transfers.

## What to do now
1. **Contact the company that issued the bill and request a temporary "bereavement hold" on the account.**
   - Inform them that the account holder has died and you are currently securing legal authority to manage the estate.
   - Request that they pause any late fees, interest, or disconnection processes for at least 30 days.
   - Ask for a written confirmation of this hold via email or post.
2. **Request to speak specifically with the bank’s "Bereavement" or "Estate" department rather than general customer service.**
   - General staff may not have the authority to bypass standard security protocols for a third party.
   - Ask the bereavement team for their "Requirements Checklist" for third-party communication.
3. **Ask the bank if they can make a "Direct Payment" to the biller from the deceased person's account as an exception.**
   - Under the BBA Bereavement Principles, many banks will pay essential bills (such as funeral costs, Inheritance Tax, or urgent utility bills) directly to the provider before probate is granted.
   - You will usually need to provide an original or certified copy of the death certificate and the specific invoice you want paid.
4. **Use the "Tell Us Once" service to notify government departments if the bill is related to council tax or benefits.**
   - This service notifies the DWP, HMRC, and the local council simultaneously.
   - You will receive a reference number from the registrar when you register the death to access this service.
5. **Use the Death Notification Service (DNS) to notify multiple financial institutions at once.**
   - This free service (0333 207 6574) notifies participating banks and building societies, reducing the need for repetitive calls.
   - Note that it can take up to 10 working days for individual banks to contact you after using this service.
6. **Lodge a formal complaint with the bank if they refuse to provide a clear path for urgent payments.**
   - State clearly that this is an "urgent bereavement complaint regarding a time-sensitive payment."
   - Request a formal complaint reference number and an acknowledgement in writing.
7. **Keep a detailed log of every interaction including the date, time, and the name of the person you spoke to.**
   - Note exactly what you were told regarding document requirements and timelines.
   - Save all reference numbers for both the bank and the biller.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle the entire estate or distribute assets today.
- You do not need to obtain a Grant of Probate or Letters of Administration immediately for small urgent bills.
- You do not need to close the accounts or transfer the remaining balance right now.
- You do not need to decide which solicitor or professional to use for the full probate process today.

## Important reassurance
It is standard procedure for banks to freeze sole accounts immediately upon notification of death to protect the estate. While this causes a temporary block on communication and payments, most billers have established protocols for bereavement and will grant extensions if notified promptly. The bank's refusal to speak with you is usually a security requirement that can be resolved by providing the specific documents they request.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate billing deadlines following a death. It does not cover long-term probate law, tax liabilities, or the final distribution of estate assets.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Bank policies regarding pre-probate payments vary. Under UK law, banks have up to 8 weeks to provide a Final Response to a complaint before you can escalate the matter to the Financial Ombudsman Service.
