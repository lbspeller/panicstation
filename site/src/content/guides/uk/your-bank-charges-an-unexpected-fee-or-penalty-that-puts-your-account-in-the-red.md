# What to do if… your bank charges an unexpected fee or penalty that puts your account in the red (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the fee from snowballing: pause non-essential spending, check pending payments, and contact your bank now to ask what caused the charge and whether they can reverse it or pause knock-on charges while they review it.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep spending from the account until you know what is still pending.
- Don’t repeatedly retry a payment that is failing; it may create more returned-payment problems.
- Don’t take high-cost borrowing or buy-now-pay-later in a panic just to cover today’s gap.
- Don’t close the account while payments, refunds, or disputes are still unresolved.
- Don’t ignore rent, mortgage, council tax, utilities, childcare, or travel payments that may now fail.
- Don’t delete bank messages, app notifications, emails, or screenshots.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the immediate cascade**
   - Pause non-essential card use from this account.
   - Check pending card payments, Direct Debits, standing orders, and subscriptions due in the next few days.
   - If you can safely move money in without risking essentials, reduce or clear the overdrawn balance.

2. **Pin down the charge**
   - In your banking app or online banking, note the charge name, amount, date, and current balance.
   - Look at the transactions just before it, such as a returned payment, account fee, overdraft interest, Direct Debit, or card payment.
   - Take screenshots of the fee, the surrounding transactions, any pending payments, and the balance.

3. **Use the right route if a payment caused the problem**
   - If a Direct Debit was taken on the wrong date, for the wrong amount, or without proper authority, contact your bank and ask about a Direct Debit Guarantee refund.
   - If a card payment was taken wrongly, duplicated, or the merchant will not refund, ask your bank how to raise a card dispute or chargeback. Chargeback is not guaranteed, so ask what evidence they need.
   - If the charge is the bank’s own fee, ask the bank to explain exactly what rule or account term triggered it.

4. **Contact the bank and ask for clear help**
   - Use secure message or in-app chat if available, because it creates a record. If you call, ask for a reference number.
   - Ask whether they can refund or reverse the unexpected fee.
   - Ask whether they can pause, reduce, or waive further interest or charges linked to this issue while they review it.
   - If the fee means you cannot cover essentials, say clearly: “This has put me in financial difficulty.”

5. **Protect essential payments**
   - Check what may fail before your next money comes in, especially rent, mortgage, council tax, utilities, childcare, insurance, and travel.
   - If an essential payment is likely to bounce, contact the biller today and ask whether they can delay collection, change the date, or stop repeat attempts.
   - Keep a note of who you contacted, when, and what they agreed.

6. **Make it a formal complaint if it is not fixed quickly**
   - Tell the bank you want this treated as a complaint.
   - Keep it short: what the fee was, when it happened, why it was unexpected, how it put you in the red, and what you want the bank to do.
   - Ask for the complaint reference and save all replies.

7. **Know the escalation point**
   - If the bank sends a final response and you are still unhappy, you can usually take the complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service within six months of the final response date.
   - If the bank does not send a final response in time, you can usually go to the Financial Ombudsman Service after the response deadline has passed.
   - Banks normally have 15 business days for payment services or e-money complaints, and 8 weeks for most other complaints.

8. **If this is part of a wider money crisis, get free debt help**
   - If this fee means you cannot cover food, housing, energy, or several debts, contact a free debt adviser.
   - Ask whether Breathing Space may be suitable. It is not automatic, and a debt adviser has to apply if it is right for you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to switch banks today.
- You do not need to close the account today.
- You do not need a full budget today.
- You do not need to write a long complaint today; a short timeline and screenshots are enough to start.

## Important reassurance
An unexpected bank fee can make everything feel urgent at once. You can slow it down by stopping new transactions, checking what is due next, and asking the bank to review the charge and prevent avoidable knock-on harm.

## Scope note
This is first-steps guidance to stabilise the situation, avoid further fees, and start the right bank, payment, or complaint route. Later decisions about borrowing, debt, or switching accounts may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide provides general information for urgent first steps and is not legal, financial, debt, or professional advice. Bank policies and complaint outcomes vary, and fee refunds may be discretionary unless a specific payment-protection route applies.
