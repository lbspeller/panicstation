# What to do if… someone slips a note under your hotel door pressuring you to open it or call a number right away (UK)

## Short answer
Do **not** open the door, call the provided number, or scan any QR codes. Lock all door latches, move to a safe distance from the door, and contact the hotel front desk immediately using the in-room telephone or a verified official number.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door to look for the person or "check" the hallway.
- Do not call the phone number written on the note or reply via text/messaging apps.
- Do not scan QR codes or visit URLs printed on the note.
- Do not believe claims of "urgent maintenance," "emergency inspections," or "credit card issues" without independent verification.
- Do not leave the room alone until you have confirmed the corridor is secure with hotel staff.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the door and move away from the entryway immediately.**  
   Engage the deadbolt, security chain, or swing latch. Turn on the room lights to show the room is occupied, then move to a part of the room where you are not visible through the peephole or the gap under the door.

2. **Contact the hotel reception using a verified internal line.**  
   Use the hotel’s desk phone and select the 'Front Desk' or 'Reception' button. If you use a mobile phone, call the official number found on your booking confirmation or the hotel’s verified website.  
   - State your room number clearly.  
   - Inform them that a note was just slipped under your door pressuring you to call a number or open up.  
   - Request that security or a manager be sent to your floor immediately to inspect the corridor.

3. **Verify the identity of anyone who knocks after your call.**  
   Even if they claim to be the security you requested, do not open the door until you have looked through the peephole and confirmed they are in a proper hotel uniform. If in doubt, keep the door locked and call the front desk again to ask: "Is a member of staff named [Name] standing outside room [X] right now?"

4. **Contact the police if you observe signs of attempted entry.**  
   **Call 999** if you hear someone trying the handle, scratching at the lock, or making verbal threats through the door. If the situation is suspicious but there is no active attempt to enter, you can call **101** to report the incident and obtain a reference number for your insurance or hotel complaint.

5. **Document the note as evidence without excessive handling.**  
   Take a high-resolution photograph of the note where it lies. If you must move it, use a tissue or a clean bag to pick it up by the edges to preserve any potential forensic evidence, then place it in a drawer or envelope.

6. **Request a room change or an escort if you no longer feel safe.**  
   You have the right to ask for a room on a different floor or a different section of the hotel. If you need to leave the building, ask for a security escort to your vehicle or a licensed taxi.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the motive behind the note right now.
- You do not need to provide a statement to the hotel’s head office or corporate brand until you are in a secure location.
- You do not need to check your bank statements immediately unless you have already provided financial details to a number on the note.
- Reporting the number to the **7726** service (text "Call" followed by the number to 7726) can be done once the immediate physical threat is resolved.

## Important reassurance
Scams and intimidation tactics often rely on creating a false sense of urgency to bypass your natural caution. It is a standard and professional safety response to refuse entry and insist on verification through the front desk. Hotel staff are trained to handle these reports and will not be offended by your caution.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate security concern in a hotel setting. It does not cover long-term legal claims against hotel chains or criminal prosecution procedures.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance and does not constitute legal advice. In an emergency where you believe a crime is being committed or your safety is at risk, always prioritize calling **999**. For fraud reporting in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use the **Report Fraud** service (0300 123 2040). In Scotland, report fraud directly to Police Scotland via **101**. If you have shared bank details, call **159** to be connected to your bank's fraud department.
