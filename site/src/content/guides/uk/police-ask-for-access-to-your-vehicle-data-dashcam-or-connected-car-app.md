# What to do if… police ask for access to your vehicle data, dashcam, or connected car app (UK)

## Short answer
Do not unlock an app, hand over a password, or give “just a quick look” as a voluntary on-the-spot favour. Calmly ask what exact data they want, whether it is voluntary or required, and say you want legal advice before deciding about digital access.

## Do not do these things
- Do not give your phone PIN, car-app password, biometric unlock, recovery code, or account login just because someone asks informally.
- Do not delete footage, format an SD card, factory-reset the dashcam, reset the car system, uninstall the app, or “tidy up” logs.
- Do not argue about what speed, GPS, driver-assist, crash, or journey data proves at the roadside.
- Do not sign a consent form, voluntary statement, or data extraction agreement that you do not understand or feel pressured into.
- Do not let anyone scroll through your phone or connected-car app while you are distracted.
- Do not physically obstruct police if they seize hardware or tell you they are using a formal power.

## What to do now
1. **Make the situation safe first.** If this is happening at the roadside, move only if you are told to or it is safe to do so, switch the engine off if appropriate, and keep your hands and voice calm.
2. **Clarify the exact request.** Say: “Can you tell me exactly what you want: dashcam footage, the SD card, the car’s built-in system, crash data, trip logs, location history, my phone, or my connected-car app?”
3. **Ask whether it is voluntary or required.** Say: “Are you asking me to consent voluntarily, or are you requiring this under a legal power? If it is required, what power are you using?”
4. **If it is voluntary, pause.** Say: “I am not refusing to cooperate, but I am not consenting to digital access right now. I want legal advice first.”
5. **If they ask for a password, PIN, login, or key, slow it down further.** Say: “I need legal advice before I answer that.” If they serve a formal written requirement or notice, do not guess, argue, ignore it, or discuss how to comply before legal advice.
6. **If you are arrested, detained at a police station, or about to be questioned, ask for legal advice.** Say clearly: “I want to speak to the duty solicitor or my own solicitor.” Keep answers minimal until you have had that advice.
7. **Preserve what exists without changing it.** Once you are safely parked, avoid anything that deletes, edits, overwrites, uploads selectively, exports selectively, or changes the device state more than necessary. If you are worried footage may overwrite, tell the officer that and ask how they want it preserved without you granting account access.
8. **If hardware is seized, do not resist.** Ask what power is being used, what exactly is being taken, and for a receipt, property record, reference number, or incident number.
9. **If they want cloud or manufacturer-held data, do not log in for them on the spot.** Say: “If you need provider-held data, please contact the provider through the appropriate legal process.”
10. **Write down the basics as soon as you can.** Note the officer’s name or number, police force, time, location, incident or reference number, what was requested, what you said, and what was taken or copied.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether you will later provide a copy, statement, download, password, login, app access, or account authorisation.
- You do not need to interpret speed, GPS trails, braking, driver-assist alerts, crash logs, or journey history on the spot.
- You do not need to contact your insurer, employer, lease company, car manufacturer, app provider, or garage immediately unless there is a separate urgent safety or policy reason.
- You can wait until you are calmer and have legal advice before responding in a documented way.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel intimidated when an officer asks for “just a quick look.” For a voluntary request, asking for the request to be clear, recorded, and checked with legal advice is a reasonable boundary.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The best next move depends on whether you are a witness, victim, suspect, registered keeper, employee driver, app account holder, or passenger; what data is being sought; and what legal power or consent process is being used. Police powers and forms can vary across England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, so later decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. If you are under investigation, unsure whether you are being compelled, served with a formal notice, or feeling pressured, ask for legal advice as soon as possible and avoid on-the-spot decisions about digital access.
