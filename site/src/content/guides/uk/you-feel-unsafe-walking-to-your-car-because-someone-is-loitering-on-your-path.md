# What to do if… you feel unsafe walking to your car because someone is loitering on your path (UK)

## Short answer
Do not walk past them. Go back to a staffed, well-lit place and ask for help or an escort if one is available. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “push through” just to avoid seeming rude or overreacting.
- Do not confront the person, argue, stare them down, or try to test what they want.
- Do not take a quieter shortcut, such as between cars, through a stairwell, or through a side exit, to avoid them.
- Do not dig for keys while walking or stop to message people in the open.
- Do not unlock your car and then pause outside it.
- Do not get into your car and sit there with the doors unlocked while you decide what to do.

## What to do now
1. **Stop and change the plan.** Turn around and go back to the nearest place with people and staff, such as a shop, reception desk, petrol station kiosk, venue entrance, hotel lobby, or transport office.
2. **Put people and visibility around you.** Stand near a counter, front desk, main entrance, pay station, or other place with footfall. Do not wait alone outside or in a quiet corner.
3. **Ask for an escort if one is available.** Keep it simple: “I don’t feel safe walking to my car. Can someone walk with me to bay or level X?” If they cannot, ask if you can wait inside until a friend, relative, colleague, taxi, or private hire vehicle arrives.
4. **Move the pickup point to where you are safe.** Call someone from inside and ask them to meet you at the staffed entrance, not at your car. If you need paid transport, book a licensed taxi or private hire vehicle from inside and wait where staff can see you.
5. **Use 999 or 101 depending on urgency.**
   - **Call 999** if you feel in immediate danger, the person is approaching you, following you, threatening you, or preventing you from leaving.
   - **Call 101** if you are not in immediate danger but want police advice or want to report concerning behaviour.
   Give your exact location, such as the car park name, site name, nearest entrance, level, row, or bay, and say where you are now: “I’m inside by reception.”
   - **If you cannot speak on a mobile 999 call:** stay on the line and follow the operator prompts. You may be asked to press 55 or tap to confirm you need police help.
6. **When you do reach the car, leave without delay.** Have your keys ready before you leave the safe place. Get in, lock the doors immediately, start the engine, and leave the area if you can drive safely. If you still feel followed, drive to a busy, well-lit place with staff, such as a petrol station, and call 999 if you need urgent help.
7. **If you cannot safely go to the car, do not force it.** Stay inside the staffed place and ask staff to call security, contact the car park operator, or help you arrange a safer way home.
8. **After you are safe, tell the site.** If this happened on private property, such as a store, venue, workplace, hotel, hospital, or managed car park, consider notifying management or security so they can check the area and watch for repeated concerns.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether the person is “actually dangerous.”
- You do not need to take photos, collect proof, or confront anyone to justify leaving.
- You do not need to make a formal report immediately if you are shaken.
- You do not need to stick to your original plan, route, or pickup point.
- You do not need to explain yourself to the person or to bystanders.

## Important reassurance
Feeling unsafe is enough reason to step back and change course. Many people freeze or second-guess themselves in moments like this; choosing distance, light, staff, and other people is a sensible response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you avoid being isolated and get to a safer pause. Later decisions, such as making a report, raising a car park safety concern, or changing routines, can be handled after the immediate situation is over and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 999.
