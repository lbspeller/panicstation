# What to do if… someone offers you a drink you did not see poured or opened and you feel uneasy (UK)

## Short answer
Do not drink it. Create distance and get support from venue staff, a trusted friend, or another safe person so you can leave or stay away from the person on your own terms.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drink it to be polite or to stop the pressure.
- Do not keep holding the drink while you explain yourself; put it down and move away.
- Do not go somewhere more private with the person to talk it out, such as outside, a car, toilets, or a quiet corner.
- Do not let the person choose how you get home if you feel uneasy.
- Do not confront them, accuse them, or try to investigate in the moment.
- Do not blame yourself for being unsure; unease is enough reason to stop.

## What to do now
1. **End the drink conversation.** Use a short line such as: “No thanks.” Put the drink down on a table or bar, step away, and keep your hands free.
2. **Move to a safer visible place.** Go to the bar, a staffed area, venue security, or a group you trust. If you are with friends, say clearly: “I am not comfortable. Stay with me.”
3. **Tell staff what you need in plain words.** Say: “Someone is pressuring me to drink something I do not trust. I need help getting away from them and leaving safely.”
4. **Use Ask for Angela only if it helps.** In London, some participating venues use the code phrase “Ask for Angela” for people who feel unsafe, vulnerable, or threatened. If staff do not recognise it, use the direct words in step 3.
5. **Make the exit plan yours, not theirs.** Ask staff to keep the person away, walk you to a taxi rank, help you meet a friend at the door, wait with you while you arrange transport, or call security or police if needed.
6. **Treat sudden illness as urgent.** If you feel suddenly unwell, confused, faint, unable to stay awake, or much more drunk than expected, tell staff or a trusted person: “I feel unwell and need help now.” Call 999 if you are seriously unwell, injured, in immediate danger, or need urgent support right away. For urgent non-emergency medical advice, use NHS 111 where available, or your local NHS urgent advice service.
7. **Get specialist support if spiking or sexual contact may have happened.** You can contact NHS services, a Sexual Assault Referral Centre, or a sexual violence support service without deciding about police now.
8. **Preserve options lightly, only if manageable.** Keep receipts, messages, ride-history, and the venue name or approximate time. You do not need to confront anyone or prove anything in the moment.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide tonight whether to report to police.
- You do not have to work out exactly what happened before getting help or leaving.
- You do not have to send messages, explain yourself, or smooth things over with the person.
- You do not have to keep the night going.
- You do not have to make long-term decisions while you are scared, unwell, or under pressure.

## Important reassurance
Feeling uneasy is a valid reason to stop. Many people freeze, fawn, go quiet, or try to avoid making a scene when they feel pressured; that does not mean you agreed. The aim right now is to get you seen by safer people, supported, and away from the pressure.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: immediate safety, getting support, and preserving your choices. Later decisions about medical care, reporting, evidence, or follow-up support can be taken with specialist help when you are safe and steadier.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel at immediate risk or seriously unwell, call 999. If you have worrying symptoms and it is not an emergency, use NHS 111 where available, or your local NHS urgent advice service.
