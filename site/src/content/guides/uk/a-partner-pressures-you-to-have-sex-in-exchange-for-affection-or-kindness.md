# What to do if… a partner pressures you to have sex in exchange for affection or kindness (UK)

## Short answer
You do not owe sex to receive affection, attention, or basic kindness. Create distance from the pressure, get to a safer pause, and contact confidential specialist support.

## Do not do these things
- Do not treat sex as something you have to provide to earn kindness, affection, attention, peace, or a basic level of care.
- Do not negotiate consent while you feel cornered, guilty, frightened, worn down, numb, or under threat of anger or withdrawal.
- Do not confront them if that may escalate, especially if they block exits, take your phone, shout, follow you, or you fear violence.
- Do not isolate yourself afterwards; pressure is often easier to recognise when you speak to someone safe.
- Do not assume it “doesn’t count” because you are partners, married, living together, or there was no physical force.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If you can, move to a room with a door and an exit route. Take your phone, keys, shoes, coat, medication, or anything else you would need if you had to leave quickly.
2. **Use one short line, then stop debating.** For example: “I’m not having sex. Stop asking.” Repeat it once if needed, then focus on ending the interaction rather than persuading them.
3. **Create an out that does not need their permission.** Step outside for air, take a shower, say you need the toilet, go to a shop, call someone, or move to a public place if that is safer than staying.
4. **Contact a real person now.** Message or call someone you trust with a simple line such as: “Can you call me now? I need a reason to leave.” If speaking is not safe, send a brief code message you have used before.
5. **Call 999 if you are in immediate danger.** Use 999 if you are at risk right now, if you cannot leave safely, if they are threatening you, or if you need urgent medical help.
6. **Contact specialist sexual violence support, even if you are unsure what to call it.**
   - In England and Wales, if you are 16 or over, the 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line can be contacted free on 0808 500 2222 or by online chat.
   - In Scotland, Rape Crisis Scotland can be contacted free on 08088 01 03 02 every day, 5pm to midnight.
   - In Northern Ireland, Rape Crisis NI has an information and support line on 0800 0246 991; check the website for current opening hours.
   - In Northern Ireland, The Rowan Sexual Assault Referral Centre provides 24-hour support and services.
7. **If there has been recent unwanted sexual contact and you want medical or practical support, consider a Sexual Assault Referral Centre.** In many areas, SARCs can offer medical, practical, and emotional support, and you do not have to decide about reporting to police before asking for help. Access routes vary by area.
8. **Only if it is safe, make a private note for yourself.** Write the date, time, what was said or done, and what you remember. Keep it brief and somewhere the other person cannot access.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to report anything to the police.
- You do not need to label it as abuse, assault, coercion, or anything else before getting support.
- You do not need to decide the future of the relationship tonight.
- You do not need perfect wording, a complete explanation, or proof before you speak to a specialist service.
- You do not need to convince your partner that the pressure was wrong before you protect your own space.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, going quiet, appeasing, or going along with something to reduce conflict can be survival responses. Pressure through withdrawal of affection, guilt, sulking, anger, or fear can be deeply harmful, and you can ask for support without having to justify why it affected you.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on safety, stabilising the moment, and getting confidential support. Later decisions about safety planning, housing, reporting, health care, legal protections, or counselling may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If you are not sure what to do next, contacting a specialist sexual violence or domestic abuse service is usually a safer next step than handling it alone.
