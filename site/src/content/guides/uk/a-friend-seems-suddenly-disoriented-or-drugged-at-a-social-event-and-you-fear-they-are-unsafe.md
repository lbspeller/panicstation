# What to do if… a friend seems suddenly disoriented or drugged at a social event and you fear they are unsafe (UK)

## Short answer
Stay with your friend, move them to a safer controlled place with one other trusted person if possible, and get venue staff or security involved now. Call 999 or 112 if they are very unwell, injured, collapsing, hard to wake, having breathing problems, or in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t leave them alone to “sleep it off” or let them go outside with someone you do not trust.
- Don’t confront a suspected person by yourself or start a fight at the venue.
- Don’t force food or drinks into them, don’t give them alcohol, and don’t use “sobering up” tricks.
- Don’t pressure them to explain, decide what happened, or report to police on the spot.
- Don’t post about it on social media or share details with the wider group while things are unclear.
- Don’t assume they are “just drunk” if the change is sudden, unusual, or worrying.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safety bubble.** Go to your friend, use their name, and say something simple like: “I’m staying with you. Let’s go somewhere quieter.” Bring one other trusted person with you if possible.
2. **Move to a controlled space and involve staff.** Ask the bar, club, event staff, or security for a welfare space, office, medical point, or other supervised area. Tell them: “My friend is suddenly disoriented and may have been targeted. Please help us stay safe and stop anyone else taking them outside.”
3. **Keep the person of concern away without escalating.** If someone is hovering, pressuring, or trying to separate your friend from you, ask staff or security to manage that person. Stay focused on getting your friend away from the situation, not on proving what happened.
4. **Check for emergency signs and call 999 or 112 if needed.** Call now if your friend is collapsing, hard to wake, having breathing problems, having a seizure, repeatedly vomiting, severely confused, injured, or in immediate danger.
5. **Keep them monitored while help is coming or while you leave safely.** Keep speaking calmly, watch their breathing and responsiveness, and do not leave them alone. If they become very drowsy or vomit, place them on their side in the recovery position if you can do so safely and keep monitoring them.
6. **Get medical help if they are unwell but it is not an immediate emergency.** Use NHS 111 where available, local urgent medical help, venue medical staff, or A&E if symptoms cannot be safely managed at the venue.
7. **Use the police route only if it helps safety or your friend wants it.** If it is not an emergency but you need police advice or want to report spiking or a related concern, call 101 or use the local police reporting route. If the risk becomes urgent, call 999 or 112 instead.
8. **Get specialist sexual violence support without committing to reporting.** If your friend thinks something sexual may have happened, or they are unsure, a local sexual assault referral service can offer medical care and support. They can usually explain options without forcing a police report.
9. **Capture basic details quietly if it is safe.** Note the time symptoms began, what they drank or ate, where you were, who was nearby, and any names or descriptions of a person of concern. If there is a remaining drink, container, or item, keep it with you only if that is safe and does not escalate the situation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether this was spiking, assault, alcohol, illness, or something else.
- You do not need to confront anyone, gather proof, or persuade others to believe you.
- You do not need to decide about police reporting right now unless there is immediate danger.
- If your friend may want to report later, avoid washing clothes or throwing away items for now and keep anything they were wearing or carrying safe and separate, but only if that is practical and does not distress them.

## Important reassurance
A frightened, drugged, overwhelmed, or unwell person may seem confused, embarrassed, blank, or not like themselves. Your job is not to solve the mystery tonight; it is to stay with them, reduce risk, and get them to safer support.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the earliest safe pause. Later choices about medical tests, reporting, statements, evidence, or ongoing support can be made when your friend is safer, steadier, and supported by appropriate specialist services.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, investigative, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If someone is in immediate danger or severely unwell, call 999 or 112. Your friend’s consent and wellbeing come first; specialist services can support them even if they are unsure what happened or do not want to report to police.
