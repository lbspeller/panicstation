# What to do if… you reset a device and realise your backups are not available to restore (UK)

## Short answer
Treat a sudden loss of SMS as a potential security breach (SIM-swap) or a network provisioning error. Secure your primary email and bank accounts immediately using non-SMS methods and contact your mobile provider to check for unauthorized account changes.

## Do not do these things
- Don't repeatedly request new codes every few minutes; this often triggers security rate-limits that block delivery for several hours.
- Don't follow instructions or click links in any "verification successful" or "account change" texts you did not initiate.
- Don't provide any security codes to anyone over the phone, even if they claim to be from your network provider or bank.
- Don't factory reset your device as a first step, as this may delete the authenticator apps or digital keys you need for account recovery.
- Don't assume the issue is a temporary network glitch if you have also lost the ability to make voice calls or see a "No Service" alert.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate indicators of an unauthorized SIM-swap.**
   - **Observe your signal bars or network status.** If you see "No Service," "SOS only," or "SIM Not Provisioned" while in a known good coverage area, the SIM may have been deactivated.
   - **Check your primary email for alerts.** Look for notifications regarding a PAC (Porting Authorisation Code) request, a SIM swap confirmation, or an eSIM activation.
   - **Ask a contact to call your number.** If the call rings on another device or goes to an unfamiliar voicemail, your number has been ported or swapped.

2. **Secure your financial and primary digital accounts.**
   - **Call 159 immediately.** This is the UK's secure hotline to connect you directly to your bank's fraud department if you suspect your mobile number has been compromised.
   - **Log into your primary email on a trusted computer.** Change the password and, if possible, switch your Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) from SMS to an authenticator app or a Passkey.
   - **Update your password manager.** Ensure the master password is changed if it was previously accessible via SMS recovery.

3. **Contact your mobile network provider from a different phone.**
   - **State clearly that you suspect a SIM-swap or unauthorized port.**
   - **Verify the status of your SIM.** Ask when the last SIM change occurred and if there is an active request to move your number to another network.
   - **Request a "Port-Out Lock" or "Account Freeze."** Ask the provider to block any further changes to the account until you can verify your identity in person.
   - **Check for "Premium SMS" or "Short-code" barring.** If your line is active but codes aren't arriving, ask if a network-level bar is preventing messages from 5-digit or 6-digit short-codes.

4. **Report the incident to the appropriate UK authorities.**
   - **In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland:** Report the fraud or cybercrime to the **Report Fraud** service (formerly Action Fraud) at reportfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040.
   - **In Scotland:** Report the incident to **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
   - **Forward any suspicious "phishing" texts to 7726.** This is the free UK-wide service for reporting scam messages to mobile providers.

5. **Initiate an official complaint if the provider fails to resolve the issue.**
   - **Request a formal complaint reference number.** Record the time of the call and the name of the representative.
   - **Note the escalation timeline.** For complaints raised on or after 8 April 2026, you can escalate to an Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) scheme (Communications Ombudsman or CISAS) after 6 weeks if the issue remains unresolved.

## What can wait
- You do not need to purchase a new handset or change your long-term phone number immediately.
- You can delay the process of notifying all your casual contacts about the number issue until your primary accounts (bank, email) are secured.
- You do not need to perform a full security audit of your social media accounts until your primary recovery email is under your control.

## Important reassurance
Loss of SMS is frequently caused by mundane technical issues, such as a faulty SIM card or a network update that has incorrectly barred "short-code" messages. While it can be a sign of fraud, taking immediate steps to secure your bank and email accounts significantly reduces the risk of loss while the network issue is investigated.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a sudden loss of SMS and verification codes in the UK. It does not cover long-term identity theft recovery or technical troubleshooting for specific mobile handset hardware.

## Important note
This information is provided for immediate stabilization and is not legal, technical, or financial advice. Jurisdictional procedures for reporting fraud changed in early 2026; always use official government or police websites to verify the latest contact methods. If you suspect you have already lost money, contact your bank via the number on the back of your card or via the 159 service immediately.
