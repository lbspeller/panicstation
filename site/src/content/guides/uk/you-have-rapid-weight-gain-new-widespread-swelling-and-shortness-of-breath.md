# What to do if… you have rapid weight gain, new widespread swelling, and shortness of breath (UK)

## Short answer
Treat rapid new swelling with shortness of breath as urgent. If breathing is severe, your chest feels tight or heavy, you are suddenly confused, you faint, or your lips or skin look very pale, blue, or grey, call 999 now or go to A&E.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “sleep it off” if you are breathless at rest, getting worse, or cannot lie flat.
- Do not take extra water tablets, diuretics, laxatives, or “detox” products unless a clinician has told you to.
- Do not drink large amounts quickly to “flush it out”, or suddenly stop all fluids, unless you have been given a clear clinical plan.
- Do not start or increase NSAID painkillers such as ibuprofen or naproxen unless a clinician or pharmacist tells you they are safe for you.
- Do not drive yourself to hospital if you are significantly short of breath, dizzy, faint, or confused.

## What to do now
1. **Check for 999 signs first.** Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if you have severe difficulty breathing, gasping, choking, or cannot get words out; chest tightness, heaviness, or chest pain; lips or skin that look very pale, blue, or grey; sudden confusion; fainting or feeling about to faint; or swelling of the face, tongue, or throat with hives, hoarseness, wheeze, or trouble swallowing.
2. **If it is not at 999 level, get urgent medical advice today.** Call 111, or use 111 online if you are in England. If you are elsewhere in the UK, use your local NHS urgent-care route if available. Say: “rapid weight gain, new widespread swelling over one to two days, and shortness of breath.” Ask where you should be seen today.
3. **Get into a safer breathing position while help is arranged.** Sit upright, lean slightly forward if that helps, and support yourself with pillows. Avoid lying flat and keep exertion minimal.
4. **Write down the key symptoms in one minute.** Note whether you can speak a full sentence, whether breathing is worse lying down, whether you woke breathless, whether you have chest tightness or pain, a new wheeze, a fast pounding heartbeat, much less urine than usual, and where the swelling is: ankles, legs, belly, hands, rings, face, or eyelids.
5. **Put essential information in one place.** Take your medicines, recent medication changes, supplements, missed doses, weight today and yesterday if known, when the swelling started, and any heart, kidney, liver, lung, or recent infection history.
6. **If you are pregnant or gave birth in the last few weeks, say this clearly.** Contact 111, your maternity unit or maternity triage number if you have one, or A&E today, and mention the swelling and breathlessness. Also mention any severe headache, vision changes, pain below the ribs, vomiting, feeling very unwell, or sudden swelling of the face, hands, or feet.
7. **If you already have a heart, kidney, or fluid-balance action plan, use it now.** Follow the plan you were given, including any red-flag instructions, and still seek urgent review through the contact route on the plan, 111, 999, or A&E as appropriate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to decide long-term salt, fluid, diet, or medication changes right now unless you have a clear plan from your clinical team.
- You do not need perfect measurements before asking for help.
- You do not need to pack everything before being seen; medicines and the key notes are enough if you can get them safely.

## Important reassurance
Rapid swelling with breathlessness can feel frightening, and it is reasonable to ask for urgent help. You are not wasting anyone’s time by getting checked early.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Tests, diagnosis, medicine changes, and follow-up plans need clinical assessment, and later decisions may need specialist medical help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical or professional advice, and it cannot diagnose the cause of your symptoms. If symptoms are severe, worsening, or feel unsafe, use emergency services.
