# What to do if… you develop a rapidly enlarging neck swelling that makes swallowing uncomfortable (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as urgent. If the swelling is growing quickly, swallowing is getting harder, your throat feels tight, your voice changes, or breathing feels affected, call 999 now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait it out if the swelling is enlarging over minutes or hours, or your swallowing is worsening.
- Do not eat or drink to test it if swallowing is uncomfortable or getting worse.
- Do not stand, pace, or walk around if you feel faint, dizzy, very weak, or suddenly unwell.
- Do not take a new medicine, antihistamine, supplement, or sedative instead of getting urgent help.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you feel faint, short of breath, confused, drowsy, or the swelling is progressing quickly.

## What to do now
1. **Check for airway warning signs now. Call 999 if any are present:**
   - you are struggling to swallow, especially your own saliva, or you are drooling
   - your throat feels tight, your voice becomes hoarse or muffled, or you cannot speak clearly
   - your breathing is fast or noisy, you are wheezing, gasping, or feel like you are choking
   - you feel suddenly dizzy, very weak, confused, drowsy, or you collapse
   - your lips, mouth, tongue, throat, face, or neck are suddenly swelling

2. **If you have a prescribed adrenaline auto-injector and this could be a severe allergic reaction:**
   - Use it immediately, following the instructions on the device.
   - Call 999 straight after and say “anaphylaxis” or “severe allergic reaction”, even if you start to feel better.
   - If you are alone, use the injector first, then call 999.
   - If symptoms have not improved after 5 minutes and you have a second injector, use the second injector.

3. **Position yourself safely while help is on the way:**
   - If you feel faint, dizzy, or very unwell, lie flat and raise your legs if you can.
   - If breathing is difficult, sit upright enough to make breathing easier.
   - Avoid standing or walking around.

4. **If you are not in immediate danger but the swelling is enlarging quickly or swallowing is uncomfortable or worsening:**
   - Get urgent medical help today.
   - In England, Scotland, or Wales, call 111 for urgent advice.
   - In Northern Ireland, use your GP out-of-hours or local urgent care phone service for advice on where to be seen today.
   - If you are told to go to A&E, go. If symptoms worsen while you are deciding or travelling, call 999.

5. **Gather key information for clinicians if it is safe and takes less than a minute:**
   - note the time it started and how fast it is growing
   - take one quick photo of the swelling if it does not delay getting help
   - list any new medicines in the last few weeks, especially blood pressure medicines such as ACE inhibitors
   - note any recent stings, foods, infections, dental problems, injuries, or known allergies
   - bring your medicines, or a photo of the labels

6. **If you might pass out or you are alone:**
   - unlock your door if you can do this safely
   - keep your phone close and charged
   - call or message someone nearby to stay in contact until you are seen

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause right now.
- You do not need to decide whether it is allergy, infection, a gland, thyroid swelling, dental swelling, or something else.
- You do not need to book routine GP tests before urgent triage.
- You do not need to decide about referrals or specialists now.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to feel alarmed by neck swelling that changes quickly. Urgent assessment is the safest next step because clinicians can check your airway, swallowing, and possible causes.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, aimed at keeping you safe and getting you to the right urgent service. Later decisions about tests, medicines, treatment, or referral may need specialist medical help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, or a substitute for a clinician’s assessment. If swallowing or breathing is affected, or the swelling is rapidly increasing, treat it as an emergency.
