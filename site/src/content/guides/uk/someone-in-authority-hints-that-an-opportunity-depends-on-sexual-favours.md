# What to do if… someone in authority hints that an opportunity depends on sexual favours (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself to a safer pause and stop any one-to-one private contact with them for now. You do not need to argue, negotiate, explain yourself, or prove anything in the moment.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree to sexual contact to keep the opportunity open if you don’t want to.
- Don’t go to a private meeting, car, hotel room, home, or off-site chat with them alone.
- Don’t delete messages, emails, DMs, calendar invites, call logs, or notes, even if you feel embarrassed or scared.
- Don’t confront them alone. If you choose to raise it later, use a safer route rather than an in-person showdown.
- Don’t make irreversible moves, such as quitting, withdrawing, or moving out, while you are frightened or under pressure. Buy time first.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance and end the interaction.** If you are still in contact, use a neutral exit: “I need to go now,” “I’ll follow up by email,” or “I’m not available for that.” Move to a public or safer space.
2. **Move any necessary contact into writing.** You do not have to reply immediately. If a reply is necessary, keep it brief and professional, and use email or text where possible. Avoid being pulled into a private call or closed-door meeting.
3. **Write a memory note now.** Record the date, time, location, what was said as closely as you can remember, what opportunity was being offered or withheld, any implied threat, who else was nearby, and what you replied. Save it somewhere you control, such as emailing it to yourself.
4. **Preserve what already exists.** Save screenshots or exports of messages, emails, meeting invites, call logs, and documents connected to the opportunity. Keep originals where possible and avoid editing them.
5. **Tell one safer person today.** Choose someone who can steady you and help you avoid being alone with the person. If this is linked to work, consider a union rep, trusted HR contact, or another safer reporting route. If it is linked to education or training, consider student support, welfare, safeguarding, or a trusted staff member. If it is linked to housing, consider a senior contact, letting agent, housing provider, council/private renting support, or independent housing advice.
6. **Get confidential specialist support, even if you are unsure what you want to do.** A sexual violence support service or general victim support service can help you think through immediate safety and options without forcing you to report.
7. **If you feel at immediate risk, prioritise safety.** Get to somewhere safer and call **999** or **112** if there is immediate danger, violence, a threat of violence, or a crime happening now.

## What can wait
- You do **not** have to decide today whether to make a formal complaint, report to the police, resign, withdraw, move out, or take legal action.
- You do **not** need perfect wording, perfect evidence, or a clear label for what happened before getting support.
- You do **not** need to confront the person or keep engaging with them to confirm what they meant.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, staying polite, trying to appease them, or feeling confused can happen when someone uses power in a sexual way. Not responding perfectly does not make this your fault. You deserve safety and fairness without having to trade sexual access for an opportunity.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance to help you stabilise, reduce risk, and keep your options open. Later decisions about complaints, employment or education procedures, housing action, police reporting, or legal advice can be taken with specialist support when you are ready.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999 or 112. For tailored help, consider confidential specialist support and, where relevant, independent employment, education, housing, or legal advice.
