# What to do if… you notice someone repeatedly positioning themselves to intercept you at doors or narrow passages (UK)

## Short answer
Do not go through the next door, lift, stairwell, narrow corridor, or other pinch point with them. Step back into a safer, staffed or occupied place, and call 999 if you feel in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not enter a lift, stairwell, narrow corridor, car park stair door, or doorway if they appear to be waiting to funnel you through it.
- Do not test the situation by letting them get close at a door to see what happens.
- Do not confront them in a confined space or let them block you between a door and a wall.
- Do not go straight to your home, a quiet side entrance, or an isolated parking area if you think you are being targeted.
- Do not get distracted unlocking doors, finding keys, or looking down at your phone while they are close behind you.
- Do not accept “help” that moves you into a more secluded place, such as “come this way” or “use this entrance”.

## What to do now
1. **Break the pattern before the next pinch point.** Stop before the next door, lift, stairwell, narrow corridor, gate, turnstile, or underpass. Turn around, step back into a busier area, or enter the nearest open, staffed place such as a shop, café, reception, station desk, hotel lobby, or security point.
2. **Keep space and keep an exit.** Stand where you are not boxed in, near other people, and with a clear route back out. If you are at a building entrance, do not go in alone; wait for other people, re-route to a staffed entrance, or ask staff to let you through safely.
3. **Use people and authority on-site.** Tell a staff member, security guard, concierge, driver, or reception worker plainly: “I do not feel safe. Someone keeps positioning themselves to intercept me at doors. Can you stay with me, call security, or help me leave safely?” If you are in a residential building, return to the main entrance or concierge rather than using a quieter side door.
4. **Call 999 if it feels urgent.** If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 999 and ask for the police. If you cannot safely speak, call 999, listen to the operator, cough or tap if you can, and press 55 if prompted on a mobile.
5. **If it is not an emergency, report it while it is fresh.** Call 101, or use the online reporting route signposted from GOV.UK or Police.uk for your area. Say that the same person has repeatedly positioned themselves at specific doors or chokepoints, and give the exact locations, times, and what happened.
6. **Capture only the details you can safely note.** Without moving closer or escalating, note the location, time, door or chokepoint, direction of travel, and a brief description such as clothing, height, build, bag, or distinguishing features. If you are in a staffed place, ask staff to note it too.
7. **Use StreetSafe only for public-place safety concerns.** If you are not reporting a crime or emergency, but want to flag a public place where you felt unsafe because someone was following, waiting, or verbally abusing you, you can use StreetSafe anonymously. If someone is in immediate danger or a crime is happening, use 999 instead.
8. **Reset your route once you are safer.** Choose a more public route, travel with others if possible, ask staff or security for an escort, or arrange to meet someone at a busy nearby place before going to your front door.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this is definitely stalking, harassment, loitering, or a misunderstanding.
- You do not need to gather perfect evidence before contacting police, staff, or security.
- You do not need to take photos or videos if that would bring them closer, provoke confrontation, or slow you down.
- You do not need to write a detailed statement, contact building management, or post publicly while you are still trying to get safe.
- If you might need emergency help by text because of a hearing or speech impairment, you can later check Relay UK and emergencySMS registration. This is not something to set up during the incident.

## Important reassurance
Repeatedly noticing someone position themselves at doors, exits, lifts, stairwells, or other chokepoints is enough reason to change course and get support. You do not have to prove their intent before creating distance and choosing a safer route.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the moment and reduce immediate risk. If this keeps happening near your home, work, commute, or regular places, later decisions may need help from police, building security, victim support, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe or threatened, treat it as a safety issue and contact emergency services.
