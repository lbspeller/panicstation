# What to do if… you receive an official letter saying you missed a court date you did not know about (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the letter’s authenticity through official channels and contact the issuing court immediately to confirm if a conviction, judgment, or warrant has been issued in your absence.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the letter; missing court proceedings can lead to arrest warrants, bailiff action, or high-interest debts.
- Do not use any phone numbers or web links provided in the letter until you have verified them through an independent government website.
- Do not pay any "immediate" fines or settlements mentioned in the letter without first confirming the case exists with the court office.
- Do not assume the matter is a mistake that will "sort itself out" if you just wait.
- Do not provide bank details or sensitive personal data to any unsolicited caller claiming to be from "the court."

## What to do now
1. **Verify the letter using the official "Find a court or tribunal" service.**
   - Visit the official GOV.UK "Find a court or tribunal" website to get a verified phone number or email for the court named in your letter.
   - Search by the court name or the location provided in the letter.

2. **Call the court office to confirm the status of the case.**
   - Provide the case reference number or your full name and date of birth to the staff.
   - Ask specifically if the case was heard in your absence and if any "warrant with bail" or "warrant without bail" (arrest warrant) has been issued.
   - Confirm if a conviction has been recorded (criminal) or a default judgment entered (civil/CCJ).

3. **Request a 'Statutory Declaration' if it is a Magistrates' Court case (England & Wales).**
   - **Inform the court staff that you had no knowledge of the proceedings.**
   - Ask for a "Statutory Declaration" form (under Section 14 or 16E of the Magistrates’ Courts Act 1980) if you were convicted without knowing about the case.
   - Note that you usually have 21 days from the moment you found out about the case to submit this declaration.
   - Prepare to make the declaration under oath before a magistrate or a solicitor; be aware that lying in a statutory declaration is perjury.

4. **Prepare a plea if the case involved a Single Justice Procedure (SJP) notice.**
   - **Ensure you have a plea ready (Guilty or Not Guilty) to submit alongside your statutory declaration.**
   - The court will usually require you to respond to the original charges at the same time the declaration is made.

5. **Apply for a 'Set Aside' if it is a County Court civil case (CCJ).**
   - **Ask the court for Form N244 (Application Notice) to set aside a default judgment.**
   - Be prepared to explain why you did not receive the papers (e.g., proof of a previous address or postal issues).
   - Check if you are eligible for "Help with Fees" if you cannot afford the application fee.

6. **Update your contact details on the court system immediately.**
   - **Ensure the court has your current, correct service address.**
   - Request that all future correspondence regarding this specific case number be sent to this verified address.

7. **Gather evidence of non-receipt.**
   - **Collect documents that prove you lived elsewhere or could not have received the notice.**
   - Indented items for evidence:
     - Council tax bills from your actual address at the time.
     - Utility bills or tenancy agreements.
     - Mail redirection confirmations.

8. **Contact a solicitor if the court mentions an active warrant.**
   - **Seek professional legal advice before attending court in person if an arrest warrant is active.**
   - Ask the solicitor about "surrendering" to the warrant in a controlled way to avoid being arrested at home or work.

9. **In Scotland or Northern Ireland, ask for the specific local equivalent process.**
   - **Ask the court staff for the procedure to "re-open" or "recall" a case heard in absence.**
   - Procedures in the Sheriff Court (Scotland) or Magistrates' Court (NI) differ significantly from England & Wales.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare a full trial defense or witness statements today.
- You do not need to pay the underlying fine or debt until the court confirms the process for setting it aside or reopening the case.
- You do not need to contact the "Claimant" or the other party in a civil case until you have spoken to the court.

## Important reassurance
It is common for court paperwork to be sent to an old address or lost in the post. The legal system has specific, established procedures like "statutory declarations" and "setting aside" judgments specifically to help people who genuinely did not know a case was happening. Taking these steps promptly usually stops enforcement action.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying and responding to unknown court proceedings. It does not cover the legal strategy for the trial itself or long-term appeals.

## Important note
This information is for guidance and does not constitute legal advice. If you are facing criminal charges or a significant civil judgment, you should consult a qualified legal professional or Citizens Advice. Providing false information in a statutory declaration is a serious criminal offense.
