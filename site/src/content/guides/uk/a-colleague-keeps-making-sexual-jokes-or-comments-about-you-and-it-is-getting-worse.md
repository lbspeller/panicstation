# What to do if… a colleague keeps making sexual jokes or comments about you and it is getting worse (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself to the safest available pause at work, then make a brief record of what happened and use a trusted workplace route to raise it. If there is immediate danger, violence, threats of violence, or you are trapped, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel you have to laugh it off or join in to keep the peace.
- Do not meet the person alone to clear the air if the behaviour is escalating or you feel uneasy.
- Do not delete messages, emails, chat logs, notes, or calendar records that show what has happened.
- Do not send a long message while you are upset; keep any communication short, factual, and safe to show someone else.
- Do not secretly record, access files you should not access, or move workplace data in a way that could breach workplace rules.
- Do not assume it has to become physical before you can ask for help.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause today.** Move near other people if you can, change where you are working, and avoid being alone with the colleague in enclosed spaces. If there is immediate danger, violence, threats of violence, or you are trapped, leave the area if you can and call 999.
2. **Tell one safe person at work what is happening.** Choose a manager, HR contact, dignity or harassment contact, union representative, or another trusted senior person. Say plainly: “A colleague keeps making sexual jokes or comments about me, it is getting worse, and I need help to stop it.”
3. **Start a simple incident record.** Write down the date, time, place, what was said or done, the exact words if you remember them, who saw or heard it, and how it affected your work. Keep the record somewhere the colleague cannot access.
4. **Preserve written evidence carefully.** Keep relevant emails, chat messages, screenshots, meeting invites, notes, or rota details where you are allowed to keep them. If you are unsure what is allowed, ask HR, a manager you trust, or your union representative how to preserve records without breaching workplace rules.
5. **Use one clear boundary only if it feels safe.** In person or in writing, you can say: “Stop making sexual jokes or comments about me. It is not welcome.” Then disengage. You do not need to debate it, justify yourself, or give repeated warnings.
6. **Use the workplace route that feels safest.** You can raise it informally with a manager or HR, ask for it to be handled under a dignity or harassment policy, or raise a formal grievance if it is too serious to handle informally or it is not stopping. If your manager is involved or unsafe, go to another manager, HR, a union representative, or a named harassment contact.
7. **Ask for immediate practical protections.** Ask for specific steps such as different seating, different rota arrangements, not being scheduled alone with the colleague, a different reporting line, or a named person to contact if it happens again.
8. **Say clearly if you fear backlash.** When you report it, say: “I am worried about being treated badly for raising this.” Ask who you should tell if there are changes to your hours, duties, treatment, rota, workload, or workplace relationships after you raise it.
9. **Get outside workplace advice if you feel stuck.** Acas can explain workplace options and grievance steps. A union representative, if you have one, may be able to help you prepare what to say and attend meetings with you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a police report.
- You do not need a perfect timeline before raising the concern.
- You do not need to prove what happened beyond doubt before asking your workplace to act.
- You do not need to confront the colleague again if it feels unsafe or you have already been clear.
- You do not need to make big job decisions today, such as resigning, transferring, or going public.

## Important reassurance
Sexual jokes or comments at work can be serious even when someone calls them banter or says they did not mean harm. Feeling anxious, frozen, angry, ashamed, or unsure is a common reaction, and you are allowed to ask for the behaviour to stop and for your workplace to protect you.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stabilise the situation and reduce harm. Employers in the UK generally have legal duties to take reasonable steps to prevent sexual harassment and to handle concerns fairly and sensitively, but later decisions may need specialist help from Acas, a union representative, an adviser, or a legal professional.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or have been threatened or assaulted, prioritise getting to safety and contacting emergency services.
