# What to do if… a buy-now-pay-later payment is about to retry and you cannot afford it (UK)

## Short answer
Act before the retry if you can: either pay the instalment safely, or ask the BNPL provider to move or pause the payment and confirm what fees may apply.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore the warning and hope it disappears; missed or failed payments can lead to late fees, debt contact, or account consequences depending on the provider.
- Don’t let the retry take money needed for rent, council tax, utilities, food, travel, childcare, or essential medicines.
- Don’t rely on getting a replacement card as the fix; some recurring card payments may continue after a card is replaced.
- Don’t try several cards without noting what you authorised, because it can make the payment record harder to follow.
- Don’t agree to a new payment plan you already know you cannot meet just to end the chat.

## What to do now
1. **Open the BNPL app or website and check the retry details.**
   - Note the amount, retry date or time, payment method, and any late fee shown.
   - Screenshot the warning, payment schedule, order number, and any fee message.

2. **Protect essential money first.**
   - Check whether the retry would leave you short for essentials before you press pay.
   - If paying now is safe, pay before the retry and save the confirmation.
   - If paying now is not safe, do not drain essential money just to stop the warning.

3. **Use the provider’s in-app options before the retry.**
   - Look for wording such as change due date, move payment, reschedule, payment arrangement, hardship, pause, or contact support.
   - If the app lets you move the payment, choose a date you can realistically meet and save the confirmation.
   - If there is no option, use chat or the provider’s help route and keep the written record.

4. **Send a short message to the BNPL provider.**
   - Say: “I cannot safely pay this instalment today. Please pause the retry, move the due date to [date], confirm what fees may apply, and confirm whether any fee can be waived.”
   - Ask them to confirm whether they will try the same card again before the new date.
   - Keep screenshots of your message and their reply.

5. **If the retry could cause overdraft or bank charges, contact your card issuer.**
   - If the BNPL payment is a recurring card payment, also called a continuous payment authority, you can ask the card issuer to stop future payments.
   - Do this by the end of the business day before the payment is due, if possible.
   - Say clearly: “I withdraw consent for future recurring card payments to [provider]. Please stop the next payment and confirm this in writing.”
   - Cancelling the card payment does not cancel the BNPL debt. You still need to contact the BNPL provider about how the missed instalment will be handled.

6. **If money is taken after you asked the card issuer to stop it, ask the card issuer to put it right.**
   - Ask for the payment and any related charges to be refunded.
   - Give the date, time, and method you used to cancel the recurring card payment.
   - If the card issuer does not resolve it, use its complaints process and then the Financial Ombudsman Service if the complaint remains unresolved.

7. **If the item is being returned, cancelled, or disputed, tell both sides today.**
   - Tell the retailer what is happening and keep return tracking, cancellation confirmation, or dispute messages.
   - Tell the BNPL provider the order is being returned, cancelled, or disputed, and ask them to pause instalments and fees while it is checked.
   - Do not assume the retailer has told the BNPL provider.

8. **If this is part of wider problem debt, contact free debt advice.**
   - If you live in England or Wales, ask a free debt adviser whether Breathing Space could help. It can give temporary protection from creditor contact, enforcement action, interest, and charges on included debts, but you still need to make repayments.
   - If you live in Scotland or Northern Ireland, ask a free debt advice service about the local options that apply there.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to close every BNPL account or repair your credit file.
- You do not need to write a long explanation; the amount, due date, order number, screenshots, and a short message are enough for now.
- You do not need to argue about fairness before the retry is handled; stop or move the payment first, then challenge fees or treatment if needed.

## Important reassurance
A retry warning is stressful because it creates a countdown, but it is still a controllable admin problem. One clear action before the retry, such as paying safely, moving the date, contacting support, or asking your card issuer to stop a recurring card payment, can reduce the chance of extra harm.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate fees, protect essential money, and regain control of the timing. Later decisions about complaints, affordability, credit records, or debt solutions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not financial, legal, debt, or professional advice. BNPL terms, fee rules, and complaint routes vary by provider and product, and some BNPL protections are changing from 15 July 2026. Ask the BNPL provider, your card issuer, or a free debt advice service to confirm what applies to your situation.
