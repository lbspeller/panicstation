# What to do if… a stranger keeps trying to start conversation after you clearly disengage (UK)

## Short answer
Create immediate physical distance by moving toward staff or a busy, well-lit area and stop all verbal engagement with the individual.

## Do not do these things
- Do not prioritize politeness over your intuition or comfort.
- Do not provide justifications, excuses, or "reasons" why you cannot talk.
- Do not disclose your destination, home address, workplace, or contact details.
- Do not move to isolated areas like side streets, empty stairwells, or lifts.
- Do not accept items such as drinks, cigarettes, or a phone they are holding out.
- Do not lead the person toward your parked car or front door.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause immediately.** Identify the nearest staffed location, such as a shop, café, ticket office, or a group of people, and move toward it without checking for the person's reaction.
2. **Set one final, neutral boundary if they continue to follow.** Use a firm, level voice: "I am not available to talk. Do not follow me." Do not say "sorry" or "I'm busy."
3. **Engage a third party as a shield.** Approach a staff member or a group and state clearly: "This person is bothering me and won't leave me alone. May I stand here with you?"
4. **Utilize rail network safety protocols if you are on a train or at a station.**
   - **Text British Transport Police on 61016** to report the behavior discreetly (standard network charges apply).
   - **Include your exact location**, the service you are on (e.g., "Southbound Northern Line"), a description of the person, and what they are doing.
   - **Use a station Help Point** or speak to a member of staff if you do not have phone signal or if you need an immediate presence.
5. **Use your phone to connect with a trusted person.** Call someone and explicitly state your location and that you are heading to a safe spot; keep the phone to your ear but stay visually aware of your surroundings.
6. **Escalate to emergency services if the threat is immediate.** Call **999** if the person touches you, blocks your path, uses threatening language, or continues to follow you after you have sought help.
7. **Note physical descriptions once you are in a secure location.** Record key details for your own records or a future report:
   - Approximate height and build.
   - Clothing (colour and type of jacket, hat, or shoes).
   - Distinguishing features (tattoos, facial hair, glasses).

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to file a formal police report for harassment.
- Reviewing your own behavior or wondering if you were "too rude."
- Checking your social media or apps to see if the person is "local."
- Deciding on long-term changes to your route or routine.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a "rush" of adrenaline, shaky hands, or a sense of confusion when someone ignores your boundaries. These are natural physiological responses to a boundary violation. Your only priority is your immediate physical safety, not the other person's feelings.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute interaction with a persistent stranger. It does not cover long-term stalking or domestic harassment, which require different legal and support frameworks.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and does not constitute legal advice. In any situation involving an active threat or a crime in progress, dial 999. For non-emergencies where the person is no longer present, you can call 101.
