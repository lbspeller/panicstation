# What to do if… you receive bills or debt collection notices addressed to someone who has died (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay the bill from your own money just to make it stop. Put it somewhere safe, note the details, and either tell the sender the person has died or pass it to the person handling the estate.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to take over the debt, set up a payment plan, or put the bill in your own name unless you have taken advice and you are sure that is correct.
- Do not pay from your own money because a letter sounds urgent.
- Do not use the person’s online banking, email, or customer account unless you are authorised to do so.
- Do not send your only original death certificate in the post; ask whether a copy or certified copy is accepted.
- Do not ignore letters that look genuine; slow the situation down by logging them and notifying the sender of the death.
- Do not cancel payments from an account you are not authorised to manage; ask the bank, building society, or person handling the estate what can be stopped.

## What to do now
1. **Put every bill in one place.** Write down the sender, date received, amount, account or reference number, and what the bill seems to be for.

2. **Do not take responsibility for the bill in your own name.** If you speak to the sender, keep it factual: say that the named person has died, give the date of death if you know it, and ask them to mark the account as bereavement or deceased while the estate is being handled.

3. **Work out who should handle the paperwork next.** If there is an executor, solicitor, or another person already handling the estate, give them the bill and your log. If that is you, keep going one bill at a time.

4. **Ask the sender what proof they need.** Many organisations will ask for a death certificate or reference details. Ask where to send it, whether a copy is enough, and whether collection letters or charges can be paused while they update the account.

5. **Use Tell Us Once if the registrar made it available.** This can tell many government organisations about the death, but it does not replace telling banks, utility companies, landlords, insurers, lenders, or other private organisations.

6. **For banks and financial firms, use a bereavement route.** Contact the bank or building society’s bereavement team, or use the Death Notification Service for member organisations. Ask what happens to direct debits, standing orders, and card payments before trying to change anything yourself.

7. **Check whether the bill is for an ongoing property service.** Utilities, water, rent, mortgage, insurance, and council tax may need a quick update if someone is still living in the property, the property is empty, or a joint account holder remains. Ask for the account to be reviewed from the date of death and for future bills to be addressed correctly.

8. **Keep credit debts separate from urgent household services.** Credit cards, loans, catalogues, and overdrafts are usually handled through the estate process. Log them and notify the creditor, but do not personally pay unless you are jointly liable, a guarantor, or have taken advice.

9. **Treat sudden payment pressure as unverified.** If a letter demands immediate payment, asks for unusual payment methods, or looks suspicious, contact the organisation using a trusted number or website you find independently, not just the details on the letter.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide the full order of paying estate debts today. You do not need to close every account, negotiate every balance, or solve formal estate authority immediately. The immediate job is to stop accidental personal responsibility, notify the right organisations, and keep a clear record.

## Important reassurance
Bills arriving straight after a death can feel harsh, but many systems keep sending automatic letters until someone tells them what has happened. Taking one bill at a time and keeping everything routed through the estate is a safe first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about formal estate authority, disputed bills, joint debts, insolvent estates, or who must pay may need specialist legal or debt advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, debt, or professional advice. If the estate may not have enough money, if you are being pressured to pay, or if you are unsure whether you are personally liable, get independent advice before paying anything.
