# What to do if… you get an unexpected carrier alert that call forwarding was enabled (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible mobile account takeover. Contact your mobile provider now through a trusted route, such as its official app, website, or the number on your bill, and ask them to remove all call forwarding and secure your account.

## Do not do these things
- Do not dial fix codes or follow instructions sent by a random text, call, or voicemail, even if the message claims to be from your network or bank.
- Do not call back a number from the alert text or from a missed-call notification unless you already know it is genuine.
- Do not share one-time passcodes, PAC details, account PINs, passphrases, or security answers with anyone who contacts you first.
- Do not assume turning off forwarding in your handset settings is enough; some diverts may be set on the network side.
- Do not delete the alert, provider messages, or bank messages until you have taken screenshots or saved the details.

## What to do now
1. **Contact your mobile network using a verified route.**  
   Use the number on your bill or contract, the provider’s official website, or the provider’s official app. Ask for the fraud or security team if available.

2. **Ask them to confirm what changed, then clear it at network level.**  
   Ask what type of forwarding or divert was enabled, when it was added, and whether any other diverts are active. Ask them to remove or clear all call forwarding and diverts on the network, not just on your handset.

3. **Ask them to check for SIM swap and port-out activity.**  
   Ask whether there have been recent SIM or eSIM activations, device changes, PAC requests, porting requests, added authorised users, address changes, password resets, or failed verification attempts.

4. **Lock down your mobile account with the provider’s help.**  
   Reset or add an account PIN or passphrase. Ask what account-change restrictions they can add, such as extra verification for SIM swaps, number transfers, new eSIMs, or account access.

5. **Secure voicemail.**  
   Change your voicemail PIN or passcode. Ask whether voicemail was recently reset or accessed, and confirm it cannot be accessed from another phone without the PIN.

6. **Treat phone-call and SMS verification as unreliable until the provider confirms the fix.**  
   Until your provider confirms the divert is removed and your account is secured, avoid relying on SMS or phone-call verification where an account offers another option. For your most important accounts, use app-based authentication or saved backup codes where those options are available.

7. **Check your highest-risk accounts in this order.**  
   First check your primary email account: change the password, review recent sign-ins, remove unknown sessions or devices, and check for new forwarding rules or recovery changes. Then check banking, credit, mobile wallet, app store, Apple, Google, and other accounts tied to your phone number.

8. **If someone contacted you claiming to be your bank, switch to a safe calling method.**  
   Hang up. Dial 159 if your bank supports it. If 159 does not connect to your bank, use the number on your bank card or in your banking app; do not accept a transfer from an incoming call.

9. **Create an official record if you suspect fraud or if losses occur.**  
   In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report cyber crime or fraud through Report Fraud. In Scotland, report by calling 101. Keep a simple timeline, screenshots, provider reference numbers, and bank reference numbers.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly how this happened right now.
- You do not need to confront anyone who may have targeted you.
- You do not need to change every password immediately; focus first on your mobile account, voicemail, primary email, banking, and accounts that use your phone number for recovery.
- You do not need to decide today whether this was a scam, provider error, SIM swap, or porting attempt; your first job is to stop the forwarding and protect the accounts most likely to be affected.

## Important reassurance
A call-forwarding alert you did not trigger is unsettling, but acting quickly can reduce the risk. The priority is to regain control of your number, remove network-level forwarding, and protect the accounts that depend on calls or texts to that number.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, such as account recovery, fraud claims, identity protection, or complaint routes, may need specialist help from your provider, bank, police reporting route, or a qualified adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cyber-security, technical, or other professional advice. If you see unauthorised transactions, account lockouts, or evidence that your identity has been misused, contact the relevant provider or bank promptly and use the appropriate UK reporting route.
