# What to do if… you receive a notice that your parcel has been seized or detained (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before replying, paying, clicking, or calling. First check whether the notice is genuine, then keep the documents and use only a contact route shown on GOV.UK guidance or on a genuine notice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links or call numbers from an unexpected text or email about a detained parcel.
- Do not pay a release fee, fine, verification charge, or delivery fee by bank transfer, crypto, gift card, or payment link.
- Do not give ID, bank details, passwords, or a signature until you have checked who is contacting you.
- Do not make a rushed phone admission or written explanation if the notice mentions prohibited items, controlled goods, an investigation, an interview, or possible offences.
- Do not throw away the notice, envelope, tracking details, order records, or messages.

## What to do now
1. **Separate a possible real notice from a scam message.**  
   A genuine HMRC or Border Force seizure should be supported by a seizure information notice or notice of seizure. Treat any unexpected text or email demanding urgent payment, personal details, or a link click as unverified.

2. **Check the process independently.**  
   Open GOV.UK yourself and look up guidance on things seized by HMRC or Border Force. Compare the notice with the GOV.UK process. If you are unsure who seized the parcel, use independently found official contact routes rather than a link or callback number in a message.

3. **If it is a suspicious parcel text, report it and stop.**  
   Do not click the link. Forward the text to 7726, then delete it. Check any real delivery only through the courier’s official website or app that you open yourself.

4. **Save evidence before you contact anyone.**  
   Photograph or scan the notice, the envelope, the postmark, the parcel tracking page, the order confirmation, the item description, the seller details, payment records, and any messages. Write down when you received the notice and any deadline it gives.

5. **Find the deadline on your notice today.**  
   If you want to challenge whether the seizure was lawful, HMRC or Border Force must usually receive a notice of claim within one calendar month of the seizure date shown on the notice. Asking for the goods back is a separate restoration request; do it quickly because goods may be destroyed or sold, often after 45 days for non-perishable goods.

6. **Respond in writing if you choose to respond.**  
   Keep your reply factual. Include the reference number, your name and address, what parcel or goods you are asking about, and copies of evidence you rely on. Keep a copy of everything and use a method that gives proof of posting or delivery.

7. **Get advice before speaking if there may be an offence.**  
   If the notice mentions prohibited items, controlled drugs, weapons, an interview, an investigation, or prosecution, consider speaking to a solicitor experienced in criminal or customs matters before replying or calling.

8. **If you have already paid or shared bank details, act now.**  
   Contact your bank using the number on your card or in your banking app and say you suspect fraud. Save screenshots, call logs, payment details, and the exact wording of the message. You can also report fraud through the UK fraud reporting service; if you live in Scotland, report via 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to challenge the seizure or let the parcel go; first check authenticity and note any deadline.
- You do not need to explain the whole situation in one rushed message.
- You do not need to contact the seller or sender until you have saved the records.
- You do not need to search every possible rule tonight; focus on the notice, the reference number, the deadline, and safe contact details.

## Important reassurance
These notices and scam messages can feel intimidating. A short pause to verify the source, preserve records, and avoid rushed payments or admissions is a sensible protective step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: verify the notice, avoid common mistakes, protect documents, and make a safer first response. Later decisions about a notice of claim, restoration request, fraud report, or legal reply may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice, financial advice, or professional advice. Processes and time limits can depend on what was seized, who seized it, and what your notice says.
