# What to do if… someone you care about is taken into custody and you need to arrange support or collect belongings (UK)

## Short answer
Confirm where they are, ask the official route for welfare messages and property release, and do not travel to a police station, court, or prison until staff tell you what authorisation and ID they need.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t argue about the alleged offence with police, court, or prison staff; focus on welfare, location, and belongings.
- Don’t assume you can collect property just because you are family, a partner, or a close friend.
- Don’t take loose cash, cards, phones, keys, or documents from another person without writing down exactly what you received, when, and from whom.
- Don’t hand medication, phones, chargers, clothing, or toiletries to police or prison staff unless they have told you the current process.
- Don’t post about the arrest online or message many people for help; it can add stress and create avoidable complications.

## What to do now

1. **Confirm the safest contact route and the holding location.**  
   If you do not know where they are, call 101 or use the police force’s published non-emergency contact route. Give their full name, date of birth if you know it, and any incident details if asked. Staff may be limited in what they can confirm, so ask: “Can you tell me whether they are in police custody, at court, or elsewhere, and who I should contact next?”

2. **If you know the police station, ask for the custody route.**  
   Ask for the custody desk or custody sergeant. Keep the call practical: confirm whether they are still there, whether you can pass on urgent welfare information, and whether there is any property-release process you can follow.

3. **Pass on urgent welfare information only.**  
   Tell custody staff about immediate needs such as serious medical issues, disability needs, pregnancy, mental health risks, childcare responsibilities, dependants, or essential medication. For medication, give the name, dose if known, and where it is, then ask staff how they want it handled; do not hand in medication unless staff tell you how.

4. **Ask about legal advice without trying to manage the case.**  
   If you can speak to the person, keep it simple: “Ask for free legal advice before any interview, ask for the duty solicitor if you do not have your own solicitor, and tell staff any urgent health or safety needs.” If you cannot speak to them, you can still ask custody staff whether welfare information can be passed on.

5. **Ask what property exists and what can be released.**  
   Ask whether their possessions have been recorded, whether any items are being kept as evidence, and whether any non-evidence items can be collected. Ask exactly what ID, written permission, nomination by the detained person, reference number, opening hours, and entrance you need before travelling.

6. **If you are asked to collect belongings from a home, car, workplace, or arrest scene, keep it narrow.**  
   Take only clearly practical items you can safely store, such as keys, wallet, glasses, essential documents, basic clothing, or a written note of medication and health needs. Photograph what you take before you leave, keep it together in one bag, and write down where each item came from.

7. **If police have removed or seized items, ask for the record.**  
   If officers are present, ask for the officer’s name or collar number and any property receipt, seizure record, crime reference, or custody reference they can give you. If they cannot give it immediately, ask which team or station you should contact later.

8. **If they are moved to court or prison, switch to that place’s property rules.**  
   Ask where they have gone and what reference details you can use. If it is a prison, contact the prison’s published route before sending or bringing anything. Ask what has travelled with them, what is stored, what can be handed in or collected, and what identification or authorisation is needed.

9. **Keep one clear note.**  
   Record the date and time of each call or visit, who you spoke to, the station, court, or prison, any reference numbers, what you were told, and what you collected or handed over.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to complain, contact the media, or argue the case right now.
- You do not need to gather character references, long explanations, screenshots, or a full timeline today unless a solicitor specifically asks.
- You do not need to make wider case decisions for them today; first make sure they know they can ask for legal advice in custody.
- You do not need to buy or send clothing, toiletries, phones, chargers, books, or money until the police station or prison has confirmed the current rules.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressure to fix everything is normal. In the first hours, useful support is usually practical and calm: confirm where they are, pass on urgent welfare information, follow the property process, and keep accurate notes.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about the case, complaints, prison visits, money, property disputes, or ongoing support may need specialist legal or prison-advice help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Police forces, courts, and prisons can have different local procedures, and some official guidance applies differently across the UK. Follow the process given by the specific police force, court, or prison you are dealing with.
