# What to do if… a person says they are a tow operator for your vehicle but you did not request service (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself to a safer pause (locked car, indoors, or with other people) and verify the operator's authority independently before agreeing to any action. Call 999 if you feel threatened, if they attempt to force access to the vehicle, or if they prevent you from leaving.

## Do not do these things
- Don't hand over your keys, vehicle logbook (V5C), bank card, or ID for "verification" purposes.
- Don't sign any documents or scan QR codes provided by the operator while under pressure.
- Don't follow the operator to a second location to "complete paperwork" or "discuss the matter".
- Don't pay cash or make an immediate bank transfer to "cancel" the tow or "release" the vehicle.
- Don't physically block the tow truck or engage in a physical confrontation, as this can escalate the risk of harm.
- Don't allow the person into your home or garage based on "official" claims until you have verified their identity through independent channels.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause immediately.**  
   **If you are in or near the vehicle, get inside, lock all doors, and keep windows mostly closed.** If you are at home, go inside and lock the door. Communicate only through a closed window or door if necessary.

2. **If you feel unsafe or a crime is in progress, call the police.**  
   - **Call 999** if you are being threatened, if the person is trying to force entry to your vehicle/property, or if you believe a vehicle theft is occurring.  
   - **Call 101** if the person is present but not immediate threat, and you require police attendance to verify their authority.

3. **Request identification and authority through a barrier.**  
   **Ask the person for their name, their company name, and the name of the organization that allegedly authorized the tow.** Ask them to hold their ID and any "job sheet" or "seizure notice" up to the window glass so you can photograph it. Do not open the door to take documents.

4. **Identify the specific legal authority they claim to represent.**  
   **Ask if the tow was ordered by the Police, the local Council, the DVLA, the DVSA, or a court/bailiff.**  
   - Note that under the Protection of Freedoms Act 2012, private landowners and parking companies generally cannot legally tow or clamp vehicles on private land without specific statutory authority (e.g., if acting for the DVLA).

5. **Verify the instruction using a number you find yourself.**  
   **Do not use any phone number provided by the operator.**  
   - If they claim to be from your insurer or breakdown provider, call the number on your policy documents or official app.  
   - If they claim to be from a Council or the DVLA, look up the official contact number for that organization independently.

6. **Verify bailiff or enforcement agent status if applicable.**  
   **In England and Wales, you can check the Certificated Enforcement Agent (Bailiff) Register online.**  
   - Ask for the agent's full name and their employer.  
   - Search the register yourself. If they are not listed or refuse to provide details, do not engage.  
   - In Scotland or Northern Ireland, procedures differ; treat "immediate" pressure as high risk and contact the claiming court or creditor directly using independently found details.

7. **Document the situation from a safe distance.**  
   **Take photographs or notes of the operator’s appearance, the tow truck’s registration plate, and any branding or phone numbers visible on their vehicle.** If your vehicle has a dashcam or you have a doorbell camera, ensure the footage is saved.

8. **Prioritize your physical safety over the vehicle.**  
   **If the operator begins to hook up the vehicle, do not physically intervene.** Stay in your safe location, continue documenting, and update the police if they are already on the way.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if this is a "scam" or a "mistake" right now; you only need to safely verify.
- You do not need to negotiate storage fees or release payments while at the roadside or your front door.
- You do not need to provide a formal statement to anyone other than the police.
- You do not need to resolve underlying disputes (such as unpaid fines or insurance issues) while the operator is present.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressured or startled when someone acts with "official" authority is a common reaction. Legitimate recovery operators and enforcement agents are used to verification delays and will wait for you to confirm their identity through official channels. Taking a moment to pause and check is a standard safety procedure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions for an immediate confrontation. It does not cover long-term legal disputes regarding vehicle seizure, debt management, or insurance litigation.

## Important note
This information is for immediate crisis stabilization and does not constitute legal advice. In the UK, if you suspect fraud or a rogue operator, report it to the "Report Fraud" service (England, Wales, NI) or Police Scotland (111). Always call 999 in an emergency.
