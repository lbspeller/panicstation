# What to do if… your wheelchair, mobility scooter, or other mobility aid is damaged during transport and you still need to travel (UK)

## Short answer
Stop immediately to check for safety hazards, report the damage to the operator to obtain a written reference, and request mandatory "Turn Up and Go" assistance or alternative accessible transport to complete your journey.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not** attempt to use the device if the battery casing is cracked, hissing, or overheating, as this indicates a fire risk.
- **Do not** continue using the aid if structural elements like the frame, axle, or brakes appear bent, loose, or unstable.
- **Do not** leave the transport hub (station, airport, or terminal) without a written damage report or Property Irregularity Report (PIR).
- **Do not** perform temporary repairs using tape or force that might obscure further damage or compromise your insurance claim.
- **Do not** assume you are stuck; UK operators are legally required to provide alternative accessible transport if your aid becomes unusable during their service.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the device and check for immediate hazards.**  
   **Turn off the power and move to a safe, level spot away from passenger flow.** 
   - Check the battery casing for cracks, heat, or leaking fluids.
   - Look for loose bolts, snapped casters, or dragging cables.
   - If the device is stuck in "powered" mode, locate the freewheel levers to allow manual pushing.

2. **Alert the operator and request an accessibility supervisor.**  
   **Speak to the nearest staff member or use a help point to state you have sustained damage to a mobility aid.** 
   - Inform them clearly: "My mobility aid has been damaged during your service, and I am now unable to travel safely without assistance."
   - Ask for a staff member trained in "Accessible Travel Policy" (ATP) or "Special Assistance."

3. **Secure a formal incident record and evidence.**  
   **Request a written damage report and a reference number before proceeding.** 
   - Use your phone to take clear, dated photos of the damage, any handling tags still attached, and the surrounding area.
   - If at an airport, ensure you receive a Property Irregularity Report (PIR).
   - Note the names of the staff members you speak to.

4. **Request immediate "Turn Up and Go" support or alternative transport.**  
   **Ask the operator to arrange for your onward journey using their emergency accessibility protocols.** 
   - For Rail: Ask for "Turn Up and Go" assistance to reach your destination or a station-to-station accessible taxi if your device is unsafe to board a train.
   - For Air: Demand a temporary replacement mobility aid; under UK CAA rules, the airport is responsible for providing a temporary alternative (though not necessarily like-for-like).
   - For Bus/Coach: Ask the driver or inspector for a "Bus Replacement" or accessible taxi if the damage occurred during their handling of the device.

5. **Arrange for safe storage if the device is non-functional.**  
   **If the aid cannot be safely moved or repaired on-site, ask the operator to secure it in a locked luggage area.** 
   - Ensure the battery is disconnected.
   - Retain the storage receipt and confirm the process for later collection by a repair specialist.

6. **Prioritise a safe arrival over speed.**  
   **Accept a slower, multi-stage route if it involves staff assistance rather than trying to manage a damaged device alone.** 
   - If you feel unsafe continuing, ask the operator to provide a "Safe Stop" location with accessible facilities while they arrange a specialized vehicle for you.

## What can wait
- Calculating the exact cost of repairs or replacement.
- Contacting your private insurance provider.
- Filing a formal legal claim for compensation or distress.
- Posting on social media or responding to non-emergency messages.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed or stranded when your primary means of mobility is compromised. You are entitled to assistance under UK law (the Equality Act 2010 and specific transport regulations), and asking for staff support is a procedural necessity, not a burden. Operators have established protocols for these exact scenarios to ensure you are not left at a station or terminal.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and immediate travel continuity actions only. It does not cover the long-term claims process, technical repair instructions, or legal litigation against transport operators.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. Liability limits for damage to mobility aids vary between rail, bus, and air travel (often capped around £1,300 for international flights under the Montreal Convention). Always verify specific operator terms regarding "negligence" versus "unavoidable accident" when pursuing a claim after your journey is complete.
