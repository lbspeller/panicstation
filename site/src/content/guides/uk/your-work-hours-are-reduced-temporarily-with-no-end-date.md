# What to do if… your work hours are reduced temporarily with no end date (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before agreeing to anything permanent. Ask for the reduction, the start date, and the review point in writing, then check whether your contract allows lay-off or short-time working before you accept a change to your hours.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign on the spot or stop turning up; it can affect your income and options.
- Do not agree verbally to a permanent contract change if you only mean that you will try the new hours briefly.
- Do not sign a new contract, variation letter, or rota agreement until you have read it and kept a copy.
- Do not rely on “temporary” or “we’ll sort it soon” unless there is a written review date, trigger, or end point.
- Do not assume the employer can reduce pay just because work is quiet; the contract, custom and practice, union agreement, or your agreement may matter.
- Do not send a formal redundancy-payment notice without advice if you are unsure, because that route can bring the job to an end.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the proposal in writing today.** Ask for the new weekly hours or shift pattern, the start date, how rotas will be set, whether pay is being reduced, and what will end or review the “temporary” period. Keep emails, messages, rotas, and payslips.

2. **Check your contract, handbook, and any collective agreement.** Look for words such as **“lay-off”**, **“short-time working”**, **“variation of hours”**, **“reduced pay”**, **“flexibility clause”**, or **“guaranteed hours”**. This helps you see whether the employer says it already has a power to reduce hours, or whether it is asking you to agree to a contract change.

3. **Reply in writing with a holding position.** Say that you are not refusing work, you are asking for clarity, and you will keep working your scheduled shifts while you review the proposal. If you do not agree to a permanent reduction, say that clearly.

4. **Ask which route the employer is using.** Use a simple question: “Are you treating this as lay-off or short-time working under my contract, or are you asking me to agree to a change to my contractual hours?”

5. **Ask for a meeting and a written outcome.** If you are in a union, contact your rep before the meeting. If not, ask whether you can bring a workplace companion or note-taker. After the meeting, send a short email saying what you understood was agreed or still unclear.

6. **Check whether any full workless days trigger guarantee pay.** Statutory guarantee pay can apply in some lay-off or short-time situations when you are not given work on a day you would normally be required to work. There is a statutory daily cap and a limit on the number of days that can be claimed in a 3-month period; check the current official rate before relying on it. Eligibility can depend on factors such as employee status, at least 1 month’s continuous employment, being available for work, not refusing reasonable alternative work, and the lay-off not being because of a strike, lockout, or industrial action.

7. **If your pay is very low for several weeks, get advice before using the redundancy-payment route.** Employees with at least 2 years’ continuous employment may be able to apply for redundancy pay after being laid off without pay or put on short-time and receiving less than half a week’s pay for **4 or more weeks in a row**, or **6 or more weeks in a 13-week period**. The written claim usually has to be made within **4 weeks of the end of the relevant lay-off or short-time period**, and the employer generally has **7 days** to accept or issue a written counter-notice. This can end your employment, so speak to Acas, your union, nidirect if you work in Northern Ireland, or an adviser before sending formal notice.

8. **Protect your income while the work issue is being clarified.** If the reduction affects rent, bills, food, childcare, or travel, check whether reduced earnings mean you may qualify for Universal Credit or New Style Jobseeker’s Allowance. Ask Acas, Citizens Advice, nidirect if you work in Northern Ireland, your union, or a local advice service what to do before missing essential payments.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to leave the job.
- You do not need to prove the employer’s motives today; start by keeping a clear written record.
- You do not need to negotiate every detail immediately; first get the proposed hours, pay effect, contract basis, and review point in writing.
- You do not need to send a formal grievance or tribunal paperwork before getting basic advice on your contract and timeline.

## Important reassurance
A reduction with no end date can feel destabilising, especially if your pay is already tight. Asking for the proposal in writing and checking the contract is a normal, practical step, not a refusal to work.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and avoid rushed decisions. Later choices about grievances, redundancy-payment notices, unlawful deduction from wages, discrimination concerns, or tribunal routes may need specialist advice based on your contract, employment status, location, and exact dates.

## Important note
This guide gives general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Employment rights can vary by employee or worker status, contract type, sector, union agreement, and whether you work in England, Scotland, Wales, or Northern Ireland. If you work in Northern Ireland or are unsure about your status, check nidirect or another Northern Ireland-specific advice source before signing anything, resigning, or sending formal notice.
