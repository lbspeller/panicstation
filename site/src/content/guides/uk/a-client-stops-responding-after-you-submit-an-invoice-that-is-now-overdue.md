# What to do if… a client stops responding after you submit an invoice that is now overdue (UK)

## Short answer
Request a written statement of the main terms (the "principal statement") via email before performing any labor. Under UK law, most workers and all employees have a legal right to receive this document on or before their first day of work.

## Do not do these things
- Do not perform "trial shifts," "orientation tasks," or "brief assignments" without a written agreement on pay, hours, and the identity of your legal employer.
- Do not provide sensitive data—including passport scans, National Insurance numbers, or bank details—to any recruiter whose identity you have not independently verified.
- Do not pay upfront fees for "training," "IT equipment," or "background checks," as legitimate UK employers do not charge for the right to work.
- Do not resign from current employment or cease other job-seeking activities until you have a signed offer letter or written statement of particulars.
- Do not accept "we will sort the paperwork next week" as a valid reason to begin working without established terms.

## What to do now
1. **Request the "principal statement" of employment particulars immediately via email.** **This document must legally include your employer’s name, job title, start date, pay rate, pay frequency, working hours, and holiday entitlement.**
   - Ensure the statement includes the 2026-mandatory notification of your right to join a trade union.
2. **Clarify the exact employment structure if you are working through an intermediary.** **Ask specifically if you are employed directly by the client, by the recruitment agency (the "employment business"), or via an umbrella company.**
3. **Demand a "Key Information Document" (KID) if the role involves an agency.** **Recruitment agencies must provide this one-to-two-page summary before you agree to terms; it must clearly show your expected pay, all statutory deductions, and any agency fees.**
4. **Conduct an independent legitimacy check on the hiring entity.** **Verify the company name via Companies House or the Charity Commission and contact the organization using a phone number found on their official website, rather than a number provided by the recruiter.**
5. **Report suspected recruitment fraud or identity harvesting to the authorities.** **If the recruiter disappears after receiving your data or demands money, contact the Report Fraud service (formerly Action Fraud) and notify your bank if you have shared financial details.**
6. **Contact the Fair Work Agency (FWA) if a legitimate agency refuses to provide paperwork.** **As of April 2026, the FWA oversees the conduct of employment agencies and can investigate failures to provide mandatory documents like the KID.**
7. **Seek confidential advice from the Acas helpline at 0300 123 1100.** **They are available Monday to Friday, 8am to 6pm, to help you draft a response to pressure or verify your specific worker status rights.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on long-term career goals or benefit packages during this initial verification phase.
- You do not need to negotiate non-essential clauses (such as social media policies or non-compete terms) until the core pay and employer identity are confirmed.
- You do not need to justify your request for paperwork beyond stating your legal right to a day-one written statement.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel anxious when a recruiter creates a sense of urgency or "missing out." Legitimate businesses, even those moving quickly, are equipped to provide basic terms in writing. Insisting on these details is a standard professional practice and does not indicate a lack of commitment on your part.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance intended to prevent data theft, unpaid labor, or misclassification of employment status. It does not replace detailed legal review of a full employment contract once provided.

## Important note
This guide is for general informational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Employment rights vary significantly between "employees," "workers," and "self-employed contractors." For specific disputes, consult Acas or a qualified employment law professional.
