# What to do if… your scheduled work hours are suddenly cut to zero without explanation (UK)

## Short answer
Get the change confirmed in writing, check what your contract says about guaranteed hours, and keep a record that you are ready and available to work. Do not resign or agree to new hours while you are still trying to confirm your status and pay.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign “to make a point” before you know whether you are still employed, laid off, on short-time working, or on a zero-hours arrangement.
- Do not accept a verbal explanation as the final word; ask for written confirmation of what is happening and from when.
- Do not sign or agree to any “new hours”, “temporary change”, or “unpaid leave” on the spot if you are not sure what it means.
- Do not go quiet or disappear; keep a written record that you are available to work and asking for shifts and pay clarity.
- Do not assume this is automatically redundancy or dismissal; different steps and rights can apply.
- Do not ignore your next normal pay date or any possible claim deadline if the employer does not clarify the situation.

## What to do now
1. **Save proof of the change today.** Screenshot the rota or app showing zero hours, any messages about removed shifts, your recent payslips, and any timesheets showing your usual pattern.

2. **Check what your contract says about hours.** Look for:
   - minimum hours, contracted hours, or guaranteed hours
   - zero-hours, casual, or worker-status wording
   - any clause about lay-off or short-time working
   - any clause allowing your employer to change hours, reduce pay, or send you home without work
   - anything about notice for rota changes or cancelled shifts

3. **Send one calm written message asking for status and dates.** Ask:
   - whether you are still employed
   - whether this is lay-off, short-time working, a disciplinary or HR action, or a proposed contract change
   - the date the change started and when it will be reviewed
   - whether you should keep yourself available for work
   - what you will be paid while no hours are scheduled, and when payroll will reflect that

4. **If you have contracted or guaranteed hours, put your availability on record.** For example: “My contract states X hours. I am ready and available to work those hours. Please confirm when shifts will be scheduled and how pay will be handled if no work is provided.”

5. **If they say it is unpaid lay-off or short-time working, check the basis for it.** Ask them to point to the contract clause, agreed change, workplace custom, national agreement, or union agreement they are relying on. If you have guaranteed hours and they cannot explain the basis, you may still be owed pay, so get advice quickly.

6. **Ask about statutory guarantee pay if you are an employee.** It may apply in some lay-off or short-time working situations, but do not rely on an old saved figure because rates can change. Ask your employer in writing whether they will pay it, and keep evidence that you were available for work and did not refuse reasonable alternative work.

7. **Escalate if the answer is unclear or your pay date is at risk.** Contact your union rep if you have one. If you do not, contact Acas for free, confidential advice on how to raise the issue and what deadlines may matter.

8. **Stabilise your money for the next few weeks.** If your income has stopped or dropped sharply, check whether you may be eligible for Universal Credit or other local help. Keep rent, bills, and food costs separate from longer-term decisions about the job.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, bring a formal claim, or call it redundancy.
- You do not need to write a long grievance immediately; first get the employer’s position in writing and gather your documents.
- You do not need to guess the reason before taking protective steps.
- Formal grievance routes and Acas early conciliation can be later steps, but do not ignore employment tribunal time limits if pay, dismissal, or employment status becomes disputed.

## Important reassurance
A rota suddenly dropping to zero can feel personal and frightening. The safest first move is to slow the situation down, keep everything written, and protect your pay position while the facts are pinned down.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the hours stay at zero, the next steps may depend on your contract wording, employment status, length of service, pay dates, and whether the employer says this is lay-off, short-time working, a contract change, dismissal, or something else.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Employment rights can depend on your contract, your employment status, what has been agreed, and what normally happens in your workplace.
