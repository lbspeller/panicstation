# What to do if… you are told you are the subject of an investigation and you fear you could say the wrong thing (UK)

## Short answer
Stop speaking about the allegation immediately and request a solicitor. In the UK, you have a right to free, independent legal advice before and during any police interview, whether you are under arrest or attending voluntarily.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attempt to "clear things up" via informal chats, texts, or emails with the investigating officer.
- Don’t attend a voluntary interview without first confirming your legal representation is arranged.
- Don’t guess, speculate, or fill in memory gaps during any interaction.
- Don’t delete any digital evidence, messages, or call logs, as this may be treated as perverting the course of justice.
- Don’t contact witnesses or other parties involved in the matter to discuss the case.
- Don’t sign any written statements or "pro-forma" documents without a solicitor's review.
- Don’t agree to a "live link" or remote video interview without specific legal advice on that format.

## What to do now
1. **State clearly that you require legal advice before answering any questions.** **This applies even if the officer suggests the matter is minor or just a formality.**
2. **Ask the officer to clarify your current legal status and the nature of the interview.** **Specifically, ask if you are under arrest, if the interview is "under caution," and what specific allegations are being investigated.**
   - Ask for the officer's name, rank, and station.
   - Request the "CRN" or case reference number if available.
3. **Contact a solicitor or request the duty solicitor immediately.** **If you are already at a police station, the custody officer must arrange this for you at no cost.**
   - If in England/Wales: Request the Duty Solicitor if you do not have your own.
   - If in Scotland: Request to see the "Letter of Rights" and ask for the duty solicitor scheme.
4. **Identify any need for an Appropriate Adult or interpreter right away.** **If you are under 18 or have any condition that makes you "vulnerable" (such as a mental health condition, learning difficulty, or communication issue), the police must arrange an Appropriate Adult to support you.**
5. **Provide only your mandatory identification details if requested.** **In all UK jurisdictions, you must provide your name, date of birth, address, and nationality.**
   - In Scotland, you must also provide your place of birth.
   - Beyond these details, you have the right to remain silent.
6. **Request a private consultation with your solicitor before the interview begins.** **You are entitled to speak with them in a private room or via a secure telephone line to discuss the evidence and your response.**
7. **Document the circumstances of the initial contact for your solicitor.** **Write down the time, date, and exactly what the officer said to you, but do not share this document with anyone except your legal representative.**

## What can wait
- You do-not need to decide your "plea" or final defense strategy right now.
- You do-not need to gather evidence or witness statements today; your solicitor will advise on the process for disclosure and evidence gathering.
- You do-not need to explain your side of the story to friends, family, or employers until you have received legal guidance on confidentiality.

## Important reassurance
Feeling significant anxiety about saying the "wrong thing" is a common and rational response to a legal investigation. Requesting a solicitor is a procedural right, not an indication of guilt, and it is the most effective way to ensure your words are not misinterpreted.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate period following notification of an investigation. It does not cover trial procedures, court appearances, or specific defense tactics, which must be handled by a qualified legal professional.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding police procedure and evidence vary between England/Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. Always prioritize the specific advice given by your solicitor.
