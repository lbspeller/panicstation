# What to do if… you are told your job is being eliminated effective immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and ask for the decision, exit date, notice position, and pay details in writing before you agree to anything. Do not sign a settlement agreement, resignation, or “acceptance” message while you are in shock.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a settlement agreement, resignation letter, or acceptance email in the meeting or on the same day just to make it end.
- Do not say “I resign” or agree this is “mutual” unless you genuinely mean it and have had time to think.
- Do not take, forward, screenshot, or copy confidential business, customer, client, or colleague information.
- Do not bypass access controls or copy work files to protect yourself.
- Do not hand over equipment before asking how you will receive personal employment documents you can no longer access.
- Do not post about it on social media or send angry messages to colleagues while emotions are high.
- Do not assume “effective immediately” means “no notice” or “no pay”.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for written confirmation before the meeting ends, or immediately afterwards by email.** Ask HR or your manager to confirm:
   - whether this is a confirmed redundancy dismissal today or whether you are “at risk” pending consultation
   - the stated reason for the role elimination
   - your effective termination date, if there is one
   - whether you are expected to work notice, be placed on garden leave, or receive payment in lieu of notice
   - who your HR contact is

2. **Ask what redundancy process they say applies.** Use calm, direct wording: “Please confirm the redundancy process being followed, whether there has been consultation, and whether there is an appeal or review route.” If the employer is proposing 20 or more redundancies within 90 days at one establishment, ask whether collective consultation rules apply and what consultation has happened.

3. **Secure personal employment records without taking confidential work material.** Focus only on documents about you, such as:
   - your contract or offer letter
   - any redundancy or termination letter
   - recent payslips and P60s
   - holiday balance
   - benefits information
   - bonus, commission, or expenses records that relate to your own pay
   If you cannot access these without breaching policy, ask HR to email them to your personal email address or post them to you.

4. **Write down the key facts while they are fresh.** Record the date and time, who was present, the exact words used for the reason, whether they said “redundancy”, “role elimination”, “dismissal”, “at risk”, or “settlement agreement”, and anything said about notice, pay, equipment, and access.

5. **Ask for an itemised final pay breakdown.** Ask HR to list:
   - final salary period covered and pay date
   - notice pay or payment in lieu of notice
   - accrued but untaken holiday pay
   - statutory or contractual redundancy pay, if eligible
   - bonus, commission, expenses, benefits, and pension treatment
   - when your final payslip and P45 will be issued

6. **If they mention a settlement agreement, slow the conversation down.** Say: “I cannot consider this properly today. Please send the full written offer and proposed agreement, and I will review it after independent advice.” A settlement agreement requires advice from a relevant independent adviser to be legally valid.

7. **Ask how equipment return and system access will work.** Confirm what must be returned, when, how return costs will be handled, and how you will receive any personal documents after access ends.

8. **If you already claim benefits, report the job change when you are able.** If you may need support, you may be able to look at Universal Credit, New Style Jobseeker’s Allowance, or other benefits after you have the basic employment and pay details.

9. **If the explanation feels inconsistent or pressured, contact Acas with your written notes.** Ask what first steps should usually happen in a redundancy situation like yours and what time limits or documents you should be aware of.

## What can wait
- Deciding your full legal strategy, negotiation position, or whether to challenge the decision.
- Writing a long email explaining your side.
- Updating your CV, LinkedIn, recruiters, or your wider network.
- Making big financial decisions beyond covering immediate essentials.
- Deciding whether this was fair or unfair before you have the written reason, pay breakdown, and process details.

## Important reassurance
Being told your role is ending “effective immediately” can make it hard to remember details or respond clearly. You do not need to solve everything in the meeting. Getting the facts in writing, protecting your personal paperwork, and delaying signatures are strong first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours or day. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if there is a settlement agreement, a short deadline, disputed pay, unclear employment status, long service, discrimination concerns, or a wider redundancy programme.
