# What to do if… your cane, walker, or other walking aid breaks while you are out (UK)
## Short answer
Stop and make yourself safe first: do not keep walking on a broken aid. Get seated or supported, then arrange assisted travel, staff help, or a lift rather than risking a fall.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep walking on a cracked, wobbling, or collapsed aid just to finish the trip.
- Do not lean hard on loose fences, bins, thin poles, or anything that could move.
- Do not let someone lift or carry you if you feel unsteady; that can injure you or them.
- Do not step into the road to wave vehicles down.
- Do not rush for a bus, train, taxi, or appointment if that means moving without safe support.
- Do not try to force a bent frame, snapped cane, loose wheel, or sharp broken part back into use.

## What to do now
1. **Get to the safest nearby pause point, within a few steps if possible.** Aim for a bench, café, shop doorway, station concourse, staffed counter, or anywhere flat, lit, and out of foot traffic. If you cannot safely take even a few steps, stay where you are and ask someone nearby to bring help to you.
2. **Stabilise your body before deciding anything.** Sit down if you can. Take a short pause and check for pain, dizziness, new weakness, or whether you nearly fell.
   - If you have fallen and may have injured your head, hip, back, or neck, or you cannot get up, call 999.
   - If you may be injured, in pain, or unwell after a stumble or fall but it does not seem life-threatening, use NHS 111.
3. **Move the broken aid out of the way if you can do that safely.** Keep it out of foot traffic so it does not trip you or someone else. If it has a sharp edge, dangling part, loose wheel, or collapsed frame, fold or secure it only if that does not require force.
4. **Ask for specific help where you are.** In a station, shopping centre, café, shop, clinic, or large venue, ask staff for a chair, a safe place to wait, and help contacting a taxi, a trusted person, or station assistance.
5. **If you are at a rail station, ask for Passenger Assist or turn up and go help.** Tell staff your walking aid has broken and you cannot move safely without assistance. Ask for help getting to a safe exit, waiting area, taxi rank, platform, or connection. It may take time, so ask for somewhere safe to sit while staff organise help.
6. **Choose the lowest-risk way to get to a safe place.** Use one option only for now:
   - Ask someone you trust to pick you up. Send your exact location, a nearby landmark, and whether you can walk a few steps.
   - Ask staff to help you reach or book a licensed taxi or private hire vehicle from a safe pick-up point.
   - If you are mid-journey, pause the journey and get help to a safe waiting place instead of trying to complete the trip as planned.
7. **If you must move a short distance indoors, reduce fall risk.** Move slowly on flat ground. Ask for a steady arm only if you want it and it feels safe. If you feel unsure, stay seated and wait for assistance instead.
8. **Put one replacement contact in place, then stop there for now.** If the aid was issued or borrowed, contact the service or person who supplied it when you are safe. If you are unsure who supplied it, put a reminder to contact your GP, physiotherapist, occupational therapy service, or hospital team for repair or replacement guidance.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to change your mobility setup long-term.
- You do not need to prove you can manage alone by finishing the trip as planned.
- You do not need to argue about fault, refunds, complaints, or compensation right now.
- You can deal with repair, replacement, shopping, and paperwork later, once you are safe.

## Important reassurance
It makes sense to feel embarrassed, exposed, or panicky when a walking aid fails in public. Stopping and asking for help is a controlled safety decision, not a failure.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent injury and get you safely to a workable place. Later decisions about repair, replacement type, funding, assessments, or complaints may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you may be injured, feel unwell, or cannot move safely, use urgent services or ask staff to call on your behalf.
