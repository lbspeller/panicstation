# What to do if… you are told a person who died left minor children and custody decisions are suddenly urgent (UK)

## Short answer
Ensure the children are in a safe location with a known adult and verify who holds legal parental responsibility before making any changes to their residence or care.

## Do not do these things
- Do not move the children to a new town or address without the consent of everyone who holds parental responsibility.
- Do not assume a surviving parent has the automatic right to take the children if there was a "lives with" court order in favour of the person who died.
- Do not rely on verbal promises or old "letters of intent" that are not witnessed or part of a formal will.
- Do not get into a physical or heated confrontation in front of the children at the family home, school, or hospital.
- Do not sign any document that claims to "transfer" or "give up" rights to the children without seeking legal advice.
- Do not post details of the children's situation, location, or the death on social media.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the children’s current physical location and the identity of the adult with them.**  
   **If the children are in immediate danger or their location is unknown, call 999.** If they are safe, establish a single point of contact with the adult currently supervising them and request that no one moves the children until legal authority is confirmed.

2. **Determine who holds Parental Responsibility (PR) immediately.**  
   **Identify if there is a surviving parent or a named guardian.**  
   - A mother always has PR.  
   - A father has PR if he was married to the mother at the time of birth, or is named on the birth certificate (for births after Dec 2003 in England/Wales/NI), or has a PR agreement/order.  
   - In Scotland, a father has PR if he is on the birth certificate (since May 2006) or was married to the mother.

3. **Check for a "lives with" Child Arrangements Order or Special Guardianship Order.**  
   **Locate any court papers regarding the children's living arrangements.** If the deceased parent had a court order stating the child "lived with" them, any "testamentary guardian" named in their will takes on PR immediately upon the death, even if another parent survives.

4. **Identify any "testamentary guardian" named in the deceased person's will.**  
   **Search for the most recent will or contact the deceased’s solicitor.**  
   - In England, Wales, and NI: The appointment usually only takes effect if no surviving parent has PR, UNLESS the deceased had a "lives with" order.  
   - In Scotland: The appointment of a guardian in a will takes effect immediately on death even if another parent survives.

5. **Contact the local council's Children's Services if there is no clear legal guardian.**  
   **Call the local authority where the children are currently located and ask for the "Emergency Duty Team" (EDT) if it is outside office hours.** Explain that the sole carer has died and you need to verify safe temporary care. Children’s Services can provide "Section 20" voluntary accommodation or help formalise kinship care if there is a dispute.

6. **Inform the children’s school or childcare provider of the situation.**  
   **Provide clear instructions on who is authorised to collect the children today.** Ensure they have your contact details and a copy of any documents you have located. Schools should not release children to adults without confirmed PR if there is any doubt about safety.

7. **Use the urgent Family Court pathway if there is a risk of child abduction or harm.**  
   **Contact a family law solicitor or the local family court office to request an "emergency/without-notice" Child Arrangements Order.** In a genuine emergency following a death, you are exempt from the requirement to attend a Mediation Information and Assessment Meeting (MIAM) before applying.

8. **Keep a log of all communications and documents shown to you.**  
   **Note down the time, date, and names of everyone who claims authority over the children.** Photograph or scan birth certificates, marriage certificates, wills, and court orders as soon as they are found.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle the "permanent" home for the children in the first 24 hours.
- You do not need to sort out the deceased's estate, bank accounts, or house clearance.
- You do not need to respond to every extended family member's request for "visits" or "contact" immediately.
- You do not need to decide on long-term school changes or benefit claims (like Guardian's Allowance) today.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for families to feel a sense of extreme urgency or conflict when a parent dies. Focus only on the children’s immediate stability. If you are acting in good faith to keep children in a familiar, safe environment while you verify legal rights, you are taking the correct first step. Panic is common, but legal clarity usually resolves the immediate tension within a few days.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hours following a death. It does not cover long-term Special Guardianship applications, adoption, or complex inheritance issues, which require specialist legal advice.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. In the UK, parental responsibility rules differ slightly between England/Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. If a child's safety is at risk, contact the police or Children's Services immediately.
