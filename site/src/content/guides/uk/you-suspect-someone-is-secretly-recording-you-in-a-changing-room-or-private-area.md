# What to do if… you suspect someone is secretly recording you in a changing room or private area (UK)

## Short answer
Get dressed or covered, move to a safer public place, and tell the duty manager or security immediately so they can stop people entering the area and help contact police if needed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the person yourself or try to physically stop them.
- Do not search the area alone or in a way that leaves you more exposed.
- Do not handle, move, delete, or share any device, image, or video unless you need to do so for immediate safety.
- Do not post details, images, or accusations online.
- Do not stay in the changing room to “check again” if you already feel unsafe.
- Do not blame yourself or wait until you feel certain before asking for help.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** Get dressed or cover yourself, leave the changing room, toilet, cubicle, fitting room, or private area, and go to reception, the shop floor, a front desk, or another public place. If someone you trust is nearby, ask them to stay with you.

2. **Tell the duty manager or security clearly.** Say: “I suspect someone is secretly recording in or near the changing rooms or another private area. I need you to treat this as urgent.”

3. **Ask the venue for immediate safety steps.** Ask them to:
   - stop anyone else entering that changing room or private area for now,
   - send the duty manager or security to the area,
   - note the time and exact location, such as the cubicle, mirror, toilet, locker area, or fitting room,
   - keep relevant CCTV, door-entry records, booking records, or staff rota information from being overwritten,
   - avoid moving any suspected device unless it is needed for immediate safety.

4. **Contact police if there is danger, a crime in progress, or you want to report now.**
   - Call **999** if someone is in immediate danger, the person may still be there, or the situation is happening now.
   - Call **101** or use police online reporting if it is not an emergency.
   - You can ask the venue to call while you stay in a public place.

5. **If you think you or someone else may have been recorded, consider confidential specialist support.** A Sexual Assault Referral Centre or sexual violence support service can usually offer practical and emotional support, and can talk through options without forcing you to report to police. The right route depends on where you are in the UK; use the resources below for England, Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland, or England and Wales support.

6. **Make a brief note while it is fresh.** Write down the location, date, time, exact area, what you noticed, any description of the person or device, and who you told at the venue. Keep it short.

7. **If a child, teenager, or vulnerable person is involved, prioritise getting them safely dressed and away from the area.** Report the concern to the duty manager and police if there is immediate risk, a possible crime in progress, or you need urgent help.

8. **Keep your own phone as it is for now.** If you took a photo of a suspicious object or received any messages, do not delete, edit, forward, or post them while you are deciding what support you want.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to give a formal statement.
- You do not need to identify the exact law, device type, or how the recording may have happened.
- You do not need to contact head office, write a full complaint, or post online today.
- You do not need to keep using the facility to see whether it happens again.
- You do not need to work out takedowns, legal action, or compensation right now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shocked, embarrassed, angry, numb, or unsure is a common response to a privacy or sexual boundary violation. Acting on a serious suspicion is reasonable; early action is about safety, not proving everything yourself.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the moment, reduce risk, and connect you with support. Later decisions about reporting, complaints, legal options, image takedowns, or medical support may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
