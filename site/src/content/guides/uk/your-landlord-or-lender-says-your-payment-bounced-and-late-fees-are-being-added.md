# What to do if… your landlord or lender says your payment bounced and late fees are being added (UK)

## Short answer
Confirm with your bank whether the payment failed, returned, reversed, or is still pending. Before sending any replacement payment, verify the payee details through a route you already trust and ask the landlord, agent, lender, or loan provider for a written breakdown of what they say is unpaid and how any charge is being calculated.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send money to new bank details received by text, WhatsApp, email, or an unexpected link until you have verified them using a trusted number, statement, tenancy agreement, lender letter, app, or portal you already use.
- Don’t pay twice “just in case” before checking whether the original payment is pending, reversed, returned, or delayed.
- Don’t ignore the message because you feel overwhelmed; a quick written reply can help stop confusion and escalation.
- Don’t keep it phone-only. Keep a written trail of dates, amounts, references, and what each side says happened.
- Don’t write that you accept you are “in arrears” unless you know that is true. Use neutral wording such as “I am checking the payment status.”
- Don’t cancel or change future Direct Debits, standing orders, or card instructions in panic unless your bank or provider confirms that is needed.

## What to do now
1. **Check for a possible payment-diversion scam before sharing details or sending money.**  
   Contact the landlord, agent, lender, or loan provider using a number, app, portal, statement, or agreement you already trust. Do not use only the contact details in the unexpected message. Ask them to confirm the notice is genuine and to confirm the correct payment details.

2. **Ask your bank for the exact payment status.**  
   Check your banking app and, if anything is unclear, contact your bank and ask:
   - whether the payment was rejected, returned, reversed after leaving, still pending, or successfully completed
   - the date and time recorded for the payment attempt
   - the amount, reference, and payee details used
   - the reason shown, such as insufficient funds, wrong details, security hold, processing delay, Direct Debit issue, standing order issue, card decline, or system error

3. **Save proof before replying.**  
   Save screenshots or PDFs showing the payment attempt, any returned or reversed entry, the amount, date, time, reference, and payment method. Keep copies of the landlord, agent, lender, or provider message and any statement or ledger they send.

4. **Reply in writing and ask for a short hold on extra charges while the payment is traced.**  
   Use email, letter, secure message, or the official portal. Ask for:
   - the exact payment they say is missing, including amount and due date
   - a current rent ledger, mortgage statement, loan statement, or account statement
   - the late charge or interest calculation
   - the contract term, tenancy term, mortgage condition, or loan term they rely on
   - written confirmation that they will pause adding further charges while you and the bank check the payment

5. **If the bank confirms the payment failed or was returned, make one replacement payment to verified details.**  
   Pay the core amount promptly using a traceable method, such as bank transfer to already-confirmed details or card payment through the official portal. Use a clear reference such as “rent replacement payment” or “mortgage replacement payment” and save the confirmation.

6. **If the bank transfer went to the wrong details, contact your bank straight away.**  
   Ask whether they can trace, recall, or raise a mistaken-payment or fraud process. Give them the payee details, amount, date, reference, and any messages that gave the payment instructions.

7. **If this is rent, check whether the late charge is allowed for where you live.**  
   Ask the landlord or agent to point to the exact agreement term and written calculation. As a practical first check:
   - **England:** for covered private tenancies, a landlord or agent can generally charge late rent interest only if rent is overdue by at least 2 weeks, the tenancy allows it, and the rate is no more than 3% above the Bank of England base rate.
   - **Wales:** for covered contracts, late rent interest is generally limited to 3% above the Bank of England base rate and is usually only allowed once the rent is more than 7 days overdue.
   - **Scotland and Northern Ireland:** rules and tenancy types differ. Do not assume the England or Wales limits apply. Still ask for the clause, the calculation, and written proof that the charge is allowed.
   If the explanation is unclear, get fast housing advice before paying a disputed fee.

8. **If this is a mortgage, loan, card, Direct Debit, or payment-provider problem, raise a written complaint if the charge looks wrong.**  
   Say what happened, attach proof, and ask them to remove any charge or adverse marker caused by an error or processing problem. Financial firms usually need a chance to respond before the Financial Ombudsman Service can look at it. Some payment-services complaints have shorter response times, while many other complaints have an 8-week response deadline.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to move, refinance, change banks, start a legal claim, or make a long complaint.
- You do not need to prove who is at fault right now. The immediate priorities are to stop avoidable charges, avoid paying twice, verify the correct payee, and get the facts in writing.
- You do not need to agree a long-term repayment plan unless it turns out the payment genuinely cannot be made.

## Important reassurance
Bounced-payment notices often come from ordinary problems such as processing delays, wrong references, Direct Debit timing, card checks, bank security holds, or a returned payment. You are not making things worse by pausing briefly to verify the facts; that is the safer way to avoid a duplicate payment or a scam.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and reduce avoidable fees or escalation. If the payment genuinely cannot be made, if you are threatened with eviction or repossession, or if your credit file may be affected, later decisions may need specialist housing, debt, legal, or financial help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, debt, housing, mortgage, banking, or professional advice. UK housing and fee rules vary by nation, tenancy type, lender, product, and contract wording. Keep a written trail, use cautious wording, and rely on bank transaction records and written statements from the landlord, agent, lender, or provider.
