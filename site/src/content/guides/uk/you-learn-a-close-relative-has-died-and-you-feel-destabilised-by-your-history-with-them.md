# What to do if… you learn a close relative has died and you feel destabilised by your history with them (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before you respond to family or make decisions. Get physically safe, reduce incoming messages, and contact one trusted person or urgent support if you feel unable to stay safe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to funeral, house, financial, or family decisions today just to stop pressure.
- Do not stay in a phone call where you feel frozen, fawning, or unable to speak clearly; end it and switch to text.
- Do not post about the death online until you know the main family contact has told the people they need to tell.
- Do not use alcohol, drugs, or extra sedatives to force yourself to sleep or feel numb.
- Do not drive, travel, or go to a family home if you feel spaced out, shaking, or unsafe to concentrate.
- Do not assume your reaction has to look like ordinary grief; numbness, anger, relief, guilt, and confusion can happen after a traumatic relationship.

## What to do now
1. **Make a 20-minute safe pause.** Sit somewhere you are physically safe and not likely to be interrupted. Silence non-essential notifications. Put water nearby and eat something small if you can.
2. **Confirm only the minimum facts.** If the news came through social media, a vague message, or a third party, ask for one written confirmation: “Can you text me who confirmed this, when it happened, and who is the main contact?” You do not need the full story now.
3. **Send one support text.** Message one safe person: “I’ve had distressing news about a death in my family. I have trauma history with this person and I feel destabilised. Can you stay with me or be on the phone for 10 minutes?”
4. **Use urgent help if you might not stay safe.** If you might harm yourself, have already harmed yourself, or feel you cannot keep yourself safe, call 999 or go to A&E. If you need urgent mental health help but it is not an immediate emergency, call NHS 111 and select the mental health option, or ask your GP surgery for an urgent appointment. Local urgent mental health routes can vary.
5. **Use a listening line if you need a human voice now.** Samaritans is available on 116 123 at any time, day or night. You do not have to explain the whole family history; you can say, “A relative has died and I’m having a trauma reaction.”
6. **Set a 24-hour contact boundary.** Choose one simple rule: no calls, text updates only, or one update from one person. Send: “I’ve received the news. I’m not able to talk right now. Please text key practical updates only.”
7. **Find out who is handling practical matters without volunteering.** Ask: “Who is arranging the funeral or registering the death?” and “Who is dealing with the estate or official notifications?” If the person who died lived in England, Scotland or Wales, Tell Us Once may be used after the death is registered, or after there is a final or interim death certificate from the coroner; it is not available if the person was living in Northern Ireland when they died.
8. **Refuse triggering tasks for today.** If someone asks you to clear a home, read papers, identify belongings, contact officials, or visit a place connected with the person who died, reply: “I can’t do that today. Put it in writing and I’ll look later.”
9. **Pause funeral and viewing decisions.** You do not have to decide today whether to view the body, attend the funeral, speak to relatives, or explain your relationship. Ask for dates, times, and deadlines in writing, then stop.
10. **Flag the situation to a professional contact if you already have one.** If you have a GP, therapist, care coordinator, or support worker, send a short message: “Bereavement plus trauma history; feeling destabilised; need support.” You do not have to explain the whole history.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to attend the funeral, travel, see relatives, or take on responsibilities.
- You do not need to write a tribute, answer everyone, or explain what the relationship was really like.
- You do not need to sort belongings, paperwork, wills, inheritance, or “who gets what” discussions.
- You do not need to forgive, feel sad, feel relieved, or make your reaction acceptable to anyone else.

## Important reassurance
A death can trigger trauma responses even when the relationship was painful or complicated. Numbness, relief, anger, guilt, sadness, or feeling nothing at all are not a moral verdict. The aim for now is to get through the next few hours safely and avoid decisions you cannot easily undo.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on stabilising, setting boundaries, and avoiding being pulled into practical, legal, or family roles while you are destabilised. Later decisions may need specialist bereavement support, trauma-informed support, legal guidance, or help from someone dealing with the estate.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are at risk of harming yourself, or you feel unable to stay safe, call 999 or go to A&E.
