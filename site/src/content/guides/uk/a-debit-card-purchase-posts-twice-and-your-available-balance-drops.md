# What to do if… a debit card purchase posts twice and your available balance drops (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as an urgent account problem: capture what you can see, contact your bank now, and ask them to protect you from knock-on overdraft charges or failed payments while the duplicate is checked.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t make test purchases, cash withdrawals, or transfers from that account if the available balance is low or negative.
- Don’t assume it will fix itself if both entries are posted, or if the duplicate is already causing missed payments or overdraft use.
- Don’t call it fraud if you recognise the purchase and the problem is that it appears twice; describe it as a duplicate card payment or duplicate posting.
- Don’t ignore possible unauthorised activity if one entry is not yours; use the bank’s unauthorised transaction or fraud route instead.
- Don’t cancel standing orders, Direct Debits, or the whole account in a panic unless the bank tells you there is wider account risk.
- Don’t delete receipts, emails, banking-app alerts, merchant messages, or screenshots.

## What to do now
1. **Check the two entries before you call.**
   - Open each transaction in your banking app.
   - Note whether each entry is pending or posted.
   - Write down the amount, merchant name, date, time, reference, and transaction ID if shown.
   - Take screenshots showing both entries and the collapsed available balance.

2. **Contact your bank through its secure app, card disputes route, or phone number on your card.**
   - Say: “One debit card purchase appears to have posted twice. My available balance has dropped and I need one duplicate checked and reversed.”
   - Ask whether the bank is treating it as a duplicate presentment, an incorrect card transaction, chargeback, or another debit-card dispute route.
   - Ask when your available balance may be corrected and whether a temporary credit is possible while the duplicate is investigated.

3. **Use the unauthorised transaction route if one entry is not yours.**
   - If you do not recognise one of the entries, freeze or lock your card in the app if you can do that safely.
   - Tell the bank clearly: “I do not recognise this transaction and I did not authorise it.”
   - Ask the bank what it needs from you now and whether it will restore the account while it investigates. UK rules usually require prompt refunding of unauthorised payment transactions, but there can be exceptions, so use the bank’s exact unauthorised transaction process.

4. **Ask the bank to limit knock-on harm.**
   - Ask the bank to add a note that the low or negative balance is caused by a disputed duplicate debit.
   - Ask it to waive or refund overdraft interest, fees, or failed-payment charges if they arise because of the duplicate.
   - Ask whether it can stop or re-present any payments due today without charging you.

5. **Protect essential spending and urgent bills today.**
   - Use another account or payment method for essential food, travel, medicines, or childcare if you have one.
   - If rent, mortgage, utilities, insurance, or council tax may fail, contact the biller today and say a duplicate debit has temporarily reduced your available balance.
   - Ask the biller for a short hold, a reattempt date, or written confirmation that they will not treat today’s failed payment as deliberate non-payment.

6. **Contact the merchant if it is quick and calm to do so.**
   - Say: “I can see two posted card payments for one purchase. Can you confirm whether you received one payment or two?”
   - Ask the merchant to reverse one duplicate payment if it can see two settled payments.
   - Keep any email, chat transcript, receipt, or refund confirmation.

7. **Start a formal complaint if the bank does not protect the account or explain the route.**
   - Use the word “complaint” in a secure message, email, app chat, or letter.
   - Keep the complaint reference number and a short timeline of what happened.
   - For most financial complaints, firms generally have up to 8 weeks to respond, but payment service providers and e-money issuers must normally respond to certain payment complaints within 15 business days, with a final response by 35 business days if they need more time.
   - If you are unhappy with the final response, or the right response deadline passes, you can usually ask the Financial Ombudsman Service to look at it. You usually need to do this within 6 months of the firm’s final response.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to switch banks.
- You do not need to write a long complaint before contacting the bank; screenshots, dates, amounts, and a clear sentence are enough to start.
- You do not need to cancel all cards or payment arrangements unless there are signs of wider unauthorised activity.
- You do not need to decide who is at fault today; the immediate job is to get the account protected and the duplicate investigated.

## Important reassurance
A duplicate debit can feel alarming because the available balance changes before the problem is explained. You are not being unreasonable by asking the bank to treat this as urgent, protect essential payments, and explain the exact reversal route.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, aimed at stabilising your account, reducing missed-payment harm, and getting the bank’s dispute or complaint process moving. Later decisions, including switching accounts, debt help, or a wider complaint, can wait until the immediate balance problem is clearer.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, debt, or professional advice. Bank and card-scheme processes vary, and the right route depends on whether the second debit is pending, posted, unauthorised, incorrectly executed, or a merchant duplicate.
