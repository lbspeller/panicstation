# What to do if… you suspect your partner is interfering with your contraception or pregnancy choices (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause and contact confidential specialist support from a device or account your partner cannot monitor. If pregnancy is possible, seek confidential NHS sexual health or contraception support as soon as you safely can.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront them in the moment if you fear it could escalate, especially if they have threatened you, blocked you from leaving, or monitor your phone.
- Do not delay sexual health advice because you feel unsure whether it “counts” as abuse.
- Do not use a shared device, shared browser, shared Apple or Google account, shared tablet, or shared location account to search for help if monitoring is possible.
- Do not turn off location sharing, delete accounts, or change passwords suddenly if that could alert them and increase danger; ask a specialist service about safer timing.
- Do not delete messages, photos, packaging, or notes in a panic if keeping them somewhere safe would not put you at risk.
- Do not assume you have to report to police, end the relationship, or explain everything before you can get help.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** If you can, move to somewhere you can think without them present, such as a public place, a trusted person’s home, a workplace, or another place with an exit and people nearby. If you are in immediate danger, call **999**.
2. **Use a safer way to reach help.** If your phone or accounts may be monitored, use a trusted friend’s phone, a work phone, or a library device. Avoid shared accounts and shared browsers where possible. Do not rely only on quick-exit buttons if your partner may check your device.
3. **Contact a domestic abuse helpline that fits you.** Choose the option that feels safest and most relevant:
   - **National Domestic Abuse Helpline (Refuge)** for women experiencing domestic abuse.
   - **Men’s Advice Line** for male victims of domestic abuse, including trans and non-binary people who feel this is the right service for them.
   - **Galop** for LGBT+ people experiencing domestic abuse, sexual violence, threats, or harassment.
   Ask for safety planning about monitored devices, contraception interference, safer communication, and what to prepare if you need to leave quickly.
4. **Get confidential contraception and sexual health support.** Contact an NHS sexual health clinic, contraception service, some pharmacies, or your GP and say you need confidential advice because you are worried a partner may be interfering with contraception or consent. Ask about:
   - Pregnancy testing and what timing makes sense for you.
   - Emergency contraception if pregnancy may be possible.
   - Contraception methods that may be harder for someone else to interfere with, if you want that.
   - STI testing if there was condom interference, non-consensual sex, or pressure you could not safely refuse.
   - What the confidentiality limits are before you share details, if you are worried about information reaching your partner.
5. **If anything sexual happened without your consent, or you are not sure, consider a SARC.** A Sexual Assault Referral Centre can offer medical, practical, and emotional support. You do not have to report to police to get help.
6. **Make one brief record only if it is safe.** You do not have to collect proof. If it will not put you at risk, write down dates and what made you concerned, such as missing pills, damaged condoms, threats, pressure, refusal to let you use contraception, or monitoring. Keep it somewhere your partner cannot access, such as with a trusted person or on paper outside the home.
7. **Tell one safe person and set a check-in.** Keep it simple: “I’m not safe to talk freely, but I’m worried my partner is interfering with contraception.” Agree a neutral code word meaning “call me” or “I need help.”
8. **Use specialist sexual violence support if you want to talk it through.** Rape Crisis England & Wales can support you if something sexual happened without your consent, or if you are unsure. You can talk about what happened, what you want now, and what can wait, without pressure to report.

## What can wait
- You do **not** have to decide today whether to report to police, end the relationship, move out, or tell family.
- You do **not** have to prove anything before contacting a helpline, SARC, GP, pharmacy, or sexual health clinic.
- You do **not** have to choose a long-term contraception plan today; the immediate step is safe, confidential advice.
- You can decide later whether to keep documentation, speak to an advocate, or ask about legal options.

## Important reassurance
Feeling confused, unsure, or like you might be “overreacting” is common when someone undermines your reproductive choices. Wanting control over whether and when you become pregnant is basic bodily autonomy, and you deserve support, privacy, and safety.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate risk, protect your options, and connect you with confidential support. Later decisions about the relationship, housing, health care, reporting, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If pregnancy, STI risk, injury, or health harm is possible, seek NHS clinical care as soon as you safely can.
