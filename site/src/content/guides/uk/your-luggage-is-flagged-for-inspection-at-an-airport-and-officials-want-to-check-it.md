# What to do if… your luggage is flagged for inspection at an airport and officials want to check it (UK)

## Short answer
Stay calm, keep your hands visible, and let the officials direct the inspection. Ask what rule or authority applies, and if anything is taken, ask for a receipt, seizure information, notice, or contact reference before you leave if one is available.

## Do not do these things
- Do not argue, joke about weapons, explosives, drugs, or threats, or raise your voice.
- Do not reach into your bag, grab items, unlock cases, or move anything unless you are asked to.
- Do not try to hide, throw away, pass to someone else, or quickly repack the item.
- Do not sign anything you do not understand; ask for it to be explained in plain English.
- Do not leave the screening or inspection point unless you are told you may go.
- Do not rely on memory if anything is taken; ask what written record or reference will be provided, and when.

## What to do now
1. **Stay in the official inspection area.** Keep your bag where they ask you to keep it, keep your hands visible, and wait for clear instructions.

2. **Check who is speaking to you if you are unsure.** Look for uniform, ID, or an official desk. Calmly ask, “Can I see your ID or know which team you are with, please?”

3. **Ask what the concern is.** Say, “Which item are you concerned about, and which rule or authority applies?” This helps separate aviation security, airline dangerous-goods rules, customs rules, and Border Force action.

4. **Let them control the search.** If you need to unlock a case, open a pocket, or move an item, ask first: “Please tell me exactly what you want me to do.”

5. **Give short, factual answers.** Explain what the item is, whether it is yours, where you packed it, and whether it contains batteries, liquids, fuel, sharp parts, food, medication, or goods you bought abroad. Do not guess if you do not know.

6. **Ask what safe options are allowed now.** Depending on the item, the airport, your airline, and where you are in the journey, officials may allow different options, such as moving an allowed item to hold luggage, surrendering an item, contacting the airline, or having an item seized. Do not move, repack, or dispose of anything unless they say it is allowed.

7. **If anything is taken, ask for the record.** Ask for:
   - what item was taken,
   - who took it,
   - whether it was surrendered, refused, retained, or seized,
   - the reason or rule given,
   - any receipt, seizure information notice, notice of seizure, case reference, or contact route,
   - when and how you will receive anything not given immediately.

8. **If HMRC or Border Force seize goods, protect the dates.** If you want to challenge whether the seizure was lawful, the notice of claim generally must be received within one calendar month of the seizure date or the date shown on the notice. If you want to ask for seized goods to be returned, called restoration, HMRC or Border Force should generally receive that request within 45 days of the seizure date or the date shown on the notice. Keep the notice, seizure date, reference number, and any contact details together.

9. **If you do not understand, ask for help before agreeing.** Say, “I need this explained more clearly,” or “I need an interpreter to understand what I am being asked to agree to.”

10. **Reduce knock-on problems only if phone use is allowed.** Message anyone meeting you that you are delayed, contact your airline if boarding may be affected, and note the time, airport location, and any visible names or ID numbers for your own record.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to complain, challenge a seizure, ask for restoration, or seek specialist advice.
- You do not need to give a long explanation; short, factual answers are enough at this stage.
- You do not need to repack everything unless you are specifically told to.
- You do not need to solve missed-flight, insurance, or complaint issues before you have the record or reference.

## Important reassurance
Bags are often checked because an ordinary item needs a closer look, such as batteries, power banks, liquids, tools, food or powders, lighters, duty goods, or other items that may be restricted. Being stopped for inspection does not by itself mean you have done something deliberately wrong; staying calm, following instructions, and keeping the paperwork usually protects your position.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the inspection moment only. If an item is seized, you are interviewed further, or you later want to challenge what happened, later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, travel, financial, medical, or professional advice. Airport security, airlines, HMRC, and Border Force may apply different rules depending on the item, the airport, the flight, and the circumstances. If you feel unwell, unsafe, or unable to understand what is happening, ask for a pause and for help to understand.
