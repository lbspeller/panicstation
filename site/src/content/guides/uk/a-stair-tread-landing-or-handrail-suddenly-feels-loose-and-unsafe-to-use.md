# What to do if… a stair tread, landing, or handrail suddenly feels loose and unsafe to use (UK)
## Short answer
Stop using that stair tread, landing, or handrail now, and keep other people away from it. Treat it as a fall or failure risk until the responsible person or a competent tradesperson has checked it and made it safe.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t test it again with your full weight or yank the rail to see how bad it is.
- Don’t let children, older adults, visitors, pets, or anyone carrying items use it “just this once”.
- Don’t rely on the loose handrail for balance.
- Don’t add a quick screw, nail, tape, or glue fix that hides the problem unless a competent person has confirmed it is safe.
- Don’t assume it is only noise if the tread, landing, post, rail, or fixing visibly moves.
- Don’t block the only exit route in a way that could trap someone in a fire or emergency.

## What to do now
1. **Get to the nearest safe pause.** Move slowly to stable flooring. Keep one hand free, do not carry anything, and do not put weight on the loose tread, landing, rail, or post if you can avoid it.
2. **Stop other people using it.** Warn anyone nearby in plain words: “Do not use this stair or rail; it is loose.” Keep children and pets away from the area.
3. **Mark the hazard clearly.** Put a light, visible warning at the top and bottom if you can do that without creating another trip hazard or blocking an emergency exit. Use lights, a note at eye level, or a chair set back from the stair opening.
4. **Use another route if there is one.** Use a different staircase or exit. If there is no safe route, stay on the safer side of the staircase and ask a neighbour, building manager, landlord, or repair service for urgent help rather than making repeated trips.
5. **If someone is injured, get the right help.** Call 999 for a life-threatening emergency, a serious injury, or if someone cannot be moved or taken for urgent care safely. If it is not immediately life-threatening but you are unsure what medical help is needed, use the local urgent health advice service, such as NHS 111 where available.
6. **Record the problem without re-testing it.** Take a few photos or a short video from a safe position. Note where it is, such as top, middle, bottom, left side, right side, communal landing, or inside the home, and what moved, such as tread, landing board, handrail bracket, banister, or newel post.
7. **Report it urgently if you rent or the stairs are communal.** Contact the landlord, agent, council or housing association repairs service, building manager, freeholder, or factor as relevant. Use writing if you can, include the photos, say it is an urgent safety issue, and say whether the stairs are the only safe route in or out.
8. **If you own the home, arrange an urgent inspection.** Contact a competent carpenter, joiner, builder, stair specialist, or structural professional as appropriate. Say the area is out of use because a stair tread, landing, or handrail is loose and unsafe.
9. **If the responsible person does not act and people are still at risk, contact the local council.** Ask for the team that deals with unsafe rented housing, housing standards, environmental health, or dangerous buildings, depending on your situation and local area.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out why it happened right now.
- You do not need to compare quotes, choose a permanent design, or plan a wider renovation today.
- You do not need to decide about complaints, compensation, or longer disputes until the hazard is controlled and the urgent report is made, unless people are still at immediate risk.

## Important reassurance
Taking this seriously is sensible. A loose stair tread, landing, handrail, banister, or post can turn an ordinary trip up or down the stairs into a fall risk, and blocking off the area calmly is a protective first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stop use, reduce the fall risk, document what happened, and get the right person involved. Repair responsibility, building defects, insurance, complaints, and liability may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, financial, building, or professional advice. A loose stair or handrail needs assessment by a competent person, and injuries or immediate danger should be treated as urgent.
