# What to do if… you receive a message that an imaging result is urgent and you are not sure what it means (UK)

## Short answer
Treat “urgent” as “needs prompt follow-up”, not as a diagnosis. Verify the message through a route you already trust, then contact the clinician or service that ordered the scan today and ask what action is needed and by when.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume “urgent” means cancer, or any other specific diagnosis, from the wording alone.
- Do not ignore it because you feel well; some urgent findings need action even without obvious symptoms.
- Do not click links, call unknown numbers back, or share personal details until you have checked the message through a trusted route.
- Do not use internet search results to decide whether the report matters.
- Do not keep calling unrelated services once you have one confirmed route and a clear timeframe.
- Do not wait for a call back if you develop symptoms that feel dangerous or rapidly worse.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether this is an emergency right now.**  
   If you have life-threatening symptoms, such as severe chest pain, signs of stroke, heavy bleeding, collapse, or severe breathing difficulty, call **999** or go to **A&E**. If you are not sure whether it is an emergency, use the urgent-care route for your part of the UK.

2. **Treat the message as “possibly real, not yet verified”.**  
   Take a screenshot or write down the time, date, sender name, phone number, hospital or clinic name, and any reference number. Do not reply with your NHS number, date of birth, address, payment details, or other personal information.

3. **Check the message through a route you already trust.**  
   If it appears in the NHS App, your GP online record, or a local patient portal you normally use, open that app or website yourself rather than following a link in the message. If it names a hospital, GP practice, imaging centre, or clinic, find the contact details from its official website or a letter you already have.

4. **Contact the service that ordered the scan.**  
   Start with the referrer, usually your GP practice, outpatient clinic, hospital team, or the team looking after you. Say: “I have received a message saying my imaging result is urgent. Please can someone check the report and tell me, in plain language, what I need to do next and by when?”

5. **Ask four specific questions.**  
   - “What does the report say in plain language?”
   - “Who is responsible for arranging the next step?”
   - “What is the timeframe: today, within a few days, or later?”
   - “What symptoms would mean I should call 999 or go to A&E?”

6. **If you cannot reach the referrer, use the right urgent-care route rather than guessing.**  
   In England or Wales, use NHS 111. In Scotland, phone NHS 24 on 111. In Northern Ireland, use nidirect guidance, the number on your hospital paperwork, or the local Health and Social Care Trust route. Say: “I have an urgent imaging result message, I cannot reach the ordering clinician, and I need help finding the right service today.”

7. **If the scan was done at a hospital, ask for the clinic or team that ordered it.**  
   Call the hospital’s main switchboard or the number on a previous appointment letter, not an unverified number in the message. Ask for the secretary, booking office, specialist nurse, or clinic team connected with the appointment that led to the scan.

8. **Ask where the written report will appear.**  
   Some results may appear in GP online records, the NHS App, or local hospital portals, but not all results appear in every system. Ask: “Where will I be able to see the report, and should I wait for it or speak to someone first?”

9. **If you are being passed around, ask for help unblocking communication.**  
   In England, you can contact the hospital’s PALS. Elsewhere in the UK, ask the hospital or Health and Social Care Trust for its patient advice, liaison, complaints, feedback, or helpline route. Say: “I have an urgent imaging result notification and need help reaching the responsible clinical team today.”

10. **Make sure they can reach you.**  
   Confirm your current phone number, ask whether they will call from a withheld or hospital number, and ask what you should do if you have not heard back by the agreed time.

11. **Write down the plan before you end the call.**  
   Note what the finding is, what the next step is, who is arranging it, the expected timeframe, and the safest number to call if nothing happens.

## What can wait
- You do not need to interpret the medical terms by yourself.
- You do not need to decide on treatment options before a clinician explains the result.
- You do not need to tell everyone right now.
- You do not need to search worst-case stories online to prepare.
- You do not need to chase every department once you have a confirmed route, a named team, and a timeframe.

## Important reassurance
“Urgent” is often used to make sure a result is reviewed and acted on promptly. It does not, by itself, tell you the cause or mean that you are in immediate danger. Your next job is simply to confirm the message is genuine and get the right clinical team to tell you the next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours after an urgent imaging result message. Later decisions about follow-up, repeat imaging, referral, or treatment may need specialist clinical help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, or professional advice. If you feel seriously unwell, your symptoms matter more than the message; use 999 or A&E for emergencies and the urgent-care route for your part of the UK if you need help deciding what to do.
