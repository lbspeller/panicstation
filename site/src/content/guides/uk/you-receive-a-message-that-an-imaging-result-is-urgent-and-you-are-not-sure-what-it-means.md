# What to do if… you receive a message that an imaging result is urgent and you are not sure what it means (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the healthcare provider who ordered the scan (the referrer) immediately to ask for a clinical explanation and a specific timeframe for action. "Urgent" in UK imaging typically means a finding needs medical review within 24 hours, whereas "Critical" requires immediate emergency assessment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume "urgent" is a confirmed diagnosis of cancer or a life-threatening event without clinical confirmation.
- Do not search individual phrases from the report online, as non-clinical interpretations of radiology terminology are frequently inaccurate.
- Do not click links or provide personal data in response to a text message until you have verified the source via official channels.
- Do not wait for a letter in the post if the digital notification specifically uses the word "urgent".
- Do not ignore the message even if you currently have no physical symptoms.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency symptoms that require immediate 999 or A&E attendance.**
   - Severe, sudden chest pain or difficulty breathing.
   - New weakness in the face or arms, or slurred speech.
   - Sudden loss of bladder or bowel control, or numbness in the "saddle" area (groin/buttocks).
   - The "worst headache of your life" that started suddenly.
   - Heavy, uncontrollable bleeding or sudden collapse.

2. **Verify the notification through the NHS App or official hospital portal.**
   - Log in to the **NHS App** directly (do not use a link from a text).
   - Check under **"GP health record"** and then **"Test results"** for imaging reports.
   - Note that imaging reports often have a 4-week delay before they appear in the app to ensure the doctor speaks to you first; if you see the "urgent" alert but no report, this is standard procedure.

3. **Call the clinician or department that requested the scan immediately.**
   - This is usually your **GP surgery** or a specific **Hospital Outpatient Clinic**.
   - If you are unsure who ordered it, check your appointment letter or the "Requested by" section on any paperwork.
   - State clearly: "I have received an urgent notification regarding my imaging results and I need to speak to a clinician about the required next steps."

4. **Ask for specific clinical instructions and deadlines.**
   - **"What is the finding in plain English?"**
   - **"What action is needed (e.g., new medication, a referral, a repeat scan)?"**
   - **"When does this action need to happen (today, within 24 hours, or this week)?"**
   - **"Are there specific symptoms I should watch for while waiting for the next step?"**

5. **Escalate to the hospital's main switchboard if the specific clinic is unreachable.**
   - Ask for the **"Radiology Department"** or the **"Imaging Secretary"** for the specialty involved (e.g., "Neurology Imaging").
   - Ask them to confirm if the "urgent" flag requires you to attend A&E or if a doctor is currently reviewing the file.

6. **Contact NHS 111 if it is out of hours and the message implies immediate action.**
   - Tell the operator: "I have received an urgent imaging result notification and cannot reach the ordering clinician."
   - They can check for "Clinical Assessment Service" (CAS) availability to provide a phone consultation with a doctor.

7. **Use PALS as a secondary route for communication barriers.**
   - If you have verified the message is real but cannot get a response from the clinical team, contact the hospital's **Patient Advice and Liaison Service (PALS)**.
   - Request that they facilitate a callback from the "Responsible Clinician" regarding an urgent result.

## What can wait
- You do not need to understand the technical "Classification" codes (e.g., RCR alert codes) used by radiologists.
- You do not need to arrange private consultations or second opinions before the original referrer has explained the finding.
- You do not need to decide on long-term treatment plans or lifestyle changes until the "urgent" finding is clinically staged or confirmed.

## Important reassurance
In the UK health system, the word "urgent" is a management tool used to ensure cases are seen within safe windows (usually 24 hours to 2 weeks) and does not mean a situation is currently unmanageable. It is common to feel a spike in heart rate or anxiety upon seeing these messages; this is a standard response to medical uncertainty.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate verification and communication steps following an "urgent" imaging alert in the UK. It does not provide medical interpretation of scan findings or long-term diagnostic advice.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not replace professional medical advice. If you feel acutely unwell or believe you are having a medical emergency, call 999 or attend your nearest A&E regardless of any test result or message.
