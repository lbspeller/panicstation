# What to do if… many conflicted copy versions of your files appear across your folders overnight (UK)

## Short answer
Pause syncing and stop the affected device from making more changes. Disconnect it from the internet first, then use a clean device to secure the cloud account and check version history or recent activity before deleting anything.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t bulk-delete “conflicted copy” files yet; one of them may be the only good version.
- Don’t move everything into new folders to tidy it up; that can make activity history and recovery harder to follow.
- Don’t leave several devices online and opening the same synced folders; that can create more conflicts.
- Don’t run cleanup scripts, deduping tools, or bulk rename tools yet.
- Don’t reinstall the cloud app, reset the computer, or wipe the account while you still need the recovery trail.
- Don’t connect a backup drive if you see ransomware signs, such as files that will not open, ransom notes, or strange new file extensions.

## What to do now
1. **Stop automatic changes.**
   - Disconnect the affected device from Wi-Fi, mobile data, and Ethernet.
   - Pause or quit the sync apps you use, such as OneDrive, Dropbox, iCloud Drive, or Google Drive.
   - If you cannot find the pause button, keep the device offline.

2. **Capture what you can see before it changes.**
   - Take screenshots or phone photos of one affected folder, the filenames, any sync error messages, and the clock or date where visible.
   - Write down which cloud service is involved and which devices were switched on or syncing overnight.
   - Note whether the changed files are in a personal account, a work account, or a shared folder.

3. **Look for signs of a simple sync conflict.**
   - Sync conflicts often create duplicates with words such as “conflicted copy”, a device name, a username, or a timestamp.
   - They can happen when more than one device or person edits the same file, or when a cloud app cannot decide which version is current.
   - If the files still open normally and there are no ransom notes or strange extensions, treat this as a recovery and version-checking problem first.

4. **Check for signs of malware or ransomware.**
   - Keep the device offline if many files suddenly will not open.
   - Keep it offline if filenames have strange new extensions, ransom notes appear, or whole folders look encrypted or renamed.
   - If this is a work, school, charity, or organisation device, stop experimenting and contact IT, your helpdesk, or the account administrator.

5. **Secure the cloud account from a different clean device.**
   - Sign in to the cloud service through its official website on a device you do not think is affected.
   - Change the password if you see suspicious activity or do not recognise a device.
   - Turn on two-step verification if it is available.
   - Review recent sign-ins, connected devices, and sharing settings.
   - Sign out unknown sessions or devices if the service offers that option.

6. **Use activity and version tools before choosing files by name.**
   - Check the service’s activity, recent changes, or version history to find when the duplication started.
   - Restore one or two important files first, so you can confirm you have the correct version.
   - If the service offers a wider restore or rollback feature, use it only after you have confirmed the time window and secured the account.
   - Avoid guessing by filename alone; conflicted copies can have similar names but different contents.

7. **Make a cautious copy only if there are no ransomware signs.**
   - Copy your most important folders to storage that does not auto-sync.
   - Disconnect that storage as soon as the copy finishes.
   - Do not reconnect it to the affected device until the account is secured and the device has been checked.

8. **Use UK reporting or support routes if this may be a cyber incident.**
   - Use the UK government cyber incident signposting service if you are unsure where to report or get help.
   - If you have been affected by cyber crime or fraud in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud.
   - If you live in Scotland, or the cybercrime happened there, use Police Scotland’s online, phone, or in-person reporting route.
   - If there is an immediate threat to life or serious danger, use 999.

## What can wait
- Deciding which of hundreds of copied files to keep.
- Deleting, deduping, or bulk-renaming the conflicted copies.
- Reinstalling cloud apps or resetting the device.
- Re-sharing folders or asking everyone to resend files.
- Working out the final cause before the account is secured and syncing is paused.

## Important reassurance
Many “conflicted copy” files can be caused by cloud sync confusion, not deliberate damage. The safer first move is to stop new changes, preserve what you can see, and use the cloud service’s activity or version tools before cleaning anything up.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent further loss and keep recovery options open. Full recovery across several devices, shared folders, or work accounts may need specialist IT or cyber support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, technical, cyber security, financial, or professional advice. If you see signs of ransomware, account compromise, or a targeted attack, keep the affected device offline and prioritise qualified help.
