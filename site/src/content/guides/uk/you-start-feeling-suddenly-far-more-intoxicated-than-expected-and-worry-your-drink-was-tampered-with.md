# What to do if… you start feeling suddenly far more intoxicated than expected and worry your drink was tampered with (UK)

## Short answer
Stop drinking immediately, alert a trusted person or venue staff, and move to a safe, supervised area. Call **999** if you feel dangerously unwell or are in immediate danger; otherwise, seek medical assessment and report the incident as soon as possible, as some substances leave the system within 12 hours.

## Do not do these things
- Do not finish the drink or consume any more alcohol to "test" your reaction.
- Do not leave the venue alone or with someone you do not know well and trust.
- Do not go home alone via public transport or an unbooked taxi if you are feeling confused or unsteady.
- Do not attempt to drive or cycle.
- Do not let a stranger "help" you away from a crowded area or into a private space.
- Do not confront a person you suspect of spiking you; prioritize your physical exit and safety.
- Do not wait until the next day to seek help if you are experiencing severe symptoms like difficulty breathing or loss of consciousness.

## What to do now
1. **Alert venue staff or security immediately.** Use the "Ask for Angela" protocol if available, or tell staff directly that you think your drink was spiked.
2. **Move to a safe, well-lit, and monitored area.** Stay within sight of staff or trusted friends. 
3. **Contact a trusted friend or family member.** Ask them to come to your location and stay with you for at least 24 hours.
4. **Call 999 for an ambulance if emergency symptoms occur.**
   - Difficulty breathing or shallow breathing.
   - Losing consciousness or being hard to wake.
   - Seizures or uncontrollable shaking.
   - Severe confusion or inability to communicate.
5. **Call NHS 111 if you are unwell but do not require an emergency ambulance.** They can provide guidance on the nearest appropriate medical facility for assessment.
6. **Report the incident to the police by calling 101 or 999 in an emergency.** State clearly that you suspect spiking. 
   - Early reporting increases the chance of a successful forensic urine or blood test, as some drugs leave the body very quickly.
7. **Contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) if you suspect a sexual assault has occurred.** You can find your nearest SARC at nhs.uk/sarcs.
   - You can receive medical care and a forensic examination at a SARC without being required to report to the police immediately.
8. **Preserve evidence if it is safe to do so.**
   - If you still have the drink, ask venue staff to keep it in a safe place for the police.
   - If you suspect sexual assault, try to avoid washing, brushing your teeth, or changing clothes until you have spoken to a SARC or the police.
   - If you must change clothes, place the items you were wearing in a clean paper bag.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the specific drug or substance used before seeking help.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to pursue a full criminal prosecution.
- You do not need to provide a comprehensive, chronological statement of the night's events right now.
- You do not need to apologize for being intoxicated or feel responsible for the actions of others.

## Important reassurance
Sudden and extreme intoxication, confusion, or loss of motor control is a physical reaction to a substance and is not your fault. It is normal to feel frightened, disoriented, or unsure of what happened. Taking steps to get into a safe environment with professional or trusted support is the most effective way to manage the situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety actions for the immediate aftermath of suspected spiking. It does not cover long-term medical recovery, legal proceedings, or investigative techniques.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional medical or legal advice. If you are experiencing a medical emergency or are in immediate physical danger, contact the emergency services on **999** immediately.
