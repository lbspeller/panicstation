# What to do if… someone keeps making unwanted sexual comments to you (UK)

## Short answer
Create distance, reduce their access to you, and tell one trusted person what is happening. If this is happening at work, ask a manager, HR, a union rep, or another responsible person for help so you are not handling it alone.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel you have to laugh, flirt back, reply quickly, or keep the conversation friendly.
- Do not meet them one-to-one to “clear the air” if you feel uneasy.
- Do not give extra personal information, such as where you live, your routines, or your relationship status, to try to calm the situation.
- Do not delete messages out of panic if you may want a record later, unless keeping them would put you at risk.
- Do not confront them alone, especially if they have power over you or you think they may retaliate.
- Do not spend the next hour trying to decide whether it is “serious enough” before taking simple protective steps.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Move near other people, end the conversation, leave the room, or stop replying. If this is online, use platform safety tools such as mute, block, restrict, or report only if that feels safe.
2. **Use one clear line if you feel safe to do so.** You can say: “Stop making sexual comments to me.” Or: “That is not okay. Do not say things like that to me.” You do not need to explain, soften it, or debate it.
3. **Change the access conditions.** Avoid being alone with them, keep doors open, ask someone else to be present, use group chats instead of direct messages, and keep work communication in work channels.
4. **Make a short record for your own clarity, only if safe.** Note the date, time, place, what was said, who saw or heard it, and where any messages are.
5. **Tell one trusted person today.** Choose someone who can help you stay less isolated: a friend, colleague, manager, HR, union rep, safeguarding lead, tutor, support worker, or another responsible person.
6. **If this is at work, ask for help through a workplace route.** Unwanted sexual comments can be sexual harassment at work, including comments framed as jokes or banter. You can ask about informal steps, a formal complaint, changing shifts, changing contact arrangements, or having someone else present.
7. **Get confidential specialist support if you feel shaken, scared, or unsure.** If you are aged 16 or over in England or Wales, the 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line is free on 0808 500 2222 and has online chat. It is not an emergency service.
8. **If there has been unwanted sexual contact or assault, get specialist help rather than handling it alone.** A Sexual Assault Referral Centre can offer medical, practical, and emotional support. You do not have to decide about police reporting before asking for help.
9. **If you are in immediate danger, call 999.** If you need urgent medical advice and it is not an emergency, use NHS 111.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to make a formal complaint, report to police, resign, confront them, or send a perfect message.
- You do not need to prove their motive before taking steps to reduce contact.
- You do not need a complete timeline before telling a responsible person or contacting a support service.
- You do not need to work out tonight whether they are “dangerous” or “just awkward” before protecting your space.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel confused, freeze, laugh, go quiet, or minimise it when someone pushes a boundary in small steps. Wanting the comments to stop is enough reason to create distance and get support.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, reducing access, preserving your options, and getting support. Later decisions about complaints, reporting, employment steps, or legal routes can be made with specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
