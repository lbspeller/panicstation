# What to do if… you feel suddenly far more intoxicated than expected and worry your drink was tampered with (UK)

## Short answer
Stop drinking, get to a safer place with a trusted person or venue staff, and get medical help if you feel very unwell or unsafe. Call 999 now if you are in immediate danger, seriously unwell, or hard to keep awake.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “push through” or keep drinking alcohol to see whether it passes.
- Do not go home alone, or leave with someone you do not fully trust, even if they seem helpful.
- Do not drive, cycle, or take unaccompanied public transport if you feel confused, sleepy, sick, or unsteady.
- Do not let anyone pressure you to “sleep it off” somewhere unfamiliar.
- Do not confront a suspected person yourself.
- Do not rely on self-testing kits to decide whether to get help.
- If you think you may have been sexually assaulted and you might want help later, try not to shower or change clothes yet only if you can stay safe and comfortable. Your wellbeing comes first.

## What to do now
1. **Tell a safe person immediately.** If you are in a bar, club, festival, restaurant, taxi queue, or public place, tell venue staff, security, event staff, or another safe adult. Ask them to help you move to a quieter, monitored area.
2. **Stay with someone you trust.** Ask that person or venue staff to stay with you, help you keep your phone, and contact a trusted friend, family member, or taxi you choose.
3. **Call 999 for urgent danger or serious symptoms.** Call for an ambulance or police help if you are very drowsy, hard to wake, collapsing, having a seizure, struggling to breathe, vomiting repeatedly, severely confused, injured, or feel in immediate danger.
4. **Get urgent medical advice if it is not a 999 emergency but you are worried.** Use NHS 111 online or call 111 where available, or go to A&E if symptoms feel significant or are getting worse.
5. **If you might have been sexually assaulted, consider a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC).** A SARC can offer medical care, practical support, and options around forensic evidence. You do not have to decide right now whether to report to police.
6. **If you want confidential specialist support now, use a specialist support line.** If you are 16 or over, you can call the 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line on 0808 500 2222. If you are under 19, you can contact Childline on 0800 1111.
7. **Report spiking to police when you can, if you choose to.** Call 999 if you are in danger or need urgent help; otherwise call 101. Some drugs can leave the body in less than 12 hours, so early reporting can make testing more useful.
8. **Keep simple details if you can.** Note the venue name, approximate time, who you were with, what you drank, and anything unusual you remember, even if it is patchy.
9. **Preserve one item only if it is safe and you want that option later.** Do not throw away your drink or container; ask staff to keep it aside. If that creates risk, pressure, or conflict, skip it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly what substance it was right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a formal police statement or support an investigation.
- You do not need to tell everyone what happened.
- You do not need to write a perfect timeline. A few brief notes are enough.
- You do not need to decide now about complaints to a venue, counselling, or longer-term support.

## Important reassurance
Feeling suddenly “too drunk”, confused, sleepy, sick, or unlike yourself can be frightening and disorienting. You deserve help even if you are not completely sure what happened, and it is reasonable to prioritise safety and medical care first.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you get safer, get medical support if needed, and preserve options. Later decisions about testing, reporting, venue complaints, counselling, or legal processes may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general first-step information, not medical, legal, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you think you are in danger or seriously unwell, call 999 now.
