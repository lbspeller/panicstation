# What to do if… a body donation is declined and you need an alternative funeral plan quickly (UK)

## Short answer
Create time first: confirm where the person is being kept and whether they are in refrigerated care. Then arrange transfer to a funeral director or another suitable mortuary arrangement so you can choose cremation or burial without being rushed.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume registration, a form, or stated wishes mean the body must be accepted; acceptance is usually decided after death and can be declined.
- Don’t agree to embalming just to “buy time” unless you are sure no further donation route is being considered.
- Don’t wait for repeated call-backs while nobody is responsible for safe storage or transfer.
- Don’t sign a funeral estimate you do not understand; ask for a written, itemised price before agreeing.
- Don’t let several people give different instructions; where possible, choose one person to make calls and keep notes.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm where the person is and how they are being cared for.**  
   Phone the hospital bereavement office, ward, mortuary, care home, funeral director, or donation office involved. Ask: “Where is the person now, are they refrigerated, and who needs to arrange collection?”

2. **Ask the donation programme for the minimum facts.**  
   Ask them to confirm that acceptance has been declined, whether they arranged any transport, and whether you need to arrange collection now. If they can send a brief email without delaying transfer, ask for that too.

3. **Choose a funeral director or local mortuary route to take over care.**  
   Say: “A body donation has been declined and we need urgent transfer into your care.” Ask for:
   - earliest collection time
   - where the person will be kept
   - any refrigeration or daily care fees
   - a written price for direct cremation
   - a written price for a simple burial or cremation
   - what paperwork, crematorium or cemetery booking, and release requirements they will help with

4. **If money is the immediate barrier, say that clearly before agreeing to anything.**  
   Ask the funeral director for the lowest-cost lawful option they can provide. Also contact the local council or local authority where the person died and ask what happens if nobody is able to arrange or pay for a funeral, including whether a public health funeral or other local route may apply.

5. **Make only the decision needed to keep things moving today.**  
   You do not need a full service plan now. The urgent decision is usually who will take the person into care and whether you are leaning towards cremation or burial.

6. **Only check another medical school if it will not delay safe care.**  
   If you already have the person’s body-donation paperwork, contact the relevant anatomy or bequest office for the area and ask whether acceptance can still be considered now. If they cannot confirm quickly, or the paperwork is not in place, continue with the funeral plan rather than chasing uncertain alternatives.

7. **Write down each call.**  
   Keep a simple note of the time, name, organisation, phone number, and what they said about collection, storage, costs, and paperwork. This reduces repeated explanations and helps if family members are distressed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose music, readings, flowers, a wake, an obituary, or a full guest list now.
- You do not need to explain the refusal to everyone today.
- You do not need to settle every family disagreement before arranging safe care of the body.
- You do not need to resolve all estate, benefit, or payment questions before asking about the lowest-cost immediate option.

## Important reassurance
A declined body donation is not a failure by you or a judgement on the person who died. It is a known possibility because acceptance can depend on timing, paperwork, medical circumstances, transport, location, and capacity.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, intended to stabilise the situation and prevent rushed decisions. Later decisions about costs, disputed instructions, estate money, repatriation, or complex family disagreement may need help from a funeral director, bereavement office, local authority, solicitor, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Processes vary across the UK and by the circumstances of the death, so ask the hospital bereavement office, donation office, local authority, or funeral director to confirm what applies and to put key points in writing.
