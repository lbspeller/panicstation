# What to do if… you are placed on a performance improvement plan unexpectedly (UK)

## Short answer
Obtain the request and recording method in writing, then maintain a private, contemporaneous log of all additional hours worked. Do not continue working extra hours until you have clarified whether they are paid, time off in lieu (TOIL), or contractually unpaid.

## Do not do these things
- Do not clock out and continue working or "volunteer" time if you intend for those hours to be recognized.
- Do not sign new opt-out agreements or contract amendments while under immediate pressure.
- Do not rely on verbal assurances or vague promises that the hours will be "sorted later."
- Do not intentionally omit hours from your timesheet to avoid conflict or "help out."
- Do not post details of the dispute on social media or in internal company group chats.
- Do not resign abruptly without assessing the impact on your notice period and future claims.

## What to do now
1. **Request a written record of the specific overtime instruction.**
   **Send a factual email or message to the manager who made the request.**
   - State the date and specific hours you have been asked to work.
   - Ask for confirmation of how these hours should be recorded.
   - Ask whether the hours are compensated via pay, TOIL, or are considered part of your basic salary.

2. **Establish a private, off-system overtime log immediately.**
   **Record every instance of additional work in a document you control outside of work devices.**
   - Include the date, exact start and finish times, and any breaks taken.
   - Note who requested the work and the method used (e.g., verbal, email, rota change).
   - Save copies or screenshots of relevant rotas, timesheets, and pay slips.

3. **Review your written statement of employment particulars or contract.**
   **Locate the specific clauses regarding working hours and overtime.**
   - Check if the contract includes a "reasonable overtime" requirement.
   - Look for the definitions of "normal working hours" and "additional hours."
   - Identify whether the contract explicitly states that overtime is unpaid or compensated only by TOIL.

4. **Perform a National Minimum Wage (NMW) calculation.**
   **Verify that your average hourly pay for the relevant period does not fall below the legal minimum.**
   - As of April 1, 2026, the National Living Wage for those aged 21 and over is £12.71 per hour.
   - Divide your total gross pay for the period by the actual hours worked (including the unpaid overtime).
   - If the resulting figure is below the statutory minimum for your age, this is a legal breach regardless of your contract terms.

5. **Utilize the formal grievance process if informal resolution fails.**
   **Follow the employer's written grievance procedure exactly.**
   - Submit your complaint in writing, detailing the dates and hours in question.
   - Mention any potential breaches of the National Minimum Wage or Working Time Regulations if applicable.
   - Retain a copy of the grievance and any response received.

6. **Monitor the 48-hour weekly limit and opt-out status.**
   **Assess whether your total working hours exceed an average of 48 hours per week.**
   - This average is typically calculated over a rolling 17-week period.
   - If you have not signed a written opt-out, you cannot usually be compelled to work beyond this limit.
   - If you wish to cancel an existing opt-out, check your contract for the notice period (typically 7 days, maximum 3 months).

7. **Contact the Fair Work Agency (FWA) for enforcement support.**
   **Report suspected breaches of the National Minimum Wage to the enforcement body.**
   - From April 7, 2026, the Fair Work Agency (FWA) handles NMW and holiday pay enforcement.
   - You can make a complaint anonymously or on behalf of a group of workers.
   - The FWA has powers to inspect records and issue penalties to employers.

8. **Observe statutory time limits for legal claims.**
   **Note that most tribunal claims must be initiated within 3 months minus 1 day of the breach.**
   - Be aware that most time limits are scheduled to extend to 6 months starting in October 2026 under the Employment Rights Act 2025.
   - You must notify Acas for early conciliation before you can lodge a tribunal claim.
   - Early conciliation can now last up to 12 weeks as of the 2026 standard.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on resigning or pursuing a tribunal today.
- You do not need a completed forensic audit of all past hours before starting your log now.
- You do not need to respond to verbal pressure immediately; you can state you need to check your schedule or contract first.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant pressure when asked to work for free, particularly in high-demand environments. Asking for written clarity and keeping your own records are standard professional actions and do not indicate a lack of commitment to your role.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate employment dispute. Complex contractual interpretations or the filing of formal legal claims require detailed review by an employment specialist or trade union representative.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment status (worker vs. employee), specific industry regulations, and individual contract terms significantly affect your rights. If you are threatened with dismissal or face harassment for asserting your rights, seek immediate confidential advice from Acas, a trade union, or a solicitor.
