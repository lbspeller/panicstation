# What to do if… you notice new device admin or monitoring settings on your phone and fear your data is exposed (UK)

## Short answer
Do not confront the person you suspect or make sudden changes on the phone while you are scared. Use a safer device to contact specialist support and protect the accounts that could expose intimate data.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the person you suspect through the phone you are worried about.
- Do not keep using that phone for private searches, new passwords, legal advice, safety planning, or messages about leaving.
- Do not assume private browsing, deleting history, or closing apps makes your activity invisible.
- Do not rush to uninstall apps, factory-reset the phone, or switch everything off if doing that could alert someone or put you at more risk.
- Do not hand over more access, passcodes, fingerprints, or face unlock to prove anything.
- Do not blame yourself for not spotting this sooner.

## What to do now
1. **Move your next important step to a safer device if you can.** Use a trusted friend’s phone, a work device, a library computer, or another device the person cannot access. Use that safer device for support, account recovery, intimate image concerns, and safety planning.

2. **Get specialist support before making big phone changes.** Contact the National Domestic Abuse Helpline if this may involve a partner, ex-partner, coercive control, or fear at home. They can help you think through tech abuse and safer next steps without pressuring you to report. If there is immediate danger, call 999.

3. **Make a brief record somewhere safer.** Write down the date, time, what setting or warning appeared, the name of any unknown app or administrator, and what data you fear may be exposed. Keep it factual and short. If taking a screenshot on that phone feels risky, do not force it.

4. **Treat the phone as possibly visible for now.** Avoid using it for private calls, intimate disclosures, new secret email addresses, account recovery, or conversations about reporting, leaving, or changing passwords.

5. **Protect the accounts that unlock the rest from a safer device.** Start with the email account used for password resets, then cloud photos, messaging apps, your mobile account, and any account that holds intimate images or backups. Change passwords, review signed-in devices, and sign out of devices you do not recognise where it is safe to do so.

6. **Check sharing only when you have a safer pause.** From a safer device or place where possible, review location sharing, cloud photo sharing, shared albums, linked tablets, linked watches, family-sharing features, and backup settings. Turn off sharing only if doing so is unlikely to alert someone in a way that increases risk.

7. **If you use an iPhone, consider Apple Safety Check if the phone supports it.** Safety Check is available on iPhone with iOS 16 or later and can help review sharing and account access. Use it only when you have a safer pause. If touching the phone feels unsafe, leave this and contact support first.

8. **If you use Android, avoid deep investigation while frightened.** If it feels safe, you can note unfamiliar high-access apps, permission changes, or administrator-style settings. Android wording varies by phone and version, so you do not need to solve the menu path right now.

9. **Think beyond the handset.** If someone had physical access to the phone or knew your passcode, they may also have access to email, cloud backups, photos, location sharing, or linked devices. Put your first energy into the accounts that expose the most private information.

10. **If intimate images may be involved, contact specialist support without pressure.** You do not need to decide now whether to report, confront anyone, or preserve every possible detail. A domestic abuse service or the Revenge Porn Helpline can help you choose a safer next step.

11. **If you may want to report later, avoid changing more than necessary until you have spoken to specialist support.** A short note of what you saw and when is enough for this first pause.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to report to police, replace the phone, leave a relationship, confront anyone, or carry out a full technical investigation. You also do not need to check every account in one sitting.

## Important reassurance
Phone monitoring, stalkerware, and forced access to intimate data can be part of abuse and control. Feeling confused, embarrassed, frozen, or unsure whether you are overreacting is common. You are allowed to slow down and choose the safest next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about reporting, device checks, account recovery, housing, legal protection, or intimate image removal may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, technical, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. In the UK, unwanted phone monitoring by a partner or ex-partner may be part of domestic abuse, coercive control, or other harmful behaviour. The safest immediate step is usually support and safety planning rather than trying to investigate the phone alone.
