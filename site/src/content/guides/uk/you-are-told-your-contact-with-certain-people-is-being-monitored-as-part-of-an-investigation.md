# What to do if… you are told your contact with certain people is being monitored as part of an investigation (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all discussion of the investigation with everyone except a qualified solicitor and assume all current communications are being recorded. Obtain professional legal advice immediately to clarify your status and any active bail or remand conditions.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attempt to "test" the monitoring by sending coded messages or using different apps.
- Don’t contact the people mentioned to "warn" them or ask what they have told investigators.
- Don’t delete any existing messages, call logs, or data, as this can be interpreted as perverting the course of justice.
- Don’t ask third parties to pass messages or check on people for you.
- Don’t post about the situation or the investigation on social media.
- Don’t assume that "disappearing" messages or encrypted apps provide total protection from professional surveillance.

## What to do now
1. **Pause all non-essential communication with the people identified immediately.** **Assume that every text, call, and social media interaction is being logged and may be used as evidence later in the investigation.** 

2. **Secure a solicitor before engaging with any official questioning.** **If you are currently at a police station or are approached for a voluntary interview, state clearly that you wish to receive legal advice and will not answer questions until your solicitor is present.** 
   - In England and Wales, this is protected under PACE Code C.
   - In Scotland, the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Act 2016 grants you the right to have a solicitor present during an interview.

3. **Check your official paperwork for bail or remand conditions.** **Locate any documents provided by the police or the court to see if there are specific "non-contact" clauses or "electronic monitoring" requirements established under the Sentencing Act 2026.**
   - Look for names of specific individuals you must not contact directly or indirectly.
   - Check for restrictions on using specific devices or social media platforms.

4. **Verify the status of your "privileged" communications if you are in prison.** **If you are on remand or serving a sentence, ensure your solicitor’s phone number is officially registered and "cleared" on the prison system to ensure calls are treated as legally privileged and not recorded.**
   - Do not discuss case details on "social" calls to family or friends.
   - Ask for a "legal visit" or a "Rule 39" letter for confidential correspondence.

5. **Document how and when you were informed of the monitoring.** **Write down a factual timeline of the notification, including the name and role of the person who told you and their exact phrasing, then keep this note in a secure place to show your solicitor.**

6. **Prepare a "neutral" response for those who contact you.** **If a person you are avoiding contacts you, use a standard script such as: "I cannot discuss this right now; I am following legal advice" and then end the interaction.**

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to prove the monitoring is unlawful or "unfair" right now.
- You do **not** need to decide if you are going to plead guilty or not guilty today.
- You do **not** need to explain yourself to friends, family, or employers.
- You do **not** need to find out "who told on you" or who the informant is.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel watched, anxious, or overwhelmed when told you are under surveillance. These feelings are a standard response to high-stress legal situations. By pausing your communications and speaking to a solicitor, you are taking the most effective step toward protecting your rights and stabilising your situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for individuals informed of communication monitoring. It does not cover technical counter-surveillance or long-term legal defense strategies, which must be handled by a certified legal professional.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding surveillance and police powers vary across the UK (England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland). Always consult a solicitor qualified in your specific jurisdiction immediately if you are under investigation.
