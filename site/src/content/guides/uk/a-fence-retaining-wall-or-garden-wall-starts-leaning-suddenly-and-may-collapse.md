# What to do if… a fence, retaining wall, or garden wall starts leaning suddenly and may collapse (UK)

## Short answer
Evacuate the immediate area and create a physical exclusion zone to prevent anyone from entering the potential fall path. If the structure poses an immediate danger to a public road or pavement, call 999; otherwise, contact the local council Building Control department.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to push the wall back, prop it up with temporary supports, or remove soil from behind a retaining wall.
- Do not enter the fall zone to inspect cracks, take measurements, or retrieve items.
- Do not allow children or pets into the garden or near the structure until it is professionally secured.
- Do not remove fence panels or drill into the structure, as this can trigger a sudden total collapse.
- Do not assume a small garden wall is harmless; masonry is heavy and can cause fatal injuries or significant property damage.

## What to do now
1. **Evacuate the danger zone immediately.** Move all people and animals to a safe distance, ideally indoors or to a part of the property separated by a permanent structure.
2. **Establish a physical barrier from a safe distance.** 
   - Use bins, chairs, or garden furniture to block access paths.
   - Close and lock any gates leading to the affected area.
   - Do not cross the potential fall line to set up these barriers.
3. **Identify potential impact points without approaching.** Observe the structure from a distance to determine if a collapse would strike:
   - Public footpaths, pavements, or highways.
   - Neighbouring properties or outbuildings.
   - Utility meters, gas pipes, or overhead electricity lines.
4. **Contact emergency services if there is an immediate risk to life.** Call 999 if the wall is actively moving, overhanging a public area, or if you hear loud cracking or popping sounds.
5. **Report the structure to Local Authority Building Control.** 
   - For structures impacting public areas in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use the local council "Dangerous Structures" reporting line.
   - In Scotland, contact the local authority Building Standards department.
   - Note: If the wall is strictly private (between two gardens) and does not affect the public, the council may have limited powers; you will need a private structural engineer.
6. **Address utility hazards if present.**
   - If you smell gas or see a damaged gas pipe: Move away, do not use mobile phones near the leak, and call the **National Gas Emergency Service (0800 111 999)**.
   - If electricity cables or cabinets are affected: Stay 10 metres away and call **105** (England, Scotland, Wales) or **03457 643643** (Northern Ireland).
7. **Document the situation from a safe position.** Take photographs or video that clearly shows the degree of lean and any surrounding hazards to assist with insurance or engineering assessments.
8. **Warn occupants of neighbouring properties.** Advise them to stay clear of the boundary and keep pets and children indoors until an assessment is completed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine who owns the wall or who is liable for the cost of repairs right now.
- You do not need to contact builders or fence contractors for quotes until the structure is stabilized or declared safe.
- You do not need to clear debris or fallen soil if a partial collapse has already occurred.
- You can wait to resolve boundary disputes; safety takes precedence over legal ownership in the first 24 hours.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant stress when a major part of your property becomes unstable. Taking the immediate step of moving away and preventing access is the most effective way to prevent injury. Most sudden leans do not lead to instant collapse if they are left untouched and the area is cleared.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. Once the area is secured, you will likely require a structural engineer to determine the cause and a specialist contractor to perform remediation.

## Important note
This guide is for general safety information and does not constitute professional engineering or legal advice. Always prioritize the instructions of emergency services or local building control officers.
