# What to do if… a fence, retaining wall, or garden wall starts leaning suddenly and may collapse (UK)

## Short answer
Keep people and pets well away from the wall and anything it could fall onto. If anyone is in immediate danger, call 999; otherwise report the dangerous structure through the correct local council or local authority route.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t try to push it back, prop it up, strap it, or remove soil behind it.
- Don’t stand in the likely fall path, even briefly to inspect it or take a photo.
- Don’t let children, visitors, delivery drivers, or pets near it.
- Don’t dig, drill, cut fence panels, remove posts, or disturb the ground beside it.
- Don’t assume it is safe because it has not fallen yet.
- Don’t argue about boundaries or blame while the area may still be unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Move away and make a no-go zone.** Get people and pets indoors or to another safe area. If you can do it without going near the wall, close gates, lock doors, or block access with bins, chairs, tape, or a clear note.
2. **Look from a safe distance at what it could hit.** Check whether it could fall onto a pavement, road, footpath, neighbour’s garden, driveway, parked car, door, meter box, cable, cabinet, shed, greenhouse, or the only route in or out.
3. **Call 999 if there is immediate danger.** This includes a wall moving now, a wall overhanging a public path or road, debris already falling, someone injured or trapped, a blocked road, or a risk that someone could walk or drive into the danger area before it can be secured.
4. **Report it to the local authority if it is not a 999 emergency.**
   - In England and Wales, use the GOV.UK dangerous building or structure reporting route to find the relevant local council.
   - In Scotland, contact the local authority building standards or dangerous buildings route.
   - In Northern Ireland, contact the local council building control or dangerous structures route.
5. **Keep utility hazards separate.**
   - If you smell gas, suspect a gas leak, or think a gas pipe may have been hit, move away and call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999.
   - If there is a fallen or low cable, sparking, a damaged electricity cabinet, or damage near electrical equipment, keep well clear. Call 105 in England, Scotland and Wales, or 03457 643643 in Northern Ireland. Call 999 as well if there is immediate danger.
6. **Warn neighbours from a safe place.** Ask them to keep away from their side too, especially if the wall is on or near a boundary.
7. **Take evidence only from safety.** If you can do it without approaching, take a wide photo or short video showing the lean, new cracks, nearby paths, public areas, vehicles, and utilities. Do not step closer for a better image.
8. **Treat retaining walls as higher risk.** If the wall is holding back soil, steps, a raised garden, a drive, or stored materials, keep clear of both the lower side and the higher ground behind it because soil or material may move as well.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who owns the wall or who is at fault.
- You do not need to get repair quotes before the danger area is made safe.
- You do not need to move heavy items, clear rubble, or rescue belongings unless emergency services or the local authority tell you it is safe.
- You can contact your insurer after the immediate safety risk has been reported and people are away from danger.
- You can deal with boundary, neighbour, contractor, or surveyor questions later.

## Important reassurance
Stepping back is the right first move. A sudden lean can feel urgent, but the safest useful actions are simple: keep people away, stop access to the fall area, report the danger, and avoid quick fixes.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repair, ownership, insurance, structural design, drainage, or boundary responsibility may need help from a qualified structural professional, insurer, surveyor, local authority, or legal adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional engineering, legal, emergency, financial, insurance, or safety advice. If there is any immediate risk to life or serious injury, treat it as an emergency and call 999.
