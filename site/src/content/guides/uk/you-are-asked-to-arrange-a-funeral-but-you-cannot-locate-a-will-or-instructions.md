# What to do if… you are asked to arrange a funeral but you cannot locate a will or instructions (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and determine who has the legal authority to give instructions (the personal representative) while conducting a focused 90-minute search for a will or funeral plan. Do not sign a final funeral contract or pay a significant deposit until authority is confirmed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a comprehensive funeral contract or pay for non-essential upgrades until you confirm you have the authority to act.
- Do not assume that being "next of kin" automatically grants the final legal say if an executor is named in a hidden will or if a dispute exists.
- Do not dispose of any papers, even if they appear to be junk mail, until the search is complete.
- Do not remove money from the deceased’s bank accounts or sell their belongings to pay for the funeral before probate or letters of administration are granted.
- Do not feel pressured by a funeral director to make irreversible choices (such as cremation versus burial) during the first 24 hours.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the current location and status of the body.** If the death occurred in a hospital or care home, ask the bereavement office if the body has been referred to the coroner or if a Medical Certificate of Cause of Death (MCCD) has been issued.
2. **Determine the legal "Personal Representative" hierarchy.** If a will is found, the named executor has the absolute legal right to arrange the funeral; if no will exists, the right follows the "order of priority" under intestacy rules:
   - Lawful spouse or civil partner.
   - Adult children (over 18).
   - Parents.
   - Siblings of the whole blood.
3. **Perform a high-intensity 90-minute search for documents.**
   - Check "the usual places": bedside drawers, home safes, filing cabinets, and any folders marked "Solicitor," "Insurance," or "Life Plan."
   - Search digital records for keywords such as "Will," "Funeral," "Instructions," or names of local law firms.
   - Look for a "Will Registration Certificate" which may point to the National Will Register.
4. **Contact the deceased's bank and local solicitor.** **Banks often keep a record of whether they hold a will in their vaults, even if the person did not have a safe deposit box.** Call any solicitor the deceased used for property or previous family matters to ask if they hold a will or a "letter of wishes."
5. **Conduct a search on the National Will Register.** **If local searches fail, use the National Will Register (Certainty) to check for registered wills or to send a "Missing Will Notice" to solicitors in the areas where the person lived.** This search is often a disbursement that can be reclaimed from the estate.
6. **Check for a prepaid funeral plan or funeral insurance.** **Examine bank statements for regular payments to providers like Co-op, SunLife, or specialist funeral plan companies.** You can contact the Funeral Planning Authority (FPA) or the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) register to verify a provider's status.
7. **Make "reversible-only" arrangements with a funeral director.** **Tell the funeral director clearly: "We have not yet located the will and are confirming authority."**
   - You can ask them to collect the body (if the hospital/coroner has released it) and provide a quote for a "basic" or "direct" service without committing to a full ceremony.
8. **Verify eligibility for government financial support.** **If funds are unavailable and you are on qualifying benefits (e.g., Universal Credit, Pension Credit), check the 2026 DWP Funeral Expenses Payment criteria.** In 2026, this typically covers essential burial/cremation fees plus a capped amount of £1,000 for other expenses.
9. **Notify the local authority if no one is "willing or able" to pay.** **If there are genuinely no funds and no executor, the local council has a statutory duty under Section 46 of the Public Health Act 1984 to arrange a public health funeral.** This is a basic service and the council may recover costs from any remaining assets in the estate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose hymns, readings, or floral tributes in the first 48 hours.
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family disputes regarding the "ideal" send-off; focus only on identifying the person with legal priority.
- You do not need to apply for probate or letters of administration today.
- You do not need to clear the deceased's property or cancel all utilities immediately.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for the location of a will to be unknown immediately following a death. Taking 24 to 48 hours to locate the correct paperwork prevents legal complications later and ensures the deceased’s actual wishes—which may be very different from family assumptions—are respected.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying authority and locating instructions. It does not cover the long-term probate process, inheritance tax, or the legal resolution of estate disputes.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. In the UK, funeral wishes expressed in a will are generally not legally binding on the executor, although they are almost always followed. If multiple family members claim the right to arrange the funeral and no will is found, seek legal advice before signing any contracts.
