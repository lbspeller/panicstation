# What to do if… your bank says a transaction is pending or on hold and you need the money now (UK)

## Short answer
Ask your bank to identify the exact transaction type, whether there is a hold or review, and the earliest time the money will be available to spend. Then bridge only the essential gap: ask the sender or bank about a faster route if the money has not already been sent, and ask your bank and urgent payees for short-term help if it has.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume a pending or incoming amount will cover payments due today; it may not be spendable yet and can sometimes be reversed.
- Don’t repeatedly cancel and re-send the same transfer without bank guidance; it can cause confusion or trigger checks.
- Don’t take a high-cost loan in a panic before asking your bank about short-term options, fee relief, or hardship support.
- Don’t share one-time passcodes, card reader codes, or approve-in-app prompts with anyone who contacted you first.
- Don’t pay a fee to “release” the money unless you have verified it directly through your bank’s official app, website, or phone number.
- Don’t rely on screenshots from a sender as proof that money is spendable in your account.

## What to do now
1. **Find the transaction type first.**  
   In your banking app or message, look for words such as Faster Payments, Bacs, CHAPS, card refund, cheque deposit, international transfer, SWIFT, pending, hold, review, available balance, or cleared balance. Write down the amount, date, time, reference, sender, and any status wording.

2. **Ask your bank these exact questions.**  
   Say: “Is this a normal processing timeline for this payment type, or is there a hold or review on the transaction or account?”  
   Then ask: “What is the earliest time it will be available to spend or withdraw, not just received?”

3. **Use the payment type to choose the next move.**  
   - **Faster Payments:** these are usually quick and funds are often available almost immediately, but they can take longer, including because of bank checks or timing. Ask whether it is delayed, rejected, under review, or waiting for the receiving account to accept it.  
   - **Bacs:** this is usually a three-English-bank-working-day cycle and is commonly used for salaries and bulk payments. If the payment has not already been sent, ask the sender whether they can make an emergency payment by Faster Payments or another faster method.  
   - **CHAPS:** this is for same-day sterling payments on working days and is often used for urgent, high-value or time-critical payments. Ask the sender’s bank for its customer cut-off time, because a bank may set an earlier deadline than the CHAPS system.  
   - **Cheque deposit:** ask whether it is being processed through cheque imaging, when it becomes withdrawable, and whether any exception or hold applies.  
   - **Card refund:** ask the merchant for the refund reference and processing date, then ask your bank when it is expected to post.

4. **If the money has not been sent yet, ask the sender for the fastest safe route.**  
   Ask payroll, finance, the buyer, or the person sending money: “Can this be sent by Faster Payments today, or by CHAPS if it is genuinely urgent and you can meet your bank’s cut-off?” Ask them to confirm the method, reference, and time sent.

5. **If the money has already been sent, do not chase the wrong person repeatedly.**  
   Ask the sender for the payment method and reference, then take that to your own bank. If your bank says it cannot see the incoming payment, ask the sender to contact their bank to check whether it has left, failed, or been held.

6. **If your bank says there is a hold or review, ask what would unblock it.**  
   Say: “I need to cover essentials. What exact information do you need from me, and how do I provide it through an official channel?” Provide documents or answers only through your bank’s app, secure message, branch, or a phone number you found yourself.

7. **Protect the next 24–48 hours of essentials.**  
   List only payments that would cause immediate harm if missed, such as rent, mortgage, utilities, food, travel to work, childcare, or essential debt payments. Contact those payees now and ask for a short extension, due-date move, partial payment arrangement, or late-fee waiver.

8. **Ask your bank for a temporary bridge.**  
   Say: “This incoming payment delay may cause essential payments to fail. Can you consider a temporary overdraft, a temporary limit increase, pausing charges, refunding charges caused by this delay, or hardship support?” Ask them to confirm any agreement in writing or secure message.

9. **Avoid scam routes while you are under pressure.**  
   If anyone says they can speed up the payment for a fee, asks for remote access to your device, asks you to move money to a “safe account”, or asks you to approve something in your app, stop and contact your bank using a number from your card, statement, or official website.

10. **If the delay causes harm or the bank’s answer does not make sense, make a formal complaint.**  
   Ask for a complaint reference and write down the date. For payment services complaints, firms normally have 15 business days to respond; if they cannot give a final response in that time, they must explain the delay and usually provide a final response within 35 business days. Most other financial complaints may have an 8-week response time. If you are unhappy with the final response, or the firm misses the relevant time limit, you can usually contact the Financial Ombudsman Service.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide today whether to switch banks, close the account, or change every payment method.
- You don’t need to prove who is at fault before you ask for an exact availability time and short-term help.
- You don’t need a full budget today; focus only on essentials due before the money becomes spendable.
- You don’t need to argue about compensation today unless the immediate payment risk is already contained.

## Important reassurance
A payment can be visible, expected, or confirmed by the sender without being available to spend yet. That does not mean you have failed or that every bill will automatically collapse. The useful next step is to pin down the transaction type, the blocker, and the earliest spendable time, then ask for a short bridge.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the next 24–72 hours. Later decisions, including complaints, compensation, changing banks, or longer-term money planning, may need specialist help once the immediate pressure has passed.

## Important note
This guide gives general information and first actions only. It is not financial, legal, debt, or professional advice, and bank processes can vary by provider, account type, payment method, and fraud-prevention checks.
