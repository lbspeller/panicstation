# What to do if… a neighbour or acquaintance is banging on your door or wall and the behaviour is escalating (UK)

## Short answer
Get behind a locked barrier, move away from the door or wall, and call 999 if there is immediate danger, attempted entry, threats happening now, or damage happening now. If it is not an emergency, use 101 or online police reporting and start a simple incident log.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door to calm them down or step into the hallway, landing, or street to negotiate.
- Do not shout back, bang back, insult them, or trade threats.
- Do not follow them if they move away from your door.
- Do not hint at weapons or try to scare them off.
- Do not move toward the door to film, check, or prove who it is.
- Do not post about it publicly while it is happening.

## What to do now
1. Create a safer pause inside your home. Move away from the door or wall they are striking. If you can, go to an inner room, lock the door, and keep a solid barrier between you and them. Take your phone, keys, and shoes with you.

2. Decide whether this is an emergency. Call 999 if you or someone else is in immediate danger, they are trying to force entry, threatening violence, causing damage, or the incident is in progress and you feel unsafe. If it is not an emergency, report it through 101 or your local police force’s online reporting route.

3. If you cannot safely speak, still call 999. Try to speak or whisper if you can. If you cannot, listen to the operator and respond by coughing or tapping if possible. If you are on a mobile and are prompted, press 55 or tap when asked so the call can be passed to police.

4. Make it easy for responders to find you. If you can speak, give your exact address, flat number, floor, entry code or buzzer details, and the safest way to reach you. If you are in a block, say whether the person is in a communal corridor or landing and whether there is visible damage.

5. Keep any necessary words short and non-escalating. If you must speak, do it through the closed door: “I can’t open the door. Please leave.” Then stop engaging.

6. Keep a simple record from a safe place. Note the time, what you heard, any threats, any damage, and whether anyone else witnessed it. If you can record audio or video safely from inside your home without moving toward the door, keep it for your own record and do not share it publicly.

7. Bring in another person without creating a confrontation. Message or call someone you trust and ask them to stay on the phone. If you have a trusted neighbour nearby, you can ask them to call 999 from their own home if you are frightened to call yourself.

8. If you think the person may need urgent mental health help and there is no immediate danger, call 111 or use NHS 111 online for advice on the right local help. If you cannot keep yourself or someone else safe, or anyone’s life is at risk, call 999.

9. After the immediate danger has passed, report the right parts to the right place. If you felt threatened, there was damage, or the behaviour may be criminal, report it to police. If it is ongoing antisocial behaviour affecting the area, report it to your council. If you rent, especially from a council or housing association, report it to your landlord or housing officer.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to confront them, move home, start a formal complaint, or take legal action.
- You do not need perfect evidence before contacting police, the council, or your landlord.
- You can write a fuller timeline, take photos of damage, and gather messages or witness details once you are safe and the incident has stopped.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaky, frozen, or unsure whether you are overreacting is a common stress response. If behaviour at your door or wall is escalating and you feel unsafe, it is reasonable to prioritise distance, barriers, and help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stabilise the situation and avoid irreversible mistakes. Repeated incidents may need support from police, your council, your landlord, a housing adviser, or legal advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, housing, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger or the incident is in progress and you feel unsafe, call 999.
