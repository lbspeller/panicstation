# What to do if… your replacement bank card has not arrived and your current card is deactivated (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank now using a trusted route, say the replacement card has not arrived and your current card is deactivated, and ask them to cancel the missing card and give you a safe way to access money today.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t share your PIN, passcodes, passwords, or one-time security codes with anyone, even if they say they are from your bank or the police.
- Don’t hand over your card, cash, or bank details to a courier, collector, taxi driver, or visitor.
- Don’t call a number from a text, email, pop-up, delivery message, or voicemail about your card.
- Don’t approve an in-app prompt, new device login, digital wallet link, or payment because someone on the phone tells you to.
- Don’t move money to a “safe account” suggested by a caller or message.
- Don’t assume it is only a postal delay once the old card has stopped working; treat the missing replacement as a possible security issue until your bank confirms otherwise.

## What to do now
1. **Contact your bank using a trusted route.**
   - Use your banking app, secure chat, a number from a recent statement, or the bank’s official website.
   - If you have had a suspicious call about the card, hang up first. You can dial 159 where your bank and phone provider are covered.

2. **Say this clearly: “My replacement card has not arrived and my current card is deactivated.”**
   - Ask the bank to cancel the missing replacement card so it cannot be activated or used.
   - Ask them to reissue a new card.
   - Ask them to confirm the delivery address they used.
   - Ask whether any address, phone number, email, device, app login, or digital wallet change has been made recently.

3. **Ask how you can access money today.**
   - Ask whether they can offer cardless cash, get cash, emergency cash, or branch withdrawal with ID.
   - Ask whether a temporary digital card, in-app card details, or digital wallet access is available for your account.
   - If you need essentials today, ask the bank what safe options they can enable immediately.

4. **Check for signs the missing card or account access may have been interfered with.**
   - Look for transactions you do not recognise, including pending transactions.
   - Check for new payees, new devices, new app logins, or contact-detail changes.
   - Tell the bank immediately if anything looks wrong and ask what they have blocked while they investigate.

5. **Move urgent payments away from the missing card for the next few days, until the replacement arrives and works.**
   - Use bank transfer for urgent bills or invoices where you know the payee is genuine.
   - Update essential subscriptions only through the official service website or app, not from a message link.
   - If someone trusted is helping you buy essentials, transfer only the amount needed and only after checking the details yourself.

6. **If you think this may be fraud, report it after you have contacted the bank.**
   - Use Report Fraud where it is the right route for your area, or use your local police force’s reporting route.
   - For non-emergency police advice, call 101.
   - If someone is at your door, threatening you, or you feel in immediate danger, call 999.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide today whether to switch banks, close the account, or make a formal complaint.
- You don’t need to prove whether it was a postal delay, wrong address, misdelivery, or interception before you act.
- You don’t need to chase the postal service before contacting your bank.
- You don’t need to update every saved card payment immediately; deal with essentials first.

## Important reassurance
This is stressful because it blocks ordinary things like food, travel, bills, and cash. The safest first move is not to solve the whole problem at once; it is to reach your bank through a trusted route, block the missing card, and get a temporary way to pay.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to restore access and reduce fraud risk. Later decisions, such as a complaint, reimbursement request, account security review, or switching banks, may need help from your bank, an ombudsman service, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not financial, legal, security, or professional advice. Options such as cardless cash, temporary digital cards, branch withdrawals, delivery checks, and fraud blocks vary by bank, card type, and account status.
