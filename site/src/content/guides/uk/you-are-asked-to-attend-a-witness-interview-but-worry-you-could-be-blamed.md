# What to do if… you are asked to attend a witness interview but worry you could be blamed (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before answering detailed questions or giving a statement. Ask whether you are being treated as a witness or a suspect, and get advice from a criminal defence solicitor if there is any chance you could be blamed.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the word “witness” means there is no risk to you. If police begin to suspect you, they may need to caution you and use suspect-interview safeguards.
- Don’t give an on-the-spot written statement, timeline, phone access, or document bundle because you feel embarrassed to say you need advice first.
- Don’t guess details to be helpful. If you are not sure, say you are not sure.
- Don’t chat informally about the incident in corridors, cars, reception areas, phone calls, or messages. What you say may be noted and used later.
- Don’t ignore vulnerability, panic, language needs, disability, or mental health needs. Say clearly if you need help understanding the process, an interpreter, an appropriate adult, or time to speak to a solicitor.

## What to do now
1. **Clarify what the interview is before you go.** Ask, preferably in writing:
   - “Am I being asked to attend as a witness or as a suspect?”
   - “Will I be interviewed under caution?”
   - “Am I free to leave at any time?”
   - “What incident or alleged offence is this about?”
   - “Are you asking me for a witness statement, a suspect interview, documents, messages, or access to a device?”
2. **Ask to delay detailed questioning until you have legal advice.** You can say: “I am willing to cooperate, but I want legal advice before I attend, answer detailed questions, or give a statement.”
3. **Speak to a criminal defence solicitor.** Tell them you have been invited as a witness but are worried you could be blamed. Ask whether you should attend, what to do if the interview changes into a suspect interview, and how legal advice can be arranged for the interview.
4. **If it is a voluntary police interview or you are put under caution, ask for free independent legal advice before questions continue.** In England and Wales, the official voluntary-interview notice says you are not under arrest, do not have to consent to being interviewed, and can ask for a free solicitor. Police interview procedure and terminology can differ across the UK, so ask a local criminal defence solicitor to confirm the position where you are.
5. **Set a clear boundary at the start.** Say: “I understand I am here as a witness. If you think I may be suspected of an offence, I want the interview paused so I can get legal advice before answering further questions.”
6. **Take only simple factual notes for yourself.** Bring photo ID, any reference number, and a short note of dates, places, names, and points you are sure about. Mark anything uncertain as uncertain.
7. **Move informal contact into a safer setup.** If someone asks for a quick phone chat, doorstep chat, message exchange, or immediate statement, ask to schedule it properly after legal advice.
8. **After any contact, make a private factual note.** Record who contacted you, the date and time, where it happened, whether you were cautioned, what was requested, and any deadline given.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today how much detail to give beyond confirming basic arrangements.
- You do not need to hand over every message, document, or device immediately unless a solicitor has advised you or there is a clear legal requirement you understand.
- You do not need to prove everything in the room. Your first priority is to avoid guessing, panicking, or accidentally making your position worse.

## Important reassurance
Asking for legal advice is normal and does not mean you have done anything wrong. It can help you give accurate information without panic, guessing, or being misunderstood.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the moment you are invited to a witness interview and are worried you could be blamed. Later decisions, including whether to attend, what to say, and what to provide, may need help from a criminal defence solicitor.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Police procedures and legal safeguards vary across the UK and depend on whether you are a witness, a suspect, cautioned, detained, or attending voluntarily. If you may be blamed, get advice from a qualified criminal defence solicitor before speaking in detail.
