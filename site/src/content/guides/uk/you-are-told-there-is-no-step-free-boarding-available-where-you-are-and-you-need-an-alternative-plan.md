# What to do if… you are told there is no step-free boarding available where you are and you need an alternative plan (UK)

## Short answer
Do not attempt an unsafe boarding. Immediately request "Turn Up and Go" assistance to arrange a step-free alternative, such as an accessible taxi or a rerouted journey, which the operator must provide at no extra cost.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to use stairs, escalators, or be physically lifted by staff or bystanders.
- Do not board the train if the gap or step exceeds your mobility aid's safe threshold.
- Do not leave the station without a confirmed alternative plan if you require operator-funded transport.
- Do not pay for a private taxi or alternative transport yourself before asking staff to arrange it.
- Do not abandon your mobility aid to board unless you have already determined it is safe and desired.

## What to do now
1. **Locate the nearest Help Point or staffed information desk immediately.** If the platform is unstaffed, use the emergency/information button on the platform Help Point to speak to a controller.
2. **State clearly that you are a "Turn Up and Go" passenger requiring an accessible alternative.** Under UK rail regulations, you do not need to have booked in advance to receive assistance or alternative transport when facilities fail.
3. **Request that the operator arranges alternative accessible transport (AAT) at no extra cost.**
   - This usually involves a wheelchair-accessible taxi to the nearest accessible station or your final destination.
   - The operator is responsible for the cost of this transport if the station is inaccessible to you.
4. **Use the Passenger Assist app to check the real-time status of lifts at your destination and interchange stations.** If you are using London Underground, use the TfL Go app to confirm "Step-Free" status is currently active for your entire route.
5. **Ask for a "Service Failure" reference or the name of the staff member arranging your transport.** This ensures accountability if the taxi or alternative assistance does not arrive as planned.
6. **Identify yourself to the train guard if you are already on a train and learn your destination is now inaccessible.** Ask them to radio ahead to arrange for your alternative transport to meet you at the most appropriate accessible station.
7. **Move to a "Safe Waiting Area" or Assisted Travel Lounge if available.** If you are experiencing pain, fatigue, or cold, inform staff so they can prioritize your alternative transport request.
8. **Keep your original ticket.** Even if your route changes or you are moved to a taxi, your rail ticket remains your proof of travel and is required for any future redress claims.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a formal complaint while still at the station.
- You do not need to provide a medical diagnosis to justify your need for step-free access.
- You do not need to decide on a claim for "Redress" (compensation for failed assistance) until you have completed your journey.
- You do not need to argue about the cause of the facility failure (e.g., staff shortages or technical faults).

## Important reassurance
Sudden access failures are a systemic issue, not a personal one. You are entitled to complete your journey safely and with dignity. Taking the time to wait for a safe, step-free alternative is a necessary safety decision, not an inconvenience to the network.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and alternative planning when physical access fails on the UK rail and TFL network. It provides first-step guidance only and does not replace long-term legal advice or formal complaint procedures.

## Important note
Rail operators in the UK are legally required under their Accessible Travel Policy (ATP) to provide alternative transport at no extra cost if a station is inaccessible. If you feel your safety is at immediate risk or you are stranded without communication at an unstaffed station at night, contact the British Transport Police by texting 61016 or calling 0800 40 50 40.
