# What to do if… you receive official paperwork with a deadline that has already passed (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as urgent, but not hopeless. Verify the paperwork using an official route, contact the issuing office today, and send a short response saying when you actually received it and asking what they need from you now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore it because the date has already passed; late contact can still reduce harm.
- Do not pay money, scan a QR code, or share extra personal details until you have checked the paperwork is genuine.
- Do not rely only on a phone number, payment link, or email address printed on paperwork that looks unusual.
- Do not invent reasons, backdate forms, or guess at facts you do not know.
- Do not contact anyone you are forbidden to contact, including under bail, licence, restraining order, injunction, or court conditions.
- Do not send a long argument if you are overwhelmed; a short factual response is better than silence.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the paperwork and keep proof of when it arrived.**
   - Look for who issued it, the reference or case number, the respond-by date, and what it says happens next.
   - Keep every page and the envelope, including postmarks, barcode labels, and delivery labels.
   - Take photos of the paperwork and envelope before you move or throw anything away.

2. **Check it is real using an official route.**
   - For courts and tribunals in England and Wales, use the official GOV.UK court and tribunal finder.
   - For Scotland, use Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service contact routes.
   - For Northern Ireland, use Northern Ireland Courts and Tribunals Service contact routes.
   - If it claims to be from police, probation, or a prison, use an official website, a number you have used before, or the main switchboard. Do not rely on a new number from a suspicious page.

3. **Contact the issuing office today and use one clear sentence.**
   - Say: “I received this on [date], but the respond-by date [date] had already passed. What do you need from me now, and how should I send it?”
   - Ask them to confirm whether anything has already been ordered, issued, listed, cancelled, or escalated because no response was received.
   - Ask for the correct email address, postal address, online portal, subject line, and reference number to use.

4. **Send a same-day holding response if you cannot complete everything yet.**
   Include:
   - your full name, address, and reference or case number;
   - the date you actually received the paperwork;
   - the fact that the respond-by date had already passed when it arrived;
   - a short request for the late response to be accepted, or for the correct route to ask for more time;
   - one factual line if relevant, such as “This was sent to an old address”, “I did not receive any earlier letter”, or “I was unwell”.
   Keep a copy, screenshot, or sent-email receipt.

5. **If it is an England and Wales civil court claim, judgment, or order, ask what status is on the file.**
   - Ask: “Has default judgment, an order, a hearing date, or enforcement action been recorded because nothing was filed in time?”
   - If something has already happened, ask the court office what application route is normally used for that type of case, and whether Form N244 is the relevant form.
   - Do not assume N244 is right for every court, tribunal, or UK nation; check the route before paying any fee or sending an application.

6. **If it is police bail, a station return date, or a notice to attend police, act the same day.**
   - Contact the custody suite, officer in the case, or verified police contact route.
   - Say: “I received this late and the date has passed. I need to know what I must do now and whether I should attend a station.”
   - If a solicitor previously acted for you, including a duty solicitor, contact them and send them a photo of the paperwork.

7. **If it is probation paperwork, contact probation immediately.**
   - Contact your offender manager or probation office using a verified number or email.
   - Say when you received the paperwork and ask for the next appointment or instruction.
   - Write down the date, time, name of the person you spoke to, and what was agreed.

8. **Make a five-minute proof pack.**
   - Save photos of the paperwork and envelope.
   - Write a short timeline: when you received it, where it was sent, why it may have arrived late, and every call or email you make.
   - Put the paperwork, envelope, timeline, and sent response in one folder or envelope.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain your whole case today.
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain about late delivery or service unless the office tells you that a complaint is the urgent route.
- You do not need perfect wording before you make contact.
- You do not need to solve every legal or practical consequence before finding out the current status of the file.

## Important reassurance
A missed date can feel final, but the most protective first step is usually prompt, factual contact once you discover the problem. Keeping the envelope, recording the timeline, and showing that you acted quickly can help the next person understand what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist legal help, especially if there is a court order, enforcement, arrest risk, immigration detention, licence recall, prison matter, or any restriction on your liberty.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Processes, forms, deadlines, and available options vary by court, tribunal, agency, case type, and UK nation.
