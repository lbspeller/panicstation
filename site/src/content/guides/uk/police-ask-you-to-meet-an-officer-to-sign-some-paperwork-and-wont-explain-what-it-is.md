# What to do if… police ask you to meet an officer “to sign some paperwork” and won’t explain what it is (UK)

## Short answer
Do not attend or sign any documents until you have secured independent legal advice. Ask the officer to confirm if the meeting is a **voluntary interview under caution** and state that you wish to have a **solicitor** present.

## Do not do these things
- Don't attend "just to see what they want" or because the request sounds informal or routine.
- Don't sign any document described as a "statement," "admission," "caution," or "community resolution" without a lawyer reviewing it first.
- Don't engage in "off the record" chats in hallways, cars, or over the phone; these are rarely truly off the record.
- Don't feel pressured to "clear things up quickly" by declining your right to a solicitor.
- Don't hand over your phone or provide passcodes "to be helpful" before speaking to a legal representative.
- Don't assume that "voluntary" means the situation is not serious; the evidence gathered has the same legal weight as an interview under arrest.

## What to do now
1. **Request the specific details of the request.** Ask the officer directly:
   - "Am I a suspect or a witness?"
   - "Is this a voluntary interview under caution?"
   - "What is the specific allegation or offence being investigated?"
   - "What is the exact name of the paperwork I am being asked to sign?"
2. **State your requirement for legal advice immediately.** Even if you are not under arrest, you are entitled to free, independent legal advice for any interview under caution. Say:
   - "I am happy to cooperate, but I require a solicitor to be present before I attend or sign anything."
3. **Verify the officer's identity.** If the contact was by phone or at your door, ask for their name, rank, station, and a reference number. Call 101 and ask to be put through to that specific officer to ensure the request is legitimate.
4. **Request "Pre-Interview Disclosure" in writing.** Ask the officer to provide a written summary of the allegations to you or your chosen solicitor before the meeting. 
5. **Establish if you need an Appropriate Adult.** If you are under 18 or have a mental health condition or learning disability, tell the officer.
   - The police must arrange an Appropriate Adult to support you during any formal interaction.
6. **Set a clear boundary for future contact.** If you feel pressured, say:
   - "Please provide the details of this request in writing. I will have my solicitor contact you to arrange a suitable time."
7. **Prepare for the possibility of arrest.** If the officer states you will be arrested if you do not attend voluntarily, do not argue. Contact a solicitor immediately to arrange a "voluntary surrender" or to be present at the station.

## What can wait
- You do not need to give your version of events right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to "go no comment" or "give a statement" until your solicitor has seen the police disclosure.
- You do not need to provide names of other people or evidence immediately.
- You do not need to attend at the exact time the officer first suggests if you need time to secure a lawyer.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard tactical approach for police to frame a formal investigation as "just signing paperwork" to lower your guard. Requesting a solicitor is a legal right and does not make you look guilty; it is what legal professionals and police officers themselves would do in the same situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for initial police contact. It does not cover trial procedures, the specifics of different criminal charges, or long-term legal strategy.

## Important note
Laws regarding police detention and voluntary attendance differ between England & Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional legal advice from a qualified solicitor in your specific jurisdiction.
