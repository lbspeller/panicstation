# What to do if… the only entrance to a station or terminal closes suddenly and you need an alternative route inside (UK)

## Short answer
Step back from the crowd, find staff or official signage, and use only a confirmed open public entrance or confirmed step-free route. Do not force entry, tailgate through barriers, or use emergency exits unless there is an emergency or staff tell you to.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t push into a dense crowd or stay in the pinch-point by doors, barriers, or stairs.
- Don’t force open a closed gate or door.
- Don’t follow people through staff-only doors.
- Don’t use emergency exits except in an emergency or when instructed by staff.
- Don’t jump ticket barriers or tailgate someone through; it may create a safety or ticketing problem.
- Don’t assume another entrance is step-free, open, or for your route without checking.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Step out of the main flow of people, move to a wall or wider area, and take 10–30 seconds to steady yourself before choosing a route.

2. **Look for the most reliable direction first.** Check, in this order:
   - station, terminal, gateline, or concourse staff
   - electronic signs and PA announcements
   - temporary closure notices pointing to another entrance
   - an official station map or information screen

3. **Ask one clear question.** Say: “Which entrance is open for [your line, operator, platform, or destination], and is there a step-free way in?”

4. **If you need step-free access or help right now, say so immediately.** Ask staff to direct you to the step-free alternative entrance and, if needed, to open an accessible gate or guide you by lifts, ramps, or a staffed route. If you had booked Passenger Assist or arranged staff help, say that.

5. **If there are no staff visible, use an official Help Point or information point.** Ask for the open entrance, whether it is step-free, and whether your line, platform, or terminal area is still reachable from there.

6. **Use a verified public route, not a guessed door.** Go only to the named open entrance, linked ticket hall, connected concourse, or other route shown by official signs or staff. If the signs stop or contradict each other, pause and ask again.

7. **If the closure seems safety-related, follow staff directions first.** This includes fire alerts, police activity, overcrowding, smoke, a security cordon, or a one-way crowd-control route. If the crowd feels unsafe because of crushing, falls, or panic, move away from the pressure point and tell staff: “I need space; it is not safe here.”

8. **Protect your journey without blocking others.** Keep your ticket, contactless card, phone, or booking reference ready. If it is safe and you are not in the way, take one quick photo of the closure notice or sign. Once you are out of the pinch-point, check official live departure or disruption information.

9. **If you cannot reach an open public entrance, choose safety over the journey.** Move to a less crowded public area and ask staff, security, or police for directions. If there is immediate danger, such as smoke, fire, violence, crushing, or a medical emergency, call 999. For a non-emergency rail safety concern, you can contact British Transport Police by text on 61016.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now about complaints, refunds, delay compensation, or proving what happened.
- You do not need the perfect reroute right now; first get to a safe, open entrance or a safe nearby alternative station or stop.
- You do not need to argue with other passengers or challenge staff instructions in the moment.
- You do not need to solve the whole journey while standing in a crowded doorway.

## Important reassurance
Stations may close entrances to manage safety, security, service disruption, or crowding. Feeling rushed or disoriented is normal; the safest reset is to step aside, get one confirmed instruction, and then move.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, for the first few minutes after a sudden entrance closure at a UK station or public transport terminal. Later decisions about missed trains, refunds, delay compensation, accessibility failures, complaints, or alternative travel may need help from the operator, station staff, Passenger Assist, or another relevant specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Local procedures vary by station, operator, and incident type, so follow staff instructions, official signs, and emergency announcements.
