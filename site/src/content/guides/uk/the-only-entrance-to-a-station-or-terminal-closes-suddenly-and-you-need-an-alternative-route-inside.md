# What to do if… the only entrance to a station or terminal closes suddenly and you need an alternative route inside (UK)

## Short answer
Step back from the entrance to a safe distance, locate station staff or a Help Point, and follow instructions for the nearest alternative public entrance or designated step-free route.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t push into crowds gathered at the closed doors or shutters.
- Don’t attempt to lift, force, or slide under closing shutters or gates.
- Don’t follow staff through "Staff Only" or "No Entry" doors unless explicitly told to do so.
- Don’t use emergency-only exits to gain entry to the station.
- Don’t use the emergency alarm on a platform or train to ask for travel directions.
- Don’t assume a secondary entrance is open if the main entrance is closed for safety reasons.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause point immediately.** Step at least 5-10 metres away from the closed entrance to avoid being caught in a crowd surge or blocking emergency access.
2. **Identify the reason for closure through sensory cues.**
   - Listen for automated announcements ("Due to a reported emergency...") or staff with megaphones.
   - Look for red flashing lights or the sound of motorised shutters.
   - Check for physical barriers like yellow "Station Closed" boards or police tape.
3. **Locate the nearest official information source.**
   - **Station staff** wearing high-visibility vests or uniforms.
   - **Station Help Points** (usually a yellow or silver pillar with an 'i' or 'emergency' button).
   - **Electronic dot-matrix signs** above the entrance or in the ticket hall.
4. **Request the specific alternative route for your needs.**
   - Ask: "Which entrance is open for [Line/Operator]?"
   - If applicable, ask: "Where is the nearest **step-free** alternative entrance?"
5. **Use official apps to verify accessibility status.**
   - Check the **TfL Go** app or **National Rail Enquiries** for real-time lift and station status updates.
   - Look for the "Live Lift Status" to ensure the alternative route is viable for your mobility needs.
6. **Follow the signed diversion route.**
   - Follow temporary signage (often "Alternative Entrance" arrows).
   - If the route takes you to a different ticket hall via street level, ensure you "touch out" and "touch in" as instructed by staff to avoid overcharging.
7. **Contact the British Transport Police for safety concerns.**
   - Use **61016** (text) or **0800 40 50 40** (call) if you feel unsafe due to overcrowding or suspicious behaviour.
   - Call **999** only if there is an immediate threat to life or a fire.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a delay Repay or compensation claim while still at the station entrance.
- You do not need to determine the long-term cause of the closure (e.g., technical fault vs. staffing).
- You do not need to find a manager to complain about the disruption immediately.
- You do not need to re-book Passenger Assist if you are simply being diverted to another entrance at the same station.

## Important reassurance
Station entrances are often closed temporarily to manage platform crowding or respond to minor fire alarm triggers. This is a standard safety procedure designed to keep the environment inside the station manageable. It does not always mean your train is cancelled or the entire station is out of action.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and finding an alternative entry point during a sudden closure. It does not cover long-term travel planning, compensation law, or emergency evacuation from inside a train.

## Important note
Procedures for station closures can change rapidly based on the type of incident (security, overcrowding, or technical). Always prioritise instructions given by uniformed staff or emergency services over app data or third-party information. This guide is for informational purposes for the first minutes of a disruption.
