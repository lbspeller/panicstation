# What to do if… someone claims to be a partner after a death and demands access to information or plans (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and do not share documents, passwords, account details, plans, or extra personal information. Use one calm boundary and route the person to whoever is formally dealing with the estate, or to that person’s solicitor.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hand over copies, photos, screenshots, passwords, ID documents, bank details, will papers, funeral plan papers, insurance letters, or “just one document”.
- Don’t let urgency, guilt, anger, threats, or “I need it today” pressure make you decide in the moment.
- Don’t confirm extra details about the person who died, such as date of birth, previous addresses, account providers, NHS number, passwords, or security answers.
- Don’t decide for yourself whether the person was or was not a partner.
- Don’t meet them alone or let them into the home if you feel unsure or unsafe.
- Don’t post public updates with details that could help identity fraud, such as dates, addresses, paperwork, accounts, or funeral arrangements not already public.
- Don’t encourage others to identify, shame, or contact the person online.

## What to do now
1. **Send one boundary message.**  
   “I’m not able to share plans, documents, or personal information. Anything relevant needs to go through the person formally dealing with the estate, or their solicitor.”

2. **Give only one safe contact route if you have one.**  
   If a solicitor, executor, administrator, or other person is already formally dealing with the estate, give the claimant that office email or phone number. Do not give private addresses, private phone numbers, passwords, copies of papers, or family group-chat details.

3. **Do not assess their proof yourself.**  
   If they send photos, messages, relationship evidence, or claims about being entitled to something, acknowledge receipt only if needed and pass it to the person handling the estate or their solicitor. You do not need to decide whether it is valid.

4. **Secure the documents and plans you already have.**  
   Put will papers, funeral plan papers, insurance letters, bank letters, identity documents, keys, and devices somewhere not visible to visitors. Keep digital copies in a protected folder and avoid sharing from your phone while upset or under pressure.

5. **Make a simple record.**  
   Save messages, call logs, voicemails, envelopes, usernames, phone numbers, dates, times, and what was asked for. Keep your replies short and factual.

6. **Check the immediate safety risk.**  
   If they are at the door, threatening violence, trying to force entry, refusing to leave, or you believe anyone is in immediate danger, call 999. If there is no immediate danger but the contact feels harassing, intimidating, or fraudulent, use 101 or the relevant police online contact route.

7. **Report suspected fraud through the official route.**  
   If you think the request may be fraud, identity misuse, or an attempt to get estate information dishonestly, use the UK government fraud-reporting guidance and keep your screenshots and notes. If a crime is happening now, use 999 in an emergency or 101 for a non-emergency.

8. **Tell the person handling the estate before any more contact.**  
   Send them the record you made and say what has not been shared. Ask them how they want future contact handled.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether the person was really a partner.
- You do not need to decide who is entitled to anything.
- You do not need to share the will, funeral plan, financial information, account details, or private family information to seem fair.
- You do not need to argue, investigate, accuse, or prove the person is lying.
- You do not need to answer repeated messages once you have given the boundary and safe route.
- You do not need to make funeral, estate, money, property, or relationship decisions while shocked or exhausted.

## Important reassurance
Unexpected requests after a death can be real, mistaken, confused, or unsafe. Keeping everything on the formal estate route is not unkind; it protects the person who died, the family, and anyone with a genuine claim from rushed or irreversible mistakes.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment you are contacted. Questions about relationship claims, inheritance, estate documents, funeral plans, property, and disputes may need help from a solicitor or another appropriately qualified adviser later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe, prioritise immediate safety and contact emergency services.
