# What to do if… police ask to take a quick look inside your home as part of an inquiry (UK)

## Short answer
Do not agree to a “quick look” on the spot. Verify who they are, keep a calm boundary, and ask whether they are asking for your consent or relying on a warrant or specific legal power.

## Do not do these things
- Do not let officers in just because the request sounds casual, friendly, or awkward to refuse.
- Do not argue on the doorstep or try to explain the whole situation there.
- Do not physically block, push, or struggle with officers if they say they are using a power to enter.
- Do not sign written consent unless you understand what you are consenting to and feel able to choose freely.
- Do not voluntarily hand over, unlock, or show devices “for a quick check” without first asking the legal basis and getting legal advice where possible.
- Do not guess whether a warrant or power is valid; ask to see it, note what is said, and get advice afterwards.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** If officers are at the door, keep the door closed while you speak, if it is safe to do so. If they called or messaged, you can say: “Please put your request in writing. I need to check who I am speaking to before I agree to anything.”

2. **Verify who is contacting you.** Ask for names, collar or warrant numbers, the police force, station or team, and a reference number. If you are unsure whether they are genuine, end the call or pause the doorstep conversation and use 101 or the force’s published contact route to check.

3. **Ask the key question before anything else.** Say: “Are you asking for my consent to enter, or are you relying on a warrant or a specific legal power?” Then ask: “What exactly do you want to look at, and what is the reason?”

4. **If they are only asking for consent, you can say no.** Use plain words: “I do not consent to a quick look, entry, or a search today. Please leave your details and put the request and legal basis in writing.” If you share the home, avoid speaking for anyone else’s consent.

5. **If they show a warrant or say they have a power to enter, do not obstruct.** Ask to see the paperwork or hear the specific power. If possible, check the address, the areas covered, what they are looking for, and whether entry is allowed now. You can say: “I am not consenting, but I will not obstruct.”

6. **If they begin a search, keep your role simple.** Ask who is in charge of the search. Ask whether a friend, neighbour, or other person can be present, understanding that officers may refuse or may not delay the search. Do not interfere with the search.

7. **Ask for written records before they leave.** Depending on where you are in the UK and what power is used, this may be a notice, search record, copy warrant, receipt, reference number, or details of how to get a copy later. Ask for a list or receipt for anything taken.

8. **Write down your own record as soon as you can.** Note the date, time, force, names or numbers, exact words used such as “quick look”, what legal basis they gave, what rooms or items were involved, and anything taken or damaged.

9. **Get legal advice before answering questions about the inquiry.** If you are arrested, taken to a police station, or asked to attend an interview, ask for a solicitor and ask for legal advice before answering questions about the matter.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide now whether you are “cooperating fully” or “fighting it”.
- You do not have to give a full account of your life, relationships, devices, documents, or movements on the doorstep.
- You do not have to understand the whole law immediately; your first job is to avoid accidental consent, avoid escalation, and get the request into a recorded form.
- You can deal with complaints, return of property, damage, or longer-term legal steps after the immediate contact is over.

## Important reassurance
A casual request from police can feel hard to refuse, especially if it is framed as only taking a minute. Asking who they are, what power they rely on, and whether you are free to refuse is a normal boundary, not a sign that you have done anything wrong.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the first contact or earliest safe pause. Police powers, forms, and search procedures differ between England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, and later decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. If officers are present, the safest approach is to stay calm, avoid obstruction, avoid accidental consent, ask for the legal basis, and seek legal advice as soon as possible.
