# What to do if… you are pressured to resolve a sexual misconduct issue informally with the person involved (UK)

## Short answer
You can pause and say you are not comfortable resolving this directly with the person involved. Ask for the formal reporting, complaints, or safeguarding route, and for any no-contact or support options, in writing before you agree to any meeting.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree on the spot to meet them alone, talk it through, or take part in mediation or a restorative-style discussion because someone says it is simpler.
- Do not let anyone rush you into deciding whether to use an informal process, make a formal report, or sign wording about what happened.
- Do not put yourself in a situation where the person involved can contact you privately, such as isolated meetings, late-night calls, lifts home, or one-to-one chats.
- Do not sign an informal agreement, confidentiality wording, meeting note, or withdrawal statement while you feel pressured.
- Do not delete messages, emails, screenshots, notes, or calendar entries about what happened or about being told to handle it informally.
- Do not negotiate directly with the person involved just to prove you are being reasonable.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safe pause and use a boundary you can repeat in writing.**  
   You can write: “I am not comfortable resolving this directly with the person involved or meeting one-to-one. Please send me the formal reporting, complaints, or safeguarding process and my support options in writing.”

2. **Ask what process they are asking you to use, and say what you are not agreeing to.**  
   Ask for the written policy, the formal route, who will handle it, who is independent of the person involved, and what immediate support or no-contact measures can be considered. Add: “I am not agreeing to mediation, a restorative-style discussion, or any process requiring direct contact with them.”

3. **Use the safest official route for your setting.**
   - **Workplace:** ask HR or a senior manager not connected to the person involved for the sexual harassment policy or formal grievance route. If your line manager is part of the problem, ask who you can go to instead. If you are in a union, contact your rep early.
   - **University or college:** contact student support, the sexual misconduct or harassment reporting route, the complaints team, your personal tutor, or the students’ union. Ask what support measures can be considered now, such as timetable, placement, workspace, supervision, accommodation, or contact arrangements.
   - **School or under-18 setting:** ask for the safeguarding lead, named safeguarding contact, or deputy safeguarding contact. If the concern is about that person, ask who handles safeguarding concerns about staff or adults in that setting, or ask another trusted adult to help you find the right route.

4. **Make a brief record while it is fresh.**  
   For your own memory, note what happened, dates and times you remember, who told you to resolve it informally, what they asked you to do, and what you want instead. Keep screenshots or copies of relevant messages if you can do so safely.

5. **Bring in someone independent before any meeting or decision.**  
   This could be a union rep, trusted colleague, friend, student adviser, safeguarding staff member, sexual violence support worker, or another person you trust. If a meeting is proposed, ask for the purpose in writing, an agenda, notes afterwards, a support person present, and confirmation that you will not be placed in a face-to-face discussion with the person involved.

6. **If you feel unsafe or at risk of escalation, prioritise safety over process.**  
   Consider steps you control today: limit or block contact routes, avoid isolated meetings, change a routine temporarily, ask for a different supervisor, tutor, manager, placement contact, or point of contact, and tell someone you trust where you will be. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.

7. **If you want medical or specialist support, you can ask without deciding about police reporting.**  
   Depending on urgency, you can contact A&E, a GP, a sexual health clinic, NHS 111 where appropriate, or a Sexual Assault Referral Centre. Specialist sexual violence services can also help you talk through options without pressuring you to report.

8. **Keep your options open if you might want to take action later.**  
   Keep basic notes and communications, and avoid sending pressured replies. You do not need to decide today whether to make a formal complaint, report to police, or take employment, education, or legal steps.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to report to the police.
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a formal workplace, university, college, or school complaint.
- You do not need to write a perfect statement or gather everything right now.
- You do not need to reply immediately to pressure messages.
- You do not need to negotiate directly with the person involved to be taken seriously.
- You do not need to decide what outcome you want before you have support and the written process.

## Important reassurance
Being pushed toward informal resolution can feel confusing and isolating, especially if it is framed as the mature or easy option. Wanting a formal route, safeguards, support, or no contact is a reasonable safety response. You are allowed to slow the situation down and ask for a process that does not put you face-to-face with the person involved.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation, reduce pressure, and avoid harmful early moves. Later decisions about formal complaints, employment steps, education processes, legal advice, medical care, or police reporting may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Procedures vary by workplace, school, college, university, and local area. If you feel threatened or in immediate danger, call 999.
