# What to do if… a wire transfer you sent is marked pending and you need to stop it (UK)

## Short answer
Call your bank immediately using a trusted route and ask whether the transfer is still queued inside the bank or has already been sent. If it is still queued, ask for it to be stopped; if it has been sent, ask whether the bank can start an urgent recall, trace, or fraud process.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send another payment to “fix”, “verify”, “release”, or “unlock” the first one.
- Don’t pay a cancellation fee, release fee, admin charge, tax, or deposit to stop the transfer.
- Don’t rely only on the word “pending” in an app; it can mean different things for different payment types.
- Don’t call numbers from texts, emails, pop-ups, search ads, or a payment instruction message.
- Don’t keep messaging a suspected scammer while you are trying to stop or recall the payment.
- Don’t delete payment confirmations, messages, invoices, emails, or screenshots.

## What to do now
1. **Stop any related outgoing money.**  
   Do not send a test payment, replacement payment, refund fee, or “verification” amount.

2. **Use a trusted route to reach your bank.**  
   Call the number on your card, use the secure number or chat inside your banking app, or use the bank’s official website typed in yourself. Say: “I need urgent help with a pending bank transfer that may need to be stopped or recalled.”

3. **Ask for the exact payment status.**  
   Ask: “Has this payment already left my account or is it still queued, pending, future-dated, under review, or waiting for release?” Write down the wording they use.

4. **If it has not left the bank, ask for an immediate stop.**  
   Say: “Please stop or cancel it before release. Please tell me who can action that now and confirm that the request has been logged.”

5. **If it has already been sent, ask for the fastest recovery route.**  
   Say: “Please start an urgent recall request or trace, and contact the receiving bank if that is available for this payment type.” Ask for the payment reference, time sent, amount, receiving account details used, and any case number.

6. **If fraud, impersonation, or changed payment details may be involved, say that clearly.**  
   Tell the bank: “I think this may be an authorised push payment scam or payment diversion.” Ask to speak to the fraud team and ask whether the APP scam reimbursement rules for Faster Payments or CHAPS may apply to your case.

7. **Secure access if compromise is possible.**  
   If your email, banking app, device, or online banking may have been accessed by someone else, change passwords using a device you trust. Start with email if it controls bank password resets, then secure banking access and turn on two-step verification where available.

8. **If the intended recipient is legitimate, contact them through a verified route.**  
   Use a phone number or contact route you already trust, not only the email thread that gave you the payment details. Ask them to hold any funds they receive and to respond quickly if their bank contacts them about returning the money.

9. **Report suspected fraud to the right police route.**  
   If you are in England, Wales or Northern Ireland, report it to Report Fraud and keep the reference. If you are in Scotland, report it to Police Scotland using 101 for non-emergencies, or 999 if there is immediate danger.

10. **Preserve a short evidence record.**  
   Screenshot the pending status, confirmation screen, payment instruction, invoice, bank messages, and any suspicious messages. Write down the times you contacted the bank, who you spoke to, and what they said they would do.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a formal complaint or go to the Financial Ombudsman Service.
- You do not need to write a long explanation to the recipient, bank, police, or anyone else; short factual notes are enough for now.
- You do not need to prove fraud before asking your bank to try to stop, recall, trace, or review the payment.
- You do not need to work out the final legal position while the immediate priority is stopping further loss.

## Important reassurance
A pending status can sometimes mean the bank has not fully released the payment yet, so acting quickly can still matter. Even if the money has already been sent, asking for a recall, trace, and fraud review is still a sensible first step, although recovery is not guaranteed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stop further loss, contact the bank, preserve evidence, and report suspected fraud. Later decisions about recovery, reimbursement, complaints, or legal action may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general practical information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. A bank’s ability to stop or recover a transfer depends on the payment type, the processing stage, the receiving bank, and whether any reimbursement rules apply.
