# What to do if… you notice a pending payment or transfer that you did not authorise (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately using a trusted route, such as your banking app, the number on your card or statement, or 159 if your bank is covered, and say the payment is unauthorised and still pending.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume “pending” means it will disappear; treat it as urgent until your bank confirms what it is.
- Don’t call numbers from a text, email, pop-up, or unexpected call about the payment.
- Don’t approve in-app prompts, one-time passcodes, card reader codes, or “verification” requests you did not start.
- Don’t move money to a “safe account”; banks and police do not ask you to do this.
- Don’t delete messages, screenshots, app alerts, or transaction details yet.
- Don’t contact the merchant or payee before your bank if the payment may still be stopped or secured.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a trusted contact route.** End any unexpected call about the payment. Open your bank’s app yourself, use the number on your card or statement, or dial 159 if your bank is covered.
2. **Tell the bank the key words early.** Say: “I can see a pending payment or transfer that I did not authorise.” Ask the bank to secure the account while you are on the call or chat.
3. **Freeze what you can safely freeze.** If your app lets you do it, freeze or lock the card linked to the payment. Do not approve any prompt that appears unexpectedly while you are doing this.
4. **Capture the transaction details.** Note or screenshot the amount, date and time, merchant or beneficiary name, reference, and whether it appears as a card payment, bank transfer, Direct Debit, standing order, mobile wallet payment, or new payee.
5. **Ask the bank what can still be stopped.** Ask whether the payment can be stopped, cancelled, blocked, traced, recalled, disputed, or monitored before it settles. If it is a bank transfer, ask the bank to attempt recovery immediately.
6. **Ask the bank to remove or block suspicious access.** Ask them to check for new payees, standing orders, Direct Debits, saved beneficiaries, unknown devices, card tokens, and mobile wallet links, and remove anything you do not recognise.
7. **If it is a Direct Debit, ask for the Direct Debit route.** Ask the bank to cancel the Direct Debit instruction. If money has already been taken in error, ask for a full and immediate refund under the Direct Debit Guarantee.
8. **Change passwords from a trusted device.** Change your email password first, then your banking password or passcode. Use strong sign-in options offered by the bank, such as biometrics or two-step verification.
9. **Record what the bank says.** Keep the time, date, staff name or chat reference, fraud case number, and any promise to stop, refund, trace, or investigate the payment.
10. **Report the fraud after the bank has secured the account.** If you are in England, Wales or Northern Ireland, you can report it to Report Fraud. If you are in Scotland, Report Fraud directs you to report via 101; Police Scotland also has scams and frauds advice and non-emergency reporting routes.
11. **If the bank refuses to help or you disagree with the outcome, complain in writing.** Use the bank’s formal complaints process. If you receive a final response you disagree with, or the bank has not given a final response within 8 weeks for most complaints, you can contact the Financial Ombudsman Service.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it is fraud, a merchant error, a card hold, or a banking error.
- You do not need to write a long statement before speaking to the bank.
- You do not need to replace your phone or laptop immediately unless the bank or a trusted security professional advises it.
- You do not need to argue with the merchant or payee while the bank is still checking whether the payment can be stopped.
- You do not need to decide today whether to close the whole account.

## Important reassurance
A pending payment you do not recognise is alarming, but acting quickly is the right first move. Freezing access, using a trusted contact route, and getting the bank’s fraud team involved gives the bank the best chance to limit further harm and explain what type of transaction it is.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the first minutes and hours. Later decisions about refunds, complaints, recovery, account changes, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, fraud investigation, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Bank processes, refund rules, and complaint routes can vary by payment type and circumstances.
