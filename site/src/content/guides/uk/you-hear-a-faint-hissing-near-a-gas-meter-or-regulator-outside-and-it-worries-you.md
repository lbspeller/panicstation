# What to do if… you hear a faint hissing near a gas meter or regulator outside and it worries you (UK)

## Short answer
Move at least 10 metres away from the hissing sound and call the **National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999** immediately from a safe distance.

## Do not do these things
- Don't smoke, vape, use matches, or use any naked flames anywhere near the meter box.
- Don't use a mobile phone, flashlight, or any electronic device in the immediate area of the hissing.
- Don't operate electrical switches, doorbells, or gate motors near the suspected leak.
- Don't attempt to tighten, dismantle, or "fix" any part of the gas meter or regulator.
- Don't put your face close to the pipes to try to "pinpoint" the source of the sound.
- Don't go back toward the meter to check if the sound has stopped.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance from the suspected leak immediately.** Move yourself, children, and pets at least 10 metres away from the meter or regulator.
2. **Open all doors and windows if the meter is near an entry point to the building.** This prevents gas from building up inside the property if it is drifting from the outside meter.
3. **Call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999 from a safe location.** This line is free and open 24 / 7; hearing-impaired users can use the Relay UK prefix 18001 before the number.
4. **Evacuate the property if you can smell gas indoors.** If the outside leak is strong enough to be detected inside, everyone should leave the building and wait in fresh air.
5. **Only turn off the gas at the Emergency Control Valve (ECV) if you can do so without entering the hissing area.**
   - The ECV is usually a handle next to the meter.
   - Turn it 90 degrees so it is at a right angle to the pipe.
   - If the hissing is loud or right at the handle, **do not approach it** and wait for the emergency engineer.
6. **Alert your neighbours by shouting or knocking on their doors.** Avoid using doorbells or electronic intercoms if the gas smell or sound is near their property.
7. **Wait for the gas emergency engineer to arrive.** They aim to attend uncontrolled escapes within one hour and will tell you when the area is safe to re-enter.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact source of the hissing yourself.
- You do not need to contact your gas supplier (e.g., British Gas, Octopus, E.ON) until the emergency engineer has secured the site.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of the call-out; the National Gas Emergency Service does not charge for safety inspections.
- You do not need to gather tools or attempt to shut off appliances if you have already evacuated.

## Important reassurance
Hearing a hissing sound near gas infrastructure is a valid reason to call for an emergency inspection. Gas regulators can sometimes make faint operational noises, but the emergency service expects and prefers you to report any audible escape so they can verify it is safe.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only. This guide addresses the immediate safety actions required when a leak is suspected and does not cover the repair of gas appliances or internal pipework.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and does not replace the instructions given by the National Gas Emergency Service operator. If there is a fire or an immediate threat to life, call 999.
