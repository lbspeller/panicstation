# What to do if… you receive a tax balance due notice that you cannot pay by the deadline (UK)

## Short answer
Do not ignore the notice; pay any amount you can safely afford immediately to reduce interest charges, then contact the issuer (HMRC or your local council) to request a "Time to Pay" arrangement or payment plan.

## Do not do these things
- Don't assume that being unable to pay in full means you should wait to respond; early contact prevents court action and extra penalties.
- Don't use high-interest short-term loans or payday lending to cover a tax bill without first exploring official interest-free or low-interest payment plans.
- Don't click links or provide bank details in response to unexpected texts or emails; HMRC never asks for personal or financial information via text or email.
- Don't stop filing your tax returns or council documents; late filing carries separate, often more expensive, fixed penalties regardless of what you owe.
- Don't use money required for absolute essentials—rent, food, heating, or medication—to pay a tax bill before requesting a hardship arrangement.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the issuer and the type of tax immediately.**
   - Determine if the letter is from **HMRC** (Self Assessment, VAT, PAYE, Corporation Tax) or your **local council** (Council Tax). 
   - Check the letterhead for a specific reference number, such as a 10-digit UTR for Self Assessment or an 8-digit Accounts Office reference for PAYE.

2. **Confirm the notice is genuine through official channels.**
   - Sign in to your personal tax account or business tax account via the GOV.UK website to see if the balance matches. 
   - Report suspicious texts by forwarding them to **60599** and forward suspicious emails to **phishing@hmrc.gov.uk** before deleting them.

3. **Make a partial payment today if you have any safe surplus.**
   - **Pay whatever amount you can afford now** using the reference number on the notice. 
   - Even a small payment reduces the total amount of interest (currently 7.75% for late payments) that HMRC calculates daily from the day after the deadline.

4. **Set up an HMRC "Time to Pay" arrangement online if you meet the thresholds.**
   - For Self Assessment: You can usually set this up online if you owe £30,000 or less and it is within 60 days of the deadline.
   - For VAT: You can usually set this up online if you owe £50,000 or less and it is within 28 days of the deadline.
   - If you are above these amounts or outside these windows, you must call the HMRC Payment Support Service.

5. **Contact your local council immediately for Council Tax arrears.**
   - **Call the council number on the bill** and ask for a payment arrangement to prevent the issuance of a liability order.
   - Request to spread the current year's bill over 12 months instead of 10 if you have not already done so, as this reduces monthly costs.

6. **Gather your financial evidence for the arrangement call.**
   - Have a list of your monthly income and essential spending (housing, food, utilities) ready. 
   - Be prepared to propose a specific monthly amount you can realistically commit to without defaulting later.

7. **Check your eligibility for "Breathing Space" (Debt Respite Scheme).**
   - If you live in England or Wales, contact a professional debt adviser to see if you qualify for a 60-day pause on enforcement and interest.
   - Note that this only applies to existing arrears; you must continue to pay new/ongoing tax liabilities that fall due during the 60 days.

8. **Create a record of all interactions.**
   - **Write down the date, time, and name of anyone you speak to.**
   - Save copies of any online confirmation screens or reference numbers provided during the setup of a payment plan.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle the final accuracy of the tax calculation today if the deadline is immediate—stabilise the payment situation first, then appeal the amount later.
- You do not need to find a way to pay the entire balance at once if it puts your basic survival at risk.
- You do not need to hire a specialist accountant or tax solicitor for the initial contact; payment plans are standard procedures you can initiate yourself.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a demand for money you do not have is a high-stress event, and feeling a sense of panic or the urge to avoid the letter is a standard physiological response. Most tax authorities, especially HMRC, have established "Time to Pay" protocols specifically for this situation. Taking the first step of making a partial payment or logging in to the official portal often reduces the immediate pressure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate tax shortfall. It does not cover the long-term management of tax debt, insolvency proceedings, or the technicalities of tax appeals.

## Important note
This information is for guidance and does not constitute legal, financial, or tax advice. Interest on late payments is a statutory requirement and typically continues to accrue even when a payment plan is in place. If you are in a wider debt crisis, seek advice from a free, accredited service such as StepChange, National Debtline, or Citizens Advice.
