# What to do if… you have a fever with a stiff neck or sensitivity to light (UK)

## Short answer
Treat fever with neck stiffness or sensitivity to light as urgent: call 999 for an ambulance or go to A&E now if you think it could be meningitis or sepsis. Do not wait for a rash.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see whether a rash appears.
- Do not rely on online symptom checkers, group chats, or social media for reassurance.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel faint, confused, very sleepy, or in severe pain.
- Do not take alcohol, sleeping tablets, or anything that could make the unwell person harder to wake.
- Do not send a child to school or nursery while they are unwell with these symptoms.

## What to do now
1. **Get urgent medical help now.**
   - Call **999** and say: “fever plus stiff neck or sensitivity to light” and that you are worried about **meningitis or sepsis**.
   - If you are already travelling for help, go to the nearest **A&E** rather than waiting for a GP appointment.
   - If the person is stable and you are genuinely unsure how urgent this is, contact **NHS 111** or the relevant local 111 service for urgent advice, but do not delay 999 or A&E if they are getting worse or you are worried.

2. **Keep the unwell person watched and easy to reach.**
   - Stay with them, or ask another adult to stay with them.
   - Keep them within sight and check that they can be woken and can answer simple questions.
   - Keep the phone nearby, on loudspeaker if useful, and charge it if possible.

3. **Tell 999 or A&E about any danger signs.**
   - Confusion, unusual drowsiness, being hard to wake, or becoming unresponsive.
   - Seizure or fit.
   - Breathing that looks difficult or unusually fast.
   - Cold hands and feet, vomiting, diarrhoea, or pale, mottled, blotchy, or very unusual-looking skin.
   - Severe or worsening headache, stiff neck, dislike of bright lights, or severe muscle, joint, or limb pain.
   - For a baby: refusing feeds, a high-pitched cry, unusual irritability, a stiff or floppy body, being hard to wake, or a bulging soft spot on the head.
   - A rash or spots that do not fade under pressure: press the side of a clear glass firmly against the rash and see whether it fades.
   - A rash may not appear, and it may be harder to see on brown or black skin. Check paler areas such as the palms of the hands, soles of the feet, whites of the eyes, inside the eyelids, roof of the mouth, and tummy.

4. **Write down only the key facts if this does not delay help.**
   - When the fever started.
   - When the neck stiffness or light sensitivity began.
   - Any recent infection, close contact with someone very unwell, or recent travel.
   - Current medicines, allergies, major conditions, and pregnancy status if relevant.

5. **Use only simple comfort steps while help is being arranged.**
   - Offer small sips of water if the person is fully awake and able to swallow.
   - Use only medicines you know are safe for that person, as directed on the packet, and do not delay getting urgent help to see whether medicine “works”.
   - Keep the room calm and avoid bright light if it is painful.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether this is meningitis, sepsis, flu, migraine, or another illness right now.
- You do not need to message work, school, or everyone in the family before getting help.
- You do not need to pack thoroughly; take essentials only if they are close by, such as phone, charger, keys, and a medication or allergy list.

## Important reassurance
Feeling scared is understandable when these symptoms appear together. Many illnesses can cause fever and headache, but fever with neck stiffness or sensitivity to light is a combination where urgent assessment is the safest next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes and hours only. Later decisions, tests, treatment, and follow-up should be guided by healthcare professionals.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, diagnosis, or a substitute for urgent professional care. If symptoms are severe, worsening, or you are worried, call 999 or go to A&E now.
