# What to do if… water starts leaking into your home around a chimney or fireplace (UK)
## Short answer
Keep people away from the wet area and treat it as an electrical and appliance safety risk first. Switch off electricity at the consumer unit only if water could reach sockets, wiring, or appliances and you can do it without stepping into water or touching wet surfaces.

## Do not do these things
- Do not touch sockets, switches, extension leads, plugs, or appliances if the wall, floor, or your hands are wet.
- Do not keep using an open fire, stove, wood burner, gas fire, or any appliance using that chimney or flue to “dry it out”.
- Do not climb onto the roof, chimney, or ladder in bad weather, darkness, or while you are alone.
- Do not seal the leak from inside while water is actively coming in, because it can trap water and hide worsening damage.
- Do not sleep or sit under soft plaster, a sagging ceiling, cracking, or falling debris near the chimney breast.
- Do not use electrical heaters or fans near the wet area.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Move children, pets, and anyone vulnerable out of the room. Put on shoes. Keep clear of wet walls, wet floors, the hearth, sockets, extension leads, TVs, lamps, and other electrics.
2. **Deal with possible electrical danger.** If water could reach electrics, switch off the electricity at the consumer unit or main switch only if you can reach it safely. If there is sparking, smoke, fire, electric shock, or someone is injured, move away and call 999.
3. **Call the right electricity contact if the supply may be affected.** If you cannot safely isolate power, there is a power cut, or you are worried about electricity supply equipment or external cables, call 105 in England, Scotland, or Wales. In Northern Ireland, call NIE Networks on 03457 643 643. For internal wiring, sockets, lights, or a wet consumer unit, use a qualified electrician.
4. **Stop using anything connected to that chimney or flue.** Leave open fires, stoves, wood burners, gas fires, and linked appliances off until the leak and flue area have been checked. Do not relight them just because the visible dripping slows.
5. **Act on gas or carbon monoxide warning signs.** If a carbon monoxide alarm sounds, you smell gas or fumes, smoke comes back into the room, or anyone feels dizzy, sick, breathless, confused, or unusually sleepy, get everyone into fresh air. Do not operate electrical switches. Call 999 for immediate medical danger. For a gas emergency, call 0800 111 999 in England, Scotland, or Wales, or 0800 002 001 in Northern Ireland.
6. **Contain the water without touching electrics.** Put a bucket, bowl, or trug where water is dripping. Lay towels around the hearth and skirting. If water is running down a wall, use a plastic sheet or towel to channel it into a container, keeping it well away from sockets, appliances, and hot surfaces.
7. **Reduce the chance of injury from falling material.** If plaster is bubbling, soft, cracking, sagging, or shedding grit, keep the area underneath clear. Avoid using or sleeping in that room until it has been checked.
8. **Record what is happening.** Take quick photos or video of the dripping point, wet wall, hearth, ceiling stain, chimney breast, sockets nearby, and any falling plaster. Do this from a safe dry position.
9. **Contact the responsible repair route.** If you rent, report it to your landlord or agent straight away as a leak that may affect electrics, structure, or a fireplace appliance. Use their emergency or out-of-hours repair route if one is available. If you own, contact your home insurer or home emergency provider if you have one, and arrange a roofer, chimney specialist, or suitable heating appliance engineer.
10. **Before reuse, get the appliance route checked if it was affected.** For a gas fire or gas appliance, use a Gas Safe registered engineer. For a solid fuel stove, wood burner, or open fire, use a suitable chimney or solid fuel professional, such as a HETAS registrant or approved chimney sweep.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact cause now; it may be from the roof, flashing, pointing, pot, cowl, or flue area.
- You do not need to strip plaster, repaint, redecorate, or run dehumidifiers while water is still coming in or electrics have not been confirmed safe.
- You do not need to choose the final repair plan now; focus on safety, photos, and stopping further damage.
- You can check insurance wording, excess, and non-urgent paperwork after the immediate electrical, appliance, and falling-material risks are controlled.

## Important reassurance
A chimney or fireplace leak can look frightening, especially in heavy rain, but the first steps are manageable: keep away from wet electrics, stop using the fireplace or appliance, contain the water safely, and get the roof, chimney, flue, and any affected electrics checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repairs, drying, insurance, tenancy responsibilities, or appliance safety may need help from a roofer, chimney specialist, electrician, Gas Safe registered engineer, HETAS registrant, landlord, insurer, or other suitable professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, electrical, gas safety, structural, insurance, or other professional advice. If you suspect electrical danger, structural instability, a gas leak, carbon monoxide, fire, or injury, treat it as urgent and get professional help.
