# What to do if… you realise your pet’s travel health certificate date window may not match your departure date (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the exact issue or endorsement date on your document against your landing time at the destination border. If you will arrive outside the strict 10-day validity window, you must have the document re-issued by an Official Veterinarian before you depart.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to alter, white-out, or overwrite any dates on the official document.
- Do not assume a one-day or two-day "overrun" will be overlooked by airline staff or border officials.
- Do not travel to the airport or ferry port without a confirmed plan if the document is technically expired.
- Do not rely on verbal assurances from a non-official vet that the document "should be fine."
- Do not forget that the 10-day window includes the day of issuance as "Day 1."

## What to do now
1. **Calculate your "Arrival Window" precisely using the document's signature date.**  
   - For an Animal Health Certificate (AHC) to enter the EU: You must arrive at the EU border within 10 days of the date the Official Veterinarian (OV) signed and dated the document.
   - For a Great Britain pet health certificate to enter the UK: You must enter GB within 10 days of the date the certificate was issued or, if required, endorsed by the competent authority in the departing country.

2. **Verify the "5-Day Rule" for non-commercial travel status.**  
   - Confirm that you (the owner) or an authorised person are travelling within 5 days of your pet's movement.
   - If the pet's travel date has moved more than 5 days away from your own travel date, the certificate type must change from "non-commercial" to "commercial," which requires different paperwork.

3. **Check the tapeworm treatment window if you are travelling with a dog.**  
   - Ensure the tapeworm treatment (Praziquantel or equivalent) was administered not less than 24 hours and not more than 120 hours before your scheduled arrival time at the destination border.
   - If your departure delay pushes your arrival beyond the 120-hour limit, the treatment must be re-administered and re-recorded by a vet on the certificate.

4. **Contact your issuing Official Veterinarian immediately for a "replacement issuance."**  
   - Ask if they can re-issue the certificate based on the existing rabies vaccination and microchip records.
   - Provide your new, confirmed itinerary so the vet can time the signature to ensure you land well within the 10-day window.
   - Confirm if you need to bring the pet back in for a fresh clinical examination; this is often required for the vet to sign a new certificate.

5. **Notify your carrier (airline, ferry, or rail) of the document status.**  
   - Inform the pet travel desk of your new expected arrival time.
   - Ask for their specific cut-off times for document checks at the point of departure to ensure your new certificate will be accepted during boarding.

## What can wait
- You do not need to restart the rabies vaccination process if the current vaccination is still valid and was administered after the microchip was implanted.
- You do not need to apply for a new GB Pet Health Certificate if you are already in the EU and your original AHC is still within its 4-month return-to-UK validity window.
- You do not need to worry about long-term pet passport changes for future years while resolving this specific journey.

## Important reassurance
Timing errors with pet travel paperwork are one of the most common issues handled by Official Veterinarians. While the rules are strict, they are procedural; as long as your pet's rabies vaccination is valid and the microchip is working, a document can almost always be re-issued to match a new travel window.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for document timing mismatches for non-commercial travel of dogs, cats, and ferrets. It does not cover commercial imports, rescue animal transfers, or specific requirements for countries outside the EU/UK framework.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or veterinary advice. Pet travel regulations are subject to strict enforcement at international borders and requirements may change. Always verify your specific route requirements with the Animal and Plant Health Agency (APHA) or the destination's competent authority.
