# What to do if… a colleague is pressuring you to meet alone in a hotel room (UK)

## Short answer
Move to a staffed public place now, such as reception, the lobby, or a busy café, and do not meet them alone in a room. Tell a work contact that you have said no, feel unsafe, and need help staying separate.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to their room to keep the peace or avoid awkwardness.
- Do not keep negotiating, apologising, or explaining if they are ignoring your no.
- Do not move into an isolated place with them, such as a room, quiet corridor, or lift, if you can move to a staffed area.
- Do not blame yourself for being polite, freezing, or trying not to make things worse.
- Do not delete messages, call logs, or room-service records in the heat of the moment.
- Do not feel you must report to police or make a formal workplace complaint tonight.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Go to hotel reception, the lobby, a staffed bar, a busy café, or another public place. If you are in your room, lock the door, use the door chain if there is one, do not open the door to them, and call reception.
2. **Use one short refusal and stop debating.** You can say:
   - “No. I’m not meeting in a hotel room.”
   - “I’m staying in the lobby.”
   - “This is not appropriate. Please stop contacting me tonight.”
   You do not owe a detailed reason.
3. **Ask hotel staff for practical separation.** Tell reception: “Another guest from my work group is pressuring me to meet alone. I do not want contact with them. Can you help me stay separate?” Ask them to screen calls to your room, avoid giving out your room number, help with a room move if available, or arrange a staff escort between your room, lifts, and reception.
4. **Tell your workplace while it is happening, or as soon as you are in a safer place.** Contact your line manager, HR contact, travel contact, security contact, event organiser, or another trusted senior colleague. Use factual wording: “A colleague is pressuring me to meet alone in a hotel room. I said no. I feel unsafe and need support.” Ask for concrete help: a different room or hotel, separate transport, changed seating or schedule, and no one-to-one contact with that colleague.
5. **Create separation for the rest of the trip.** Use reception as the meeting point for any necessary work contact. Ask a trusted colleague or hotel staff member to walk with you if you need to move through corridors or lifts. Do not attend after-hours drinks, private meetings, or travel arrangements that would put you alone with the colleague.
6. **Make a short private note.** Write down the date, time, location, what they said or did, how you responded, and who may have seen or heard it. Save screenshots of messages, missed calls, voicemails, emails, room-call logs, or work-chat messages.
7. **If anything sexual happened without your consent, or you are not sure, consider confidential specialist support.** NHS and sexual assault referral services can usually offer medical, practical, and emotional support. You can ask about options without committing to a police report.
8. **Call emergency services if you feel in immediate danger.** In the UK, call 999 if someone is in immediate danger, violence is being threatened, or a crime is happening now. For non-emergency police contact or advice, call 101 or use the police online contact route.
9. **Use specialist support if you need to steady yourself or talk through options.** Rape Crisis England & Wales runs the 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line for people aged 16+ in England and Wales. You can call 0808 500 2222 or use online chat; it is not an emergency service.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to make a formal grievance, change jobs, confront them, or report to police.
- You do not need to write a perfect statement now; a brief note and saved messages are enough for the moment.
- You do not need to prove whether it “counts” as harassment before asking for separation and support.
- You do not need to smooth things over, get an apology, or meet them privately to clear the air.
- You do not need to decide long-term work consequences while you are still trying to feel safe.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, being overly polite, laughing it off, or feeling confused can happen under pressure. Your immediate job is not to find the perfect words; it is to stay away from isolation, keep control of where you are, and bring other people into the situation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stabilise the situation and reduce immediate risk. Later decisions about workplace complaints, medical care, police contact, employment rights, or travel changes may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, employment, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or a life-threatening emergency, call 999.
