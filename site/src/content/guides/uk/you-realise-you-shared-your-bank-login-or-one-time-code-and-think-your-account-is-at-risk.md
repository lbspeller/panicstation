# What to do if… you realise you shared your bank login or one-time code and think your account is at risk (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately using a trusted route—your banking app, the number on the back of your card, or by dialling 159—and tell them you have shared security details. Request an immediate lock on your digital banking to prevent unauthorized transfers.

## Do not do these things
- Do not share any further codes, passwords, or "approve" any mobile app notifications.
- Do not use any phone number provided by a caller or sent via text/email, even if the caller ID appears to be your bank.
- Do not move money to a "safe account" or any other account for "protection"—banks will never ask you to do this.
- Do not grant anyone remote access to your phone or computer or install software (like AnyDesk or TeamViewer) to "fix" the issue.
- Do not assume your account is safe just because no money has left yet; fraudsters often wait for a quiet period to move funds.
- Do not delete the fraudulent text or call log, as these are evidence for the police.

## What to do now
1. **End all current contact with the suspected fraudster and use a secure line.**  
   Hang up the phone or close the chat immediately. Contact your bank using one of these verified methods:
   - **Dial 159** from a UK phone (this connects you directly to the fraud departments of 99% of UK banks).
   - Use the official phone number printed on the back of your physical bank card.
   - Use the "Contact Us" or "Report Fraud" feature inside your official mobile banking app.

2. **Tell the bank's fraud team exactly what was shared and request a security lockdown.**  
   State clearly: "I have shared my online banking login/one-time passcode and believe my account is at risk." Ask the agent to:
   - **Disable digital and mobile banking access** until security credentials are reset.
   - **Check for recently added payees** or changes to your contact details (phone/email).
   - **Block or recall any pending or recent outgoing payments** that you did not authorize.
   - **Provide a reference number** for your report and a direct number to call back if the line drops.

3. **Secure your associated recovery "keys" from a clean device.**  
   If you have a separate, secure device (like a tablet or a different phone), use it to:
   - Change your **email account password**, as scammers often use email access to intercept bank password resets.
   - Update your **online banking password** and "memorable information" once the bank confirms it is safe to do so.
   - Log out of all active sessions across all devices in your banking settings.

4. **Report the incident to the national fraud reporting service.**  
   Official reporting helps the police track criminal networks.
   - If you are in **England, Wales, or Northern Ireland**, report to **Report Fraud (formerly Action Fraud)** via their website or by calling 0300 123 2040.
   - If you are in **Scotland**, report the crime to **Police Scotland by dialling 101**.

5. **Apply for additional identity protection if personal data was exposed.**  
   If you shared more than just a code (such as your address, DOB, or passport details), consider:
   - **Cifas Protective Registration**, which places a flag on your credit file to ensure lenders carry out extra checks before opening new accounts in your name.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to close the account permanently today; focus on the temporary lock first.
- You do not need to confront the scammer or reply to their messages to "tell them off."
- You do not need to make a formal complaint about how the bank handled the call yet; prioritize securing the funds.
- You do not need to replace your physical cards unless you shared the card number, CVV, or PIN.

## Important reassurance
Scammers use high-pressure tactics and sophisticated technology to bypass your natural caution. Feeling overwhelmed or embarrassed is a common reaction, but it does not prevent you from taking control now. Acting immediately via a trusted number like 159 is the most effective way to stop a scam in progress.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for UK banking customers who have shared credentials. It does not cover long-term credit repair, legal disputes with banks, or recovery of funds already moved, which require specialist advice once the account is secured.

## Important note
This guide is for information only and does not constitute financial or legal advice. If you are in immediate physical danger or being threatened by a fraudster in person, call 999 immediately.
