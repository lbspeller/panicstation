# What to do if… you are being pressured into unwanted sexual acts to avoid conflict (UK)

## Short answer
You do not need to “compromise” on sexual acts to prevent conflict. Create space now, keep your phone and way out available, and contact specialist support so you are not handling this alone.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to sexual acts just to keep the peace, stop an argument, or stop repeated asking.
- Do not debate, justify, or negotiate your consent while you feel pressured, scared, frozen, or worn down.
- Do not treat guilt, sulking, threats to leave, or accusations as a problem you must fix by giving in.
- Do not let someone take your phone, block the door, stop you leaving, or stop you contacting someone.
- Do not blame yourself for freezing, complying, changing your words, or doing what felt safest in the moment.
- Do not delete messages or call logs in a panic if keeping them is safe.

## What to do now
1. **Make a clear pause happen.** Use a short line such as “No”, “Stop”, “I am not doing that”, or “I need to leave the room.” Do not add reasons if reasons will invite more pressure.
2. **Move toward a safer pause.** Go to a doorway, hallway, bathroom, outside space, neighbour, shop, station, or any place where you are less isolated. Keep your phone, keys, bag, shoes, and route out near you if you can.
3. **If you are in immediate danger, call 999.** If you call 999 from a mobile and cannot speak, follow the operator instructions; when prompted, press 55 to show the call is a real emergency.
4. **Bring another person into the situation remotely.** Call or message someone you trust and say something concrete, such as “I need you on the phone now” or “I need help leaving safely.” Staying connected can reduce pressure and make it easier to get away from the immediate situation.
5. **Contact specialist sexual violence support.** If something sexual happened without your consent, or you are unsure, you can contact the 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line on 0808 500 2222 or use online chat. It is free, confidential, for people aged 16 or over in England and Wales, and it is not an emergency service.
6. **Find local rape and sexual abuse support if that line is not the right route for where you are.** The Survivors Trust can help you find specialist rape and sexual abuse services in the UK.
7. **Consider medical or practical support from a SARC.** A Sexual Assault Referral Centre can offer medical, practical, emotional, and forensic support. You can contact a SARC even if you do not want to report to police, and any examination is your choice.
8. **Keep options open only if it is safe.** If you may want medical or forensic support, try not to wash, change clothes, or delete messages until you have spoken to a clinician or specialist support worker, but do not do anything that increases danger or distress.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide now whether to report to police.
- You do not have to decide now what to call what happened.
- You do not have to confront the person with perfect wording.
- You do not have to decide the future of the relationship today.
- You do not have to explain yourself to mutual friends, family, or anyone pressuring you for details.

## Important reassurance
Pressure, persistence, guilt, fear, and “compromise to avoid conflict” are not the same as freely choosing sexual activity. Freezing, going along with something, or saying what you thought would end the pressure can be a survival response, not a failure.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you reach a safer pause, steady yourself, and connect with specialist support. Later decisions about reporting, boundaries, housing, the relationship, legal steps, or medical follow-up may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or need urgent medical help, call 999. If you feel at risk of harming yourself, you can call Samaritans free on 116 123, any time, day or night.
