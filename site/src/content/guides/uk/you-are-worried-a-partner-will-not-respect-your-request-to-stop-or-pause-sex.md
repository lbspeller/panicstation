# What to do if… you are worried a partner will not respect your request to stop or pause sex (UK)

## Short answer
Choose the safer pause: do not continue or start sex right now if you can safely avoid it. Your immediate priority is getting space, keeping access to help, and contacting specialist support if anything sexual happened without your consent or you are unsure.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force yourself to continue because stopping feels awkward, embarrassing, or difficult to explain.
- Do not use alcohol or drugs to make the situation easier to tolerate.
- Do not agree to be alone somewhere you cannot easily leave, contact someone, or reach your phone.
- Do not treat arguing, sulking, pressure, anger, or "just a bit more" as something you have to manage by continuing.
- Do not assume you must label what happened, confront them, or report anything before asking for support.

## What to do now
1. **Choose distance if you can.** If you are not confident they will stop when asked, avoid starting or continuing sex with them right now. You do not need a better reason than not feeling safe.
2. **If you are already with them, aim for the safest pause available.** Create space, stop moving further into the sexual situation, get dressed if you can, and move toward your phone, a door, another person, or a more public place.
3. **Use short wording if speaking feels safe.** Say only what is needed, such as: "Stop." "I am not continuing." "I am getting dressed now." "I need space." You do not have to debate it.
4. **Get another person into the loop.** Message or call someone you trust with a simple line such as: "I feel unsafe and need help leaving." If you cannot safely explain, send your location or ask them to call you.
5. **Keep control of practical exits before you are alone together again.** Keep your phone with you, arrange your own way home, meet somewhere you can leave from, and tell a trusted person where you are. If those basics cannot happen safely, treat that as a reason not to be alone with them.
6. **If anything sexual happened without consent, or you are not sure, you can ask for help without deciding about police now.** Support routes differ across the UK, but sexual assault services and rape crisis services can usually talk through medical care, emotional support, forensic options, and reporting choices at your pace.
7. **If you are in immediate danger, call 999.** If you are not in immediate danger, use a specialist support service for your part of the UK or contact NHS services for urgent health concerns.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether the relationship continues.
- You do not need to decide now whether to report to police.
- You do not need to write a perfect account, collect proof, or work out the exact legal label before getting support.
- You do not need to persuade them to understand your boundary before you protect your own safety.

## Important reassurance
Wanting to pause or stop is enough. Freezing, going quiet, complying out of fear, or feeling confused afterwards are common responses to pressure or threat, and they do not mean you agreed freely.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, reducing pressure, and connecting with support. Later decisions may need help from specialist sexual violence, domestic abuse, medical, legal, safeguarding, or advocacy services.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, safeguarding, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If something sexual happened without your consent or you are unsure, specialist services can help you talk through options without forcing you to report.
