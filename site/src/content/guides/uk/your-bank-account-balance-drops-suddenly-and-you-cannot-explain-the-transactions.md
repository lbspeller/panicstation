# What to do if… your bank account balance drops suddenly and you cannot explain the transactions (UK)

## Short answer
Freeze your cards and account access immediately via your banking app, then contact your bank's fraud department using the official number on the back of your card. Prioritise stopping further activity before attempting to reconcile the missing funds.

## Do not do these things
- Don't use any phone numbers or links provided in a text or email notification about the balance drop.
- Don't share full PINs, passwords, or one-time passcodes (OTPs) with anyone, even if they claim to be from the bank's fraud team.
- Don't move remaining money to a "safe account" suggested by a caller; banks will never ask you to move money to protect it.
- Don't install software like AnyDesk or TeamViewer to allow a "representative" to help you secure the account.
- Don't delete any suspicious messages or call logs, as these are evidence for the investigation.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the account and cards immediately**
   - Use your banking app to **freeze all debit and credit cards** and disable "online payments" or "ATM withdrawals" if the app allows.
   - If you cannot access your app, **call the lost and stolen cards number** printed on your physical card or found on your bank's official website.

2. **Call your bank using a verified secure route**
   - Call the **number on the back of your card** or dial **159** (the secure hotline for most UK banks) to be connected to the fraud department.
   - If you were on a suspicious call just before noticing the drop, **use a different phone** to make this call to ensure the line was not held open by the scammer.

3. **Provide specific details of the unexplained activity**
   - Tell the bank precisely which transactions you do not recognise, including the **date, amount, and merchant name/reference**.
   - State clearly if you believe your **security credentials** (app PIN or online password) have been compromised.

4. **Request immediate protective blocks**
   - Ask the bank to **cancel and replace all affected cards** and to log out all other devices from your mobile banking.
   - Check for and **remove any newly added digital wallets** (like Apple Pay or Google Pay) that you did not authorise.

5. **Utilise the Direct Debit Guarantee for unauthorised mandates**
   - If the money left via an unauthorised Direct Debit, inform the bank you are claiming an **immediate refund under the Direct Debit Guarantee**.
   - Cancel the mandate in your app or over the phone to prevent further collections.

6. **Log an Authorised Push Payment (APP) scam claim if applicable**
   - If you were tricked into sending the money yourself (e.g., via a "safe account" scam), tell the bank this was an **APP scam**.
   - Note that for most bank transfers, there is a **mandatory reimbursement limit of £85,000**, though banks may apply a **£100 excess** to the claim.

7. **Update your wider digital security**
   - Change your **email password** immediately, as hackers often use email access to intercept banking notifications.
   - Enable **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** on your email and banking profiles using an authenticator app rather than SMS where possible.

8. **Report the incident to the national authorities**
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** Report to **Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) online or by calling 0300 123 2040.
   - **Scotland:** Report to **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
   - If you are currently being threatened or a crime is in progress, call **999**.

9. **Secure your identity for the long term**
   - Check your credit report via **Experian, Equifax, or TransUnion** for any searches you do not recognise.
   - Consider applying for **Cifas Protective Registration** (costs £30 for 2 years) to place a flag on your credit file that triggers extra identity checks.

10. **Report the phishing source**
   - Forward suspicious texts to **7726**.
   - Forward suspicious emails to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.

## What can wait
- Calculating exactly how the breach happened; your first priority is stopping the leak.
- Contacting the merchants shown on the statement; the bank’s fraud team handles the primary recovery process.
- Closing all other unrelated accounts; focus on the compromised account first.
- Paying for "private recovery experts" who claim they can get your money back faster for a fee.

## Important reassurance
Seeing money vanish from your account is a high-stress event, but UK banking regulations provide significant protections for victims of unauthorised transactions and scams. Most banks have 24/7 fraud teams specifically trained to stabilize your account and guide you through the recovery process.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for UK banking fraud. It does not cover long-term credit repair, complex legal disputes, or Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) appeals.

## Important note
This information is for guidance and does not constitute financial or legal advice. Banks may "stop the clock" on the standard 5-day reimbursement window for up to 35 business days if they require further investigation. Always use official, independently verified contact details for your financial institution.
