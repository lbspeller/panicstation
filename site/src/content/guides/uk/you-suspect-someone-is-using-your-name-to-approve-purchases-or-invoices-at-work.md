# What to do if… you suspect someone is using your name to approve purchases or invoices at work (UK)

## Short answer
Stop using the approval function, report it in writing to your manager and Finance or Accounts Payable, and ask them to suspend approvals or hold payments linked to your name while the audit trail is preserved.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the person you suspect or make accusations in a group chat, open email thread, or meeting.
- Do not re-approve, backdate, edit, or delete records to make anything look consistent.
- Do not delete emails, approval notifications, messages, screenshots, or log entries.
- Do not forward invoices, supplier bank details, system screenshots, or personal data to a personal account.
- Do not keep approving purchases or invoices just to avoid making a fuss.
- Do not wait for complete proof if approvals or payments may still be happening.

## What to do now
1. **Pause anything that could add to the problem:**  
   Stop using the approval function for the affected system. Ask Finance, Accounts Payable, or Procurement to temporarily suspend approvals under your user ID or name, require a second approver, and hold any pending payments linked to approvals in your name until they have checked them.

2. **Report it to the right internal owners in writing:**  
   Email your manager and Finance or Accounts Payable. Include only facts you can point to: invoice numbers, purchase order numbers, supplier names, amounts if visible, dates, times, system names, and where your name appears. Ask them to confirm in writing that they have started a review.

3. **Ask IT to secure your account and preserve access evidence:**  
   Contact IT or the service desk and ask them to reset your password, confirm MFA or 2FA is enabled, check for mailbox rules or forwarding, check suspicious logins, and check delegated access or “approve on behalf of” permissions. Ask them to preserve relevant logs rather than investigating the system yourself.

4. **Keep a simple record without changing evidence:**  
   Make a short timeline on an approved work system: when you noticed the issue, what you saw, and who you told. Keep approval notifications and screenshots only where workplace policy allows, and do not alter the finance or procurement records.

5. **Ask Finance or Procurement to check the controls around your name:**  
   Ask them to check the approval matrix, workflow roles, delegated authority settings, recent supplier bank-detail changes, duplicate invoices, and invoices split into smaller amounts. Also ask them to check whether your name appears because of a workflow role rather than your actual login.

6. **Ask for a temporary protective arrangement:**  
   Ask for your approval role to be removed, paused, or routed to another named approver while the issue is reviewed. Ask for the interim arrangement to be confirmed in writing so you are not pressured to approve items you cannot verify.

7. **Use a safer reporting route if normal line management is part of the concern:**  
   If the issue may involve your manager, senior staff, or retaliation risk, use your employer’s whistleblowing, fraud, compliance, audit, or confidential reporting channel if one exists. In the UK, whistleblowing may be protected in some circumstances, but keep the report factual and avoid naming someone as responsible unless you have clear evidence.

8. **Ask who is handling any external fraud report:**  
   If this appears to be fraud rather than an internal mistake, ask your employer who will report it externally. If you are personally affected, cannot safely use internal routes, or need to make your own report, Report Fraud is the official route for England, Wales, and Northern Ireland. If you live in Scotland or the incident happened there, use Police Scotland, usually via 101. If there is an immediate emergency, call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to resign, confront anyone, threaten legal action, or make a formal grievance.
- You do not need to identify the person responsible before reporting the issue.
- You do not need to prove fraud before asking for approvals to be paused and logs preserved.
- You can wait on wider personal identity steps, such as credit checks, unless you see signs your identity is being used outside work.

## Important reassurance
Finding your name on approvals you do not recognise can feel frightening, especially if you worry you may be blamed. Prompt, factual reporting and log preservation are reasonable protective steps and help separate what you actually did from what the system shows.

## Scope note
These are first steps to contain possible misuse, preserve evidence, and protect you from being wrongly linked to approvals. Later decisions about investigations, disciplinary processes, payment recovery, whistleblowing, or grievances may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general first-step information, not legal, financial, IT-security, employment, or other professional advice. Workplace systems and reporting routes vary by employer and sector. If you are pressured to approve or sign off anything you do not understand, it is reasonable to pause and ask for the issue to be handled through documented internal channels.
