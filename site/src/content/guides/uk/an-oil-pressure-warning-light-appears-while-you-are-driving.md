# What to do if… an oil pressure warning light appears while you are driving (UK)

## Short answer
Treat an oil pressure warning as a stop-engine warning. Keep the engine off, get yourself to a place of relative safety, and arrange help rather than trying to restart.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep driving to get home, reach a garage, or see whether the warning clears.
- Do not keep cranking or restarting the engine to test it.
- Do not rev the engine to try to build pressure.
- Do not put a warning triangle out on a motorway.
- Do not attempt roadside repairs on a motorway or other high-speed road.
- Do not go under the car near traffic, or while it is only supported by the car’s jack.
- Do not assume the engine is safe because it sounded normal for a few seconds.

## What to do now
1. **Reach the safest pause you can without ignoring immediate danger.** Indicate, slow smoothly, and aim for a service area, lay-by, car park, emergency area, or hard shoulder if there is no safer option. Put your hazard warning lights on.
2. **Switch the engine off and keep it off.** If the engine has already stopped, do not restart it.
3. **If you are stopped in a dangerous position, treat that as an emergency.** If you are in or partly in a live lane, cannot get left, or cannot reach a place of relative safety, keep hazards on and call 999. Only restart as a last resort if you are in immediate personal danger and emergency services cannot guide you first; move only as far as needed to reduce that danger, then switch off again.
4. **On a motorway or other high-speed road, get people safe before looking at the car.**
   - If it is safe and possible, leave by the side furthest from traffic and wait well away from the vehicle, behind a safety barrier where there is one.
   - If you cannot leave safely, stay in the vehicle with seat belts on and hazards on, then call 999.
   - If your vehicle has an SOS or eCall button, you can use it to ask for emergency help.
5. **Use the best location help available.** If you can safely use an emergency roadside telephone, use it because it helps identify where you are. If using a mobile, give the road name, direction of travel, nearest junction, marker post, driver location sign, landmark, or service area.
6. **Check only what you can check safely.** If you are well away from traffic on level ground, keep the engine off, wait a few minutes, then look for obvious oil leaking under the engine area and check the dipstick if you know how. A very low level or visible leak means do not restart; a normal oil level still does not prove the engine is safe to restart or drive.
7. **Call for recovery or roadside assistance.** Tell them you have an oil pressure warning and are not restarting the engine. If you feel unsafe where you are, call 999 instead.
8. **Be ready to describe the warning exactly.** Say whether it was red, flashing, said “oil pressure” or “stop engine”, whether the engine cut out, whether there is smoke, a burning smell, knocking, or visible oil on the ground.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the problem is a sensor, pump, filter, wiring fault, oil level issue, or leak at the roadside.
- You do not need to decide repair costs, garage choice, or whether the car is worth fixing right now.
- You do not need to try dashboard resets, repeated restarts, or internet fixes before getting the vehicle recovered.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel unsure when the car seems as if it might still run. With an oil pressure warning, keeping the engine off is a cautious and sensible default that protects both safety and the engine.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: get to a safer pause, avoid worsening engine damage, and arrange help. A recovery service, garage, or qualified mechanic may need to inspect the vehicle and may need to measure actual oil pressure before it is driven again.

## Important note
This guide is general information for immediate safety and harm-prevention, not mechanical, legal, insurance, or professional advice. Dashboard warnings vary by make and model; check your vehicle handbook when you can, but do not restart at the roadside unless a professional route or your handbook clearly says it is safe and your personal safety is not at risk.
