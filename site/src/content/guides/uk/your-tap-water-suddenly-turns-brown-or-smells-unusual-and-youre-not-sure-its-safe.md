# What to do if… your tap water suddenly turns brown or smells unusual and you’re not sure it’s safe (UK)

## Short answer
Stop using the tap water for drinking or food preparation and contact your water supplier immediately to check for local incidents or "do not drink" notices.

## Do not do these things
- Don't drink the water or use it for baby formula, tooth brushing, or pets until you have verified it is safe.
- Don't run hot taps, showers, or water-dependent appliances like dishwashers, as this can pull sediment into your internal heating system and cause damage.
- Don't boil the water as a "fix-all" — boiling kills bacteria but can concentrate chemical contaminants or metals like lead and iron.
- Don't ignore smells that resemble petrol, diesel, or strong chemicals; these require immediate isolation of the supply.
- Don't assume the water is safe just because the discolouration is faint or "straw-coloured."

## What to do now
1. **Switch to bottled or stored safe water for all oral consumption and hygiene.** Use this for drinking, cooking, making ice, and cleaning any equipment used for infant feeding or by those with weakened immune systems.
2. **Perform a "bottom-up" check if the water is white or cloudy.** Fill a glass and let it stand for two minutes; if it clears from the bottom upwards, the "discolouration" is just harmless air bubbles and the water is safe to use once clear.
3. **Run the first incoming cold tap at a slow, pencil-thin trickle.** Use the cold tap closest to your internal stop tap (usually the kitchen tap) and let it run for up to 20 minutes to see if it clears sediment from your service pipe.
   - If it clears, run it for another minute then use as normal.
   - If it does not clear after 20 minutes, stop and move to the next step.
4. **Check with your immediate neighbours to see if they are affected.** If your neighbours have clear water, the issue is likely restricted to your internal plumbing or your specific service pipe; if they are also affected, it is a mains supply issue.
5. **Contact your water supplier's 24-hour emergency line.** Inform them of the colour, any specific smell (e.g., metallic, chlorine, earthy, or chemical), and whether the issue is affecting neighbours.
   - Ask if there is a "Boil Water" or "Do Not Drink" notice in effect for your postcode.
   - Ask for a reference number for your report.
6. **Report issues with private water supplies (wells or boreholes) to your local council.** Contact the Environmental Health department to report a sudden change in quality and request guidance on testing or temporary alternatives.
7. **Take a photo of the discoloured water in a clear glass against a white background.** Note the exact time the change was first noticed to provide evidence if the supplier needs to investigate a recurring issue.

## What can wait
- You don't need to arrange for a private plumber until you have confirmed with the water supplier that the fault is not on their network.
- You don't need to replace water filters or jugs until the source issue is resolved, as new filters will be instantly clogged by sediment.
- You don't need to drain your hot water cylinder or header tank unless specifically advised by a professional after the mains supply is clear.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for water to turn brown or orange after a nearby burst pipe or maintenance work, as the change in pressure disturbs harmless iron or manganese deposits. While it looks alarming, it is usually a temporary physical change rather than a permanent contamination. Taking a pause and checking with your supplier is the correct and safest response.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for sudden water quality changes. It does not replace official notices from water companies or local authorities, which must be followed over these general instructions.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute medical or legal advice. If you feel unwell after consuming discoloured or unusual-smelling water, contact NHS 111 or your GP. In the event of a suspected major chemical or fuel leak into the water system, evacuate the immediate area if smells are overpowering and follow emergency services' directions.
