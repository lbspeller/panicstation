# What to do if… your manager repeatedly pressures you to respond immediately when you are off the clock (UK)

## Short answer
Unless you are on an agreed on-call arrangement or there is a genuine emergency cover arrangement, you usually should not treat every out-of-hours message as automatically urgent. Set one clear written boundary, keep a simple record, and ask for the expectation to be confirmed in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete messages, call logs, emails, or screenshots to keep the peace.
- Do not reply in anger late at night or at the weekend.
- Do not casually agree that you are “on call” without written clarity on hours, duties, pay, time off in lieu, and who authorises it.
- Do not silently start working longer and longer days just to avoid conflict.
- Do not share the messages publicly or post about them on social media.
- Do not resign, threaten legal action, or send a long complaint while you are panicked.

## What to do now
1. **Make a simple record.**  
   Write down the date and time of each contact, the channel used, what your manager asked for, whether they demanded an immediate reply, whether you replied, and how long the work took. Keep screenshots or copies where you can.

2. **Check what is already agreed.**  
   Look at your contract, rota, staff handbook, and any policy on working hours, overtime, on-call work, flexible working, or work communications. Check whether you are actually required to be available outside working hours, what counts as urgent, and whether there is any agreed payment or time off in lieu.

3. **Send one calm boundary message in writing.**  
   Keep it short. For example: “To avoid misunderstanding, I will respond during my working hours unless I am on an agreed on-call rota or we have agreed urgent cover in advance. If out-of-hours cover is needed, please confirm the arrangement in writing.”

4. **Ask for the expectation to be made specific.**  
   If your manager says you must reply immediately, ask: “Please confirm which hours I am expected to be available, what counts as urgent, how this will be recorded as working time, and what pay or time off arrangement applies.”

5. **Record any effect on rest.**  
   If repeated contact is stopping you from switching off, sleeping, or getting rest between shifts, write that down. UK workers are usually entitled to daily and weekly rest, but exceptions can apply, so keep the note factual rather than arguing the law in the moment.

6. **Reduce snap-replies while keeping evidence.**  
   Use Do Not Disturb outside working hours, mute non-urgent work app notifications, or remove work alerts from your personal phone if that is allowed in your workplace. Do not delete the messages.

7. **Escalate if the pressure continues.**  
   If your manager keeps pressing for instant replies, send the log and your short boundary message to HR, a more senior manager, or your union representative if you have one. Ask for a written answer to whether immediate out-of-hours responses are required and, if so, under what formal arrangement.

8. **Use the grievance route if informal steps do not stop it.**  
   If the issue is not resolved, consider raising it under your workplace grievance procedure. If there is a grievance meeting, ask whether you can bring a companion; the statutory right to be accompanied applies to grievance meetings about legal or contractual duties, and many employers allow this more widely.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to quit.
- You do not need to prove every legal point before setting a basic boundary.
- You do not need to write a long complaint now; a short timeline and a clear request are enough.
- You do not need to answer every out-of-hours message just to show you are cooperative.
- You do not need to decide now whether this is bullying, unpaid overtime, or a working-time issue.

## Important reassurance
Repeated pressure to reply immediately outside working hours can make you feel constantly on alert. It is reasonable to ask for clear expectations, a workable boundary, and a written arrangement if your employer wants out-of-hours cover.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about a grievance, employment tribunal time limits, union support, health impact, or legal advice may need specialist help based on your contract, role, worker status, and workplace policies.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Workplace rights and the best next step can vary by your contract, job type, and circumstances.
