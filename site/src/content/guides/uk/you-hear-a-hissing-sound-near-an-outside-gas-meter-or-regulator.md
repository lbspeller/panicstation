# What to do if… you hear a hissing sound near an outside gas meter or regulator (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible gas escape: move people away from the meter or regulator area, then call the gas emergency number from a safe distance. For mains natural gas, call 0800 111 999 in England, Wales or Scotland, or 0800 002 001 in Northern Ireland.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t smoke, vape, or use any naked flame near the meter or regulator area.
- Don’t operate electrical switches, doorbells, or anything that could spark close to where you heard the hissing.
- Don’t try to find the leak with a lighter, flame, water, soapy water, spray, or by putting your face close to fittings.
- Don’t loosen, tighten, dismantle, or adjust the regulator, meter, pipework, or any fittings.
- Don’t go back and forth to double-check the sound once you’ve moved away.
- Don’t turn the gas back on yourself if it has been turned off.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance first.** Step away from the meter or regulator area and keep children, pets, visitors, and nearby neighbours back from it.
2. **Avoid ignition sources near the suspected leak area.** No smoking; don’t ring doorbells, press switches, use outdoor sockets, or start equipment close to where you heard the hissing.
3. **Move people into fresh air if gas may be near the building.** If you smell gas, the hissing is close to doors, windows, vents, or a shared entrance, calmly get anyone inside to leave without stopping to investigate.
4. **Call the gas emergency number from a safe location.** For mains natural gas, call 0800 111 999 in England, Wales or Scotland, or 0800 002 001 in Northern Ireland.
5. **If the supply is LPG,** use the emergency number shown on the bulk storage vessel, meter, cylinder supplier paperwork, or site information. If you cannot find it quickly, stay away from the suspected leak area and call 999 if there is immediate danger.
6. **Only if it is safe to approach and you are advised to do so,** turn off the gas at the emergency control valve by the meter. This is usually a quarter-turn so the handle is across the pipe. If the hissing is right at the meter or regulator and approaching would put you close to it, skip this and wait for the emergency engineer.
7. **Warn nearby neighbours if the meter or regulator is close to other homes.** If you need to alert someone very nearby, knock rather than using a doorbell near the suspected leak area.
8. **Wait for instructions and keep the area clear** until the emergency engineer or emergency services say it is safe.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it is definitely a leak, normal regulator noise, or a meter issue.
- You do not need to contact your gas supplier, landlord, or insurer until the immediate safety check is underway.
- You do not need to gather tools, take photos, record the sound, or troubleshoot the meter.
- You do not need to arrange repairs yourself before the emergency safety advice is given.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to take a faint hissing near a gas meter or regulator seriously. Reporting something that worries you is a normal safety step, even if it turns out not to be a dangerous leak.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and get the right emergency help. Later decisions about repairs, appliances, landlord responsibilities, insurance, or ongoing gas work may need a qualified gas professional or another specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, engineering, or other professional advice. If there is fire, an explosion risk, a collapse risk, injury, or someone is seriously unwell, move away and call 999.
