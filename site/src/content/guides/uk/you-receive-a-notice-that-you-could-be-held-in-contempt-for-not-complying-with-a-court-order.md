# What to do if… you receive a notice that you could be held in contempt for not complying with a court order (UK)

## Short answer
Identify the hearing date and the specific breach alleged immediately. If the notice mentions committal or imprisonment, seek legal representation as you may be entitled to legal aid regardless of the underlying civil case.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice as the court can proceed and issue a warrant for your arrest or imprisonment in your absence.
- Do not contact the judge or the other party's legal team to "explain" outside of the formal witness statement process.
- Do not destroy or alter any evidence related to your compliance or the reasons for non-compliance.
- Do not assume your right to silence prevents the court from drawing "adverse inferences" (assuming your silence implies you have no defense) if the case reaches a hearing.
- Do not attend the hearing without a copy of the original order and your evidence of compliance.

## What to do now
1. **Locate the specific alleged breach and the hearing date.** **The notice must clearly state what you were supposed to do, when you were supposed to do it, and how you failed.**
   - Note the court claim number and the division (e.g., King's Bench, Family Court, or County Court).
2. **Determine your specific UK jurisdiction.** **Procedures for contempt vary between England & Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland.**
   - England & Wales: Governed by Civil Procedure Rules (CPR) Part 81.
   - Scotland: May involve a "Certification of non-compliance" to the Court of Session.
   - Northern Ireland: Governed by the Rules of the Court of Judicature (NI).
3. **Verify the presence of a Penal Notice.** **Under 2026 rules, a Penal Notice is a prominent warning added to the front of an order explaining that disobedience leads to imprisonment or fines.**
   - If no Penal Notice was served with the original order, this may be a primary defense to committal.
4. **Secure legal representation immediately.** **If there is a risk of imprisonment, you may qualify for "criminal" legal aid even if the main case is civil.**
   - In England & Wales, ask a solicitor about a "Representation Order" for committal proceedings.
   - If you have an existing solicitor, forward the notice to them within the same hour of receipt.
5. **Request a transcript or copy of the original order if you do not have it.** **You cannot defend a breach without knowing the exact wording of the instructions you are accused of ignoring.**
6. **Gather physical proof of any compliance attempts.** **Collect time-stamped evidence such as bank receipts, sent emails, delivery logs, or medical certificates that explain a delay.**
7. **Draft a timeline of events leading to the alleged breach.** **List every attempt you made to comply and the specific obstacles (e.g., technical failure, lack of information from the other party) that occurred.**
8. **Check for an "Adverse Inference" warning on the notice.** **The court is required to warn you that if you do not give evidence, it may conclude that your silence supports the accusation against you.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a formal "Application to Vary" the original order today, though you should prepare to do so if the order is impossible to follow.
- You do not need to respond to the merits of the original dispute; the contempt hearing is focused only on whether the order was breached.
- You do not need to prepare a closing argument; your initial priority is ensuring you have representation and a clear timeline of facts.

## Important reassurance
A notice of contempt is a procedural step, not an automatic conviction. In the UK, the "standard of proof" for committal (sending someone to prison) for contempt is the same as in a criminal trial: the court must be satisfied "beyond reasonable doubt" that you deliberately breached the order. If the breach was accidental or resulted from a genuine misunderstanding of an unclear order, the court has the power to dismiss the application or give you a further chance to comply.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate stabilization steps after receiving a notice of contempt or a committal application. It does not cover the long-term legal strategy for the underlying litigation or the specific defenses for criminal contempt of court.

## Important note
Contempt of court is a serious matter that can lead to a prison sentence of up to two years, an unlimited fine, or the seizure of assets. This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. You should consult a qualified solicitor or a law centre immediately upon receipt of any document mentioning "committal" or "contempt."
