# What to do if… you are asked to decide about moving a relative to comfort-focused care and you feel unprepared (UK)

## Short answer
Request a brief pause to clarify exactly which treatments will stop and which comfort measures will start, and verify who the legal decision-maker is for this specific medical choice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to a shift in care until you understand what specific interventions (such as fluids, antibiotics, or blood tests) are being stopped and why.
- Do not assume that being "next of kin" gives you the legal right to give or refuse consent; clinical teams in the UK must follow specific capacity frameworks (MCA 2005 in England/Wales, AWI 2000 in Scotland).
- Do not feel pressured to provide an immediate answer if the patient is stable and you have not yet seen a senior clinician (Consultant or Registrar).
- Do not interpret "comfort-focused care" as "no care"; it is an active shift toward managing symptoms like pain, breathlessness, and agitation.
- Do not conduct sensitive discussions in corridors or public ward areas; ask for a private room.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the observable cues that have triggered this conversation.**  
   **Ask the clinician to describe what they are seeing in your relative that makes this change necessary.** 
   - Observe if your relative is showing increased physical distress, such as labored breathing, restlessness, or a decreased level of consciousness.
   - Ask: "What specific medical change in the last 24 hours has led to this recommendation?"

2. **Verify your relative’s current mental capacity to make this decision.**  
   **Ask the doctor: "Do you believe my relative currently lacks the capacity to make this specific decision for themselves?"** 
   - If they have capacity, the decision is theirs. 
   - If they lack capacity, ask for the "Best Interests" process (England/Wales) or the "Section 47" status (Scotland) to be explained.

3. **Establish who the legally recognized decision-maker or consultee is.**  
   **Tell the team immediately if you know of any legal documents or appointments.** 
   - In England/Wales: Check for a Health and Welfare Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) or an Advance Decision to Refuse Treatment (ADRT).
   - In Scotland: Check for a Welfare Power of Attorney (WPA) or a Welfare Guardian.
   - In Northern Ireland: Identify the "Nominated Person" or check for any valid Advance Decision.

4. **Request a formal "Best Interests" or family meeting today.**  
   **Ask for the most senior doctor available and a member of the specialist palliative care team to be present.** 
   - If you cannot be there in person, request a scheduled video or telephone call where you can take notes.

5. **Ask for a clear definition of "Comfort-Focused Care" in this specific case.**  
   **Use a standard set of questions to reduce ambiguity.** 
   - "What active treatments are being withdrawn and what is the clinical risk of doing so?"
   - "What new medications will be started to prevent pain or breathlessness?"
   - "If a new, reversible problem occurs (like a minor infection), will that still be treated if it helps their comfort?"

6. **Separate the "Comfort Care" plan from the "Emergency" plan (CPR).**  
   **Ask if there is a ReSPECT form (or local equivalent) being updated and request to go through it section by section.** 
   - Ensure the distinction between "Do Not Attempt CPR" (DNACPR) and the broader goal of care is clear; a person can have a DNACPR in place but still receive active treatment for other issues.

7. **Escalate the situation if you feel the process is being rushed or is unsafe.**  
   **In England, ask to trigger "Martha’s Rule" if you have a serious concern about a sudden deterioration or a care plan you believe is harmful.** 
   - If you need help with communication or navigating rights, ask for the **Patient Advice and Liaison Service (PALS)** in England/Wales, or the **Patient Advice and Support Service (PASS)** in Scotland.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a funeral director or final arrangements right now.
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family disagreements about the relative's past wishes today; focus on the current clinical reality.
- You do not need to understand every medical term used; it is the clinician's job to explain them in plain English.
- You do not need to manage the emotions of other family members while you are gathering information.

## Important reassurance
It is entirely normal to feel overwhelmed when asked to shift the goal of care. This is a complex medical and legal transition, and your request for clarity is a vital part of safeguarding your relative. Choosing "comfort" is not "giving up"; it is an active choice to prioritize quality of life and dignity over treatments that the medical team believes are no longer beneficial.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for families in UK hospitals. It is intended to help you navigate the first few hours of a care transition and does not replace professional legal or medical advice regarding specific cases.

## Important note
Legal frameworks for capacity vary by jurisdiction: the Mental Capacity Act 2005 (England/Wales), the Adults with Incapacity (Scotland) Act 2000, and the Mental Capacity Act (Northern Ireland) 2016 (and common law) all have different specific requirements. Always ask the clinical team to document the legal basis for any "Best Interests" decision in the medical notes.
