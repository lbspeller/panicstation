# What to do if… your device asks for a different Apple ID or Google Account to update apps (UK)

## Short answer
Do not type a password for an Apple Account or Google Account you do not recognise. First check which account your device is using for app purchases and updates, then secure your accounts before deleting anything.

## Do not do these things
- Do not type a password just because a pop-up asks for it, especially for an account you do not recognise.
- Do not factory reset the device in a panic; it may make lockouts and data loss harder to recover from.
- Do not remove work, school, family, VPN, or device management profiles until you know what they control.
- Do not install “helper” apps, configuration profiles, remote-support tools, or update-fix downloads from unsolicited messages or calls.
- Do not use unofficial app stores or risky downloads just to make updates work.
- Do not delete an affected app unless you can accept losing local app data that is not backed up or synced.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and verify the prompt.** If this started after a link, attachment, new app, call, text, or “security alert”, treat it as suspicious until you have checked your settings yourself.

2. **Work out which pattern you are seeing.**
   - If only one or two apps ask for a different account, those apps may have been installed with a different Apple Account or Google Account.
   - If many apps ask, or you see an unfamiliar email address in settings, treat it as a possible account, device-management, or security problem.

3. **If this is an iPhone or iPad, check the Apple Account used for purchases.**
   - Open **Settings → your name → Media & Purchases** and check the account shown there.
   - Open the **App Store**, tap your profile picture, and check whether the account shown matches the one you expect.
   - If the prompt names an old or other Apple Account you recognise as yours, only sign in through Apple settings or the App Store, not through a strange link or message.
   - If only one app is affected and you no longer want to use the old account, delete and reinstall that app with your current Apple Account only if you can accept possible loss of local app data.

4. **If this is Android or Google Play, check which Google Account Play Store is using.**
   - Open **Play Store → profile icon → down arrow or account switcher** and check which account is selected.
   - Switch to the Google Account you normally use for apps and try the update again.
   - If one app appears tied to another Google Account you recognise, you may need to switch to that account to update it, or uninstall and reinstall it under your preferred account if you can accept possible loss of local app data.

5. **Check for work, school, or device management.**
   - On iPhone or iPad, check **Settings → General → VPN & Device Management**.
   - On Android, check **Settings → Passwords & accounts**, **Accounts**, **Work profile**, or **Device admin apps**; wording varies by device.
   - If this is a work or school device, stop and contact your IT helpdesk before removing anything.
   - If this is a personal device and you see a profile, work profile, administrator, or management item you do not recognise, take screenshots and secure your accounts before deciding whether to remove it.

6. **Secure the account before deeper troubleshooting.**
   - From a trusted device or browser you control, check that your recovery email and phone number are still yours.
   - Change the password for the affected Apple Account or Google Account if you suspect anyone else may know it.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication or 2-Step Verification if it is not already on.
   - Review signed-in devices and remove any device you do not recognise.

7. **If you entered details into a suspicious prompt, or you think fraud or cyber crime may be involved, preserve the basics.**
   - Save screenshots, dates, times, email addresses, phone numbers, and messages.
   - Contact your bank or card provider promptly if payment details may have been exposed.
   - For England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report cyber crime or fraud through Report Fraud.
   - For Scotland, contact Police Scotland on 101 for non-emergencies, or call 999 if a crime is happening now or someone is in immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to merge accounts, reorganise family sharing, or rebuild your app setup.
- You do not need to erase the device as a first step.
- You do not need to update every app immediately; start with account checks and account security.
- You do not need to remove work, school, VPN, or management settings until you know whether they are legitimate.

## Important reassurance
This can happen for ordinary reasons, such as an app installed years ago under an old account, a shared device, family purchases, or a work profile. Moving slowly helps you avoid entering a password in the wrong place, deleting useful data, or locking yourself out.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the situation, confirm which account is being used, and protect access. If you confirm an unknown management profile, a compromised account, or fraud, later decisions may need help from Apple, Google, your workplace or school IT team, your bank, or the police.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. Device menus and wording can vary by model, operating system version, and account setup; when in doubt, use official support routes and prioritise account security.
