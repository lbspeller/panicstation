# What to do if… a supervisor suggests your work shifts or opportunities depend on sexual attention (UK)

## Short answer
Create a safe pause and put distance between you and the supervisor. Write down what you can remember and keep any messages, rota, or scheduling evidence before it is lost or changed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel you have to agree, flirt, apologise, or make promises to protect your shifts; you can buy time without consenting.
- Do not meet them alone, especially off-site, after hours, in a private room, in a car, or somewhere isolated.
- Do not delete texts, DMs, emails, voicemails, call logs, rota changes, or scheduling messages, even if they are upsetting.
- Do not confront them in a place or moment where you could be put at risk.
- Do not sign warnings, agreements, settlement papers, confidentiality wording, or resignation documents on the spot.
- Do not start investigating or trying to catch them out; focus on safety, preserving what already exists, and getting support.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer, calmer position.** If you can, end the interaction with a short line such as: “I’m not comfortable with this. I need to get back to work.” Move to a public area, near coworkers, or somewhere you can leave. If you feel in immediate danger, call **999**.
2. **Make a private note while it is fresh.** Record the date, time, place, who was present, what was said or implied about shifts, schedule, opportunities, or work treatment, and how you responded. Keep it factual; brief notes are enough.
3. **Preserve what already exists.** Save screenshots or copies of messages, emails, call logs, voicemails, rota changes, shift allocations, meeting invites, and written comments. If safe and permitted, keep copies somewhere the supervisor cannot access. Avoid collecting other people’s private information unless you need it to show what happened.
4. **Reduce one-to-one exposure.** Ask a trusted colleague to stay nearby, keep any scheduling conversation in an open area, ask for meetings to include another person, or ask for scheduling to be handled by someone else.
5. **Use a written boundary to buy time.** If shifts, rota, hours, promotion, training, or opportunities are raised again, you can say: “Please put any scheduling decision and the reason for it in writing.” You do not need to debate it in the moment.
6. **Tell one safe person today.** Choose someone who can support you and help you stay grounded, such as a trusted colleague, friend, partner, union rep, or another manager who is not connected to the supervisor.
7. **Use a safer workplace route if you want to raise it.** Options may include HR, a senior manager not connected to the supervisor, a safeguarding or complaints contact, or a union rep. You can keep the first message minimal: “A scheduling decision was linked to sexual attention. I am asking for no one-to-one contact and for scheduling to be handled by someone else while this is addressed.”
8. **Ask for the employer’s process without committing to everything now.** You can ask for the harassment policy, grievance process, who will see your information, and what immediate protections can be put in place. Acas can explain workplace complaint and grievance options.
9. **If anything sexual happened without consent, or you feel physically unsafe, treat safety and medical support as the priority.** You can seek urgent medical care, contact a sexual assault referral centre, contact specialist sexual violence support, and/or contact police. Reporting is your choice unless there is an immediate emergency or another safeguarding risk.

*Optional, only if you may want to report later:* Try not to alter or add to the original messages. Keep what already exists and save copies.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a formal complaint, contact police, make a tribunal claim, resign, or take legal action.
- You do not need to prove everything right now; your immediate job is to stabilise, preserve what exists, and reduce risk.
- You do not need to write a perfect statement. A short factual note is enough for now.
- You do not need to speak to the supervisor alone to “clear it up”.

## Important reassurance
A supervisor linking shifts, schedules, hours, promotion, training, or work opportunities to sexual attention may be sexual harassment. Freezing, placating, feeling shocked, or being unsure what to do are common responses to pressure from someone with power over your work. You are allowed to set boundaries and ask for support.

## Scope note
These are first steps to protect your safety, preserve key information, and buy time. Later decisions about workplace complaints, legal options, medical care, reporting, or wellbeing support may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If you are unsure what to do next, consider specialist sexual violence support, a union rep if you have one, Acas, or independent legal advice.
