# What to do if… you are immunocompromised and develop a fever even if you otherwise feel okay (UK)

## Short answer
Treat a fever as urgent when your immune system is suppressed. Call your specialist urgent advice line now if you have one, or call NHS 111 if you do not; call 999 if you have emergency symptoms.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “sleep it off” or wait until morning just because you feel okay.
- Do not take fever-lowering medicine such as paracetamol before you have called for advice, unless your team has already told you to; it can mask a fever that needs urgent assessment.
- Do not go to a GP surgery, pharmacy, walk-in centre, or urgent treatment centre instead of your agreed specialist route, unless your specialist team or NHS 111 tells you to.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel faint, confused, very weak, breathless, or not right.
- Do not ignore shaking chills, sudden worsening, a very low temperature, or possible sepsis symptoms even if the temperature reading is not very high.

## What to do now
1. **Check and write down your temperature.**  
   Use a reliable thermometer. Note the number, the time, and how you took it, such as mouth, ear, or forehead. If the reading is clearly high, or you have shaking chills or feel suddenly worse, do not delay calling while you re-check.

2. **Use your specialist urgent contact route now.**  
   If you are under oncology, haematology, transplant, immunology, rheumatology, gastroenterology, or another team for immune-suppressing treatment, call the urgent advice number they gave you. Say: “I am immunocompromised and I have a fever.” Mention chemotherapy, transplant medicines, steroids, biologics, immune-suppressing tablets, a central line, or recent low white blood cells if any apply.

3. **If you do not have a specialist number, call NHS 111.**  
   Call 111 rather than relying only on online advice if you have a complex condition, a care plan, recent cancer treatment, transplant medicines, or you are not sure which route to use. Tell them you are immunocompromised before describing the fever.

4. **Treat your team’s temperature threshold as the one that matters.**  
   Follow any written fever plan, alert card, chemotherapy card, transplant card, or clinic instructions you were given. Cancer and neutropenic-sepsis pathways often treat fever as urgent at around 37.5°C or 38°C, and some also treat a low temperature as urgent, so do not wait for a higher reading if your plan says to call.

5. **Call 999 or go to A&E now if you have emergency symptoms.**  
   This includes acting confused, slurred speech, severe breathlessness or very fast breathing, blue, grey, very pale or blotchy skin, lips or tongue, collapse, fainting, or a rash that does not fade when pressed with a glass. Do not drive yourself to A&E; ask someone to drive you or call 999.

6. **Get ready in case you are told to attend hospital.**  
   Put together your alert card, medicines list, allergies, recent treatment dates, temperature readings, and details of any PICC line, port, Hickman line, transplant medicines, steroids, biologics, chemotherapy, or recent blood test results.

7. **Reduce avoidable exposure while arranging care.**  
   Wash your hands, avoid close contact and crowds, and avoid public transport if you feel unwell or might collapse. Follow the route your specialist team, NHS 111, or 999 gives you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause of the fever now.
- You do not need to decide whether to stop or change long-term medicines now, unless a clinician tells you to.
- You do not need to wait for more symptoms to prove it is serious.
- You do not need to search for every possible infection sign before calling.

## Important reassurance
Feeling mostly fine does not rule out a serious infection when your immune system is suppressed. Calling early is a normal and expected use of specialist advice lines, NHS 111, or emergency care.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours only. Later decisions about tests, antibiotics, admission, or changes to immune-suppressing treatment may need help from your specialist team or hospital clinicians.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical or professional advice. If you are immunocompromised, fever can be urgent even without other symptoms, so seek prompt medical assessment through your specialist team, NHS 111, or 999 in an emergency.
