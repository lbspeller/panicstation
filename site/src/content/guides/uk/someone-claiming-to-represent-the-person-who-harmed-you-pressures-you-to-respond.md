# What to do if… someone claiming to represent the person who harmed you pressures you to respond (UK)

## Short answer
Do not respond in the moment. Move to a safer pause, keep the message if you safely can, and contact specialist support or police if you feel threatened.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t argue, explain, apologise, negotiate, or try to set the record straight in messages.
- Don’t agree to a call, meeting, or quick chat to clear things up.
- Don’t click links, open unknown attachments, or share personal details such as your address, workplace, routine, or private messages.
- Don’t let a deadline or official-sounding wording rush you.
- Don’t call back a number they gave you to verify who they are.
- Don’t delete messages, voicemails, or call logs if you can keep them safely.

## What to do now
1. **Create a pause and get somewhere safer.** Put the phone down, move away from the person or place that makes you feel unsafe, and contact someone you trust if you do not want to be alone. If you feel in immediate danger, call **999**.

2. **Do not engage with the pressure.** You do not have to answer a stranger, explain yourself, correct them, or prove anything to them.

3. **Keep the contact if you safely can.** If it will not put you at more risk, keep the message, voicemail, email, call log, name, number, handle, and time received; then stop reading it.

4. **Reduce how much it can reach you.** Mute the conversation, silence unknown callers, or ask a trusted person to hold the phone for a while. If you need immediate relief, it is okay to block after keeping what you can safely keep.

5. **Treat “I represent them” as unverified.** Do not use a link, number, or email address they sent you to check them. If they claim to be a solicitor regulated in England and Wales, you can search the Solicitors Regulation Authority Solicitors Register yourself.

6. **Tell one trusted person what happened.** Send them the message or ask them to sit with you while you decide the next safe step. Pressure works best when you are isolated.

7. **Contact specialist support.** A sexual violence support service can help you think through safe next steps without pressuring you to report.

8. **If the contact feels threatening, harassing, or intimidating, consider contacting police.** For a non-emergency, call **101** or use Police.uk to report online where available. If there is already a police report or case, ask how to update the officer or team handling it.

9. **If this is part of wider harassment, stalking, domestic abuse, or repeated unwanted contact, ask for specialist advice about protection options.** You do not have to work out the legal route on your own before getting support.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to report, make a statement, apply for protection, or confront anyone.
- You do not need to craft the perfect reply.
- You do not need to justify your boundaries.
- You do not need to work out whether the message is legally valid before getting support.

## Important reassurance
Official-sounding pressure can make your body go into panic, freeze, shame, or confusion. Slowing down is allowed, and not replying immediately is a reasonable way to protect yourself.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the moment and prevent avoidable harm. Later decisions may need help from a specialist sexual violence service, an independent advocate, police, or a qualified legal adviser.

## Important note
This is general information only, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
