# What to do if… you receive a student loan default warning or a threat of collection action (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the warning as time-sensitive, but do not panic-pay. First check whether it is a Student Loans Company loan or a private loan, then contact the real organisation using official contact details and ask what would stop escalation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay immediately just because the notice says “final”, “default” or “collection”.
- Do not use contact details from a message or letter if anything looks suspicious.
- Do not share one-time passcodes, bank passwords, or full card details with someone who contacts you unexpectedly.
- Do not agree to a payment plan you cannot realistically keep.
- Do not ignore letters marked “arrears”, “default”, “pre-action” or “court”.
- Do not assume a debt pause scheme applies to a student loan; ask a free debt adviser what applies where you live.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and record the notice.** Write down who contacted you, how they contacted you, the reference number, the amount claimed, and exactly what action is threatened.
2. **Identify the type of student loan.**
   - If it is a UK government student loan, it is usually handled by Student Loans Company.
   - If it is a private loan used for study, it may follow ordinary credit and debt collection routes.
3. **Check whether repayments are already being taken.**
   - Look at your most recent payslips for student loan or postgraduate loan deductions.
   - Check your P60 if you have one.
   - Check your student loan repayment account if you can access it.
   - If deductions are being taken but the notice says you are in arrears, treat this as an urgent mismatch to raise with Student Loans Company.
4. **Verify the contact through an official route.**
   - If the notice says it is from Student Loans Company, use the official GOV.UK contact page.
   - If it says it is from a private lender or debt collector, independently find the firm’s official website or phone number before discussing the account.
5. **Ask one clear question.** Say: “What is my current status, and what action would stop escalation?” Ask them to confirm whether they mean arrears, default, collection activity, or court action.
6. **Ask for the claimed debt details in writing.** Ask for the creditor name, account reference, balance, dates covered, and the reason they say the amount is owed before you agree to any payment.
7. **If you have lived or worked outside the UK, deal with the overseas assessment first.** If you have been outside the UK for 3 months or more after your student loan entered repayment, Student Loans Company generally expects you to update your employment details and may expect repayments based on your overseas situation. Ask exactly what documents they need from you now.
8. **If you cannot afford the amount demanded, contact free debt advice.** Tell the lender or collector you are seeking debt advice and ask them to hold action while you do this. For a private lender or FCA-regulated collector, there may be rules about giving a reasonable pause while a debt counsellor helps with a repayment plan.
9. **Keep an evidence file.** Save the notice, envelopes, texts, emails, screenshots, call dates, names, and summaries of what was said.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to settle the whole balance, make extra payments, or borrow money to pay this. You can wait on long-term budgeting until you have confirmed the type of loan, the current status, the amount claimed, and the step that would stop escalation.

## Important reassurance
A default or collection warning can feel frightening, but the first job is not to solve the whole debt. The first job is to verify the notice, stop any avoidable escalation, and get the account corrected or paused where possible.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist debt advice, especially if the loan is private, the balance is disputed, or court papers have arrived.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, debt, or professional advice. Student loan rules vary by loan type, repayment plan, and where you live or work, so use official contact routes and free specialist debt advice before making commitments you cannot afford.
