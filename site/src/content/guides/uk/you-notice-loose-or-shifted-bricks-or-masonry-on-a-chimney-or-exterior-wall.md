# What to do if… you notice loose or shifted bricks or masonry on a chimney or exterior wall (UK)

## Short answer
Treat loose chimney or wall masonry as a falling-object risk. Keep people away from the area underneath and arrange an urgent assessment by a competent roofer, chimney specialist, builder, or structural engineer.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go onto the roof, climb ladders, lean out of windows, or try to touch the loose bricks.
- Don’t push, pull, remove, or chip at the masonry to test how loose it is.
- Don’t use foam, sealant, random mortar, or a quick patch to hold it in place.
- Don’t stand, park, or let anyone pass directly below the affected area.
- Don’t use a fireplace, stove, open fire, or appliance that vents through a chimney or flue you think may be damaged.
- Don’t delay calling for help if bricks are falling, the chimney or wall is moving, or the area below is public.

## What to do now
1. **Make a clear no-go zone.** Keep people and pets away from the ground below the loose or shifted masonry, including paths, doorways, driveways, gardens, and any area where pieces could fall. Use bins, chairs, cones, or tape if you can do this without going underneath the hazard.
2. **Move things only if it is safe.** Move cars, bikes, bins, garden furniture, and deliveries away from the fall zone only from the side, not from directly underneath.
3. **Stop using the chimney or flue if it may be involved.** Do not light a fire, stove, or open fire, and do not use an appliance that vents through the affected chimney or flue until it has been checked.
4. **Check from a safe distance.** Look for active falling debris, fresh cracks, a chimney stack leaning, a wall bulging outward, a widening gap, or masonry above a public pavement, road, shared entrance, or neighbour’s route.
5. **Call 999 if there is immediate danger to people.** Use 999 if bricks are falling now, the chimney or wall looks like it may collapse soon, someone could be hit, or you cannot keep people away.
6. **Report public risk to the local council.** If the masonry could affect a pavement, road, shared access, neighbouring property, or public area, contact your local council’s dangerous structures, building control, or building standards route. Many councils have an out-of-hours emergency route.
7. **Arrange an urgent professional assessment.** For chimney stacks, contact a competent roofer or chimney specialist. For bulging, leaning, cracked, or shifted exterior walls, contact a competent builder or structural engineer. Say what has moved, where it is, whether anything is falling, and whether it is above a public or shared route.
8. **Take evidence only from safety.** Take photos or video from ground level and from a safe distance. Note when you first noticed it and any recent high winds, storm, freezing weather, impact, nearby building work, or water damage.
9. **If you rent, tell your landlord or agent in writing now.** Say there is loose or shifted chimney or exterior wall masonry and that you have kept people away because of a falling-masonry risk.
10. **If you smell gas, suspect a gas leak, or a carbon monoxide alarm sounds, leave the property.** Call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999 from outside. Call 999 as well if anyone is unwell, has collapsed, or is in immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether it needs repointing, partial rebuilding, or a full rebuild.
- You do not need to measure the cracks, identify the exact cause, or choose matching bricks today.
- Cosmetic work, repainting, tidy repointing, and non-urgent upgrades can wait until the area has been made safe and assessed.

## Important reassurance
This is frightening because it is visible and overhead, but the first useful step is simple: keep people out of the fall zone. Once nobody is underneath it, you have bought time to get the right person to assess it.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The safe repair may need a roofer, chimney specialist, builder, structural engineer, landlord, insurer, or local council, depending on what has moved and where it could fall.

## Important note
This is general first-step information, not legal, building, engineering, gas-safety, medical, financial, insurance, or professional advice. If you think there is an immediate danger to people, use emergency services.
