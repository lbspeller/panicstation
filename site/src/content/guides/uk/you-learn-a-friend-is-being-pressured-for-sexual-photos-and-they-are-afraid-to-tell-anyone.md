# What to do if… you learn a friend is being pressured for sexual photos and they are afraid to tell anyone (UK)

## Short answer

Treat this as a safety issue, not a secret to manage alone. Stay calm, believe them, and help them reach one safe person or specialist service today.

## Do not do these things

- Do not tell them to “just ignore it” or “just block them” before checking whether that feels safe for them.
- Do not promise absolute secrecy if they may be in immediate danger, are under 18, or another child may be at risk.
- Do not confront the person pressuring them yourself.
- Do not ask to see, save, forward, copy, or store sexual photos or videos.
- Do not blame them for replying, sending anything before, or staying in contact.
- Do not take over their phone or accounts unless they ask you to stay with them while they do something.
- Do not post about it, message mutual friends, or try to “warn everyone” in the moment.

## What to do now

1. **Move the conversation somewhere calmer and more private.**  
   Ask simple questions like: “Are you safe right now?” and “Do you want me to stay with you while we tell one person?” If they are with the person pressuring them, being followed, threatened with violence, or a crime is happening right now, call **999**.

2. **Help them tell one safe person today, not everyone.**  
   Offer concrete options: a parent, carer, older sibling, school safeguarding or pastoral lead, teacher, university safeguarding or wellbeing team, or another adult they already trust. If speaking feels too hard, help them send a short message such as: “I need help with something sexual online and I’m scared. Can you stay calm and help me deal with it today?”

3. **Use specialist support instead of carrying this alone.**  
   If they are an adult in the UK and the pressure involves threats to share intimate images, the Revenge Porn Helpline supports adult victims of intimate image abuse in the UK, including threats to share images without consent. If they are not sure whether it fits, they can still check the helpline’s guidance.

4. **If they are under 18, or you think another child may be at risk, treat it as safeguarding today.**  
   Help them tell a trusted adult the same day, even if they are frightened to do that. If the pressure is happening online, CEOP is a UK route for reporting online sexual abuse or grooming involving children and young people. Childline can help them talk it through, and Report Remove may help if sexual images or videos of someone under 18 have already been shared online.

5. **Lower the immediate pressure without forcing big decisions.**  
   Sit with them while they mute notifications, turn off message previews, move away from the app, or block or report the account if that feels safe to them. The aim is to stop the stream of threats, not to push them into a confrontation.

6. **Keep only basic non-image details if they may want help later.**  
   If details are already visible, they can note the username, platform, date, and a brief description of what happened. Do not ask them to send you sexual images, and do not continue the conversation with the person pressuring them to “get proof”.

7. **Stay with them through the next handover.**  
   The goal right now is not to solve everything. The goal is to get them from being alone with this to being supported by one safer person or service.

## What can wait

They do not need to decide today whether to make a full police report, explain everything to lots of people, or work out the whole story. They also do not need to reply to the person pressuring them, defend themselves, or make any permanent decisions about friendships or relationships right now.

## Important reassurance

It is common for someone being sexually pressured or threatened to freeze, minimise it, or feel ashamed about telling anyone. Fear, silence, and mixed feelings do not mean they are okay with what is happening. Staying calm and helping them reach one safe person is already a meaningful step.

## Scope note

These are first steps only. Later choices about reporting, safeguarding, image removal, or ongoing support may need specialist help.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, safeguarding, or professional advice. In highly sensitive situations like this, the safest next step is usually support from a trusted adult or specialist service rather than trying to investigate or handle it alone.
