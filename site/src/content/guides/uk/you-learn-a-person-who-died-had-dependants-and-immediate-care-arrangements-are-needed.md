# What to do if… you learn a person who died had dependants and immediate care arrangements are needed (UK)

## Short answer
Ensure all dependants are supervised by a safe adult immediately. Contact the local council’s Children's Services or Adult Social Care duty team to arrange emergency care.

## Do not do these things
- Don't leave a child or vulnerable adult alone "just for a minute" while you make calls or deal with the deceased.
- Don't assume a relative is coming unless you have spoken to them and they have confirmed they are with the dependant right now.
- Don't move a dependant to a new location without notifying the council or police, as this can be mistaken for a missing person case or abduction.
- Don't attempt to manage complex medical needs or equipment yourself if you are not trained to use them.
- Don't delay calling for help because you are worried about "social services taking the kids away"; their first priority is safe family placement.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the location and safety of every dependant.**  
   **Identify exactly where the children or vulnerable adults are and who is with them at this second.** If they are at home alone, at school, or with a provider who is unaware of the death, this is a life-safety emergency.
2. **Call 999 if anyone is currently alone or in danger.**  
   **Request a welfare check immediately if you cannot get to the property or if a vulnerable person is locked inside.** Inform the operator that the primary caregiver has died.
3. **Contact the local council Children’s Services or Adult Social Care.**  
   **Ask specifically for the "Duty Social Worker" or the "MASH (Multi-Agency Safeguarding Hub) team".** 
   - If it is after 5:00 PM or a weekend, ask for the **Emergency Duty Team (EDT)**. 
   - State clearly: "I am reporting a death where dependants are left without care."
4. **Notify schools, nurseries, or existing care agencies.**  
   **Tell the manager or Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) that the parent or carer has died.** They have emergency protocols to keep the dependant safe on-site until a social worker or approved relative arrives.
5. **Establish if the deceased was a registered foster carer.**  
   **Contact the council's Fostering Service immediately if the children were in foster care.** The local authority has a specific legal responsibility to relocate these children into new emergency placements.
6. **Gather essential "Life-Sustainment" information.**  
   **Write down a list of mandatory requirements for the dependants.**
   - Names and dates of birth.
   - Prescribed medications and dosages.
   - Serious allergies.
   - Imminent care needs (e.g., dialysis, evening feed, bedtime routine).
   - Any disability that affects communication or mobility.
7. **Secure the property only once dependants are safe.**  
   **Ensure you have the keys and that the property is locked after the dependants have been moved to safety.** Do not remove items or start "sorting through" the house yet.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide who will look after the dependants permanently today.
- You do not need to start the "Tell Us Once" service or notify the DWP yet.
- You do not need to find the Will or identify the legal guardians right this second; the council provides temporary "Section 17" care while this is researched.
- You do not need to pay for any emergency care or placements arranged by the council today.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when balancing the shock of a death with the needs of survivors. Contacting the local authority is the correct and responsible action; they are legally required to step in when a child or vulnerable person has no one to care for them. You are not "reporting" the family to the authorities; you are activating a safety net designed for this exact crisis.

## Scope note
This guide covers only the first 24 hours of emergency stabilization for dependants. It does not cover long-term custody, probate, or funeral arrangements.

## Important note
This guide is for information only and does not constitute legal advice. In the UK, local authorities have a statutory duty under the Children Act 1989 and the Care Act 2014 to provide emergency assistance when a caregiver dies.
