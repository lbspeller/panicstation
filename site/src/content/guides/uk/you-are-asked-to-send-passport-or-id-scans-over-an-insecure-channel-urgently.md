# What to do if… you are asked to send passport or ID scans over an insecure channel urgently (UK)

## Short answer
Stop and do not answer substantive questions immediately. Request the inquiry in writing, verify the official's identity via an independent channel, and notify your employer’s legal or compliance department before providing any data or statements.

## Do not do these things
- Don't attempt to "clear things up" with a quick informal chat on the phone.
- Don't speculate, guess, or provide "background context" that wasn't specifically requested.
- Don't use contact details provided in an unexpected email or text to verify the person; find the official regulator switchboard independently.
- Don't delete, modify, or "tidy up" any digital or physical records once you are aware of an inquiry.
- Don't sign any "summaries of conversation" or voluntary statements on the spot.
- Don't discuss the details of the inquiry with colleagues or on social media.
- Don't hand over personal mobile phones or passwords without specific legal advice on your rights.
- Don't ignore a formal notice or a statutory deadline even if the request seems broad or unfair.

## What to do now
1. **Request that all inquiries and questions are sent to you in writing via an official email address.** This ensures there is a clear record of what is being asked and under what statutory power the regulator is acting.
2. **Verify the identity and authority of the individual contacting you immediately.** Use the official government website (e.g., GOV.UK) to find the regulator’s main contact number and ask to be put through to the named officer or their department to confirm they are genuine.
3. **Notify your employer’s legal, compliance, or senior management team as soon as possible.** Provide them with the exact text or a copy of the notice you received rather than a verbal summary of what you think it means.
4. **Determine if there is a conflict of interest between you and your employer.** Recognise that company lawyers primarily protect the organisation; if you personally are the subject of the investigation, you may need independent legal advice from a solicitor or your trade union.
5. **Issue a standard holding response if you are pressured for an immediate answer.** Use a neutral phrase such as: "I intend to cooperate fully, but I need to review my records to ensure my response is accurate. Please put your specific questions in writing."
6. **Identify and preserve all relevant documents and electronic data immediately.** Stop any routine "auto-delete" or "clean-up" functions on your email or messaging apps if they relate to the period or topic mentioned by the regulator.
7. **Confirm whether the request is voluntary or mandatory under a specific law.** Ask the regulator to cite the specific section of the Act (e.g., Section 165 of the Financial Services and Markets Act or Section 20 of the Health and Safety at Work Act) they are using to compel information.
8. **Request a formal "Letter of Rights" or written explanation if you are invited to an interview under caution.** Do not attend such an interview without a legal representative present, and ensure you understand whether it is a voluntary interview or one conducted under PACE (Police and Criminal Evidence Act) codes.
9. **Check if the inquiry relates to a data protection complaint and if your internal process is active.** Under the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, from June 2026, you must follow your organisation's mandatory internal complaint handling process before the ICO typically intervenes.
10. **Start a contemporaneous log of every interaction related to this matter.** Record the date, time, name of the person you spoke to, what was requested, and any deadlines provided.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you have done something wrong or need to resign.
- You do not need to provide a "full story" or narrative timeline today.
- You do not need to contact witnesses or other people mentioned in the inquiry to "compare notes."
- You do not need to respond to the substance of the inquiry in the first hour of contact.

## Important reassurance
Regulatory contact is a standard procedural event in many industries and does not automatically mean you are in trouble or have committed an offence. Taking a moment to verify details and move to a written format is a professional and expected response that helps prevent factual errors.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization only. It does not provide legal strategy for specific sectors like healthcare (CQC), finance (FCA), or safety (HSE), which have differing levels of enforcement power.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Regulatory powers in the UK, including those updated by the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 and the Online Safety Act, carry significant penalties for non-compliance; you should consult a qualified solicitor or your union representative for advice on your specific situation.
