# What to do if… you are pressured to clear a loved one’s room in a care facility quickly after they die (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before agreeing to a deadline, disposal, or extra payment. Ask the care home for the relevant contract terms, their bereavement or room-clearance policy, and any proposed charges in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign anything you have not read, especially anything saying all belongings were collected, fees are accepted, or refunds are waived.
- Don’t agree to disposal, donation, sale, or off-site movement of belongings without written confirmation of what will happen and when.
- Don’t remove valuables, documents, keys, or sentimental items without recording what you took and confirming you are allowed to take them.
- Don’t assume you must clear everything the same day; ask for a short written extension or secure storage if you cannot do it safely or calmly.
- Don’t try to settle fees, family disputes, and belongings all in one distressed conversation.

## What to do now
1. **Ask the manager to pause the room clearance conversation.**  
   Say that you need the deadline, the policy, and the contract terms in writing before you can agree to anything. Ask them not to dispose of, donate, sell, or move belongings until you have had a written response.

2. **Confirm who the home can release belongings to.**  
   Ask who is recorded as the resident’s representative, main contact, executor, administrator, or person the home is recorded as dealing with for property matters. If estate authority is unclear, ask the home what proof they need before releasing items. If there is any family disagreement, ask the home to keep the room or belongings secure and not release items until the position is clarified.

3. **Ask what the deadline is based on.**  
   Ask the home to confirm:
   - the exact date and time they want the room cleared,
   - the contract clause or policy they are relying on,
   - whether any fees continue after death,
   - whether any storage, packing, or removal charge may apply,
   - what happens if you cannot meet the requested date.

4. **Request a short written extension or secure storage.**  
   If you cannot clear the room now, ask the home to either keep the room locked until an agreed collection time or move belongings to secure storage with an inventory. Ask where the items will be kept, who will have access, how long they will be held, and whether there is any charge.

5. **Do a quick “must-not-lose” record before moving anything.**  
   If you are allowed to collect items and feel able, photograph the room, cupboards, drawers, valuables, documents, phone, wallet or purse, keys, jewellery, sentimental items, and any care-home paperwork. Make a simple list of anything you remove and ask a staff member to acknowledge it.

6. **Ask the home to log valuables you cannot take today.**  
   Ask staff to list valuables on their property or valuables record, place small items in a labelled envelope or bag if possible, and note who witnessed this. If you think something is missing, ask for it to be logged as a concern the same day.

7. **Keep belongings separate from the fees discussion.**  
   If the home asks for payment, says fees are still running, or mentions a deduction from a refund, ask for an itemised written explanation and the contract clause. Say you will respond after reviewing the paperwork.

8. **Use the complaints route if you are still being pressured.**  
   Ask for the provider’s complaints process and put your request for an extension, storage, and an inventory in writing. If the provider’s final response does not resolve it, the next route depends on where the care home is: England usually uses the Local Government and Social Care Ombudsman for adult social care complaints; Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland have different regulator and complaint routes.

9. **Get consumer advice quickly if there is a charge or unfair pressure.**  
   Contact Citizens Advice about care home contract terms, post-death charges, storage charges, or pressure to accept a fee. Keep the contract, invoices, emails, texts, photographs, and your inventory together.

## What can wait
- You do not need to sort, divide, sell, donate, or value belongings today.
- You do not need to decide probate, inheritance, or estate distribution at the care home.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements in front of staff.
- You do not need to answer a bill immediately; ask for it in writing and review it later.
- You do not need to make one exhausting visit do everything; the immediate task is to prevent loss and create a clear record.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to be slower than the home wants you to be. Asking for written terms, secure handling of belongings, and a short pause is a practical way to protect your loved one’s things while you are grieving.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce pressure, protect belongings, and create a paper trail. Later decisions about estate authority, refunds, contract disputes, or complaints may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, probate, therapeutic, or professional advice. Contracts and local complaint routes vary, so check the written paperwork and get advice if belongings may be removed, disposed of, or charged for.
