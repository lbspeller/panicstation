# What to do if… you receive a court order you do not understand and it takes effect immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the order as legally binding from the second you receive it and do not take any action that could be interpreted as a breach. Contact the issuing court immediately to confirm the specific prohibitions and the date of the next hearing or deadline.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the order because it contains errors or you believe it was intended for someone else.
- Do not contact any protected person or visit restricted locations to explain your situation if the order prohibits contact or approach.
- Do not rely on the other party, their solicitor, or social media for an explanation of what the document requires.
- Do not destroy, alter, or discard any part of the paperwork or the envelope it arrived in.
- Do not wait for a formal letter or secondary notification if the document states it has "immediate effect."
- Do not assume you can "briefly" breach the order to collect medication, pets, or essentials unless the text explicitly allows a supervised visit.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific prohibitions and requirements immediately.** Locate the sections of the order that list exactly what you must do or must not do, often found under headings like "Prohibitions" or "Directions."
   - Check for a "Penal Notice" (usually in bold or a box), which warns that a breach may lead to imprisonment for Contempt of Court.
   - Look for a "Power of Arrest" which allows police to detain you immediately if they believe a breach has occurred.
2. **Assume the most restrictive interpretation of the wording until confirmed otherwise.** If you are unsure if "no contact" includes liking a social media post or sending a message via a third party, assume it does and stop all such activity.
3. **Contact the issuing court to verify the case details.** Use the official "Find a court or tribunal" service on GOV.UK for England and Wales, or the Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service / Northern Ireland Courts and Tribunals Service websites.
   - Provide the case number found at the top right of the order.
   - Ask the staff to confirm the date and time of the next "Return Hearing" or the deadline for your response.
4. **Request a procedural explanation from court staff.** Ask: "Can you confirm the specific wording of the prohibitions in this order and tell me what the next procedural deadline is?"
   - Note that court staff can explain the meaning of the words used and the court process, but they cannot tell you if your specific planned actions are "safe" or "legal."
5. **Establish if the order was made "Without Notice" (ex parte).** Check if the order mentions it was made in your absence; if so, there is usually a "Return Date" where a judge will hear your side.
   - In England and Wales civil cases, use Form N244 to apply to "vary" (change) or "set aside" (cancel) an order.
   - In family cases involving non-molestation orders, use Form FL403 to apply to vary or discharge the order.
6. **Adjust your physical movements to prevent accidental breaches.** If the order bans you from a specific "exclusion zone" or street, map it out and change your commute or routine immediately.
   - If the order prevents contact with children, do not use "indirect" contact like gaming platforms or shared apps unless the order says you can.
7. **Check your eligibility for urgent Legal Aid.** Use the GOV.UK legal aid checker or contact Civil Legal Advice (CLA) at 0345 345 4 345 (9am–8pm Mon–Fri, 9am–12:30pm Sat).
   - If you have an existing solicitor, send them high-quality photos of every page of the order immediately.
8. **Commence a detailed compliance log.** Record the exact date and time you received the order, every step you took to comply (e.g., leaving a property, deleting an app), and the details of all correspondence with the court.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove the other party is lying or gather evidence for the full case background in the first few hours.
- You do not need to decide on a long-term legal strategy before you have secured professional advice.
- You do not need to worry about permanent records or "findings of fact" today; an immediate-effect order is often a temporary holding measure until a full hearing can take place.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an immediate court order is a high-stress event designed to create a "status quo" quickly. Following the rules of the order precisely in the short term does not mean you agree with them or that they are permanent; it is simply the safest way to avoid arrest or a Contempt of Court charge while you arrange a legal challenge.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for the minutes and hours after being served with an urgent UK court order. It does not provide legal strategy or address specific outcomes for criminal, family, or civil litigation.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. Court procedures and forms differ between England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. If you are served with an order carrying a penal notice or power of arrest, prioritize immediate compliance and seek a qualified solicitor.
