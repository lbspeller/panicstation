# What to do if… your child tells you someone online is asking to keep a “special secret” and it feels sexual (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the disclosure as a priority safeguarding event and stop all contact between the child and the individual immediately. Thank your child for telling you and report the concern to the Child Exploitation and Online Protection Command (CEOP) or the police.

## Do not do these things
- Do not blame the child, suggest they encouraged the behavior, or ask why they didn't tell you sooner.
- Do not delete evidence, messages, or the app before you have recorded the details needed for a report.
- Do not contact the individual yourself to confront them, as this can alert them to hide evidence or escalate threats.
- Do not ask the child to continue the conversation to "see what happens" or gather more information.
- Do not view, save, or share any sexual images of the child if they exist; this is a criminal offense even for parents.
- Do not promise the child that you will keep the situation a secret from authorities.

## What to do now
1. **Listen to your child without asking them to repeat the story multiple times.** Reassure them with a simple statement: "I believe you, you are not in trouble, and I will help you stay safe."
2. **End all contact immediately and secure the account.** Help your child block the user and adjust privacy settings to prevent further messages or "friend requests," but do not delete the account yet.
3. **Turn off location services on the child's device and within the specific app.** This prevents the individual from tracking the child’s physical location while you manage the situation.
4. **Record the individual's digital details for reporting.** Take screenshots of the following:
   - The individual’s profile page, username, and ID.
   - The platform or app name.
   - Samples of the concerning messages or "special secret" requests.
5. **Check for immediate physical risk.** Determine if the individual knows the child's real name, home address, or school, or if a physical meeting has been planned. If you believe the child is in immediate danger, call **999**.
6. **Report the incident to CEOP (Child Exploitation and Online Protection Command).** Use the CEOP Safety Centre online reporting tool for any concerns regarding online grooming or sexual pressure.
7. **Contact the police via 101 if no immediate danger is present.** This is for reporting the crime and receiving local police support if you prefer not to use the online CEOP form initially.
8. **Seek removal of any shared sexual images via "Report Remove".** If images have been shared, help your child (under 18) use the **Childline/IWF Report Remove** tool. **Do not view the images yourself.**
9. **Access immediate professional support.**
   - Contact the **NSPCC Helpline** on **0808 800 5000** for adult guidance.
   - Encourage your child to call **Childline** on **0800 1111** for confidential support.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the individual's "true intent" or identity yourself.
- You do not need to decide on permanent "grounding" or new long-term internet rules tonight.
- You do not need to notify the child’s school or other parents until you have spoken with the police or CEOP.

## Important reassurance
Grooming relies on creating a "special" bond and using secrecy to isolate a child. If a child has participated in sexual conversations or shared images, it is a result of adult manipulation, not a "choice" by the child. Feeling confused, scared, or even protective of the individual is a common reaction for children in these situations.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and reporting procedures for UK residents. It does not replace the need for professional safeguarding or police investigation.

## Important note
Under UK law, online grooming is a serious criminal offense. You should report your concerns to CEOP or the police even if you are not 100% certain that the contact was sexual. Do not attempt to investigate the individual yourself.
