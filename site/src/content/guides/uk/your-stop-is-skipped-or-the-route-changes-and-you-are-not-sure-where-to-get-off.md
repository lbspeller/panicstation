# What to do if… your stop is skipped or the route changes and you are not sure where to get off (UK)

## Short answer
Stay on until the next safe, public stop or station, then ask the driver, guard, conductor, station staff, or a help point to confirm where you are and the safest way back to your intended stop.

## Do not do these things
- Do not jump off between stops, force doors, or step onto tracks or a road.
- Do not use emergency alarms or emergency controls unless there is immediate danger, fire, violence, threat, or a medical emergency.
- Do not leave a stop or station in a rush if you are not sure where you are.
- Do not wander into quiet streets, empty car parks, or shortcuts to look for a landmark.
- Do not argue while the vehicle is moving; focus on getting to the next safe stopping point.

## What to do now
1. **Switch to the next safe stop or station.**  
   If your stop has been skipped or the route has changed, plan to get off at the next stop or station that looks busy, well-lit, or staffed. If the next stop looks isolated and you feel unsafe, stay on until a busier interchange or station if that is safer.

2. **Check where you are without trying to solve everything.**  
   Look at the next-stop display, listen for announcements, check the stop or station signs, or open your phone map and drop a pin. You only need enough information to explain where you are now.

3. **Ask for the best stop to use.**  
   Use one simple sentence: “My stop was skipped or the route changed. What is the best stop to get off for [your stop]?”  
   - On a bus, ring the bell for the next suitable stop and speak to the driver when the bus is stopped.  
   - On a train, tram, Tube, or metro service, speak to the guard, conductor, station staff, or use a non-emergency help point or intercom if available.

4. **If you have been carried past a rail station, speak to staff before travelling back.**  
   At the next station, say: “I was carried past my station because of a stop or route issue. Can you tell me the correct way to return?” Your ticket may still be fine, or staff may need to confirm what is valid before you travel back.

5. **Choose a safe waiting place before planning the return.**  
   Stay near other passengers, staff, a ticket office, a help point, CCTV, or a well-lit entrance. Avoid quiet exits until you know your next step.

6. **If you feel unsafe, are being followed, or are being harassed, move towards help.**  
   Move near staff or other passengers, use a help point if there is one, and call 999 if there is immediate danger. For non-emergency crime or safety concerns on the rail network or at a rail station, contact British Transport Police by calling 0800 40 50 40 or texting 61016.

7. **Note only the details you can see quickly.**  
   Write down the time, route or service number, direction of travel, stop or station names, and any vehicle or unit number if it is visible. Do not delay getting to a safe place to collect details.

8. **Report it later only if that helps you.**  
   For London buses or TfL services, use TfL contact routes. For buses elsewhere, start with the operator or local transport body; in England outside London or in Wales, GOV.UK also gives a DVSA reporting route for buses that arrive late, early, do not stop, or do not turn up. This can wait until you are safe and calm.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, ask for a refund, or claim costs.
- You do not need to find the perfect route while you are stressed.
- You do not need to prove everything before asking staff for help.
- Your immediate order is: safe stop or station, confirm location, ask for the next clear step.

## Important reassurance
This is usually fixable. Stops are missed, routes are diverted, services terminate early, and announcements can be unclear. Feeling disoriented does not mean you have done anything wrong; slow down and use the next public or staffed point as a reset.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about tickets, refunds, complaints, missed connections, taxis, or extra costs may need the operator, transport authority, rail staff, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, transport, or professional advice. Transport rules and ticket conditions vary by operator, route, and ticket type. If you are in immediate danger or need urgent help, call 999.
