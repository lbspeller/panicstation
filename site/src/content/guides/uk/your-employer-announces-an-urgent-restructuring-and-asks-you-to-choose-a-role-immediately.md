# What to do if… your employer announces an urgent restructuring and asks you to choose a role immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Request the claim in writing immediately and do not sign any repayment agreement or authorize a payroll deduction until you have verified the specific contractual clause or policy allowing the recovery.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to or sign a new "repayment agreement" or contract variation during an initial meeting.
- Do not make a manual payment from your personal bank account before confirming whether the recovery should be gross or net and how tax/NI will be adjusted.
- Do not ignore a "Statutory Demand" or formal letter of claim; these are legal steps that can escalate to court or insolvency proceedings regardless of an internal dispute.
- Do not threaten to resign immediately as this may trigger further contractual "bad leaver" clauses that strengthen the employer's clawback position.
- Do not rely on verbal assurances that the deduction "won't be much" or will be "sorted out later."

## What to do now
1. **Request a formal written breakdown of the claim today.** Ask HR or payroll to provide:
   - the exact amount claimed and whether it is the **gross** (before tax) or **net** (take-home) figure
   - the **specific contractual clause**, bonus plan rule, or signed authorization they are relying on
   - a detailed **calculation** showing how the figure was reached
   - the **reason** for the clawback (e.g., an administrative overpayment, leaving during a retention period, or a conduct-related malus).
2. **Secure all relevant documentation immediately.** Download or print copies of:
   - your original employment contract and any subsequent "side letters" or variations
   - the specific bonus scheme rules or "Plan Text" for the period the bonus relates to
   - the letter or email confirming the bonus award and any conditions attached to it at the time
   - payslips from the month the bonus was paid and the current month.
3. **Determine if the claim is a "recovery of overpayment" or a "contractual clawback."** 
   - If it is a **payroll error/overpayment**, employers generally have a legal right to recover it under the Employment Rights Act 1996, but they should agree a reasonable repayment plan that does not cause financial hardship.
   - If it is a **contractual clawback** (e.g., because you have given notice), the employer can only deduct the money if a specific term in your contract or a separate written agreement authorized that deduction *before* the event happened.
4. **Send a formal "holding" dispute email.** State clearly:
   - "I acknowledge your request for a bonus repayment. I currently dispute the validity/amount of this claim. Please provide the documents requested and confirm that no deductions will be made from my next salary payment until this review is complete."
5. **Verify the handling of Tax and National Insurance (NI).** 
   - If you are still employed, the most accurate way to recover the money is usually by reducing your **gross** pay in a future month. This ensures the tax and NI are recalculated automatically by the payroll system.
   - If they ask for a **net** payment via bank transfer, insist on receiving a corrected P60 or an "Additional FPS" (Full Payment Submission) notification to prove they have updated HMRC, otherwise you may overpay tax for the year.
6. **Check the enforceability of the clause.** 
   - Look for "tapering" rules (e.g., you pay back 100% if you leave in month 1, but only 50% in month 6). 
   - Check if the clause only triggers if *you* give notice, or if it also triggers if *they* make you redundant (some clauses are "penalty clauses" and may be unenforceable if they are viewed as an unreasonable restraint of trade).
7. **Initiate the formal grievance procedure if the employer refuses to pause.** If a deduction is threatened or has already occurred without your agreement, file a formal written grievance to create a paper trail of the dispute.
8. **Contact ACAS for Early Conciliation.** If the deduction has happened or the dispute is reaching a deadlock, you must generally start **ACAS Early Conciliation** before you can take the matter to an Employment Tribunal. 
   - Note the strict deadline: you usually have **3 months minus one day** from the date of the deduction to start this process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to leave the company today based on the clawback demand.
- You do not need to accept the employer's first proposed repayment schedule if it leaves you unable to pay essential bills.
- You do not need to hire a solicitor immediately for the initial information-gathering stage.

## Important reassurance
It is common for employers to make administrative errors or to trigger clawback clauses automatically without reviewing individual circumstances. Feeling stressed or blindsided is a normal reaction to a sudden financial demand. Slowing down the process and insisting on written evidence is a standard professional response, not a sign of "being difficult."

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for bonus disputes in the UK. Bonus law is highly dependent on the specific wording of your contract. If the sum involved is significant (e.g., equivalent to several months' salary) or involves complex financial services regulations (FCA/PRA Remuneration Codes), specialist legal advice may be necessary after these initial steps.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment rights regarding deductions and clawbacks are governed by the Employment Rights Act 1996 and specific case law which may change. Whether a deduction is "lawful" depends on the timing of your written consent and the specific facts of your bonus award.
