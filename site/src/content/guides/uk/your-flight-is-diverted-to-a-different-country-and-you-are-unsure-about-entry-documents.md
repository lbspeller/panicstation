# What to do if… your flight is diverted to a different country and you are unsure about entry documents (UK)

## Short answer
Stay in the international or transit area unless airport staff or a uniformed official directs you to move. Before going landside if possible, ask the airline whether you are staying airside or entering the country, and ask for a formal document check.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave the international or transit area just to “sort it out” if you are unsure you are allowed to enter.
- Do not rely on other passengers’ advice about visas or entry rules; rules can depend on nationality, airport, documents, and whether you must clear immigration.
- Do not let your passport out of your sight unless airline staff or a uniformed official needs to inspect or handle it.
- Do not sign a form you do not understand without asking what it means and whether you are requesting entry, refusing entry, or accepting any penalty.
- Do not throw away boarding passes, bag tags, screenshots, or diversion paperwork.
- Do not accept a hotel voucher or collect checked bags until you have asked whether doing so requires you to clear immigration.

## What to do now
1. **Ask whether you are airside or entering the country.**  
   Ask airline ground staff: “Are we remaining airside, or do we have to clear immigration?” If they say you must clear immigration, ask the reason, such as terminal closure, safety, baggage, or an overnight stop.

2. **Ask for a formal document check.**  
   Say: “Please check my passport nationality and this diversion airport in Timatic or your airline document-check system. Do I need a visa or any other document to enter or transit?” If you can safely get online, also check the IATA Travel Centre and GOV.UK foreign travel advice for the diversion country.

3. **Say early if you do not have the documents.**  
   Use a clear sentence: “I do not think I have permission to enter this country. If possible, I need to remain in international transit. Please escalate this to a supervisor and coordinate with border control.” This helps avoid moving landside before the document issue is clear, unless an official directs you.

4. **If you are sent to immigration, ask what is happening before you move on.**  
   At the border desk, calmly ask: “Am I being admitted, refused entry, or given temporary transit or landing permission?” Also ask: “How is this recorded, and how long is it valid?” Show your passport, original destination itinerary, boarding pass if you have one, and any proof of onward travel.

5. **Pause before accepting a hotel, transport, or baggage collection.**  
   Ask the airline: “Will this require me to pass immigration, and has border control confirmed I am allowed to do that?” If you may not be allowed to enter, ask what airside, supervised, or escorted option exists.

6. **Keep a simple paper trail.**  
   Save or photograph the diversion airport name, arrival time, flight status screens, written instructions, hotel or meal vouchers, baggage tags, and any new boarding pass. Note the airline desk, queue, or staff role you spoke to if you can do that without causing tension.

7. **If you are stuck, ask for consular help.**  
   If you are a British national and there is no clear safe route forward, ask airport staff to help you contact the nearest British embassy, high commission, consulate, or the FCDO’s 24/7 consular team. If you are not British, ask for help contacting your own embassy or consulate.

8. **If your passport is missing or damaged, treat that as urgent.**  
   Tell airline staff and border officials immediately. If you are a British national, contact the nearest British embassy, high commission, consulate, or GOV.UK emergency travel document service and ask the airline for a printed or emailed itinerary.

## What can wait
- You do not need to argue about compensation, refunds, insurance, or who is at fault right now.
- You do not need to post publicly or make a complaint while you are still unsure about immigration.
- You do not need a full new travel plan yet. Focus only on whether you are staying airside, whether you have permission to enter if required, and how you will continue travel safely.

## Important reassurance
Diversions can make normal travel rules feel sudden and confusing. Slowing down to ask whether you are airside, landside, or being given temporary permission is a sensible way to protect yourself.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate hours after a diversion. Entry and transit options depend on the diversion country, your nationality, your documents, the airline, and airport procedures, so later decisions may need help from border officials, the airline, consular staff, or a qualified adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, travel, financial, or professional advice. Entry and transit rules can change and may be applied differently in emergencies; follow instructions from uniformed border or immigration officers and ask for consular help if you are stuck.
