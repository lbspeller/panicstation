# What to do if… your travel booking was changed to a different departure time without your consent (UK)

## Short answer
Secure proof first: screenshot the original booking and the new departure time, then contact the operator immediately and say you have not accepted the change. Ask for your original departure back, a usable alternative today, or a refund if you cannot travel.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click “accept”, “change”, “confirm”, or similar wording in the app or email unless you are sure you can use the new departure time.
- Don’t cancel in a rush if you still need to travel today. If staff tell you to cancel as part of fixing it, ask them to confirm in writing that they instructed this because of an involuntary change.
- Don’t rely on verbal promises alone. Ask for an email, app notification, replacement ticket, or chat transcript.
- Don’t buy an expensive replacement immediately unless you have captured evidence and asked the operator to fix it first, unless waiting would leave you stranded.
- Don’t assume the label used by the operator is final. Ask for the practical outcome you need: a usable departure, a refund, and the operator’s written position.

## What to do now
1. **Lock in evidence.**
   - Screenshot the original confirmation showing the date, time, booking reference, route, passengers, and price paid.
   - Screenshot the new itinerary and any message saying you were rebooked, moved, exchanged, confirmed, or changed.
   - Save the email, PDF ticket, app ticket, and the time you noticed the change.

2. **Check whether the system says you accepted the change.**
   - Look for wording such as “confirmed”, “accepted”, “exchanged”, “voluntary change”, “new ticket”, or “new receipt”.
   - If you see this and you did not choose it, screenshot it before doing anything else.

3. **Contact the operator through the fastest live channel.**
   - Use phone, live chat, station desk, airport desk, port desk, or in-app messaging if available.
   - Say: “My booking was changed without my consent. I have not accepted the new departure time. Please reinstate my original departure or rebook me on a departure I can use today. If you cannot do that, I want a refund to my original payment method.”
   - Ask them to record it as an involuntary schedule change, cancellation, rescheduling, or the closest wording they use.

4. **If you are already at the station, port, or airport, go to a staffed desk.**
   - Ask for an on-the-spot fix before the new departure closes.
   - Ask for a supervisor if the first person cannot access rebooking options.
   - If the original time is not available, ask for the next usable departure on the same route, or a reasonable alternative route, with written confirmation.

5. **If this is a flight, check whether cancellation rights apply.**
   - If the original flight is shown as cancelled, or the airline says cancellation rights apply, ask for your choice of a refund or rerouting under comparable conditions.
   - If you are waiting because of a covered cancellation or long delay, ask what care and assistance they will provide, such as food, communications, accommodation, and transport where the rules apply.
   - If the airline says it is only a schedule change, ask them to put that in writing and still ask for a usable alternative or refund. Avoid arguing about compensation while time is tight.

6. **If this is National Rail, protect the refund position.**
   - If your train is cancelled, delayed, rescheduled, or your reservation will not be honoured and you do not travel, ask the retailer that sold you the ticket for a refund and keep evidence of the disruption.
   - For tickets bought on or after 1 April 2026, Anytime, Off-Peak, Super Off-Peak, Day Travelcards, and most Ranger or Rover tickets generally need a refund request by 23:59 the day before the ticket becomes valid if you simply choose not to use them.
   - After the ticket becomes valid, refund requests are usually limited to disruption, rescheduling, an unhonoured reservation, or exceptional circumstances. For tickets bought on or after 1 April 2026, this kind of later request must usually be made no later than 28 days after the ticket expires.
   - Advance and Season tickets have their own rules.
   - If the change was not your choice, do not describe it as a change of mind. Describe the exact operator change and attach screenshots.

7. **If this is a ferry or other covered sea journey, ask about rerouting or refund.**
   - If the sailing is delayed by 90 minutes or more, ask whether you can be rerouted or refunded.
   - If you are waiting at the port, ask what refreshments or assistance are available.
   - If an overnight wait is involved, ask whether accommodation applies, because there are exceptions for weather and safe-operation issues.

8. **Create one clean written record.**
   - Send one short message saying: you did not consent, you have not accepted the new time, your departure is imminent, and you need a usable rebooking or refund.
   - Include your booking reference, original departure, new departure, and screenshots.
   - Save the message and any response.

9. **If time is minutes and you must travel, make the least-regret backup purchase.**
   - Choose the cheapest usable option with the clearest refund or change terms you can find in the moment.
   - Keep every receipt and screenshot showing why you had to act fast.
   - Do not delete the original ticket or messages, even if the replacement gets you moving.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to pursue compensation or make a formal complaint. First get moving or secure the refund position.
- You do not need to prove every rule while you are at the gate, platform, or port. Ask for the outcome and get the operator’s position in writing.
- You do not need a perfect timeline now. Save evidence now and write the full summary later.

## Important reassurance
A sudden departure-time change can make you feel trapped, especially when travel is close. Taking a few minutes to capture proof and avoid accidentally accepting the change can stop a rushed moment turning into a harder dispute.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance to stabilise the situation and avoid irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about complaints, compensation, chargebacks, insurance, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, travel, or professional advice. Rules vary by operator, ticket type, route, booking channel, and the reason for the change, so prioritise reversible actions, written confirmations, and evidence of the involuntary change.
