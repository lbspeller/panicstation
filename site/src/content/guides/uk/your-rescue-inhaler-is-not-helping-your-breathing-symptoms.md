# What to do if… your rescue inhaler is not helping your breathing symptoms (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as potentially serious: sit upright, use your reliever exactly as your asthma action plan or NHS asthma-attack advice says, and call 999 if you are getting worse or not improving after the maximum dose.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait it out if you are struggling to breathe or your reliever is not helping as usual.
- Do not lie flat or curl up; sitting upright usually makes breathing easier.
- Do not take random extra doses beyond the maximum in your asthma action plan or NHS asthma-attack advice.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if this may be an asthma attack.
- Do not keep relying on an inhaler you think is empty, blocked, or misfiring without switching to a working reliever if you have one and getting urgent help.

## What to do now
1. **Sit upright and make breathing as easy as possible.** Loosen tight clothing, keep your shoulders relaxed, and try to breathe steadily.
2. **If you have a personal asthma action plan, follow it now.** If you do not have it with you, use the NHS asthma-attack steps below for the type of reliever you have.
3. **Do quick inhaler checks while you use it.**
   - Shake the inhaler if it is a metered-dose inhaler.
   - Check the mouthpiece is clear.
   - Use a spacer if you have one.
   - If the dose counter shows zero, or the inhaler seems empty or faulty, switch to another prescribed reliever if you have one.
4. **If this might be an asthma attack, use the NHS asthma-attack dosing for your reliever.**
   - If you have a blue reliever inhaler: take 1 puff every 30–60 seconds, shaking between puffs, up to 10 puffs. Use a spacer if you have one.
   - If you use an AIR or MART inhaler, used for both prevention and relief: take 1 puff every 1–3 minutes, up to 6 puffs.
   - If your own asthma action plan gives a different maximum for an asthma attack, follow your plan.
5. **Call 999 for an ambulance immediately if any of these are true:**
   - you are getting worse at any time
   - you do not feel better after the maximum dose
   - you do not have an inhaler
   - your inhaler will not work
   Tell the call handler you are having an asthma attack or breathing emergency.
6. **While waiting for help, stay sitting upright.** If symptoms are no better after 10 minutes and the ambulance has not arrived, use the inhaler again up to the same maximum dose.
7. **If symptoms are still no better after repeating the maximum dose and the ambulance has still not arrived, contact 999 again immediately.**
8. **If you are not at asthma-attack level but your reliever is clearly weaker than usual, treat it as urgent today.**
   - If your GP is open, ask for an urgent same-day GP or asthma nurse appointment.
   - If your GP is closed, call NHS 111.
   - If your breathing worsens, your inhaler is not helping, or you are unsure whether this is becoming an asthma attack, call 999 instead.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now, such as a trigger, infection, weather, stress, or a missed dose.
- You do not need to make long-term medication changes in the moment unless your asthma action plan already tells you to.
- You do not need to clean, replace, or troubleshoot equipment beyond quick checks until you are stable and breathing comfortably.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel panicky when your usual reliever does not feel reliable. Acting early is safer, and calling 999 is appropriate if you are getting worse or not improving after the emergency reliever steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps to keep you safe in the moment and get urgent care if needed. Once you are stable, later decisions about asthma control, triggers, inhaler technique, or medication changes may need help from a GP, asthma nurse, pharmacist, or specialist.

## Important note
This is general information only, not a diagnosis or legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you think you are having an asthma attack or your breathing is worsening, treat it as urgent and seek emergency help.
