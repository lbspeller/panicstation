# What to do if… you are told you are being investigated for a data breach at work (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and preserve the situation: do not delete, edit, tidy up, move, or investigate anything on your own. Ask for the allegation and meeting details in writing, then arrange support before any disciplinary hearing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete emails, messages, files, browser history, logs, tickets, or anything else connected to the issue.
- Do not use personal email, WhatsApp, personal cloud storage, or a personal device to move work information.
- Do not contact colleagues to compare notes or coordinate an explanation, unless your representative, adviser, or employer has told you this is appropriate.
- Do not run unauthorised searches, checks, exports, screenshots, or downloads in work systems to prove your point.
- Do not guess, speculate, or fill silences in an interview. It is OK to say you do not know or need to check.
- Do not secretly record a meeting. Ask first and follow the employer’s process.
- Do not resign, accept blame, or sign anything in the heat of the moment.

## What to do now
1. **Stop making changes.** If you are mid-task, stop safely and note the time, what you were doing, and what system, file, device, ticket, or email was involved. Do not rename, move, delete, or reconfigure anything.
2. **Ask for the basics in writing.** Ask what you are being investigated for, the date range, what data or systems are involved, and whether the meeting is an investigation meeting or a disciplinary hearing. Ask who will run it and what documents, policies, logs, or evidence they expect to rely on.
3. **Arrange support early.** If you are in a union, contact your rep and ask them to attend. If it is a disciplinary hearing that could lead to disciplinary action, you can ask to be accompanied by a work colleague, an official employed by a trade union, or a workplace trade union representative certified by their union to act as a companion. If it is an investigation meeting, there is generally no legal right to be accompanied, but you can still ask for someone to attend as support.
4. **Write a private factual timeline.** Use your own notes and keep them factual: dates, times, systems used, what you were asked to do, who gave instructions, approvals you had, tickets or requests you followed, and anything you are unsure about. Do not add theories about blame.
5. **Identify legitimate records without pulling extra data.** Make a list of relevant items you already know about, such as the meeting notice, your job description, relevant policies, training records, change requests, tickets, approvals, or written instructions. Only access, copy, save, or print material if you are allowed to do so under policy and your normal role.
6. **Use the internal incident route.** If you know something that may help contain or assess the breach, report it through the designated internal route, such as your manager, incident response contact, security team, or data protection lead. Keep it narrow and factual.
7. **If asked to hand over a device or account access, keep it documented.** Ask for the request in writing, who is taking the device or access, what will happen next, and how any personal material on the device will be handled. Do not obstruct the process.
8. **Ask for a short postponement if you need support or documents.** You can say: “I want to cooperate, but I need a short delay so I can arrange support and review the information you are relying on.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, raise a grievance, make a formal complaint, or go public.
- You do not need to produce a perfect explanation before you know the allegation and evidence.
- You do not need to contact the ICO, affected individuals, customers, suppliers, or the police yourself unless your role or an authorised internal process requires it.
- You do not need to accept blame or agree to an outcome before you have seen the allegation and had a chance to get support.

## Important reassurance
A data breach investigation does not automatically mean you have done something wrong. The safest first move is to slow the situation down, preserve evidence, use the proper internal route, and keep your answers factual.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours or days after you are told about the investigation. Employment and data protection issues can become case-specific quickly, and later decisions may need help from a union representative, employment adviser, solicitor, or data protection specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, employment, data protection, technical, or professional advice. Your position may depend on your contract, employment status, workplace policy, role, and whether the meeting is investigatory or disciplinary.
