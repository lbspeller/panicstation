# What to do if… police ask to search your phone on the spot and you feel pressured to agree (UK)

## Short answer
State clearly: **"I do not consent to a search of my phone or its contents."** Ask the officer: **"Under what legal power are you requiring me to unlock this device?"**

## Do not do these things
- Don't provide your PIN, password, or biometric unlock (Face ID / Fingerprint) **just to be helpful**.
- Don't hand over your phone while it is already unlocked.
- Don't physically resist if an officer forcibly seizes the phone; state your lack of consent verbally instead.
- Don't delete messages, apps, or history during the encounter, as this can be interpreted as obstructing justice.
- Don't believe that "having nothing to hide" means you must waive your right to privacy on the street.

## What to do now
1. **Verbalize your lack of consent immediately.**  
   Say: **"I do not consent to you searching the data on my phone."** This ensures that any subsequent search is recorded as non-consensual, which is vital for later legal challenges.
2. **Identify the legal power being used.**  
   Ask: **"Am I being detained for a search, and what is the statutory power and grounds for that search?"**  
   - Standard stop and search powers (PACE Section 1) are for physical items like weapons or drugs and do not automatically grant a right to browse your digital data.
3. **Refuse to provide your passcode or PIN on the street.**  
   If asked for a code, say: **"I am not providing my passcode. If you are serving a Section 49 RIPA notice, I require a written copy and legal advice."**  
   - A legal obligation to provide a PIN (under RIPA 2000) requires a formal written notice authorized by a judge or senior officer; it cannot be issued verbally on the spot.
4. **Request a Digital Processing Notice (DPN) if they ask for "voluntary" access.**  
   If the officer suggests they just want a "quick look" with your help, ask: **"Where is the written notice explaining my right to refuse and the reasons for this request?"**  
   - Under the Police, Crime, Sentencing and Courts Act 2022, police must provide written information before extracting data voluntarily.
5. **Record the officer's details if the phone is seized.**  
   If they take the phone, ask: **"Can I have your name, shoulder number, and station?"** and **"Will I be provided with a receipt for this property?"**  
   - If they do not have a receipt book, write down the time, location, and the officer's collar number immediately after the encounter.
6. **Withdraw consent if you have already given it.**  
   If you unlocked the phone under pressure, say: **"I am withdrawing my consent for this search immediately. Please stop looking at the device."**  
7. **Differentiate if you are at a port or airport.**  
   Ask: **"Is this a Schedule 7 examination?"**  
   - If it is a Schedule 7 (Terrorism Act 2000) stop at a border, the rules are different; you may be legally required to provide your PIN and answer questions even without "reasonable suspicion."

## What can wait
- You do **not** have to explain why you want to keep your phone private.
- You do **not** have to decide whether to file a complaint against the officer right now.
- You do **not** have to provide your social media or cloud storage passwords on the street.
- You can seek a specialist solicitor to challenge the seizure or search **after the encounter is over**.

## Important reassurance
Feeling intimidated or "frozen" when a police officer makes a request is a natural reaction. You have the right to change your mind and withdraw consent at any time. Simply stating your boundary clearly is a calm, legal way to protect your rights.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization for street encounters and on-the-spot requests. It does not cover the detailed legal process of contesting a RIPA notice in court or the specific rules for post-arrest station searches.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Police powers regarding digital data are complex and subject to specific thresholds of "necessity and proportionality." If your phone is seized or searched, contact a solicitor or a legal rights organization (such as Liberty or Netpol) as soon as possible.
