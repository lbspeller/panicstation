# What to do if… police ask to search your phone on the spot and you feel pressured to agree (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and say clearly: **“I do not consent to you searching my phone.”** Ask: **“Am I being stopped or detained for a search, and what power are you using?”** Do not unlock the phone or give a passcode just to end the pressure.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t unlock your phone or share your PIN, password, fingerprint, or face unlock just to relieve pressure.
- Don’t hand the phone over already unlocked for “a quick look”.
- Don’t argue about the law on the street.
- Don’t physically resist if officers insist they are using a legal power to take or search something.
- Don’t lie, guess passwords, or make up a PIN.
- Don’t delete, change, or move anything on the phone during the encounter.
- Don’t ignore formal written paperwork about device access or decryption.

## What to do now
1. **Use one clear refusal sentence.**  
   Say: **“I do not consent to a search of my phone.”** If pressed, repeat it once.

2. **Make them name the power before you do anything with the phone.**  
   Ask: **“Am I being stopped or detained for a search?”** Then ask: **“What power are you using, what are your grounds, and what are you looking for?”**

3. **Ask for the officer details and record route.**  
   Ask for the officer’s **name or identification number, and station**. Ask: **“How do I get the stop and search record?”**

4. **If they ask you to unlock it, keep the phone locked.**  
   Say: **“I am not unlocking my device or giving any passcode. I do not consent. I want legal advice.”**  
   If they say you “have to”, ask: **“Please show or explain the legal power you are relying on.”**

5. **If they say they will take the phone, do not resist.**  
   Ask: **“Am I being given a property receipt, reference number, or record for the phone?”** Note the time, place, officer details, what power they said they were using, and the exact words used about your phone.

6. **If you already agreed, withdraw agreement clearly.**  
   Say: **“I withdraw consent. I do not agree to any search or extraction from my phone.”**  
   If extraction has not already happened, ask them to stop and record that you have withdrawn agreement.

7. **If you are given a formal written notice about access, passwords, or decryption, slow down.**  
   Say: **“I want legal advice before I respond to this notice.”** Keep the paperwork. A formal notice is different from an informal on-the-spot request and may have legal consequences.

8. **If this is an immigration or border-type encounter, identify the role and power.**  
   Ask: **“Are you a police officer or an immigration officer, and what power are you using regarding my phone?”** Then repeat: **“I do not consent to a phone search or unlocking. I want legal advice.”**

9. **If you are arrested or taken to a station, switch to solicitor mode.**  
   Say: **“I want legal advice and a solicitor.”** Avoid explaining the phone, the contents, or the passcode before getting advice.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to explain your whole situation on the street.
- You do **not** need to prove you have nothing to hide by letting anyone browse your phone.
- You do **not** need to decide now whether to complain or take legal action.
- You do **not** need to sort account security, backups, or remote access while the encounter is still happening.
- You can write a fuller note and get advice after you are safe and away from the immediate pressure.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, fawning, or agreeing under pressure is a common stress response. A calm refusal sentence, a question about the legal power, and a request for legal advice are enough to slow the situation down without escalating it.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for an on-the-spot request. Police and device-access powers can differ depending on where you are in the UK, whether this is a stop and search, an arrest, a station interview, a witness request, or an immigration or border context. If your phone is taken, searched, or you receive written paperwork about device access, get specialist legal help as soon as you can.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you cannot confirm what power is being used, it is usually safer to refuse consent, keep the phone locked, stay calm, and ask for legal advice before giving any device access.
