# What to do if… you are told you are the next of kin and need to make urgent decisions (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and ask exactly what decision is needed, who is asking, and whether you are being treated as the contact person, the executor, or the person responsible for this specific step. Make only the smallest safe holding decisions while you check for a will, known wishes, and whether a coroner, medical examiner, or procurator fiscal process applies.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign anything you do not understand just to get it done.
- Do not assume "next of kin" automatically gives you final authority over the estate, funeral, medical paperwork, or every family decision.
- Do not pay large costs from your own money or personal credit in a rush if you are unsure who is responsible; ask for costs in writing and keep receipts for anything unavoidable.
- Do not throw away, tidy away, or hand out important documents, especially anything that could be a will, funeral plan, insurance document, bank letter, solicitor letter, or digital account information.
- Do not let family pressure push you into an irreversible decision, such as burial or cremation, before you have checked for known wishes and whether anyone else has formal authority.
- Do not finalise funeral details if a coroner, medical examiner, registrar, or procurator fiscal contact has told you that paperwork or consent is still outstanding.

## What to do now
1. Ask the person contacting you: "What decision is needed today, what is the real deadline, and what happens if I do not decide yet?"
   Ask them to separate urgent safety or paperwork steps from choices that can wait, such as service details, announcements, flowers, music, or who attends.

2. Confirm what role they think you have.
   Say: "Are you asking me as the emergency contact, the nearest relative, the executor, or the person who can authorise this particular step?" If they need an executor or administrator, ask them to put that in writing and move to checking for a will.

3. Check quickly for a will, executor, funeral plan, or written wishes.
   Look for a will packet, solicitor letter, funeral plan, life insurance document, "important papers" folder, safe, desk drawer, email from a solicitor, or message to a close friend or partner. If you find a will, identify the executor or executors and tell the organisation you are dealing with that you are checking authority before deciding.

4. Ask which process applies where the death occurred.
   Ask whether the death occurred in England, Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland, and whether it has been referred for investigation or certification review. Then ask: "Who is my contact, what document are we waiting for, and what must not be decided until that document is issued?"

5. If the death was in England or Wales, ask about the medical examiner or coroner route.
   For deaths not being investigated by a coroner, the medical examiner process is usually part of confirming the medical cause of death before registration. Ask who will contact you, when the registrar can be contacted, and whether any funeral step is only provisional until registration or coroner paperwork is complete.

6. If the death was in Scotland or Northern Ireland, ask the local contact what is paused.
   In Scotland, if the death has been reported to the procurator fiscal, ask who will update you and what decisions should wait. In Northern Ireland, if the death has been referred to the coroner, ask whether funeral arrangements need coroner consent before they are made final.

7. Make only reversible holding arrangements.
   You can ask for the person’s body and belongings to be kept safe, ask for belongings to be logged rather than distributed, ask a funeral director for written costs without finalising burial or cremation, and delay public announcements until known wishes and authority are clearer.

8. Use one calm message to reduce family pressure.
   Send: "I am checking who has authority, what paperwork is needed, and whether anything is genuinely urgent. Please send me any known wishes, will details, funeral plan information, or solicitor details. I will share confirmed next steps when I have them."

9. Secure immediate practical things if it is safe and appropriate.
   Lock the home, secure pets, collect keys, and keep ID, will or funeral paperwork, phone, address book, and insurance details together. Take photos of paperwork piles before moving them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the full funeral format, wording, music, flowers, clothing, wake, or guest list right now.
- You do not need to sort the whole estate today.
- You do not need to answer every relative, message, or call yourself.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements today.
- You do not need to pay large funeral costs before you have asked what funds, plans, insurance, or estate payment options may exist.

## Important reassurance
Being asked to decide quickly after a death can make it feel as if every answer has to be immediate and perfect. It is reasonable to slow the process down, ask for things in writing, and make only the smallest necessary decisions while you check authority, paperwork, and known wishes.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the first hours or days. Later decisions about probate, disputes, funeral contracts, benefits, debts, overseas issues, or complex estates may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, bereavement, or other professional advice. Processes vary across the UK and by the circumstances of the death, so ask the organisation you are dealing with what authority they require, what can be paused, and what would be irreversible today.
