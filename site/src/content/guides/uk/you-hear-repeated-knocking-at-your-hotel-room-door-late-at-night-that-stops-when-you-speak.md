# What to do if… you hear repeated knocking at your hotel room door late at night that stops when you speak (UK)

## Short answer
Do not open the door. Keep it locked, move away from the doorway, and call reception from inside the room to verify what is happening and request a staff or security check.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door just to see who it is, even if the person claims to be hotel staff.
- Do not undo the deadbolt, chain, bar, or secondary latch while you listen, look, or talk.
- Do not confirm that you are alone, or share your name, room number, travel plans, or payment details through the door.
- Do not go into the corridor to investigate or confront anyone.
- Do not keep talking through the door if the knocking stops whenever you respond.
- Do not rely on a phone number, message, or instruction given by the person outside the door.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the door and step back.** Engage the main lock, deadbolt, chain, bar, or any built-in secondary latch your room has. Move away from the door and keep your phone, room key, glasses if you need them, and shoes within reach.
2. **Turn on the lights.** Switch on the main room lights so you can see clearly and move safely.
3. **Limit contact through the door.** If the knocking happens again, you can say once, firmly: “Who is it?” Then stop talking and keep the door closed.
4. **Call reception from inside the room.** Use the in-room phone if you can, or the hotel’s official number from your booking information. Tell them:
   - your room number
   - there has been repeated knocking that stops whenever you speak
   - you are not opening the door
   - you want the corridor checked
5. **Verify any claimed staff visit through reception.** Ask reception to confirm the person’s name, role, and reason for being at your door. If reception cannot clearly confirm it, treat the person as not verified and keep the door closed.
6. **Ask for safer hotel support.** Ask reception to send two staff members or security to check the corridor, and ask them to stay on the phone until the check is complete.
7. **Call police if the risk feels immediate.** Call 999 if someone is trying to get in, tampering with the lock or handle, threatening you, refusing to leave, or you feel in immediate danger. If it is suspicious but not an emergency, call 101 or report through the police contact route.
8. **Change the situation if you still feel unsafe.** Ask reception for a room move nearer reception, a staffed area, or a lift; ask for a staff escort if you need to leave the room.
9. **Make a brief note once you are safer.** Write down the time, what happened, who you spoke to at reception, and what they did. Keep it short.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to make a formal complaint, ask for compensation, or leave the hotel.
- You do not need to identify the person yourself before asking for help.
- You do not need to keep listening at the door; staying behind a locked barrier and getting verification is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to treat repeated knocking that stops when you speak as a safety concern. Keeping the door closed, using reception to verify, and calling police if the danger feels immediate are appropriate first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and reduce immediate risk. Later decisions may need help from hotel management, police, your travel provider, or another specialist support service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, travel, medical, therapeutic, financial, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe or believe someone is trying to enter your room, prioritise immediate help from reception, hotel security, and emergency services.
