# What to do if… an officer asks you to write down your account of events immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and ask for free legal advice before you write, dictate, or sign any account of events. If you cannot get advice immediately, say clearly that you are not able to provide a written account right now and you do not want to guess.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t write an on-the-spot narrative just to make pressure stop.
- Don’t fill gaps with guesses, rough timings, distances, or details you heard from someone else.
- Don’t sign anything you have not read slowly, understood, and corrected.
- Don’t accept wording that is not yours if someone else writes it down.
- Don’t hand over your phone to check notes or messages unless you understand the lawful basis and have asked for legal advice.

## What to do now
1. **Ask what your status is.** Say: “Am I under arrest or detained? Am I under caution? Is this a voluntary interview?”
2. **Ask for legal advice before writing.** Say: “I want free legal advice before I make any written statement or sign anything.” If you are at a police station, ask for the duty solicitor or your own solicitor if you have one.
3. **Use a short holding line if you are pushed.** Say: “I am not providing a written account from memory. I am requesting legal advice first.”
4. **If they insist you write something, keep it to the refusal.** Write only: “I am not making a statement of events. I request legal advice before I write or sign anything.” Add your name, date, and time. Do not add an account of events.
5. **If you are under caution and being interviewed, do not treat a written statement as automatic.** In England and Wales, recorded interviews usually mean a written statement under caution is not normally needed unless you expressly wish to make one. Say: “I do not wish to make a written statement. I want legal advice first.”
6. **If you decide to write despite not having advice, do not guess.** Use “I do not remember”, “I am not sure”, or “I need advice before answering that” instead of completing a timeline you are unsure about.
7. **If someone else writes anything for you, read every line before signing.** Ask for changes where the words are wrong or incomplete. Do not sign if it is not accurate.
8. **Ask for support if you need it.** If you are under 18, have a mental health condition, learning disability, or are struggling to understand, ask for an appropriate adult. If you do not understand English well, ask for an interpreter. If you are deaf, have a speech difficulty, or need help communicating, ask for communication support before anything is written or signed.
9. **Make a simple note for yourself.** When you safely can, write down the officer’s name or number, where you were, the time, what they asked you to write, and that you asked for legal advice.

## What can wait
- You do not need to produce a perfect timeline, exact wording, or a full narrative right now.
- You do not need to decide now whether you will ever make a full statement.
- You do not need to prove you are cooperating by writing while shocked, tired, confused, or unsupported.
- You do not need to explain every gap in your memory before getting legal advice.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for memory to feel patchy after a stressful event. Asking for legal advice before writing or signing is a standard safeguard, not an admission of guilt and not making things difficult.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment you are being asked to write immediately. Police procedures can vary within the UK, and later decisions about interviews, statements, complaints, or court issues may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. The safest immediate focus is to pause, ask for legal advice, avoid guesses, and avoid signing words you do not understand or agree with.
