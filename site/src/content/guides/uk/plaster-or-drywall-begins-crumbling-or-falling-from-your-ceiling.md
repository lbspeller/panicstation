# What to do if… plaster or drywall begins crumbling or falling from your ceiling (UK)

## Short answer
Get everyone out from under the damaged area and keep the room closed off. Treat it as a possible partial ceiling collapse, possible electrical risk, and possible hazardous dust until it has been checked.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t stand underneath the damaged area to inspect it or try to catch falling pieces.
- Don’t poke, push, pull, drill, scrape, or sand the ceiling.
- Don’t dry-sweep the debris or use a normal household vacuum until the material is known to be safe.
- Don’t move furniture under the damaged area unless it is essential for immediate safety and you can do it without going underneath.
- Don’t touch switches, fittings, appliances, or the consumer unit if they are wet, sparking, buzzing, scorched, or smell of burning.

## What to do now
1. **Move people and pets away from the area immediately.** Use another room, hallway, or outside space if needed. Keep clear of beds, sofas, desks, or doorways directly under the damaged ceiling.
2. **Stop anyone going back in.** Close the door. If you can do it without going near the debris, place a towel at the bottom of the door to reduce dust spreading.
3. **Call 999 now if there is immediate danger.** Call if anyone is injured, there is fire, smoke, sparks, a burning smell, a serious electrical danger, large sections are still falling, or you think the structure could fail further right now.
4. **Look only from a safe position.** From the doorway or another safe place, check for water staining, dripping, a bulge, widening cracks, a hanging light fitting, exposed wiring, or fresh debris still falling.
5. **If water or electrics may be involved, avoid the room.** If you can reach a dry consumer unit safely without passing under the damaged ceiling or standing in water, switch off power to the affected area or the whole property if you are unsure. If the consumer unit or floor near it is wet, do not touch it.
6. **If there is an active leak, try to stop only the source you can reach safely.** Turn off an accessible stop tap if you can do so without entering the unsafe area. If the leak may be from above, contact the neighbour, building management, landlord, or emergency plumber so the source can be checked quickly.
7. **If you rent or lease, report it as urgent.** Contact your landlord, letting agent, housing officer, freeholder, or building management and say: “Ceiling material is falling, the room is unsafe, and there may be a leak, electrical risk, structural issue, or hazardous dust.”
8. **If you own the property, contact suitable emergency help.** Use an emergency plumber for an active leak, a qualified electrician for electrical concerns, and a structural engineer or reputable building contractor if the ceiling is sagging, cracking, or continuing to fail.
9. **If you cannot get a response and the room may be unsafe, contact your local council.** Ask for the relevant housing, environmental health, building control, or emergency out-of-hours route available in your area.
10. **Document without disturbing the debris.** Take photos or video from the doorway, including a wide shot, a close-up using zoom, any water staining, and the time the problem started.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now who is at fault, who will pay, or whether to make an insurance claim.
- You do not need to clean the debris right now.
- You do not need to choose a permanent repair before the area has been made safe.
- You do not need to move everything from the room unless it can be done safely and is needed to prevent immediate harm.

## Important reassurance
This is alarming because it happens suddenly and creates mess, but the first job is small: get clear, keep others out, avoid disturbing dust or electrics, and contact the right help. A ceiling problem may be localised, especially if linked to a leak, but it should not be treated as minor until it has been checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce danger and buy time. Repairs, insurance, responsibility, rent issues, and longer-term housing decisions may need specialist help after the immediate risk is controlled.
