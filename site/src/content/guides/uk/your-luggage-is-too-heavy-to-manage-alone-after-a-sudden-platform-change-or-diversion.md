# What to do if… your luggage is too heavy to manage alone after a sudden platform change or diversion (UK)

## Short answer
Stop where you are, make the luggage stable, and ask station staff or use a Help Point before attempting stairs, rushing, or lifting.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t try to lift or drag a heavy bag up steps if it does not feel safe; you could injure yourself, drop it, or block others.
- Don’t run for the new platform while pulling heavy luggage.
- Don’t leave luggage unattended while you check boards, find a lift, or ask for help.
- Don’t accept help from someone who makes you feel pressured or unsafe; look for staff, a Help Point, or a staffed ticket or information area.
- Don’t wedge luggage in doorways, on stairs, near platform edges, or across walkways.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safe pause point.** Move out of the main flow of people if you can do so safely, such as beside a wall, pillar, staffed area, waiting area, or Help Point. Put bags upright with handles facing you so they are less likely to roll or be taken by mistake.
2. **Check the change before moving everything.** Look at the nearest departure board, station screen, or official journey app so you know whether the new platform is on the same level or needs stairs, a bridge, a subway, or a lift.
3. **Ask through an official station route.** If you can see staff, say: “My luggage is too heavy to move safely after the platform change. Can you tell me the safest way to get to platform X?” If you cannot see staff, use a Help Point or go to a staffed information or ticket office if one is nearby.
4. **Be clear about what you need.** Ask for step-free route options, lift locations, ramps if relevant, and whether any lifts are out of service before you start moving.
5. **If disability, age, injury, pain, pregnancy, or reduced mobility is part of the problem, say so.** Ask staff whether unbooked assistance or “turn up and go” assistance is available. Staff may be able to help you navigate the station, board or leave the train, arrange a ramp, or assist with manageable luggage where the service rules allow.
6. **Do not assume staff can carry an excessively heavy bag.** Staff may be limited by safety rules, luggage limits, staffing, and the station layout. Ask what safe options are available, such as a step-free route, waiting for the next service, a luggage trolley if the station has them, or another safe place to pause.
7. **If you may miss the train, protect yourself first.** Tell staff: “I may miss this train because I cannot move the luggage safely.” Ask where to wait, what the next practical service is, and how to keep your luggage with you.
8. **If you are already on the train when a diversion or platform issue is announced, tell on-board staff as soon as practical.** Say that you need extra time or help to get off with heavy luggage, so you are not forced into a last-moment scramble.
9. **If you feel pain, dizziness, or you have already strained something, stop lifting.** Ask staff for help getting to a seat or calmer area. If you need urgent medical help, ask staff to call for assistance.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, claim compensation, or rearrange the whole journey.
- You do not need to repack every bag in public unless making one item lighter is the safest immediate option.
- You do not need to rush onto the next train if doing so risks injury, losing your luggage, or blocking other passengers.
- You do not need to solve onward travel until you and your luggage are safely out of the immediate station crush.

## Important reassurance
A sudden platform change or diversion can turn normal luggage into a real safety problem. Pausing, asking for the safest route, and accepting that you may need the next service is a sensible response.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the moment, reduce injury risk, and avoid losing your luggage. Later decisions about re-routing, refunds, complaints, or onward transport may need help from the train operator, station staff, or another appropriate service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Assistance varies by station, train operator, staffing, and safety rules. If you are hurt or feel unwell, prioritise getting medical help.
