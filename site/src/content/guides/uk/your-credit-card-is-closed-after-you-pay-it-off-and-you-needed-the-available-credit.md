# What to do if… your credit card is closed after you pay it off and you needed the available credit (UK)

## Short answer
Secure an alternative payment method for any urgent transactions today, then contact the card issuer to determine if the closure is a temporary restriction or a permanent termination.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t submit multiple new credit applications today to replace the lost limit (this can lower your credit score further).
- Don’t assume the closure is a mistake or fraud until you speak to the provider; lenders can legally "restrict or defer" credit use immediately.
- Don’t close other active accounts in a panic, as this further reduces your total available credit.
- Don’t use high-cost emergency lenders or "payday" loans as your first backup if you have an existing bank overdraft or other cards.
- Don’t ignore any "trailing" interest or fees that may still be added to the account even if the card is closed.

## What to do now
1. **Switch to an alternative payment method for immediate needs.**
   - If you need to pay a deposit for car hire or a hotel, check if they accept **debit cards or bank transfers** (note: they may take a higher "hold" amount from a debit card).
   - If the payment is online, check if **PayPal, Apple Pay, or Google Pay** are accepted using a linked debit card.

2. **Call the card issuer to identify the specific status of the account.**
   - **Ask whether the account is "fully terminated" or "temporarily restricted/suspended"** due to a recent large payment or internal review.
   - **Request the specific reason for the closure** (e.g., inactivity, risk profile change, or breach of terms).
   - **Ask if the "withdrawal of credit" was done under Section 98 or 98A** of the Consumer Credit Act, and if they sent a notice period (usually 2 months).

3. **Check for "pending" transactions or refunds.**
   - **Confirm that your final payoff has fully cleared** and that no "shadow" balance (accrued interest) remains.
   - **Check if any regular Direct Debits** were due to be paid from that card in the next 48 hours and move them to a different account immediately.

4. **Verify your status across all UK credit reference agencies.**
   - **Request statutory credit reports** from Experian, Equifax, TransUnion, and Crediva to see how the closure is being reported.
   - **Check for a "notice of correction" or any markers** that suggest why the lender viewed the account as a risk.

5. **Request a reinstatement if the closure was due to a misunderstanding.**
   - **Explain why the available credit is urgently needed** (e.g., stranded while traveling) and ask if a "temporary override" is possible.
   - **Ask for the closure to be reversed** if it was triggered automatically by inactivity immediately following your payment.

6. **Initiate a formal complaint if you believe the lender failed to give notice.**
   - **Inform the provider you are making a formal complaint** and ask for a reference number.
   - **Note the response timeline:** For complaints about "payment services," they generally have 15 business days to respond; for general credit issues, they have up to 8 weeks.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a permanent "replacement" card today; focus only on the immediate transaction.
- You do not need to contact the Credit Reference Agencies to "fix" your score today; the report takes weeks to update.
- You do not need to close other credit facilities to "tidy up" your finances right now.

## Important reassurance
It is common for lenders to close accounts that have been dormant or where the risk profile has changed once the balance hits zero. This is often an automated process and not a personal judgment on your character. Securing a temporary bridge (like a bank overdraft) is a standard way to manage this while you seek a longer-term solution.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a sudden loss of credit access only. It does not provide long-term debt management or legal advice regarding credit agreements.

## Important note
Credit card issuers in the UK have a legal right to restrict or withdraw credit facilities without notice if they have "objectively justified" reasons, such as a significant increase in credit risk. While they must generally give notice to terminate the entire agreement, the "available credit" can disappear instantly.
