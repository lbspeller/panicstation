# What to do if… you receive a notice that a past-due bill will be escalated to enforcement unless paid today (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay only because the notice says “today”. First verify the sender, the debt, the amount, and the enforcement stage using contact details you find independently.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links, scan QR codes, or call a number in the message until you have verified the sender through a trusted route.
- Do not pay until the original creditor or official enforcement route confirms the account, reference, amount, and next step.
- Do not pay by gift cards, crypto, cash collection, or bank transfer to new details because someone says it is the only way to stop action today.
- Do not share one-time passcodes, card PINs, online banking logins, or full security answers with anyone who contacted you.
- Do not agree to anything you do not understand, including a controlled goods agreement, while panicked.
- Do not let someone into your home just because they say they are an enforcement agent or bailiff. You can speak through a closed door.

## What to do now
1. **Save the notice without engaging with it.** Screenshot or photograph the sender details, date, amount, reference number, payment instructions, and every page or message.

2. **Find the trusted contact route yourself.** Use a recent statement, the back of your card, a contract, a court notice, a council website, or the organisation’s official website typed into your browser. Do not use contact details from the urgent notice until they have been checked.

3. **Ask the creditor or official route four direct questions.** Ask: “Is this account mine?”, “What is the balance?”, “Has it been passed to enforcement?”, and “What is the next action date?” Write down who you spoke to, the time, and what they said.

4. **If it says “bailiff”, “enforcement agent”, or “notice of enforcement”, check the stage carefully.** In England and Wales, enforcement agents must usually give 14 clear days’ notice before a first visit to take control of goods. Clear days usually exclude the day you receive the notice, the day of the visit, Sundays, Christmas Day and bank holidays. If the notice is for Scotland or Northern Ireland, do not rely on England and Wales timing; check through free debt advice or the relevant official body.

5. **Check whether the notice has the basic details.** Look for your correct name and address, the creditor, the amount, the type of debt, a reference number, the enforcement company or court details, and clear dates. If details are missing, wrong, or do not match what the creditor confirms, treat it as suspicious and ask for everything in writing.

6. **If someone visits or calls claiming to be enforcement, verify before doing anything.** Keep the door closed. Ask for proof of identity, the company name, the creditor, and a breakdown of the amount owed through the letterbox, at the window, or in writing. If they say they are a certificated enforcement agent in England and Wales, check the official register. If they say they are a court-based bailiff or another type of officer, verify through the relevant court using contact details you find yourself.

7. **If it is real and you cannot pay today, ask for a pause and written details.** Ask for a full balance breakdown, the date of the next action, and written confirmation of any hold, payment plan, or refusal. If you dispute the debt, say: “I am verifying or disputing this. Please put the details in writing.”

8. **If you live in England or Wales, consider Breathing Space urgently.** Contact a free debt advice provider and ask whether the Breathing Space scheme could apply. It can temporarily pause many creditor actions and freeze most interest and charges on included debts while you get debt advice, but it does not remove the debt and you still need to keep up ongoing bills where you can.

9. **If you suspect a scam or already paid, act quickly.** Contact your bank or card provider immediately and say you may have been scammed. Then use the official police fraud reporting route for your UK nation and keep any reference number.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your long-term debt solution today.
- You do not need to negotiate by phone while you are unsure who you are dealing with.
- You do not need to let anyone into your home to prove who you are.
- You do not need to send documents, payslips, bank statements, or ID to an unverified sender.
- You do not need to prioritise this over immediate safety, medication, childcare, or getting through the next hour while you verify it.

## Important reassurance
Urgent payment wording is meant to make you act fast. Taking a short pause to verify the sender and the stage of the debt is not ignoring it; it is a protective first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: verify, avoid scam payments, avoid rushed agreements, and get the situation into writing. Later decisions about payment, disputes, complaints, or formal debt options may need free debt advice or other specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Debt enforcement rules and names differ across the UK and by debt type, so use official routes and free independent debt advice if you are unsure what you have received.
