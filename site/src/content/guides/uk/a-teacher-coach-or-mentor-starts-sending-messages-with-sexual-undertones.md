# What to do if… a teacher, coach, or mentor starts sending messages with sexual undertones (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all one-to-one messaging immediately and speak to a designated safeguarding lead as soon as possible. You do not need to prove the sender’s intent or handle this interaction alone.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to keep the messages a secret or move the conversation to private or "disappearing" apps.
- Do not delete the messages even if you feel embarrassed or worried about what people will think.
- Do not reply to the messages to try and "be polite" or to explain why the contact is making you uncomfortable.
- Do not confront the adult in person to ask what they meant.
- Do not share screenshots publicly on social media or with large groups of friends.

## What to do now
1. **Stop interacting with the adult immediately.** **Do not send any further replies, even to say "stop" or "leave me alone," as any response can be used to prolong the contact.**
2. **Take screenshots of the messages and save them in a safe place.** **Ensure the screenshots show the sender’s name or handle, the date and time, and the platform used.**
   - Once you have taken a few key screenshots, stop looking at the messages to reduce your distress.
3. **Contact the Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) at your school or college.** **Ask to speak to the DSL or a deputy DSL privately and tell them you have a safeguarding concern regarding an adult’s conduct.**
   - If this is a sports club, ask for the Welfare Officer or Safeguarding Lead.
4. **Inform the safeguarding person that you want this handled as a formal allegation against an adult.** **In England, schools must follow statutory guidance (KCSIE) which involves notifying the Local Authority Designated Officer (LADO) for managing allegations against staff.**
5. **Call the NSPCC Report Abuse in Education helpline if you want independent advice before or after speaking to your school.** **You can reach them on 0800 136 663 for confidential support specifically regarding sexual abuse or harassment in education settings.**
6. **Report the contact to CEOP if the interaction happened online.** **The Child Exploitation and Online Protection Command (CEOP) is a law enforcement agency specifically for reporting online grooming or sexual communication.**
7. **Contact the police on 101 if you believe a crime has been committed or 999 if you are in immediate danger.** **Inform the operator that an adult in a position of trust is sending sexually suggestive messages.**
8. **Reach out to Childline for confidential support if you are under 19.** **You can call 0800 1111 or use their online 1-2-1 chat to talk about how you are feeling without having to give your name.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now if you want to make a police statement or take legal action.
- You do not need to have a perfect explanation for why you didn't report it sooner or why you replied to earlier messages.
- You do not need to manage the adult’s reaction or worry about their job; that is the responsibility of the safeguarding authorities.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel confused, sick, or even guilty when a trusted adult crosses a professional boundary. These feelings are often a result of "grooming" tactics used by adults to make their behavior seem normal or to make you feel responsible for the interaction. This is not your fault, and you have the right to feel safe and respected in your learning or coaching environment.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safeguarding instructions only. Further steps involving professional misconduct panels (such as the Teaching Regulation Agency in England or GTCS in Scotland) will be handled by the relevant authorities during the investigation.

## Important note
This information is based on UK statutory safeguarding guidance as of 2026. Professional misconduct reporting routes vary by nation: in England, contact the Teaching Regulation Agency (TRA); in Scotland, the General Teaching Council for Scotland (GTCS); in Wales, the Education Workforce Council (EWC); and in Northern Ireland, the General Teaching Council for Northern Ireland (GTCNI).
