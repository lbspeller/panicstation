# What to do if… you develop sudden wheezing or your asthma symptoms worsen unexpectedly (UK)

## Short answer
Sit upright and use your reliever inhaler immediately. Call **999** for an ambulance if you feel worse at any point, you are not better after the maximum reliever dose below, or you do not have a reliever inhaler you can use.

## Do not do these things
- Do not lie down flat or curl up if sitting upright helps you breathe.
- Do not “wait it out” if you are getting worse, struggling to speak, or your reliever is not helping.
- Do not take more than the emergency reliever doses below without getting urgent medical help.
- Do not leave someone alone if they are having a significant asthma attack.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you are very breathless, dizzy, or getting worse; call **999**.
- Do not use alcohol or sedating medicines to manage panic or breathlessness; get urgent help if breathing is worsening.
- Do not rely on someone else’s prescription inhaler. If you do not have your own reliever inhaler, call **999**.

## What to do now
1. **Get into the best position for breathing.**  
   Sit upright, lean slightly forward if that helps, loosen tight clothing, and try to keep your breathing steady.

2. **Use your reliever inhaler right away, using the option that matches your inhaler.**  
   - **If you have a “blue” reliever inhaler, usually salbutamol:**  
     Take **1 puff every 30–60 seconds** until you feel better, **up to 10 puffs**. Shake the inhaler between puffs and use a **spacer** if you have one.
   - **If you use an AIR or MART inhaler, used for both prevention and relief:**  
     Take **1 puff every 1–3 minutes** until you feel better, **up to 6 puffs**.
   - If your personal asthma action plan gives emergency instructions from your clinician, follow it, but still call **999** if you feel worse, you are not improving after the emergency reliever dose, or you cannot use your reliever.

3. **Call 999 for an ambulance immediately if any of these are true:**
   - You feel **worse at any point**, even while using the inhaler.
   - You are **not better after 10 puffs** of a blue reliever, or **not better after 6 puffs** of an AIR or MART inhaler.
   - You are struggling to speak more than a few words, you are exhausted, drowsy, confused, or your lips or face look bluish.
   - You do not have your reliever inhaler, it is empty, or you cannot use it.

4. **If you have called 999 and help has not arrived after 10 minutes, and you are not improving:**  
   Repeat step 2, using the same puff timing and maximum. If you are still not improving after repeating it, **contact 999 again immediately**.

5. **If you improve enough that you do not need 999, still treat this as urgent.**
   - If you managed the attack **yourself at home**, ask your GP practice for an **urgent same-day** appointment with a GP or asthma nurse.
   - If you were treated by **paramedics** or in **hospital or emergency care**, ask your GP practice for follow-up **no later than 2 working days** after treatment or discharge.
   - If you cannot reach your GP practice or it is out of hours, use **NHS 111**.

6. **Once you are steady, reduce the chance of another flare in the next few hours.**
   - Move away from obvious triggers such as smoke, fumes, strong fragrance, cold air, or an allergen that is clearly making symptoms worse.
   - Check that your reliever inhaler is not empty.
   - Use a spacer if you have one and it fits your inhaler.
   - If you use a **peak flow meter** and know your personal best, check it and note the number.

7. **Write down a quick event note while it is fresh.**  
   Note the time symptoms started, what may have triggered them, what you took, how many puffs you used, and whether you called 111 or 999. This can help a clinician review your asthma plan safely.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you need a new diagnosis or what your long-term medication should be.
- You do not need to deep-clean your home, change your whole routine, or identify every possible trigger today.
- Repeat prescriptions and paperwork can wait until you are breathing normally, unless your reliever inhaler is empty or nearly empty. Focus on urgent review first.

## Important reassurance
Worsening asthma can feel frightening and can escalate quickly, even if you have been well for a long time. Acting early by sitting up, using your reliever correctly, and calling for help when needed is the safest first step.

## Scope note
This is first-steps guidance for the next minutes to 1–2 days. Ongoing control, medication changes, and trigger management should be done with a clinician, such as your GP or asthma nurse, using your asthma action plan.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not a medical diagnosis or personalised treatment plan. If you are worried at any point, symptoms are severe, or you are not improving quickly with your reliever, call **999**.
