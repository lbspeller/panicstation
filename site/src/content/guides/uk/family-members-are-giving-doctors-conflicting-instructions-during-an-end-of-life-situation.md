# What to do if… family members are giving doctors conflicting instructions during an end-of-life situation (UK)

## Short answer
Ask the senior clinician or nurse in charge to convene an urgent family meeting to clarify the patient’s **recorded wishes**, identify the **legal decision-maker**, and document a **Best Interests** plan.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "outvote" other relatives or pressure medical staff to choose a side during ward rounds.
- Do not give clinical instructions to staff unless you have confirmed legal authority (e.g., as a registered Attorney or Guardian).
- Do not engage in arguments in the presence of the patient, as this can cause physical and emotional distress.
- Do not assume that being "next of kin" grants you the legal right to override clinical advice or other family members.
- Do not record conversations with staff without explicit hospital consent, as this often blocks open communication and trust.
- Do not demand specific treatments that clinicians have deemed non-beneficial; instead, ask for a formal second opinion or clinical review.

## What to do now
1. **Ask the clinical team to identify the legal decision-maker for this specific admission.**  
   **Request that the nurse in charge or consultant clarifies who holds legal authority according to the patient's records.**  
   - In England/Wales: Check for a Health & Welfare Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) or an Advance Decision to Refuse Treatment (ADRT).  
   - In Scotland: Check for a Welfare Power of Attorney (WPA) or a Welfare Guardian under the Adults with Incapacity framework.  
   - If no legal document exists, the senior clinician is the decision-maker, but they must consult family to determine "Best Interests."

2. **Request a formal assessment and documentation of the patient's mental capacity.**  
   **Ask the doctor to confirm if the patient currently lacks the capacity to make this specific treatment decision.**  
   - If the patient has capacity, their decision is final, and family instructions are secondary.  
   - If the patient lacks capacity, ask that this is recorded in the notes alongside the evidence used for the assessment.

3. **Request an urgent, structured family meeting with the senior medical team.**  
   **Ask for a meeting that includes the consultant, the lead nurse, and a representative from the Palliative Care team if available.**  
   - Use this meeting to move discussions away from the bedside.  
   - Ask the team to explain the clinical options and the reasoning behind their recommendations.  
   - Ensure the patient’s known values, faith, and past statements are the primary focus of the discussion.

4. **Establish a single point of contact for the family to reduce communication errors.**  
   **Propose one named spokesperson and one backup person to receive and distribute all medical updates.**  
   - Request that the ward documents these names in the patient's communication plan.  
   - This prevents staff from receiving "conflicting instructions" from different relatives throughout the day.

5. **Provide copies of all relevant legal documents to the ward staff immediately.**  
   **Ensure the hospital has physical or digital copies of any LPA, WPA, or Advance Statement.**  
   - If the documents are at home, ask someone to retrieve them or send a high-quality photo to the ward's secure email.  
   - Do not rely on verbal claims of "power of attorney" without the registered paperwork.

6. **Involve hospital mediation or advocacy services to stabilize the situation.**  
   **Contact the relevant patient support service to help facilitate communication and resolve disputes.**  
   - In England/Wales: Contact PALS (Patient Advice and Liaison Service).  
   - In Scotland: Contact PASS (Patient Advice and Support Service) or the Mental Welfare Commission.  
   - If the patient has no one to speak for them and there is a major dispute, ask if an Independent Mental Capacity Advocate (IMCA) is required.

7. **Ask for the hospital's formal escalation protocol if a consensus cannot be reached.**  
   **Request information on how the hospital handles "Best Interests" disputes that remain unresolved after a family meeting.**  
   - This may involve a second clinical opinion from a different consultant.  
   - In extreme cases, the hospital may need to seek a declaration from the Court of Protection (England/Wales) or the Sheriff Court (Scotland).

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family grievances or inheritance issues during the clinical crisis.
- You do not need to file a formal complaint today; focus on stabilizing the current care plan and ensuring patient comfort first.
- You do not need to decide on funeral arrangements while treatment escalation or ceiling-of-care decisions are still being finalized.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for families to feel overwhelmed and disagree when a loved one is at the end of life. These disagreements often stem from a shared desire to do the "right thing" under extreme stress. Moving the conversation into a structured medical framework—focusing on legal authority and the patient's own recorded wishes—is a standard process designed to protect the patient and reduce the burden on you.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate steps to stabilize communication and decision-making during an acute hospital crisis. It does not cover long-term legal disputes or the process of applying for guardianship after a crisis has passed.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or medical advice. Procedures vary between NHS Trusts and across the different nations of the UK. If you believe a patient is at immediate risk of harm or their rights are being breached, ask to speak with the hospital's Duty Manager or the Consultant on-call immediately.
