# What to do if… your messaging app shows a linked device you do not recognise (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as possible unauthorised access. Unlink the device inside the messaging app first, then secure the account, email, and phone-number access that could let someone link it again.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply to the unknown device, send test messages, or send codes or screenshots to anyone who asks for them.
- Do not approve QR pairing, companion-device prompts, login approvals, or security-code requests you did not start.
- Do not change lots of settings at random before removing the unknown linked device.
- Do not reuse an old password or a small variation of one.
- Do not ignore the alert because the app still looks normal; linked devices may keep access to current or future messages, depending on the app.

## What to do now
1. **Use the safest device and connection you have available.** Use your own phone or computer, and switch from public Wi-Fi to mobile data or a trusted network if you can do that quickly.
2. **Unlink the unknown companion device inside the messaging app.**
   - Look for menus such as **Linked devices**, **Companion devices**, **Device pairing**, **Web**, or **Sessions**.
   - Log out, unlink, or remove any device you do not recognise.
   - If there is more than one unknown entry, remove each one.
3. **Take a quick record for yourself before you change more settings.** Screenshot the linked-device page if you can do it safely, including device names, dates, times, and any “last active” details.
4. **Secure the account route that could let the device come back.**
   - Change the password for the account behind the app, such as your email, Apple account, Google account, Microsoft account, or the app account itself.
   - Change the password for any email account that can reset the messaging account.
   - Turn on two-step verification or two-factor authentication where the app or account offers it.
   - Use the account’s security page to sign out other sessions or devices you do not recognise.
5. **Check the email account that can reset the messaging app.** Look for unfamiliar forwarding addresses, mail rules, filters, recovery email addresses, or recovery phone numbers, and remove anything you did not set.
6. **If the unknown device will not unlink, or it comes back, use the provider’s official support route.** Go to the app or account provider’s official website yourself and search for account recovery or compromised account support. Avoid links in unexpected messages or emails.
7. **Check for SIM-swap warning signs if the app uses SMS codes.** If your phone lost signal, you received SIM-change or porting messages, or you cannot receive calls or texts, contact your mobile network provider using a number from its official website or your bill. Ask whether there has been an unauthorised SIM swap or porting request, and ask what extra account security is available.
8. **Warn only the people who need to know right now.** Use a different channel if possible and say: “My messaging account may have been accessed. If you get unusual messages, links, or payment requests from me, do not act on them.”
9. **Report it if money, threats, fraud, or impersonation are involved.**
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud for cyber crime or fraud.
   - If you live in Scotland, or the crime happened there, contact Police Scotland on 101.
   - If the crime is happening now or anyone is in immediate danger, call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly how it happened right now.
- You do not need to confront anyone or post a public explanation.
- You do not need to gather more proof than a quick screenshot before securing the account.
- You do not need to factory-reset your phone unless there are later signs of malware, repeated relinking, or advice from the provider or a trusted specialist.

## Important reassurance
A linked-device alert is unsettling, but there are clear first steps. Removing the unknown device, securing the account route, and warning close contacts usually reduces the immediate risk quickly.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stop access and prevent immediate harm. Later decisions, such as deeper device checks, workplace reporting, or formal evidence handling, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide gives general practical information only. It is not legal, technical-forensics, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice.
