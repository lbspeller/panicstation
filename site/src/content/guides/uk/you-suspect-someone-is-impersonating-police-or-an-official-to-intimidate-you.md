# What to do if… you suspect someone is impersonating police or an official to intimidate you (UK)

## Short answer
Create distance and verify independently. If you feel at risk now, or someone is trying to force entry, make you leave with them, or take money or property, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t let them inside, follow them, or get into a vehicle just because they sound official.
- Don’t hand over cash, bank cards, bank details, PINs, one-time codes, gift cards, crypto, passwords, or your phone for checking.
- Don’t transfer money, withdraw cash, buy currency, or give cards to a courier because someone says it is part of an investigation.
- Don’t call back a number they give you, click their links, open attachments, or trust caller ID on its own.
- Don’t show identity documents through an open doorway or share personal details to confirm who you are.
- Don’t argue, accuse, or try to catch them out if you feel unsafe; prioritise distance and independent verification.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If they are at your door, keep the door locked and speak through the door, a window, or an intercom. If you are outside, move toward a public, well-lit place with other people nearby.
2. **Call 999 if there is immediate risk.** Say: “I think someone is impersonating an officer or official and intimidating me. I’m at [location].”
3. **If it is not an emergency, end the contact and verify through 101.** If the contact was by phone, wait five minutes before using the same phone line, check you can hear a dial tone, or use another phone. Then call 101 and ask whether the officer, visit, call, or request is genuine.
4. **Ask for details only if you can do it safely.** For someone claiming to be police, ask for their name, shoulder number or warrant number, police force, and reason for contact. If they are in person, ask to see their warrant card without opening the door or closing the distance.
5. **If they claim to be from the National Crime Agency, use the official NCA officer-verification route.** Use only contact details you find independently on the NCA website, not details given by the person contacting you.
6. **If they claim to be from another official authority, verify through an official channel you find yourself.** This includes council officers, court staff, immigration officials, enforcement agents, utility investigators, or other official-sounding roles. Do not use phone numbers, links, QR codes, or email addresses they provide.
7. **Preserve simple evidence without putting yourself at risk.** Save messages, emails, voicemails, screenshots, caller numbers, reference numbers, names used, times, locations, and what they demanded or threatened.
8. **Report through the right route.**
   - For immediate danger or a crime in progress, call 999.
   - For non-emergency police impersonation or intimidation, call 101 or use your local police force’s online reporting route.
   - If you are in Scotland, Police Scotland says police impersonation fraud can be reported online, by phone, or in person; use 101 for non-emergency reporting.
   - If money, bank details, account access, passwords, or online fraud were involved and you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report it to Report Fraud.
   - Suspicious emails can be forwarded to report@phishing.gov.uk. Suspicious texts can be forwarded to 7726.
9. **If they obtained something sensitive, take one containment step after you are safe.** Contact your bank using the number on your card or banking app, change the password for the account they targeted, or turn on two-step verification.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a formal statement, pursue a complaint, or take legal action.
- You do not need to prove the person was fake before calling 999 or 101.
- You can organise screenshots, longer reports, bank follow-up, and account clean-up after you are away from the person and no longer under pressure.
- You do not need to keep engaging with them to be polite or to avoid seeming difficult.

## Important reassurance
People impersonating authority rely on shock, urgency, and the fear of getting into trouble. Feeling rattled, frozen, or unsure is a normal stress response. Slowing the situation down and checking through independently found official routes is a sensible safety step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: they are meant to help you stay safe, avoid irreversible mistakes, and preserve enough information to report what happened. Later decisions may need help from police, victim support services, your bank, a regulated adviser, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 999. If you are unsure, prioritise safety and independent verification over cooperating with on-the-spot demands.
