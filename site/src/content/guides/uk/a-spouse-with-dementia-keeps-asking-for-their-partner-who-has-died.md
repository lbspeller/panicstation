# What to do if… a spouse with dementia keeps asking for their partner who has died (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before you speak: the aim is to reduce distress and keep them safe, not to make them fully understand in one conversation. It may be right to tell them clearly once, especially if they can understand and hold the information, then focus on reassurance if repeated reminders cause fresh shock.

## Do not do these things
- Do not blurt it out in a busy moment, in front of a group, or while they are already agitated.
- Do not use euphemisms like “gone to sleep” if you decide to tell them, because this can confuse or frighten them.
- Do not argue, prove it, or force them to accept reality if they cannot hold onto it.
- Do not repeatedly tell them again and again if each reminder causes fresh shock and intense distress.
- Do not leave them unsupported straight after telling them if they may panic, leave the home, or be unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a calm, safe pause.** Choose a quiet room, reduce noise, sit where they can see you, and make sure someone has time to stay with them afterwards.
2. **Decide your approach for the next hour, not forever.**
   - **If they can usually understand and retain important information:** plan to tell them plainly once and support the reaction.
   - **If they cannot retain new information and reminders repeatedly distress them:** plan to prioritise comfort and avoid repeatedly re-telling them today.
3. **If you decide to tell them now, keep it simple and direct.** One person should speak. Use short sentences and clear words, such as: “I’m so sorry. [Name] died.” Then stop and allow a reaction.
4. **If distress rises, move from facts to feelings.** Use a calm voice, offer gentle touch only if welcomed, and reflect the emotion: “You miss them. This feels awful.” Offer one grounding next step, such as tea, a familiar object, sitting together, or a short indoor walk.
5. **If they ask again later, use one repeatable script for today.** Choose either:
   - a brief, gentle reminder, if it helps without overwhelming them, or
   - a comfort-first response, if reminders keep causing fresh shock.
6. **Brief the people around them.** Tell close family, carers, care agency staff, or care home staff what you are saying today, what not to say, and what usually calms them.
7. **Reduce the chance of them going to search for their partner.** Stay with them during high-risk periods, reduce obvious cues to leave if this helps, and ask one trusted neighbour or staff member to contact you if they see them outside alone.
8. **Use UK support if you need guidance today.** Alzheimer’s Society Dementia Support Line is 0333 150 3456. Dementia UK Admiral Nurse Dementia Helpline is 0800 888 6678.
9. **Use urgent help if safety is at risk.** Contact NHS 111 if you need urgent medical advice and it is not life-threatening. Call 999 if anyone is in immediate danger or there is a life-threatening emergency.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether they should attend the funeral, see the body, or be told every time.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements about truth and comfort right now.
- You do not need to make big care decisions today, such as moving home or long-term care, unless there is an immediate safety risk.

## Important reassurance
This is a deeply hard situation, and there often is not one perfect answer. Many carers find that the kindest response changes with the person’s dementia, distress, and the moment.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the next hours and days. Later decisions about communication, grief support, care arrangements, or best interests may need help from dementia-specialist, health, social care, or legal professionals.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, therapeutic, financial, or professional advice. If you are unsure what is in their best interests, or their distress or wandering becomes unsafe, involve their GP, memory service, care team, NHS 111, or 999 as appropriate.
