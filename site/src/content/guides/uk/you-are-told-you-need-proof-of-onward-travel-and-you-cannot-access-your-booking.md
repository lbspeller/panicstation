# What to do if… you are told you need proof of onward travel and you cannot access your booking (UK)

## Short answer
Retrieve your booking reference (PNR) or e-ticket number immediately via email, bank records, or the airline desk to show a verifiable itinerary. If you cannot produce this, ask the airline to print your itinerary from their system using your passport, but be aware they may deny boarding if documentation remains missing.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attempt to use a verbal-only explanation of your travel plans as this is not verifiable.
- Don’t purchase a non-refundable "panic ticket" unless you are certain you will use it or it is the only way to satisfy the requirement.
- Don’t delete any apps, browser history, or emails in a rush as these may contain the cached data you need.
- Don’t engage in an argument with check-in staff; request a supervisor if a technical retrieval is possible but being refused.
- Don’t assume a hotel booking or bank statement alone will satisfy a "proof of onward travel" requirement.

## What to do now
1. **Clarify exactly what form of proof the airline will accept for your specific route.**
   - Ask if they require a six-character booking reference (PNR), a 13-digit e-ticket number, or a printed travel itinerary.
   - Confirm if they will accept a digital PDF, a screenshot, or if they require a physical printout.

2. **Search your local device storage and accounts for cached itinerary data.**
   - **Check your phone "Downloads," "Files," or "Screenshots" folders for any previously saved PDFs or images.**
   - **Search your email inbox and trash folders for keywords such as "itinerary," "confirmation," "receipt," "PNR," or the airline name.**
   - **Open your mobile banking app to find the transaction for the ticket, which often includes the merchant name and date to help the airline find your file.**

3. **Use the airport's self-service kiosks to attempt a passport-based retrieval.**
   - **Scan your passport at an airline kiosk to see if your full itinerary (including the return or onward leg) appears automatically.**
   - **Attempt to "Manage Booking" on the kiosk using only your surname and the credit card used for the purchase.**

4. **Request a manual booking retrieval at the airline service desk.**
   - **Present your passport and ask the agent to pull up your "Passenger Name Record" (PNR) to verify your onward flights.**
   - **Ask the agent to print a "Travel Itinerary" or "Passenger Receipt" that clearly shows your name and departure date from the country.**
   - **If the airline is not the one you are flying onward with, ask them to check the IATA Timatic system to confirm if your specific nationality truly requires the proof for this route.**

5. **Contact your travel agent or the airline’s phone support if the desk cannot help.**
   - **Call the agency or airline and request that they "Resend the e-ticket itinerary" to your email or via SMS immediately.**
   - **Note down the PNR and e-ticket number verbally over the phone if you cannot access data/WiFi.**

6. **Request the mandatory written notice of your rights if boarding is denied.**
   - **Ask for the "Written Notice" explaining your rights under UK261 if you are officially refused carriage.**
   - **Note that under UK law, airlines may reasonably deny boarding for "inadequate travel documentation," which usually means you are not entitled to standard cash compensation.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the airline's policy is "fair" or legally correct while standing at the desk.
- You do not need to file a formal complaint or claim for "denied boarding" expenses in this moment.
- You do not need to worry about your luggage if it has not yet been checked; it remains your responsibility.

## Important reassurance
It is a very common occurrence for travellers to lose access to digital bookings due to dead batteries, lack of roaming data, or app errors. Airline systems are designed to store this information centrally; as long as your booking exists, it can almost always be retrieved using your passport at a service counter or kiosk.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for documentation access at an airport. It does not cover visa applications, Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA) processing times, or long-term legal claims regarding denied boarding.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is based on UK aviation and consumer rules as of 2026. Airlines have a legal obligation to ensure passengers have correct documentation for their destination to avoid fines; they may strictly enforce these rules even if you have a valid booking that you simply cannot display.
