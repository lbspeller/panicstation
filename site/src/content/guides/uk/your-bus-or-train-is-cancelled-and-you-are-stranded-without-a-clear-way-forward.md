# What to do if… your bus or train is cancelled and you are stranded without a clear way forward (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself to a safe, staffed place if you can, then confirm what is actually running and ask staff or the operator what alternative they will honour. Keep your ticket and a quick screenshot of the cancellation before you spend money on a taxi, hotel, or new ticket.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t walk away from a staffed station, terminal, or help point in frustration until you have checked whether another service, replacement bus, or authorised route can get you closer.
- Don’t rely on one announcement only; platforms, departure points, and cancellation decisions can change.
- Don’t board another train operator’s service unless staff, the operator, or official disruption information says your ticket will be accepted.
- Don’t buy an expensive last-minute alternative before asking the operator what they can arrange and what evidence they need if you pay for something yourself.
- Don’t throw away tickets, screenshots, booking emails, or receipts.
- Don’t post your exact location, route, or personal details publicly while you are still stranded or travelling.

## What to do now
1. **Move to the safest practical base.**  
   Use a staffed station area, bus or coach terminal, ticket office, shop, café, hotel lobby, or other well-lit public indoor place. Keep your phone charged and stay near staff, lighting, and other people where possible.

2. **Confirm the cancellation from two sources.**  
   Check the live board, operator app, National Rail, bus or coach operator updates, and ask staff if any are present. Find out whether the service is cancelled, heavily delayed, moved to another stand or platform, replaced by a bus, or running by a different route.

3. **If it is a train, ask for the authorised next step.**  
   Ask station staff or the train company: “What service or route can I use with this ticket now?” If another operator, route, or replacement bus is being accepted, follow the official instruction and keep a screenshot, photo, or brief note of what you were told.

4. **If you decide not to travel by train, keep the refund route simple.**  
   If your train is cancelled or delayed and you choose not to travel, you can generally request a fee-free refund from the retailer that sold you the ticket. Compensation for a completed but delayed journey is usually claimed from the train company, not the ticket retailer.

5. **If it is a bus or coach, get the operator’s concrete option.**  
   Ask the driver, help point, ticket counter, operator app, or customer service channel: “What is the next departure you will honour this ticket on?” and “Is there a replacement service or alternative departure point?” Rights and refunds can depend on the operator, route, distance, and ticket terms, so focus first on getting a clear next movement or a safe place to wait.

6. **If it is late, closing, or you may be stranded overnight, prioritise shelter and safety.**  
   Decide where you will safely wait for the next 30–60 minutes before the station, stop, or nearby shops close. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999. On the rail network, you can also text British Transport Police on 61016 for safety concerns, or call 999 in an emergency.

7. **Capture enough evidence, then stop.**  
   Take screenshots or photos of the cancelled service, live board or app status, your ticket or booking, and any written disruption notice. Note the time and, if you spoke to staff, the role or location of the person you spoke to.

8. **Send one pressure-reducing message if someone is expecting you.**  
   Send something short, such as: “My bus or train has been cancelled. I’m safe but delayed. I’m checking the next official option now and will update you when I know more.”

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide tonight whether to make a formal complaint.
- You don’t need to calculate Delay Repay, compensation, or refund amounts while you are still stranded.
- You don’t need to prove every detail immediately; keep the basic evidence and focus on a safe next step.
- You don’t need to book a taxi or hotel until you have checked what the operator will arrange, unless waiting would leave you unsafe.

## Important reassurance
A sudden cancellation can make everything feel urgent, especially at night or when the last connection is involved. Slowing down is sensible: get somewhere safer, check what is really running, and take the smallest next step that keeps you warm, charged, and not isolated.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about refunds, Delay Repay, complaints, extra costs, missed connections, or operator disputes may need help from the retailer, train company, bus or coach operator, regulator, advice service, or another specialist source.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, travel, safety, or professional advice. Transport policies and passenger rights can vary by operator, ticket type, route, distance, disruption cause, and where you bought the ticket, so ask the operator or retailer to state your options clearly and keep a record.
