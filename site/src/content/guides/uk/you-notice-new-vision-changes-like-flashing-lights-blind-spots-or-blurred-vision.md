# What to do if… you notice new vision changes like flashing lights, blind spots, or blurred vision (UK)

## Short answer
Treat **new or sudden vision changes** as a medical emergency. If you **suddenly lose vision** in one or both eyes, have **severe eye pain**, or show signs of a **stroke**, **call 999 or go to A&E immediately** and do not drive yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not **drive or cycle** to the hospital or optician; ask someone to take you or use a taxi/ambulance.
- Do not **rub, press, or apply pressure** to the affected eye.
- Do not assume the change is "just a migraine" if it is **persistent**, **one-sided**, or includes a **dark shadow** or **sudden increase** in floaters.
- Do not use **anyone else's eye drops** or medications to treat the symptoms yourself.
- Do not **wait for a routine appointment** if you notice a "curtain" or "veil" moving across your sight.
- Do not **keep testing your vision** by straining the eye or staring at bright lights.

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if you experience emergency triggers:**
   - You **suddenly cannot see** out of one or both eyes.
   - You have **severe, sudden eye pain** or pain that makes you feel physically sick.
   - You have vision changes alongside **stroke symptoms** (Face drooping, Arm weakness, or Speech difficulty).
2. **Contact an urgent optician or NHS 111 if you have these specific symptoms:**
   - You see a **new dark shadow, curtain, or veil** moving across your field of vision.
   - You notice a **sudden, significant increase** in the number of floaters (dots, lines, or cobwebs).
   - You see **frequent new flashes of light** or rainbow-coloured **halos around lights**.
   - Your vision is **suddenly blurred, distorted, or double**, especially after a recent eye surgery or injury.
3. **Perform a simple check of each eye separately:**
   - **Cover your left eye** and look at a fixed point, then **cover your right eye** and repeat.
   - Note if the problem is **restricted to one eye** or affects both.
4. **Prepare a concise summary for the clinician or call handler:**
   - Note the **exact time** symptoms started and if they are **constant or intermittent**.
   - List the specific changes: **flashes, floaters, blur, shadows, wavy lines, or pain**.
   - Mention any **recent eye surgeries, injuries, or new medications**.
5. **Manage contact lenses safely:**
   - If you wear contact lenses and have redness or pain, **remove them carefully**.
   - **Place the lenses in their case** and take them with you to the appointment for the clinician to inspect.
6. **Arrange for a companion or transport home:**
   - Expect to have **pupil-dilating drops** during your assessment, which will cause **temporary blurred vision and light sensitivity** for several hours.
   - Do not plan to drive back from the appointment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the underlying condition (such as retinal detachment or glaucoma) before seeking help.
- You do not need to compare your symptoms with others' experiences online.
- You can leave administrative tasks, such as informing your workplace or rearranging non-urgent commitments, until after the clinical assessment.

## Important reassurance
Sudden vision changes are startling, but many causes are highly treatable when caught early. Medical professionals are trained to triage these symptoms rapidly to protect your sight, and seeking help is the correct response to any new visual disturbance.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage guidance only. It is intended to help users identify the correct level of urgent care and avoid common mistakes during the first minutes of a visual crisis.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace professional medical diagnosis. If you suspect you are experiencing a sight-threatening emergency, **seek urgent medical attention immediately**.
