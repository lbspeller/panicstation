# What to do if… a private folder was accidentally shared publicly and you need to stop access immediately (UK)
## Short answer
Remove public access at the source now: set the folder to **Restricted** or **Only people added**, then remove or disable any **anyone with the link** sharing link.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume renaming or moving the folder stops old access; change the sharing settings and remove the link explicitly.
- Don’t post “please ignore” into the same thread where the link was shared; that can spread it further.
- Don’t assume view-only access is safe. If someone could view it, they may have been able to copy, download, photograph, or screenshot it.
- Don’t delete emails, chats, access messages, or logs about what happened if this is a workplace or security incident.
- Don’t spend the first few minutes trying to prove who opened it; lock access down first.

## What to do now
1. **Open the folder’s sharing settings and switch off public access.**
   - Change **General access**, **Who has access**, or the closest matching setting to **Restricted** or **Only people added**.
   - Remove broad options such as **Anyone with the link**, **Public**, or **Anyone in the organisation** if that is wider than intended.

2. **Break the old link.**
   - Look for controls such as **Remove link**, **Disable link**, **Delete link**, or **Unshare**.
   - Check for more than one link type, such as view, edit, download, or preview links.
   - If you can, test the old link in a private or incognito browser window after signing out. It should no longer open the folder.

3. **Remove unexpected people, guests, and broad groups.**
   - In **Manage access**, remove anyone you do not recognise.
   - Remove large groups, guest users, shared-drive members, or organisation-wide access that you did not mean to include.
   - Leave access only for people who genuinely need it right now.

4. **Check the parent folder or shared drive.**
   - Open the parent folder’s sharing settings and check whether it is public, link-shared, organisation-wide, or shared with a broad group.
   - If the folder keeps becoming accessible again because of inherited access, move it into a clearly private location after you have removed the public link.

5. **If this is work-related, treat it as a security incident now.**
   - Contact your IT, helpdesk, manager, data protection contact, or security team through your normal incident route.
   - Tell them what folder was shared, roughly when it may have become public, when you noticed, what access you revoked, and whether personal, confidential, client, staff, financial, or security information was inside.
   - Ask them to check or preserve available logs and help confirm whether access occurred.

6. **If passwords, tokens, keys, or private configuration files were inside, rotate them now.**
   - Revoke or rotate passwords, API keys, tokens, certificates, database credentials, backup keys, private configuration files, and similar secrets before doing a detailed investigation.
   - Tell the owner of any shared secret that it may have been exposed.

7. **Make a brief record while it is fresh.**
   - Note the time you discovered it, what sharing setting you saw, what link type existed, what you changed, and who you told.
   - If you screenshot the final **Manage access** view, keep it in the proper incident record and do not post it publicly or into a broad chat.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify every viewer before you remove access.
- You do not need to send a detailed explanation before you know what was exposed.
- You do not need to reorganise all of your cloud storage today.
- You do not need to decide blame, discipline, or long-term policy changes in the first few minutes.

## Important reassurance
This is a common, high-pressure mistake because cloud-sharing controls can be easy to mis-click. The most useful first move is containment: stop the link, narrow access, and involve the right support if the folder contained sensitive information.

## Scope note
These are first steps to remove access fast and reduce harm. Later decisions, especially where personal data, client information, employment records, regulated data, or confidential business material may be involved, may need specialist technical, legal, data protection, or organisational support.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, technical, data protection, or professional advice. If personal data may have been exposed, follow your organisation’s breach process; if it may be a notifiable personal data breach, the organisation should assess promptly and may need to report to the ICO, where feasible, within 72 hours of becoming aware.
