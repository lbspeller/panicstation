# What to do if… water is coming in around a window or door during a storm (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate electrical hazards immediately if water is near power sources, then contain the water from the inside using containers and towels. Do not attempt to repair the leak from the outside until the storm has passed.

## Do not do these things
- Don't touch light switches, power sockets, or appliances if the surrounding area is wet or if you are standing on a damp floor.
- Don't go outside or climb ladders to inspect the leak while high winds, heavy rain, or lightning are occurring.
- Don't use mains-powered vacuum cleaners or heaters to dry out the area while water is still entering the building.
- Don't ignore signs of structural distress such as bulging plaster, new cracks, or loud creaking noises in the walls or ceiling.
- Don't attempt to use "do-it-yourself" electrical fixes or temporary wiring to bypass wet areas.

## What to do now
1. **Move people and pets to a dry room and close the door to the affected area.** **Closing internal doors can help contain moisture and prevent drafts from spreading water further into the property.**
2. **Turn off the electricity supply if water is approaching any power sockets or electrical equipment.** **Only attempt to use the consumer unit (fuse box) if the floor beneath it is dry and there are no signs of water on the unit itself.**
   - If there is sparking, buzzing, or a smell of burning, do not touch the unit; call 105 immediately.
   - For general storm-related electrical safety advice, contact the 24-hour National Floodline on 0345 988 1188.
3. **Position buckets, bowls, or trays directly under the point of entry.** **If water is running down a wall or frame, use a piece of string or a strip of fabric to "wick" the water into a container to prevent it from soaking into the floor.**
4. **Use towels or heavy fabrics to create a barrier on the floor.** **Lay towels in a "U" shape around the leak to trap the spread, and replace them regularly to prevent them from becoming completely saturated.**
5. **Clear the immediate area of items that could be damaged or cause a hazard.** **Prioritise moving electronics, important documents, and medications to a higher floor or a tabletop.**
6. **Protect window and door fabrics from soaking up moisture.** **Lift curtains and blinds away from wet sills and secure them with a clip or string to prevent capillary action from pulling water upward.**
7. **Treat any water entering from the ground or exterior as potentially contaminated.** **Wear gloves if you must handle wet items, avoid splashes to the face, and wash hands thoroughly after contact.**
8. **Call 999 if the situation poses an immediate danger to life.** **This includes situations where water is causing electrical arcing or if the building shows signs of potential collapse.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause of the leak (such as a failed seal or blocked gutter) until weather conditions improve.
- You do not need to start permanent repairs or deep-cleaning of carpets while the storm is active.
- You do not need to contact your insurer or landlord immediately unless you require emergency alternative accommodation.
- You do not need to worry about the long-term impact on the building fabric in the first few hours.

## Important reassurance
Water ingress during extreme storms is a common occurrence and can often be managed through containment. Focus on the immediate safety of occupants and the isolation of electricity; property damage can be addressed once the storm has subsided.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Professional assessment by an electrician or building contractor will be required to ensure the property is safe once the storm has ended.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance and does not replace professional trade advice. If water has entered any electrical fittings, do not turn the power back on until a qualified electrician has inspected the system.
