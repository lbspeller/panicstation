# What to do if… you are pressured to sign a settlement agreement or resignation letter on the spot (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign anything on the spot if you feel pressured or unsure. Ask for a copy to take away and say you will respond after getting independent advice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a settlement agreement, resignation letter, acknowledgement, or “receipt” just to end the meeting.
- Do not write or email a resignation if you do not genuinely want to resign.
- Do not rely on verbal promises about pay, references, notice, confidentiality, or your record unless they are written into the document.
- Do not hand over your only copy of anything you are given.
- Do not argue every detail in the room if you feel pressured; focus on pausing the decision and getting the paperwork.
- Do not use only work email or work devices for notes you may need later, where you can safely and appropriately avoid that.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause if the meeting feels intimidating.** Say: “I need to pause this meeting. I am not signing anything today.” If you feel unsafe, leave the conversation if you can do so safely.
2. **Use one clear line to stop the signature pressure.** Say: “Please give me a copy of everything you want me to sign. I will respond after I have had independent advice.”
3. **Ask for all the documents.** Ask for the settlement agreement or resignation letter, any reference wording, confidentiality wording, restrictive covenant wording, payment schedule, annexes, and side letters. If they will not hand you copies, ask them to send the documents to a personal email address if that is appropriate for your role and the documents.
4. **Clarify what they say is happening.** Ask: “Is this a settlement agreement?” and “Are you asking me to resign, or are you ending my employment?” Write down the exact answer, the names of the people present, and any deadline or ultimatum.
5. **If it is a UK settlement agreement, say you need advice before signing.** A valid UK settlement agreement must be in writing and must meet legal conditions, including advice from a relevant independent adviser. Ask whether the employer will contribute to the adviser’s fee, because employers usually do.
6. **If it is actually a disciplinary or grievance hearing, ask for a companion.** If the meeting could lead to disciplinary action, or it is a qualifying grievance meeting, you can make a reasonable request to be accompanied by a colleague or trade union representative. If your chosen companion cannot attend, ask to postpone and suggest a reasonable time within five working days of the original meeting where possible.
7. **Make a calm record as soon as you are out of the room.** Write the date, time, who was there, what you were asked to sign, what you were told would happen if you did not sign, and what documents you received. Then send a short message confirming that you have received documents, are considering them, and have not agreed or resigned.
8. **Contact the right help quickly.** If you are in a union, contact them. Otherwise, contact an employment solicitor or advice service and say: “I have been given a settlement agreement or asked to resign on the spot, and I need independent advice before I sign anything.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to accept the settlement, negotiate terms, or resign.
- You do not need to prove your whole case in the meeting.
- You do not need to decide today whether to bring a claim, make a grievance, or challenge the employer.
- You do not need to sign just to “get the payment started”; you can ask for time and written terms first.

## Important reassurance
Being pushed to sign immediately can make people freeze. Pausing, taking the documents away, and getting independent advice is a normal and reasonable response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you slow the situation down and avoid an irreversible mistake. Later decisions may need specialist help because the right move can depend on your contract, length of service, documents, deadlines, and what was said.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Employment situations are fact-specific, and rules can vary by contract and circumstances.
