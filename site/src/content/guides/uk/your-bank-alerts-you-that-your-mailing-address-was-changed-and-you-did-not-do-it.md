# What to do if… your bank alerts you that your mailing address was changed and you did not do it (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as possible account takeover. Contact your bank now using a trusted route, ask them to secure the account, reverse the address change, and pause risky changes or payments where possible while they investigate.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links or call numbers from the alert message if you can avoid it; use the number on the back of your card, your bank’s official app, or your bank’s official website.
- Don’t stay on the phone with someone who contacted you unexpectedly about your account; end the call and contact the bank yourself.
- Don’t assume it is just a typo until the bank confirms what changed, when it changed, and how it was authorised.
- Don’t keep using the same passwords, passcodes, memorable information, or app security settings if there is any chance your banking login or email has been compromised.
- Don’t let the bank only change the address back without checking phone number, email address, paperless settings, new payees, replacement cards, new devices, and pending payments.
- Don’t move money because a caller tells you to move it to a “safe account”.

## What to do now
1. **Contact your bank’s fraud or security team immediately using a trusted channel.**  
   Use the number on your card, the bank’s official app, or the bank’s official website. If the alert followed an unexpected call or text, you can also hang up and dial **159** where supported to be connected to your bank. Ask the bank to:
   - Revert the mailing address to the correct one.
   - Stop further profile changes unless extra checks are completed.
   - Put temporary holds or blocks where possible on outbound transfers, new payees, replacement cards, cheque book orders, and other high-risk changes.
   - Check recent activity with you, including new payees, standing orders, direct debits, card reissues, PIN or password resets, new devices, failed logins, and pending payments they may still be able to stop.
   - Tell you whether any card, PIN, statement, security code, or letter has already been sent to the wrong address.

2. **Secure the accounts that can reset your bank access, especially email and phone.**
   - Change your email password from a device you trust.
   - Turn on two-step verification for email.
   - Check your email recovery phone number, recovery email, forwarding rules, filters, and signed-in devices.
   - Then change your banking password, passcode, and memorable information using the bank’s official app or website.
   - Sign out of other banking sessions or devices if your bank gives that option.
   - If your phone suddenly shows “no service” or stops receiving calls or texts, contact your mobile network urgently and tell the bank, because your number may be at risk.

3. **Turn on bank alerts that show further takeover attempts.**  
   In the bank app or online banking, enable alerts for logins, new devices, new payees, transfers, card replacements, and profile or contact-detail changes.

4. **Treat post interception as a real risk.**
   - Ask the bank not to send replacement cards, PINs, cheque books, or sensitive letters until the account is secured.
   - Ask whether anything sensitive has already been posted and what can be cancelled or reissued safely.
   - If you suspect your post has been redirected or interfered with, contact Royal Mail and ask how to check or challenge a redirection affecting your address.
   - If you will be away or mail may pile up, ask Royal Mail whether Keepsafe is suitable.

5. **Create a simple paper trail while it is fresh.**
   - Save screenshots of the alert and the wrong address if you can see it.
   - Write down the date and time you noticed it, when you last logged in normally, and any unusual calls, texts, emails, or missing post.
   - Note the name, department, date, and time of anyone you speak to at the bank.

6. **Add protective friction beyond the bank if your details may be misused.**
   - Consider reporting identity fraud through Report Fraud.
   - Consider Cifas Protective Registration if you think your personal details are being used or may be used to apply for credit, accounts, or services in your name.
   - Keep checking messages from your bank, mobile network, email provider, and any lender you already use.

7. **Check your credit files for unfamiliar addresses or new credit.**
   - Check all three main UK credit reference agencies: TransUnion, Equifax, and Experian.
   - Look for addresses, credit searches, accounts, loans, mobile contracts, or credit cards you do not recognise.
   - If you find something wrong, raise a dispute with the relevant credit reference agency and keep dated notes of what you reported.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to close the account or switch banks.
- You do not need to work out exactly how the address change happened before you act.
- You do not need to write a full complaint while you are still securing the account.
- You can deal with longer complaints, compensation questions, and formal escalation after no further changes or payments are happening.

## Important reassurance
An unexpected bank address change can be a sign that someone is trying to take control of the account or intercept letters, cards, or security information. Acting quickly through trusted routes gives the bank the best chance to stop further changes and limit harm.

## Scope note
These are first-step containment actions only. Later decisions may need help from your bank, credit reference agencies, Report Fraud, Cifas, Royal Mail, or a specialist adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes vary by bank and by circumstances. If you can only do two things now, contact your bank through a trusted route and secure your email account.
