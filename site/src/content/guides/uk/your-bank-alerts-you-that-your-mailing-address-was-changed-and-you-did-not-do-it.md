# What to do if… your bank alerts you that your mailing address was changed and you did not do it (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the alert as a high-priority account takeover attempt: contact your bank immediately through the secure **159** hotline or the number on your card to revert the change and freeze high-risk activity.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click any links or call any phone numbers provided within the alert message itself.
- Don’t provide your PIN, full password, or any one-time passcodes (OTP) to anyone claiming to be from the bank over the phone.
- Don’t assume the alert is a technical glitch or a harmless error until you have spoken to a verified bank representative.
- Don’t ignore the alert if you cannot see the change reflected in your app immediately; some systems update at different speeds.
- Don’t allow the bank to simply "change it back" without also requesting a full security audit of recent login locations and new payee additions.

## What to do now
1. **Contact your bank’s fraud department immediately using the 159 hotline.**  
   **159** is a secure, memorable number that connects you directly to most major UK banks and is the safest way to avoid number-spoofing scams.
   - If your bank does not support 159, use the number printed on the back of your debit or credit card.
   - If you are in Scotland, you should also report the incident to Police Scotland via **101**.
2. **Instruct the bank to revert the address and implement a temporary security freeze.**  
   **Ask the bank to specifically perform the following actions during the call:**
   - Revert the mailing address to your verified home address.
   - Place a temporary hold on the dispatch of any physical items, such as replacement cards, cheque books, or PIN reminders.
   - Review and remove any "new payees" or "standing orders" created in the last 48 hours.
   - Request "Enhanced Verification" for any future changes to your contact details.
3. **Secure the email account associated with your banking profile.**  
   **Change your email password immediately and enable two-factor authentication (2FA) if it is not already active.**
   - Fraudsters often change bank addresses after gaining access to the user's email to intercept password reset notifications or physical mail.
   - Check your email "deleted items" or "archive" folders for any bank notifications you might have missed.
4. **Update your banking credentials and log out of all active sessions.**  
   **Create a new, unique passcode or password for your banking app and use the "Sign out of all devices" feature if available.**
   - This ensures that if a fraudster has a live session on another device, they are immediately disconnected.
5. **Forward the fraudulent alert to the national reporting services.**  
   **Forward suspicious text messages to 7726 and suspicious emails to report@phishing.gov.uk.**
   - If you have lost money or believe your identity is fully compromised, report it to the **Report Fraud** service (formerly Action Fraud) at reportfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040.
6. **Apply for Cifas Protective Registration to prevent further identity theft.**  
   **Visit the Cifas website to place a protective flag on your credit file for a fee of £30 for two years.**
   - This forces lenders to carry out extra identity checks whenever your details are used to apply for credit or services.
7. **Contact Royal Mail if you suspect mail redirection fraud.**  
   **Check if a Redirection service has been set up in your name without your consent and consider using the "Keepsafe" service to hold your mail securely.**
   - Fraudsters often use official Redirection to ensure that even if the bank sends a confirmation letter to your old address, it is diverted to them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify exactly how the fraudster gained access to your account before taking protective action.
- You do not need to visit a physical bank branch today unless explicitly instructed by their fraud team.
- You do not need to cancel your account entirely; temporary blocks are usually sufficient for the first hour of stabilization.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a legitimate alert about an address change means the bank's security systems are working. By acting within the first few minutes to lock down your email and revert the bank details, you can often prevent the fraudster from receiving the physical cards or documents they were attempting to intercept.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for suspected account takeover in the UK. Subsequent steps will involve working with the bank’s formal investigation team and reviewing your credit reports over the next 3 months.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute financial or legal advice. Procedures and support for 159 vary by provider. Always prioritize the instructions given by your bank’s official fraud department.
