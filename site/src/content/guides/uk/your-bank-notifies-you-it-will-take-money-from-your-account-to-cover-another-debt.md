# What to do if… your bank notifies you it will take money from your account to cover another debt (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately to state you are in financial hardship and demand they leave enough funds for priority bills, then exercise your "first right of appropriation" for any incoming income.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for the money to disappear before contacting the bank.
- Do not call any number provided in a text or email; use the number on the back of your debit card.
- Do not withdraw all funds if it will cause you to exceed a sanctioned overdraft limit.
- Do not agree to a repayment plan over the phone without seeing the terms in writing.
- Do not assume "right of set-off" is illegal; it is a standard banking right, but must be exercised fairly under FCA Consumer Duty rules.

## What to do now
1. **Call your bank's financial difficulty team immediately to report hardship.** **Inform the bank that taking these funds will leave you unable to meet essential living costs or pay priority debts.**
   - Request a "hold" or "stay" on the set-off for at least 14 days.
   - Mention the "FCA Consumer Duty" and state that the bank has a responsibility to avoid causing foreseeable harm.
2. **Exercise your "first right of appropriation" in writing for all upcoming income.** **Send a formal instruction to the bank (via secure message or email) stating exactly how incoming funds must be used.**
   - List specific amounts and the names of the priority bills they must cover (e.g., rent, mortgage, council tax, energy).
   - Be aware that the bank may respond to this by freezing your account or withdrawing your overdraft facility.
3. **Verify if the accounts meet the "same capacity" legal requirement.** **Check that the debt and the account with funds are held in the exact same name and capacity.**
   - A bank generally cannot take money from a joint account to pay a debt held by only one person, unless the terms specifically allow it.
   - If the debt is with a different banking group, the bank has no automatic right of set-off; challenge this as an unauthorised transaction.
4. **Open a "sole" basic bank account at a different banking group immediately.** **Redirect your next salary, pension, or benefit payment to this new account to prevent further automated seizures.**
   - Ensure the new bank is not part of the same group as your current bank (e.g., Lloyds and Halifax are in the same group).
   - Contact your employer or the DWP to update your payment details as soon as the new account is open.
5. **Contact priority creditors if you expect payments to fail.** **Notify your landlord, mortgage lender, or local council that a bank set-off has occurred and you may need a short delay on this month's payment.**
   - This prevents them from initiating enforcement action for what they might otherwise see as a deliberate non-payment.
6. **Check for 2026 DWP direct deduction notices if the debt is to the government.** **Identify if the deduction is being made under the 2026 "direct deduction" powers which allow the DWP to bypass court orders for benefit overpayments.**
   - If this is a DWP deduction, you must contact the DWP Debt Management line directly to appeal on the grounds of "extraordinary hardship."
7. **Submit a formal complaint to the bank to start the 15-day clock.** **Request a final response letter and a complaint reference number regarding the lack of notice or the resulting hardship.**
   - Under 2026 payment service rules, banks should provide a final response within 15 business days for most payment-related disputes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to agree to a full debt settlement or consolidation loan today.
- You do not need to provide a full "Income and Expenditure" (I&E) statement in the first 24 hours; a summary of immediate priority costs is sufficient to request a temporary hold.
- You do not need to escalate to the Financial Ombudsman Service until you have a final response or 15 business days have passed.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of violation when money is moved without a specific transaction request. The "right of set-off" is a legal mechanism, but it is subject to strict fairness rules. Most banks will reverse or pause a set-off if you can prove it will leave you without food or heat.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for UK retail banking customers. It does not cover business banking, corporate insolvency, or court-ordered Third Party Debt Orders, which follow different legal timelines.

## Important note
This information is not legal or financial advice. Banking rights vary depending on your specific terms and conditions and the jurisdiction (England/Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland). If the bank refuses to leave you with enough for essentials, contact a regulated debt advisor such as StepChange or National Debtline immediately.
