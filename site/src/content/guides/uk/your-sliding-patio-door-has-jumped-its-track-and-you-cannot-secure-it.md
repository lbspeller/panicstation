# What to do if… your sliding patio door has jumped its track and you cannot secure it (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as an urgent home-security problem. Move people away from the glass, do not force the door, block it from opening from the inside, and arrange urgent repair.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep forcing the door along the track if it is skewed, grinding, or jammed.
- Don’t try to lift the sliding panel out or re-seat it on your own if it is heavy, unstable, or partly off the track.
- Don’t assume “nearly shut” is secure if the lock will not engage.
- Don’t rely on a broom handle, wedge, or furniture as if it were a proper lock.
- Don’t block your only safe exit route in case of fire.
- Don’t leave notes, posts, or visible signs saying the door cannot be secured.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Bring people and pets away from the patio door. Keep your phone and keys with you. Close internal doors if that helps separate the room from the rest of the home.
2. **Check whether this is an active security incident.** From inside, look for fresh pry marks, a bent frame, damaged lock parts, broken glass, or signs someone has tried the door. If a crime is in progress, someone is nearby trying to get in, or anyone is in immediate danger, call **999**. If there is no immediate danger but you want to report suspicious activity or damage, use **101** or your local police online reporting route.
3. **Close it only as far as it will go without strain.** If the panel moves, slide it closed slowly. Stop if the glass flexes, the frame twists, or the door starts to lift or drop.
4. **Block the inside track.** Put a solid bar, dowel, sturdy piece of timber, or broom handle snugly in the inside bottom track so the moving panel cannot slide open easily.
5. **If it will not fully close, hold the safest position rather than repairing it.** Use a firm wedge or doorstop at the base to reduce movement, then place a heavy item of furniture in front of the opening only if it does not block your escape route.
6. **Reduce lift and visibility risk.** Close curtains or blinds so the fault is not obvious from outside. Move keys, bags, laptops, and other visible valuables away from the patio door. Switch on outside lighting if you already have it.
7. **Make the glass area safer.** Keep children and pets away from the panel. Do not lean heavy objects against the pane. Avoid slamming nearby doors or windows, especially if there are cracks or the panel is under strain.
8. **Arrange urgent repair.**
   - **Owner-occupier:** contact an emergency locksmith, patio-door repairer, or glazier and say: “Sliding patio door off track and cannot be locked or secured.”
   - **Tenant:** report it to your landlord or letting agent as an urgent security problem in writing, with the time you found it and the words “the patio door cannot be locked or secured.”
   - **If you are renting and cannot get a response:** check your local council or local authority private-renting route, or a recognised housing advice service, and keep a record of what you reported and when.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the rollers, track, latch, or frame tonight.
- You do not need to choose replacement doors, new locks, alarms, or upgrades right now.
- You do not need to settle fault, liability, deposit, or insurance questions before the door is made safer.
- You can take fuller photos, gather paperwork, and compare repair quotes after the immediate security risk is controlled.

## Important reassurance
A patio door that will not lock can make a home feel suddenly exposed. The immediate goal is not a perfect repair; it is to make the door harder to open, keep people away from the glass, and get the right help started.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation until a qualified person can inspect and repair the door. Sliding patio doors vary, and hands-on repair can be hazardous when the panel is heavy, misaligned, or made of large glass.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not professional building, locksmith, legal, insurance, or emergency-services advice. If you believe someone is attempting entry or anyone is in immediate danger, call 999.
