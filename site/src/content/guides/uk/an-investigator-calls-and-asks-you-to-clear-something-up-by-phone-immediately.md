# What to do if… an investigator calls and asks you to clear something up by phone immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Do not discuss the issue by phone immediately. Pause, verify who contacted you through an official route, and get legal advice before answering questions about what happened.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t have a quick chat to be helpful; what you say may later matter.
- Don’t explain, justify, correct details, or fill gaps over the phone.
- Don’t agree to an interview, meeting, or attendance time until you have had legal advice.
- Don’t assume the caller is genuine because they know your name, address, or a few facts.
- Don’t give personal details beyond what is needed to verify who is contacting you.
- Don’t use a number supplied only by the caller if you are trying to verify them.
- Don’t delete voicemails, call logs, texts, emails, or notes about the contact.

## What to do now
1. **Create a pause.**  
   If you are on the call, say: “I’m not able to discuss this by phone. Please give me your name, role, organisation, office base, and reference number. I’ll verify this through an official route.”

2. **Write down the contact details only.**  
   Record the date, time, caller number, name given, organisation, unit or office, reference number, and the exact words used to ask you to speak. Do not write a statement or start reconstructing the whole event yet.

3. **Verify the contact independently.**  
   Use an official website or main published number, not a number sent only by the caller. If they claim to be police, call 101 or use the publicly listed contact route for the relevant force and ask whether that person or unit is trying to contact you. If anyone is in immediate danger, call 999.

4. **Keep verification separate from answering questions.**  
   When you reach a switchboard, contact centre, or office, keep it to: “I’m checking whether this contact is genuine and who I should speak to.” Do not explain the allegation, give your account, or answer questions about the incident during verification.

5. **Ask what kind of contact they want.**  
   Once the contact is verified, ask: “Am I being asked to give a witness account, or am I suspected of an offence?” If they mention a voluntary interview, an interview under caution, or that you may be suspected of an offence, say: “I want legal advice before answering questions.”

6. **If it is a police suspect interview, treat it as formal.**  
   In England and Wales, voluntary suspect interviews have safeguards, including being told about your rights and access to free legal advice. Scotland and Northern Ireland use different procedures, but the safe first step is the same: ask for legal advice before questioning and do not try to clear it up by phone.

7. **Move the next contact into a safer format.**  
   Ask for the request in writing with the reference number, the organisation’s contact details, and what they say the contact is about in broad terms. Say that any interview or questioning should be arranged after you have spoken to a solicitor.

8. **If you are on bail, licence, parole, probation, immigration reporting, or prison-related supervision, check conditions before missing anything.**  
   Look at any written conditions or reporting instructions you already have. If a condition or appointment requires action, try to comply with that requirement while keeping any extra detail to the minimum until you have legal advice.

9. **If the contact feels threatening or fake, prioritise safety and evidence.**  
   End the call, keep messages and call logs, and verify through official routes. Contact police on 101 if you think it may be harassment, impersonation, or a scam; use 999 if there is immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to cooperate overall right now.
- You do not need to prove your innocence during the first call.
- You do not need to prepare a full timeline tonight.
- You do not need to tell friends, family, or an employer the details unless there is an immediate safety or condition-related reason.
- You do not need to choose a long-term strategy before you know whether you are a witness, suspect, or something else.

## Important reassurance
A request to “clear something up” can feel urgent, but pausing is normal and sensible. Asking to verify the caller and get legal advice is not an admission of guilt; it helps prevent rushed answers, misunderstandings, and avoidable mistakes.

## Scope note
These are first steps for an unexpected investigator phone request. Later decisions, including whether to attend an interview, provide a statement, or respond formally, may need specialist legal help based on the exact organisation, allegation, and part of the UK involved.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. UK procedures can differ between England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, and your own bail, licence, court, supervision, employment, or immigration conditions may change what you must do.
