# What to do if… you are asked to sign a police statement and have not had time to read it carefully (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign a police statement unless you have read it carefully and understood what you are agreeing is true. Ask for time, ask for corrections, and ask for legal advice before signing if you are being questioned, arrested, in custody, or may be treated as a suspect.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign just to get it over with.
- Do not rely on someone summarising it instead of reading it yourself, unless reading is not possible and you have asked for it to be read back properly.
- Do not assume it says exactly what you said out loud.
- Do not sign blank spaces, missing pages, or pages you have not seen.
- Do not sign while you are too tired, distressed, unwell, medicated, or confused to check it properly without saying so.
- Do not accept “it can be fixed later” as a reason to sign something you do not agree is accurate.
- Do not keep discussing the detail of what happened while you are trying to decide whether to sign.

## What to do now
1. **Say one clear sentence.**  
   “I am not signing this until I have read it carefully and I understand it.”

2. **Ask for time to read every page.**  
   Say: “I need time to read this properly. Please wait while I check it.”

3. **Ask what kind of document it is.**  
   Say: “Is this a witness statement, a victim statement, a statement under caution, or another form?”  
   Do not guess. The safest step is to know what you are being asked to sign.

4. **Ask for legal advice if you are being questioned, arrested, in custody, or may be treated as a suspect.**  
   Say: “I want legal advice before I sign anything.”  
   If you already said no to legal advice, say clearly: “I have changed my mind. I want legal advice now.”

5. **Check the statement line by line.**  
   Look for:
   - your name and contact details;
   - dates, times, places, and names;
   - words that make you sound more certain than you are;
   - anything you did not say;
   - anything important you did say that is missing;
   - anything that mixes up what you saw, what you heard, and what you only assumed.

6. **Ask for corrections before signing.**  
   Say: “That is not accurate. Please change it to: [your words].”  
   Ask to read the corrected version again before you decide whether to sign.

7. **If it still is not accurate, do not sign it as true.**  
   Say: “I do not agree that this is accurate, so I am not signing it as true.”  
   Ask for your disagreement to be recorded in writing.

8. **If you cannot read or understand it properly, say what support you need.**  
   Use plain words such as:
   - “I need an interpreter.”
   - “I have difficulty reading.”
   - “I am too tired to check this properly.”
   - “I need this read back slowly.”
   - “I need help because I am under 18 or vulnerable.”

9. **If you are under 18 or vulnerable, ask for the required support before signing.**  
   Say: “I need an appropriate adult or other required support person where I am before I sign anything.”  
   Also ask for legal advice before signing.

10. **Ask for a copy, or ask how to get one.**  
   Say: “Can I have a copy of anything I am asked to sign?”  
   If the answer is no, ask: “How can I get a copy, and when?”

## What can wait
You do not need to decide right now whether to make a complaint, give a fuller account, contact other people, or work out the best wording for everything. The immediate task is to avoid signing something you have not properly checked or do not agree is accurate.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel foggy or pressured when police paperwork is put in front of you. Asking for time to read, corrections, support, or legal advice is a sensible way to slow the situation down.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment you are asked to sign. Later decisions, including whether to give a statement, answer questions, add another statement, or complain about pressure, may need specialist legal advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Police processes and terminology vary across the UK, including Scotland and Northern Ireland, so ask for legal advice where you are if you are unsure how the document may be used.
