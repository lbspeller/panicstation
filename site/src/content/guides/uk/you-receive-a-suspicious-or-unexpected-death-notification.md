# What to do if… you receive a suspicious or unexpected death notification (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and do not give details, send money, click links, or call back using the contact route that reached you. Verify the claim through contact details you find yourself and already trust.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money, bank details, one-time passcodes, copies of ID, or your address “to confirm who you are”.
- Do not call back on the number in the message or click links or QR codes to “view documents” or “open a case file”.
- Do not post the details on social media to “find out if it’s true”; it can spread the scam and expose private information.
- Do not forward the message widely in panic; if you warn someone, say clearly that it is unverified.
- Do not assume it is genuine just because the caller knows your name, a relative’s name, or other personal details.
- Do not respond to threats about delays, legal consequences, release fees, or extra costs before you have verified the claim independently.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the conversation cleanly.** Say: “I can’t discuss this. I will verify independently.” Then hang up or stop replying.
2. **Save what you received.** Note the date and time, phone number, email address, exact wording, any reference number, and what they asked for. Screenshot texts, emails, call logs, links, and payment requests without clicking anything.
3. **Check the person through a trusted contact route.** If the person is known to you, contact a close relative, friend, carer, workplace, or regular support contact using a number you already had before this message. Ask only whether they can confirm the person is safe or whether there has been a genuine emergency.
4. **Check the claimed organisation through details you find yourself.** If the message claims to be from police, a hospital, a coroner, a council, a registrar, or a funeral director, find the main public number yourself from an official website or a trusted existing document. Ask to be put through to the relevant team. Give only the minimum details needed to check whether the contact was genuine.
5. **Treat payment requests as suspicious until verified.** If anyone asks for funeral costs, release fees, travel money, inheritance processing fees, courier fees, or a payment to “unlock” documents, stop. Say you will only consider anything in writing after you have independently verified who they are.
6. **If they mention death registration or Tell Us Once, slow down.** Tell Us Once is generally explained by a registrar after a death has been registered, or after coroner involvement with the right certificate and reference. Do not share personal details with a caller or link sender claiming to “start” it for you; check through the registrar, council, or GOV.UK route you find yourself.
7. **Report the suspicious contact through the right route.**
   - Suspicious text: forward it to 7726, which most UK mobile providers use for scam text reporting.
   - Suspicious email: forward it to report@phishing.gov.uk.
   - Suspicious website or link: use the GOV.UK or NCSC phishing reporting route.
   - Scam call: follow the NCSC scam call guidance, or report a mobile scam call to 7726 where your provider supports it.
   - If you lost money, authorised a payment, or were hacked: report it to Report Fraud if you are in England, Wales or Northern Ireland. If you are in Scotland, report it to Police Scotland; use 101 if it is not an emergency.
8. **Secure anything you may have exposed.**
   - If you sent money or shared bank details, contact your bank immediately using the number on your card, bank statement, or banking app.
   - If you shared copies of ID, your address, passwords, or security answers, assume there may be follow-up impersonation. Do not share anything else until you have verified the person or organisation independently.
   - If you clicked a link, entered passwords, or downloaded a file, change affected passwords from a different trusted device and follow NCSC guidance on suspicious messages.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether this is definitely a scam, a mistake, or a real death notification; first stop contact, verify, and secure money or accounts.
- You do not need to contact every organisation just in case until you have confirmation that the death notification is genuine.
- You do not need to arrange funeral, travel, estate, or paperwork decisions during the first shocked minutes.
- You do not need to answer urgent messages about fees, deadlines, or consequences while the claim is still unverified.

## Important reassurance
A death notification can make it hard to think clearly, especially when it arrives suddenly or from an unknown person. Shock, confusion, and doubt are common reactions. Slowing the situation down and using contact routes you control is the safest first move.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: they are meant to reduce harm, verify what is real, and stop panic-driven decisions. If the notification is genuine, later decisions about bereavement support, practical arrangements, legal matters, or finances may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. Scams and official processes can vary across the UK and over time; when unsure, do not share personal information and verify through independently found official contact details.
