# What to do if… a joint account holder makes a major change to account settings without telling you (UK)

## Short answer
Call your bank’s dedicated fraud or security line immediately to register a formal account dispute and request a temporary "freeze" on further settings changes.

## Do not do these things
- Don't attempt to "test" the account by making your own retaliatory changes, as this can make it harder for the bank to identify the primary source of the dispute.
- Don't confront the other account holder while you are logged into the account or using a shared device, as this may lead to further access being restricted.
- Don't click on any links in "security alert" emails or texts that arrived around the same time; go directly through the official app or website.
- Don't use a phone number provided in a suspicious message; only use the number on the back of your physical bank card or dial **159**.
- Don't withdraw the entire balance in a panic, as this may violate the account mandate and complicates future legal or ombudsman reviews.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a private device and a secure network immediately.** Avoid using shared computers or tablets where the other holder might have saved passwords or tracking software.
2. **Contact your bank via their official security number or by dialing 159.** Use the number on the back of your card to reach the fraud department directly and state that you are a joint holder reporting a dispute over account control.
3. **Request an immediate "lock" on all administrative and security settings.** Ask the bank to prevent any further changes to contact details, daily limits, or device registrations while the dispute is active.
   - Specifically ask to verify if your primary mobile number and email address are still the ones registered for alerts.
4. **Conduct a verbal "audit" of recent security changes with the bank representative.** Ask the agent to list every change made in the last 48 hours, including:
   - New devices registered for mobile banking
   - Changes to "paperless" or statement delivery preferences
   - Updates to daily transfer or ATM limits
   - Addition of new payees or "saved" beneficiaries
5. **Ask for the account mandate to be updated to "both to sign" if safety is at risk.** Many UK banks can move an account from "either to sign" (where one person can do anything) to "both to sign" (where both must agree to any action), though this often freezes the account for daily use.
6. **Review the last 10 transactions for any "test" payments.** Look for small, unusual transfers to new accounts or card registrations for digital wallets like Apple Pay or Google Pay.
7. **Change the login credentials for your personal email and sole accounts.** If your joint account settings were changed, your email (which often receives the "one-time codes") may also be at risk.
8. **Report the situation as a "disputed mandate" if the bank refuses to help.** Use the word "dispute" specifically, as this triggers internal protocols that differ from standard customer service.
9. **Secure your future incoming payments if you suspect a permanent loss of control.** If your salary or benefits are due to be paid into this account, contact your employer or the DWP to divert the next payment to a sole account in your name only.
10. **Report to "Report Fraud" (formerly Action Fraud) if you believe a crime has occurred.** Call **0300 123 2040** if you suspect the other holder is committing fraud or if an external third party has taken over their access.
   - If you are in **Scotland**, report via **101**.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to end the relationship or the financial partnership permanently.
- Reversing minor settings like "paperless" preferences, as long as the funds and access are secure.
- Formal complaints to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS), which usually requires an initial 8-week period for the bank to respond.
- Detailed legal forensic reviews of the account history.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sudden sense of panic when financial control is shifted without your consent. In the UK, joint accounts are governed by strict "mandates," and while most allow either person to act, banks have established "dispute" protocols to handle exactly this situation. Taking action now does not mean the money is gone; it is a standard protective step.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for UK banking disputes only. It does not cover long-term debt liability or the division of assets during divorce or separation.

## Important note
Joint account holders are usually "jointly and severally liable" for the account. This means the bank may be legally permitted to follow instructions from either holder until a formal dispute is registered. Always refer to your specific account Terms and Conditions.
