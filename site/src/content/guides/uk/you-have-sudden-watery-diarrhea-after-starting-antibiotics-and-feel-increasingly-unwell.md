# What to do if… you have sudden watery diarrhea after starting antibiotics and feel increasingly unwell (UK)

## Short answer
Call NHS 111 now for urgent same-day advice, or use 111 online where it is available. Call 999 or go to A&E now if you are confused, difficult to wake, have sudden severe tummy pain, severe breathing difficulty, cold or blotchy skin, or feel dangerously unwell.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take medicines to stop diarrhoea, such as loperamide or Imodium, unless a clinician tells you to.
- Do not rely on alcohol hand gel alone if C. difficile is possible; wash hands with soap and water.
- Do not decide on your own whether to stop or continue the antibiotic while you are getting worse; get urgent advice before the next dose if you can do so safely.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics, someone else’s antibiotics, or colon cleanse products.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E if you feel faint, confused, very weak, or severely unwell.

## What to do now
1. **Get urgent triage first.**
   - Call **999** or go to **A&E** now if you are confused, difficult to wake, fainting or collapsing, have sudden severe tummy pain, severe breathing difficulty, cold or blotchy skin, or you feel dangerously unwell.
   - Otherwise, call **NHS 111 now** for same-day advice. Use **111 online** where it is available, or phone 111 if you are unsure.
   - Ask for urgent advice today if you have diarrhoea while taking, or soon after taking, antibiotics; bloody diarrhoea; worsening weakness; dizziness on standing; repeated vomiting; or signs of dehydration.

2. **Say clearly that this started after antibiotics.**
   - Give the name of the antibiotic, when you started it, and when you last took it.
   - Say how many watery stools you have had in the last 24 hours and whether it is getting worse.
   - Mention fever, tummy pain or cramps, blood, recent hospital or care home contact, a previous C. difficile infection, pregnancy, age over 65, a weakened immune system, or medicines that reduce stomach acid such as PPIs.
   - Say whether you are peeing less than normal, very thirsty, dizzy when standing, very weak, or unable to keep fluids down.

3. **Start rehydrating while you arrange care.**
   - Take small, frequent sips of water or an oral rehydration drink.
   - Ask a pharmacist about oral rehydration sachets if you can do that without delaying urgent advice.
   - Escalate to 111 again, urgent care, 999, or A&E if you cannot keep fluids down, become confused or very drowsy, or feel much worse.

4. **Reduce spread at home until you have been assessed.**
   - Wash your hands with soap and water after using the toilet and before preparing food.
   - Use a separate towel if you can.
   - Use a separate toilet if possible. If not, clean the toilet seat, flush handle, taps, and nearby high-touch surfaces after use with a household disinfectant or cleaning product used as directed.
   - Do not prepare food for other people if you can avoid it.

5. **Be ready for same-day next steps.**
   - You may be asked for a stool sample if C. difficile is suspected.
   - Keep the antibiotic packet, your regular medicine list, and any recent discharge paperwork nearby.
   - Follow the plan you are given about whether to continue, pause, change, or stop any medicine.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to decide today about probiotics, supplements, or a special diet.
- You do not need to deep-clean your whole home now; focus on handwashing, towels, and bathroom surfaces.
- You do not need to search for treatment options before getting urgent triage.

## Important reassurance
Diarrhoea can happen with antibiotics and does not always mean C. difficile. Feeling increasingly unwell with watery diarrhoea after antibiotics is still a valid reason to get urgent help today, especially because dehydration can build up quickly.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours only. Later decisions may need a GP, NHS 111 clinician, urgent care team, pharmacist, or hospital team, depending on your symptoms, risk factors, examination, and any stool test.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice or a diagnosis. If you feel severely unwell, deteriorate quickly, become confused or very drowsy, or cannot keep fluids down, use urgent services and follow their instructions.
