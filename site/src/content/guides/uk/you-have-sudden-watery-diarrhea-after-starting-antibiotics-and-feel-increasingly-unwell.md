# What to do if… you have sudden watery diarrhea after starting antibiotics and feel increasingly unwell (UK)

## Short answer
Contact NHS 111 (phone or online) or your GP immediately for a same-day clinical assessment, as watery diarrhea after antibiotics requires urgent triage for C. difficile and dehydration.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take anti-motility medicines (like loperamide or Imodium) to stop the diarrhea unless specifically directed by a clinician; these can worsen certain infections like C. difficile.
- Do not stop taking your prescribed antibiotics without consulting a healthcare professional first, unless you are experiencing a life-threatening emergency.
- Do not rely on alcohol-based hand gels alone; they do not kill C. difficile spores.
- Do not share towels, flannels, or cutlery with others in your household.
- Do not visit hospitals, care homes, or GP surgeries in person before speaking to them on the phone if you have active diarrhea.

## What to do now
1. **Request an urgent clinical triage immediately.**
   - **Call 999 now** if you have signs of sepsis or severe complications: confusion, fainting, severe drowsiness, a cold or blotchy appearance, extremely fast breathing, or a very hard, swollen, or intensely painful abdomen.
   - **Contact NHS 111 or your GP practice now** if you have watery diarrhea while taking (or recently finished) antibiotics. Inform them specifically about the antibiotic use, as this prioritises your assessment for Clostridioides difficile (C. diff).

2. **Prepare a summary of your symptoms and history.**
   - **Identify the name and dosage** of the antibiotic you are taking or have recently finished.
   - **Count the number of watery stools** you have had in the last 24 hours.
   - **Check for specific risk factors** including age over 65, recent hospital or care home stays, or being immunocompromised.
   - **Note sensory cues** such as a high temperature, blood in the stool, or a distinctively foul or unusual smell.

3. **Monitor for worsening dehydration.**
   - **Sip small amounts of water or oral rehydration solution** frequently.
   - **Check your urine output;** if you are peeing significantly less than usual or the urine is very dark, report this to the 111 clinician.
   - **Note any lightheadedness** when moving from sitting to standing.

4. **Isolate and prevent household transmission.**
   - **Wash your hands with soap and water** thoroughly after every toilet visit and before handling food.
   - **Use a separate toilet** if available; if not, clean all high-touch surfaces (flush handles, taps, seats) with a bleach-based disinfectant after every use.
   - **Stay at home** until at least 48 hours have passed since your last episode of diarrhea.

5. **Manage contaminated items safely.**
   - **Wash any clothing or bedding** soiled with stool separately from other laundry.
   - **Use the highest temperature setting** recommended for the fabric to help eliminate bacteria and spores.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a probiotic or supplement today; focus on clinical assessment first.
- You do not need to deep-clean non-bathroom areas of your home immediately.
- You do not need to decide on long-term dietary changes while in the acute phase.

## Important reassurance
Antibiotic-associated diarrhea is common, but seeking urgent advice when you feel "increasingly unwell" is the correct safety step. Clinical triage is designed to distinguish between minor side effects and infections that require specific treatment. Medical professionals expect these calls when antibiotics are involved.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization and triage guidance only. Diagnosis and treatment plans require professional clinical assessment and potentially stool sample testing.

## Important note
This information is for guidance in acute situations and is not a substitute for professional medical diagnosis. If you deteriorate rapidly or cannot keep fluids down, contact emergency services (999) or NHS 111 immediately.
