# What to do if… you find standing water in your basement or crawl space after heavy rain (UK)

## Short answer
Treat standing water in a basement or crawl space as an electrical, gas, and contamination hazard first. Keep people and pets out, and only go near it once power to that area can be made safe from a dry, safe place.

## Do not do these things
- Do not step into the water if it could be near sockets, extension leads, appliances, a boiler, a consumer unit, or wiring.
- Do not touch switches, plugs, the consumer unit, or electrical equipment if you are wet, standing near water, or unsure what has been soaked.
- Do not turn gas or electrical appliances back on after they have been wet until a qualified person says it is safe.
- Do not use candles, matches, lighters, or open flames to see better.
- Do not operate electrical switches if you smell gas.
- Do not rapidly pump out a large amount of water if water outside the walls may still be high, or if you are unsure whether pumping could affect the structure.
- Do not throw away damaged items or rip out flooring before you have photographed the water level and damage, unless leaving them in place creates an immediate safety risk.

## What to do now
1. **Make it a no-go area.** Close the door or hatch if you can, keep children and pets away, and do not enter the basement or crawl space to “just check” while water is present.

2. **Check for immediate danger from a dry, safe place.** If you see sparks, hear buzzing or crackling, see smoke, see serious sagging or movement, or someone is in immediate danger, leave the area and call 999.

3. **If you smell gas, leave and use the gas emergency route.** Do not smoke, use flames, or turn electrical switches on or off. Get outside or to fresh air, then call the gas emergency number for your part of the UK. For natural gas, this is 0800 111 999 in England, Wales and Scotland, and 0800 002 001 in Northern Ireland. Call 999 as well if there is a fire, explosion risk, or immediate danger to life.

4. **Make electricity safe only if you can do it without going near water.**
   - If you can reach the consumer unit or main switch from a dry, safe place, switch the power off.
   - If the consumer unit, meter area, sockets, or any electrical equipment may be wet, do not touch them.
   - Contact a registered electrician. If you need help with an electricity emergency or the incoming supply, contact the electricity network operator: in Great Britain you can call 105, and in Northern Ireland contact NIE Networks on 03457 643 643.

5. **Stop more water entering only if there is a simple safe action.**
   - If water is coming through a doorway, air brick, lightwell, or window and you can act without wading, use towels, boards, sandbags, or temporary barriers.
   - If you suspect a plumbing leak as well as rainwater, turn off the stopcock only if you can reach it without stepping into water.

6. **Record what you can see without entering the water.** Take photos or video of the water depth, the likely entry points, the water line on walls or steps, wet sockets or appliances, affected boiler or utility areas, and damaged items.

7. **Contact the right people for the cause you can see.**
   - Contact your home insurer, or your landlord or letting agent if you rent, to log the incident and ask what emergency drying, cleanup, or restoration they want you to use.
   - Contact a registered electrician before using any mains-powered equipment in or near the flooded area.
   - If drains, toilets, or sinks are backing up, or the water smells of sewage, contact your wastewater company.
   - If water is coming from a flooded road, blocked roadside drain, or ditch, report it to your local council or highways authority.
   - For wider flood information, check the official flood-warning service for your part of the UK.

8. **Reduce damage from outside the wet area while you wait.** Use a battery torch. Move valuables, chemicals, paperwork, and cardboard boxes away from the access point or to a higher floor only if you can do this without stepping into water.

9. **Treat the water as contaminated unless you are sure it is clean.** Avoid splashing it on your face. If you must handle wet items from the edge of the area, wear waterproof gloves and boots, then wash your hands well.

10. **Remove water slowly and only when the situation is stable.** Do not use a wet vacuum, pump, dehumidifier, or extension lead until the electrics have been made safe. Stop and get professional advice if cracks appear, the floor or walls move, or the water keeps rising.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact cause today, such as groundwater, surface water, blocked drains, or failed waterproofing.
- You do not need to decide on permanent drainage, tanking, sump pumps, or waterproofing now.
- You do not need to start ripping out plaster, flooring, insulation, or stored items before your insurer, landlord, electrician, or restoration contractor gives instructions, unless there is an immediate safety reason.
- You do not need to make a full repair plan while the water, electrics, gas, and contamination risks are still unresolved.

## Important reassurance
Finding water under your home after heavy rain is alarming, but you do not have to solve the whole cause immediately. The safest first move is to keep out of the water, make utilities safe without touching wet equipment, document what happened, and get the right emergency help involved.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and prevent irreversible mistakes. Full cleanup, drying, mould prevention, electrical checks, drainage work, and structural repairs may need specialist help and may be directed by your insurer or landlord.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, building, electrical, gas, insurance, or other professional advice. Follow emergency service instructions, utility emergency instructions, and advice from qualified professionals for your property.
