# What to do if… you think a death may become public and you need to choose a family spokesperson quickly (UK)

## Short answer
Designate one person as the sole point of contact and use a brief, pre-written statement to redirect all media enquiries. This prevents contradictory information spreading and protects other family members from direct intrusion.

## Do not do these things
- Do not allow multiple family members to give individual "off the record" comments to neighbours or reporters.
- Do not attempt to correct every online rumour or social media post in real-time.
- Do not share sensitive details such as the cause of death, specific timelines, or names before official confirmation.
- Do not engage in arguments regarding who "should" speak; select the person most capable of remaining calm under pressure.
- Do not answer the door or the phone to unknown callers if you feel overwhelmed.

## What to do now
1. **Appoint one spokesperson and one backup immediately.** 
   The primary spokesperson should be someone who can communicate clearly and stick to a script, while the backup only covers the role if the primary is unavailable.
2. **Draft a two-sentence "holding statement" for the whole family.** 
   **The statement should be kept simple and focused on privacy.**
   - Example: "We are devastated by our loss and ask for privacy to support one another at this difficult time. We will not be making any further comments or taking questions at this stage."
3. **Distribute a "no-comment" template to all family members.** 
   **Ensure everyone has a standard response saved on their phone to copy and paste.**
   - Template: "Thank you for reaching out. Please direct all enquiries to our family spokesperson at [Email/Phone]. We are not speaking individually."
4. **Place a physical note on your front door.** 
   **This is a recognized method to deter doorstep journalists and maintain boundaries.**
   - The note should state: "This family is grieving and does not wish to be disturbed by the media. Please contact [Name/Email] for any enquiries. No door-knocks please."
5. **Contact the relevant professional press office for redirection.** 
   **If the death involved the police, a hospital, or a large employer, use their existing media infrastructure.**
   - For police investigations: Ask for the "Force Press Office" and request they handle factual media updates. 
   - For hospital deaths: Contact the "Communications Department" or "Trust Press Office."
6. **Activate "Privacy Mode" on all family social media accounts.** 
   **Lock down profiles to prevent journalists from harvesting photos or "RIP" comments.**
   - Turn off tagging and set accounts to private.
   - Advise family not to post any tributes until the media situation is stabilized.
7. **Identify your escalation contacts for harassment.** 
   **If journalists persist after being asked to stop, use official regulatory channels.**
   - IPSO (Independent Press Standards Organisation) has a 24-hour emergency harassment line: 07659 152656.
   - Ofcom handles complaints regarding broadcast (TV/Radio) intrusion.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a photograph of the deceased immediately.
- You do not need to decide on funeral arrangements or an obituary today.
- You do not need to respond to "urgent" media deadlines; they are not your responsibility.
- You do not need to explain the circumstances of the death to anyone outside the investigation.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel hunted or overwhelmed when a private tragedy becomes a public interest story. Taking control of the "flow" of information is not a sign of being secretive; it is a necessary act of protection for your family's mental health and the integrity of any ongoing investigation.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only, focused on the immediate 24 to 48 hours following a death. Managing long-term media interest or public inquests may require professional PR or legal assistance.

## Important note
This guide is for information purposes and does not constitute legal advice. If a death is subject to a police investigation or a Coroner's inquest, be aware that certain facts are matters of public record, but you still retain the right to refuse personal interviews and protect your home from harassment.
