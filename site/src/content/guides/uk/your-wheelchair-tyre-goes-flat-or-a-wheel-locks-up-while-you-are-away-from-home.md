# What to do if… your wheelchair tyre goes flat or a wheel locks up while you are away from home (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself to the safest nearby stopping place first. If you are stuck in live traffic or another dangerous road position and cannot get clear, call 999 now; otherwise, stabilise the chair and arrange assistance rather than forcing it or attempting risky repairs.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep pushing hard on a flat tyre or locked wheel; it can tip you, damage the rim or motor, or make the problem worse.
- Do not try to fix the chair at a kerb edge, in the road, in a cycle lane, or in car park traffic.
- Do not let strangers lift you or the chair unless you have agreed exactly what is being lifted, where hands will go, and whether you can safely transfer.
- Do not attempt an unsafe transfer just to get moving unless staying where you are is clearly more dangerous.
- Do not remove wheels, release drive levers, or adjust mechanisms you do not already handle confidently in normal conditions.
- Do not feel you have to diagnose the fault before asking for help.

## What to do now
1. **Get to the nearest safer pause.**
   - Aim for a pavement, wide entrance, recessed doorway, bus stop area, station concourse, shop, library, cafe, or other staffed place.
   - If you are stuck in live traffic or a dangerous road position and cannot get clear, call 999 and say you are a wheelchair user stranded in traffic and need urgent assistance.
   - If someone is with you, ask them to stand where they can be seen and help direct people around you without stepping into danger.

2. **Stabilise the chair without forcing anything.**
   - Secure the chair if you can do so safely, but do not force a brake, wheel, lever, or joystick that feels jammed.
   - Keep hands, clothing, bags, blankets, and straps clear of spokes, caster forks, tyres, and pinch points.
   - If the chair is on a slope, ask someone to stand steadying the chair from a safe position while you arrange help.

3. **Do a quick, no-tools check only.**
   - **Flat tyre:** the tyre looks or feels soft, the chair pulls to one side, or the rim is closer to the ground.
   - **Wheel jam or lock:** a wheel will not rotate, scrapes, catches, or stops the chair even though the tyre looks firm.
   - **Powered wheelchair issue:** the chair will not drive, one side drags, it beeps, shows a fault, or moves unpredictably.

4. **If a wheel seems jammed, check only what you can see safely.**
   - Look for stones, mud, clothing, bag straps, leaves, or other debris caught around the caster, wheel fork, axle area, tyre, or frame.
   - Check whether a manual brake is partly on or rubbing, but do not force it.
   - For a powered wheelchair, do not switch drive or freewheel levers unless you are sure what they do, you are on level ground, and someone is steadying the chair.

5. **Ask for practical help where you are.**
   - Go into the nearest staffed place if you can, or ask someone nearby: “Can someone stay with me while I arrange assistance, and can we move me just inside or out of the flow?”
   - If you are at a rail station, tell station staff you are stranded because your wheelchair has failed. If staff are not visible, use a Help Point, intercom, assistance point, or ticket office where available.
   - Passenger Assist is usually arranged in advance, but National Rail also describes last-minute turn-up-and-go help where staff can assist if they can.
   - On TfL services in London, ask station staff for help or use a Help Point. TfL has turn-up-and-go assistance on the Tube, London Overground, and Elizabeth line, but help can depend on the service and location.

6. **Arrange recovery or support rather than a risky repair.**
   - If you lease a scooter or powered wheelchair through Motability, contact Motability Assist or use Motability’s recovery route for scooters and powered wheelchairs.
   - If you are not a Motability customer, call your wheelchair supplier, repairer, NHS wheelchair service contact, insurer, breakdown cover, or the number on your chair sticker or paperwork.
   - If the chair cannot roll safely, arrange an accessible taxi, community transport option, suitable lift, or trusted person to help you get home or to a safe indoor place.
   - When you call, say where you are, whether you are indoors or near traffic, whether the chair is manual or powered, what wheel is affected, and whether you can transfer safely.

7. **Only do a low-risk temporary fix if you are safely off the route and already know how.**
   - If you have air-filled tyres, have a pump, and know the correct tyre range, inflate only within the range shown on the tyre sidewall or in your chair documentation.
   - Do not exceed the stated maximum tyre pressure.
   - If you do not already remove wheels, tubes, or casters confidently in normal conditions, do not attempt it while stranded. Focus on recovery and a proper repair.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether you need a new tyre, tube, wheel, caster, battery, motor, or service.
- You do not need to work out exactly why it happened before you get safe.
- You do not need to deal with costs, complaints, warranties, or replacement parts while you are stranded.
- You do not need to accept the first suggestion from a stranger if it feels unsafe or rushed.

## Important reassurance
A sudden wheelchair problem away from home can cause an instant panic spike because your mobility and safety are affected at once. Treating it like a breakdown, get safe, stabilise, ask for help, and arrange recovery, is a calm and practical response.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes to a couple of hours: getting safe, avoiding injury, and arranging help. Repairs, parts, liability, complaints, and longer-term maintenance decisions can come later with your usual provider or a specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, engineering, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, including being stuck in live traffic, call 999. If moving the chair could cause a fall or injury, treat it as unsafe and get assistance.
