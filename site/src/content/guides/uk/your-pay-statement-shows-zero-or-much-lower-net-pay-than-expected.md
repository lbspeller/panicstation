# What to do if… your pay statement shows zero or much lower net pay than expected (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as urgent but fixable: check the pay period, gross pay, hours, and deduction lines, then contact payroll or HR today for a written explanation and the earliest correction route.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume it is normal if you cannot cover essentials.
- Do not quit, walk out, or send angry messages before you know what caused the zero or low net pay.
- Do not post your payslip, bank details, National Insurance number, or payroll details in group chats or on social media.
- Do not ignore a deduction you do not recognise.
- Do not accept a vague verbal answer if your pay is missing or sharply reduced; ask for the reason and next step in writing.

## What to do now
1. **Check the payslip basics first.** Look at:
   - the pay period dates
   - hours worked, if your pay changes with hours
   - gross pay before deductions
   - net pay after deductions
   - each deduction line, especially anything new or unusually large

2. **Separate “pay was deducted” from “pay did not arrive”.**
   - If gross pay looks normal but net pay is £0 or very low, the issue is probably a deduction, tax code, pension, benefits, salary sacrifice, court-ordered deduction, or overpayment recovery issue.
   - If the payslip shows normal net pay but your bank received nothing, the issue is probably payment delivery, bank details, payroll timing, or a failed payment.

3. **If the money did not arrive in your bank, check the simple payment points.** Look for pending or returned incoming payments, then privately confirm with payroll that they hold the correct bank account details and that the payment was actually sent.

4. **Contact payroll or HR today and ask for three things in writing.**
   - The plain-English reason the net pay is zero or far lower than expected.
   - A corrected calculation or corrected payslip if an error is found.
   - The earliest possible way to pay any missing wages, including whether an urgent or off-cycle payment is possible.

5. **Ask them to check the common causes one by one.** Ask whether the low net pay is due to:
   - recovery of a previous overpayment
   - pension, benefits, or salary sacrifice deductions
   - tax, National Insurance, or student loan deductions
   - an attachment of earnings order or other court-ordered deduction shown on the payslip
   - missing hours, missing overtime, or an incorrect pay period
   - a new job, leaving date, unpaid leave, sickness, or timesheet approval issue

6. **If tax looks wrong, check your tax code.** Compare the tax code on your payslip with the tax code HMRC has for you in the Check your Income Tax service or HMRC app. If they differ, ask payroll what code they have been told to use and update your details with HMRC if anything is wrong or missing.

7. **Save a small evidence bundle before anything changes.** Keep:
   - the payslip that shows the problem
   - your last normal payslip, if you have one
   - rota, timesheet, overtime, or approval records for that pay period
   - bank evidence showing what was or was not received
   - one written message to payroll or HR summarising the issue and what you are asking them to fix

8. **If you do not get a clear explanation or prompt correction, escalate calmly in writing.** Send the issue to your manager and HR, use your employer’s internal process, and consider contacting Acas for early advice. A formal grievance can wait until you have asked for the correction and the issue still has not been resolved or explained.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to leave the job.
- You do not need to start a formal claim today.
- You do not need a perfect calculation before asking payroll to explain the cause and correction route.
- You do not need to contact several organisations at once; start with payroll or HR, then keep written records.

## Important reassurance
A zero or unexpectedly low net pay is frightening, especially on payday. Many cases come from a payroll input, deduction, tax code, missing-hours, or payment-delivery problem, and a written breakdown usually makes the next step clearer.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: they are meant to stabilise the situation, protect your pay record, and help you get a clear internal answer. Later decisions may need help from Acas, a trade union, an adviser, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, employment, or professional advice. If missing pay means you cannot cover essentials, you may need to contact your rent, mortgage, utility, childcare, or lender provider and explain that your wages are in dispute while you seek an urgent correction.
