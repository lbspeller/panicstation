# What to do if… your vehicle alarm won’t stop and you are worried it will drain the battery or escalate the situation (UK)

## Short answer
Create physical distance from the vehicle to assess for tampering, then attempt a sequence of physical key unlocks and engine starts to reset the system. If the alarm is a persistent malfunction without criminal activity, it is a local council Environmental Health matter rather than a police emergency.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t stay in the immediate vicinity if a crowd is forming or if the situation feels aggressive.
- Don’t repeatedly mash the key fob buttons; this can desynchronise the rolling code or lock the system.
- Don’t attempt to disconnect high-voltage batteries on Electric Vehicles (EVs) or hybrids.
- Don’t call 999 for noise alone; only use 999 if you see active vehicle theft, broken glass, or a person attempting to break in.
- Don’t ignore the alarm if it stops and starts; it may indicate a low 12V battery or a faulty sensor that will eventually drain the power.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe observation point.**
   **Step at least five metres away from the vehicle to check for signs of a break-in.**
   - Look for shattered windows or door handles that appear forced.
   - Check if anyone is acting suspiciously nearby.
   - If you see an active crime, call **999** immediately.

2. **Execute a manual key reset.**
   **Insert the physical key blade into the driver’s door lock and turn it to the unlock position.**
   - Hold it in the unlock position for three seconds; many UK-spec vehicles use this as a global "silence" command.
   - If the door opens, immediately insert the key into the ignition (if applicable) and turn to "On" or "Start."

3. **Check for "Panic Mode" triggers.**
   **Press and hold the 'Unlock' or 'Panic' button on your fob for five seconds.**
   - Some systems require a long press to cancel a panic activation triggered by the fob being squeezed in a pocket.

4. **Verify all closure points.**
   **Ensure the bonnet, boot, and all doors are physically latched.**
   - A partially latched bonnet is a common cause for UK car alarms to trigger "intruder" sensors.
   - Do not slam the doors repeatedly; a firm, single close is sufficient to seat the sensors.

5. **Isolate the noise if safe to do so.**
   **If you are certain there is no crime and you are mechanically confident, locate the horn or alarm fuse in the fuse box.**
   - Refer to the vehicle handbook to identify the exact fuse number.
   - Be aware that pulling fuses on modern CAN-bus vehicles may trigger immobiliser faults or dashboard warning lights.

6. **Manage the social escalation.**
   **If neighbours approach, state clearly: "I am the owner and I am working to silence the fault now."**
   - If you feel physically threatened or harassed by others due to the noise, move to a safe location and call **101**.

7. **Contact professional recovery or local authority.**
   **If the alarm continues and you cannot stop it, call your breakdown provider or the local council’s Environmental Health department.**
   - Most UK councils have a 24-hour "Noise Nuisance" reporting line.
   - Under the Environmental Protection Act 1990, councils can serve a notice to silence an alarm that sounds for more than 20 minutes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify which specific sensor (ultrasonic, tilt, or door) is faulty right now.
- You do not need to replace the 12V battery immediately, though a low battery is the most likely cause of a "ghost" alarm.
- You do not need to apologise individually to everyone in the area; focus on the mechanical resolution or professional recovery.

## Important reassurance
Car alarms are designed to be stressful to encourage quick action, but a malfunctioning alarm is a common mechanical issue. Provided you have the key and the vehicle is yours, you are not in legal trouble for a technical fault. Most modern systems have a "time-out" feature that will eventually silence the siren to prevent total battery drain.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active alarm to prevent escalation and battery failure. It does not cover long-term electrical diagnostics or anti-theft system reprogramming.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace the specific instructions in your vehicle’s manufacturer handbook. Electrical systems in modern vehicles, especially hybrids and EVs, carry risks of shock or system damage if handled incorrectly. Always prioritise personal safety over vehicle noise.
