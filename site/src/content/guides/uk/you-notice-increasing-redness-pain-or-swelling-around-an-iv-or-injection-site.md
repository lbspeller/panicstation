# What to do if… you notice increasing redness, pain, or swelling around an IV or injection site (UK)

## Short answer
If an IV, cannula, or line site is getting more red, painful, swollen, hot, leaky, or blistered, stop using it if it is still in place and contact the team that treated you or NHS 111 now. Call 999 if you have breathing difficulty, severe chest pain, collapse, confusion, or rapidly worsening swelling of the arm, neck, or face.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore a site that is clearly getting worse.
- Do not massage or firmly rub the area unless a clinician specifically tells you to.
- Do not squeeze, pick, or try to drain the area yourself.
- Do not apply heat, ice, creams, or ointments unless a clinician tells you to, because the right treatment can depend on what went into the vein.
- Do not flush, use, or put anything through the IV, cannula, or line if it is painful, red, swollen, leaking, or not working, unless your clinical team has assessed it and told you to.
- Do not wrap the area tightly or put pressure over swelling.
- Do not wait for normal opening hours if you feel unwell or the change is spreading quickly.

## What to do now
1. **If the IV, cannula, or line is still in place, stop using it.** If you are still in hospital, clinic, or a day unit, tell a nurse immediately. If you are at home, call the ward, infusion unit, day unit, community IV team, or advice number on your discharge paperwork.
2. **Use emergency help for severe symptoms.** Call 999 now if you have trouble breathing, severe chest pain, collapse or fainting, confusion, or rapidly worsening swelling of the arm, neck, or face.
3. **Get urgent same-day medical advice for serious site changes.** Use NHS 111, A&E, or your treating team urgently if you have fever, shaking chills, pus or drainage, rapidly spreading redness, red streaks, severe or escalating pain, new numbness or weakness in the hand, or skin that is blistered, very tight, pale, blue, unusually cold, or turning darker.
4. **Give the exact words when you call.** Say: “My IV or cannula site is getting more red, painful, or swollen after an infusion or injection. I am worried about infection or medicine leaking into the tissue.”
5. **While waiting for advice, protect the area.** Rest and support the limb on a pillow, remove rings or watches from that arm if it is swelling, keep any dressing clean and dry, and avoid tight clothing over the site.
6. **Do a quick record if it does not delay help.** Take one clear photo, note the time, and mark the edge of the redness lightly with a pen so you can tell whether it is spreading.
7. **If this followed chemotherapy or cancer treatment, use the oncology or chemotherapy advice number urgently.** Tell them what was given if you know. If you do not have a number or cannot get through, use NHS 111 or A&E and say it followed chemotherapy or cancer treatment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether this is infection, bruising, phlebitis, allergy, infiltration, or extravasation right now.
- You do not need to research treatments before calling.
- You do not need to apply creams, heat, ice, or dressings while you are unsure.
- You do not need to make a complaint or prove what happened today; focus on being assessed and keeping the site safe.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to feel alarmed when an IV site gets worse after you leave. Many causes can be treated, and getting prompt advice is the safest next step, especially when redness, pain, or swelling is increasing rather than settling.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours after noticing worsening redness, pain, or swelling around an IV, cannula, line, infusion site, or injection site. Later decisions, such as antibiotics, dressings, scans, pain relief, line removal, or specific extravasation treatment, need a clinician who can examine the area and knows what was given.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical advice, diagnosis, or professional care. If you feel significantly unwell, symptoms are rapidly worsening, or you are unsure what to do, seek urgent medical help.
