# What to do if… you notice someone photographing or filming you in a way that feels targeted (UK)

## Short answer
Move immediately to a busy, staffed area to create a safe pause, involve a third party or security for support, and call 999 if the person follows you or the situation escalates.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the individual alone or attempt to physically block the camera.
- Do not attempt to grab the phone or device, as this can escalate to a physical altercation.
- Do not follow the person to see where they go or to "get evidence" by yourself.
- Do not head toward isolated areas, quiet side streets, or your own home or car while being followed.
- Do not engage in a debate about the legality of filming in public; focus entirely on your physical exit and safety.
- Do not ignore your instincts even if the recording is subtle; targeted behavior is a valid safety concern.

## What to do now
1. **Relocate to a populated and staffed environment immediately.** Move toward a shop, café, station concourse, or a reception desk where other people and staff are present. 
   - If outside, cross the street to a well-lit area or stand near a group of people. 
2. **Involve a member of staff or security once you reach a safe pause point.** State clearly that you feel targeted by someone filming you and ask for assistance.
   - Request that the staff member stays with you until you are ready to leave.
   - Ask if they can escort you to a taxi, a different platform, or a secure exit.
3. **Call 999 if you feel at immediate risk or the person continues to follow you.** If you cannot speak, use the "Silent Solution" system on a mobile.
   - **Stay on the line and press 55 when prompted by the automated message to signal a genuine emergency.**
   - Listen to the operator's instructions; they may ask you to cough or tap the handset to answer questions.
4. **Use the British Transport Police text service if you are on a train or at a station.** You can discreetly **text 61016** or call **0800 40 50 40** to report non-emergency harassment.
   - Provide the train service details, your carriage number, and the next station.
   - For regional buses, check for local text-to-report services like the "SeeSay" 81018 service in the West Midlands.
5. **Document the incident details only once you are in a secure location.** Record the basic facts on your phone or in a notebook.
   - Note the time, exact location, and a description of the individual (clothing, approximate height, equipment used).
   - Record any specific actions or words that made the filming feel targeted or intimidating.
6. **Request that the venue retains CCTV footage as soon as possible.** Ask the manager of the shop, station, or building to log the incident and save relevant footage, as many systems overwrite data within 24 to 72 hours.
   - Obtain an incident reference number if the venue provides one.
7. **Report the behavior to your local police force via 101 or their online portal.** This is essential if the behavior is part of a pattern or falls under the Protection from Sex-based Harassment in Public Act 2026.
   - Explain that the behavior caused you alarm, distress, or fear for your safety.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the specific law or offence before asking for help.
- You do not need to have a clear photo of the individual to make a report.
- You do not need to decide whether you want to press charges in the first few minutes.
- You do not need to check your own social media or post about the incident immediately.

## Important reassurance
It is a common and normal reaction to feel hyper-vigilant or startled when you notice someone filming you. You have the right to move away and seek help whenever an interaction feels intrusive or threatening. Prioritizing your physical distance and involving others is a standard safety protocol, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate encounter with targeted filming or photography in public. It does not cover long-term legal action, data protection claims, or psychological support for stalking.

## Important note
This information is for guidance and does not constitute legal advice. While filming in public is generally lawful in the UK, it is a criminal offence if the behavior is used to intentionally harass, alarm, or distress an individual, particularly under 2026 sex-based harassment protections.
