# What to do if… you notice someone photographing or filming you in a way that feels targeted (UK)

## Short answer
Move towards other people and a staffed place, then ask staff or security to stay with you. Call 999 if you are in immediate danger, a crime is happening now, or the situation is escalating.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront them alone, argue about legality, or try to win the interaction.
- Don’t try to grab their phone or camera.
- Don’t use physical contact to block filming.
- Don’t follow them to prove what they are doing.
- Don’t lead them to your home, car, workplace entrance, or a quiet place.
- Don’t demand deletion in the moment; you generally cannot force someone to delete photos or footage yourself.
- Don’t post their face or details publicly while you are still in the situation.
- Don’t dismiss your concern just because filming in public can be lawful; targeted, repeated, threatening, or stalking-like behaviour may still need help.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause point.** Go into a busy, staffed place such as a shop, café, station concourse, reception desk, ticket office, or transport information point. Stay where staff and other people can see you.
2. **Bring another person into it.** Say clearly: “I think that person is filming me and it feels targeted. Can you stay with me while I move away and get help?” Ask staff or security to stay nearby and, if possible, walk you to a safer exit, taxi rank, platform, reception area, or waiting point.
3. **Call 999 if there is immediate danger.** Use 999 if they are following you, blocking you, threatening you, waiting for you, trying to isolate you, or you cannot safely leave. If you cannot speak on a mobile 999 call, try to whisper, cough, tap, or follow the operator’s prompts; press 55 or tap only when prompted.
4. **Use rail-specific help if you are on the rail network.** Tell rail staff what is happening. For a non-emergency rail concern, text British Transport Police on 61016 or call 0800 40 50 40. Use 999 if you need urgent help.
5. **Record details once you are safer.** Text yourself or write down the time, exact location, direction of travel, what they used, what they did, anything said, a description of the person, and any vehicle details. If it is safe, take a photo or short video from a distance; do not move closer to get evidence.
6. **Ask the place to preserve its own record.** In a shop, station, gym, venue, workplace, or building reception, ask staff to note the exact time and location and to retain CCTV as soon as possible. Ask for an incident log number if they create one.
7. **Report non-emergency concerns once you are out of immediate stress.** Call 101 or use your local police force’s online reporting route. If it has happened more than once, say that you are concerned about harassment or stalking and give the pattern: dates, places, repeat sightings, same person, same route, or links to someone you know.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether the filming was legal.
- You do not need to identify the exact offence before asking for help.
- You do not need a perfect account before speaking to staff, security, or police.
- You do not need to confront them, demand deletion, or make a public post today.
- You do not need to decide longer-term steps until you are somewhere safer and calmer.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel unsettled when recording feels targeted. Moving towards people, involving staff, and keeping simple notes are calm safety steps, not overreacting.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the moment and reduce immediate risk. Later decisions, especially if this is repeated or linked to someone you know, may need help from police, a safeguarding lead, an employer, a venue manager, or a specialist support service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you feel at immediate risk, call 999.
