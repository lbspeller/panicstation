# What to do if… you are asked to authorise a cremation but are unsure if you have the legal right (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign the cremation application while you are unsure. Ask the funeral provider or cremation authority to pause the application until the correct applicant, any will or named person, and any known objections have been checked.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign just to keep arrangements moving if your authority is unclear.
- Don’t guess that “next of kin” means you are automatically the correct applicant.
- Don’t sign if you know, or strongly suspect, that someone with a relevant role objects.
- Don’t assume a Power of Attorney gives authority after death.
- Don’t give ashes instructions on an application if you are not sure you should be the applicant.
- Don’t let anyone complete or sign an applicant declaration for you unless the cremation authority confirms that is allowed.

## What to do now
1. **Say, in writing if possible: “Please pause the cremation application until authority is confirmed.”**  
   Send this to the funeral director, direct cremation provider, or crematorium. Ask them not to submit or rely on any cremation application from you yet.

2. **Ask which legal process applies.**  
   Ask: “Where did the death occur, where will the cremation take place, and which current application form applies?” This matters because England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland do not use one single UK-wide process.

3. **Get the exact form before deciding whether to sign.**  
   For a death in England or Wales, the main form is usually Cremation 1, “Application for cremation of the body of a person who has died.” Read the applicant questions about whether you are a near relative or executor, whether others have been informed, and whether anyone has objected. For Scotland or Northern Ireland, ask the provider or cremation authority for the correct local form and the applicant requirements before signing.

4. **Check for a will, executor, or named person.**  
   Ask close family or the person holding papers: “Is there a will, and does it name an executor or someone to arrange the funeral?” If someone has the will, ask for a photo of the page naming the relevant person. Do not delay for non-essential funeral choices; this check is about who should apply.

5. **Check for objections before anyone signs.**  
   Contact the small group most likely to have a relevant role, such as a spouse or civil partner, adult children, parents, siblings, or a named executor. A simple message is enough: “I have been asked to sign the cremation application. Are you aware of any objection, or is someone else the correct applicant?” If anyone objects, tell the provider immediately and do not sign until the cremation authority says what it needs.

6. **If this is England or Wales, use the form checks as a stop sign.**  
   The applicant is normally an executor or near relative over 16. If you are not either, you will usually need to explain on the form why you are applying rather than a near relative or executor. Near relatives and executors should be informed wherever possible, and any objection must be recorded on the form.

7. **If this is Scotland, ask about the named-person and priority order.**  
   Scotland has its own cremation forms and rules. A named person, executor, or priority order may matter. Ask the cremation authority or funeral director to identify the correct Scottish route before you sign.

8. **If this is Northern Ireland, ask for the current local route before signing.**  
   Ask the funeral provider or cremation authority which application, medical certification, and coroner-related steps apply locally. Do not rely on England and Wales or Scotland wording unless the provider confirms it is the correct process for this case.

9. **If you are worried about the death itself, raise that separately and immediately.**  
   Tell the funeral provider that you have concerns about the cause or circumstances of death and ask who to contact before cremation paperwork continues. In England and Wales, this may involve the Medical Examiner or coroner route; in Scotland or Northern Ireland, ask for the correct local contact.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now about flowers, music, obituary wording, or a memorial.
- You do not need to decide what happens to ashes until the correct applicant is clear.
- You do not need to solve the whole family dispute today.
- You only need to stop an irreversible application being signed by the wrong person.

## Important reassurance
Pausing a cremation application is a responsible protective step, not an accusation. Cremation is irreversible, and it is reasonable to ask for time to confirm the correct applicant and record any objection.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stop the wrong person authorising cremation in a pressured moment. If there is a dispute, unclear family status, uncertainty about a will, or disagreement about ashes, later decisions may need advice from a solicitor or confirmation from the cremation authority.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and immediate harm-prevention steps, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Cremation requirements can depend on where the death occurred, where the cremation will take place, the form being used, and the individual facts.
