# What to do if… you are blamed for a data protection incident and asked for an immediate response (UK)

## Short answer
Slow it down safely: get the allegation and questions in writing, ask for a short window to check facts, preserve records without moving them to unsafe places, and use your organisation’s data protection or security incident route.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess, speculate, or fill gaps to be helpful; stick to what you personally know.
- Do not apologise for “the breach” or accept blame before you understand what happened. You can acknowledge concern without admitting fault.
- Do not delete, edit, forward, rename, or tidy up emails, files, chats, tickets, or logs.
- Do not access personal data you do not need to access in order to answer the question.
- Do not move work data onto personal devices, personal email, cloud storage, or messaging apps.
- Do not discuss it widely with colleagues, clients, friends, or on social media.
- Do not secretly record a meeting unless this has been agreed or you have taken advice.
- Do not sign a meeting note or statement that you have not read carefully or that you think is inaccurate.

## What to do now
1. **Get the request into writing, or create a written record.**  
   Reply briefly and ask what incident they mean, what they believe your involvement is, what exact questions they want answered, and when they need the response. If the request was verbal, write a factual note straight away with the date, time, who spoke, what was said, and what you were asked to do.

2. **Ask for a short fact-check window.**  
   Use wording like: “I want to respond accurately. I can give an initial factual response today at 4pm after checking my records. If something is needed sooner, please send the exact questions you need answered first.”

3. **Ask what process is being used.**  
   Ask whether this is an initial fact-finding request, an investigation meeting, or a disciplinary process. Ask for the relevant internal policy, incident reporting route, data handling policy, and any investigation or disciplinary procedure they are relying on.

4. **Preserve relevant records without copying them somewhere unsafe.**  
   Keep relevant emails, files, chats, tickets, access records, screenshots already in work systems, and meeting notes in place. Stop any deletion rule you personally control if you can do so safely. Do not move work data to a personal account or device.

5. **Ask IT, security, or the data protection lead to preserve records that may disappear.**  
   If logs, access records, mailbox data, CCTV, audit trails, or system alerts may expire or be overwritten, ask the appropriate internal team to preserve them under the organisation’s process. Keep your request factual.

6. **Use the organisation’s data protection or security incident route.**  
   Notify the responsible team or person, such as the data protection lead, information governance team, security team, incident mailbox, or ticket queue. Say what you saw, when you became aware, what systems or personal data may be involved, and what you have done so far.

7. **Send a safe holding response if you are being pressured.**  
   Keep it short and factual:
   - what you were asked to answer
   - what you personally observed
   - what you have not yet checked
   - which records you are checking
   - when you will provide an initial response
   - that you have preserved relevant records and used the internal incident route

8. **If they want a meeting now, clarify the type of meeting before answering in detail.**  
   Ask: “Is this an investigation meeting, a disciplinary hearing, a grievance meeting, or an informal fact-finding discussion?” For a disciplinary investigation meeting, there is generally no legal right to be accompanied, but employers may allow it and their policy may say more. If it is a disciplinary hearing that could lead to a formal warning or other disciplinary action, or a grievance meeting, ask to be accompanied by a trade union representative or a colleague if you want support.

9. **If you are in a union, contact them now.**  
   Send them the written request, the deadline, any meeting invite, and your draft holding response. If you are not in a union and the issue may become disciplinary, consider getting independent employment advice before giving a detailed written statement.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to resign, offer to take responsibility, raise a grievance, or propose a settlement.
- You do not need to reconstruct the whole incident immediately. Your first job is to preserve records, use the proper incident route, and give a careful factual first response.
- You do not need to argue legal thresholds or regulator reporting rules in your first reply.
- You do not need to accept a rushed meeting note or statement as final. You can ask to check it and correct inaccuracies.

## Important reassurance
Being asked for an immediate response often reflects internal pressure, incident deadlines, audit trails, or regulator risk. It does not prove you are at fault. A calm written response, careful record preservation, and the correct internal escalation route are usually safer than a rushed explanation.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours or day. Later decisions about formal statements, disciplinary meetings, grievances, regulator communications, settlement discussions, or legal claims may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice, employment advice, data protection advice, or professional advice. Workplace and data protection processes vary by employer and sector. If you believe you are being pressured to misstate facts, conceal information, delete records, or take blame for something you did not do, consider independent advice quickly and keep everything documented.
