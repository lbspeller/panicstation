# What to do if… you realise you brought an old or cancelled passport instead of your current one (UK)

## Short answer
Stop, check exactly what passport you have, and get to a staffed airline, ferry, rail or check-in desk as soon as possible. Most international travel depends on physically showing the valid current passport before you board.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to travel internationally on a cancelled, expired or invalid passport just to see if it works.
- Do not rely on a photo, scan or saved passport number as a substitute for the physical passport.
- Do not keep changing booking or check-in details in a panic; ask staff to correct the travel-document details once.
- Do not hand your passport or documents to unofficial helpers at the airport, port or outside an embassy or consulate.
- Do not cancel everything before checking whether your valid passport can still be brought to you in time.

## What to do now
1. **Check the passport in your hand.**  
   Look at the name, date of birth, passport number, expiry date, and whether it is the newest passport you were issued. Check your coat, hand luggage, document wallet, hotel safe, car and another bag before assuming it is missing.

2. **If your valid passport is nearby, get it moving now.**  
   Ask someone you trust to bring your current passport to the airport, port or station and meet you before check-in closes. If it is in a hotel safe, locker or stored bag, ask staff how quickly it can be retrieved.

3. **Go to a staffed travel desk.**  
   Say: “I have brought an old or cancelled passport. My valid passport is [where it is]. What are my options for this departure?” Ask whether they can hold check-in, move you to a later departure, or update your document details once the valid passport arrives.

4. **If online check-in or API used the wrong passport details, ask staff to correct them.**  
   Give the accurate current passport details only when you have confirmed them. Correcting passport details does not usually remove the need to physically show the valid passport before boarding.

5. **If the valid passport cannot reach you in time, switch to damage-limiting.**  
   Ask the travel provider what rebooking options exist and what fees apply before you cancel anything yourself. Ask for written confirmation of what happened or keep a note of the time, desk, staff role and advice given; this may help later with insurance or a complaint.

6. **If you are abroad and cannot use a valid UK passport, use official consular routes.**  
   A UK Emergency Travel Document may be possible if you are a British national abroad, need to travel urgently, cannot use your UK passport, and cannot renew or replace it before you travel. Check whether an emergency travel document will be accepted for every country you will leave, enter or transit.

7. **Prepare only the details you need for the next conversation.**  
   Write down your booking reference, departure time, where the valid passport is, who can access it, and the fastest realistic time it can reach you. Use that single clear version when speaking to staff.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, argue about fees, or make an insurance claim.
- You do not need to start a full passport replacement process unless your current passport is genuinely lost, stolen, damaged or inaccessible.
- You do not need to solve the whole trip at once; first find out whether the valid passport can arrive, whether the booking can be corrected, or whether you need to rebook.

## Important reassurance
This is a stressful but practical document problem. Once you know where the valid passport is and have spoken to the staffed desk, the next step is usually clearer.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce the chance of denied boarding, missed travel or document loss. Later decisions about rebooking, insurance, complaints or replacement documents may need specialist help from the travel provider, insurer, passport office or consular staff.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, travel-agent or professional advice. Airlines, ferry companies, rail operators and border authorities can apply document rules strictly, and destination entry requirements can vary.
