# What to do if… your phone is missing and the location tracking shows an unexpected place (UK)

## Short answer
Do not go to the mapped location or confront anyone. Use another device to lock the phone, then contact your mobile network provider to bar the SIM and block the handset.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go to the location shown on the map to get the phone back, especially if it is a private address or unfamiliar place.
- Don’t arrange a meet-up with someone who claims they found your phone.
- Don’t click links in texts or emails claiming your phone has been found.
- Don’t enter your Apple, Google, bank, or email password through a link sent after the phone went missing.
- Don’t remove an iPhone from Apple Find My; this can remove Activation Lock and make it easier to unlock or resell.
- Don’t erase or remove an Android phone from Google Find Hub unless you accept that location access may stop after erasing.
- Don’t delay contacting your mobile network provider, because you may be liable for unauthorised charges until you report the phone missing.

## What to do now
1. Get to a safer, calmer place and use a different device, such as a friend’s phone, laptop, tablet, or work computer. If someone is in danger, a crime is happening now, or a suspect is nearby, call 999.

2. Use Apple Find My or Google Find Hub to secure the phone:
   - Mark it as lost, put it in Lost Mode, or use the lock option.
   - Add a short lock-screen message with a contact method you control now, such as a trusted person’s number.
   - Take a screenshot or note of the location, time shown, and whether the phone appears online or offline.
   - Consider remote erase only if protecting the data matters more than keeping location access. Erasing cannot easily be undone, and on Android the phone’s location may no longer be available after erase.

3. Contact your mobile network provider straight away and ask them to:
   - bar the SIM or eSIM so your number cannot be used for calls, texts, or data;
   - block the handset using the IMEI so it cannot use mobile networks;
   - tell you how to find the IMEI if they need it;
   - add any extra account security they offer, such as a PIN, passcode, or note against SIM changes.

4. Secure the accounts that could let someone reset everything else:
   1. email accounts;
   2. Apple Account or Google Account;
   3. banking, card, and payment apps;
   4. messaging apps and social media.

   Change passwords from a trusted device. Review signed-in devices or sessions, and sign out anything you do not recognise.

5. If the map shows the phone at an address or place you do not recognise, treat it as possible theft but do not go there yourself.
   - For non-emergency theft reporting, use 101 or your local police force’s online reporting route.
   - Keep the screenshot or note of the mapped location and time.
   - Ask for or keep the crime reference number if one is created, especially if you may need insurance.

6. Protect your money quickly:
   - Check bank, card, and payment accounts for transactions you do not recognise.
   - If anything looks wrong, contact the bank or card provider using the number on their official app, card, or website.
   - Ask them to secure the account and stop or hold suspicious payments where possible.

7. Watch for follow-on scams over the next few days.
   - Apple says it will not contact you to say your iPhone or iPad has been found.
   - Only sign in by typing the official website yourself or opening the official app.
   - If there is fraud, cybercrime, phishing, account takeover, or payment fraud linked to the missing phone, report it to Report Fraud in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland. In Scotland, report it to Police Scotland by 101 or an official Police Scotland reporting route.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to make an insurance claim.
- Buying a replacement phone.
- Reconstructing exactly where you last had the phone.
- Moving every account to a new sign-in method, once your email, phone number, Apple or Google account, and bank are secured.
- Deciding whether the location was definitely theft, a delay, or an inaccurate map reading.

## Important reassurance
A phone location can be approximate, delayed, or based on the last known signal. Taking the unexpected location seriously is still sensible: locking the phone, securing the SIM, and protecting key accounts reduces the risk whether the phone is lost, stolen, or mislocated.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and reduce account, phone-number, and financial risk. Later decisions about recovery, insurance, complaints, or digital security may need specialist help from your provider, bank, insurer, police, or a qualified adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, technical, or other professional advice. Follow the instructions from your mobile provider, bank, device maker, insurer, and the police for your specific situation.
