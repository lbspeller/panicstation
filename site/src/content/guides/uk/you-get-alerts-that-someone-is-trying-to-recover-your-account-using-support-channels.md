# What to do if… you get alerts that someone is trying to recover your account using support channels (UK)

## Short answer
Do not interact with the notification; instead, navigate directly to the official website or app of the service provider to change your security credentials and verify your recovery settings immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click any links, "Secure your account" buttons, or "No, it wasn't me" options within the alert message itself.
- Don’t provide any one-time passcodes (OTP), verification numbers, or MFA prompts to anyone, even if they claim to be from the provider's support team.
- Don’t call any telephone numbers or use any chat links provided in the suspicious notification.
- Don’t disable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) or Two-Step Verification (2SV) in an attempt to stop the prompts, as this removes your final layer of defense.
- Don’t ignore the alert; while the message itself may be a lure, it often indicates an active attempt to bypass your security.

## What to do now
1. **Access the service through a verified, independent route.** Use a known-good bookmark, the official mobile app, or type the website address directly into your browser rather than following any link provided in the alert.
2. **Change your account password to a strong, unique value immediately.** Create a password using three random words or a password manager, ensuring it is not reused on any other service.
   - If you have reused your previous password elsewhere, prioritize changing it on your primary email account and any banking services next.
3. **Audit and update your account recovery information.** Navigate to the security or privacy settings and remove any secondary email addresses, phone numbers, or "trusted" contacts that you do not recognize.
4. **Terminate all active sessions and unrecognised devices.** Look for a "Sign out of all devices" or "Review active sessions" option and execute it to force the attacker out of any existing connections.
5. **Generate fresh recovery codes or backup keys.** If the service provides one-time recovery codes, generate a new set; this usually invalidates any old codes an attacker might have obtained through support-channel social engineering.
6. **Enable or upgrade your Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).** If you are currently using SMS-based codes, switch to an authenticator app or a physical security key (FIDO2/Passkey) if the service supports it.
7. **Check your primary email account for malicious forwarding rules.** Attackers often set up rules to "auto-forward" or "mark as read" security alerts so you don't see their progress; check your email settings for any "Filters" or "Forwarding" you did not create.
8. **Contact the provider’s official support team to report the activity.** Use the "Help" or "Contact" section found on their official site to notify them that someone is attempting to impersonate you through support channels.
9. **Report the incident to the appropriate UK authorities.** If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report the attempted takeover to Report Fraud (reportfraud.police.uk).
   - If you are in Scotland, report the incident to Police Scotland by calling 101.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to identify the specific individual or location behind the attempt.
- You don’t need to replace your hardware or devices unless you have reason to believe the device itself is compromised.
- You don’t need to notify your entire contact list until you have confirmed your account is secure and you have checked for sent messages.
- You don’t need to decide on long-term legal action in the first few minutes.

## Important reassurance
Receiving these alerts means your security system is working. If you have MFA enabled and you do not provide codes to the attacker, they are often blocked from completing the takeover. By taking these steps, you are actively cutting off their access before they can lock you out.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a specific type of social engineering attack targeting account recovery. It does not cover the recovery of already lost accounts or the removal of malware from a compromised device.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. Digital threats evolve rapidly; always refer to the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) or your service provider's official documentation for the most current security protocols. If financial loss has occurred, contact your bank immediately using the number on the back of your card.
