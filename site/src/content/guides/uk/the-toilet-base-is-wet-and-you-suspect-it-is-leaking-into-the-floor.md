# What to do if… the toilet base is wet and you suspect it is leaking into the floor (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the water supply to the toilet using the local valve or main stopcock and stop using the fixture immediately to prevent structural water damage or waste contamination.

## Do not do these things
- Don't flush the toilet to "test" the source of the leak once water has appeared at the base.
- Don't apply silicone or caulk around the base to stop the water; this traps moisture inside the floor structure and hides the problem.
- Don't ignore the leak if it is small; water tracking under floorboards can cause rot or electrical failure in the ceiling below.
- Don't stand in water while touching any electrical switches, pull cords, or consumer units.
- Don't force a stiff or seized isolation valve or stopcock, as this can cause a pipe burst.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the water supply to the toilet cistern.** **Locate the isolation valve on the pipe leading into the toilet (often a small chrome valve with a screwdriver slot or a small handle) and turn it 90 degrees so the slot is perpendicular to the pipe.**
   - If the valve has a handle, turn it clockwise until it stops.
   - If no local valve is present, move to the next step.
2. **Turn off the main water supply if the local valve cannot be found or is seized.** **Locate your internal stopcock (usually under the kitchen sink, in a utility room, or under the stairs) and turn it clockwise slowly until the water flow stops.**
   - Open a cold tap elsewhere in the property to verify the water has been successfully shut off.
3. **Prevent further usage and contain the surface water.** **Place a clear "Do Not Use" note on the toilet lid and use towels to mop up standing water on the floor to prevent it from soaking into the subfloor.**
   - If the water is discoloured or has a foul odour, wear waterproof gloves and avoid skin contact; this is "blackwater" and contains pathogens.
4. **Assess for electrical risks in the vicinity.** **If water is dripping into light fittings or near sockets in the room below, or if you hear buzzing from the bathroom walls, go to your consumer unit and switch off the circuit labelled "Lights" or "Bathroom."**
   - Call an emergency electrician if you cannot safely isolate the power or if you see smoke or sparking.
5. **Check for secondary damage in the property.** **Inspect the ceiling directly below the bathroom and the base of the walls for damp patches, bulging plaster, or peeling paint.**
   - Clear any furniture or electronics from the area below the leak to prevent further property loss.
6. **Notify the relevant parties for urgent repair.** **Contact a qualified plumber or your landlord immediately, stating that there is a suspected waste or supply leak affecting the floor structure.**
   - Under UK 2026 standards, if this is the only toilet in the property, it is classified as an emergency repair that should be addressed within 24 hours.
7. **Document the situation for insurance or deposit purposes.** **Take clear photographs of the water at the base, any damage to the flooring, and any dampness on the ceiling below before the area is cleaned or repaired.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact technical cause (e.g., wax ring failure vs. pan connector leak) before calling for help.
- You do not need to remove floor tiles or the toilet pan yourself; this requires professional tools to avoid cracking the porcelain.
- You do not need to file a full insurance claim immediately, provided you have documented the initial damage and taken steps to stop the water.

## Important reassurance
Finding water at the base of a toilet is a common occurrence and usually indicates a failed seal or a slow weep from a connection. By isolating the water now and stopping usage, you have already taken the most critical steps to prevent significant structural damage or expensive restoration work.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. A professional plumber is required to diagnose whether the leak is from the clean water feed, the cistern-to-pan joint, or the waste soil pipe.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and does not constitute professional plumbing, electrical, or legal advice. If you cannot stop the leak, if water is in direct contact with electrical components, or if you feel unwell after contact with waste water (contact NHS 111), seek professional assistance immediately.
