# What to do if… your messaging app says your account is being registered on another device (UK)

## Short answer
Do not share any verification code or approve any sign-in prompt. Use only the app’s own sign-in flow, then check linked devices; if your mobile service has stopped or changed, contact your mobile provider through an official route.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t tell anyone an SMS, voice, or in-app verification code, even if they claim to be a friend, your bank, your mobile provider, or app support.
- Don’t tap “approve”, “allow”, or similar prompts unless you started the sign-in yourself.
- Don’t use help links or phone numbers sent by text, direct message, or chat. Use the app, the provider’s official website, or a number from your bill, card, or provider app.
- Don’t keep replying to suspicious messages from the same account; your contacts may receive impersonation messages or code requests.
- Don’t factory reset your phone as the first move. Secure the app, your mobile number, your email, and money-related accounts first.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether you caused the alert.** Did you just reinstall the app, change phone, change SIM or eSIM, or request a sign-in code? If not, treat it as a possible takeover attempt.

2. **Open the messaging app yourself and try to sign in.** Use only a verification code that you requested in the app just now. If someone is asking you to read out or forward a code, stop replying.

3. **Check linked devices or active sessions.** In the app settings, remove any device, browser, desktop session, or login you do not recognise.

4. **If mobile service has stopped, contact your mobile provider.** Use another phone if needed. Say: “I may have had a SIM swap or unauthorised number transfer. Please check my number, stop any unauthorised transfer, and help me restore control.” Ask about adding or resetting an account PIN, passcode, or any available number-transfer protection.

5. **Secure the email account linked to the app.** Change its password to a unique one, check recovery details, check forwarding rules, and sign out of devices you do not recognise.

6. **Protect money-related accounts that use your phone number.** Use the official banking app or the number on your card. Tell them your phone number or messaging account may be compromised and ask them to check for unauthorised activity.

7. **Turn on the messaging app’s extra protection when you are back in.** This may be called two-step verification, a PIN, a password, or registration lock, depending on the app. Store the PIN or password somewhere safe.

8. **Warn a few key contacts using a different route.** Send a brief message by phone call, email, or another trusted channel: “My messaging account may be under attack. Ignore requests for money or codes from me until I confirm.”

9. **Report scam texts or fraud if relevant.** Suspicious texts can usually be forwarded to 7726. If you suspect cyber crime or fraud in England, Wales or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud. If you are in Scotland, or the incident happened there, contact Police Scotland on 101. Use 999 only for an emergency.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to change your phone number.
- You do not need to audit every account at once; start with the app, mobile provider, email, and money-related accounts.
- You do not need to confront the person you suspect or reply to scam messages.
- You do not need to explain the whole situation to every contact immediately; warn the people most likely to be targeted first.

## Important reassurance
A registration alert does not always mean the attacker has full control, and not sharing the code is a strong first defence. The most useful first steps are simple: keep the code private, remove unknown sessions, protect your number, and secure the accounts that could reset everything else.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, aimed at stabilising the situation and reducing immediate harm. Later decisions, such as identity protection, complaints, recovery steps, or technical support, may need help from the app provider, your mobile provider, your bank, police reporting services, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, technical, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you have lost money, cannot regain mobile service, or see signs that your bank, email, or identity documents were accessed, contact the relevant provider directly using official channels.
