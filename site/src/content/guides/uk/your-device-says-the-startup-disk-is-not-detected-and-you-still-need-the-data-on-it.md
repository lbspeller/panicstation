# What to do if… your device says the startup disk is not detected and you still need the data on it (UK)

## Short answer
Stop trying to fix the boot problem for now. Power off, check whether the files already exist in a backup or cloud account, then use the safest copy-first route before running repairs, resets, reinstalls, or erase options.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t reinstall the operating system, reset the device, erase the disk, or run recovery options that may remove files while the data still matters.
- Don’t click **Initialize**, **Format**, **Erase**, **Partition**, or anything that says it will set up the disk.
- Don’t run repeated repair attempts just to see whether the disk comes back.
- Don’t keep the device powered on if you hear clicking, grinding, or beeping, smell overheating, or see the drive repeatedly disconnect and reconnect.
- Don’t copy recovered files back onto the same disk you are trying to save.
- Don’t open sealed devices or force connectors if you are not confident.

## What to do now
1. **Pause, record the message, and power off.**  
   Photograph the exact screen. Note what happened just before it: drop, spill, update, power cut, low storage warning, unusual noise, or sudden shutdown. Then shut the device down fully.

2. **Check whether the data is already somewhere safer.**  
   From another phone, tablet, or computer, check cloud storage and backups you already use: iCloud Drive, iCloud Photos, OneDrive, Google Drive, Time Machine, File History, an external backup drive, a NAS, or work or school storage. If the files are there, leave the failed device alone until you have copied the important files somewhere safe.

3. **Do one careful detection check without writing to the disk.**  
   Disconnect non-essential USB devices, hubs, and docks. Start the device once and check whether the drive is visible:
   - **Mac:** start macOS Recovery, open **Disk Utility**, and use **View > Show All Devices** so the physical drive can appear if it is detectable.
   - **Windows PC:** enter the BIOS or UEFI screen for your exact model and check whether the internal drive is listed.
   If you do not know the correct key or steps, look up the official instructions for your exact model rather than guessing repeatedly.

4. **If the drive appears, copy first.**  
   Treat the disk as fragile until the important data is copied. Copy the most important folders first to a different drive or cloud location. Prioritise family photos and videos, work documents, finance and tax files, current projects, and password manager recovery information only if it is already safely accessible.

5. **If encryption may be involved, find the recovery key before doing more.**  
   If the device used **FileVault** on a Mac or **BitLocker** on Windows, you may need the recovery key before files can be accessed outside the normal startup route. Check saved printouts, USB drives, your Apple Account or Microsoft account, and any work or school account or IT administrator that managed the device.

6. **Use a copy-only access route where possible.**  
   On compatible Macs, Target Disk Mode or macOS Recovery may let you access files from another Mac or external drive. On a Windows PC, a repair technician may be able to connect the drive to another computer using a suitable adapter or enclosure. If any screen asks to repair, initialise, format, erase, partition, reset, or reinstall, cancel and reassess.

7. **Stop DIY attempts if the drive may be physically failing.**  
   Stop and consider a reputable data recovery service if the drive is not detected in BIOS, UEFI, or Disk Utility; if it clicks, beeps, overheats, or smells hot; or if it repeatedly appears and disappears. Fewer power-on attempts may help preserve what can still be recovered.

8. **Protect warranty and service options.**  
   Keep photos and notes of the fault. If the device is under warranty or within a return window, avoid disassembly unless you have decided that data recovery matters more than straightforward hardware service.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to buy a new computer.
- You do not need to diagnose the root cause before protecting the files.
- You do not need to choose recovery software while the disk may be failing.
- You do not need to make the computer boot normally before copying the data.

## Important reassurance
A startup disk error means the computer cannot start from that disk at this moment. It does not always mean the files are gone, and the safest next move is to avoid actions that overwrite data or stress a failing drive.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce avoidable data loss and get you to a safer recovery route. Later decisions about repair, replacement, recovery software, warranty service, or specialist recovery may need professional help.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional IT, legal, financial, forensic, or data recovery advice. If the data is high-stakes, or if there are signs of physical failure, minimise further power-on attempts and use professional help.
