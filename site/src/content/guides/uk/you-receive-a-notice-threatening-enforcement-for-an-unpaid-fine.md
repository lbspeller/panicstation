# What to do if… you receive a notice threatening enforcement for an unpaid fine (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and verify the notice through an official route you look up yourself before you pay, click anything, call any number in the notice, or share personal information.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, scan QR codes, or download attachments from the notice to pay now.
- Don’t call phone numbers, use WhatsApp, or use payment links printed in the notice until you have independently verified who sent it.
- Don’t pay by gift cards, crypto, bank transfer to a personal account, or unusual voucher schemes.
- Don’t share one-time passcodes, card PINs, bank logins, or ID photos just to confirm identity.
- Don’t open the door to someone because you received a text, email, or letter; keep the door closed while you verify.
- Don’t ignore it completely; treat it as unverified until you have checked.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the fast-payment loop.** Do not reply, click, scan, download, or pay. Take screenshots or photos of the whole notice, including sender details, reference numbers, payment instructions, links, and dates.
2. **Identify what the notice claims to be about.** Look for whether it says court fine, council penalty, parking, bus lane, congestion charge, toll charge, or another type of unpaid fine. The right place to check depends on the type of fine.
3. **Treat same-day or within-hours pressure as suspicious.** Real enforcement processes should have paperwork and a notice period you can check. Do not rely on a deadline in a text, email, QR code, or payment page; verify the notice period through official guidance or an advice service before paying.
4. **Verify a court fine through official court contact details.** If it claims to be from HM Courts and Tribunals Service, use GOV.UK to find the relevant court or tribunal contact details yourself, then ask whether the reference number, amount, and enforcement status are genuine.
5. **Verify a council or traffic penalty through the issuing body.** Type the council, transport authority, or toll provider’s website address yourself, or find it through GOV.UK or the official body’s known website. Check the reference number and amount there, not through the notice link.
6. **If it names an enforcement agent or bailiff in England and Wales, check identity before engaging.** Use the official GOV.UK bailiff-rights page to see how to check the type of bailiff and their identity. A certificated enforcement agent is not the same as every other type of bailiff, so check the role named on the paperwork.
7. **If you are in Scotland, treat “bailiff” or “enforcement agent” wording as a warning sign.** Bailiffs from elsewhere in the UK do not have enforcement powers in Scotland. Scottish debt enforcement is handled through Scottish legal routes, often by sheriff officers or messengers-at-arms. Verify with the issuing body and, if a sheriff officer firm is named, check the firm through the SMASO directory or by using contact details you find independently.
8. **If you are in Northern Ireland, check the Northern Ireland route before paying.** Civil judgments may involve the Enforcement of Judgments Office, and court matters should be checked through Northern Ireland Courts and Tribunals Service or another official NI government route. Do not assume an England and Wales bailiff process applies.
9. **If someone comes to your home, keep the door closed while you check.** Ask them to show ID and paperwork through a window or letterbox. Verify by contacting the issuing authority, court, or enforcement company using contact details you found independently, not details the visitor gives you under pressure.
10. **If you think it is a scam, report it and protect your accounts.** If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report fraud through Report Fraud. If you are in Scotland, report fraud to Police Scotland on 101. If you already paid or shared bank details, contact your bank or card provider immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to dispute the fine; first check whether the notice is real and who issued it.
- You do not need to give detailed financial information to a caller who says they can set up a payment plan.
- You do not need to let anyone into your home because a notice says immediate enforcement.
- You do not need to solve every related debt or penalty today; the first task is to verify the notice safely.

## Important reassurance
Scam notices and real enforcement notices can both feel frightening and urgent. Pausing to check through official contact details is a reasonable first step, and it helps prevent rushed payments or sharing information with the wrong person.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to help you verify authenticity, reduce pressure, and avoid irreversible mistakes. What to do next depends on the type of fine, who issued it, and whether you are in England and Wales, Scotland, or Northern Ireland.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Enforcement rules and routes differ across the UK, so use official contact details and get independent advice before paying, agreeing to a plan, or disputing the fine.
