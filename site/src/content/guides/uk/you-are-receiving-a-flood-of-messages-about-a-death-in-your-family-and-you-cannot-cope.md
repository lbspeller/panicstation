# What to do if… you are receiving a flood of messages about a death in your family and you cannot cope (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate yourself from the digital stream immediately by activating Do Not Disturb mode and appointing a single point of contact to manage communications on your behalf.

## Do not do these things
- Don't feel obligated to respond to any message immediately to maintain etiquette or politeness.
- Don't check social media or post public announcements while in the initial state of shock.
- Don't commit to any practical arrangements, financial decisions, or travel plans via text.
- Don't attempt to verify conflicting rumours by engaging with multiple different message threads.
- Don't use alcohol or unprescribed medication to suppress the immediate emotional or physical reaction.

## What to do now
1. **Activate a total digital pause.** Put your phone on **Do Not Disturb** or **Airplane Mode** immediately to stop the sound and vibration of incoming alerts.
   - Configure "Allowed Notifications" for only one or two people if you are waiting for official news.
   - Place the device in a different room if the physical presence of it causes distress.
2. **Appoint a communications lead.** Call or text one trusted person who is not as centrally affected and ask them to **filter all incoming news.**
   - Use a clear request: "I am overwhelmed by messages. Please can you tell people I am not replying right now and hold any essential updates for me."
3. **Issue a single holding statement.** Copy and paste a **standardized response** to your main family group or social circle, then close the app.
   - Draft: "I have seen the news and messages. I am unable to take calls or reply individually at the moment. Please contact [NAME] for urgent matters. I will be in touch when I can."
4. **Verify the situation through one official channel.** Contact the **specific institution** where the death occurred (e.g., the hospital ward, hospice, or care home) or the police if they were involved in the notification.
   - Ignore all second-hand information until you have spoken directly to a professional or the legal next-of-kin.
5. **Silence specific notification triggers.** Mute high-volume group chats **specifically** rather than just relying on global phone settings.
   - In WhatsApp or iMessage, select "Mute" for 1 year or "Always" for the specific family or friend groups involved.
   - Turn off "Show Previews" in your notification settings so you do not see the content of messages on your lock screen.
6. **Access immediate crisis support if you cannot stabilize.**  
   - Call **116 123** for Samaritans (available 24/7 for confidential emotional support).
   - Call **NHS 111** and select the **mental health option** (usually Press 2) for urgent professional assessment.
   - Contact **Cruse Bereavement Support** at 0808 808 1677 during their operating hours (Monday/Wednesday/Thursday/Friday 9:30–17:00, Tuesday 13:00–20:00).
   - Dial **999** or attend the nearest A&E if you feel you are in immediate physical danger or cannot keep yourself safe.

## What can wait
- You do not need to acknowledge condolences or "thank" people for their messages today.
- You do not need to look at photos or videos sent by others.
- You do not need to provide details about the cause of death or the circumstances to anyone.
- You do not need to start the "Tell Us Once" service or other legal notifications in the first few hours.

## Important reassurance
The feeling of being "flooded" or "hunted" by messages is a common physiological response to sudden stress. Your brain is trying to process a significant event, and the constant pings of a phone interfere with that process. Taking yourself offline is a necessary act of stabilization, not a sign of weakness or rudeness.

## Scope note
This guide is for the first 60 minutes of digital overwhelm following a death. It does not cover long-term bereavement support or the legal administration of an estate.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute medical, legal, or professional mental health advice. Always follow the instructions of emergency services or healthcare professionals in a crisis.
