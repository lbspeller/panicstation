# What to do if… you receive a notice ordering you to appear in court on short notice (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the hearing through the official government court-finder service for your jurisdiction and contact a solicitor or the court's listings office immediately to confirm your attendance or request an urgent adjournment.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore the notice even if you believe it is a scam or sent in error.
- Don’t call the phone numbers printed on the notice itself until you have verified them against official government websites.
- Don’t contact the opposing party or their legal representatives directly to argue about the short notice.
- Don’t post images of the summons or hearing notice on social media, as this may breach privacy rules or "contempt of court" restrictions.
- Don’t assume that work commitments, pre-booked travel, or childcare automatically excuse you from attending a mandatory hearing.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the core details on the document immediately.**
   Locate and write down the following:
   - The specific court name (e.g., Manchester Magistrates' Court, Glasgow Sheriff Court).
   - The case or reference number (usually top right).
   - The date and time of the hearing.
   - The hearing type (e.g., "First Hearing", "Trial", "Possession Hearing").

2. **Verify the notice through the official jurisdictional service.**
   Do not use the contact details on the letter yet; find the court's public contact information:
   - **England & Wales:** Use the "Find a court or tribunal" service on GOV.UK.
   - **Scotland:** Use the "Find a court" tool on the Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service (SCTS) website.
   - **Northern Ireland:** Contact the Northern Ireland Courts and Tribunals Service (NICTS) via the nidirect portal.
   Call the "Listings" or "General Enquiries" number and ask: "Can you confirm the hearing for case number [X] on [Date] at [Time]?"

3. **Determine if you are a "Party" or a "Witness".**
   Check if the notice calls you a **Defendant**, **Respondent**, or **Claimant** (Party), or if it is a **Witness Summons** (England/Wales/NI) or **Witness Citation** (Scotland).
   - If you are a Party, your attendance is usually mandatory; failure to attend may result in an arrest warrant or a decision made in your absence.
   - If you are a Witness and have received a formal summons/citation, you are legally required to attend.

4. **Seek urgent legal advice or representation.**
   - **Criminal cases:** Contact a criminal defence solicitor immediately. If the hearing is tomorrow and you have no lawyer, tell the Court Usher upon arrival that you need to speak to the **Duty Solicitor**.
   - **Civil/Housing cases:** If the notice concerns eviction, look for the **Court Duty Advice Scheme** when you arrive at court; they often provide free help on the day.
   - **Family cases:** Contact a family law solicitor or "Support Through Court" for procedural guidance.

5. **Submit an urgent request if you physically cannot attend.**
   If you are hospitalized or have another life-critical emergency, call the court's listings office immediately.
   - Follow up the call with an email including your case number in the subject line.
   - Attach evidence (e.g., a medical "fit note" or accident report).
   - Ask: "How do I apply for an urgent adjournment or permission to attend remotely?"
   - **Crucial:** You must assume you are required to attend until the court explicitly confirms the hearing has been vacated or adjourned.

6. **Prepare for arrival if you can attend.**
   - Locate your photo ID (passport or driving licence) and the original notice.
   - Plan your route to arrive at least 45 minutes before the stated time to clear security.
   - Prepare a brief list of facts or dates relevant to the case to help your memory.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to have your entire legal argument or every witness statement ready today.
- You do **not** need to worry about the long-term outcome of the case in this moment.
- You do **not** need to respond to the "other side's" solicitors if you are about to get your own legal representation.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a court notice on short notice is a high-stress event, but courts deal with administrative errors and short listings daily. The most effective way to protect your position is to acknowledge the notice and communicate through official channels immediately. Taking these steps demonstrates that you are taking the court's authority seriously, which is looked upon favorably by judges.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate stabilization steps for receiving a sudden court notice in the UK. It does not provide legal strategy, trial preparation, or advice on the merits of any specific legal case.

## Important note
Procedures and terminology differ between England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. This information is for general guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. If you are at risk of arrest or losing your home, seek advice from a qualified solicitor or Citizens Advice immediately.
