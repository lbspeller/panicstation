# What to do if… a regulator asks you to provide passwords or passcodes during an inquiry (UK)

## Short answer
Do not provide passwords, PINs, or biometric access on the spot without a formal written notice citing a specific legal power. Request the demand in writing and contact a solicitor immediately to verify if you are legally compelled to comply.

## Do not do these things
- Don't disclose passwords, PINs, or two-factor authentication (2FA) codes verbally during an initial verbal request.
- Don't approve "push" notifications on authenticator apps or hand over a device that is currently logged in.
- Don't delete data, clear browser histories, or wipe devices once an inquiry has commenced, as this may constitute obstruction or perverting the course of justice.
- Don't assume that a general "duty to cooperate" automatically overrides your right to legal advice regarding encryption keys.
- Don't attempt to guess or "test" the official's knowledge of the law by engaging in a technical debate.

## What to do now
1. **Request the legal basis for the demand in writing.** **Ask the official to specify exactly which statutory power they are using to compel the disclosure of a password or encryption key.** 
   - Note if they mention Section 49 of the Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act (RIPA) 2000 or the Border Security, Asylum and Immigration Act 2025.
2. **Verify and record the identity of all officials present.** **Ask for the name, badge or ID number, and the department of every person requesting access to your devices.**
3. **Bring in legal representation before taking any further action.** **If you are at a place of business, notify your legal or compliance lead; if you are an individual, call a solicitor and ask the official to wait until your representative is present or on the line.**
4. **Distinguish between a request for documents and a request for credentials.** **Clarify if the regulator is asking you to provide specific data (which you can often do without sharing passwords) or if they are demanding direct access to the account or device itself.**
5. **Check for a formal Section 49 Notice.** **If a RIPA Section 49 notice is served, verify that it is signed by the appropriate authority (such as a judge or a senior officer) and that it specifies the device or data it covers.**
   - In 2026, these notices must specify a reasonable deadline for compliance; you are usually not required to provide the key "this second" unless it is a border or terrorism-related emergency.
6. **Follow "safe-pause" protocols at UK ports or borders.** **If stopped under Schedule 7 (Terrorism Act 2000) or the BSAI Act 2025, state clearly: "I wish to consult with a solicitor before providing credentials," even if the officer suggests this will cause a delay.**
   - Note that border powers are broader and failure to comply can lead to immediate detention or criminal charges, but you still have a right to record the request and ask for legal counsel.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to provide a "voluntary" password to be helpful; you can wait for a formal compulsion.
- You do not need to explain why certain files are encrypted or why you use specific security software.
- You do not need to negotiate the "scope" of a digital search yourself; your solicitor can handle the limits of the inquiry.
- You do not need to remember a forgotten password under immediate pressure; "reasonable excuse" (including genuine loss of memory) is a valid legal consideration that can be addressed later.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard legal procedure to request that all demands for sensitive credentials be put in writing. Exercising your right to legal advice is not an admission of guilt, and regulators are accustomed to professional pauses while legal counsel is sought. The law provides specific protections against self-incrimination that your solicitor will help you navigate.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate first steps when a UK regulator or law enforcement officer requests digital access credentials. It does not cover long-term litigation strategy or the technical process of data extraction.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. The Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act (RIPA) 2000 and the Border Security, Asylum and Immigration Act 2025 carry criminal penalties for non-compliance with valid notices. Always consult a qualified solicitor when served with a formal notice to disclose passwords.
