# What to do if… you learn a loved one is missing and you fear the worst but have no confirmation (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the police immediately to report them missing; call 999 if there is an immediate threat to life or 101 for other reports. There is no requirement to wait 24 hours before reporting someone missing.

## Do not do these things
- Don't wait 24 hours to see if they return if the absence is out of character or the person is vulnerable.
- Don't assume a report is "wasting police time" if you have a genuine concern for someone's safety.
- Don't post unconfirmed news of a death or serious injury on social media before the police or family have made an official statement.
- Don't share sensitive personal details like the missing person's home address or medical history in public search appeals.
- Don't enter or search potentially dangerous locations alone or attempt to confront individuals you suspect may be involved.
- Don't delete any digital evidence such as recent texts, call logs, or location history from your own devices.
- Don't pay money to individuals or services online claiming they can find the person; be alert to scams targeting families in crisis.

## What to do now
1. **Report the person as missing to the police immediately.**
   - **Call 999** if the person is a child, a vulnerable adult, or if you believe they are in immediate danger.
   - **Call 101** or use the online reporting tool on your local police force's website for non-emergency missing person reports.
   - **Request a reference number** (incident number) and record it for all future communications.
2. **Gather essential identifiers for the police report.**
   - Full name, date of birth, and current address.
   - A recent, clear photograph.
   - Physical description including height, build, and any distinguishing marks like tattoos or scars.
   - Description of clothing last seen wearing and any items they may have with them.
   - Details of any vehicles used (make, model, color, and registration).
   - Known medical conditions, required medications, and any specific vulnerabilities (e.g., dementia, mental health crisis).
3. **Conduct a focused 15-minute search of the last known location.**
   - **Check their residence** for missing items like keys, passport, or a note, ensuring you do not disturb potential evidence or DNA sources like toothbrushes.
   - **Look in unusual spaces** if the missing person is a child or has cognitive impairments (e.g., cupboards, lofts, or garden sheds).
4. **Identify and contact the most likely contacts for a quick check.**
   - **Call the person's place of work, school, or planned destination** for today to confirm if they arrived or when they were last seen.
   - **Check with immediate family or friends** who may have had contact without your knowledge.
5. **Establish a central log for all information and sightings.**
   - **Record a timeline** of events starting from the last confirmed contact.
   - **Note down all phone calls**, names of officers spoken to, and any information received from the public.
6. **Contact the charity Missing People for specialist support.**
   - **Call or text 116 000** at any time for confidential advice, emotional support, and assistance with publicity.
   - **Ask about SafeCall** if the missing person is a young person who may be at risk of exploitation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a public or media appeal immediately; the police will advise on when this is appropriate.
- You do not need to notify every extended family member or acquaintance in the first few hours.
- You do not need to access the missing person's bank accounts, cancel contracts, or make decisions about their property right now.

## Important reassurance
It is a natural response to feel overwhelmed or to imagine the worst possible outcome when someone is unexpectedly absent. Focusing on the practical steps of reporting, gathering details, and maintaining a log can help stabilize the situation while professionals begin their work.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hours after discovering a person is missing. Long-term search strategies and legal processes require ongoing police coordination.

## Important note
This information is for guidance purposes only and does not constitute legal or professional advice. Always follow the instructions of the police and emergency services during an active missing person investigation.
