# What to do if… your vehicle starts but the accelerator pedal is unresponsive while you are in a safe place (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pull into traffic. Keep the vehicle stationary and secure, check for an obvious pedal obstruction or gear-selection issue, and arrange breakdown recovery or a mechanic if the problem persists.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “test it” by driving off anyway, even if it seems to move a little.
- Do not repeatedly rev the engine hard to “wake it up.”
- Do not keep restarting if you smell burning, see smoke, or get an urgent red warning or “stop” message.
- Do not reach into the pedal area while the car is in gear, moving, or able to move.
- Do not ignore warning lights or messages that appear with the problem.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the car before checking anything**
   - Keep the car in **Park** if it is automatic, or **Neutral** if it is manual.
   - Apply the **parking brake**.
   - Keep your foot on the brake if the car might roll or lurch.
   - If other road users may not expect you to be stopped, use **hazard warning lights**.

2. **Check the pedal area without putting yourself at risk**
   - With the car secured, look in the footwell for a **floor mat, bottle, bag, cable, or loose item** touching or blocking the accelerator.
   - If a mat is bunched up, remove it or refit it properly before any further check.
   - Do not put your hand near the pedals while the vehicle is in gear or able to move.

3. **Look at the gear selection**
   - **Automatic:** with your foot firmly on the brake, check the dashboard or selector shows the gear you intended, not **Neutral**.
   - **Manual:** check you are selecting **1st gear** or **reverse**, and that the clutch is being released normally.
   - If the engine revs but the car does not move, treat it as a possible **clutch, drivetrain, or transmission** problem and do not try to force the vehicle into motion.

4. **Stop testing if there are warning signs**
   - If you see a **red warning light**, a **stop** message, smoke, a strong burning smell, grinding, or unusual vibration, switch off if it is safe to do so.
   - Step away from the vehicle if there is smoke, heat, or a smell that makes you think there could be fire risk.
   - If the vehicle shows **reduced power** or a similar message, treat it as not safe for normal driving until checked.

5. **Do one very small response check only if it is safe**
   - If there is clear space, no obstruction, and no serious warning sign, restart once and check whether the accelerator responds normally.
   - Use only gentle pressure.
   - If the pedal is still unresponsive, stop. Do not keep trying to diagnose it from the driver’s seat.

6. **Arrange help rather than driving**
   - Call your breakdown provider, recovery service, garage, or a mobile mechanic.
   - Tell them the engine starts but the accelerator is unresponsive, and mention any warning lights, smells, smoke, or gear-selection symptoms.
   - If your position becomes immediately dangerous, such as a hazardous obstruction or risk of collision, call **999** and ask for the police.

7. **If this seems like a serious safety defect**
   - If the problem is repeatable, dangerous, or happens again after repair, keep brief notes of when it happens.
   - Check for recalls and consider reporting it as a serious safety defect through GOV.UK.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it is a major repair.
- You do not need to identify the exact component, such as the pedal sensor, throttle body, wiring, clutch, or transmission.
- You do not need to prove the fault by attempting a short drive.
- You do not need to decide whether to keep or sell the vehicle today.

## Important reassurance
This feels alarming because the car starts but does not respond as expected. Finding the problem before entering traffic is the safest moment to stop, secure the vehicle, and get help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repair, recovery, inspection, recalls, complaints, or insurance may need help from a qualified mechanic, recovery service, garage, insurer, or other specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information for the first moments of a vehicle problem. It is not professional mechanical, legal, insurance, or safety advice.
