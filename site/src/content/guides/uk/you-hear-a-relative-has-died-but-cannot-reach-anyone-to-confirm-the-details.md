# What to do if… you hear a relative has died but cannot reach anyone to confirm the details (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before you travel, pay money, or share the news publicly. Verify the information through an independently found official route in the area where your relative lived or may have died.

## Do not do these things
- Do not announce the death on social media or in group chats until you have reliable confirmation.
- Do not send money, bank details, passwords, or copies of ID to anyone who contacts you unexpectedly about arrangements.
- Do not use phone numbers or links from an unexpected message; look up official contact details yourself.
- Do not treat silence as confirmation; phones may be off, people may be travelling, and families may be overwhelmed.
- Do not keep calling dozens of people at once; it can create confusion and make details harder to track.
- Do not rush into travel until you have checked where the death may have happened and who is handling the immediate arrangements.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the facts you have.**  
   Note who told you, how they contacted you, the exact wording, the relative’s full name, their last known address, and any place mentioned, such as a hospital, care home, police force, or funeral director.

2. **Check whether the message might be fraudulent.**  
   If the news came from social media, an unknown number, or someone asking for money, do not click links or call numbers they sent. Use official websites or a phone number you already trust.

3. **If someone may still be in immediate danger, call 999.**  
   Use 999 if there is an immediate risk to life or serious harm, for example if you think your relative may still be alive but unreachable and in danger.

4. **If you cannot confirm whether your relative is alive, contact police on a non-emergency route.**  
   Call 101, or use the official website for the police force where your relative is believed to be. Say that you have received a report of a death but cannot confirm it, and ask whether a concern for welfare is appropriate. Give their full name, address if known, age or date of birth if known, and why you are worried.

5. **If a hospital or care setting was mentioned, call its main switchboard.**  
   Ask for the bereavement office, ward, site team, or patient liaison route. In England, this may include PALS. Say: “I am trying to verify urgent information about a close relative. I cannot reach the family. Can you tell me the correct process for confirming whether they are a patient, or whether a death has been reported?” They may not be able to give details, but they can usually explain the right route.

6. **If the death may have been sudden, unexplained, or under investigation, contact the local death investigation route.**  
   In England and Wales this may be the coroner’s office for the area where the death happened. Use the local council or official GOV.UK route to find contact details. Ask what they can confirm, what information they need from you, and whether there is another official contact point.

7. **Send one calm message to one or two key family contacts.**  
   Use a short message such as: “I have heard that [Name] may have died, but I do not have confirmation. I am checking through official routes. Please reply with who is handling arrangements and the confirmed location, if you know.” Avoid sending repeated messages to many people.

8. **Keep a simple call log.**  
   For each call, write the time, number used, organisation, person or department spoken to, and what they said they can or cannot confirm. This helps you avoid repeating yourself while you are shocked.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide about funerals, inheritance, belongings, travel, or family disputes now.
- You do not need to contact banks, employers, landlords, insurers, or government departments until the death has been confirmed and basic details are known.
- You do not need to order certificates immediately. In England and Wales, certificates are usually ordered after registration through the General Register Office or the local register office; Scotland and Northern Ireland have different certificate routes.
- You do not need to use Tell Us Once now. It is for later, after the death is registered or after you have the required coroner certificate or reference.

## Important reassurance
Being stuck without details is frightening, but it does not mean you have to solve everything at once. The safe job right now is only to confirm what has happened, avoid scams or public announcements, and identify the official person or office handling the next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about registration, certificates, funerals, travel, estates, benefits, or family disagreement may need help from the relevant registrar, coroner or death investigation office, funeral director, solicitor, embassy, or bereavement support service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Processes can vary across England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, so use official contact details for the place where the death may have happened.
