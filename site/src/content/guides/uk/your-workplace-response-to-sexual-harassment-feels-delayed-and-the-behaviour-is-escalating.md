# What to do if… your workplace response to sexual harassment feels delayed and the behaviour is escalating (UK)

## Short answer
Prioritise your physical safety by moving to a secure location and submit a written report of the escalation immediately, requesting "interim protective measures" to stop all contact.

## Do not do these things
- Do not remain in a physical space where you feel an immediate threat of harm.
- Do not agree to informal, unrecorded, or one-to-one meetings with the person involved.
- Do not delete any evidence, including "disappearing" messages, screenshots, or call logs.
- Do not assume you must exhaust an informal process before filing a formal grievance.
- Do not feel obligated to stay on the premises if you believe your safety is at risk.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer location immediately.** If you are in active danger or a crime is occurring, leave the area and call **999**. If you cannot leave the building, find a lockable room or move to a crowded public area.
2. **Submit a written notification of the escalation today.** Email HR or a senior manager (outside the chain of the person involved) stating that the harassment is worsening.
   - Use the subject line: "Urgent: Escalating Sexual Harassment - Safety Measures Required".
   - State clearly that you are making a "protected disclosure" (as of April 2026, this carries specific whistleblowing protections).
3. **Formalise your request for interim safety measures.** Request that the employer takes immediate steps to protect you while the investigation is pending. Specific requests can include:
   - a "no contact" instruction issued to the other person
   - temporary change of shift, department, or work location
   - permission to work from home
   - a change in your reporting line if the person is your manager.
4. **Draft a concise record of the specific escalations.** Note exactly what has changed (e.g., more frequent comments, closer physical proximity, new threats). Keep this log on a personal device or in a physical notebook that you take home with you.
5. **Contact a trade union representative or Acas.** If you are a union member, notify your rep immediately. They can provide advice on the employer's legal duty to take "all reasonable steps" to prevent harassment (2026 standard).
6. **File a formal grievance if the response remains stalled.** If the employer does not implement safety measures within 24–48 hours of your report, follow the formal grievance procedure outlined in your staff handbook.
7. **Access confidential specialist support.** You do not need to involve the police to get help.
   - **Rape Crisis England & Wales:** Call the 24/7 support line on **0808 500 2222**.
   - **Rape Crisis Scotland:** Call **08088 01 03 02** (6pm–midnight).
   - **SurvivorsUK:** Specialist support for men and non-binary people.
8. **Request a companion for formal meetings.** You have a statutory right to be accompanied by a colleague or a union representative at any formal grievance hearing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you want to resign or pursue a Tribunal claim.
- You do not need to provide a full, exhaustive witness statement in your first urgent report.
- You do not need to justify why you feel unsafe; the fact that behaviour is escalating is sufficient for a report.
- You do not need to respond to any "informal" reaching out from the person harassing you.

## Important reassurance
It is common for the body to react with high alertness, numbness, or difficulty concentrating when a workplace situation feels out of control. A delay in an employer's response often increases this sense of alarm. Taking the step of putting your safety needs in writing is a proactive way to create a paper trail and signal the urgency of the situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an escalating workplace crisis. It does not replace legal advice regarding the Equality Act 2010 or specific Employment Tribunal deadlines (typically 3 months less one day, though some extensions apply for harassment claims as of late 2026).

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or clinical advice. If you are in immediate danger, contact emergency services on 999. Employers have a legal duty to take all reasonable steps to protect staff; if they fail to do so, they may be liable for increased compensation in legal proceedings.
