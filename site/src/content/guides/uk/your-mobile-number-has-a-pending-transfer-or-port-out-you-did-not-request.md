# What to do if… your mobile number has a pending transfer or port-out you did not request (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your mobile network provider now using an official route, tell them the number transfer or PAC request is unauthorised, and ask them to stop it and secure your account.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links or call numbers in the transfer, PAC, or SIM message unless you have independently checked they are your provider’s official details.
- Don’t share one-time passcodes, PAC codes, account PINs, passwords, or security answers with anyone who contacts you first.
- Don’t wait to see what happens if the transfer is pending.
- Don’t rely on SMS login codes for important accounts right now if another sign-in method is available.
- Don’t delete the message or reset your phone before taking a screenshot or making a brief note of what happened.

## What to do now
1. **Contact your mobile network provider using an official route.**  
   Use your provider’s app, website, a recent bill, or an in-store route. Say: **“My number has a pending transfer or PAC request that I did not make. Please stop it and secure my account.”**

2. **Ask for specific protections while you are speaking to them.**  
   Ask the provider to:
   - cancel or stop the pending number transfer if it can be stopped;
   - invalidate any PAC or transfer request that you did not make, if possible;
   - add the strongest available account security note, transfer block, or porting restriction;
   - reset your account PIN or passphrase;
   - remove any email address, postal address, contact number, or authorised contact you do not recognise;
   - confirm whether your SIM, eSIM, PAC, or account login was changed.

3. **If your phone has lost service, use another route to keep going.**  
   Use Wi-Fi calling if it still works, a trusted person’s phone, a landline, web chat through the provider’s official site, or a provider shop. If you go in person, take photo ID and ask for urgent account recovery.

4. **Secure your main email account before other online accounts.**  
   From a device you still control, change your email password, sign out of other sessions, and add a non-SMS second factor such as an authenticator app, passkey, or security key. Do not remove existing recovery options until the new sign-in method works.

5. **Contact your bank and any account that uses SMS codes.**  
   Tell them: **“My mobile number may be being ported or SIM-swapped, so SMS codes may be intercepted.”** Ask them to add extra checks and review recent transfers, new payees, password resets, and device changes.

6. **Make a brief incident record.**  
   Screenshot the transfer, PAC, or SIM message. Note the date, time, provider, whether your phone showed “No Service”, and any calls, emails, app alerts, or banking alerts that looked unusual.

7. **Report fraud or suspected fraud through the right UK route.**  
   If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report it to Report Fraud. If you are in Scotland, report it to Police Scotland online, by phone, or in person; use 101 where appropriate.

8. **Report suspicious texts or emails connected to this.**  
   Most UK mobile customers can forward suspicious texts to **7726**. If 7726 does not work, ask your mobile provider how to report the text. Forward suspicious emails to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out who did this right now.
- You do not need to understand exactly how they got your details before calling your provider.
- You do not need to replace your phone today unless your provider, bank, or a trusted technical support route identifies a device problem.
- You do not need to post publicly about it while the number transfer and key accounts are still unsecured.
- Longer-term monitoring can wait until your number, email, and bank accounts are stable.

## Important reassurance
A pending number transfer or unexpected PAC message is urgent, but there are clear first steps. The main priority is to get your provider to stop or restrict the transfer, then protect email and banking routes that might depend on SMS.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate account takeover and fraud risk. Later decisions, especially if money was taken or accounts were accessed, may need help from your bank, mobile provider, police reporting route, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, technical, investigative, or professional advice. Ask each organisation you contact to repeat back what protections they have applied and what you should expect next.
