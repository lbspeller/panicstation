# What to do if… you get a flood of password reset messages for accounts you did not request (UK)

## Short answer
Do not click any links or buttons inside the messages. Secure your primary email account immediately by changing the password and ensuring two-step verification is active.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click "reset password," "verify," "unsubscribe," or any other links in the messages.
- Do not reply to the emails or use any phone numbers provided within them.
- Do not delete the messages in bulk immediately; a genuine alert regarding a high-value account may be hidden among the spam.
- Do not share any security codes or one-time passcodes (OTPs) with anyone, even if they claim to be from a security team.
- Do not disable two-step verification (2SV) or multi-factor authentication (MFA) to stop the notifications.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate yourself from the notification flood**
   - **Silence your phone or computer notifications for five minutes to reduce the pressure to act quickly.**
   - **Open a fresh browser window and manually type in the web addresses of your accounts.**
   - **Avoid using any "quick links" from your bookmarks if they were originally sourced from emails.**

2. **Secure your primary email account**
   - **Change your email password to a strong, unique one using three random words.**
   - **Verify that two-step verification (2SV) is turned on for your email provider.**
   - **Go to your account security settings and select "sign out of all other sessions" or "remove other devices."**

3. **Audit your email for hidden "silent" rules**
   - **Check your email "Rules," "Filters," or "Auto-forwarding" settings for any entries you did not create.**
     - Look for rules that move emails to "Archive," "Trash," or "Mark as Read" automatically.
     - Look for any external email addresses listed as forwarding targets.
   - **Delete any unknown rules or forwarding addresses immediately.**

4. **Identify high-priority alerts in the noise**
   - **Use your email search bar to look specifically for terms like "password changed," "security alert," "new sign-in," or "purchase confirmation."**
   - **Focus exclusively on messages from "high-risk" services such as your bank, HMRC, payment apps, or mobile network provider.**
   - **If you find a genuine "password changed" or "purchase" email, log in to that service via their official app or a manually typed address to verify.**

5. **Report the incident to the authorities**
   - **Forward any suspicious or phishing emails to the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) at report@phishing.gov.uk.**
   - **Report any fraudulent websites linked in the messages via the NCSC "Report a suspicious website" tool.**
   - **If money has been stolen or your identity has been compromised, report the crime.**
     - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland: Contact Report Fraud (formerly Action Fraud) online or via 0300 123 2040.
     - In Scotland: Contact Police Scotland by calling 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to unsubscribe from every service that sent a message today.
- You do not need to delete every single spam message immediately once your email is secure.
- You do not need to identify the attacker or understand how they got your email address right now.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a password reset email usually only indicates that someone knows your email address or username. It does not mean they have successfully accessed your account. By not interacting with the messages and securing your email first, you are effectively blocking the most common path an attacker uses to take over accounts.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization to prevent account lockouts and data loss. Later steps may include a full digital security audit or professional technical support if a breach is confirmed.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute technical, legal, or financial advice. Jurisdictional processes in the UK are subject to change; always verify reporting procedures through official gov.uk or police.uk websites.
