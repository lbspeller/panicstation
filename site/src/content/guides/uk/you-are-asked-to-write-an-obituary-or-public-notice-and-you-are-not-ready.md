# What to do if… you are asked to write an obituary or public notice and you are not ready (UK)

## Short answer
Pause the task to identify if the request is for an optional tribute, a funeral announcement, or a legal "deceased estates notice," then use a minimal "holding" statement to meet immediate deadlines.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to an arbitrary deadline under social pressure if it causes significant distress.
- Do not publish specific personal details such as a full home address, full date of birth, or mother’s maiden name.
- Do not include sensitive information about the cause of death or family disputes without unanimous agreement from the legal next of kin.
- Do not assume you are legally required to write a tribute; optional obituaries have no statutory requirement.
- Do not pay for "urgent" publication fees to callers or emailers without verifying the invoice through a known contact number for the newspaper or funeral director.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific type of notice required immediately.**
   - Ask: "Is this for the funeral details, a life story/tribute, or a legal requirement for creditors?"
   - If it is for funeral details, you only need the name, date, time, and location of the service.
2. **Issue a minimal holding notice to satisfy newspaper deadlines.**
   - Provide only the name of the deceased, the town of residence, and the phrase: "Funeral details to follow" or "Private funeral."
   - You can add: "Enquiries to [Name of Funeral Director]" to route questions away from yourself.
3. **Verify the status of the legal Deceased Estates Notice.**
   - In England and Wales, this is a "Section 27" notice under the Trustee Act 1925; similar provisions exist in Scotland and Northern Ireland.
   - If you are not the Executor or Administrator (Personal Representative), pass this request immediately to the person who is.
   - If you are the Executor, you typically wait until after the Grant of Probate/Confirmation to place this notice in The Gazette and a local newspaper.
4. **Delegate the first draft to a third party.**
   - Ask the funeral director or a friend to write a "facts-only" version (e.g., date of birth, date of death, surviving relatives).
   - You only act as the final "approval" point, which requires less emotional labor than starting from a blank page.
5. **Set a formal boundary for the extended tribute/obituary.**
   - Tell family members: "I am not ready to write the full tribute yet. We will publish the funeral notice now and the life story at a later date when it can be done properly."
6. **Protect the estate from identity fraud.**
   - Ensure the notice does not contain enough information to reconstruct the deceased's identity for credit applications.
   - Consider registering the death with the Bereavement Register or a protective registration service like Cifas.

## What can wait
- You do not have to write a detailed life story or "tribute" today.
- You do not have to include names of every surviving relative if it is too complex to manage right now.
- You do not have to decide on charity donation details immediately; "donations in lieu of flowers if desired" can be specified later.
- You do not have to respond to feedback or "edits" from distant relatives while in the acute phase of grief.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel "brain fog" or an inability to find the right words shortly after a death. Choosing to delay a public statement is a protective action, not a sign of neglect. A short, factual notice is standard and respectful.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for handling requests for public notices. It does not provide legal advice on the administration of estates or the resolution of inheritance disputes.

## Important note
Legal requirements for "Deceased Estates Notices" vary by jurisdiction (England and Wales vs Scotland vs Northern Ireland). While placing a notice protects the Personal Representative from personal liability for unknown debts, it does not prevent creditors from pursuing the estate's beneficiaries. Consult a solicitor or a professional executor if you are unsure of your statutory duties.
