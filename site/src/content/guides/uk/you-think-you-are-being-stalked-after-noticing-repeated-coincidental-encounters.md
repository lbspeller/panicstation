# What to do if… you think you are being stalked after noticing repeated “coincidental” encounters (UK)

## Short answer
Move immediately to a populated, well-lit public space and inform a trusted contact of your location. If you believe you are in immediate danger or are currently being followed, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "test" the individual by moving to isolated areas to see if they follow.
- Do not confront the individual or attempt to "reason" with them.
- Do not post your current location or travel plans on social media.
- Do not delete any digital evidence including messages, call logs, or photos of the individual.
- Do not wait for "hard proof" before seeking safety or contacting the police if you feel threatened.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause immediately.** Enter a busy public building such as a supermarket, café, or police station.
   - If driving, do not go home; drive to a 24-hour petrol station or a staffed public area.
   - Stay in the sight of staff, security, or CCTV cameras.
2. **Call 999 if you feel in immediate danger.** 
   - From a mobile: If you cannot speak, **press 55** when prompted by the automated system (the Silent Solution) to alert the operator that you need the police.
   - From a landline: If you cannot speak, stay on the line; operators will listen for background noise and may connect you directly to the police.
   - Clearly state your location if you are able to speak.
3. **Notify a trusted contact of your situation and location.** Share your live location via your phone and describe the individual and any vehicle involved. If you are at a workplace or university, inform security or management so they can facilitate a safe exit.
4. **Create a chronological incident log.** Record the date, time, and specific location of every encounter you have noticed.
   - Include physical descriptions (height, clothing, build).
   - Note vehicle registration numbers, makes, and colors.
   - List any potential witnesses or locations where CCTV may have captured the event.
5. **Vary your travel routes and routines.** Avoid predictable patterns by using different roads, transport methods, or timings for your commute. Ensure someone knows when you are expected to arrive at your destination.
6. **Request protective measures from building management.** If encounters happen near your home or work, ask staff to monitor entrances and prevent unauthorized access. Ask for an escort to your vehicle or public transport if you feel unsafe leaving alone.
7. **Secure your digital accounts and location privacy.** Disable all non-essential location-sharing features on your apps and social media. Change passwords for your primary email and banking accounts from a device you know is secure.
8. **Report the pattern to the police via 101 or online.** Explain that you are concerned about a "course of conduct" involving repeated encounters.
   - In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland: Ask the officer about applying for a **Stalking Protection Order (SPO)**.
   - In Scotland: Ask about a **Non-Harassment Order** or reporting under the Stalking (Scotland) Act 2010.
   - Always request an incident or crime reference number.
9. **Contact specialist stalking support services for safety planning.**
   - National Stalking Helpline (Suzy Lamplugh Trust): 0808 802 0300 (Mon/Wed 09:30-20:00; Tue/Thu/Fri 09:30-16:00).
   - Victim Support (England/Wales): 0808 168 9111 (24/7).
   - Action Against Stalking (Scotland): 0800 820 2427.
   - Paladin National Stalking Advocacy Service (High-risk cases): 020 3866 4107.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you want to go to court or press full charges.
- You do not need to understand why the person is doing this or what their motive is.
- You do not need to permanently move house or change jobs as an immediate reaction.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel hyper-vigilant or doubt your own observations when encounters seem "coincidental." Your intuition is a safety tool; acknowledging a pattern early is a proactive step toward your security and does not mean you are overreacting.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm reduction for individuals noticing a pattern of behavior. It does not replace a professional risk assessment or legal advice from specialist stalking advocates.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional legal or safety advice. If you are in danger, always contact the emergency services immediately.
