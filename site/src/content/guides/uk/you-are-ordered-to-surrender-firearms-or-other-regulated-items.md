# What to do if… you are ordered to surrender firearms or other regulated items (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as time-critical: move towards safe compliance using the exact wording of the order, and get written proof of what was surrendered and when. If anything is unclear, contact the issuing court using official court contact details and contact police through 101 or your police force’s official online route before moving or handing over anything.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hide, move, lend, store with someone else, sell, deactivate, destroy, or transfer any listed item unless the order or police explicitly tells you to do that.
- Do not bring a firearm, shotgun, ammunition, certificate, or other listed regulated item to a police station unless police have specifically told you to and explained the safe method.
- Do not assume an appeal, complaint, or request for review pauses the order unless the court confirms that in writing.
- Do not handle firearms or ammunition more than necessary.
- Do not ignore related items if the order lists them, such as ammunition, certificates, keys, access codes, magazines, component parts, or other regulated items.
- Do not rely on memory; write down who you spoke to, when, and what they told you.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safe pause before doing anything else.** If there is immediate danger to anyone, call 999. Otherwise, keep the items secured where they are and do not move them until you have official instructions.
2. **Read the order once, slowly.** Identify the court name, case number, deadline, the exact items covered, and any wording about handover, collection, storage, transport, certificates, keys, or access to secure storage.
3. **Verify the order using official details.** If the order came by email, text, post from someone unexpected, or through a third party, check it with the issuing court using official court contact details, not just a number or link supplied in the message.
4. **Contact police through an official non-emergency route.** Call 101 or use your police force’s official online contact route. Say: “I have a court order requiring surrender of firearms or regulated items. Please tell me the safe process for handover or collection.”
5. **Ask for the handover method before moving anything.** Ask whether police will collect the items, whether an appointment is needed, what paperwork or ID is needed, and whether certificates, ammunition, keys, access codes, or component parts must also be handed over.
6. **Make a simple inventory without unnecessary handling.** Note the make, model, serial number, certificate number, ammunition quantity, and any other identifying detail you can safely record for each item covered by the order.
7. **Follow the instructed method exactly.** If police tell you to transport anything, ask them to explain the safe transport method and write it down before you move it.
8. **Ask for an itemised receipt or written record.** Ask for written confirmation showing the date, time, place, officer or staff details if available, and a list of every item, certificate, key, access device, and ammunition quantity surrendered or taken.
9. **If something listed is missing, inaccessible, already transferred, or not in your possession, say so immediately in writing.** Keep the message short: state what the order lists, what you can surrender, what you cannot surrender, and ask what the court or police want you to do next.
10. **Get urgent legal advice if the order seems wrong, impossible to comply with, or has a hearing or response deadline.** Do not delay surrender unless the court or other lawful authority changes or pauses the order in writing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to challenge the order unless the order itself gives a hearing or response deadline.
- You do not need to explain the whole background to police contact staff; you only need enough information to arrange safe compliance.
- You do not need to settle long-term ownership, sale, storage, appeal, or disposal questions right now.
- You do not need to contact friends, clubs, dealers, or buyers before you have checked whether the order allows any transfer or movement.

## Important reassurance
A court order involving firearms or regulated items can feel frightening and unfair. The safest first steps are narrow: keep items secure, verify the official route, follow the stated process, and keep a written record of compliance.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about appeal, variation, storage, return, sale, disposal, criminal risk, or certificate status may need specialist legal advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Firearms law, court processes, police procedures, and terminology can differ across England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, and can also differ by police force, item, certificate, and the exact order. Follow the exact order and use official court and police contact routes to confirm what applies before acting.
