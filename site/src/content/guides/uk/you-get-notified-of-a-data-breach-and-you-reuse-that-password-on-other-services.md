# What to do if… you get notified of a data breach and you reuse that password on other services (UK)

## Short answer
Immediately change the password on the breached account and your primary email account, then systematically update every other service where that password was reused.

## Do not do these things
- Don't click any "reset password" or "login" links contained within the breach notification email itself.
- Don't create variations of the old password (such as adding a digit or exclamation mark) as these are easily predicted by automated tools.
- Don't assume a "low-value" account is safe to ignore; attackers use minor accounts to gather personal data for more sophisticated fraud.
- Don't save your new passwords in a plain text file, "Notes" app, or as a screenshot on your phone.
- Don't wait for "suspicious activity" to appear before acting, as credential stuffing often occurs in waves weeks after a breach.

## What to do now
1. **Change your email account password immediately to a strong, unique value.**  
   Your email is the "master key" used for password resets on almost all other services. If this account is compromised, attackers can lock you out of everything else.

2. **Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) or Passkeys on your email and banking accounts.**  
   - Use an authenticator app (like Google or Microsoft Authenticator) or a physical security key rather than SMS codes where possible.
   - If the service offers "Passkeys," set one up to replace the password entirely.

3. **Log into the breached service via its official website or app and change the password.**  
   Use a password that is completely different from any you have used before. If the site offers a "log out of all sessions" option, select it immediately.

4. **Review your email settings for unauthorized filters or forwarding rules.**  
   Attackers often set up rules to automatically delete or forward "security alert" emails so you do not see them. Remove any rules you did not create.

5. **Identify every other account where you reused the compromised password.**  
   Priority should be given to financial services, government accounts (HMRC, DWP), mobile network providers, and social media. Change these passwords to unique values.

6. **Check breach aggregation tools to see if other data was exposed.**  
   Use a reputable service such as "Have I Been Pwned" to check if your email address or phone number has appeared in other recent breaches you may have missed.

7. **Monitor your bank statements and credit reports for the next three months.**  
   Look for small "test" transactions or applications for credit you did not make. You can check your credit report for free via services like ClearScore, Experian, or TransUnion.

8. **Apply for Cifas Protective Registration if your personal details were also leaked.**  
   If the breach included your address, date of birth, or ID numbers, you can pay £30 for two years of protection. This places a flag on your credit file requiring lenders to perform extra identity checks.

## What can wait
- You do not need to delete your accounts or change your primary email address today unless they are actively being hijacked.
- You do not need to contact the police (Action Fraud) unless you have actually lost money or your identity has already been stolen.
- You do not need to hire a private cybersecurity firm; following the standard NCSC steps is sufficient for individual stabilization.

## Important reassurance
Password reuse is a very common habit, and receiving a breach notice is a standard part of modern digital life. Taking these steps systematically, starting with your email, effectively neutralizes the immediate threat and prevents the "domino effect" of multiple account takeovers.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization only. If you find that money has already been stolen or you are locked out of your primary email, you will need to follow specific recovery procedures for those institutions.

## Important note
This information is for guidance purposes and does not constitute professional cybersecurity or legal advice. If you suspect active financial fraud, contact your bank immediately. For serious cybercrime, report the incident to Action Fraud via their website or by calling 0300 123 2040.
