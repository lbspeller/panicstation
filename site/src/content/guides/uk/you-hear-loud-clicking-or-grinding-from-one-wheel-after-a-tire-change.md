# What to do if… you hear loud clicking or grinding from one wheel after a tire change (UK)

## Short answer
Do not continue the journey normally. Treat a new loud clicking or grinding noise from one wheel after a tyre change as a possible wheel or brake-area fault until the vehicle has been checked safely.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep driving to “see if it clears”.
- Don’t drive faster to test the noise.
- Don’t stand on the traffic side of the vehicle to look or listen.
- Don’t put any part of your body under the vehicle at the roadside.
- Don’t attempt repairs on a motorway or beside moving traffic.
- Don’t use a warning triangle on a motorway.
- Don’t move the vehicle again if you can see missing or loose wheel bolts or nuts, a tilted wheel, fresh metal scraping, smoke, or fluid leaking near that wheel.

## What to do now
1. **Make the stopping place as safe as you can.**
   - Put your hazard warning lights on.
   - If you are already in a safe car park, forecourt, lay-by, service area, or other place away from moving traffic, stay there.
   - If you are on a live road and the vehicle still steers and brakes normally, leave the carriageway at the next safe opportunity or pull into a service area, emergency area, lay-by, or hard shoulder if one is available.
   - If the wheel looks tilted, bolts or nuts appear missing, the grinding is severe, or the vehicle feels unstable, stop moving as soon as it is safe and call for help.

2. **If this is on a motorway or high-speed road, follow breakdown safety first.**
   - Switch on hazard warning lights.
   - Exit by the side furthest from traffic only if it is safe and possible.
   - Keep well away from the vehicle and moving traffic, behind a safety barrier where there is one.
   - If you cannot safely leave the vehicle, are stopped in a live lane, or feel in immediate danger, stay belted with hazards on and call 999, asking for the police.
   - Use a free emergency roadside telephone if one is nearby and you can reach it safely.

3. **Do a fast visual check only if you are safely away from moving traffic.**
   - Look at the noisy wheel from the outside: is it upright, centred, and sitting like the other wheels?
   - Look for any wheel bolts or nuts that are missing, sitting proud, or obviously backed off.
   - Look for fresh shiny scrape marks, metal dust, smoke, or rubbing inside the wheel area.
   - Check whether a wheel trim or hubcap is loose and touching the wheel.

4. **If anything looks wrong, or you are not sure, do not drive it.**
   - Contact your breakdown provider and say: “Possible loose wheel after tyre change; loud clicking or grinding from one wheel; vehicle stopped.”
   - If you are in an emergency area on a motorway, use the emergency telephone if you can do so safely and follow the operator’s advice.
   - In England, National Highways motorway advice may also apply; if you are in danger or cannot safely exit the vehicle, call 999.

5. **If the wheel looks normal but the noise was loud, keep movement to the minimum needed for safety.**
   - Do not rejoin fast traffic just to continue the journey.
   - Move only to a nearby safer stopping place if the vehicle still feels controllable and the move is safer than staying where you are.
   - Arrange a professional inspection before driving normally again.

6. **Tell the tyre fitter what happened.**
   - Say the noise started immediately after the tyre change and that you have stopped.
   - Ask whether they want the vehicle recovered to them rather than driven.
   - Note the time, place, wheel affected, and what you saw; take photos only if you can do so safely.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact cause now, such as wheel bolt torque, wheel seating, a rubbing brake shield, or a loose trim.
- You do not need to decide complaints, refunds, or fault at the roadside.
- You do not need to remove the wheel.
- You do not need to argue with the fitter while you are still in an unsafe place.

## Important reassurance
Stopping to check was the right kind of cautious response. A sudden loud clicking or grinding noise from one wheel straight after a tyre change is a sensible reason to stop, stay out of traffic, and get the vehicle checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate risk and get you to a safer pause. Diagnosis, repair, recovery, and any later complaint may need help from a qualified mechanic, breakdown provider, insurer, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, mechanical, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe where you are, prioritise personal safety and emergency services guidance over checking the vehicle.
