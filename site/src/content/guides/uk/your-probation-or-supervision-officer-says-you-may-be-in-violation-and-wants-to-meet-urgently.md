# What to do if… your probation or supervision officer says you may be in violation and wants to meet urgently (UK)

## Short answer
Confirm your attendance immediately in writing, ask exactly which condition they believe you have breached, and prepare any physical evidence (medical notes, travel proof, or work records) that explains your actions before you arrive.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore the message or stop answering your phone; being "out of touch" is often a standalone reason for immediate recall.
- Don't admit to a breach or sign any "admission of fact" documents during the meeting without speaking to a solicitor first.
- Don't send long, emotional, or aggressive explanations via text or voicemail while you are panicked.
- Don't consume alcohol or drugs before the meeting, even if you feel overwhelmed, as this can be used as evidence of increased risk.
- Don't miss the meeting to "buy time" to find a lawyer; it is better to attend and say you need legal advice than to fail to show up.

## What to do now
1. **Reply immediately and ask for the specific details of the allegation.** 
   - Ask: "Which condition or requirement is this meeting regarding?"
   - Ask: "Is this a formal breach warning or a discussion about a possible recall?"
2. **Confirm the time and location and state clearly that you will attend.** If you physically cannot attend (e.g., you are in hospital), provide the evidence for this immediately and propose a specific alternative time later the same day or the following morning.
3. **Gather any physical "proof of compliance" relevant to the issue.** 
   - Printed call logs or screenshots showing you tried to call the office.
   - Medical discharge summaries or fit notes.
   - Letters from employers or shift rotas.
   - Proof of travel disruption (train cancellation screenshots or bus tickets).
4. **Contact a criminal defence solicitor for a brief "pre-meeting" consultation.** You can find a local firm via the Law Society "Find a Solicitor" tool; tell them you have an urgent probation breach meeting and need to know your rights regarding admissions.
5. **Read your original Order or Licence paperwork carefully.** Locate the specific requirement (e.g., Requirement 3: Prohibited Activity or Condition 4: Residency) they are questioning so you understand the exact wording of your obligation.
6. **Write down a short, factual timeline of what happened.** Do not include feelings or excuses; focus on times, dates, and actions. Take this paper with you so you do not get confused under pressure.
7. **Arrive at the probation office at least 10 minutes early.** Being late for a meeting about a breach is frequently treated as a further failure to comply.
8. **Ask the officer to clarify the "enforcement track" you are on.** 
   - For Community Orders: Ask if they are referring the matter back to Court.
   - For Licence: Ask if they are considering a 56-day fixed-term recall or a standard recall.
9. **Take notes of everything the officer says during the meeting.** If they ask you to sign a statement, you are entitled to say: "I want to cooperate, but I need to show this to my solicitor before I sign anything."
10. **Request a written record of the meeting's outcome before you leave.** Ask for a "Record of Contact" or a letter stating what the next steps are and when your next mandatory appointment is scheduled.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to plead guilty or not guilty to a breach today; that happens in court or via a formal appeal.
- You do not need to notify your employer or family of the specific details unless you are actually taken into custody or your conditions change.
- You do not need to prepare a "character reference" pack yet; the current focus is solely on the immediate allegation.

## Important reassurance
An urgent meeting is often a "fact-finding" exercise. Probation officers have a duty to investigate suspected non-compliance, but this does not always lead to a return to court or prison. Showing that you are still "engaged" by turning up and providing factual evidence is the most effective way to prevent the situation from escalating into an automatic recall.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hours surrounding a breach notification. It does not cover court procedures, the 2026 "earned progression" model appeals, or specific Parole Board representations.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional legal advice. The Sentencing Act 2026 has introduced significant changes to recall lengths (now 56 days for most SDS) and release points. Always consult a qualified criminal defence solicitor regarding your specific case.
