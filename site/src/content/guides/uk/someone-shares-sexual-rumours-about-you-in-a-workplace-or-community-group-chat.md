# What to do if… someone shares sexual rumours about you in a workplace or community group chat (UK)

## Short answer
Get out of the live conversation first: mute the chat, do not argue in the thread, and save what you can without drawing more attention to it. When you feel steady enough, use a private route with a workplace contact or group admin to ask for the rumours to stop, for posts to be removed where possible, and for relevant records to be preserved.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply in the same thread while you feel flooded or angry; it can escalate the situation and create more screenshots.
- Do not delete your own evidence in a panic, unless keeping it creates an immediate safety risk.
- Do not share the rumour more widely to correct it; that can spread it further.
- Do not confront the person one-to-one if they feel unsafe, powerful over you, or unpredictable.
- Do not let someone pressure you into keeping it informal if you want a written record.
- Do not search, bait, or question other people to prove what happened; save what you already have and use a safer reporting route.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** Mute the group chat, step away from the screen for a few minutes, and ask one trusted person to sit with you or stay available while you take the next steps.

2. **Save the key evidence once, without investigating.** If it is safe to do so, take screenshots that show the rumour wording, who posted it, the date and time, and the group or workplace chat name. Also save message links if the platform allows it, and write a short note of what happened, where it appeared, who posted it, and who may have seen it.

3. **Ask privately for the spread to stop.** If it is a community group chat, message the admins or moderators privately. Ask them to remove the posts where possible, stop further discussion, preserve relevant logs, and remind members not to share sexual rumours or screenshots.

4. **Use platform controls that reduce contact.** Report the message in the app if there is a report tool. Mute, restrict, or block the person if that helps you stay safe and does not cut off evidence you still need to save.

5. **If there is any workplace connection, use a route that creates a record.** Check your staff handbook, intranet, or policy for the harassment, sexual harassment, bullying, dignity at work, or grievance route. Report to HR, your manager, another manager, or the named reporting person if your manager is involved. Ask for written acknowledgement that your concern has been received.

6. **Ask for practical protection, not a perfect outcome today.** In the same message, ask for relevant chat logs to be preserved, the rumour posts to be removed where possible, and direct contact with the person to be limited. For example, ask not to be placed alone with them, or ask for work communication to move to email for now.

7. **Use support if the situation includes threats, stalking, or repeated harassment.** If someone is in immediate danger or a crime is in progress, call 999. If it is not an emergency but you feel threatened or harassed, you can contact police on 101 or report online through the police route.

8. **Get confidential support if you feel shaken.** A trusted person, union rep, GP, employee assistance programme, or specialist support service can help you steady yourself before you decide what to do next. You do not have to decide today whether to make a formal complaint, report to police, or take legal advice.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, leave the group, clear your name publicly, or take legal action.
- You do not need to write a perfect statement now; a short factual account and the key screenshots are enough to start.
- You do not need to respond to every person who saw it.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether the behaviour is a legal claim or a crime.
- If you may later consider an employment tribunal claim, time limits are usually short, often 3 months minus 1 day from the most recent incident. You still do not need to decide today, but getting early advice can protect your options.

## Important reassurance
Sexual rumours can feel exposing, humiliating, and unsafe. A strong reaction does not mean you did anything wrong. The immediate aim is to slow the situation down, reduce further spread, and move it into a safer private channel with a record.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the moment, preserve what matters, and reduce harm. Later decisions about workplace processes, complaints, police contact, legal options, or ongoing safety may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999. If you are unsure whether something is a crime, a workplace rights issue, or a group conduct issue, you can still document what happened and ask an appropriate service for help.
