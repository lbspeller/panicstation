# What to do if… you feel pressured into sexual contact during a healthcare or support appointment and you feel unsafe (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause by stopping the interaction immediately and putting another person between you and the individual, or by leaving the premises. You are not required to provide an explanation or enter into a debate in the moment.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t remain in the room or continue the appointment out of a sense of politeness.
- Don’t agree to stay alone with the person for "just a minute" or to "finish up" if you feel unsafe.
- Don’t accept any requests for private follow-up contact via personal phone numbers, social media, or meetings outside the professional setting.
- Don’t feel obligated to give a detailed reason for leaving; a simple "I am stopping now" or "I am leaving" is sufficient.
- Don’t feel pressured to make a formal report or statement immediately if you are not ready.

## What to do now
1. **End the appointment and create physical distance.** **Use clear, short phrases to stop the interaction.** 
   - Say: "Stop," "I do not consent," or "I am leaving now." 
   - Move toward the exit or into a public area such as the reception desk.
2. **Bring another person into the space immediately.** **Ask any staff member for an impartial witness or a senior lead.** 
   - In a GP or clinic, ask for the Practice Manager or a trained chaperone. 
   - In a hospital, ask for the Nurse in Charge or the Site Manager. 
   - A formal chaperone must be an appropriately trained staff member, though you may also have a friend or relative present if you wish.
3. **Switch your provider if you still require care today.** **State that you will only continue with a different clinician or with a formal chaperone present.** 
   - Say: "I want to see a different member of staff," or "I want to rearrange this with a chaperone."
4. **Make a brief record of what happened while it is fresh.** **Write down the specific details in your phone or on paper for your own use.** 
   - Include the date, time, and exact location. 
   - Record the name or description of the individual. 
   - List exactly what was said or done and if anyone else was nearby.
5. **Report the immediate safety concern to the service management.** **Request the formal written complaints procedure to use later if you choose.** 
   - For NHS hospitals, contact the Patient Advice and Liaison Service (PALS) to flag the incident. 
   - For GP or community clinics, speak with the Practice Manager or the designated Safeguarding Lead.
6. **Contact a specialist support service for confidential advice.** **Call a dedicated helpline to speak with a trained specialist who understands sexual violence.** 
   - **England and Wales:** Call the Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line on 0808 500 2222 (24/7). 
   - **Scotland:** Call the Rape Crisis Scotland helpline on 08088 01 03 02 (5pm–midnight daily). 
   - **Northern Ireland:** Call the Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline on 0808 802 1414 (24/7).
7. **Access medical or forensic support without police involvement.** **Contact a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) to discuss your options.** 
   - In England, search "NHS SARC" to find your local 24/7 centre. 
   - In Scotland, call the SARCS self-referral line on 0800 148 88 88 (24/7).
8. **Call 999 if you are in immediate danger.** **Contact the police on 101 if you are safe now but wish to report the incident or seek police advice.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately if the interaction "counts" as a crime or a specific type of abuse.
- You do not need to write a formal statement or complaint today.
- You do not need to speak to the person again, accept an apology from them, or attend any future appointments with them.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, feeling confused, or complying with requests to "get through" the moment are common physiological responses to fear. This is especially true in healthcare environments where there is a significant power imbalance. It is a standard right to stop any appointment or request a different staff member at any time.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety actions. Decisions regarding formal complaints, regulatory reports (such as to the GMC or NMC), or police investigations can be made later with the help of specialist advocates.

## Important note
This information is for immediate stabilization following a healthcare or support appointment where you felt unsafe. It does not constitute legal or clinical advice. If you are at risk of harm right now, prioritize reaching a safe location and contacting emergency services.
