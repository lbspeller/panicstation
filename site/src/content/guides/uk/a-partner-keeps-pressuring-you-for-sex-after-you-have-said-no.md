# What to do if… a partner keeps pressuring you for sex after you have said no (UK)

## Short answer
End the sexual conversation and create space: stop replying, end the call, leave the room, or go somewhere you are not alone. If you feel pressured, frightened, or unsure what to call it, contact confidential specialist support when you can safely do so.

## Do not do these things
- Do not debate your boundary or try to prove it is reasonable.
- Do not send sexual messages, images, or recordings to keep the peace or stop them sulking, arguing, or escalating.
- Do not meet in private to “talk it through” if you feel uneasy, pressured, or afraid of their reaction.
- Do not accept “it’s just banter” as a reason to keep going after you have said stop.
- Do not delete messages in a rush unless keeping them puts you at risk, for example if they monitor your phone.
- Do not assume you must report to police to get support.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the sexual conversation briefly.** Use one line if it feels safe:
   - “Stop. I’m not discussing sex.”
   - “I said no.”
   - “Do not keep bringing this up.”
   Then stop explaining, mute the chat, leave the chat, or end the call.
2. **Create immediate space.** If you are together in person, move to another room, step outside, end the visit, or move towards other people. Keep your phone, keys, bank card, and a way home with you if you can.
3. **Switch to safer communication, or none.** If you need to send anything, keep it practical: “I’m going home now” or “I’m not discussing this.” If they keep pushing, do not keep replying to the sexual topic.
4. **Tell one trusted person what is happening.** Send a simple message such as: “I’m not okay. They keep pushing sexual talk after I say no. Can you stay on the phone while I get home?” Ask them to check in later if that would help.
5. **Make a short private note if it is safe.** Write the date, time, what you said, and what they did next. This is for your own memory and for support conversations; do not keep it somewhere they can access.
6. **Use confidential specialist support.**
   - If you are in England or Wales and aged 16 or over, Rape Crisis England & Wales runs the 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line on 0808 500 2222 and online chat.
   - If you are in Scotland, Rape Crisis Scotland’s helpline is 08088 01 03 02, every day from 5pm to midnight.
   - If you are in Northern Ireland, the Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline is 0808 802 1414 and is available 24/7.
   - If this is part of broader control, intimidation, monitoring, or fear of their reaction, the National Domestic Abuse Helpline is 0808 2000 247.
   - If you want support after something that may be a crime in England or Wales, Victim Support’s 24/7 Supportline is 08 08 16 89 111 and it also has live chat.
7. **If you are in immediate danger, call 999 now.** If it is not an emergency but you feel threatened, harassed, or need police contact, you can contact police on 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to end the relationship.
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report anything.
- You do not need to confront them “properly” or find the perfect words.
- You do not need to label it as abuse or coercion before asking for support.
- Blocking, telling other people, saving records, legal advice, and longer-term plans can wait until you are safer and steadier.

## Important reassurance
Having boundaries is normal. Someone repeatedly pushing sexual talk after you have said stop is not you being difficult; it is information about how they respond to your no. Feeling frozen, guilty, confused, or worried that you are overreacting can happen under pressure, and it does not mean you agreed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the pattern continues, or you feel afraid of their reaction, specialist sexual violence or domestic abuse services can help you think through safer next steps at your pace.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you feel at risk or in danger, prioritise immediate safety and emergency services. You can ask for confidential support without making a police report.
