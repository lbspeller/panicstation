# What to do if… a stranger claims to “know you” and insists you go somewhere with them despite your refusal (UK)

## Short answer
Do not go anywhere with them and move immediately into a staffed, public location such as a shop, café, or station. Ask staff or a bystander to call 999 while you remain in the view of others.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to a “quieter” spot, side street, car park, or vehicle to "clear things up."
- Do not let them steer you by the arm, shoulder, or by “guiding” you physically.
- Do not provide personal details like your name, address, or where you are staying.
- Do not follow them to “prove” you are not being rude or to end the awkwardness.
- Do not show them your ID, unlock your phone, or hand your phone over to them.
- Do not head straight to your home or vehicle if you think they may follow you.

## What to do now
1. **Move immediately to a staffed, public location where you are visible to others.** Aim for a shop counter, a hotel reception, or a busy queue in a well-lit area.
2. **State one clear refusal and stop engaging with the individual immediately.** Use a firm line such as **“I do not know you, and I am not going anywhere with you.”** Do not debate or explain your reasons.
3. **Turn your back to the stranger and address a specific bystander or staff member for help.** Give them a direct task to remove the ambiguity of the situation:
   - **“You in the green coat, please call 999. This person is harassing me and I am scared.”**
   - If indoors: **“I need help. Please call 999 and do not let this person near me.”**
4. **Call 999 yourself if you feel threatened or if the person refuses to leave your vicinity.** Be prepared to give your exact location, a description of the person (clothing and height), and what they are currently doing.
5. **Follow the "Silent Solution" protocol if you have dialled 999 on a mobile but cannot safely speak.** Stay on the line and listen to the operator's instructions.
   - If you are on a mobile and cannot speak, **press 55 when prompted** to signal that it is a genuine emergency; this will transfer you to the police.
   - If you are on a landline, staying silent or making background noise will usually result in a transfer to the police, as accidental landline calls are rare.
6. **Seek help from transport staff or use emergency alarms if this occurs on the rail, Tube, or tram network.** 
   - Dial **999** for immediate danger or threats.
   - For discreet, non-emergency reporting of harassment on the rail network, **text British Transport Police on 61016** or call 0800 40 50 40.
7. **Use the “Ask for Angela” code if you are in a bar, club, or participating hospitality venue.** Approach a member of staff and ask for **“Angela”** to signal that you feel unsafe and need assistance to leave discreetly.
8. **Wait for a safe transport option or a police arrival before leaving the staffed area.** Ask staff to watch you exit or wait with you indoors until your taxi or transport is at the door.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you want to press charges or make a formal statement.
- You do not need to "work out" if you actually know them or if they have confused you with someone else.
- You do not need to notify your social media followers or post about the incident immediately.
- You do not need to remember every detail perfectly in the next few minutes.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel confused, "frozen," or worried about being rude when a stranger is persistent. Being blunt, loud, and involving other people is a valid and necessary safety response. Your priority is your physical safety, not the stranger's feelings or social etiquette.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only, intended to help you reach a safe pause and obtain immediate assistance. Follow-up decisions regarding reporting or personal safety plans should be handled once you are in a secure environment.

## Important note
This guide is for general information and does not constitute legal advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, always call 999. If you feel uncomfortable or "wrong" about a situation, trust that instinct and seek help from staff or authorities immediately.
