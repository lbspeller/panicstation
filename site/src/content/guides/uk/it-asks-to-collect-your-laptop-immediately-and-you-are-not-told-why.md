# What to do if… IT asks to collect your laptop immediately and you are not told why (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all interaction immediately and report the situation to your employer to trigger their legal duty of care. Capture evidence of the threats before blocking the user, and call 999 if the person implies they know your physical location or are heading to your workplace.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply, "set the record straight," or argue with the individual.
- Do not delete the messages or the profile until you have screenshots and URLs.
- Do not attempt to "investigate" the person by looking into their personal profiles.
- Do not offer refunds or concessions to stop the harassment (this is often seen as "paying" for silence).
- Do not post about the situation on your personal social media accounts.

## What to do now
1. **Identify if there is an immediate physical risk.**
   **Call 999 immediately if the person makes specific threats to arrive at your home or office, or if they demonstrate they know your current physical location.**
   - Indicators of high risk include mentions of your home address, photos of your building, or "I am on my way" statements.

2. **Secure the digital evidence.**
   **Take clear screenshots that include the sender’s handle, the timestamp, the platform name, and the full text of the threat.**
   - Save the direct URL (link) to the specific post or profile.
   - If the threat is via email, save the message and the "header" information if possible.

3. **Formally notify your employer's safety lead.**
   **Email your line manager and Human Resources (HR) to create a written record of the incident as a workplace safety matter.**
   - Explicitly ask for this to be logged under the "Workplace Violence and Aggression" policy.
   - Request an immediate review of your outward-facing contact details (e.g., removing your direct email or profile from the company website).

4. **Isolate the hazard by routing communication.**
   **Ask your employer to take over all further contact with the client or customer.**
   - Request that the client be reassigned or "blacklisted" from personal contact with you.
   - Request that all future correspondence from this individual be sent to a generic team inbox rather than your personal work account.

5. **Report to the relevant authorities.**
   **Report the messages to the platform's safety team and, if criminal (threats of violence or extortion), to the police via 101 or their online reporting tool.**
   - If the individual is demanding money or making cyber-related threats, use the "Report Fraud" service (reportfraud.police.uk).
   - Use the "malicious communications" or "harassment" categories when reporting to the police.

6. **Brief your household or reception staff.**
   **Inform anyone who controls access to your physical space that you are being targeted.**
   - If you work in an office, tell security or the front desk to look out for that specific individual.
   - If you work from home, ensure your household knows not to open the door to unexpected visitors.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to quit or change your role.
- Filing a formal grievance against your employer (unless they refuse to act).
- Responding to negative reviews or public comments.
- Seeking legal advice for a private civil injunction.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel exposed or anxious when work-related conflict enters your personal digital space. Under UK law, including the Health and Safety at Work Act and 2026 updates to the Employment Rights Act, your employer has a legal obligation to take "all reasonable steps" to protect you from third-party harassment. You are not being "difficult" by asking for these protections.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance for immediate stabilization. It does not cover long-term litigation, employment tribunal procedures, or detailed data protection (GDPR) claims.

## Important note
This guide provides general information for stabilization and is not a substitute for legal advice or police instruction. If you are in immediate danger, always call 999. Employers in the UK have specific statutory duties to protect staff from violence and harassment; ensure you follow your specific workplace "Violence and Aggression" reporting procedures.
