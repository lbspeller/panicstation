# What to do if… your electric vehicle has very low range and your planned charger is unavailable (UK)

## Short answer
Reduce speed and non-essential power use now, then head for the nearest reachable charging option, including slower chargers. If the car may stop somewhere unsafe, treat it as a breakdown risk and get help before it becomes immobile.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t gamble on a far-away rapid charger if your range is already critical; a nearer slower charger may be safer.
- Don’t keep detouring between multiple chargers while very low; choose the safest reachable option and commit.
- Don’t keep driving fast to “get there sooner”; higher speed usually uses charge more quickly.
- Don’t run heating, air conditioning, preheating, heated seats, or other non-essential power use at full power while trying to reach a plug.
- Don’t accept an informal tow from a random vehicle; EV recovery and towing should be handled by someone who knows the vehicle and the recovery method.
- Don’t stop in a live lane if you can still reach a safer place; prioritise a service area, emergency area, lay-by, hard shoulder, or other place away from moving traffic.

## What to do now

1. **Create an immediate range buffer.**
   - Put the car into **eco** mode, or the nearest equivalent.
   - Accelerate gently and reduce speed smoothly while staying legal and predictable.
   - Reduce non-essential cabin power use: lower heating or air conditioning, switch off heated seats or steering wheel, and avoid preheating.
   - Check the car’s energy or range screen to see whether the predicted range is stabilising.

2. **Switch from “planned charger” to “nearest workable top-up.”**
   - Use the car’s navigation and a charging app or live charger map to search by **distance first**.
   - Include slower AC chargers at car parks, supermarkets, hotels, on-street bays, or nearby public sites; the aim is a top-up that prevents being stranded.
   - Check whether the charger appears available, whether it fits your connector, and whether you can pay there.
   - Pick one primary charger and one backup that you can realistically reach.

3. **If you are already at the unavailable charger, make one short attempt, then move on.**
   - Check the charger screen, app status, connector, and payment prompt once.
   - Call the charge point operator helpline shown on the unit or in the app; public charge points should display a free staffed helpline, and the operator may be able to check the fault or reset a session.
   - If the unit is clearly out of service, blocked, unsafe, or still not starting after a few minutes, leave for the nearest reachable backup before your range falls further.

4. **If you are on a motorway or fast road and may not reach a charger, move towards safety first.**
   - If possible, leave at the next exit or go to a service area.
   - If you cannot, move left and pull into an emergency area, lay-by, or hard shoulder if one is available.
   - Switch on hazard warning lights; use sidelights too if it is dark or visibility is poor.
   - If it is safe and possible, exit on the side furthest from traffic and wait well away from the vehicle, behind a safety barrier if there is one.
   - If you cannot reach a place of relative safety or cannot exit safely, stay in the vehicle with seat belts and hazard lights on, call **999**, and ask for the police.
   - On National Highways roads, if you have stopped in an emergency area, lay-by, or on a hard shoulder and are not in immediate danger, you can use the emergency telephone where provided or call **0300 123 5000** for advice and assistance.

5. **Call professional roadside help before the car becomes immobile.**
   - Contact your breakdown provider, insurer, manufacturer assistance line, or lease provider.
   - Say: **“Electric vehicle very low battery. I may not reach a charger. I need EV-safe recovery or charging advice.”**
   - Tell them your exact location, direction of travel, remaining displayed range, battery percentage if shown, number of passengers, and whether you are in a live lane or other unsafe place.

6. **If you reach any usable charger, take a short stabilising charge.**
   - Plug in and start a session as soon as you can.
   - Aim for enough charge to reach a more reliable charger, home, a safe stop, or your recovery destination.
   - You do not need to fill the battery now; a short top-up can be enough to get out of the emergency.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide who is at fault for the broken, blocked, or unavailable charger.
- You do **not** need to complain, review the charger, or report the network fault while your range is critical.
- You do **not** need to optimise price, charging speed, route comfort, or the rest of the journey yet.
- You do **not** need to troubleshoot every car setting; reduce speed, reduce non-essential power use, and secure a safe charge or safe recovery.

## Important reassurance
This situation is common and can feel urgent very quickly. The useful move is to stop treating the original charger as the plan and switch early to the nearest workable top-up or professional help.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the immediate situation. Once you are safely charging, safely stopped, or safely recovered, later decisions about route planning, charger complaints, vehicle checks, or compensation may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and practical first steps only. It is not legal, medical, financial, technical, or professional advice, and it is not a substitute for emergency services, manufacturer guidance, or professional roadside assistance.
