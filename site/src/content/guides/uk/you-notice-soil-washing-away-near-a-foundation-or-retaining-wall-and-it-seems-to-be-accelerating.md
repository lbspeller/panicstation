# What to do if… you notice soil washing away near a foundation or retaining wall and it seems to be accelerating (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the area to prevent falls and stop any controllable water sources (like leaking pipes or gutters) from feeding the washout. Arrange an urgent inspection by a Chartered Structural Engineer or a Chartered Building Surveyor to assess structural stability.

## Do not do these things
- Don't stand on or near the edge of a newly formed void, depression, or soft ground.
- Don't attempt to fill the hole with loose soil, rubble, or concrete, as this adds weight and hides the underlying cause.
- Don't dig into the area to investigate the depth of the erosion, as this can trigger a sudden collapse.
- Don't use a hose or pressure washer to clean the area, which will accelerate the washout.
- Don't park vehicles or place heavy skips/materials near the affected ground or wall.
- Don't ignore secondary signs such as new wall cracks, tilting window frames, or doors that suddenly stick.

## What to do now
1. **Establish a clear exclusion zone around the affected area immediately.** Keep residents, pets, and visitors away from the edge of any visible void or leaning wall.
2. **Determine if there is an immediate threat to life from a safe distance.** Active collapses, rapid ground movement toward a public path, or a wall showing significant movement require urgent intervention.
   - If a collapse is imminent or has occurred, **call 999**.
3. **Stop any visible water leaks that are contributing to the erosion.** Check if a broken downpipe, overflowing gutter, or burst external tap is pouring water directly into the washout.
   - Turn off the internal stopcock if you suspect a mains leak near the foundation.
   - Use a temporary tarp to cover the void if it is raining, ensuring the water sheds well away from the hole.
4. **Take clear photographs and video of the damage from a safe vantage point.** Capture the relationship between the washout and the building or wall, and include a fixed object for scale if possible.
5. **Contact a Chartered Structural Engineer or a RICS Chartered Building Surveyor for an emergency assessment.** Specify that the erosion is "accelerating" and involves "foundation or structural retaining wall instability."
   - Search for accredited professionals via the Institution of Structural Engineers (IStructE) or the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors (RICS).
6. **Evacuate the area and report the hazard if you smell gas or see exposed cables.** Soil movement frequently damages underground utility connections.
   - If you smell gas (Great Britain): **call 0800 111 999**.
   - If you smell gas (Northern Ireland): **call 0800 002 001**.
   - To report damaged electricity lines or poles: **call 105** (Great Britain and Northern Ireland) or **03457 643 643** (NIE Networks).
7. **Notify your buildings insurance provider to open a claim for potential subsidence or landslip.** Ask specifically what immediate "emergency works" are covered under your policy to prevent further loss.
8. **Contact your local authority building control department if the structure poses a risk to the public.** Councils in England and Wales have statutory powers to deal with dangerous structures that threaten public highways or neighbouring property.

## What can wait
- Permanent reconstruction or underpinning of the foundation.
- Cosmetic repairs to cracks or re-rendering of the wall surface.
- Legal disputes regarding boundary responsibilities or neighbour liability.
- Re-paving or landscaping the surface area.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant anxiety when the ground beneath your home or a major wall appears to be failing. While the movement feels urgent, most structural issues can be stabilised once the water source is identified and professional shoring or drainage management is implemented.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Professional engineering calculations and a site-specific remediation plan are required to ensure the long-term safety of the structure.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and does not replace the advice of a qualified engineer. If you observe rapid structural failure or believe the building is unsafe to occupy, vacate immediately and contact emergency services.
