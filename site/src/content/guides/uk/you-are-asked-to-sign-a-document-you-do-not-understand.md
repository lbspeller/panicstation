# What to do if… you are asked to sign a document you do not understand (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign a document you do not understand. Pause, ask for a readable version or a translator or interpreter, and get a copy before deciding.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign just to make the pressure stop if you cannot explain what the document says.
- Do not sign blank pages, incomplete forms, or pages you have not been allowed to read.
- Do not rely on “it’s standard” if no one will show you the terms or explain the consequence.
- Do not hand over your passport as “security” unless you understand why it is needed, where it will be kept, when it will be returned, and whether you can get written acknowledgement.
- Do not treat a rushed verbal summary or phone translation as enough for a waiver, payment demand, admission, police statement, immigration document, or anything about your rights.

## What to do now
1. **Create a pause.** Hold the document, step aside if you safely can, and say: “I cannot sign something I do not understand. I need a translation or interpreter.”
2. **Ask for a version you can read.** Ask for an English version, a copy in your preferred language, or time to speak to a translator, interpreter, local lawyer, insurer, tour operator, or trusted person.
3. **Ask what the document is.** Say: “What is this document called, and what happens if I sign it?” Write down the title, the person’s name or role, and the consequence they give.
4. **Get your own copy before any signature.** Ask for a photo, scan, email, or printed copy. If they refuse, treat that as a reason to delay signing.
5. **Check the highest-risk parts first.** Look for words about fees, deposits, damage, liability, waiver, release, consent, penalties, cancellation, admission, police statement, immigration, dispute, or passport.
6. **Use translation tools only as a rough back-up.** Take clear photos if it is safe and allowed, but do not sign if the translation is unclear, contradictory, or involves money, liability, an admission, detention, immigration, or your legal rights.
7. **If it is an airline or hotel, escalate once.** Ask for the duty manager or supervisor. Ask them to show the exact clause requiring your signature and to explain what happens if you do not sign immediately.
8. **If it is about charges or damage, slow it down.** Ask for an itemised amount, photos of the alleged damage, the policy or contract they rely on, and time to contact your insurer, card provider, travel company, or a translator.
9. **If it is official, police, immigration, or detention-related, repeat the same boundary.** Say: “I need an interpreter. I do not understand this document.” Ask how to contact a local lawyer, and ask the authorities to contact the nearest British embassy, high commission, or consulate if you want consular assistance.
10. **If you need urgent UK consular help abroad, use the official route.** Call the nearest British embassy, high commission, or consulate, or call the FCDO in the UK on +44 (0)20 7008 5000 and choose consular services for British nationals.
11. **Make a short record while details are fresh.** Note the time, place, names or badge numbers, what you were told, and whether a copy, translation, interpreter, supervisor, lawyer, or consular contact was offered. If safe and allowed, photograph the title page and any page with your name or signature line.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a complaint, claim on insurance, change hotel, change flight, or start a dispute.
- You do not need to solve the whole travel problem before you understand the document.
- Refunds, card disputes, formal reports, and follow-up emails can usually wait until you are away from the immediate pressure and have a copy.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to pause when someone asks you to sign something you cannot read. Asking for translation, time, and a copy is a normal way to protect yourself, not a refusal to cooperate.

## Scope note
These are first steps to slow the situation down and prevent a harmful signature. If the document relates to detention, alleged offences, immigration status, a large payment, injury, or a legal dispute, you may need urgent local legal advice, a qualified interpreter or translator, and consular support.

## Important note
This is general information for immediate harm-prevention, not legal advice, immigration advice, financial advice, travel advice, or professional translation. Local rules and consequences vary by country and situation.
