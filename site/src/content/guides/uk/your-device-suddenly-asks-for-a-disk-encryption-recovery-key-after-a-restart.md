# What to do if… your device suddenly asks for a disk-encryption recovery key after a restart (UK)

## Short answer
Initiate the official account recovery process through your email provider's website immediately and secure your secondary recovery methods.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links in "password reset" or "security alert" emails; navigate to the provider's official site manually.
- Do not repeatedly attempt old or guessed passwords, as this may trigger a permanent security lockout.
- Do not use the compromised password or variations of it for any other account.
- Do not ignore "forwarding" or "filter" settings after regaining access, as these are used to monitor your future mail.
- Do not use the compromised account to communicate with contacts until you have confirmed all other sessions are terminated.

## What to do now
1. **Access a known-secure device and connection.** If you suspect your primary device has malware—indicated by unusual pop-ups, slow performance, or unknown browser extensions—perform recovery from a different, clean device.
2. **Follow the official account recovery flow.** Navigate directly to the login page of your email provider (e.g., Gmail, Outlook, iCloud) and select "Forgot password" or "Account recovery."
   - Provide identity verification as requested (e.g., recovery codes, SMS codes, or security questions).
3. **Terminate all active sessions once access is restored.** Locate the security settings or "sign out of all devices" option to ensure the unauthorized user is disconnected.
4. **Update your password to a long, unique phrase.** Select a password consisting of at least 12 characters that you have never used on any other website.
5. **Verify and update your recovery information.** Check that the recovery mobile number and secondary email address are still yours and have not been altered.
6. **Remove unauthorized mailbox rules.** Inspect your settings for any new "forwarding," "filtering," or "auto-reply" rules that might send copies of your mail to an external address.
7. **Secure high-priority accounts linked to this email.** Change passwords and enable 2-step verification for your banking, government (HMRC/DWP), and primary social media accounts immediately.
8. **Forward any suspicious phishing emails that led to this incident.** Send the suspected phishing message to report@phishing.gov.uk to alert the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC).
9. **Report the incident to the national reporting service if money was stolen.** Contact Report Fraud (the service that replaced Action Fraud) online at reportfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040 if you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland.
   - If you are in Scotland, report via the Police Scotland online cybercrime form or call 101.
10. **Notify your employer if a work-related account or device is involved.** Contact your IT department or Data Protection Officer (DPO) to report the potential breach of organizational data.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific hacker or their location.
- You do not need to close the email account permanently if the recovery was successful and security settings were purged.
- You do not need to notify every contact in your address book; focus on those likely to be targeted for financial scams.
- You do not need to perform a full factory reset of your devices unless a security scan confirms persistent malware.

## Important reassurance
Account takeovers are frequently the result of automated mass-phishing or old data breaches and do not necessarily mean you were personally targeted. Regaining access and clearing persistence rules (like forwarding) effectively stops the immediate threat.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and recovery. It does not provide long-term cybersecurity auditing, legal advice regarding data loss, or device forensics.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only and does not constitute professional IT or legal advice. If your personal data was exposed by a third-party organization, consult the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO) website for guidance on your rights under the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025. Follow all official instructions provided by your email provider and your bank.
