# What to do if… you need to travel with cremated remains and are unsure about documentation or airport screening (UK)

## Short answer
Keep the death certificate and certificate of cremation with you, use a sealed container that can be X-rayed, and check the airline, departure airport, destination country, and any transit country before you travel.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume UK paperwork alone will be enough for an international route; departure, transit, and arrival countries can have different requirements.
- Don’t use a metal, crystal, or other hard-to-screen urn for air travel if you can avoid it.
- Don’t pack ashes in checked luggage just to avoid questions; only do this if your airline allows it and you accept the loss or damage risk.
- Don’t open a sealed urn or container at airport security; have the paperwork ready instead.
- Don’t rely only on phone photos if you can carry originals or certified paper copies.
- Don’t leave the airline check until the airport if your airline asks for advance notice.

## What to do now
1. **Put the core documents in one folder you can carry with you.**
   - Death certificate, or the official country-specific equivalent.
   - Certificate of cremation, or the official country-specific equivalent.
   - One or two paper copies, kept separately if you have them.
   - Phone photos as backup, not as the only copy.

2. **If a document is missing, ask the crematorium or funeral director now.**
   Say: “I need documentation for travelling with cremated remains.”
   Ask for the certificate of cremation or a certified copy. If available, also ask for a short letter confirming that the container holds cremated remains for the named person.

3. **Check the three route rules before you pack.**
   - Airline: search the airline’s own website for “ashes” or “cremated remains”.
   - Airport security: check your departure airport’s security page and UK aviation security guidance.
   - Countries: check the destination country’s rules, and any transit country’s rules, for entering, leaving, or transiting with human ashes.

4. **Choose a screening-friendly container.**
   Use a sealed, sturdy outer box or case. For airport security, a ceramic, plastic, or wooden container is usually safer than a metal or crystal urn because it is more likely to be screened clearly by X-ray.

5. **Keep the ashes under your control if your airline allows hand luggage.**
   Put the sealed container in hand luggage where allowed. If your airline only allows checked carriage, follow its packing rules and keep the documents with you, not inside checked luggage.

6. **Build in extra time at the airport.**
   Arrive earlier than usual. At check-in or security, calmly say: “I am travelling with cremated remains.” Keep the document folder ready in case staff need to see it.

7. **If you are bringing ashes back to the UK, separate the UK arrival question from the departure-country question.**
   You may not need a UK permit to bring ashes into the UK, but you will usually need documents when leaving the country where the person died. GOV.UK says you need to fill in a standard customs form when you arrive home.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today what will happen to the ashes at the destination.
- You do not need to buy a permanent urn now; a secure, sealed, screening-friendly travel container may be enough for the journey.
- You do not need to contact every possible office at once; start with the airline, the departure airport or aviation security guidance, and the destination or transit country rules.
- You do not need to explain your whole situation at the airport; a simple statement and the documents are enough.

## Important reassurance
This is a common bereavement travel problem, and it is reasonable to feel worried about getting one detail wrong. Having the certificates ready, using a container that can be screened, and checking the route rules reduces the chance of delay or distress at the airport.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Multi-stop routes, unusual containers, missing documents, or country-specific requirements may need help from the airline, crematorium, funeral director, embassy, consulate, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, customs, aviation, bereavement, or professional advice. Airline policies and country border rules can change and can vary by route, so check the rules that apply to your exact journey before travel.
