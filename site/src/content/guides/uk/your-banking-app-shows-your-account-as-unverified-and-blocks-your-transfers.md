# What to do if… your banking app shows your account as unverified and blocks your transfers (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank through a trusted route: the number on your bank card, the bank’s official website, or 159 if your bank is covered. Ask what exact verification step is needed and what temporary payment options are available if bills are due.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep retrying the same transfer again and again; it may trigger more security checks or make the problem harder to untangle.
- Don’t use “support” links from texts, emails, search adverts, social media posts, or direct messages about this issue.
- Don’t share one-time passcodes, card-reader codes, security answers, or app approval prompts with anyone.
- Don’t approve a prompt unless you started that action yourself.
- Don’t install screen-sharing or remote-access apps because someone claiming to be from the bank tells you to.
- Don’t move money to a “safe account” because someone is rushing, frightening, or pressuring you.

## What to do now
1. **Work out what is blocked and what still works.**  
   Check whether you can view your balance, receive money, pay by debit card, withdraw cash, use a standing order already set up, or use online banking in a browser. Write down any payments due in the next 24–72 hours, such as rent, mortgage, utilities, childcare, tax, wages, or debt payments.

2. **Do only low-risk app checks.**  
   Confirm the app is the official app from your phone’s app store. Update it if an update is available, force close it, restart your phone, and sign in again. Check that your phone’s date and time are set automatically. Try mobile data instead of Wi-Fi, or Wi-Fi instead of mobile data.

3. **Contact the bank safely.**  
   Use the phone number on the back of your card, the bank’s official website, or 159 if your bank is listed. Say: “After the app update, my account shows as unverified and transfers are blocked. Please tell me, as much as you can, whether this is a security hold, an identity check, missing information, or a technical issue. What exact step do you need from me to restore transfers?”

4. **Ask for a practical workaround if money needs to move today.**  
   Ask whether the bank can help you make the payment by telephone banking, in a branch, by card, through an existing bill-payment route, or by another safe method they can confirm. If you need cash for essentials, ask what cash access is still available.

5. **Warn urgent payees before a missed payment happens.**  
   If rent, a bill, wages, childcare, or another essential payment may be late, contact the payee now. Say your bank has blocked transfers after a verification problem, ask them to note it, and ask whether they can pause fees, retry later, or accept another payment method.

6. **Check for signs of fraud without spiralling.**  
   Look at recent transactions and pending payments. If you see anything you do not recognise, tell the bank immediately and ask them to secure the account before transfers are restored.

7. **Keep a simple record.**  
   Screenshot the “unverified” or blocked-transfer message, error code, app version, and time. Note who you contacted, when you contacted them, what they said, and any reference number.

8. **If access is not restored, make a formal complaint.**  
   Ask the bank to log a formal complaint about blocked payment access and give you the complaint reference. For certain payment-services complaints, firms normally have 15 business days to respond, or to explain why they cannot yet give a final response; they must then normally give a final response within 35 business days from when you first complained. If you are unhappy with the response, or the bank does not respond in time, you can take the complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to switch banks.
- You do not need to factory-reset your phone, buy a new device, or repeatedly delete and reinstall the app.
- You do not need to decide about legal options or ombudsman escalation while you are still trying to restore access.
- You do not need to explain the whole situation to everyone; focus only on urgent payees and the bank.

## Important reassurance
A blocked transfer or verification flag does not automatically mean the account is gone. Banks may temporarily restrict transfers while they check identity, security, fraud risk, or technical information. Calmly using a trusted contact route and keeping a clear record gives you the best chance of getting the block understood and fixed.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation, protect you from scams, and restore access to transfers. If the restriction continues, later decisions may need help from the bank’s complaints team, the Financial Ombudsman Service, a debt adviser, or another suitable specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not financial, legal, technical, or professional advice. Banks may restrict accounts or payments for security, fraud-prevention, technical, or regulatory reasons and may not be able to share every detail immediately. If anyone pressures you to move money, share codes, approve prompts, or install remote-access software, stop and contact your bank through a trusted route.
