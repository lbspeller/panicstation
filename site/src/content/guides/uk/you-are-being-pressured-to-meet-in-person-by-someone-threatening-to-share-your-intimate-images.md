# What to do if… you are being pressured to meet in person by someone threatening to share your intimate images (UK)

## Short answer
Do not meet them. Get to a safer pause, stop handling the threat alone, and bring in a trusted person, police, or a specialist support service.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to an in-person meeting to try to make the threat stop.
- Do not send more images, videos, live calls, or “verification” selfies.
- Do not pay money or agree to new demands.
- Do not argue, bargain, or try to prove anything in a live conversation.
- Do not delete chats, call logs, usernames, numbers, or payment details in panic.
- Do not share the images yourself to “get ahead of it” or punish yourself.

## What to do now
1. **Create immediate physical safety distance.**  
   If you are outside, or they know where you are, move towards other people, a staffed place, or someone you trust. If you feel at immediate risk of harm, call **999**.

2. **Do not meet, and reduce the real-time pressure.**  
   If it feels safe to reply, send one brief written message such as: “I am not meeting you. Do not contact me again.” Then mute notifications if you can. If replying feels unsafe, do not reply; get support first. Blocking can wait until you have saved the key messages or a support service has helped you decide the safest next step.

3. **Keep a simple record of the threat.**  
   Take screenshots of their username or number, the demand to meet, any threat to share images, and any proof they have the images. Note the date and time if you can. Do not forward the images to friends, group chats, or the person threatening you.

4. **Ask one trusted person to sit with you now.**  
   Show them the message if you can. Ask them to stay with you while you report, contact support, or change settings so you are not isolated with the pressure.

5. **Use the right specialist route for your age and the image.**  
   If you are **18+** and the images were taken when you were **18+**, contact the **Revenge Porn Helpline** for confidential UK support and help with reporting or removal. You can also use **StopNCII.org** if you still have the image or video and meet its criteria. If you are **under 18**, or the image was taken when you were under 18, use **Childline Report Remove** and consider reporting to **CEOP**.

6. **Report the threats if you feel able to.**  
   If there is danger now, call **999**. Otherwise, contact police on **101** or through your local police force’s online reporting route. Use plain words: “They have intimate images of me and are pressuring me to meet in person.”

7. **If you are under 18, tell a safe adult now.**  
   Tell a parent, carer, teacher, college safeguarding lead, youth worker, or another safe adult. This is not something you have to manage alone, and you should not have to meet or negotiate with the person.

8. **If there has been sexual contact, assault, or you fear this may happen, contact urgent specialist care.**  
   A Sexual Assault Referral Centre can offer confidential medical, practical, and emotional support. You do not have to decide about police reporting before asking for help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a formal police report or support an investigation.
- You do not need to write a perfect statement before asking for help.
- You do not need to collect every detail before contacting a helpline, police, CEOP, or a SARC.
- You do not need to warn everyone, change your whole life, or answer every message today.
- You do not need to solve where the images came from before you focus on safety and support.

## Important reassurance
Being threatened with intimate images is a coercion tactic, and the pressure to comply can feel intense. Not meeting, getting another person involved, and using specialist support are steady first steps that reduce the person’s control over what you do next.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate risk and prevent irreversible choices. Later decisions about takedowns, reporting, safety planning, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call **999**. If you are unsure what applies to you, a specialist helpline, police, CEOP, or a Sexual Assault Referral Centre can talk through options without pressure.
