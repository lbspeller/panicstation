# What to do if… you realise you boarded the wrong train or bus (UK)

## Short answer
Get to the next safe stopping point and ask staff how to correct the journey. Acting early, keeping proof of your ticket or payment, and not trying to force a stop are the safest ways to reduce avoidable ticket problems.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pull an emergency alarm or handle just because you are on the wrong service.
- Don’t try to get off between stops, open doors yourself, cross tracks, enter depots, or use non-public areas.
- Don’t avoid staff or wait to be challenged if your ticket may not be valid for the service you are on.
- Don’t argue with revenue or ticket staff; keep the explanation calm, short, and factual.
- Don’t delete tickets, receipts, booking confirmations, screenshots, or payment records.
- Don’t tap in, tap out, or re-enter gates repeatedly to try to “undo” a contactless or Oyster mistake.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm what you are on:** Check the destination display, next stop announcement, route number, platform board history, or your live map. Decide whether you are going the wrong way, on the wrong route, or on a faster service that will not stop where you expected.

2. **Aim for the next safe stop:**
   - **Train:** get off at the next safe station if that is the quickest sensible correction point. If the next stop is far away, look for on-board staff and ask what to do before you get there.
   - **Bus:** stay on until the next normal bus stop. Ask the driver for the best stop to change when the bus is stopped or it is otherwise safe to speak.

3. **Tell staff early:** Use a simple line such as: “I boarded the wrong train or bus by mistake at [place]. I am trying to correct it now. What is the official way to get back on the right route or pay any difference?”

4. **Keep your ticket and payment proof ready:** Keep open or available:
   - your ticket, railcard, booking confirmation, or barcode;
   - the card, device, Oyster card, or app you used to pay;
   - the time and place you boarded;
   - the service number, train destination, route number, or vehicle number if visible.

5. **If your ticket may not be valid, ask before travelling further:** Some rail tickets are restricted by train company, route, time, destination, or booked service. You may be told to pay the difference, buy another ticket, leave the train at a station, or follow a penalty fare or unpaid fare process. Follow staff instructions and keep any notice or receipt.

6. **If you used contactless or Oyster:** On trains, Tube, DLR, London Overground, Elizabeth line, and some National Rail services, an incomplete or wrong journey can create a maximum fare or other charge. On buses and trams you normally only touch in. After you are safe and off the wrong service, check your journey history through the official account or ask station staff.

7. **Reset the route before moving again:** At the stop or station, check the correct direction, final destination, platform, route number, and stopping pattern before boarding. If there is a ticket office, help point, driver, conductor, station staff member, or official journey planner, ask or check before you board again.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, appeal, or request a refund.
- You do not need to write a full explanation while you are still travelling in the wrong direction.
- You do not need to re-plan the whole day; first get to a safe stop, confirm the correct route, and keep your ticket or payment proof.
- You can check refund, contactless, Oyster, or penalty fare processes later, using any notice, receipt, account record, or journey history if one exists.

## Important reassurance
Boarding the wrong train or bus is a common mistake, especially when services share platforms, routes change, stops are crowded, or announcements are unclear. Correcting it early and calmly is usually easier than rushing into another boarding mistake.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: they are to help you stop, stay safe, correct the route, and preserve the information you may need. Later decisions about refunds, complaints, appeals, or disputed fares may need operator guidance, passenger support, or specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, transport, or professional advice. Ticket validity, penalty fares, refunds, and contactless charges depend on the operator, location, ticket type, route, and what staff record at the time.
