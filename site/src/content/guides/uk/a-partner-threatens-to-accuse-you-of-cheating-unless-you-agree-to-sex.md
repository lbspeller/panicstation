# What to do if… a partner threatens to accuse you of cheating unless you agree to sex (UK)

## Short answer
You are not required to agree to sexual activity to stop a threat or blackmail. Move to a safer location immediately and contact a confidential sexual violence or domestic abuse service to plan your next steps.

## Do not do these things
- Do not comply with the demand "just once" to make the threat stop, as this often increases the risk of further coercion.
- Do not attempt to prove your innocence or argue against the false accusation in the moment.
- Do not agree to meet the person privately to negotiate or "talk them out" of the threat.
- Do not delete messages, call logs, or recordings of the threat in a panic.
- Do not send aggressive or highly emotional responses that could be used to further the threat.
- Do not assume you must involve the police to receive confidential support or medical care.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safe physical distance immediately.** Leave the room, leave the building, or move to a public space with other people present.
   - If you are in immediate danger and cannot speak safely, call **999** and press **55** when prompted (the Silent Solution).
2. **Issue one final refusal and stop the interaction.** Use a neutral, firm statement and do not respond to follow-up messages.
   - Example: "I am not having sex. I will not be threatened. I am stopping this conversation now."
3. **Contact a specialist 24/7 helpline for confidential advice.** These services are independent of the police and will not pressure you to report.
   - **England and Wales:** Call the Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line on **0808 500 2222**.
   - **Scotland:** Call the Rape Crisis Scotland Helpline on **08088 010 302** (daily 5pm to midnight).
   - **Northern Ireland:** Call the Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline on **0808 802 1414**.
4. **Preserve evidence of the threat if it is safe to do so.** Save screenshots of messages or recordings of voicemails to a secure cloud folder or email them to a trusted friend.
   - Do not do this if the person has access to your phone and could find the evidence, as this may increase your risk.
5. **Access medical or forensic support via a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC).** You can self-refer for medical care, emergency contraception, and evidence collection without police involvement.
   - **In England:** Find your nearest centre at nhs.uk/sarcs.
   - **In Scotland:** Call the 24/7 national self-referral line on **0800 148 88 88**.
6. **Use domestic abuse services for safety planning if you live with the person.** The National Domestic Abuse Helpline (England/Wales: **0808 2000 247**) can help with tech safety and emergency housing options.
7. **Contact one safe person for immediate monitoring.** Ask a friend or relative to stay on the phone with you or meet you in a public place while you distance yourself from the person making threats.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to press charges or make a police report.
- You do not need to define exactly what the relationship "is" or "was" right now.
- You do not need to explain your situation to friends, family, or the public to "get ahead" of the threat.
- You do not need to decide if the person's behavior "counts" as a crime before seeking support.

## Important reassurance
Consent obtained through threats, blackmail, or intimidation is not valid consent. Feeling frozen, confused, or unsure how to respond is a normal reaction to coercion. You are entitled to support regardless of whether physical force was used or whether you have complied with demands in the past.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute threat. It does not replace tailored safety planning from a professional domestic abuse or sexual violence advisor.
