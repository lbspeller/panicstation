# What to do if… you are asked to handle the phone and accounts of someone who has died (UK)

## Short answer
Secure the phone, preserve what is already there, and do not try to break into accounts. Use the mobile provider’s bereavement route and each account provider’s official deceased-user process.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t guess passcodes, brute-force logins, or use password reset flows just to get access.
- Don’t pretend to be the person when speaking to banks, mobile providers, email providers, or other services.
- Don’t hand an unlocked phone around for people to check; messages, photos, settings, and accounts can be changed or deleted by mistake.
- Don’t mass-cancel from inside apps first; use official bereavement, closure, transfer, or notification routes.
- Don’t throw away SIM cards, old phones, paper bills, letters, or packaging with account details.
- Don’t use two-factor authentication codes sent to the phone unless the provider has told the authorised person to do that.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the phone like it is a key.** Put it somewhere safe, keep it charged, and do not change settings, delete messages, sign out of accounts, or remove the SIM. If the phone is already unlocked, only note obvious details without digging: the phone number, network name, visible email address, and any urgent security alerts.

2. **Make a short custody note.** On paper, record the date and time you were given the phone, who gave it to you, where it is being kept, and anything urgent you can see without opening private content, such as repeated bank texts, security codes, missed calls, or account warning messages.

3. **Check what role you have before acting.** Ask the family whether there is a will, whether an executor is known, or whether someone is dealing with the estate. If you are not the executor, administrator, or agreed family contact, your safest role for now is usually safekeeping the phone and passing information to the person handling the estate.

4. **Reduce phone-driven risks without logging in.** If two-factor codes or security alerts keep arriving, do not use the codes; write down the dates, times, sender names, and what the message appears to relate to. If unknown callers keep ringing, do not answer as the person who died; let calls go to voicemail and note repeated numbers.

5. **Contact the mobile provider’s bereavement team.** Ask for the provider’s bereavement process and say you need to prevent unauthorised use and stop ongoing charges. Larger providers often have specialist bereavement teams. They may ask for the account holder’s full name, address, telephone number, account number if known, and the death certificate or its number. If money, complaints, refunds, or estate issues are involved, they may also ask for evidence that you have authority to deal with the account.

6. **Ask the mobile provider what can be done immediately.** Depending on the account, ask whether they can close it, pause it, transfer it, keep an important number, or protect it while documents are gathered. If the phone or router was supplied under a contract, ask whether anything must be returned before disposing of equipment.

7. **Use official deceased-user routes for major online accounts.** For Apple, ask whether the person had a Legacy Contact access key or estate-planning note, then use Apple’s own deceased-account process. Apple may need legal documentation and cannot remove a passcode lock without erasing the device. For Google, use Google’s deceased-user request route; Google says it cannot provide passwords or other login details and reviews requests before acting.

8. **Use UK notification routes where they fit.** In England, Scotland, and Wales, Tell Us Once can tell most government organisations after the death has been registered, or after a coroner has issued the relevant interim paperwork. Northern Ireland uses separate government routes. For financial firms, the Death Notification Service can notify participating member organisations without logging into the person’s accounts.

9. **Choose one calm family contact.** Agree one person to hold documents, speak to providers, and record what has been done. This helps prevent duplicate calls, mixed instructions, accidental closures, and arguments about who changed what.

## What can wait
- You do not need to unlock the phone today to find every account.
- You do not need to close every subscription immediately; focus first on preventing misuse and stopping avoidable charges through official routes.
- You do not need to decide today what happens to photos, messages, email, social media, or the person’s online presence.
- You do not need to solve the whole estate before asking providers what their bereavement process is.

## Important reassurance
Being asked to handle a phone and accounts after someone dies can feel like being handed everything at once. The safest first step is not to force access; it is to preserve the phone, avoid impersonation, stop obvious risks, and use official routes one at a time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about the estate, refunds, data access, photos, contracts, social media, and disputes may need help from the executor or administrator, the relevant provider, or a specialist adviser.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, technical, bereavement, or professional advice. Account access after death depends on your role, the provider’s rules, the device settings, and the documents available. If you are unsure, default to safekeeping and official bereavement routes rather than attempting access.
