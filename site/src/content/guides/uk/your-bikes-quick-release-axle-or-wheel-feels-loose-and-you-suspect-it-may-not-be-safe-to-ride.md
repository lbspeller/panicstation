# What to do if… your bike’s quick-release, axle, or wheel feels loose and you suspect it may not be safe to ride (UK)

## Short answer
Stop riding as soon as you safely can and treat the bike as unsafe until the wheel is secure and stays secure. If you cannot confidently secure it, walk the bike or use other transport.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep riding to see if it settles; a loose wheel can shift or detach.
- Do not tighten a quick-release by spinning the lever like a wingnut; the lever is meant to clamp.
- Do not grab the front brake abruptly if you suspect the front wheel is loose; stop smoothly and get off.
- Do not assume it is only brake rub until the wheel is checked and seated.
- Do not put your fingers near a disc rotor or caliper while checking; they can be hot or sharp.
- Do not ride again just because one part seemed to tighten once.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Slow down smoothly and stop away from traffic, such as on a pavement, verge, lay-by, or driveway. If the bike feels unstable, get off and walk it immediately.
2. **Check whether the wheel is visibly or physically loose.**
   - Lift one end of the bike slightly and try to rock the wheel side-to-side at the rim.
   - Look at whether the wheel sits centred between the fork legs or frame stays.
   - Check whether the tyre is rubbing the frame or fork, or whether the brake rotor looks pulled to one side.
3. **Identify how the wheel is held on, then only secure it if you know the method.**
   - **Quick-release lever:** open the lever fully, make sure the axle is seated in the dropouts, tighten the adjusting nut until there is clear resistance as the lever closes, then close the lever firmly with your palm. It should clamp decisively. Do not secure it by turning the lever round and round.
   - **Thru-axle:** make sure the wheel is seated, then tighten the axle according to the bike, axle markings, or manufacturer instructions. If there is a lever, put it in its proper closed or stowed position so it cannot unwind.
   - **Axle nuts:** make sure the wheel is seated, then tighten both nuts firmly and evenly with the correct spanner.
4. **Stop if the wheel will not stay secure.** If the lever closes too easily, will not close, fouls the disc rotor, the axle will not thread properly, or the nuts cannot be tightened with the right tool, do not ride.
5. **Re-check before moving off.**
   - Spin the wheel and check that the tyre does not rub the frame or fork.
   - Apply the brakes gently while rocking the bike forward and back; the wheel should not shift.
   - Visually confirm that the quick-release is closed, the thru-axle is threaded and closed, or the nuts are seated.
6. **Do only a very cautious check in a safe traffic-free space if it now seems secure.** Walk the bike first, then make a very slow straight roll if appropriate, stop, and re-check the wheel retention. If anything loosens again, stop using the bike.
7. **If the bike was left unattended, check both wheels before riding later.** Check the front wheel, rear wheel, and seatpost clamp if fitted. If anything looks disturbed or damaged, do not ride until it has been checked.
8. **If you crashed or hit your head, use NHS help routes.** For non-life-threatening uncertainty after a head impact, use NHS 111. For severe symptoms, a serious collision, or immediate danger, call 999.
9. **If a part may be defective, take simple photos before repair if it is safe to do so.** Photograph the lever, axle, dropout area, and visible damage. Note the bike or wheel brand and model for a mechanic.
10. **If you believe it may be an unsafe product issue, use UK consumer safety routes later.** Check GOV.UK product recalls and alerts. You can also report consumer safety concerns through Citizens Advice, which can pass information to Trading Standards.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the problem is hub bearings, dropouts, the skewer, the axle, or the frame right now.
- You do not need to decide on upgrades, replacement wheels, locking skewers, or a different axle system today.
- You do not need to contact a seller immediately unless doing so is needed to avoid using the bike; the priority is not riding an unsafe wheel.

## Important reassurance
A wheel that feels loose is a valid reason to stop. Walking the bike, asking for a lift, or taking public transport is a sensible way to prevent a small mechanical problem becoming a crash.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the moment and prevent a crash. A bike shop or qualified mechanic may need to check for bent axles, damaged dropouts, hub bearing play, skewer wear, brake alignment, or compatibility issues.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not professional mechanical, medical, legal, or consumer-rights advice. If you cannot confidently secure the wheel and confirm it stays secure, do not ride.
