# What to do if… you are being pressured to keep a sexual relationship secret to protect someone from consequences (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and prioritise your safety and support. You do not have to agree to secrecy to protect someone else from consequences, especially if you feel trapped, unsafe, guilty, or unable to say no.

## Do not do these things
- Do not promise secrecy on the spot to calm them down.
- Do not sign anything or send messages saying you consented if you do not freely want to.
- Do not meet them alone to talk it through if they have pressured, threatened, guilt-tripped, or isolated you.
- Do not delete messages, photos, notes, or call logs in a panic if you may later want support understanding what happened.
- Do not confront them in writing if you are worried they may retaliate, escalate, or share private content.
- Do not assume it “doesn’t count” because you agreed sometimes, were in a relationship, or it was not physically violent.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause first.** If you feel in immediate danger, call **999**. If you call 999 from a mobile and cannot speak, press **55** or tap when prompted so the call is treated as a genuine emergency.
2. **Put one sentence around what is happening.** For example: “I am being pressured to keep a sexual relationship secret so they avoid consequences.” This can help you stay steady when someone is pushing you to minimise it.
3. **Contact a specialist support service before deciding anything big.** If something sexual happened without your consent, or you are not sure, the **24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line** can support people aged 16+ in England and Wales on **0808 500 2222** or by online chat. It is not an emergency service; use 999 for immediate danger or urgent medical help.
4. **Use domestic abuse support if this is part of control, isolation, threats, or an intimate or family relationship.** You can contact the **24-hour National Domestic Abuse Helpline** on **0808 2000 247**.
5. **Tell one safe person, in a small and controlled way.** Choose someone unlikely to contact the other person. You can say: “I am dealing with something sexual where I am being pressured to keep it secret. I do not need you to fix it; I need you to stay with me while I get support.”
6. **Make contact safer.** If you need to reply, keep it short: “I am not discussing this. Do not contact me right now.” If it is safe, mute or block them, switch off read receipts, avoid being alone with them, and avoid predictable places where they may find you.
7. **Keep options open without pressuring yourself to report.** If it is safe, keep existing messages, photos, notes, and call logs rather than deleting them in panic. You do not have to decide now whether to use them.
8. **Get medical support if there may be pregnancy, STIs, injury, drugging, pain, bleeding, or you feel unwell.** You can use NHS 111 or your local urgent health route, contact a GP, or use an NHS sexual health clinic or local sexual health service. You do not need to decide what label fits before seeking care.
9. **If you are under 18, or the other person has authority over you, get youth-specific help.** This includes situations involving a teacher, coach, employer, carer, faith leader, supervisor, or another person with power over your choices. Childline supports children and young people on **0800 1111** and online.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide right now whether to report to police, make a formal complaint, or prove anything.
- You do **not** need to find the exact label, such as assault, coercion, abuse, cheating, exploitation, or a bad relationship.
- You do **not** need to confront them, negotiate, or give closure today.
- You do **not** need to protect their reputation, job, relationship, family situation, or future before protecting your own safety.
- You do **not** need to make a final relationship decision in the next hour. Stabilise first.

## Important reassurance
Being pressured into secrecy because someone else could face consequences can be a control tactic. Feeling confused, guilty, numb, frightened, or pulled between protecting them and protecting yourself can happen after pressure or coercion. Asking for support does not force you to take any particular next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stabilise, reduce immediate risk, and keep your options open. Later decisions about reporting, school or workplace action, legal steps, medical follow-up, or longer-term safety planning may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal advice, medical diagnosis, counselling, safeguarding advice, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If your phone or device is not safe to use, try to reach a safer place first or use a trusted person’s device.
