# What to do if… you receive an unexpected message claiming your phone number or email is linked to a case (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and treat the notice as unverified until you confirm it through an official route you find yourself, not through any link, phone number, QR code, or reply address in the message. Do not answer case questions, send ID, or pay anything while you are still verifying it.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, open attachments, scan QR codes, or install “secure reply” apps from the notice.
- Don’t call back a number from the message or trust caller ID; it can be spoofed.
- Don’t confirm your date of birth, address, bank details, passwords, one-time passcodes, or ID documents.
- Don’t pay any “fee”, “fine”, “clearance”, “bail”, or “verification deposit” to make the case go away.
- Don’t get pulled into a long chat or call to prove innocence or help the investigation on the spot.
- Don’t delete the notice yet; keep it so you can verify or report it.

## What to do now
1. **Stop and record what arrived.** Take screenshots showing the sender address or number, message text, links, attachments, reference numbers, names used, date, and time.
2. **Separate the notice from the action it demands.** A real matter can be checked through official routes. Pressure phrases such as “must respond today”, “final warning”, “warrant”, or demands for payment can be used in impersonation scams, so verify before acting.
3. **Verify police contact through a route you choose.** Use Police.uk or the relevant police force’s official website to find a contact route, or call 101 for a non-emergency enquiry. If someone is in immediate danger, a crime is happening now, or you feel at risk, call 999.
4. **Ask only authenticity questions at first.** You can say: “I have received a message saying my phone number or email is linked to a case. I am not using the contact details in the message. Can you tell me the safe way to check whether this contact is genuine?” Be aware they may not be able to discuss case details.
5. **If it claims to be from a court or public body, find the organisation yourself.** Search the official website or GOV.UK where relevant. For court contact details, use GOV.UK’s court finder for England and Wales, the Scottish Courts and Tribunals Service for Scotland, or the Northern Ireland Courts and Tribunals Service for Northern Ireland.
6. **If police ask to question you or attend a station, move it into a formal channel.** You can say: “I am willing to cooperate, but I am not answering questions by text, email, or an unverified call. Please arrange this through official channels.”
7. **Ask about legal advice before any formal police interview.** If you are questioned at a police station, you have the right to free independent legal advice. You can ask for the duty solicitor or ask the police to contact a solicitor.
8. **Report the suspicious notice.** Forward suspicious emails to report@phishing.gov.uk. Forward suspicious texts to 7726. If you are in England, Wales or Northern Ireland and you think fraud or cyber crime is involved, report it to Report Fraud. If you are in Scotland, report fraud or cyber crime to Police Scotland by calling 101.
9. **Protect the account or number named in the notice.** Change the password for the email account involved, turn on two-step verification, check for forwarding rules you did not set, and check whether recovery email addresses or phone numbers have been changed.
10. **If you already replied, clicked, paid, or shared information, stop contact now.** Change affected passwords, contact your bank or card provider directly if payment details or money were involved, contact your mobile provider if you suspect a SIM swap or number takeover, and include what you shared when you report it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a full explanation of what happened right now.
- You do not need to decide whether you are a suspect, witness, victim, or unrelated contact based only on a message.
- You do not need to hand over a phone, laptop, email account, or passwords because a message demanded it.
- You do not need to negotiate with the sender or clear it up immediately.
- You do not need to respond through the notice while you are still checking whether it is genuine.

## Important reassurance
A notice that mentions police, courts, prison, probation, or a case can feel frightening. Verifying first is a sensible protective step. If the contact is genuine, an official route can tell you how to respond safely; if it is fake, pausing helps stop the sender from rushing you into a mistake.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce panic, preserve options, and move the situation into a verifiable process. If the matter becomes a real legal, police, fraud, account security, or financial issue, you may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cyber security, therapeutic, or professional advice. Processes can vary across the UK and by the organisation involved. If there is an immediate threat or emergency, call 999.
