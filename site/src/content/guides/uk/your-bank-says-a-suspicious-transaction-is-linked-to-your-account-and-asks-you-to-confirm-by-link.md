# What to do if… your bank says a suspicious transaction is linked to your account and asks you to “confirm” by link (UK)

## Short answer
Do not click the link or provide any information. Contact your bank directly using a verified phone number from the back of your card or the official banking app to check your account status.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click any links in the message, even if the sender name appears as your bank or the message is in a previous genuine thread.
- Don’t call any phone numbers provided in the text or follow prompts to "press a key" if the message was followed by an automated call.
- Don’t share your PIN, full password, or One-Time Passcodes (OTP) with anyone, including people claiming to be from the bank's fraud department.
- Don’t move money to a "safe account" or "holding account" if instructed to do so over the phone or via message.
- Don’t assume the message is real because it contains your name, partial account numbers, or correct transaction amounts.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the message and do not interact with it further.** Take a screenshot for evidence later, but do not click any links, reply to the sender, or forward it to anyone except the reporting service.
2. **End any active calls and clear your phone line.** If you are currently speaking to someone who directed you to the message, hang up immediately.
   - Wait at least 5 minutes before making a new call, or use a completely different phone (like a friend's or a landline) to ensure the scammer has not "trapped" your line.
3. **Contact your bank using the 159 hotline or a trusted number.** **Dial 159** from your phone to be connected directly to your bank's fraud department (this service covers over 99% of UK retail bank accounts).
   - Alternatively, call the number on the back of your physical debit or credit card.
   - State clearly: "I have received a suspicious message with a link about a transaction I do not recognise."
4. **Request an immediate security freeze if you suspect a compromise.** Ask the bank agent to freeze your cards and temporarily block online banking access while they investigate.
   - Confirm there are no new "trusted devices" or "new payees" recently added to your account.
5. **Treat your credentials as compromised if you have already clicked the link.** Tell your bank agent immediately if you entered any details or clicked the link.
   - Use a separate, clean device to change your online banking password and any other accounts (like email) that used the same password.
6. **Report the phishing attempt to the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC).** **Forward suspicious text messages to 7726** (this is a free service that alerts mobile providers).
   - Forward suspicious emails or screenshots of the link to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.
7. **Report the incident to the national fraud reporting centre.** **If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report to Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.
   - **If you are in Scotland, report the crime to Police Scotland** by calling 101 or using their online reporting form.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to move to a different bank or close your accounts permanently right now.
- You do not need to identify the exact source of the data breach or how they got your number.
- You do not need to respond to the scammer or "warn" them you have reported them.

## Important reassurance
Receiving these messages is not a sign that you have done anything wrong; they are sent to millions of people daily using automated systems. By stopping and using a trusted contact method like 159, you are taking the most effective step possible to protect your finances.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for responding to suspicious banking alerts. It does not cover long-term debt recovery, legal disputes with banks, or complex identity theft restoration.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute financial or legal advice. Always follow the specific security instructions provided by your bank's official fraud team.
