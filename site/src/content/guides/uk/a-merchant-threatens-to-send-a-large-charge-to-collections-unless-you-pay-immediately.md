# What to do if… a merchant threatens to send a large charge to collections unless you pay immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Do not pay immediately under pressure. Terminate the contact, request all details in writing, and verify the merchant’s claims using an independent contact method you find yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Don't provide bank details, card numbers, or one-time passcodes (OTPs) during an unsolicited call or message.
- Don't agree that you owe the debt or promise to pay until you have verified the breakdown in writing.
- Don't use contact details provided in a suspicious text or email to verify the claim.
- Don't use unusual payment methods such as bank transfers to individual accounts, crypto, or gift cards.
- Don't ignore the situation entirely; legitimate errors require a formal dispute to prevent credit score impact.

## What to do now
1. **End the high-pressure interaction immediately.**
   - If on a call, state: "I require a full written breakdown of this charge sent to my registered address; I will not discuss payment until I have reviewed it."
   - Hang up or close the chat window without waiting for a rebuttal.
2. **Verify the merchant and account status through a trusted channel.**
   - Navigate to the merchant's official website manually or use a phone number from a previous, verified statement.
   - Ask the official customer service department if there is an outstanding balance on your account and what it relates to.
3. **Submit a formal dispute in writing if the charge is unrecognised or incorrect.**
   - Send an email or letter stating: "I dispute this charge [Reference Number] as it is [unrecognised / incorrect / for services not received]. Please investigate and suspend collection activity while this is resolved."
   - Retain a copy of this correspondence and any delivery receipts.
4. **Invoke your right to a pause in recovery if dealing with regulated debt.**
   - If the debt relates to consumer credit (like a store card or loan), refer to the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) CONC 7.14 rules.
   - Explicitly tell the firm: "I am disputing this debt on valid grounds; under FCA rules, you must suspend recovery steps and investigate."
5. **Contact your bank to secure your account if you shared details.**
   - If you provided any financial information during the pressure call, call your bank's fraud department immediately.
   - Ask about a "Chargeback" for disputed debit card payments or "Section 75" protection for credit card payments between £100 and £30,000.
6. **Apply for the "Breathing Space" scheme if you are in financial distress.**
   - Contact a free debt advice provider (such as StepChange or National Debtline) to see if you qualify for the Debt Respite Scheme.
   - If eligible, this provides a 60-day legal pause on most enforcement action and contact from creditors.
7. **Report suspected fraudulent threats to the national authorities.**
   - In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, use the "Report Fraud" service (reportfraud.police.uk) or call 0300 123 2040.
   - In Scotland, report the matter to Police Scotland by calling 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to pay the full amount right now.
- You do not need to respond to further automated payment reminders once a formal dispute is logged.
- You do not need to calculate a long-term repayment plan until the validity of the debt is confirmed.
- You do not need to hire a solicitor for the initial dispute phase.

## Important reassurance
It is a common tactic for both aggressive legitimate merchants and scammers to use "immediate" deadlines to cause panic. Requesting written proof is a standard legal right in the UK. Legitimate businesses are required to provide a breakdown of charges and cannot legally penalise you for taking a few days to verify an unexpected bill.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling high-pressure payment demands. It does not cover long-term debt management or court-level legal defense.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Laws regarding debt collection and consumer rights differ between England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. If you are struggling with debt, seek free advice from a regulated provider.
