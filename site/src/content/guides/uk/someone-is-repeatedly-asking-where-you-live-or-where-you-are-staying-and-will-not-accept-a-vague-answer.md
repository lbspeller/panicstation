# What to do if… someone is repeatedly asking where you live or where you are staying and will not accept a vague answer (UK)

## Short answer
Do not provide your address or accommodation details under any circumstances. Immediately terminate the conversation, move to a staffed or crowded public area, and seek assistance from staff or the police.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide "partial" information such as a street name, a nearby landmark, or the name of your hotel.
- Do not allow the person to walk you to your car, home, or accommodation to "ensure you get back safely."
- Do not attempt to justify, explain, or argue your refusal to share your location.
- Do not enter a private space, taxi, or vehicle with the individual to resolve the interaction.
- Do not show the person anything on your phone screen, including maps, booking confirmations, or transport apps.
- Do not use physical force or aggressive language unless it is absolutely necessary for immediate self-defence.

## What to do now
1. **State your boundary once and immediately physically distance yourself.**  
   Use a direct statement such as "I do not share that information" or "I am not comfortable answering that," then turn away and walk toward other people.
2. **Move into a staffed, well-lit, or crowded environment immediately.**  
   Go toward a bar, reception desk, security station, or a busy retail shop where employees are visible and can intervene.
3. **Alert a third party or use a safety scheme if available.**  
   - If you are in a licensed venue (pub, club, bar), ask for "Angela" at the bar to signal you feel unsafe.
   - Tell staff plainly: "This person is following me and won't stop asking for my address; I need a safe place to wait."
4. **Call 999 and use the "Silent Solution" if you cannot speak safely.**  
   If the individual is still present and you feel in immediate danger, dial 999; if you cannot speak, wait for the operator's prompts and press 55 to signal an emergency.
5. **Contact the hotel or accommodation provider to secure your room details.**  
   If you are staying at a hotel, call the front desk and instruct them not to confirm your presence or share your room number with any callers or visitors.
6. **Arrange a secure exit that does not reveal your destination.**  
   Ask security or a friend to walk you to a taxi or your vehicle, or wait inside the building until your transport has arrived directly at the door.
7. **Record the details of the interaction once you are in a locked, safe space.**  
   Note the time, location, the person's physical description, exactly what was asked, and any identifying features of their vehicle or clothing.
8. **Contact the National Stalking Helpline for specialist safety planning.**  
   Call 0808 802 0300 (Monday/Wednesday 09:30–20:00; Tuesday/Thursday/Friday 09:30–16:00) for advice on persistent unwanted contact.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a formal police report (101) in this moment; focus only on reaching a secure location.
- You do not need to check your social media privacy settings or block the individual until you are physically safe.
- You do not need to determine if the person's intent was "malicious" or just "awkward" before seeking help.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a social pressure to be polite, but a person who ignores a vague answer is violating a boundary. Prioritising your physical safety over social comfort is a necessary and appropriate response to persistent questioning.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate encounter involving persistent or coercive questioning about your location. It does not cover long-term stalking investigations or legal injunctions.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and does not constitute legal advice. If you believe you are being followed or are in danger, contact the police immediately. In an emergency, always call 999.
