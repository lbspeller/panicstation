# What to do if… water is leaking around a skylight or roof window during rain (UK)

## Short answer
Keep people away from the drip area, contain the water from a dry position, and switch off power at the consumer unit if water is near lights, sockets, wiring, appliances, or you are unsure.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go onto the roof in rain, darkness, or windy conditions, even just to check.
- Do not touch wet light fittings, ceiling roses, smoke alarms, switches, sockets, or appliances.
- Do not run extension leads, chargers, or appliances through wet areas temporarily.
- Do not stand in water to reach the consumer unit or any electrical switch.
- Do not poke holes in a bulging ceiling to drain it; keep clear instead.
- Do not start outside sealant work or permanent repairs during the storm.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safer.** Move people and pets away from the leak area. Move rugs or mats that could slip, and put towels around the wet edge so nobody walks into it by accident.

2. **Deal with electricity before the leak.**
   - If water is dripping near ceiling lights, smoke alarms, sockets, visible wiring, downlights, or any appliance, switch off the affected circuit at the consumer unit if you can do this from a dry, safe position.
   - If you cannot confidently isolate one circuit, switch off the main switch if it is safe and dry to reach.
   - If the consumer unit is wet, sparking, buzzing, hot, or unsafe to reach, stay away from it and get urgent help.

3. **Contain the water from inside.**
   - Put a bucket, washing-up bowl, or large pan under the main drip.
   - Put a towel inside the container to reduce splashing.
   - If drips are spreading, use plastic sheeting or bin bags taped to dry surfaces to guide water into the container. Do not tape anything to a wet fitting, cable, or smoke alarm.

4. **Reduce fast damage.** Move electronics, paperwork, soft furnishings, and anything valuable away from the drip line. If water is running down a wall, pull furniture forward so it is not touching the wet surface.

5. **Only check above the ceiling if it is clearly safe.**
   - If you can reach a loft hatch from inside, have dry, stable footing, and can see a boarded route, you may look with a torch.
   - Do not step on wet plasterboard, wet insulation, or unboarded ceiling areas.
   - Do not touch wiring, junction boxes, downlights, or cables. If water is near electrics, back out and keep power off.
   - If it is safe and clear of electrics, you can place a container or plastic sheet under the drip path to reduce soaking of insulation and plaster.

6. **Watch for stop-and-leave signs.** Keep the room closed off and get urgent help if you see:
   - water dripping through a light fitting or smoke alarm
   - ceiling sagging, bulging, cracking, or dropping plaster
   - burning smell, buzzing, sparking, or repeated trips at the consumer unit
   - water spreading towards the consumer unit or fixed electrical equipment

7. **Record what is happening.** Take quick photos or video of the leak point, wet ceiling or wall, damaged belongings, and the wider room. Keep any receipts for emergency materials or make-safe work.

8. **Contact the responsible person.**
   - **Owner-occupier:** contact your home insurer’s emergency helpline and ask what emergency make-safe work your policy allows before permanent repairs. Arrange a roofer for temporary weatherproofing when conditions are safe.
   - **Tenant:** report it to your landlord or letting agent immediately as an emergency repair and send photos.
   - **Leasehold flat or managed building:** tell the managing agent, freeholder, or building maintenance contact if the roof is communal.

9. **Call the right emergency route if needed.**
   - If there is immediate danger, such as fire, sparking, serious ceiling-collapse risk, or someone at risk of electric shock, call 999.
   - In Great Britain, call 105 for a power cut, an electrical hazard involving the external supply, or damage to power lines or substations.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find the exact outside source during the rain.
- You do not need to decide now whether it is the skylight, flashing, roof covering, guttering, or condensation.
- You do not need to start drying, painting, replastering, or permanent repairs until the water ingress is stopped and electrics are checked where needed.
- You do not need to climb, inspect the roof, or argue responsibility while the area is unsafe.

## Important reassurance
A skylight or roof-window leak during heavy rain is often a flashing, seal, drainage, or nearby roof-covering problem rather than a ruined roof. The immediate job is to keep people away from water and electrics, limit damage, and get the right make-safe help.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation during or just after rain. Later decisions about roof repair, skylight repair, insurance, tenancy responsibility, electrical testing, or plaster damage may need help from a competent roofer, a qualified electrician, your insurer, landlord, managing agent, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, electrical, building, insurance, tenancy, or professional advice. If you are unsure about electrical safety, treat the area as live until it has been checked by a qualified electrician and avoid contact with wet fittings or wiring.
