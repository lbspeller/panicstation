# What to do if… you receive a work attendance warning that you believe is inaccurate (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and respond in writing. Ask for the exact dates, the attendance evidence they relied on, and the chance to correct any errors before the matter goes further, or to use the review or appeal route if a warning has already been issued.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign anything that says you “agree” or “admit” if you do not.
- Do not try to explain everything in a hurried verbal conversation where details can be misunderstood.
- Do not ignore a meeting, response, or appeal deadline, even if the warning feels unfair.
- Do not delete messages, calendar entries, rota screenshots, timesheets, or logs.
- Do not make up evidence or backfill entries.
- Do not resign in the heat of the moment.

## What to do now
1. **Get the allegation pinned down in writing.** Reply calmly and ask for:
   - the specific dates and times they say you were absent, late, or failed to follow the attendance process
   - whether this is informal coaching, an investigation, a formal disciplinary hearing, or a warning already issued
   - copies of the evidence they used, such as timekeeping reports, rota records, exception reports, manager notes, absence records, or HR portal entries
   - the attendance policy or standard they say you breached
   - whether you can provide a written correction to be kept with the record

2. **If there is a meeting, check the process before you go in.**
   - Ask what the meeting is for and what outcomes are being considered.
   - Ask for the documents in advance so you can prepare.
   - If it is a disciplinary hearing or appeal hearing, ask to be accompanied by a trade union representative, trade union official, or workplace colleague.
   - If your chosen companion cannot attend, ask for a short postponement and suggest alternative dates.

3. **Make a simple timeline pack for the disputed days.** Use records that already exist, such as:
   - the rota, schedule, shift swap approval, or leave approval you were given
   - clock-in and clock-out records, timesheet entries, or HR portal screenshots
   - emails, messages, handovers, meeting invites, job records, dispatch records, or task confirmations showing you were working
   - badge or building access records, if you already have them or can request them
   - basic sickness or fit note information, if illness is the reason for the disputed attendance entry

4. **Send a short factual correction.** Keep it focused:
   - say that you believe the attendance warning or record is inaccurate
   - list the exact dates, times, or absence codes you dispute
   - attach your timeline pack
   - ask for the record to be checked and corrected
   - ask for any warning or disciplinary outcome to be paused, withdrawn, or amended while the evidence is checked

5. **If a warning has already been issued, use the appeal route promptly.**
   - Ask for the appeal deadline and how to submit the appeal.
   - Focus on the inaccurate record and the evidence that supports your correction.
   - Say what outcome you want, such as correction of the attendance record, removal or amendment of the warning, or a corrected absence classification.

6. **If the error is not corrected, escalate cleanly.**
   - Check your employer’s grievance procedure.
   - Consider a formal grievance about inaccurate records or unfair handling.
   - Include what is wrong, the evidence you have, and what you want corrected.
   - If you need personal data held about you, such as attendance records or internal notes about you, consider a subject access request. If you only need the attendance report or policy for specific dates, ask HR for those directly first.

7. **Use support early if you have it.**
   - If you are in a union, contact your representative before any formal hearing or appeal.
   - Keep your key points in writing so there is a clear record of what you disputed and when.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to leave your job or take legal action.
- You do not need to write a long statement about your whole employment history.
- You do not need to disclose detailed medical information right now; share only what is needed to explain authorised, sickness-related, or disability-related absence.
- You do not need to confront colleagues or prove who caused the error. Focus first on correcting the record.

## Important reassurance
Attendance warnings can be triggered by messy records, rota changes, missed clockings, manager overrides, sickness coding, or payroll and HR system errors. Treat this first as a record-checking problem: get the dates, keep your evidence, and put your correction in writing.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation, correct the record, and avoid preventable mistakes. Later decisions may need specialist help based on your contract, workplace policies, employment status, and the facts.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Workplace policies and timelines vary, and the best next step can depend on whether the warning is informal or formal, whether an appeal deadline applies, and whether sickness or disability-related absence is involved.
