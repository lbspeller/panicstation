# What to do if… a partner pressures you into sexual acts you feel are unsafe or risky (UK)

## Short answer
You do not have to agree. Stop the sexual situation if you can, get to a safer pause, and contact emergency help or specialist support if you feel unsafe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep going just to keep the peace if you feel unsafe, coerced, pressured, or unsure.
- Do not treat threats, guilt, sulking, or “you owe me” arguments as consent.
- Do not use alcohol or drugs to make the situation feel bearable.
- Do not try to win an argument about consent while you feel intimidated; space matters more than explanation.
- Do not send a long message, apology, or promise while you are frightened.
- Do not assume you must report to police to get medical care or sexual violence support.

## What to do now
1. **Create a pause and stop the sexual situation if you can.**  
   Use a short line such as: “No. I’m not doing that.” If you need to leave the room, say only what helps you move: “I need the bathroom,” “I need air,” or “I’m stopping now.”

2. **Move towards distance, not persuasion.**  
   Go to a bathroom, hallway, outside, a shop, a neighbour, a friend, family, or any public place where you are less isolated. Take your phone, keys, medication, and shoes if they are within reach, but do not delay leaving to collect things.

3. **If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.**  
   If you cannot speak on a 999 call, stay on the line and listen. If prompted on a mobile, press 55 to confirm you need emergency police help.

4. **If the pressure continues by text, calls, or in person, reduce contact for the next few hours.**  
   Put the phone on silent, move to another room, lock a door if it is safe to do so, or ask someone trusted to stay on the phone while you get somewhere safer. You do not need to explain your boundary again and again.

5. **Tell one trusted person what is happening right now.**  
   A short message is enough: “I’m not OK. My partner is pressuring me sexually. Can you call me or stay with me while I get somewhere safe?” Ask them not to contact your partner unless you want that.

6. **Contact specialist sexual violence support if something sexual happened without your free agreement, or if you are not sure.**  
   If you are 16 or over in England or Wales, Rape Crisis England & Wales runs a 24/7 support line on 0808 500 2222 and online chat. It is not an emergency service, so use 999 first if you are in immediate danger.

7. **Consider a sexual assault referral centre or equivalent specialist service.**  
   These services can usually offer confidential health, practical, and emotional support whether or not you report to police. Routes vary by nation:
   - England: use the NHS “find a rape and sexual assault referral centre” service.
   - Wales: use NHS Wales information on accessing a sexual assault referral centre.
   - Scotland: call the SARCS self-referral phone service on 0800 148 88 88.
   - Northern Ireland: contact The Rowan, the regional sexual assault referral centre.

8. **If pregnancy or STI risk is possible, seek urgent clinical help.**  
   Emergency contraception is time-sensitive and some options can be used up to 5 days after sex. If HIV exposure might be possible, ask urgently about PEP; NHS guidance says to go to a sexual health clinic or A&E if you think you were exposed to HIV in the last 72 hours.

9. **Only if it feels safe and you may want more options later, keep things as they are for now.**  
   You can avoid washing, changing clothes, or deleting messages for the moment, but your safety, comfort, and urgent medical needs come first. If you already washed, changed, or deleted anything, you can still get help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether what happened “counts,” what to call it, or whether to end the relationship.
- You do not need to confront your partner or make them understand.
- You do not need to report to police now, or ever, to access support.
- You can leave decisions about statements, complaints, relationship choices, or longer-term plans until you have support and rest.

## Important reassurance
Free agreement matters. Freezing, going quiet, fawning, or saying words you did not feel free to say are common responses to pressure and fear; they do not mean you failed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: getting to a safer pause, reducing pressure, and keeping health and support options open. Later decisions may need specialist sexual violence, medical, legal, or domestic abuse support.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
