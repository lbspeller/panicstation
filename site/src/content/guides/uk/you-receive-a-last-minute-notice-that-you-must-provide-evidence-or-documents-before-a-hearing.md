# What to do if… you receive a last-minute notice that you must provide evidence or documents before a hearing (UK)

## Short answer
Identify the specific documents and deadline required by the notice, then immediately contact the court or tribunal office to confirm submission methods and request an extension or "relief from sanctions" in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice or wait until the hearing to explain why documents are missing.
- Do not send original physical documents unless the order specifically demands them; use high-quality scans or photocopies.
- Do not edit, back-date, or reconstruct documents to make them appear as if they were created earlier.
- Do not assume that a request for more time automatically pauses the hearing or the deadline.
- Do not send documents to a general court enquiry email; use the specific address or portal link provided in the notice.

## What to do now
1. **Extract the essential case data from the notice immediately.**
   - Write down the case number, hearing date, and the "Return Date" or deadline.
   - List every specific document or "exhibit" requested.
   - Note whether the documents must be "filed" (sent to court) or "served" (sent to the other parties), or both.

2. **Verify the specific court or tribunal type.**
   - Check the header: is it a County Court (Civil), Family Court, Magistrates' Court (Criminal), or an Employment Tribunal.
   - Identifying the jurisdiction is critical because the rules for late evidence (such as Civil Procedure Rules Part 3.9 or Family Procedure Rules 22.10) differ.

3. **Call the court or tribunal office to confirm the urgent filing route.**
   - Ask for the direct email address for the "listing officer" or the specific judge’s clerk.
   - Confirm if there are file size limits for email attachments (often 10MB or 25MB).
   - Ask if you are required to use a specific portal, such as "CE-File" or the "MyHMCTS" digital service.

4. **Send a written request for an extension or "relief from sanctions" today.**
   - Address the email to the court and copy in the other side’s legal representative.
   - State clearly that you are requesting an **extension of time** (if the deadline hasn't passed) or **relief from sanctions** (if it has).
   - Provide a brief, factual reason for the delay (e.g., late receipt of notice, medical emergency, or third-party delay).
   - Suggest a specific new date by which you will provide the items.

5. **Submit what you have immediately as a "partial filing."**
   - **Do not wait for a complete set of documents if some are ready now.**
   - Include a cover sheet stating: "Partial submission in response to order dated [Date]. Remaining items [List them] will follow by [Proposed Date]."
   - Label every attachment clearly with the case number and a descriptive name (e.g., "CASE123_Witness_Statement_01.pdf").

6. **Format any late witness statements or bundles correctly.**
   - For Family Court, follow the **2026 Guidance on Court Bundles for Litigants in Person**.
   - Ensure pages are numbered and that you have included a short "Index" if sending multiple files.
   - If a witness cannot sign in person, ask the court if an electronic signature or a typed "Statement of Truth" is acceptable for the interim.

7. **Retain verified proof of sending for the hearing.**
   - Save the "Sent" email and any delivery/read receipts.
   - If using a portal, take a screenshot of the "Submission Successful" page.
   - Print a copy of your request for more time to take to the hearing in case the judge has not yet seen it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare your closing arguments or trial strategy today.
- You do not need to respond to the "merits" of the other side's case right now; focus only on the document procedural hurdle.
- You do not need to decide on long-term settlement offers until the immediate evidence deadline is resolved.

## Important reassurance
Courts and tribunals deal with late evidence and missed deadlines daily. While the rules are strict, judges generally prefer to have all relevant evidence before them to make a fair decision. Providing a clear, prompt explanation and sending what you can immediately is the most effective way to protect your position.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance for handling an urgent or missed document deadline. It does not replace the specific directions in a Court Order or the requirements of the Civil, Family, or Criminal Procedure Rules.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and is not legal advice. If you are involved in proceedings involving potential imprisonment, the removal of children, or home repossession, seek urgent legal representation via a duty solicitor, Law Centre, or Citizen's Advice.
