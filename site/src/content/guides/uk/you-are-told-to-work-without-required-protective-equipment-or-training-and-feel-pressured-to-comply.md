# What to do if… you are told to work without required protective equipment or training and feel pressured to comply (UK)

## Short answer
Create a safe pause and do not start or continue the task until the required safety controls, PPE, training, or competent supervision are in place. If you are in immediate danger, move to somewhere safer first.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “just do it once” to get it over with if the required PPE, training, supervision, or safety controls are missing.
- Do not sign anything saying you have been trained, inducted, competent, or fit-tested if that is not true.
- Do not accept PPE that seems wrong for the task, damaged, badly fitting, past expiry, or unsuitable for your body or health needs.
- Do not get pulled into a heated, off-the-record argument; keep it brief and factual.
- Do not put yourself at risk to gather photos, videos, or other evidence. Your safety comes first.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safe pause.** Step back from the hazard area and stop the specific task. If you are mid-task, make it safe to stop, such as switching off, isolating, or setting something down, only if you can do that without increasing the risk.
2. **Say one clear sentence.** For example: “I can’t do this task safely without the required PPE, training, or supervision. Please provide it or give me another task.”
3. **Ask for the safe way to do this task.** Ask for the risk assessment, method statement, safe system of work, or other agreed instructions your workplace uses for that job. Ask what controls are required before the task starts.
4. **Name the exact gap.** Be specific: “I have not been trained on this machine,” “I do not have the required respirator or fit test,” “I have not been shown how to handle this chemical,” or “There is no fall protection in place.”
5. **Ask for the correct person.** Ask for the site manager, supervisor, health and safety lead, union safety representative, or employee safety representative, if there is one. Keep the conversation visible and neutral.
6. **Offer a safe alternative.** Ask to be given another task, to wait until the correct PPE or training is provided, or to work only under appropriate competent supervision.
7. **Make a quick record while it is fresh.** Write down the date, time, location, task, what PPE or training was missing, who instructed you, who was present, and what you said. Notes are enough for now.
8. **Send a short follow-up message.** Use text, email, or workplace messaging: say what task you were asked to do, what was missing, that you paused for safety, and that you are ready to work once it is made safe. Keep it calm and factual.
9. **If a photo would help, only take it if it is safe and allowed.** Do not take photos if it would put you in danger, breach a clear workplace rule, expose someone’s private information, or escalate the situation.
10. **If you are worried about retaliation, keep the reason clearly about safety.** Workers may have protection from being treated worse for certain health and safety actions, and employees may have extra protection from dismissal, but the facts and employment status matter.
11. **If the issue is not fixed, escalate outside the line manager.** Use the employer’s near-miss, incident, health and safety, or HR route. If the risk is serious and the employer has not acted, check the correct enforcing authority and report the concern to HSE or the relevant local authority.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, raise a formal grievance, contact a solicitor, or make a tribunal claim.
- You do not need to prove fault right now; focus on what must change before the task is safe.
- You do not need a perfect statement; a dated note and one calm written message are enough for now.

## Important reassurance
Pressure from a manager or team can make you doubt yourself. Pausing unsafe work and asking for the right controls, PPE, training, or supervision is a normal safety step, not overreacting.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and prevent harm or irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about grievances, whistleblowing, dismissal, detriment, or legal claims may need specialist advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you believe there is serious and imminent danger, prioritise getting to safety.
