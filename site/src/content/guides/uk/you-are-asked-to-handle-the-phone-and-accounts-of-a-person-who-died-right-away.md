# What to do if… you are asked to handle the phone and accounts of a person who died right away (UK)

## Short answer
Secure the physical devices and use official bereavement notification channels to stop charges and prevent fraud. Do not attempt to bypass security codes or impersonate the deceased, as this can permanently lock accounts.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t guess passcodes or attempt multiple "brute-force" logins on the device.
- Don’t use "forgot password" links to gain entry if you are not the legal executor.
- Don’t pretend to be the deceased person when calling banks, utility companies, or mobile providers.
- Don’t factory reset the device or delete any apps, as these may contain essential evidence of accounts or assets.
- Don’t ignore 2FA (two-factor authentication) texts; they may indicate unauthorized attempts to access the accounts by others.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the physical device and keep it powered.** Place the phone in a safe location and connect it to a charger to ensure it does not lose power, which can sometimes make subsequent official access more difficult.
2. **Log immediate observable data without unlocking.** 
   - Note the mobile network shown on the lock screen.
   - Record the phone number if it is visible.
   - Note any visible email addresses or app notifications that appear on the locked screen.
3. **Identify the person with legal authority.** Determine if there is a will and who the named executors are; if there is no will, identify who is applying to be the administrator (Next of Kin).
   - If you are not the executor, your role is safekeeping only.
4. **Notify the mobile network’s bereavement team.** Inform the provider that the account holder has died and ask them to place a "bereavement marker" on the account.
   - Most UK providers will waive Early Termination Charges (ETCs).
   - Ask them to suspend outgoing calls and data to prevent further billing.
5. **Use the "Tell Us Once" service after the death is registered.** Use the unique reference number provided by the registrar to notify multiple government departments (HMRC, DWP, DVLA, Passport Office) in a single step.
6. **Utilize the Death Notification Service (DNS) for financial accounts.** Access the DNS website to notify multiple participating UK banks and building societies simultaneously without needing to log in to the deceased's individual banking apps.
7. **Check for Digital Legacy settings if the device is an Apple or Google product.** 
   - For Apple: Look for a "Legacy Contact" access key in the deceased's physical paperwork or estate files.
   - For Google: Use the "Submit a request regarding a deceased user's account" tool on the Google support site.
8. **Document incoming security alerts.** If the phone receives 2FA codes or password reset notifications you did not trigger, record the time and the service name, as these are evidence of potential fraud attempts.

## What can wait
- You do not need to access the phone to find a list of every single account today.
- You do not need to decide which photos or messages to keep immediately.
- You do not need to worry about paying off the phone handset contract yourself; this is a debt of the estate.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel pressured to "sort everything out" immediately when handed a deceased person's device. However, digital accounts are designed to be secure, and the most helpful thing you can do right now is prevent unauthorized access and stop ongoing charges through official, slower channels.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling digital devices and accounts immediately following a death in the UK. It does not cover long-term estate administration or legal disputes over digital assets.

## Important note
This information is not legal advice. Accessing another person's account without proper authority can violate terms of service or privacy laws. Always use official bereavement routes provided by companies rather than attempting to bypass security.
