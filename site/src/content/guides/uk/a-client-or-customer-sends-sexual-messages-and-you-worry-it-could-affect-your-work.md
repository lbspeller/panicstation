# What to do if… a client or customer sends sexual messages and you worry it could affect your work (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all informal engagement with the sender immediately, preserve the evidence, and report the incident to your employer to trigger their legal duty to protect you from third-party harassment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete any messages, images, or call logs, even if they are distressing or embarrassing.
- Do not reply with apologies, jokes, or "soft" rejections intended to preserve the business relationship.
- Do not move the conversation to personal platforms like WhatsApp or personal social media.
- Do not agree to meet the person alone to "resolve the misunderstanding."
- Do not assume you must tolerate this behavior because the person is a high-value client or a paying customer.
- Do not attempt to investigate the person's history or "confirm" their intent before reporting.

## What to do now
1. **Initiate a immediate communication pause.** Stop responding to the sender on the current channel and move to a physical or digital space where you cannot see new incoming notifications while you stabilise.
2. **Preserve the evidence exactly as it appeared.**  
   - Take clear screenshots that show the sender's name, handle, or phone number alongside the timestamp.  
   - If the message contains an unsolicited sexual image (cyber-flashing), save it once and then close the view; this is a priority criminal offence.  
   - Save any associated emails, voicemails, or "disappearing" messages before they expire.
3. **Log the specific impact on your work.** Write a factual summary including the date, time, and platform.
   - Describe how the message made you feel (e.g., "felt unsafe to continue the meeting," "could not focus on the project").
   - Note if this is a repeat occurrence or an escalation of previous behavior.
4. **Identify your workplace's designated reporting route.** Check your staff handbook or intranet for "Sexual Harassment" or "Third-Party Harassment" policies to see if there is a specific contact person or confidential advisor.
5. **Make a formal report to a manager or HR lead.** State clearly that you have received unwanted conduct of a sexual nature from a third party and that you require workplace protection.
   - Mention that you are aware of your rights under the 2026 whistleblowing protections for sexual harassment reports.
   - Ask for a written acknowledgment of your report.
6. **Request immediate "reasonable steps" for your protection.** Ask for specific actions to isolate you from the sender, such as:
   - Reassigning the account or customer to a different staff member.
   - Routing all future contact through a shared departmental inbox.
   - Cancelling scheduled one-on-one meetings or moving them to recorded video calls with a second colleague present.
7. **Verify that the incident has been recorded on the company's risk assessment.** Under current law, UK employers must take all reasonable steps to prevent third-party harassment and should have an active risk assessment for client-facing roles.
8. **Contact the police on 101 if the messages involve threats or persistent stalking.** If the behavior is repeated (two or more times) or involves a threat of violence, it may meet the criminal threshold for stalking or harassment regardless of the work context.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to resign or change roles today.
- You do not need to prepare a full legal case or prove "hostile environment" thresholds yourself.
- You do not need to worry about the financial loss to the company if the client is terminated; that is the employer's responsibility to manage.

## Important reassurance
Feeling hesitant, "frozen," or worried about professional retaliation is a normal response to workplace harassment. Under 2026 UK law, you are protected from "detriment" (unfair treatment) for reporting sexual harassment, and your employer is legally required to take proactive steps to ensure your safety.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for third-party harassment. It does not cover the long-term grievance process or Employment Tribunal procedures, which may require specialist legal or union representation.

## Important note
As of April 2026, reports of sexual harassment at work are classified as "protected disclosures," meaning you have enhanced legal protection against dismissal or unfair treatment for speaking up. Employers who fail to take all reasonable steps to prevent this may face a 25% increase in compensation awards at tribunal.
