# What to do if… your water pressure drops suddenly and you suspect a major leak (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all water use immediately and close your internal stopcock (stop valve) to isolate the plumbing, then check with neighbours and your water meter to determine if the fault is internal or part of the wider network.

## Do not do these things
- Don't keep taps running to test the supply, as this increases the volume of escaping water if a pipe has burst.
- Don't ignore hissing sounds, damp patches, or bulging plaster even if you cannot see standing water.
- Don't touch any electrical switches, sockets, or the consumer unit if there is any sign of moisture or water nearby.
- Don't attempt to dismantle walls, floorboards, or pavements to find the leak yourself.
- Don't leave the property unattended until the source of the leak is isolated or help has arrived.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all active water use immediately.**  
   Ensure all taps, showers, washing machines, dishwashers, and garden hose systems are turned off.

2. **Locate and close your internal stopcock.**  
   This is usually found under the kitchen sink, in a hallway cupboard, or under the stairs. Turn the valve handle clockwise until it stops to shut off the mains water entering your property.
   - If the valve is seized, do not apply excessive force; move to the next steps and alert the plumber that the supply cannot be isolated.

3. **Determine if the issue is local or network-wide.**  
   Check with immediate neighbours to see if they are experiencing similar pressure drops.
   - If multiple properties are affected, the issue is likely a burst water main or network fault managed by your regional water company.
   - If only your property is affected, the fault is likely within your internal plumbing or private supply pipe.

4. **Perform a preliminary water meter check.**  
   If you have a water meter, ensure all taps are off and the internal stopcock is closed. Note the reading and observe the dials or digital display for at least 30 minutes.
   - Any movement or change in the reading while the stopcock is closed indicates a leak on the supply pipe between the meter and the house.
   - If the meter moves only when the stopcock is open (with all taps off), the leak is inside your home.

5. **Scan the property for hazards and damage.**  
   Listen for the sound of running water behind walls or under floors and look for fresh dampness or sagging ceilings.
   - If water is near electrical installations, keep everyone away from the area. Only isolate the power at the consumer unit if you can do so while remaining completely dry and away from any water flow.

6. **Contact the appropriate emergency service.**
   - **For network issues or leaks in the street:** Call your regional water company's 24-hour emergency line immediately.
   - **For internal leaks (Homeowners):** Contact an emergency plumber. Use the WaterSafe register to find a qualified professional.
   - **For internal leaks (Tenants):** Contact your landlord, letting agent, or building management emergency number.
   - **For vulnerable users:** If you are on the Priority Services Register (PSR), inform the water company or plumber of your status immediately.

7. **Mitigate damage if it is safe to do so.**  
   Place buckets under visible drips and move high-value items or electronics away from affected areas. 

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact point of the leak before calling for professional help.
- You do not need to start cleaning or drying affected areas until a professional has confirmed the water supply is safely isolated.
- You do not need to negotiate repair costs or insurance claims while the emergency is active.

## Important reassurance
A sudden loss of water pressure is a standard trigger for safety protocols. By closing your stopcock and identifying whether neighbours are affected, you have already taken the most effective steps to limit property damage and provide essential information to emergency responders.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for acute water pressure loss and suspected leaks. It does not cover long-term repairs, insurance negotiation, or technical plumbing diagnostics.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only. Always follow the specific directions provided by your water company or a qualified plumber. If you suspect an electrical hazard due to water ingress, do not enter the area and wait for professional assistance.
