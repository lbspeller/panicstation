# What to do if… your water pressure drops suddenly and you suspect a major leak (UK)

## Short answer
Stop using water, turn off your internal stop tap if you can, and keep away from any water near electrics. Then check whether neighbours are affected and contact the right help: your water supplier for a possible outside or network issue, or a plumber, landlord, or building manager for a likely internal leak.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep running taps to see if the pressure comes back.
- Don’t ignore hissing sounds, damp patches, water staining, a moving meter, or a sagging ceiling just because you cannot see running water.
- Don’t touch sockets, switches, plugs, appliances, or the consumer unit if there is water nearby.
- Don’t break walls, lift flooring, or dig outside in a panic.
- Don’t force a stuck stop tap hard enough to break it.
- Don’t alter communal pipework in a flat or managed building; isolate only what you can safely control and alert the building contact.

## What to do now
1. **Stop using water.**  
   Turn off taps, showers, washing machines, dishwashers, outside taps, hoses, and irrigation timers.

2. **Turn off your internal stop tap if you can.**  
   It is often under the kitchen sink, near where the mains pipe enters, in a downstairs cupboard, in a garage, or near the front of the property. Turn a wheel-type stop tap clockwise to close it. If it will not move or will not fully close, do not force it; tell the plumber, landlord, building manager, or water supplier that the stop tap is not closing.

3. **Keep away from water near electrics.**  
   Keep people out of any area where water is near sockets, lights, appliances, or the consumer unit. If someone is in immediate danger, there is fire, or you cannot stay clear of unsafe electrics, call 999.

4. **Do a quick danger scan.**  
   Look and listen for rushing or hissing water, fresh damp patches, bubbling paint, wet cupboards, wet floors near pipe routes, wet or musty smells, water stains, or a ceiling bulging with water. If a ceiling is bulging, keep people out of that room.

5. **If you can access your water meter safely, check whether it is still moving.**  
   With all water use stopped and the stop tap closed as far as it safely goes, note whether the meter or flow indicator is still moving. Check again after a short pause. Continued movement can mean water is still flowing somewhere, or that the stop tap has not fully isolated the supply.

6. **Check whether this is only your property.**  
   Ask a neighbour in the same building or street if their pressure has also dropped. If several homes are affected, it may be a burst main, planned work, or another network issue.

7. **Contact the right help.**
   - If neighbours are affected, water is appearing in the street, or you suspect an outside burst main, report it to your water supplier through their emergency or report-a-problem route.
   - If the meter keeps moving, there are damp or flooding signs inside, or the problem seems to be within your home, contact an emergency plumber.
   - If you rent, contact your landlord or letting agent urgently.
   - If you are in a flat or managed building, contact building management or the managing agent urgently, especially if water may affect another flat or communal electrics.

8. **Limit damage only if it is safe.**
   - Move phones, laptops, documents, and valuables away from wet areas.
   - Put buckets or towels down for dripping water.
   - Open cupboard doors where pipes run so you can see what is happening.
   - Keep the water supply off until the leak source is checked.

9. **Make a quick record.**  
   Take photos or video of the meter, damp patches, flooding, ceilings, and any water outside. Note the time you noticed the pressure drop and the time you turned the stop tap off.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find the exact leak location right now.
- You do not need to decide who pays, whether to make an insurance claim, or what repair option to choose until the active leak or supply issue is stabilised.
- You do not need to start major drying, cleaning, lifting flooring, or moving heavy furniture until it is safe and the water is stopped.
- You do not need to argue responsibility while water is still moving; first isolate, keep people safe, and get the right help on the way.

## Important reassurance
A sudden pressure drop can feel alarming, but the first steps are simple: stop water use, isolate what you safely can, check for danger signs, and contact the right service. Those actions reduce damage and give clear information to the person who can fix or investigate the leak.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to make the situation safer and reduce damage. Later decisions about repairs, pipe responsibility, landlord duties, water-supplier processes, or insurance may need help from a plumber, landlord, building manager, water supplier, insurer, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, insurance, electrical, plumbing, financial, or professional advice. Follow instructions from emergency services, your water supplier, your landlord or building manager, and qualified tradespeople.
