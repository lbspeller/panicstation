# What to do if… you receive an official notice of an inspection or visit with very little warning (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and verify the notice through an independent official route, then ask the issuing body to confirm the legal power, purpose, timing, and scope before you agree to anything beyond what is clearly required.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume it is genuine just because it looks official.
- Don’t use phone numbers, email addresses, payment links, or QR codes from the notice until you have checked them independently.
- Don’t let someone in just to be polite if you have doubts about identity, authority, address, or scope.
- Don’t sign a statement, admission, consent form, or receipt you do not understand in the moment.
- Don’t hand over original documents, devices, passwords, or large amounts of information without asking what power or request they are relying on.
- Don’t argue at the door. Keep it calm, slow, and factual.
- Don’t destroy, hide, alter, or backdate records.

## What to do now
1. **Check the notice itself first.**  
   Look for the issuing organisation, reference number, named officer or inspector, address to be visited, date and time, stated purpose, and any stated power of entry. If it is vague, threatening, asks for money, or pressures you to act through a link or direct number, treat it as unverified.

2. **Verify it through an independent official route.**  
   Find the organisation’s contact details yourself from GOV.UK, the regulator’s official website, or a trusted public directory. Ask them to confirm the reference number, the named person, the planned visit, and whether anyone is expected to attend your home, business, or both.

3. **Ask for the authority and scope in plain terms.**  
   Ask:
   - “What legal power, warrant, court order, or consent are you relying on?”
   - “Is this visit voluntary, or are you saying you have a power of entry?”
   - “Which address and areas does this cover?”
   - “What will be done on site: inspection, photos, copying, sampling, seizure, or interview?”
   - “Which records do you want available, and for what period?”
   - “Who will attend, and can you send the request in writing?”

4. **If the visit is to your home or other private dwelling, slow the situation down.**  
   Entry to a private dwelling may depend on consent, a specific statutory power, a warrant, or a court order. Say: “I am not refusing to cooperate. Please show or send the written authority you are relying on and confirm the power through your organisation’s main contact route.”

5. **If the visit is to business premises, set a calm access plan.**  
   Ask who will meet the inspector, who will escort them in non-public areas, where they can wait while identity is checked, and which records should be ready. Ask them to keep the visit to the stated purpose and scope, and keep your own note if you think a request goes beyond that.

6. **If you need more time, ask for a short reschedule in writing.**  
   Give a practical reason, such as the key person being unavailable, an interpreter being needed, essential records being off-site, an operational safety issue, or needing legal or compliance support present. Do not assume they must agree, and do not block a visit if they clearly identify a lawful power to proceed.

7. **If someone arrives before you are ready, use a doorstep protocol.**  
   Ask for formal ID and, where relevant, written authorisation, a warrant, a court order, or an instrument of appointment. Ask them to wait while you phone the organisation’s main public number to confirm their identity and purpose. Ask: “Are you asking for consent to enter, or are you using a legal power to enter?”

8. **If they show a warrant or court order, check the basics before stepping aside.**  
   Check the address, date, named organisation or officer, what areas it covers, what it allows them to do, and what may be copied, searched, seized, or taken. If you cannot copy it, write down the details.

9. **Keep a live record.**  
   Note the date and time, names, role titles, badge or reference numbers, who you called to verify, what was requested, what was inspected, and what you provided. If anything is copied, removed, or seized, ask for a receipt, list, or written record and keep your own list as well.

10. **If the notice may be a scam, separate verification from reporting.**  
   Do not reply using the suspect contact route. Keep the message or envelope, take screenshots if it came by email or text, and use an official UK scam or fraud reporting route once you are safe from immediate pressure.

11. **If police are involved or the consequences are serious, get legal help promptly.**  
   Ask what power is being used and say clearly that you want legal advice before answering detailed questions where that is possible. For a business or regulated setting, contact your solicitor, compliance lead, or responsible manager as soon as you can.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your whole response strategy now.
- You do not need to prepare every possible record immediately unless the power and scope are clear.
- You do not need to answer detailed questions on the spot if it is reasonable to ask to respond later in writing.
- You do not need to argue about blame, fault, or outcomes at the door.
- You do not need to report a suspected scam before you have stopped using the suspect contact route and verified the immediate situation.

## Important reassurance
A short-notice official visit can feel urgent, but you are allowed to slow down enough to check identity, authority, address, and scope. Calm verification is a normal first step and can prevent accidental over-disclosure, missed safeguards, or unnecessary conflict.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help because the right response depends on the organisation visiting, the law being used, the type of premises, and whether the visit is voluntary, consent-based, statutory, or court-authorised.

## Important note
This is general information only, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Powers of entry, notice requirements, warrants, and duties to provide information vary by authority and by the specific law being used. If you are unsure what you must allow or provide, get advice from a qualified solicitor promptly.
