# What to do if… your phone backup fails and you are about to reset or replace the device (UK)

## Short answer
Do not reset or wipe the phone yet. Keep it powered, connected to reliable Wi-Fi, and try another backup or transfer route before you hand it over, trade it in, or replace it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not factory reset “just to try again”; it can erase local data that may not be recoverable.
- Do not sign out of your Apple Account or Google account unless you are sure you can sign back in with your password and two-step verification.
- Do not wipe the phone only because a shop, insurer, or trade-in page says it is required, unless you have first copied out what you can.
- Do not rely on one app saying “synced”; check backup dates, backup details, or the same data on another device where possible.
- Do not disable Find My or device protection features in a rush unless you understand what that changes for recovery and theft protection.
- Do not delete photos, messages, notes, or app data to “make space” unless you have verified another copy exists.

## What to do now
1. **Create a no-reset pause and stabilise the phone.**  
   Plug it into power, connect to reliable Wi-Fi, and stop battery-draining tasks such as hotspot use, large downloads, or software updates. If storage is very low, only delete items you can re-download or have already verified somewhere else, such as streaming downloads.

2. **Check the age of the backup you already have.**  
   On iPhone or iPad, check iCloud Backup and the date and time of the last successful backup. On Android, check Google backup details and which data types are backed up. If the last backup is old, missing, or unclear, treat the phone as the only copy for now.

3. **Try a second backup or transfer route.**  
   For iPhone, if iCloud backup is failing, try an encrypted local backup on a trusted computer. An encrypted local backup can include items that an unencrypted local backup may not, such as saved passwords, Wi-Fi settings, website history, health data, and call history. Keep the backup password somewhere safe, because you will need it to use that backup.

4. **For Android, copy accessible files as well as checking Google backup.**  
   Check Google backup, then copy accessible photos, videos, downloads, and documents to a computer or storage location you can verify from another device. This may not capture everything, but it can protect some irreplaceable files if the full backup or transfer keeps failing.

5. **Use device-to-device transfer if you are moving to a new phone today.**  
   For iPhone to iPhone, use Quick Start if both devices are available. For Android, use the new phone’s copy apps and data setup, with a cable if that is available and works for your devices. Keep both phones charged, near each other, and undisturbed until the transfer finishes.

6. **Do a short lifeboat copy of the most painful things to lose.**  
   Copy recent photos and videos to a cloud folder, computer, or external storage you can check from another device. Check that contacts appear in your Apple, Google, or other account on another device or on the web. Save key documents, notes, or essential message screenshots to an account you can open elsewhere.

7. **Protect account access before the phone goes away.**  
   Make sure you know the password for your Apple Account or Google account. Check that you can receive verification codes by text, call, email, trusted device, or backup code. If you use an authenticator app, make sure you have backup codes or another sign-in method before you reset or surrender the phone.

8. **Slow down any pressure from a shop, insurer, repairer, or retailer.**  
   Ask them to confirm in writing whether a reset is required, whether it will erase data, and whether you can delay reset or handover until you have attempted backup or transfer. Ask them to note that backup failed and you requested time to copy data; this may help you explain what happened later.

9. **If replacement must happen today, keep the old phone intact until checks are done.**  
   Before wiping the old phone, verify on the new phone or another device that your recent photos, contacts, key messages, and account sign-ins work. If the old phone must be surrendered, ask whether handover can wait until the transfer has completed and you have checked the essentials.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose the perfect long-term backup system today.
- You do not need to clean up your whole photo library, reorganise files, or optimise storage right now.
- You do not need to troubleshoot every app; focus on photos, contacts, key messages, documents, and account access first.
- You do not need to commit to a trade-in or return at the exact moment it forces an early wipe; asking for a pause is reasonable.
- You do not need to decide now whether a failed backup means the phone is broken.

## Important reassurance
A failed backup does not mean all data is already lost. The safest move is to avoid wiping the device, try another backup or transfer path, and copy out the few items that would be hardest to replace.

## Scope note
This is first steps only: prevent irreversible loss, protect account access, and buy time. If the phone is damaged, intermittently failing, managed by an employer or school, or you suspect account compromise, you may need help from the device maker, your account provider, the repairer, or another appropriate specialist after the immediate risk is stable.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. Device menus and backup options vary by model, software version, account settings, storage plan, and app. Some data may be unrecoverable after a reset, so prioritise not wiping the device and keeping access to your main accounts.
