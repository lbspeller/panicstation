# What to do if… your laptop starts demanding a firmware or BIOS password you do not know (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your mobile network provider immediately to report an unauthorised Porting Authorisation Code (PAC) or Service Termination Authorisation Code (STAC) request and demand an immediate block on the transfer.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, call numbers, or download "security certificates" contained within the transfer notification text.
- Don’t share one-time passcodes (OTPs), PINs, or the PAC / STAC code itself with anyone who calls you claiming to be from your network.
- Don’t delay your report; UK porting systems often have a 5:00 PM deadline for stopping next-day transfers.
- Don’t assume the transfer has failed if your phone still has signal; the "No Service" state often occurs only after the transfer completes.
- Don’t rely on SMS for two-factor authentication on banking or email accounts until the account is secured.

## What to do now
1. **Call your mobile provider’s official fraud or customer service line immediately.**
   **Use a landline or another person's phone if your device has already lost service.**
   - Dial 191 (if on Vodafone / O2 / Three / EE and the SIM still works) or find the official 0333 or 0800 number on your provider's website.
   - State clearly: "I am a victim of an unauthorised porting request. Stop the pending PAC / STAC transfer immediately."
2. **Request an immediate "Account Freeze" and "Porting Block."**
   **Ask the representative to confirm that any active PAC or STAC codes have been invalidated.**
   - Request that a "high-risk" or "fraud" marker be placed on your account.
   - Ask for a "No Port" instruction to be added to your profile, requiring in-person ID verification for future transfers.
3. **Verify and reset all account security credentials.**
   **Remove any new or unrecognised email addresses or secondary contact numbers from your mobile account.**
   - Change your account PIN and security questions.
   - Confirm if any new eSIMs have been issued or downloaded to other devices.
4. **Secure your primary email account using a non-SMS method.**
   **Change your email password immediately and check for any "forwarding rules" that may have been created.**
   - Switch your two-factor authentication (2FA) from SMS to an Authenticator App or a physical security key.
   - Select the "log out of all other sessions" option in your email settings.
5. **Notify your bank and financial institutions.**
   **Inform them that your mobile number is currently compromised and SMS codes may be intercepted.**
   - Request that they temporarily disable SMS-based password resets or transaction approvals.
   - Review recent transactions for any small "test" payments or new payee setups.
6. **Report the incident to the national fraud reporting service.**
   **Submit a report even if the transfer was successfully stopped to help track the fraudster's details.**
   - England, Wales, and Northern Ireland: Contact **Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) at 0300 123 2040 or via reportfraud.police.uk.
   - Scotland: Contact **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
7. **Forward the fraudulent notification text to 7726.**
   **This free service allows UK networks to investigate and block the origin of the scam message.**
   - If your service is already cut off, do this once you have a functional SIM card or from a secondary device.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to determine the exact method of the data breach right now.
- You don’t need to check your credit report or apply for protective registration (Cifas) in the first hour.
- You don’t need to factory reset your hardware unless there is evidence of malware on the device itself.
- You don’t need to notify your entire contact list until the immediate financial and account risks are mitigated.

## Important reassurance
Unauthorised porting and SIM swapping are recognised fraud types in the UK. Mobile networks and banks have established "High Risk" protocols to handle these events. Taking immediate action to invalidate the PAC / STAC code is the most effective way to prevent the number from leaving your control.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an unauthorised transfer request. It does not cover long-term identity theft recovery, legal claims against providers, or hardware-level forensic analysis.

## Important note
This information is for guidance purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Security protocols and reporting names (such as the transition from Action Fraud to Report Fraud) may change. Always verify current reporting links through official .gov.uk or .police.uk domains.
