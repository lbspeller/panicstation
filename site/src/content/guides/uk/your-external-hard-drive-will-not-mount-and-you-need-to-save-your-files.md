# What to do if… your external hard drive will not mount and you need to save your files (UK)

## Short answer
Stop trying random fixes and stop writing to the drive. The safest first move is to disconnect it, keep it powered off, and switch to “copy out or recover” thinking, not “repair or reformat” thinking.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click **Format**, **Initialize**, **Initialise**, or **Erase** just to “make it show up”.
- Don’t run repeated repair tools in a panic, especially anything that says it will **fix errors**, if the files matter.
- Don’t keep unplugging and replugging rapidly if the drive is making unusual sounds such as clicking, grinding, or beeping.
- Don’t install “miracle recovery” apps from pop-ups, adverts, or unfamiliar sites you found mid-panic.
- Don’t open the drive enclosure or take the casing apart.
- Don’t send the drive back for repair or replacement until you have decided what matters more: the device warranty or the data on it.

## What to do now
1. **Freeze the situation to prevent overwriting.**  
   Safely eject the drive if possible, then unplug it. If it has its own power supply, turn it off. Put it somewhere stable, dry, and away from heat.

2. **Write down what happened.**  
   Note when it last worked, what you were doing, any messages such as “needs formatting”, “unreadable”, “not initialized”, or “uninitialised”, whether it spins up, and whether there are any new sounds.

3. **Do one low-risk connection check.**  
   Try once with a different USB port, a different cable, and, if it has one, the original power adaptor. Avoid repeated attempts, especially if the drive is clicking, grinding, beeping, disconnecting, or getting unusually hot.

4. **Check whether the computer can see it without changing anything.**  
   - **Windows:** open **Disk Management** and check whether the disk appears, even if it has no drive letter. Do not choose **Initialize**, **Initialise**, **Format**, **New Simple Volume**, or anything that creates a new partition.
   - **Mac:** open **Disk Utility** and check whether the device appears in the sidebar. Do not choose **Erase**. Avoid **First Aid** for now if the files are irreplaceable and you do not already have a backup.
   If it appears but says “uninitialized”, “uninitialised”, “not mounted”, “unknown”, or “unreadable”, treat this as a recovery situation, not a formatting situation.

5. **If the drive opens, copy the most irreplaceable files first.**  
   Copy small, critical items such as photos, documents, work files, or legal files to a different drive or a cloud account you trust. Copy; do not move. If copying becomes extremely slow, the drive disconnects, or errors start appearing, stop.

6. **If the drive makes clicking, grinding, or beeping noises, stop DIY attempts.**  
   Keep the drive powered off. Consider a professional data recovery service if the files are irreplaceable; this may be costly, but repeated DIY attempts can reduce the chance of recovery.

7. **If the drive is new or recently purchased, protect the data before the warranty process.**  
   A retailer or manufacturer may replace, wipe, or dispose of the device during a return or repair process. Contact them first and ask what happens to stored data, but do not hand over the drive until you are sure you can live without the files or have chosen a recovery route.

8. **If you are stuck with a retailer or manufacturer, use UK consumer support.**  
   You can contact the Citizens Advice consumer service for guidance on next steps and escalation options. Keep receipts, order numbers, emails, fault descriptions, and any warranty messages together.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide today whether to buy a new drive, change your whole backup setup, or upgrade to a NAS.
- You do **not** need to run deep scans, command-line repairs, or reformat just to test the drive.
- You do **not** need to organise all files now; focus only on preserving the files that matter most.
- You do **not** need to argue with the retailer before deciding whether the data is more important than the device.

## Important reassurance
This kind of failure is common, and pausing is useful. Formatting, initialising, erasing, or repeated repair attempts can make recovery harder, so stopping and choosing the least destructive next step is a sensible response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to prevent avoidable data loss and buy time. Later decisions about data recovery, warranty claims, replacement drives, or business-critical files may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional, legal, technical, or data recovery advice. Storage failures can have several causes, and some tools can change data on the disk. When files are important, prioritise non-destructive steps and consider a reputable data recovery specialist.
