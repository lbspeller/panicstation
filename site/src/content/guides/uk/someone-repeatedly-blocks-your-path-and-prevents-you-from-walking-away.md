# What to do if… someone repeatedly blocks your path and prevents you from walking away (UK)

## Short answer
Move towards other people, staff, security, or a well-lit busy place. If you feel in immediate danger, the person will not let you leave, violence is being threatened, or a crime is happening now, call **999**.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep negotiating or explaining yourself if they are blocking you; your priority is distance, witnesses, and help.
- Don’t let them steer you somewhere quieter, towards a car, doorway, side street, stairwell, park, or empty car park.
- Don’t try to push past or grab them unless you must create space to leave.
- Don’t turn your back on your exit route or walk backwards for long.
- Don’t get absorbed in arguing or texting on your phone; keep watching your surroundings and moving towards help.

## What to do now
1. **Move the situation towards witnesses.** Head for the nearest shop, petrol station, hotel lobby, staffed venue entrance, transport staff, security, or a well-lit busy area.
2. **Use one clear phrase and keep moving.** Say loudly and firmly: “Please move. I’m leaving.” You do not need to debate, apologise, or persuade them.
3. **Create space without escalating if you can.** Angle sideways rather than straight backwards, keep an exit in view, and put a barrier between you if there is one nearby, such as a counter, bench, bollard, parked car, or table.
4. **Ask a specific person for help.** Go to staff, security, a driver, a family group, or a group at a doorway and say: “Can you help me? This person is stopping me leaving.” Ask them to stay with you and call police if needed.
5. **If you are in a bar, club, restaurant, or licensed venue, go straight to staff or security.** In participating venues or organisations, you can discreetly ask for “Angela”; if you are not sure the venue uses the scheme, plainly ask staff for help.
6. **If you are on a train or at a station, go to rail staff, a staffed area, or a busy platform area.** Call **999** if you need urgent help. For a non-emergency rail issue, British Transport Police say you can text **61016** or call **0800 40 50 40**.
7. **Use the right police route for the risk.** Call **999** if someone is in immediate danger, violence is being used or threatened, you cannot get away, or a crime is happening now. If you are safe and want non-emergency police contact later, use **101** or your local police force’s online reporting route.
8. **If you cannot safely speak on a mobile 999 call, still dial 999.** Listen to the operator, cough or tap if you can, and if prompted press **55** to show it is a genuine emergency and be put through to police.
9. **Once you are somewhere safer, make a quick note.** Write down the time, location, what the person did, any words or threats, a description, direction of travel, witnesses, and the venue or transport location.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide now whether it counts as a specific crime.
- You do **not** need to write a full statement immediately.
- You do **not** need to decide now whether to make a formal report.
- Asking about CCTV, witnesses, or ongoing support can wait until you are safe.

## Important reassurance
Someone repeatedly blocking your path is a valid reason to feel alarmed. Wanting to leave is reasonable, and you do not owe them more conversation. In the moment, focus on getting near people, light, staff, and help.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes and hours. If the person is known to you, keeps appearing, or the behaviour repeats, later decisions may need specialist safety, police, legal, or support advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you think you are in immediate danger or the situation may become violent, call **999**.
