# What to do if… someone keeps trying to get you alone after you said you want to stay with the group (UK)

## Short answer
Physically move to the centre of your group or approach venue staff immediately and state that you feel unsafe and are being pressured to leave.

## Do not do these things
- Don't agree to "step outside for a minute" or "go somewhere quiet" just to stop the persistent questioning.
- Don't worry about being "polite" or "making a scene" if your boundaries are being ignored.
- Don't split off from the group for any reason—including using the bathroom or going to the bar—without a trusted buddy.
- Don't accept a lift, a shared taxi, or an offer to "walk you home" from the person pressuring you.
- Don't ignore your intuition; if you feel you are being targeted or "hunted," treat it as a serious safety risk.

## What to do now
1. **Move yourself into the physical centre of your group.** Stand so that you are surrounded by people you trust, with your back to a wall or the bar rather than an exit. If you are seated, swap places so you are not on the end of a bench or near a doorway.
2. **Make your discomfort a group priority by speaking clearly.** Tell your friends: "I do not want to be alone with this person—please don't let them separate me from you." This removes the "private" nature of the person's persistence and ensures your group is monitoring the situation.
3. **Use the "Ask for Angela" scheme if the venue participates.** Look for posters in the toilets or ask at the bar for "Angela." Staff trained in this scheme will:
   - Take you to a safe, private area.
   - Arrange a taxi or transport for you.
   - Alert security or door staff.
   - Help you leave the venue via a separate exit if necessary.
4. **Identify and approach venue staff directly even without a codeword.** Go to the bar or a member of security and say: "I feel unsafe; this person keeps trying to get me alone and won't take no for an answer." Ask them to keep an eye on you or help you stay separated from the individual.
5. **Implement a strict buddy system for the rest of the night.** If you need to go to the cloakroom, the bathroom, or outside for air, **take at least one trusted person with you**. Do not leave your drink unattended or accept a new one from the person bothering you.
6. **Coordinate a safe group exit.** If you feel the situation is escalating, decide as a group to leave together. Book a taxi to arrive at the door and wait inside until it is there. Ensure no one is left behind and that the person pressuring you does not follow you out.
7. **Use the "Silent Solution" if you are in immediate danger and cannot speak.** Call **999** from your mobile. If you cannot talk, listen for the prompt and press **55**. This tells the operator it is a genuine emergency and connects you to the police.
   - From a landline, if you stay silent and background noise is heard, the operator may automatically connect you to police because landline locations are known.
8. **Contact non-emergency support if you are safe but need guidance.** You can call **101** or use the "Live Chat" function on your local police force's website to report the behaviour or seek advice on what to do next.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you want to file a formal police report.
- You do not need to "prove" that the person’s intent was harmful; your feeling of being unsafe is enough to justify taking action.
- You do not need to explain your reasoning to the person who is pressuring you.

## Important reassurance
It is completely normal to feel flustered, embarrassed, or to "fawn" (act overly polite) when someone is ignoring your boundaries. These are natural survival responses. Wanting to stay with your group is a clear and valid choice, and you are not "ruining the night" by asking for help to maintain that boundary.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only, focused on immediate physical safety and hazard isolation. Further support for processing the experience or making formal complaints can be accessed through specialist services.

## Important note
This guide provides general safety information and is not a substitute for legal advice or emergency services. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. For confidential support regarding sexual harassment or violence, you can contact the Rape Crisis England & Wales 24/7 Support Line on 0808 500 2222.
