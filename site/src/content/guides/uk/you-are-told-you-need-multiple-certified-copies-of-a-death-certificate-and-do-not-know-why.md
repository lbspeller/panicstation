# What to do if… you are told you need multiple certified copies of a death certificate and do not know why (UK)

## Short answer
Order a controlled number of copies (typically 5–10) based on how many financial institutions or government bodies you must notify. Most organisations require an original certified copy—not a photocopy—to prevent fraud, and many will keep the document permanently.

## Do not do these things
- Don't assume photocopies or scanned images will be accepted; most financial and legal institutions legally require an official registrar-issued certified copy.
- Don't hand over your last certified copy to an organisation without confirming if they will return it via tracked post.
- Don't use a third-party website to order copies; only use the official GOV.UK, General Register Office (GRO), or local council registration services.
- Don't delay the initial notification to banks or insurers while waiting for "enough" copies; you can start with one and order more later if needed.
- Don't ignore the "Death Notification Service" or "Tell Us Once," which are free tools designed to reduce the number of individual certificates you need.

## What to do now
1. **Identify every institution holding assets or debts (5 minutes).** Create a list including:
   - Banks and building societies.
   - Life insurance and pension providers.
   - Investment platforms or share registrars.
   - The Probate Registry (if applying for probate).
   - HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC).
2. **Check if the "Tell Us Once" service is available in your area.** This service (available in England, Scotland, and Wales) allows you to notify most government departments (DWP, DVLA, HMRC, Local Council) with a single notification, meaning you do not need separate certificates for these agencies.
3. **Use the "Death Notification Service" for private financial institutions.** This is a free online service that allows you to notify multiple participating UK banks and building societies at once, which can significantly reduce the number of physical certificates you need to purchase.
4. **Order a baseline number of copies during the registration appointment.** In England and Wales (2026), the fee is £12.50 per copy.
   - If using "Tell Us Once" and the "Death Notification Service," 3–5 copies are often sufficient for a simple estate.
   - For complex estates with multiple insurers and non-participating banks, 8–12 copies may be required.
5. **Request a "verification of requirements" from any organisation that insists on a copy.** Ask if they will "check and return" the original or if they require a "permanent deposit" of the certificate.
6. **Keep one "Master Copy" in a safe location at home.** Never send this copy through the post; use it only for in-person appointments where the official can verify it and hand it back immediately.
7. **Use tracked or "Signed For" mail for all document submissions.** Certified copies are expensive and contain sensitive personal data; always ensure you have a tracking number if sending them to a bank or insurer.

## What can wait
- You do not need to order a massive surplus of certificates "just in case"; you can order additional copies online from the General Register Office (GRO) at any time in the future for the same standard fee.
- You do not need to notify utility companies (water, gas, electric) or broadband providers immediately with a certified copy; most will accept a notification via phone or web-form to pause the account without seeing an official document first.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed by the demand for "originals." This requirement is a standard security protocol to protect the deceased's estate from identity theft or fraud. It does not mean you are being difficult or that the process is failing.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for managing document requirements only. It does not cover the legal complexities of probate, inheritance tax, or estate distribution.

## Important note
Requirements for death certificates vary between England/Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. Fees and specific notification services like "Tell Us Once" are jurisdiction-dependent. Always verify current 2026 fees with your local register office before payment.
