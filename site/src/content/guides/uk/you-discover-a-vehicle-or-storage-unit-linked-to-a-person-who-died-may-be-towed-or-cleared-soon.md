# What to do if… you discover a vehicle or storage unit linked to a person who died may be towed or cleared soon (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the organisation that issued the notice immediately to request a temporary "bereavement hold" in writing. Do not attempt to move the vehicle or empty the unit until you have confirmed your authority to do so.

## Do not do these things
- Avoid moving the vehicle or removing contents from the unit if you are not the legally appointed executor or administrator.
- Avoid ignoring "final notice of disposal," "intention to sell," or "clearance deadline" communications.
- Avoid paying any individual demanding immediate cash or bank transfers to stop a removal unless you have verified the company and received an official invoice.
- Avoid assuming a private parking company has the legal right to tow or clamp a vehicle on private land in Great Britain; while usually illegal, they can still escalate debt collection.
- Avoid disposing of any keys, documents, or parking permits found in the vehicle or unit.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the authority responsible for the removal notice.**
   - Official notices (Council, Police, or National Highways) usually feature a government logo, a reference number, and a statutory 7-day or 14-day deadline.
   - Private notices (Parking operators or Landowners) often use yellow adhesive pouches or window stickers and refer to "breach of contract" or "unauthorised parking."
   - Storage notices are typically sent via email or post to the registered account holder, warning of a "lien" or "auction."

2. **Contact the decision-maker to request an immediate 14-day hold.**
   - For vehicles, call the Council Parking Services, the local Police non-emergency line (101), or the pound listed on the notice.
   - For storage, speak to the facility manager or the "accounts receivable" department.
   - State clearly that the owner is deceased and you are the personal representative (or acting for them).
   - Ask for a **written confirmation** that the removal or sale is paused while you provide documentation.

3. **Prepare a digital "Proof Pack" to verify the situation.**
   - Scan or photograph your ID and the death certificate (if available).
   - If probate has not been granted, prepare a signed letter from the executor (or yourself if you are the next of kin) stating your intent to deal with the asset.
   - Include the vehicle registration number (VRM) or the storage unit account number in the subject line of all emails.

4. **Declare a SORN if the vehicle is uninsured or untaxed.**
   - **Visit the GOV.UK "Make a SORN" page immediately to declare the vehicle off-road.**
   - This prevents automatic fines from the DVLA which can accumulate even after a death.
   - If you do not have the V5C (logbook), you must write to the DVLA Sensitive Casework Team (Swansea SA99 1ZZ).

5. **Request a formal statement of arrears for storage units.**
   - Ask the facility to provide a breakdown of all outstanding fees.
   - **Request their specific policy for "executor access" to retrieve essential documents.**
   - Many facilities allow a one-time supervised entry to look for a Will or insurance papers before probate is granted.

6. **Retrieve only "Critical Documents" if access is granted.**
   - Focus on finding the Will, life insurance policies, the V5C (logbook), and keys.
   - **Take photographs of the unit or vehicle interior before and after you touch anything.**
   - Do not remove high-value items or furniture until the estate's inventory is finalized.

## What can wait
- You do not need to pay the full balance of storage arrears today; a hold can often be secured with proof of death alone.
- You do not need to transfer the vehicle's ownership into your name immediately.
- You do not need to decide which beneficiaries will receive the contents of the unit.
- You do not need to start the full probate application before requesting a pause on disposal.

## Important reassurance
Finding a removal notice is a high-stress event, but most councils and reputable storage companies have established "bereavement protocols." Once they are formally notified of the death, the "clock" for disposal usually pauses. Taking these steps is a responsible way to protect the estate’s assets.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization to prevent the physical loss of assets. It does not cover the long-term distribution of the estate, inheritance tax liabilities, or legal disputes between executors.

## Important note
This information is based on UK law as of March 2026. Clamping and towing on private land by private companies is generally a criminal offence in England, Wales, and Scotland under the Protection of Freedoms Act 2012 (and earlier Scottish precedent), but statutory authorities (Police/Council) retain these powers.
