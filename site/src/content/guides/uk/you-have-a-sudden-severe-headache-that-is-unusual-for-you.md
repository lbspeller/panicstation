# What to do if… you have a sudden severe headache that is unusual for you (UK)

## Short answer
Treat any sudden, extremely severe headache—especially one that reaches peak intensity within five minutes—as a medical emergency. Call **999** or **112** immediately and do not attempt to drive yourself to the hospital.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore a "thunderclap" headache that feels like a sudden blow to the head, even if the pain begins to fade.
- Do not drive yourself or others to A&E if you are experiencing vision loss, confusion, or weakness.
- Do not take additional doses of painkillers to see if the pain subsides before seeking help.
- Do not use painkillers for more than two or three days a week without medical supervision, as this can trigger medication-overuse headaches.
- Do not consume alcohol or sedatives, which can mask worsening neurological symptoms.
- Do not wait for a GP appointment if the headache is accompanied by a fever, stiff neck, or a rash that does not fade under a glass.

## What to do now
1. **Find a safe position immediately to prevent injury from a fall.** Sit or lie down on the floor or a bed and loosen any restrictive clothing around your neck.
2. **Identify if you need an emergency ambulance (999 or 112).** Call immediately if the headache:
   - Peaked in intensity within seconds or minutes (thunderclap).
   - Followed a significant blow to the head or a fall.
   - Occurs with a fever, stiff neck, and light sensitivity.
   - Occurs with new weakness in the face, arms, or legs.
   - Occurs with sudden confusion, slurred speech, or loss of consciousness.
   - Occurs with a red, painful eye or sudden blurred/double vision.
3. **Contact NHS 111 for urgent but non-life-threatening assessment.** If the headache is severe and unusual but does not meet the emergency criteria above:
   - In England and Wales, call **111** or use the online service.
   - In Scotland, call **111** (NHS 24) or consult NHS Inform for "Right Care Right Place" guidance.
4. **Prepare a concise summary for the emergency operator or clinician.** 
   - Note the exact time the pain started and how quickly it reached its maximum level.
   - List any current medications, specifically blood thinners (like warfarin, apixaban, or rivaroxaban) or steroids.
   - Mention if you are currently pregnant or have given birth within the last six weeks.
5. **Monitor for worsening sensory cues while help is on the way.** 
   - If you feel nauseated, lie on your side (recovery position) to keep your airway clear.
   - Unlock your front door if you are alone to allow emergency responders access.
   - Keep your phone nearby and avoid looking at bright screens if light sensitivity is present.

## What can wait
- Determining the specific type of headache (e.g., migraine vs. tension) through online research.
- Contacting your regular GP if the symptoms are acute and meet "red flag" criteria.
- Completion of household chores, work tasks, or childcare arrangements that can be delegated.
- Deciding on long-term lifestyle changes or triggers.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant alarm when experiencing a sudden, intense physical sensation like a severe headache. Seeking immediate medical evaluation for an unusual headache is a standard safety protocol; clinicians are trained to rule out serious causes quickly. Many severe headaches have benign triggers, but early assessment is the most effective way to manage risks.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions for acute, unusual headaches. It does not replace professional clinical diagnosis or long-term management plans for chronic headache disorders.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance only and does not constitute medical advice. If you are in distress or believe you are experiencing a stroke or brain haemorrhage, call **999** or **112** immediately.
