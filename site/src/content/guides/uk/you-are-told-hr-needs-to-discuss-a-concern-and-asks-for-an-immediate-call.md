# What to do if… you are told HR needs to discuss a concern and asks for an immediate call (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before you call. Reply in writing asking what the call is about at a high level, who will attend, whether notes will be taken, and ask to speak from a private place at a specific time soon.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign in the heat of the moment.
- Do not guess, speculate, or fill silence with details you are not sure about.
- Do not apologise for things you do not understand yet or accept blame just to end the call.
- Do not delete, edit, or tidy up work records or messages after being told there is a concern.
- Do not secretly record the call without checking the policy and getting agreement.
- Do not share the issue widely at work or ask colleagues what they have heard.
- Do not treat an investigation chat as harmless just because it is described as informal.

## What to do now
1. **Reply in writing to slow the situation down and get the basics.** Send something short, such as: “I’m available to speak. Please can you confirm the topic at a high level, whether this is an investigation meeting, disciplinary hearing, grievance meeting, or another type of call, who will be on the call, and whether notes will be taken. I can speak at [time later today] from a private place.”

2. **Move the call to a private, practical time.** If they push for right now, ask for a short delay: “I can call in 30 to 60 minutes once I’m somewhere private and can take notes.”

3. **Check the workplace policy quickly.** Look at your disciplinary, investigation, or grievance policy on the intranet, handbook, or contract. Only look for the immediate basics: what type of meeting this is, who usually attends, what notice is usually given, and whether the policy allows a companion at this stage.

4. **Ask about a companion if this is formal, or if you need support.**
   - If it is a disciplinary hearing or a formal grievance meeting, say that you want to use your right to be accompanied and ask to arrange a time your companion can attend.
   - If it is an investigation meeting, there is not usually a legal right to be accompanied, but you can still ask whether your employer will allow a companion as good practice.
   - If you need someone there because of a disability-related need, say that and ask what adjustment can be made.
   - If you are in a union, contact your rep and tell HR you are seeking representation.

5. **Start the call by confirming the frame.** Say: “Before we start, can you confirm what type of meeting this is and what you need from me today?” Write down the answer, the names of everyone present, and whether notes are being taken.

6. **Keep your answers narrow and factual.** Answer only what you know. Use phrases like: “I do not want to guess,” “I need to check that,” or “I can come back in writing.”

7. **Ask what happens next before the call ends.** Ask what the concern is in plain terms, whether you need to provide anything, whether there will be another meeting, who will contact you, and by when.

8. **Send a short follow-up email after the call.** Summarise your understanding of the concern, what type of meeting it was, and any next steps or dates. Ask HR to correct anything you have misunderstood.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to raise a grievance, appeal, resign, or take formal legal action.
- You do not need to produce a detailed written statement during the first unexpected call.
- You do not need to prove your case immediately.
- You do not need to tell colleagues or gather workplace opinions today.

## Important reassurance
Vague HR wording can happen because the employer is still gathering facts or managing confidentiality. A calm first response is not about having the perfect answer; it is about getting clarity, avoiding rushed admissions, keeping a record, and making sure you understand the process.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the message and the initial call only. Later decisions about statements, companions, grievances, appeals, settlement discussions, or legal options may need specialist help once you know what the concern is.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. Workplace procedures vary by employer and situation. If the issue becomes a formal disciplinary or grievance process, or if you may be at risk of dismissal, consider getting support from your union, an employment adviser, or another appropriate specialist.
