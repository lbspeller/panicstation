# What to do if… you discover new email forwarding rules you did not set up (UK)

## Short answer
Delete the forwarding rules immediately to stop information theft, then use a different device to change your password and sign out of all other active sessions.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore the rule even if it seems harmless; hackers use these to intercept bank and password reset codes.
- Don't use the "forgot password" link in any suspicious emails to fix the problem; go directly to your provider's login page.
- Don't change your password on the same device if you suspect it has a virus or malware.
- Don't assume the account is safe just because you deleted the rule; you must still update your 2-step verification and passwords.
- Don't reuse a password from another site for your email account.

## What to do now
1. **Delete all unauthorized rules and forwarding addresses immediately.** 
   - Check "Forwarding and POP/IMAP" settings.
   - Check "Rules" or "Filters" for anything that says "Delete," "Move," or "Mark as read."
2. **Access your account from a known clean device.** If possible, use a different computer or a mobile phone that you know is secure to perform the following security steps.
3. **Change your email password to a strong, unique phrase.**
   - Use three random words to create a password that is hard to guess but easy to remember.
   - Ensure this password is not used on any other website.
4. **Sign out of all other active sessions and devices.** 
   - Look for "Security" or "Recent Activity" in your settings.
   - Select the option to "Sign out of all other sessions" or "Remove all trusted devices."
5. **Verify and update your recovery information.** 
   - Ensure the recovery phone number and backup email address are yours.
   - Remove any recovery options you do not recognize.
6. **Enable 2-step verification (2SV) if it is not already active.** 
   - This adds a second layer of security, such as a code sent to your phone, which prevents hackers from getting back in even if they know your password.
7. **Check your "Sent" and "Deleted" folders for suspicious activity.** 
   - Look for emails sent to your bank, social media, or other services.
   - Check for "password reset" confirmations that you did not request.
8. **Report the incident to the appropriate UK authorities.**
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** Report the cybercrime to Report Fraud (the 2026 replacement for Action Fraud) via reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.
   - **Scotland:** Contact Police Scotland by calling 101.
   - **Phishing:** Forward any suspicious emails that preceded this to report@phishing.gov.uk.

## What can wait
- You don't need to notify all your contacts right now; wait until you have confirmed the account is secure and you have checked your "Sent" folder for spam.
- You don't need to perform a full factory reset of your primary computer immediately, provided you have secured the account from a different device first.
- You don't need to identify exactly how the hacker got in before taking these protective steps.

## Important reassurance
Discovering a forwarding rule is a clear warning sign, but it means you have caught the intrusion while you still have access. By deleting the rules and updating your security settings now, you can effectively lock the intruder out and prevent further damage to your other online accounts.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a compromised email account. If you have lost money or sensitive personal data, you should seek further advice from your bank or a specialist identity protection service.

## Important note
This guide is for general information and does not constitute professional IT or legal advice. Email providers have different layouts; if you cannot find your settings, search your provider's official help pages for "delete forwarding rules." If you are locked out of your account entirely, use your provider's "Account Recovery" process immediately.
