# What to do if… you receive a penalty notice demanding payment within days and warning of enforcement action (UK)

## Short answer
Stop and verify the notice using an official government or local authority website found through an independent search before providing any payment or data. Treat bailiff or enforcement agent threats as a reason to verify immediately, but do not make a payment directly through links provided in a text or email.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links or scan QR codes contained within a text message or email demanding fine payment.
- Do not use phone numbers provided in the notice itself until you have cross-referenced them with an official government (.gov.uk) or council website.
- Do not pay using gift cards, cryptocurrency, or bank transfers to individual names.
- Do not provide personal data, bank details, or one-time passcodes (OTP) to anyone who has contacted you unexpectedly.
- Do not allow anyone entry to your home based solely on a verbal claim of being an enforcement agent.
- Do not assume a notice is genuine simply because it contains your name or a realistic-looking logo.

## What to do now
1. **Document the notice without interacting with any links or buttons.** Save a screenshot of the text or email, or take a clear photograph of the physical letter and its envelope.
2. **Forward suspicious SMS messages to 7726 and emails to the official reporting service.**
   - Forward any text message to 7726 (the UK's free reporting service).
   - Forward suspicious emails to report@phishing.gov.uk.
3. **Identify the specific type of penalty the notice claims to represent.**
   - A Penalty Charge Notice (PCN) from a council for parking or bus lane contraventions.
   - A Fixed Penalty Notice (FPN) from the police or a government agency.
   - A "Notice of Enforcement" from a bailiff firm regarding a court-ordered debt.
4. **Locate the official payment or verification portal via a search engine.** Type the name of the issuing council or agency (e.g., "Manchester City Council pay PCN" or "HMCTS pay a fine") into a search engine yourself rather than following the provided link.
5. **Enter the reference number into the official portal to check the status of the fine.** If the official government or council site does not recognize the reference number or the amount, the notice is likely fraudulent.
6. **Verify the status of any individual claiming to be an enforcement agent in England or Wales.**
   - Use the Ministry of Justice Certificated Enforcement Agent Register to search for the person’s name.
   - Confirm that at least 7 clear days (excluding Sundays and bank holidays) have passed since a written Notice of Enforcement was issued before any physical visit occurs.
7. **Contact your bank's fraud department immediately if you have already shared financial details.** Use the number on the back of your card or the bank’s official app to report that you may have interacted with a fraudulent notice.
8. **Report the incident to the appropriate national authority.**
   - In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland: Use the "Report Fraud" website (reportfraud.police.uk).
   - In Scotland: Call Police Scotland on 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file an appeal or a formal dispute while you are still verifying if the notice is real.
- You do not need to negotiate a payment plan until the debt is confirmed as genuine.
- You do not need to provide a statement or explanation to the sender while the notice is unverified.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel an immediate sense of urgency or fear when a notice mentions "enforcement action" or "court." These documents are written to command attention. Taking 15 minutes to verify the source through an official channel is a standard safety procedure and is the most effective way to protect your finances.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying the authenticity of a penalty notice and preventing fraudulent payments. It does not cover the legal process of appealing a genuine fine or managing long-term debt.

## Important note
This information applies to the UK but legal terminology and enforcement rules differ between England/Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. This is not legal advice; if a notice is found to be genuine, you may need to seek specialist advice from Citizens Advice or a solicitor.
