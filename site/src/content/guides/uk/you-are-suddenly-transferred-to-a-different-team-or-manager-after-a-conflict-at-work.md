# What to do if… you are suddenly transferred to a different team or manager after a conflict at work (UK)

## Short answer
Do not sign any documents or provide verbal agreement during the meeting. Request the full proposal in writing and state that you require reasonable time to review the terms before responding.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any "acknowledgment" or "acceptance" forms under pressure.
- Do not provide an immediate verbal agreement just to exit the meeting.
- Do not resign or walk out of the job on the spot.
- Do not assume that working in the new role without a written protest is safe; this can be seen as "implied agreement."
- Do not record the meeting covertly if your staff handbook prohibits it, as this may provide grounds for further disciplinary action.
- Do not rely on verbal promises that the demotion is "temporary" or "just for now."

## What to do now
1. **Request the full details of the proposed change in writing immediately.** **The document must specify the new job title, the specific changes to your duties, any reduction in pay or benefits, and the proposed effective date.**
   - Ask if the demotion is a disciplinary sanction, a result of redundancy/restructuring, or a capability (performance) measure.
2. **State clearly that you are not agreeing to the change today.** **Use neutral language such as, "I need to see the full terms in writing and take time to consider my position before I can respond."**
3. **Establish a written paper trail of the interaction before the end of the day.** **Send an email to the manager or HR representative who held the meeting, confirming that you were asked to accept a demotion and that you have requested the terms in writing for review.**
4. **Locate your current employment contract and any "variation of contract" letters you have received previously.** **Check for "flexibility clauses" or "variation clauses" that might allow the employer to change your duties, but note that these rarely allow a significant drop in pay without further process.**
5. **Determine if the meeting was a formal disciplinary hearing and check your right to be accompanied.** **If the demotion is a formal disciplinary outcome, you have a statutory right to be accompanied by a trade union representative or a workplace colleague at the meeting where this is decided.**
   - A companion can address the hearing and confer with you, but they cannot answer questions on your behalf unless the employer permits it.
6. **Ask for the formal appeal procedure if the demotion is being imposed as a disciplinary sanction.** **Under the Acas Code of Practice, you should be given the right to appeal the decision, typically within 5 working days of receiving the written outcome.**
7. **Submit a written "working under protest" notice if the employer imposes the change without your consent.** **If you continue to work in the new role while disputing it, you must state in writing that you are doing so "under protest" to prevent your continued presence from being interpreted as a legal acceptance of the new terms.**
8. **Contact Acas (Great Britain) or the Labour Relations Agency (Northern Ireland) for a specific assessment of your contract.** **Provide them with the exact wording of any clauses your employer is citing as justification for the change.**
9. **Check your next payslip for any unauthorised deductions.** **If your pay is reduced without your written agreement or a specific contractual right, notify payroll in writing immediately that you do not authorise the deduction.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to accept the new role permanently today.
- You do not need to decide whether to file a claim for constructive dismissal or breach of contract immediately; you generally have three months (less one day) from the date of the breach to begin the tribunal process.
- You do not need to draft a long rebuttal or emotional response to the allegations tonight.

## Important reassurance
Workplace pressure is designed to secure fast agreement, but employment law in the UK is built to prevent unilateral changes to fundamental terms like pay and status. Taking a pause to get the facts in writing is a standard procedural right, and doing so does not make you "difficult"—it makes you compliant with good practice.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only. It focuses on slowing down the immediate pressure of a demotion meeting and preserving your legal rights. Future actions will depend on your specific length of service and the reason provided for the demotion.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and does not constitute legal advice. Employment rights vary significantly based on your specific contract, length of service (specifically the current 2-year threshold for unfair dismissal protection in GB, which is scheduled to change in 2027), and whether you are in Northern Ireland. Always consult Acas, the LRA, or a qualified legal professional before making final decisions about your employment.
