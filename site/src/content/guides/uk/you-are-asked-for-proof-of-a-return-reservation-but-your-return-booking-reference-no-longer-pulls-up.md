# What to do if… you are asked for proof of a return reservation but your return booking reference no longer pulls up (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate a secondary document such as an e-ticket receipt, PDF itinerary, or bank transaction email that contains your full name and flight details to use as manual proof.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to use "fake" onward tickets or temporary reservation services that do not issue a real e-ticket number.
- Do not delete any emails or clear your browser history/cache until the issue is resolved.
- Do not hand your phone to airport staff or border officials while it is unlocked; hold the device yourself or provide a physical copy.
- Do not assume a "not found" error on an airline app means the ticket is cancelled; system sync errors and schedule changes often hide valid bookings.
- Do not post screenshots of your situation on social media, as barcodes or partial PNRs can be used to access your data.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a stationary position outside the immediate queue.** Step aside to a seating area or the side of the check-in desk so you can search your records without obstructing others.
2. **Search your email for the 13-digit e-ticket number (ETKT).** This number is distinct from the 6-character booking reference (PNR). Search for:
   - The word "receipt", "e-ticket", "itinerary", or "confirmation".
   - The airline's name or the travel agent's name.
   - The date you made the original purchase.
3. **Verify the surname format used for the lookup.** Airline systems often fail if names are entered with spaces, hyphens, or middle names incorrectly. Try these variations in the "Manage Booking" tool:
   - Surname only (no middle names).
   - Surname and First Name joined together (e.g., SMITHJOHN).
   - Surname including any hyphens or apostrophes exactly as they appear on your passport.
4. **Access the booking via the third-party agent portal if applicable.** If you booked through a site like Expedia, SkyScanner, or a high-street travel agent, log in to *their* portal rather than the airline’s. The agent may have a different internal reference number that links to the airline's system.
5. **Request a manual lookup at the airline’s customer service or sales desk.** Provide your passport and the approximate date/time of the flight. Request that they:
   - Locate the booking by name and flight number.
   - Print a physical copy of the itinerary.
   - Re-send the confirmation email to you immediately.
6. **Compile a "secondary evidence" pack for immigration.** If the PNR remains broken, gather these items to show as a single set of evidence:
   - The original booking confirmation email (even if the link is dead).
   - A bank or credit card statement showing the payment to the airline or agent.
   - Any previous boarding passes from the outbound leg of the same trip.
7. **Purchase a fully refundable ticket as a last-resort contingency.** If you are at risk of being denied boarding or entry and cannot retrieve the booking, buy a new ticket that specifically lists "fully refundable" in the fare rules. Ensure you receive a confirmed e-ticket number immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause of the technical error (e.g., system migration or agent failure) right now.
- You do not need to initiate a formal complaint or refund request until you have secured passage.
- You do not need to check your bank for a refund unless you suspect the airline has already cancelled the seat without notice.

## Important reassurance
Booking references frequently fail to "pull up" due to minor database sync issues, schedule changes that generate a new PNR, or simple character-entry errors. Having the reservation disappear from an app does not automatically mean your seat is gone; the data usually still exists in the airline's core reservation system.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for document retrieval and immediate travel authorization. It does not cover long-term legal claims or international visa applications.

## Important note
Carriers and border authorities have the legal right to deny boarding or entry if proof of return is a condition of travel (such as under ETIAS or EES rules). Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act may provide protection if the booking cost over £100 and was made directly with the airline, but may not apply if an intermediary (third-party agent) was used.
