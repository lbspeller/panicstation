# What to do if… water starts dripping through your ceiling and you cannot see the source (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as an **electrical and structural hazard**. **Isolate the electricity for the affected room at the consumer unit (fuse box), shut off your internal stopcock (stop tap), and move everyone away from the drip zone.**

## Do not do these things
- Don’t touch any light fittings, switches, or sockets that are wet or near the drip.
- Don’t stand on wet flooring or wet carpets while touching any electrical switches or the consumer unit.
- Don’t poke or attempt to "drain" a bulging or sagging ceiling, as it may collapse.
- Don’t climb into the loft or crawl space directly above a wet/sagging ceiling.
- Don’t assume the leak is small because the drip is slow; water often pools before appearing.
- Don’t use a ladder on a wet floor to investigate the ceiling.

## What to do now
1. **Clear the area underneath the drip immediately.**  
   **Move people, pets, and valuables (electronics, documents) out of the room or away from the affected area.**  
   - If the ceiling is sagging or bulging, treat it as an imminent collapse risk and do not enter the room.

2. **Isolate the electricity safely.**  
   **Go to your consumer unit (fuse box) and switch off the circuit for the affected area only if you can do so while remaining completely dry.**  
   - If you must stand in water or if the consumer unit itself is damp, do not touch it.  
   - If in doubt, turn off the **Main Switch** to kill all power to the property.  
   - If water is entering the main electrical intake or meter, stay away and call **105** (the national electricity emergency number).

3. **Contain the water to prevent floor damage.**  
   **Place a bucket or deep container under the drip and lay a towel inside it to stop splashing.**  
   - Move any nearby furniture that is not yet wet.

4. **Shut off the main water supply.**  
   **Locate your internal stopcock (usually under the kitchen sink, in a hallway cupboard, or under the stairs) and turn it fully clockwise.**  
   - If it is a lever-type valve, turn it 90 degrees until it is perpendicular to the pipe.

5. **Alert residents in the property above you.**  
   **If you live in a flat or terrace, knock on the door above and ask them to check for leaks from baths, toilets, washing machines, or boilers.**  
   - Ask them to shut off their water supply immediately while the source is investigated.

6. **Contact the appropriate emergency responder.**  
   - **If you rent:** Call your **landlord or letting agent’s emergency repair line** immediately. Under 2026 safeguarding (Awaab's Law), social landlords must respond to emergency hazards within 24 hours.
   - **If you own:** Call an **emergency plumber** (check they are WaterSafe approved).  
   - **If you are in a block of flats:** Contact the **building manager or concierge**, as the leak may be in a communal riser pipe.

7. **Document the situation for insurance.**  
   **Take photos or video of the drip, the ceiling damage, and any damage to your belongings once you are in a safe position.**

## What can wait
- Mopping up large amounts of water (focus on containment first).
- Deciding which neighbour is liable for the damage.
- Removing damp plaster or attempting to dry the ceiling with heaters.
- Filing a full insurance claim (initial notification is enough).

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when you cannot find the source of a leak. Water can travel long distances along joists and pipes before dripping. You are not expected to be a plumber; your only job right now is to **stop the flow and stay clear of electrical risks.**

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization only. Once the leak is stopped, you will require a professional assessment of the electrical system and the structural integrity of the ceiling.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization. If you see **sparks, smell burning, or the ceiling begins to crack loudly**, evacuate the property and call **999** for the Fire and Rescue Service. Do not attempt any DIY repairs on pressurized pipes or live electrics.
