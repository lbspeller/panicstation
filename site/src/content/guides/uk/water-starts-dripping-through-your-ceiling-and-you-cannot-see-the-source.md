# What to do if… water starts dripping through your ceiling and you cannot see the source (UK)

## Short answer
Move everyone away from the wet area, especially if water is near lights, sockets, switches, a smoke alarm, or appliances. If you can do it without standing in water or touching anything wet, switch off the affected circuit or the main switch, then turn off your water at the stopcock.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t touch light fittings, switches, sockets, extension leads, or plugged-in appliances near the drip.
- Don’t stand under a bulging, sagging, cracking, or water-heavy ceiling.
- Don’t poke a bulging or sagging ceiling to drain it.
- Don’t climb up under a wet ceiling to trace the leak.
- Don’t keep power on in the affected room because it looks like “only a drip”.
- Don’t turn the power back on after water has reached electrics until a qualified person has said it is safe.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safer first.**  
   Move people and pets out from under the leak. Keep clear of any wet lights, sockets, smoke alarms, appliances, or extension leads. Put a chair, box, or sign in the way if it helps stop someone walking underneath.

2. **Reduce electrical danger only if you can do it safely.**  
   If you can reach the consumer unit without standing in water or touching anything wet, switch off the affected circuit. If you are unsure which circuit it is, use the main switch. If the consumer unit, meter area, switches, sockets, or floor around them are wet or damp, keep away and get urgent help instead.

3. **Use the right urgent contact for electricity risk.**  
   If there is sparking, a burning smell, damaged electricity supply equipment, or a power-cut or electricity-network concern, call 105 or the local electricity network operator. For wet internal wiring, lights, sockets, or appliances, use a qualified electrician before anything is turned back on.

4. **Contain the drip without going under danger.**  
   If the ceiling is not bulging or sagging, place a bucket or bowl under the drip and put a towel inside it to reduce splashing. Move electronics, furniture, bedding, paperwork, and valuables away from the wet area.

5. **Turn off your water supply.**  
   Turn the stopcock or inside stop valve clockwise. It is usually under the kitchen sink, in a cupboard, near where the water pipe enters the home, or sometimes in a communal area for flats. Do not force it if it will not turn.

6. **Check the most likely source without taking risks.**  
   Look from safe, dry areas only. Check whether a bath, shower, toilet, sink, washing machine, dishwasher, boiler, water tank, or radiator above or nearby is overflowing or leaking. If it is raining hard, also treat a roof or gutter leak as possible.

7. **If you are in a flat, alert the people who may be able to stop it.**  
   Contact the flat above, adjacent neighbours if relevant, and any concierge, building manager, managing agent, or freeholder emergency line. Ask them to check bathrooms, kitchens, utility cupboards, tanks, and appliances, and to turn off their water if they find a leak.

8. **Contact the person responsible for urgent repairs.**  
   If you rent, report it to your landlord, letting agent, housing association, council landlord, or repairs line straight away, using any emergency or out-of-hours route you have. If you own, contact an emergency plumber. If the leak may involve a communal pipe, roof, riser, or shared area, contact the building manager or freeholder agent as well.

9. **Take quick evidence, then step back.**  
   Take a few photos or a short video of the drip, ceiling, affected electrics, and damaged items if you can do this safely. Then keep people out of the area and wait for the leak to be stopped and the ceiling and electrics checked.

## What can wait
- Working out exactly whose fault it is.
- Detailed insurance forms or arguments about liability.
- Full cleaning beyond safe basic containment.
- Opening the ceiling, pulling down plaster, or starting DIY drying.
- Deciding whether anything needs replacing after the leak is stopped.

## Important reassurance
You do not have to find the source before acting. The useful first moves are to keep people away, reduce electrical risk if safe, stop the water if you can, and get the right repair contact involved.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. After the leak is stopped, later decisions may need a plumber, electrician, roofer, building manager, insurer, landlord, or other specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If anyone is in immediate danger, there is fire, collapse risk, serious electrical danger, or you cannot safely stay in the property, call 999.
