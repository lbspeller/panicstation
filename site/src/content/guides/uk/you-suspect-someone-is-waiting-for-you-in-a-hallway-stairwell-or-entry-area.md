# What to do if… you suspect someone is waiting for you in a hallway, stairwell, or entry area (UK)

## Short answer
Do not enter the hallway, stairwell, or entry area to check. Move back to a safer, busier place and call 999 if there may be immediate danger, a crime happening now, violence, threats, or the situation could become heated or violent very soon.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “just go quickly” through the hallway or stairwell to get it over with.
- Do not confront, challenge, or shout to find out who is there.
- Do not unlock the entry door or hold it open while you look around.
- Do not go deeper into a stairwell, corridor, or lobby to check.
- Do not let embarrassment push you into entering alone if your instincts say no.
- Do not film, photograph, or stare at the person if doing that could make you less safe.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance immediately.** Step back to the street, a well-lit public place, or an open shop, café, reception desk, or staffed area. If you are in a vehicle, keep the doors locked and leave the area if you can do that safely.

2. **Call for help with the right urgency.**
   - **Call 999** if there is immediate danger, a crime may be happening now, someone is using or threatening violence, or the situation could become heated or violent very soon.
   - **Call 101** or use the relevant police online contact route if there is no immediate danger but you want to report suspicious behaviour or ask for police advice.

3. **Stay where other people can see you.** Do not wait in the doorway, lobby, stairwell, lift, or a quiet corridor. Choose a place with lighting, staff, passers-by, cameras, or an easy exit.

4. **Ask for an on-site escort without going back in.** Use your phone or intercom to contact a concierge, security desk, building manager, neighbour, receptionist, or trusted person. Ask them to meet you outside the entrance or watch from a safe position while you enter.

5. **Make it easy for help to find you.** Tell the call handler:
   - the exact address and which entrance you are near,
   - where you are standing now,
   - what you saw or heard,
   - whether you can still see the person,
   - a brief description only if you can give it safely.

6. **If you cannot speak safely, still call 999.** Try to speak, whisper, cough, or tap if you can. If you are calling from a mobile and you cannot speak or answer questions, press 55 or tap when prompted so the call can be transferred to the police. From a landline, stay on the line if you can.

7. **If you are already inside, move only to the nearest safer place.** Go to a staffed reception area, an occupied public area, or behind a lockable door you control. Lock the door if you can, call 999 if you feel in danger, and do not move deeper into quiet corridors or stairs to get home.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the person or find out for sure what they are doing.
- You do not need to check CCTV, knock on neighbours’ doors, or post on social media right now.
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a formal report if the immediate risk has passed.
- You do not need to explain your choice to anyone before getting to a safer place.
- If this becomes an ongoing concern about a public place feeling unsafe, you can later check whether StreetSafe is appropriate; it is not for emergencies or crimes in progress.
- If you have a hearing or speech impairment and it is not an emergency, you can later check the relevant police contact page for textphone, Relay UK, or other accessible contact options.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to treat a strong feeling that something is wrong as a safety signal. The goal is not to prove danger or be brave; it is to avoid walking into a place where you may have fewer exits and less help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the moment when you are deciding whether to enter or stay in a hallway, stairwell, or entry area. If this happens repeatedly, or you think you are being targeted, later decisions may need specialist safety, housing, legal, or police support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, security, or professional advice. If you feel at immediate risk, prioritise getting to a safer place and contacting emergency services.
