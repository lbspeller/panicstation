# What to do if… you suspect someone is waiting for you in a hallway, stairwell, or entry area (UK)

## Short answer
Do not enter the building or the specific area where you feel unsafe. Move immediately to a well-lit, public location with other people and call 999 if you believe you are in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t enter the hallway or stairwell to "get it over with" or "rush past" the person.
- Don’t confront, challenge, or shout into the area to see if anyone responds.
- Don’t unlock the main entry door or hold it open while you investigate.
- Don’t go deeper into the building or corridors to verify if the person is still there.
- Don’t ignore your instincts or let the fear of "making a scene" force you into a vulnerable space.

## What to do now
1. **Create physical distance immediately by leaving the immediate vicinity.** **Step back to the street, a busy pavement, or a nearby open shop or café where other people are present.** If you are in a vehicle, stay inside with the doors locked and drive away from the immediate area if it is safe to do so.
2. **Call the police using the level of urgency that matches your situation.** **Dial 999 if you believe a crime is about to happen, the person is following you, or you feel at risk of immediate violence.** Use 101 for non-emergencies where you want to report suspicious behavior but do not feel you are in imminent danger.
3. **Use the Silent Solution if you are in danger but cannot speak.** **Dial 999 and, if prompted by the automated system on a mobile phone, press 55 to be transferred to the police.** If calling from a landline and you cannot speak, stay on the line; if the operator hears only background noise, they will transfer the call to the police, and the line will stay active for 45 seconds even if you hang up.
4. **Involve a trusted contact without returning to the hazard area.** **Call someone you trust and ask them to stay on the phone with you until you are in a secure location.** Do not ask them to come out and "check" for you unless they are trained security or police; instead, ask them to meet you at a safe, public neutral ground.
5. **Request a verified escort or observation if the building is staffed.** **Contact your building manager, concierge, or security team via intercom or phone and ask them to meet you at the entrance.** If you are in a flat, you may ask a neighbor you know well to watch from their window or doorway while you enter, but only if this does not put them at risk.
6. **Provide specific location and descriptive details to the emergency operator.** **Tell the call handler the exact address, the specific entrance (e.g., "the rear car park door"), and a description of the person or the sounds that caused your suspicion.** State clearly whether you can still see the person or if they are hidden from view.
7. **Secure yourself in a "safe pocket" if you are already inside the building.** **Move to the nearest lockable room, a staffed reception, or a neighbor’s flat immediately rather than trying to reach your own door through quiet corridors.** Lock the door behind you and do not open it until you have verified who is on the other side or the police have arrived.

## What can wait
- You do not need to confirm the identity or intent of the person before seeking safety.
- You do not need to check CCTV footage or door-to-door logs yourself right now.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether you want to press charges or make a formal statement.
- You do not need to notify your landlord or building management until you are physically safe.

## Important reassurance
It is a normal and protective response to feel a "gut instinct" that a situation is unsafe. Choosing to avoid a quiet or enclosed space because of a suspicious presence is a logical safety precaution, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate personal safety concern. It does not cover long-term security upgrades, legal harassment claims, or ongoing stalking investigations which require professional consultation.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and cannot guarantee personal safety or specific police response times. In any situation involving potential violence or a threat to life, always prioritize reaching a public area and contacting emergency services immediately.
