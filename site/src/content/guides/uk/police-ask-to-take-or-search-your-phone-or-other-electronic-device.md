# What to do if… police ask to take or search your phone or other electronic device (UK)

## Short answer

Pause and ask: **“Is this voluntary, or are you taking it under a legal power or warrant?”** Do not volunteer to unlock the device, share a passcode, or sign an agreement while you are still unclear.

## Do not do these things

- Do not hand over your passcode, PIN, passwords, or cloud account details just to be helpful.
- Do not unlock the device in front of police unless you have chosen to do so after understanding what is being requested.
- Do not sign a “consent”, “agreement”, “extraction”, or “voluntary provision” form before you understand it.
- Do not delete messages, files, call logs, photos, apps, or accounts in a panic.
- Do not physically resist if police say they are taking the device anyway.
- Do not argue legal points in the moment if that is making things more heated; focus on asking for the legal basis and written records.

## What to do now

1. **Ask whether this is voluntary or compulsory.** Say:  
   **“I need to understand whether you are asking for my agreement, or whether you are taking it under a legal power or warrant.”**

2. **Ask what “examination” means.** Ask calmly:
   - “Are you asking to extract data from the device?”
   - “Are you asking to look at the device now, or keep it for later examination?”
   - “What information are you looking for?”
   - “What legal power or written authority are you relying on?”

3. **If they say it is voluntary, do not decide on the spot.** Say:  
   **“Please give me the written notice or form explaining what data you want, why you want it, what will be extracted, and how it may be kept. I am not agreeing until I have read and understood it.”**

4. **If they ask for a passcode or unlock method, separate an informal request from a formal requirement.** Say:  
   **“Are you asking me voluntarily, or are you serving a formal notice or using a legal power requiring this?”**  
   If a formal notice or requirement is mentioned, ask for legal advice immediately before answering if you can.

5. **If you are detained or in custody, ask for legal advice immediately.** Say:  
   **“I want legal advice before I agree to anything about my device or access to it.”**  
   If you are not detained, you can still say: **“I am not agreeing right now. I want to contact a solicitor first.”**

6. **If police say they are seizing the device, ask for the basis and a record.** Ask:
   - “What power or warrant are you using?”
   - “Please give me a written record, property record, receipt, or reference number for what is being taken.”
   - “Who should I contact about return of the device or practical access issues?”

7. **Keep the device locked and avoid sudden changes.** Do not type your PIN where it can be seen. Do not open apps, search the device, delete anything, or change settings while you are under pressure.

8. **Flag legally privileged or confidential material if relevant.** Say:  
   **“This device may contain legally privileged or confidential material. Please record that and explain how it will be handled.”**

9. **Explain immediate practical risks without unlocking the device for a search.** If losing the phone affects two-factor authentication, urgent medical access, work, benefits, caring responsibilities, or contact with children, say so and ask whether a practical accommodation is possible.

10. **Write down what happened as soon as you can.** Record the date and time, officer names or numbers, station or location, what they said the legal basis was, what you were asked to sign, what was taken, and what paperwork you received.

## What can wait

- You do not need to explain your whole digital life now.
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, challenge the seizure, or make a formal application.
- You do not need to work out every privacy issue immediately; first get clarity, avoid voluntary access you do not understand, and get legal advice.
- You do not need to remember every legal term. “Is this voluntary or are you using a legal power?” is enough for the first pause.

## Important reassurance

It is reasonable to feel pressured when police ask for a phone, laptop, tablet, or passcode. Asking whether it is voluntary, asking for paperwork, keeping the device locked, and getting legal advice are calm first steps, not an admission of anything.

## Scope note

These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and prevent irreversible mistakes. Later decisions may need specialist legal help and may depend on whether you are a witness, victim, suspect, or device owner, what legal power is being used, and where in the UK this is happening.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Police powers and device-extraction rules can apply differently depending on the facts, so prioritise safety, clarity on voluntary versus legal power, written records, and prompt legal advice.
