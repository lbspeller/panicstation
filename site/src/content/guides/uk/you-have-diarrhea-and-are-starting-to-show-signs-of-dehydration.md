# What to do if… you have diarrhea and are starting to show signs of dehydration (UK)

## Short answer
Start rehydrating now with frequent small sips, ideally with an oral rehydration solution from a pharmacy. Get urgent medical advice if you cannot keep fluids down, dehydration signs are not improving after using oral rehydration solution, or you feel confused, very drowsy, faint, or seriously unwell.

## Do not do these things
- Do not push through without drinking because you are afraid it will worsen diarrhoea.
- Do not chug a large amount quickly if you feel sick; take small sips often instead.
- Do not use fruit juice or fizzy drinks to replace fluids, especially for children, because they can make diarrhoea worse.
- Do not use alcohol to settle your stomach.
- Do not make baby formula weaker.
- Do not give children under 12 loperamide or other medicine to stop diarrhoea unless a doctor prescribes it.
- Do not use loperamide to cover up serious symptoms. If there is bloody diarrhoea, bleeding from the bottom, severe tummy pain, or you feel very unwell, get urgent advice first.

## What to do now
1. **Switch to rehydration mode now.**
   - Take small sips every few minutes.
   - If you feel sick or have vomited, start with tiny sips and slowly increase.
   - Keep water nearby so you do not have to keep getting up while dizzy.

2. **Use an oral rehydration solution if you can.**
   - Ask a pharmacist for oral rehydration solution sachets.
   - Mix the sachet exactly as the packet says.
   - Do not guess the amount of water.

3. **Check whether the dehydration signs are easing.**
   Look for:
   - peeing a bit more often,
   - pee becoming lighter,
   - dizziness on standing settling,
   - thirst, dry mouth, or dry lips easing.

4. **Ask for help before it becomes extreme.**
   - Speak to a pharmacist if you or your child over 5 has dehydration signs such as dark, strong-smelling pee or peeing less than usual.
   - Use the urgent health advice route for where you are if you still have dehydration signs after using oral rehydration solution, keep being sick and cannot keep fluid down, have bloody diarrhoea or bleeding from the bottom, or are worried about a baby under 12 months.
   - In England or Wales, call 111 or use the local NHS 111 service.
   - In Scotland, phone NHS 24 on 111.
   - In Northern Ireland, contact your GP out-of-hours service when your GP surgery is closed, or your GP surgery when it is open.

5. **Call 999 or go to A&E now if there are danger signs.**
   Call 999 or go to A&E if you or someone else:
   - is confused, not responding as usual, very sleepy, or difficult to wake,
   - has blue, grey, pale, or blotchy skin, lips, or tongue,
   - has skin that feels cold,
   - is having severe difficulty breathing or taking lots of quick, short breaths,
   - collapses,
   - vomits blood or vomit that looks like coffee grounds,
   - has a stiff neck with pain when looking at bright light,
   - has a sudden severe headache or sudden severe tummy pain,
   - may have swallowed something poisonous.

6. **Reduce spread while you recover.**
   - Wash hands often with soap and water.
   - Avoid preparing food for other people if possible.
   - Stay off work or school until at least 48 hours after diarrhoea or vomiting has stopped.
   - Do not use a swimming pool until at least 48 hours after symptoms stop.

7. **Escalate earlier for higher-risk people.**
   Get same-day advice if the unwell person is a baby, young child, older adult, pregnant, has a weakened immune system, or has a long-term condition that increases dehydration risk.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to decide about tests, antibiotics, or long-term diet changes now.
- You do not need to force food. Fluids come first; eat when you feel able.
- You do not need to clean the whole home immediately. Focus on handwashing, the toilet area, and any soiled clothes or bedding.

## Important reassurance
Diarrhoea can make you feel weak, shaky, and lightheaded because fluid and salts are being lost. Starting oral rehydration early and asking for help if the signs do not improve are sensible first steps, not overreacting.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours only. Ongoing symptoms, worsening dehydration, bloody diarrhoea, severe pain, or higher-risk situations may need assessment by a pharmacist, GP, urgent care service, 111 route, or emergency care.

## Important note
This is general information only, not medical or professional advice. If you are unsure whether symptoms are becoming serious, use the urgent health advice route for where you are or call 999 in an emergency.
