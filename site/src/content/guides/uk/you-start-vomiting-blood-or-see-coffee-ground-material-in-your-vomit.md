# What to do if… you start vomiting blood or see coffee-ground material in your vomit (UK)

## Short answer
If you are vomiting blood or material that looks like coffee grounds now, or you feel faint, dizzy, confused, breathless, clammy, pale, in severe pain, or generally unwell, call 999 or 112 now. If it has stopped and you feel well, still get urgent same-day medical help now through your local urgent care route, such as 111 or an urgent GP where available, or A&E or the nearest emergency department if that is your local route or you are told to go.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive yourself.
- Do not arrange your own transport if you are faint, dizzy, actively vomiting, confused, breathless, very weak, or getting worse; call 999 or 112.
- Do not lie flat on your back if you might vomit again.
- Do not take aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen, or similar anti-inflammatory painkillers for the pain unless a clinician has told you to.
- Do not drink alcohol, energy drinks, or use home remedies to try to settle it.
- Do not assume it is food colouring or something you ate if it looks like blood or coffee grounds.
- Do not delay getting help if it happens again, increases, or you feel worse.

## What to do now
1. Choose the urgent route now:
   - Call 999 or 112 if you are vomiting blood or coffee-ground material now, it has happened more than once, or you feel unwell.
   - Call 999 or 112 if you have black, tarry poo; severe tummy pain; chest pain; fainting; breathlessness; confusion; cold, clammy, or pale skin; or rapid or shallow breathing.
   - If the vomiting has stopped and you feel well, get urgent same-day medical help through your local urgent care route, such as 111 or an urgent GP where available, or A&E or the nearest emergency department if that is your local route or you are told to go.
2. Sit upright and lean slightly forward, or lie on your side with your head turned, so vomit can drain away from your mouth.
3. Keep a bowl, bag, or towel nearby. Move away from stairs, hard edges, hot drinks, and anything you could slip on if you vomit again.
4. If someone is with you, ask them to stay nearby, unlock the door, put pets in another room, and be ready to guide ambulance staff in if 999 or 112 is called.
5. Get the key facts ready without delaying the call:
   - roughly how much there was, such as streaks, spoonfuls, or mouthfuls
   - what it looked like, such as bright red, dark red, brown, black, or coffee grounds
   - whether you have black or tarry poo, severe tummy pain, chest pain, fainting, breathlessness, confusion, or weakness
   - any medicines you take, especially blood thinners such as warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban, or dabigatran; aspirin; steroids; ibuprofen; naproxen; or other anti-inflammatory painkillers
   - any known liver disease, stomach ulcer, recent heavy alcohol use, recent nosebleed, or recent forceful retching
6. If it is safe and quick, keep a small sample or take a photo to show clinicians. Do not delay calling 999 or 112, or getting urgent advice, to do this.
7. Do not eat a meal or drink alcohol while waiting for advice or help. If vomiting has stopped, you are fully alert, and you are very dry, small sips of water may be safer than drinking a lot at once.
8. If you worsen at any point, call 999 or 112 immediately, or tell the call handler straight away if you are already on the line.

## What can wait
- Working out the cause.
- Searching symptoms online.
- Cleaning up properly, beyond making the area safe from slips.
- Deciding whether it was “enough blood” to matter.
- Sorting routine GP appointments, work messages, travel plans, or non-urgent responsibilities.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to freeze or doubt yourself when vomit looks like blood or coffee grounds. Getting urgent medical help for this is a safety-first step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce immediate risk and get you into the right urgent care pathway. Tests, treatment, and later decisions may need help from clinicians or other specialist services.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, or a substitute for professional assessment. If you vomit blood or material that looks like coffee grounds, seek urgent medical assessment.
