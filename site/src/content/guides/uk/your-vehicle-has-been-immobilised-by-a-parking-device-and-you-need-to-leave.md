# What to do if… your vehicle has been immobilised by a parking device and you need to leave (UK)

## Short answer
Do not try to remove the device yourself. Read the notice on the vehicle, use the official contact route shown on it, and arrange another way to leave while the release process starts.

## Do not do these things
- Do not cut, smash, loosen, or drive against the device; you could be accused of damaging or taking property, and you may trigger extra fees or towing.
- Do not pay a person who approaches you unless they can show they are the named authority or operator for that device and the payment method matches the notice.
- Do not get pulled into a roadside argument. Keep it factual: “I need the official release route and a reference number.”
- Do not ignore a notice that warns the vehicle may be removed or taken to a pound.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm who authorised it.**  
   Find the printed notice or leaflet on the windscreen or device. Note the organisation name, phone number, device or case reference, vehicle registration, location, and release instructions. It may name DVLA, a local council, police, DVSA, an enforcement agent, or a contractor acting for one of them.

2. **Take quick photos before you pay or move away.**  
   Photograph the device in place, the full notice text, your number plate, nearby signs or road markings, and a wider shot showing where the vehicle is. Keep the photos for any later challenge.

3. **Use the contact route on the notice.**  
   Say: “My vehicle is immobilised and I need to leave urgently because of [medical, childcare, safety, or work-critical reason]. What is the fastest official way to get it released?” Ask what must be paid now, how to pay safely, whether release is by code or attendance, and for a reference number.

4. **If it appears to be DVLA-related, follow the leaflet or notice exactly.**  
   DVLA clamping is commonly linked to vehicle tax. Use the official route on the INF32 leaflet or notice, ask what tax evidence or payment confirmation is needed, and keep all receipts or confirmations.

5. **If it appears to be council, police, DVSA, or enforcement-agent action, follow the release process first.**  
   You may usually need to pay release or recovery charges before the vehicle is released, then challenge or make representations afterwards using the paperwork you are given. Ask for written confirmation of any right to challenge.

6. **If you suspect a private-land clamp has no lawful authority, verify rather than removing it yourself.**  
   In England, Wales and Scotland, private clamping is generally not lawful unless there is lawful authority, but do not try to remove the device yourself. If the notice does not identify a lawful authority, or if someone is pressuring you for cash, move yourself to a safe public place and contact police on 101. Call 999 if someone is threatening you, you feel in immediate danger, or there is a medical emergency.

7. **Make a leave-without-the-vehicle plan now.**  
   Book a taxi, use public transport, or ask someone to collect you. If medicine, keys, child items, work ID, or mobility aids are inside, ask the operator whether you can safely access the vehicle while it remains immobilised.

8. **Keep a simple record.**  
   Write down the time you found the device, every call you made, who you spoke to, what they said, what you paid, and any reference numbers. Keep receipts, screenshots, and release paperwork together.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether the clamp was fair.
- You do not need to write an appeal, complaint, or compensation claim on the spot.
- You do not need to argue at the roadside. Focus on the official release route, records, receipts, and getting yourself where you need to be.

## Important reassurance
This is disruptive, but it is usually a process problem rather than a problem you have to solve by force. The safest way to regain control is to identify the authority, use the official release route, document what happened, and get yourself moving by another method.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about disputing fees, legality, complaints, or compensation may need specialist advice and will depend on who authorised the immobilisation and where it happened.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Processes vary by authority and UK nation. If you feel unsafe, threatened, or unwell, prioritise immediate safety and emergency services over dealing with the vehicle.
