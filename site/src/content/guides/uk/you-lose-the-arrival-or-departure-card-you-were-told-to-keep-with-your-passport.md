# What to do if… you lose the arrival or departure card you were told to keep with your passport (UK)

## Short answer
Do not make your own replacement or alter your passport. Gather evidence of your entry and ask the local immigration or border authority what record, replacement, or exit process they need before you travel.

## Do not do these things
- Do not create your own “replacement” card.
- Do not write, stamp, tape, or glue anything into your passport.
- Do not hand your passport to unofficial “helpers” who offer to sort it for cash.
- Do not assume the airline can fix this at check-in.
- Do not wait until the final airport queue if you were specifically told to keep the card.
- Do not report your UK passport lost unless the passport itself is missing.

## What to do now
1. **Re-check the places where the card is most likely to be**: the passport cover, passport sleeve, every pocket of your wallet or document holder, luggage liners, yesterday’s clothes, the hotel safe, and the place where you last showed your passport. Ask hotel reception or local lost property whether anything was handed in.
2. **Put together a simple entry evidence pack**:
   - your passport photo page
   - any entry stamp, visa sticker, visa approval, or entry permission
   - your inbound flight, ferry, train, or border-crossing details
   - your accommodation address or booking
   - a photo of the missing-card instructions if you have them
3. **If the card may have been stolen**, report the theft locally if you can do so safely and keep any reference number or receipt. Show it to immigration staff only if they ask for it.
4. **Check the GOV.UK travel advice page for the country you are in**, especially the entry requirements and local laws sections. The name of the card and the right office can vary by country.
5. **Contact the local authority that issued or collects the card**:
   - At an airport, port, or land border, ask for the immigration or border information point before joining a long departure queue.
   - Away from the border, contact the local immigration office or the government office named on the card or entry paperwork.
   - Use the local term if you know it, such as arrival card, departure card, tourist card, landing card, exit card, entry slip, or immigration slip.
6. **Ask one direct question**: “I was told to keep this arrival or departure card with my passport, but it is missing. What do I need to show or complete before I leave?” Write down the answer, the office name, and the time you were told.
7. **If you are leaving today or tomorrow**, go to the airport, port, or border earlier than usual and ask for immigration guidance before check-in or before the final exit queue. Be ready to complete an official form or follow an official payment process if the local authority requires it.
8. **If the card showed your permitted stay or exit deadline**, ask the local immigration authority how to confirm your authorised stay in their system while the missing card is being dealt with.
9. **If you cannot get clear local instructions**, contact the nearest British embassy, high commission, consulate, or the FCDO for signposting. They can help you find the right local channel, but they cannot replace another country’s immigration card or overrule that country’s border process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, claim on insurance, or work out who lost it.
- You do not need to replace your UK passport unless the passport itself is missing, stolen, or damaged.
- You do not need to argue with the hotel, airline, or travel company now.
- You do not need a full legal plan before asking the local immigration authority what they need from you.

## Important reassurance
Many travellers sort this out by showing evidence of entry and following the local process. Being early, calm, and organised gives staff more room to help and reduces the chance of a last-minute delay.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment you realise the card is missing. Immigration rules vary by country, and later decisions may need help from the local immigration authority, a qualified local lawyer, your travel provider, or British consular staff.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, financial, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. Follow instructions from the local immigration or border authority, and ask them to repeat or write down anything you do not understand.
