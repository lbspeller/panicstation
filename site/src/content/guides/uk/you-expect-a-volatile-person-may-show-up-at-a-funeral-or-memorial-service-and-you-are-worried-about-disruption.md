# What to do if… you expect a volatile person may show up at a funeral or memorial service and you are worried about disruption (UK)

## Short answer
Make a simple front-door plan with the funeral director and venue now: who will meet them, what staff should do, and when the point person should call 999 or 101. Do not put yourself in the role of confronting them on the day.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t try to handle it alone or confront them yourself to get it over with.
- Don’t post or forward the time and location widely if you are trying to reduce the chance of disruption.
- Don’t rely on another grieving person to watch the door unless they have clearly agreed and are supported.
- Don’t physically block, restrain, touch, or follow them yourself.
- Don’t assume the venue or funeral director already knows the risk unless you tell them plainly in advance.
- Don’t argue by text or phone on the day of the service.

## What to do now
1. **Tell the organiser, funeral director, and venue in one clear message.**  
   Say: “We are worried that X may attend and disrupt the service. Please agree who will manage the entrance, who will speak to them, and when police should be called, so the family are not involved.”

2. **Ask what the venue can actually do about entry.**  
   Ask the funeral director, crematorium, cemetery, church, chapel, or other venue:
   - Whether the service is being treated as private or open
   - Who is allowed to ask someone to wait outside or leave
   - Whether staff can meet the person at the entrance
   - What staff will do if the person becomes loud, threatening, intoxicated, or refuses to move

3. **Choose one day-of point person who is not the closest bereaved person.**  
   Give that person the venue contact number, the funeral director’s number, and a short description of the concern. Their job is to speak to staff or police if needed; everyone else’s job is to attend the service.

4. **Give staff the details they need to recognise the risk quickly.**  
   If safe and appropriate, give the funeral director or venue the person’s name, a recent photo, likely arrival time, and the specific behaviour you are worried about, such as shouting, threats, intoxication, blocking mourners, or targeting one person.

5. **Agree a simple signal for help.**  
   Use a phrase such as “Please start the entrance plan.” Make sure the point person, funeral director, and venue staff know what that phrase means.

6. **Reduce flashpoints in the building or grounds.**  
   Ask for:
   - A staff member or agreed person near the entrance before the service starts
   - A separate place where close family can wait if the person arrives
   - Seating that keeps the person away from anyone they may target
   - A route for close family to arrive and leave without having to stand at the doorway

7. **Agree when to call police.**  
   Call **999** if there is immediate danger, violence, threats of violence, a weapon, forced entry, or a crime happening now. Call **101** for non-emergency police advice, information, or enquiries before the service if you want to flag a likely disturbance at a specific time and place.

8. **If there are written restrictions, make them easy to show.**  
   If you already have a court order, police bail condition, or other written restriction that may be relevant, ask the point person to keep a copy available for police or venue staff. Do not try to interpret it in the moment.

9. **Limit direct contact on the day.**  
   Put your phone on Do Not Disturb and allow calls from the point person. If the relative sends messages or calls, avoid replying in the moment; forward anything urgent to the point person and focus on getting to the service safely.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to confront them, repair the relationship, or make a statement.
- You do not need to explain the plan to every relative.
- You do not need a perfect guest list.
- You do not need to solve the wider family conflict before the service.
- You only need the entrance plan, the point person, and the police threshold.

## Important reassurance
Taking precautions does not mean you are causing drama or overreacting. A small, calm plan can help the service continue with less attention on the difficult person and more space for people to mourn.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the day of the service and reduce immediate disruption. Later decisions about family boundaries, venue complaints, police reports, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, therapeutic, or professional advice. Venue rules, police responses, and any written restrictions can vary, so confirm the plan directly with the funeral director, venue, or police as appropriate.
