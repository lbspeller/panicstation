# What to do if… you lose access to a passwordless login because the passkey device is gone (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the missing device using "Find My" tools from a secondary browser, then initiate the service's official account recovery path using a trusted email or recovery contact.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links in text messages or emails claiming your lost device has been found; these are often phishing attempts to steal your remaining credentials.
- Don’t factory-reset or sign out of your only remaining signed-in computer or tablet, as this may be your only way to revoke the lost passkey.
- Don’t attempt to "guess" recovery codes multiple times; stop if you receive a "rate limit" or "lockout" warning to avoid a permanent account freeze.
- Don’t share one-time verification codes or screenshots of your security settings with anyone, including people claiming to be "tech support" who contacted you first.
- Don’t create a new account with the same email address until you have confirmed the original account is unrecoverable.

## What to do now
1. **Remotely lock the missing device immediately from any available web browser.**
   - **For Apple devices:** Sign in to **iCloud.com/find** and enable **Mark As Lost** (Lost Mode).
   - **For Android devices:** Sign in to **google.com/android/find** and select **Secure Device**.
   - **For Windows laptops:** Sign in to your Microsoft account and use **Find my device** to lock the hardware.

2. **Revoke the lost device's access from your main account settings.**
   - Use a device where you are still signed in (such as a home computer) to go to the "Security" or "Devices" section of your account.
   - Select the missing device and choose **Remove**, **Revoke**, or **Sign out**.
   - If the service allows, delete the specific **Passkey** associated with that device.

3. **Contact your mobile network provider to suspend the SIM card.**
   - If the missing device was a phone, ask the provider to block the SIM to prevent "SIM swapping" or attackers receiving your SMS recovery codes.
   - Request a "SIM block" only; do not cancel the contract yet, as you may need the number for recovery later.

4. **Attempt to sign in using an "Alternative Method" or "Recovery Contact."**
   - On the login screen, look for a link that says **Try another way**, **Sign in another way**, or **Use a recovery contact**.
   - If you previously set up an **Account Recovery Contact** (common in Apple and Google accounts), contact that person now to receive a recovery code.

5. **Start the official "Account Recovery" process for the specific service.**
   - Open a private/incognito window in your browser to ensure no old session data interferes.
   - Navigate directly to the service’s official recovery page (e.g., **iforgot.apple.com**, **accounts.google.com/signin/recovery**, or **account.live.com/acsr**).
   - Follow the prompts precisely and provide a secure, accessible email address for updates.

6. **Document the recovery attempt for official reporting (UK).**
   - Save copies of any "Account Recovery Started" emails or reference numbers provided by the service.
   - If you suspect the device was stolen or your accounts have already been accessed, report it to **Report Fraud** (for England, Wales, and Northern Ireland) at **reportfraud.police.uk** or call **0300 123 2040**.
   - If you live in **Scotland**, report the incident to **Police Scotland** by calling **101**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to purchase a replacement device or new security keys immediately.
- You do not need to decide whether to change your primary email provider or ecosystem.
- You do not need to audit every minor app or social media account right now; focus on your "Master Accounts" (Email, Banking, and Device ID).

## Important reassurance
Losing a passkey device is a common issue that modern security systems are designed to handle. While it requires a verification period, the "Account Recovery" path exists specifically for this scenario. Moving slowly and using official channels ensures you do not accidentally lock yourself out permanently through panicked mistakes.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for digital lockout. Subsequent steps may involve multi-day waiting periods imposed by providers (like Apple or Google) to verify your identity before access is restored.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional technical or legal advice. Recovery procedures are subject to change by service providers. If you encounter unexpected prompts or signs of active account takeover (such as changed passwords you did not initiate), contact the provider’s official support team and your bank immediately.
