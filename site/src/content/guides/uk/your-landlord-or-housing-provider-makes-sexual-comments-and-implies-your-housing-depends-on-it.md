# What to do if… your landlord or housing provider makes sexual comments and implies your housing depends on it (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause, stop any non-essential in-person contact, and keep contact about housing in writing. Save the messages or details you already have, and get housing or equality support before you respond further.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t meet them alone to “clear the air” or accept a lift, home visit, or private meeting.
- Don’t agree to sexual, romantic, social, or private contact to protect your housing.
- Don’t send emotional, apologetic, or bargaining messages while you feel panicked.
- Don’t delete messages, call logs, voicemails, emails, letters, or app messages.
- Don’t post allegations publicly while you are trying to stabilise the situation.
- Don’t stop paying rent, leave permanently, or break your tenancy without housing advice if you can avoid it.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause and reduce access.**  
   If you feel in immediate danger, call **999**. If you want police help but it is not an emergency, call **101** or use the online route through your local police force. If you can, stay with someone you trust tonight or have someone with you for any unavoidable contact.

2. **Move contact to written housing-only messages.**  
   Use text, email, or the housing app if possible. If they call, let it go to voicemail. A short message is enough: “Please contact me by email only about repairs, rent, access, or tenancy matters.”

3. **Save what already exists without doing a deep dive.**  
   Take screenshots of sexual comments, pressure about housing, threats, visits, missed calls, voicemails, or emails. Write a brief timeline with dates, times, where it happened, and who else may have seen or heard it. Back it up somewhere the landlord or housing provider cannot access.

4. **Make your home contact safer.**  
   Tell one trusted person what is happening and agree a simple check-in plan. If the landlord, agent, staff member, or contractor may have keys, avoid being alone for repairs, inspections, viewings, or visits. Ask for scheduled appointments in writing and have another adult present where possible.

5. **Get housing advice before you escalate.**  
   Contact a housing advice service, local advice service, or Shelter route for your part of the UK. Say that sexual comments or sexual pressure are being linked to your tenancy, repairs, access, rent, or eviction fears. Ask what to do before replying, complaining, withholding anything, or leaving.

6. **Contact the council or housing team for your area if you fear harassment or retaliation.**  
   Ask for the team that deals with landlord harassment, illegal eviction, private rented housing, or tenancy relations; names vary by local area and UK nation. Tell them you are worried your housing is being used to pressure you and you want help to stay safely housed.

7. **If the person works for a council, housing association, letting agency, or supported accommodation provider, ask for a safer contact route.**  
   When it feels safe, use the provider’s complaint, safeguarding, or anti-harassment route and ask not to deal with that person directly. Ask for repairs, inspections, rent, access, and tenancy contact to be handled by someone else.

8. **Get equality support if this feels like sex-based harassment or discrimination.**  
   The Equality Advisory and Support Service can give information about discrimination and your rights. You do not have to decide today whether to report to police, make a formal complaint, or take legal action.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide today whether to report to police, make a formal complaint, go to court, move out, or tell everyone what happened.
- You do **not** need to write a full statement now; a short timeline and saved messages are enough for the next step.
- You do **not** need to confront the person, prove your reaction to them, or negotiate your safety alone.
- You do **not** need to know the exact legal label before asking for help.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, second-guessing yourself, or feeling trapped can happen when someone with power over your housing crosses a sexual boundary. You are allowed to prioritise safety, privacy, and keeping your housing stable while you decide what support you want.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stabilise, reduce risk, and preserve options. Housing law, council routes, complaint routes, and advice services differ across the UK, so later decisions about complaints, enforcement, court, reporting, or moving may need specialist help for your exact location and housing type.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If you are not sure whether the situation is “serious enough,” you can still ask for housing, equality, police, or specialist support.
