# What to do if… you are trying to tell extended family about a death in the family and rumours are spreading faster than facts (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and send one clear "holding message" from one agreed spokesperson to the widest groups, then focus on telling the closest next-of-kin through direct phone calls.

## Do not do these things
- Do not engage in public arguments or lengthy corrections in group chats or social media comment sections.
- Do not speculate on the cause of death or share unverified medical details to "fill the silence."
- Do not send the news via a broadcast or group message to those who should hear it one-to-one, such as a partner, child, or parent.
- Do not feel obligated to answer personal questions about the circumstances, location, or discovery of the deceased.
- Do not post a public tribute or announcement until you have confirmed that all immediate family members have been reached.

## What to do now
1. **Appoint one primary spokesperson and one backup immediately.**
   - The spokesperson should be the main contact for the hospital, GP, or coroner.
   - All other family members should direct enquiries to this person to ensure the "official" story remains consistent.
2. **Draft a minimalist holding message for digital use.**
   - Keep the text short: "[Name] died on [Date]. We are currently notifying immediate family and do not have further confirmed details to share yet. We ask for privacy and will provide an update regarding arrangements when we can."
3. **Contact the closest circle using direct phone calls only.**
   - Start with partners, children, parents, and siblings.
   - If someone does not answer, send a brief text: "I have some very difficult news to share. Please call me as soon as you can."
4. **Deploy a "contact tree" to reach extended branches.**
   - Assign one person to contact "the cousins," one for "the neighbours," and one for "the workplace."
   - Explicitly tell these contacts: "Please do not post this on social media or forward it to other groups until [Time/Date]."
5. **Shut down speculation with a standard scripted response.**
   - If asked for details you do not have, use the phrase: "We are waiting for confirmed information from the [Doctor/Coroner] and cannot comment further."
6. **Coordinate with a Police Family Liaison Officer (FLO) if one is assigned.**
   - If the death is being investigated, ask the FLO what information can be shared with the family and what must remain confidential to protect the investigation.
7. **Set your phone to "Do Not Disturb" or "Focus" mode.**
   - Allow calls only from the spokesperson and immediate family to prevent being overwhelmed by notifications from distant relatives or acquaintances.
8. **Register the death and use "Tell Us Once" (England, Scotland, and Wales).**
   - **When you register the death, ask the registrar for the "Tell Us Once" service reference.**
   - This service notifies the DWP, HMRC, DVLA, and local council in one step. 
   - Note: This service is not currently available in Northern Ireland; you must contact the Social Security Agency and other departments individually there.
9. **Post a single, final correction if a major harmful inaccuracy is circulating.**
   - If the rumor is dangerous or deeply distressing, post once: "To clarify, the family is not releasing specific details at this time. Please respect our privacy while we wait for official reports."

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a full obituary or social media tribute today.
- You do not need to decide on funeral dates, venues, or guest lists right now.
- You do not need to respond to every "condolence" text or offer of help in the first 48 hours.
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family tensions or clarify minor inaccuracies in the family history.

## Important reassurance
It is common for news to travel faster than you can manage in a digital age. Rumours are a reflection of people's shock or desire to feel "informed," not a failure of your planning. Focusing on a small, clear contact list is the most effective way to regain control.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for managing family communications immediately following a death. It does not cover legal estate administration or long-term grief support.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or professional advice. If there is police involvement or a coroner's inquest, consult with official authorities before sharing details about the cause or circumstances of death.
