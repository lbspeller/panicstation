# What to do if… an online contact pressures you for sexual chat or to move to another app (UK)

## Short answer
Pause the conversation and do not move to another app. Save only the key details you may need, then block and report the account on the platform you are already using.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t move to a new app to be polite, calm them down, or prove you are “real”.
- Don’t send sexual images, videos, voice notes, or do anything on camera because they ask.
- Don’t share details that help them find you, such as your full name, school, workplace, location, other social accounts, phone number, or email address.
- Don’t pay money, gift cards, crypto, or anything else if they threaten you.
- Don’t argue, bargain, apologise repeatedly, or try to persuade them to be kind once you feel pressured.
- Don’t delete the chat until you have saved the basic details you need for reporting.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance.** Stop replying, mute the chat, and turn off read receipts or “active” status if the app lets you, so they have fewer ways to pressure you.
2. **Save the essentials.** Screenshot the profile, username or handle, profile link if visible, and the most recent messages showing pressure, threats, requests for sexual content, or requests to move platforms. Note the date, time, and platform.
3. **Block them on the current platform.** Also block any linked accounts, backup accounts, phone numbers, or usernames they have already given you.
4. **Report them in the app.** Use the platform’s report tool for harassment, sexual pressure, grooming, threats, blackmail, or intimate image abuse, whichever fits. Attach screenshots if the platform allows.
5. **Lock down the accounts they know about.**
   - Set your social profiles to private.
   - Remove your phone number, email address, location, school, or workplace from public view.
   - Turn off location sharing and “find me by phone number or email” discovery.
   - Remove unknown recent followers or friend requests.
6. **If they have sexual images or are threatening to share them, do not pay.** Save screenshots of the threat. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999. If it is not an emergency, call 101 or report to your local police online. For financially motivated sextortion, Report Fraud can also help route a report; in Scotland, use 101 or your local police route.
7. **If you are under 18, or you are worried the target is under 18, tell a trusted adult now.** This could be a parent, carer, teacher, school safeguarding lead, youth worker, or another safe adult. You can report online sexual abuse or grooming concerns to CEOP. If sexual images of someone under 18 are involved or threatened, Report Remove can help request removal.
8. **If you are 18+ and intimate images are involved, use specialist takedown support.** StopNCII can help participating platforms detect and reduce sharing of intimate images or videos. The Revenge Porn Helpline can support adult UK victims of intimate image abuse and help with takedown steps.
9. **If this connects to sexual abuse, assault, or harassment and you want support, use a specialist support route.** The ENOUGH support page lists services for sexual violence, online harm, and related abuse. Sexual Assault Referral Centres can offer medical, practical, and emotional support after rape, sexual assault, or abuse, whether or not you report to the police.

If you may want to report later, keep the screenshots, links, usernames, and dates somewhere safe, but do not keep re-reading them.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to work out who they “really are” right now.
- You don’t need to collect every message; the key profile details, threats, requests, and platform information are enough for now.
- You don’t need to decide today whether to post publicly, warn others, or explain the whole situation.
- You don’t need to reply to any “final chance” message.
- You don’t need to decide every reporting option at once; start with safety, blocking, and one trusted route.

## Important reassurance
Feeling frozen, embarrassed, frightened, or unsure is a common response to sexual pressure online. Someone pushing fast, asking for secrecy, or trying to move you off-platform may be trying to reduce oversight and increase pressure; stopping the interaction is a reasonable first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and reduce immediate harm. If threats, image-sharing, stalking, blackmail, or repeated contact continue, later decisions may need help from specialist support services, the platform, police, or another trusted professional.

## Important note
This guide is general information for immediate safety and support. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
