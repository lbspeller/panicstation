# What to do if… you are told not to contact someone as part of an investigation and you share responsibilities with them (UK)

## Short answer
Treat any "no contact" instruction as an absolute prohibition (including indirect contact through others) and immediately move all shared logistics to a documented, third-party channel such as a solicitor or the investigating officer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send "just one" message about bills, children, or pets — any communication can be treated as a criminal breach.
- Do not ask a friend, family member, or colleague to pass a message "on your behalf" as this constitutes indirect contact.
- Do not attempt to "explain your side" or "clarify the situation" to the other person.
- Do not assume that the other person's consent to talk makes it legal; only the authority (Police / Court / Employer) can lift the restriction.
- Do not visit shared locations (home / work / school) if the order prohibits it, even if you have a "good reason" like picking up a laptop or child.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the legal basis and exact wording of the restriction.** 
   **Check your paperwork (Bail Notice, Court Order, or suspension letter) to see if it is a Police Bail condition, a Non-Molestation Order, or a workplace instruction.** 
   - Note if the condition says "not to contact [Name] directly or indirectly" or if it includes specific exceptions like "except via a solicitor" or "except for the purposes of child contact via Social Services." 
   - If you do not have the paperwork, contact your solicitor or the police station where you were processed to request the exact wording immediately.

2. **Establish a "Buffer Zone" for shared responsibilities.**
   **Identify a neutral third party who can handle essential logistics while you seek a formal variation.** 
   - For children: Use a solicitor or a pre-approved professional intermediary. 
   - For finance/housing: Use a solicitor to communicate about rent, mortgage, or urgent bills. 
   - For work: Contact HR or a senior manager to establish a communication "firewall" so you do not overlap on shifts or systems.

3. **Request a formal variation if you are a primary caregiver.**
   **Ask your solicitor to apply for a bail variation under the Sentencing Act 2026 if the current conditions prevent you from fulfilling essential caregiving duties.** 
   - New 2026 criteria require authorities to give specific weight to whether the suspect is a primary caregiver or pregnant when setting or reviewing bail conditions. 
   - If you are "Released Under Investigation" (RUI) without formal bail, you are still subject to "Witness Intimidation" laws; do not contact witnesses even if there are no formal bail conditions.

4. **Document every "Practical Need" that arises.**
   **Create a log of every shared responsibility that requires attention in the next 7 days.** 
   - Include dates for school runs, medication requirements, utility deadlines, or work handovers. 
   - Send this list only to your legal representative so they can arrange a legal workaround with the Investigating Officer (OIC).

5. **Report any contact initiated by the other person.**
   **If the person you are not allowed to contact reaches out to you, do not reply, even to say you cannot talk.** 
   - Save screenshots of messages, record call times, and notify your solicitor or the OIC immediately. 
   - Responding to a "polite" message is still a breach and can lead to immediate arrest or remand.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve long-term custody or divorce arrangements today.
- You do not need to "clear your name" with mutual friends or family members right now.
- You do not need to decide who keeps the house or the car permanently; focus only on the next 72 hours of survival logistics.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when legal instructions clash with the reality of co-parenting or shared finances. The restriction is designed to protect the integrity of the investigation, not necessarily to punish you personally. Using a professional buffer (like a solicitor) is the standard way these situations are managed and does not make you "look guilty."

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for UK residents facing no-contact restrictions during an active investigation. It does not cover long-term family court proceedings or criminal defense strategy.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Breach of bail or court orders in the UK can result in immediate arrest and imprisonment. Always consult a qualified solicitor regarding the specific terms of your conditions.
