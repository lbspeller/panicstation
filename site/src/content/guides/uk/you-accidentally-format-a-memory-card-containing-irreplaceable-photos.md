# What to do if… you accidentally format a memory card containing irreplaceable photos (UK)

## Short answer
Stop using the memory card immediately, remove it from the device, and do not save anything else to it. The main risk now is overwriting photo data that may still be recoverable.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t take “just one photo” or record anything new on that card.
- Don’t format the card again, even if the camera, phone, or computer asks.
- Don’t accept prompts to “repair”, “fix”, “initialise”, or “scan and fix” the card.
- Don’t copy files, install software, or save recovered photos to the affected card.
- Don’t keep putting the card into different devices to see what happens.
- Don’t give remote access to your computer to someone who contacted you unexpectedly.
- Don’t trust anyone promising guaranteed recovery or pressuring you to pay urgently.

## What to do now
1. **Freeze the card.**  
   Remove the card from the camera, phone, drone, recorder, or computer. Put it in a safe place and label it “DO NOT USE”.

2. **Reduce the chance of accidental writing.**  
   If it is a full-size SD card, or a microSD card in a full-size adapter, and there is a “LOCK” slider, move it to LOCK before connecting it to anything. This can help, but do not rely on it as a complete safeguard.

3. **Use a different card from now on.**  
   If you need the device today, put in a different card. Do not reuse the formatted card “temporarily”.

4. **Write down what happened without testing the card.**  
   Note the device, card type, date, and whether it was an in-camera format, quick format, full format, or secure erase if you know. Do not repeat the format to check.

5. **Choose one recovery route: professional first, or careful read-only DIY.**  
   If the photos are truly irreplaceable, or the card disconnects, does not mount, becomes hot, shows errors, or looks physically damaged, consider a reputable professional data-recovery service before trying software. If you try DIY, use a card reader, scan the card with reputable recovery software, and save recovered files only to a different drive or computer.

6. **Check for other copies before doing anything risky.**  
   Check any laptop, desktop, phone, tablet, external drive, camera import folder, cloud photo library, and “recently deleted” or “trash” area where the photos may already have been copied.

7. **If you may be dealing with a scam, pause.**  
   If someone asks for remote access, asks for passwords, demands urgent payment, or contacts you out of the blue, stop. If you have lost money or been targeted by fraud in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud. In Scotland, report to Police Scotland, usually via 101 for non-emergencies.

8. **If the card may be faulty, keep proof but deal with that later.**  
   Keep the receipt, packaging, order confirmation, and notes about the fault. In the UK, faulty goods are generally taken up with the retailer, but recovery comes first because returns or testing may overwrite data.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide today whether the card can ever be used again.
- You don’t need to organise, rename, edit, or sort any recovered files yet.
- You don’t need to work out exactly whose fault it was.
- You don’t need to choose a replacement card until the affected card is safely set aside.
- You don’t need to start a retailer complaint before protecting the photos.

## Important reassurance
This is a common and very upsetting mistake. If the card has not been used since the format, some recovery may still be possible, and the most helpful thing now is to avoid anything that writes to the card.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce the chance of permanent loss and help you choose a safer recovery route. Later decisions about professional recovery, faulty goods, privacy, or fraud may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not professional, legal, forensic, technical, financial, therapeutic, or data-recovery advice. Recovery outcomes vary by card type, device, file system, card condition, and what happened after formatting.
