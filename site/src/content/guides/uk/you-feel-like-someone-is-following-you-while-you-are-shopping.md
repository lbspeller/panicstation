# What to do if… you feel like someone is following you while you are shopping (UK)

## Short answer
Move into a staffed, public place such as a till, customer service desk, pharmacy counter, or security desk, and tell staff: “I think someone is following me. Please keep me here and call security or the police.” If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not lead the person somewhere quieter, to your car, or towards home to check whether they follow.
- Do not confront them, accuse them, or try to block them.
- Do not leave the building or shopping centre alone just to get it over with.
- Do not focus on filming them if it slows you down, makes you more visible, or distracts you from getting help.
- Do not stay silent out of embarrassment if you feel unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Create an immediate safety buffer.** Walk calmly to the nearest staffed place: a checkout, customer service desk, pharmacy counter, security desk, or shop with several staff. Stand where staff and other people can see you.
2. **Use a clear script.** Say quietly and directly: “I think I’m being followed between shops or aisles. Please keep me here and call centre security or the police.” If you are with a child or vulnerable person, add: “I’m with someone vulnerable. Please stay with us.”
3. **Ask staff for specific help.**
   - Ask them to call shopping-centre security or a manager to come to you.
   - Ask whether you can wait behind the counter, near staff, or in another staffed area until help arrives.
   - Ask them to note the time, shop name, aisle, entrance, or exact spot, in case CCTV needs to be found later.
4. **Call 999 if you feel unsafe now.**
   - Call 999 if you think the situation could get heated or violent soon, someone is threatening you, the person is trying to corner you, you are in immediate danger, or you need support right away.
   - If you cannot speak safely, call 999, listen to the operator, cough or tap if you can, and press 55 if prompted so the call is treated as a genuine emergency.
   - If it is not an emergency but you want police advice or want to report it later, call 101 or report online when you are safe.
   - Give your exact location if you can: shop name, shopping centre, floor, nearest entrance, or nearby landmark.
5. **Stop moving between aisles or shops.** Staying with staff or security is usually safer than continuing to browse. If you need to leave, ask whether staff or security can escort you to a taxi rank, bus stop, car park, or main staffed exit. Avoid quiet side exits.
6. **Send one quiet update to someone you trust.** Tell them where you are, what shop or area you are in, and that you will update when safe. Keep your phone reachable, with emergency call access available.
7. **If you must move again, move from help point to help point.** Go from one staffed area to another, such as customer service to a security office, using busy, well-lit routes.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether it legally counts as stalking, harassment, or a misunderstanding.
- Writing a detailed account; for now, a short note with time, place, and description is enough.
- Making a complaint to the shopping centre.
- Posting about it on social media.
- Any long-term safety planning. Right now the goal is: not alone, not isolated, and helped by staff or police if needed.

## Important reassurance
Feeling alarmed when someone repeatedly shadows your movements is a normal safety response. You do not need proof before asking staff for help or moving to a safer, staffed place.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes and hour. If the behaviour repeats today or on other days, you can report it or ask for specialist stalking or harassment advice when you are safe.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call 999.
