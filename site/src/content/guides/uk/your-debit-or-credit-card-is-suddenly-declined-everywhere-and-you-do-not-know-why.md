# What to do if… your debit or credit card is suddenly declined everywhere and you do not know why (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a likely card, account, security, or issuer problem: check your official banking app for a lock, fraud alert, or message, then contact your card issuer through a verified route. If an unexpected caller or message says it is your bank, stop using that contact route and use 159 if available, or the number in your official app, statement, website, or on the card.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep retrying the same payment over and over; repeated failed attempts can sometimes make the block or confusion worse.
- Don’t call a bank number from a text, email, pop-up, advert, search result, or person at the till.
- Don’t share your PIN, online banking password, one-time passcodes, or approval prompts you did not start yourself.
- Don’t hand your card to someone to “try it in the back” or “run it manually”.
- Don’t assume it is definitely insufficient funds, fraud, or a broken terminal until you have checked with the issuer.
- Don’t let embarrassment push you into rushing, borrowing from a stranger, or approving something you do not understand.

## What to do now
1. **Step away from the queue or checkout if you can.**  
   Keep the card with you. If you are in the middle of a purchase, ask the staff member to cancel or void the failed attempt before you try another way to pay.

2. **Open your official banking app or online banking.**  
   Look for a card lock or freeze toggle, fraud alert, declined transaction message, security check, expired card warning, or a notice asking you to confirm a payment.

3. **Check whether you are using the right card.**  
   Check the expiry date, whether a replacement card has arrived, whether the card is locked in the app, and whether you are accidentally using an old card saved in a wallet, app, or website.

4. **Try one controlled payment check only if it is safe and useful.**  
   If contactless failed, try Chip and PIN once; a PIN check may be needed before contactless works again. If Chip and PIN also fails, stop testing and contact the issuer.

5. **Check available funds or available credit, not just the displayed balance.**  
   Look for pending transactions, card holds from hotels, fuel stations or travel, overdraft issues, credit-limit issues, daily spending limits, or payments that have reduced what is actually available.

6. **Contact your card issuer through a verified route.**  
   Use the secure chat or call button in the official app, the number on the back of the card, the number on a statement, or the number on the issuer’s official website typed in yourself. Ask whether the card is blocked, frozen, expired, replaced, over a limit, under a security check, or affected by suspected fraud.

7. **If there was an unexpected call, text, email, or app prompt about your card, treat it as suspicious.**  
   End the call or stop replying. Dial 159 if your bank is supported, or contact your issuer through the official app or verified number. Do not approve a payment, move money, or read out codes because someone tells you the card is at risk.

8. **If you see transactions you do not recognise or think your details were compromised, secure the account.**  
   Freeze or lock the card in the official app if you can. Tell the issuer immediately. Change your banking password and the password for the email account used for bank resets, using only the official app or a website address you type yourself.

9. **Stabilise how you will pay for essentials today.**  
   Use another card, cash, or a mobile wallet only if it was already set up by you. If you cannot pay for essentials, tell the card issuer plainly that you are stuck without a working card and ask what immediate options they can offer.

10. **Use the right fraud reporting route if money is missing or you have been scammed.**  
   Contact your bank or card issuer first. In England, Wales or Northern Ireland, you can report fraud to Report Fraud. If you live in Scotland, use Police Scotland reporting routes; call 101 for non-emergencies and 999 only if there is an immediate danger or crime in progress.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact root cause before contacting the issuer.
- You do not need to cancel every card, close your account, change banks, or make a complaint today unless the issuer tells you it is necessary.
- You do not need to keep testing the card at different shops, cash machines, or websites.
- You do not need to decide about formal fraud reports unless money is missing, your details were misused, or you believe you have been scammed.
- You do not need to explain everything to shop staff; it is enough to say the card is not working and you are contacting the issuer.

## Important reassurance
A card being declined everywhere is stressful and embarrassing, but it often has a practical cause: a security check, a frozen card, an expired or replaced card, a contactless PIN check, a pending hold, a limit, or suspected fraud. The safest next step is not to guess; it is to use a verified route to ask the issuer what they can see.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation, protect the card, and get a safe answer from the issuer. If money has gone missing, you were scammed, or the issuer refuses to help, later decisions may need specialist support, a formal complaint, or fraud reporting.

## Important note
This is general information, not financial, legal, fraud investigation, or professional advice. Banking processes vary by provider; follow your card issuer’s official instructions and prioritise account security if anything feels suspicious.
