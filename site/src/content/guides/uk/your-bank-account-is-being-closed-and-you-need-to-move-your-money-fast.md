# What to do if… your bank account is being closed and you need to move your money fast (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the notice as urgent, but first confirm it is real using your banking app or the phone number on your bank card. Then get a new account ready for income and essential bills before moving the full balance.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, scan QR codes, or call numbers from the notice if you are not sure it is genuine.
- Don’t move money to a “safe account” suggested by someone who contacts you, even if they say they are from the bank or police.
- Don’t share passcodes, one-time codes, card details, passwords, or remote-access app access with anyone.
- Don’t withdraw large amounts of cash unless your bank confirms there is no safer practical option.
- Don’t cancel Direct Debits or standing orders in panic without deciding how the essential bills will be paid.
- Don’t make a one-off bill payment unless you have checked that a Direct Debit will not also be taken.
- Don’t transfer your entire balance out if you still have pending card payments, Direct Debits, or an overdraft to deal with.
- Don’t assume wages, benefits, pensions, refunds, or other incoming payments will automatically follow you unless a switch or payer update is in place.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the notice is real before moving money.**  
   Open your banking app directly, type the bank’s website address yourself, or call the number on your bank card. Do not use contact details in the notice until the bank has confirmed it.

2. **Ask the bank for the practical details and write them down.**  
   Ask:
   - the exact closure date and time, if they can give one
   - whether access could be restricted before that date
   - how any remaining balance will be returned after closure
   - what happens to pending card payments, Direct Debits, standing orders, and any overdraft
   - whether any short extension or temporary access arrangement is possible so essential payments can be moved

3. **Open a replacement account now.**  
   Apply for a standard current account if you can. If you are refused, have poor credit, are overdrawn, or need a simpler emergency option, ask about a fee-free basic bank account so wages, benefits, pensions, Direct Debits, and standing orders can still be handled.

4. **Move the most urgent payment details first.**  
   Update the account details for:
   - salary, benefits, pension, maintenance, student finance, or any regular income
   - rent or mortgage
   - council tax
   - energy, water, phone, internet, insurance, childcare, and essential loan repayments

5. **Move enough usable money to function safely.**  
   Once the new account is open and you have confirmed the notice is genuine, transfer enough for the next few weeks of essentials. Keep screenshots or receipts for transfers, and keep a note of anything still pending on the old account.

6. **Use the Current Account Switch Service if there is enough time and both accounts are eligible.**  
   The service is intended for a full current-account switch. It usually closes the old account, moves the balance and regular payments, and redirects payments sent to the old account. You must usually allow at least 7 working days, and the switch date cannot be a weekend or Bank Holiday. During that 7-working-day window, set up any new payments on the new account, not the old one.

7. **If there is not enough time for a full switch, do a manual emergency move.**  
   Transfer money yourself, then recreate the essential standing orders and Direct Debits on the new account. For Direct Debits, contact the organisation you pay and ask whether the next payment will come from the old account or the new one. If a bill is due immediately, make a one-off payment only after checking it will not also be collected automatically.

8. **Save records before access changes.**  
   Download or save recent statements, the closure notice, secure messages about the closure, payee details, standing-order details, Direct Debit lists, proof of balance, and transfer confirmations.

9. **Watch the old account until it closes.**  
   Check daily for pending card payments, returned Direct Debits, incoming payments, fees, or overdraft changes. If money arrives in the old account after you have moved, ask the bank or the payer how it will be redirected or returned.

10. **Complain separately if you think the closure is wrong or unfair.**  
   First make a brief written complaint to the bank with dates, what happened, and what you need. If you are unhappy with the final response, or the bank does not respond within the relevant complaint deadline, you can usually ask the Financial Ombudsman Service to look at it. Account-closure complaints are usually an 8-week response issue, but some payment-services, charge, or notice-period complaints can have shorter response times.

## What can wait
- Choosing the best long-term bank, interest rate, perk, app, or switching bonus.
- Writing a detailed argument about fairness before your income and bills are protected.
- Moving every small subscription immediately.
- Closing the old account yourself if the bank is already closing it.
- Deciding whether to make a wider complaint once your immediate access to money is stable.

## Important reassurance
A closure notice can feel frightening, but the first job is limited: confirm it is genuine, create a safe place for money to land, and keep essential payments working. You do not have to solve the whole dispute today.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about complaints, credit records, overdrafts, discrimination, fraud markers, or losses may need specialist help once your immediate money access is stable.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, debt, fraud, or professional advice. Banks can close accounts for different reasons and may give limited detail in some situations. If you are at risk of missing rent, mortgage, council tax, utilities, or other essential payments, get independent advice promptly.
