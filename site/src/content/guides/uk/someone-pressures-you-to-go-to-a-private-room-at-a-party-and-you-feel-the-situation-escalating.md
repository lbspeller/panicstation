# What to do if… someone pressures you to go to a private room at a party and you feel the situation escalating (UK)

## Short answer
Move immediately to a public, well-lit area with other people and contact a third party (friend, host, or venue staff) for assistance. Your priority is physical distance from the person pressuring you; you do not need to be polite or provide an explanation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go into a private space "just to talk" or to "de-escalate" if you already feel uneasy.
- Do not keep negotiating or explaining your reasons for saying no, as this allows the other person to control the pace of the interaction.
- Do not accept any drink, including water, that you did not see poured or have not kept in your sight.
- Do not worry about "causing a scene" or "overreacting" if your internal safety cues are triggered.
- Do not stay in the building if you can safely reach a public street or a staffed area.

## What to do now
1. **Create physical distance immediately and move toward light and noise.**  
   Use a short, clear statement: **"No, I am staying here,"** or **"I’m going to find my friends now."** Immediately walk toward the largest group of people or a well-lit kitchen or living area.
2. **Engage a third person to break the isolation.**  
   Do not speak to the person pressuring you; speak directly to someone else:  
   - **"Can you walk with me to the kitchen?"**  
   - **"I need to stay with you for a moment, I'm feeling uncomfortable."**
3. **Use venue-specific safety protocols.**  
   - **If at a house party:** Find the host or a group in a main room and state clearly: **"I need help, someone is following me and won't leave me alone."**  
   - **If at a pub or club:** Go to the bar and "Ask for Angela." This is a national code-phrase that alerts staff you need help to leave or get to a safe space discreetly.
4. **Transition from "managing the person" to "leaving the event."**  
   Text or call a trusted contact and tell them: **"I am at [location] and I feel unsafe. Stay on the phone with me until I am in a taxi/car."** If you came with friends, tell them you are leaving now and ask them to walk out with you.
5. **Establish a safer pause point.**  
   If you cannot leave the building yet, find a room with a lock (like a bathroom) or stay directly next to staff/security. Keep your phone in your hand with the screen on.
6. **Use the "Silent Solution" if you must call 999 but cannot speak.**  
   Dial 999. If you are on a mobile and cannot speak safely, listen to the questions from the operator. **When prompted by the automated system, press 55.** This notifies the operator that the call is a genuine emergency and transfers you to the police.
7. **Access confidential medical and emotional support.**  
   If an incident has occurred, you can access a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) for specialist care without involving the police.  
   - **In England and Wales:** Call the 24/7 Rape & Sexual Abuse Support Line on **0808 500 2222**.  
   - **In Scotland:** Call Rape Crisis Scotland on **08088 01 03 02** (6pm–midnight).  
   - **Note:** Forensic evidence (FME) is most effectively collected within the first 72 hours (3 days).

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether you want to report the person to the police or the party host.
- You do not need to analyze if your "signals" were mixed or if you were "clear enough"—boundary pushing is the responsibility of the person doing it.
- You do not need to justify your sudden departure to anyone who wasn't involved.

## Important reassurance
The feeling of "freezing" or feeling "polite" while under pressure is a natural psychological response to a perceived threat. It is not a sign of weakness or agreement. Trusting your discomfort is a vital safety skill, and taking action to get to a public space is the most effective way to disrupt an escalating situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for personal safety in social environments. It does not cover long-term legal advice or therapeutic recovery.

## Important note
If you are in immediate danger or require urgent medical attention, call 999. Support services at SARCs and helplines are confidential and available regardless of the circumstances of the event.
