# What to do if… police contact you about a death and you are not sure what to say (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before giving a detailed account. Take the officer’s details, ask whether you are being contacted as a witness, suspect, next-of-kin contact, or for identification, and ask for legal advice before any cautioned or suspect interview.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t fill gaps with guesses, motives, timelines you have not checked, or things you only half-remember.
- Don’t agree to a detailed interview just because you feel shocked, guilty, rushed, or afraid of seeming unhelpful.
- Don’t agree voluntarily to hand over your phone, passwords, messages, photos, or accounts before getting legal advice.
- Don’t sign a written statement until you have read it slowly, corrected anything unclear, and made sure it is in your own words.
- Don’t assume “it is just a quick chat” means there are no risks.
- Don’t physically obstruct officers if they say they are using a legal power to enter, search, seize property, or arrest someone.

## What to do now
1. **Slow the contact down.** Say: “I’m very shaken. I want to cooperate, but I need to be clear what this is about. Can I take your details and call back?”

2. **Check who you are speaking to.** Ask for their name, rank or role, police force, station or unit, call-back route, and incident or crime reference number. For a non-emergency check, use the police force’s official website or call 101. Use 999 only if there is immediate danger or an emergency.

3. **Ask the framing question.** Say: “Am I being contacted as a witness, as a suspect, as next of kin, for identification, or for some other reason?” If they cannot answer clearly, treat the conversation cautiously and do not give a detailed account yet.

4. **If they mention a cautioned or suspect interview, pause.** Say: “I want legal advice before any interview.” Free legal advice is generally available for police interviews, but the route and wording can vary within the UK. Ask the police to arrange the local legal advice route, or ask them to contact your own solicitor.

5. **If they ask for “just a quick chat”, keep the boundary.** Say: “I am not refusing to help, but I am not answering detailed questions until I understand my status and have had legal advice if needed.”

6. **If you are only giving basic practical details, keep them limited.** It is generally reasonable to give your name, contact details, relationship to the person who died, and whether you need support to understand what is happening. Pause before giving opinions, motives, timelines, messages, or private information about other people.

7. **If you choose to give witness information, keep it factual.** Use sentences like: “I personally saw…”, “I personally heard…”, “I do not know”, “I do not remember”, or “I would need to check my calendar or messages.” Make clear when something is only what someone else told you.

8. **Write a contact log while it is fresh.** Note the date, time, who contacted you, what they asked for, what you said, who else was present, and any reference numbers or deadlines. Keep call logs and messages.

9. **If they ask you to attend today, ask whether it can be arranged after advice.** Say: “Can you confirm what the interview or meeting is for, and whether it can be scheduled after I have spoken to a solicitor?” If they say it cannot wait, ask again for legal advice before answering questions.

10. **If police are at your door, keep it calm and specific.** You can ask what they need, whether they are asking to come in by consent, and whether they have a warrant or another legal power. If you do not want to invite them in, say: “I am not inviting anyone in right now. Please explain the power you are using or leave details so I can call back.” Do not block them if they say they are entering under a legal power.

## What can wait
- You do not need to give a full story from the beginning on the spot.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to give a formal statement, attend an interview, or hand over devices voluntarily.
- You do not need to work out the cause of death, who is responsible, what other people meant, or what everything means.
- You do not need to answer messages from everyone else before you have stabilised the police contact.

## Important reassurance
Wanting to slow down does not make you unhelpful. Shock and grief can make people guess, agree to wording they do not mean, or say more than they intended, so taking a pause can protect accuracy.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about interviews, statements, searches, devices, or your role in the investigation may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. Police and death investigation procedures can vary within the UK, so ask the officer or a solicitor what applies to the place where the contact is happening.
