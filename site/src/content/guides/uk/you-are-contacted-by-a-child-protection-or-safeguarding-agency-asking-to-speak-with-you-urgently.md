# What to do if… you are contacted by a child protection or safeguarding agency asking to speak with you urgently (UK)

## Short answer
Slow the process down: verify the caller’s identity and organization independently, then agree to a specific time for a private, recorded conversation later that day.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the caller is genuine until you have verified them through an independently sourced office number.
- Do not ignore the contact or refuse to engage once identity is verified, as this can escalate the perceived risk to the child.
- Do not conduct the conversation in the presence of children or where they can overhear details.
- Do not coach children on what to say or pressure them about what they have told teachers or doctors.
- Do not delete any evidence of the contact, including voicemails, texts, or call logs.
- Do not post details of the allegations or the names of social workers on social media platforms.
- Do not sign any "voluntary" agreements to move the child (Section 20 in England/Wales or Section 25 in Scotland) without immediate legal advice.
- Do not agree to on-the-spot interviews or home entries without first establishing the legal basis for the request.

## What to do now
1. **Request the caller’s full professional details and then end the call to verify them.** **Ask for their name, their specific team (e.g., MASH, Assessment, or Gateway), the local authority or Health and Social Care Trust they represent, and a direct office extension.**
2. **Verify the caller’s identity using an official government or council switchboard number.** **Do not use a number provided by the caller; instead, find the main number for the local council Children’s Services or the Northern Ireland HSC Trust online.**
   - Call the main switchboard and ask to be put through to the named worker or their manager.
   - If the call is out of hours, ask for the Emergency Duty Team (England/Wales), the Regional Emergency Social Work Service (Northern Ireland), or the Social Work Out of Hours service (Scotland).
3. **Ask for immediate clarity on the nature of the "urgent" concern.** **Once identity is confirmed, ask the worker to specify which child the concern relates to and what the immediate safety risk is that requires action today.**
   - In England and Wales, ask if this is being treated as a "Family Help" assessment, a "Child in Need" (Section 17) assessment, or a "Child Protection Enquiry" (Section 47).
   - In Scotland, ask if an "Inter-agency Referral Discussion" (IRD) has taken place.
   - In Northern Ireland, ask if this is a "Gateway" assessment.
4. **Set a fixed time for a formal conversation when you are in a private space.** **If you are at work, in public, or with children, state that you will call them back at a specific time within the next few hours once you have a pen, paper, and privacy.**
5. **Document every interaction in a dedicated written log.** **Record the date, time, duration of the call, the worker’s name, and the exact words used to describe the concerns (e.g., "significant harm", "neglect", or "referral").**
6. **Seek specialized advice from a family law solicitor or advocacy service immediately.** **Contact the Family Rights Group (FRG) Advice Line at 0808 801 0366 (England and Wales) for free, confidential advice on your rights and the child protection process.**
7. **Maintain a normal, calm routine for the children while the investigation is ongoing.** **Keep to standard school, meal, and bedtimes to minimize distress to the children, and avoid discussing the social worker's contact in their presence.**
8. **Clarify the purpose and duration of any requested home visit or child interview.** **If the worker asks to see the child immediately, ask if they intend to speak to the child alone and whether they have obtained your consent or a court order to do so.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a full rebuttal or written statement during the first phone call.
- You do not need to identify or confront the person you suspect made the referral.
- You do not need to agree to long-term "safety plans" or "working agreements" in the first hour.
- You do not need to gather character references or evidence for your parenting before you understand the specific allegations.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an urgent call from social services is a high-stress event, but it does not automatically mean your child will be removed. In the vast majority of cases, social workers are legally required to investigate referrals to ensure children are safe and to see if the family needs support. It is normal to feel defensive or frightened; focusing on verification and documentation will help you manage the situation professionally.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the initial hours of contact. Child protection procedures involve complex legal frameworks that vary by UK jurisdiction. Further steps will require detailed engagement with local authorities and potentially legal representation.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Statutory procedures, such as Section 47 (England/Wales) or the Children’s Hearing system (Scotland), have specific legal requirements. Always verify the identity of any person claiming to be a social worker and seek professional legal advice if you are asked to sign documents or if legal proceedings are mentioned.
