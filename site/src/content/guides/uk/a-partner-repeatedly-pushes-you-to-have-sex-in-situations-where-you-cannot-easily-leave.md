# What to do if… a partner repeatedly pushes you to have sex in situations where you cannot easily leave (UK)

## Short answer
Create a safer pause by finding any excuse to physically separate or contact someone for help. Pressure is not consent, and you are allowed to use survival strategies like "freezing" or "going along" to stay safe without it being your fault.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel obligated to provide a "good enough" reason for saying no or try to negotiate your boundaries.
- Do not move to a more private or isolated location (such as a bedroom or a parked car) in an attempt to calm the situation.
- Do not confront the partner or send accusatory messages while you are still physically with them or reliant on them for transport.
- Do not search for help or helplines on a shared or monitored device if your partner is nearby.
- Do not blame yourself for your body's survival responses, such as freezing, fawning, or feeling unable to speak.
- Do not assume you must report to the police to access medical care or specialist support.

## What to do now
1. **Create an immediate "safer pause" to get away from the direct pressure.** Find a neutral reason to leave the room or vehicle, such as needing the toilet, feeling physically unwell, or needing to check a noise. Move toward a public area, a room with a lock, or toward other people if possible.
2. **Prioritize your means of exit and communication.** **Keep your phone, keys, and any outer clothing (shoes or coat) on your person rather than leaving them in another room.** If you are in a vehicle, ask to stop at a petrol station or shop, or look for a moment to exit when the car is stopped in a well-lit area.
3. **Use a silent signal or a simple text to bring in a trusted person.** **Message a friend or family member with a pre-agreed code or a direct request like "Call me now and give me an excuse to leave."** If you are at a public venue, you can ask staff for help or ask to "speak to Angela" (a common safety protocol in many UK bars and venues).
4. **Call 999 if you are in immediate danger or feel you cannot leave safely.** **If you are unable to speak, dial 999 and, when prompted on a mobile, press 55 to use the "Silent Solution" which alerts the operator that it is a genuine emergency.** If calling from a landline and you cannot speak, the operator will usually connect you to the police if they hear background noise or suspicious activity.
5. **Contact a specialist sexual violence service for confidential guidance.** 
   - England and Wales: Call the **Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line on 0808 500 2222** (available 24 hours a day).
   - Scotland: Call the **Rape Crisis Scotland Helpline on 08088 01 03 02** (available 5pm to midnight daily).
   - Northern Ireland: Call the **Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline (managed by Nexus NI) on 0808 802 1414** (available 24 hours a day).
6. **Access medical care and support through a Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC).** **In England and Wales, you can find your nearest SARC through the NHS website; in Scotland, search for the "SARCS" service which allows for self-referral via a dedicated 24-hour phone line (0800 148 88 88).** These centers can provide emergency contraception, STI prevention, and injury checks without requiring police involvement.
7. **Use domestic abuse resources if the pressure is part of an ongoing relationship.** **Call the National Domestic Abuse Helpline (Refuge) on 0808 2000 247 (24 hours a day) or the Men’s Advice Line on 0808 801 0327 (Monday and Wednesday 9am to 8pm, Tuesday, Thursday, and Friday 9am to 5pm).** These services can help with safety planning and finding emergency accommodation if needed.
8. **Be aware of the time-sensitive nature of some medical and forensic options.** **If sexual contact occurred without your consent, certain treatments like PEP (Post-Exposure Prophylaxis for HIV) are most effective within 72 hours, and forensic evidence collection is generally most effective within 72 hours to 7 days.** You can access these through a SARC, SARCS, or a sexual health clinic.
9. **Record the events in a secure way only if it is safe to do so.** **If you have a safe way to keep notes that your partner cannot access (such as a password-protected app or a trusted friend’s phone), jot down the date, time, and a factual account of what happened.** This is for your own clarity and to preserve your memory of the events for any future choices you might make.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether what happened constitutes a crime or a specific legal term.
- You do not need to decide whether to end the relationship or report the incident to the police today.
- You do not need to confront your partner or explain why their behavior was unacceptable while you are still in a vulnerable position.
- You do not need to have "proof" or evidence to talk to a support worker or a doctor.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for people to freeze or go quiet when they feel trapped or pressured—this is a biological survival mechanism, not a sign that you agreed or that it was your fault. Being pushed for sex in an isolated situation is a serious violation of your autonomy, and it is natural to feel confused, overwhelmed, or frightened.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate stabilization and harm-reduction steps. Long-term support, legal advice, and relationship counseling require specialist professional intervention.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance in a crisis-adjacent situation and does not replace professional legal or medical advice. If you are in immediate danger, always call 999. If you suspect your devices are monitored, try to use a public computer or a device belonging to a trusted friend.
