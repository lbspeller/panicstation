# What to do if… you find your current job role being advertised online (UK)

## Short answer
Pause, keep a safe record of what you saw, and ask your manager or HR for a calm private explanation before you react. Do not resign, accuse anyone, or sign anything while you are still unclear what the advert means.

## Do not do these things
- Do not quit “to get ahead of it” or threaten resignation.
- Do not send an angry email or message accusing anyone of replacing you.
- Do not post about it on social media or warn colleagues in a way that escalates panic.
- Do not download confidential files, client lists, or restricted workplace material as “evidence”.
- Do not secretly record meetings or calls; keep calm written notes instead.
- Do not sign a settlement agreement, new contract, resignation letter, or variation of terms on the spot.
- Do not assume the advert proves dismissal is happening; it may be for expansion, cover, a restructure, or a similar but different role.

## What to do now
1. **Capture the advert safely and neutrally.** If the advert is public, use a personal device to take screenshots showing the role title, duties, location, salary or grade if shown, date, and URL. If it is only visible inside internal systems, do not copy restricted content; write down where you saw it, the reference number, date and time, and why it appears to match your role.
2. **Check whether it is actually your job.** Compare the advert with your contract, written statement of terms, job description, grade, reporting line, team, location, and main responsibilities. Look for signs it may be a different role, such as fixed-term cover, a different team, “new position”, or different seniority.
3. **Start a simple timeline log.** Write down when you discovered the advert, what it said, any recent restructure or performance discussions, and anything said to you about your role. Keep it factual and store it outside work systems.
4. **Gather only documents you are allowed to keep.** Keep copies of documents you already legitimately have, such as your contract, written statement of terms, recent payslips, formal reviews, letters, or warning notices. If policies or job descriptions are only on internal systems, follow your employer’s normal process or ask HR rather than forwarding restricted material to yourself.
5. **Request a short private meeting.** Ask your manager, and HR if your employer has HR: “I’ve seen an advert that appears to match my role. Can you explain what it relates to and whether my position is at risk?” Keep the tone neutral.
6. **Take notes during or straight after the meeting.** Record the date, who was present, what explanation was given, whether your role is said to be at risk, and any next steps mentioned.
7. **Confirm the basics in writing.** Send a brief email saying what you understood: what the advert relates to, whether it affects your role, and what happens next. Keep it short and factual.
8. **If they mention redundancy, restructure, dismissal, or a change to your role, slow the conversation down.** Ask what the proposed reason is, what consultation or process will happen, what selection pool and criteria may apply if redundancy is being considered, what your notice period is, and for any proposal to be put in writing.
9. **If the explanation is unclear or you feel pushed out, use support early.** If you are in a union, contact your rep. You can also contact Acas for impartial guidance. If an informal conversation does not resolve the issue, you may be able to raise a formal grievance.
10. **Keep doing your normal work while you clarify.** Stay professional, meet deadlines where you can, and avoid emotional messages. This helps prevent the situation turning into a conduct issue while you are trying to get clarity.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to leave, job hunt, raise a grievance, or take legal action.
- You do not need to confront colleagues or recruit support before you have spoken privately to your manager or HR.
- You do not need to write a long statement, gather every historical email, or prove everything immediately.
- You do not need to accept any proposed change on the spot; you can ask for time to read and consider it.

## Important reassurance
Seeing what looks like your own role advertised can feel shocking and personal. A short pause, a clean record, and one calm request for an explanation can stop the situation becoming harder because of a rushed resignation, angry message, or unsafe handling of work material.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise, keep a clean record, and get clarity. Later decisions about negotiation, grievance, redundancy, dismissal, discrimination, or tribunal time limits may need specialist help based on your contract, length of service, and what your employer says.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Employment situations depend on specific facts. If you feel pressured to resign or sign documents immediately, or you believe discrimination, whistleblowing, trade union activity, health and safety issues, or retaliation may be involved, get independent advice promptly from a union, Acas, Citizens Advice, or an employment adviser.
