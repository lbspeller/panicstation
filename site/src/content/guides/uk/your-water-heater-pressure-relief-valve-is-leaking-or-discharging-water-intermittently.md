# What to do if… your water heater pressure relief valve is leaking or discharging water intermittently (UK)

## Short answer
Treat any intermittent discharge from a pressure relief valve, temperature and pressure relief valve, tundish, or hot-water discharge pipe as a safety warning. Keep clear of the discharge, switch off the heat source to the hot water system if you can do that safely, and arrange a competent engineer to check it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not cap, plug, clamp, tape, or restrict the relief valve outlet, tundish, overflow, or discharge pipe.
- Do not hold, wedge, or tamper with any relief valve, tundish, or safety control.
- Do not put your hand, face, or a container you are holding under the discharge, because the water may be very hot or forceful.
- Do not keep resetting controls or turning thermostats up and down while water is discharging.
- Do not remove covers, undo fittings, drain the cylinder, or replace parts as a first step.
- If you have an unvented hot water cylinder, do not attempt DIY repairs to the safety valves, expansion vessel, thermostat controls, or discharge pipework.

## What to do now
1. **Move people away from the discharge route.**  
   Keep children, pets, and anyone barefoot away from the cylinder cupboard, tundish, outside discharge pipe, drain, or overflow point. Keep the discharge opening clear. Only place a bucket nearby if it cannot restrict the outlet, splash back, or require someone to stand close to hot water.

2. **Look without touching.**  
   Check whether water is passing through a visible tundish, coming from an outside discharge pipe, or coming from a valve near the hot water cylinder. If water is passing through the tundish or discharge pipe, treat it as a sign that a safety device is operating and needs checking.

3. **Switch off the heat source to the hot water system.**  
   - For an immersion heater, switch it off at the labelled local isolator or fused spur.
   - For a boiler-heated cylinder, turn hot water off at the programmer or controller, and turn the boiler off if needed.
   - For a gas boiler, use the normal boiler control or isolator; do not remove boiler covers.

4. **If you smell gas or suspect carbon monoxide, leave that as the priority.**  
   Do not operate electrical switches, do not use flames, and move everyone into fresh air. Call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999 from a safe place. If anyone has collapsed, is seriously unwell, or there is immediate danger, call 999.

5. **If water continues after the heat is off, only isolate the cold feed if you are certain.**  
   If the cold-water supply valve to the cylinder is clearly labelled and you are sure it is the right valve, you can turn it off to reduce refilling and ongoing discharge. If you are not sure, do not guess or try several valves; leave it for the engineer.

6. **Get the right help.**  
   Ask for an engineer competent in unvented hot water storage systems. If the boiler or any gas work is involved, use a Gas Safe registered engineer and ask them to check that their card covers the work. If you are a tenant, report it to your landlord or agent as an urgent heating and hot-water problem, using any emergency repair route they provide.

7. **Capture only simple information.**  
   From a safe distance, take a short photo or video of the tundish or discharge point if you can. Note whether it happens during heating, after hot water use, constantly, or in bursts. Stop if getting the photo would put you close to hot water or electrical equipment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact cause now.
- You do not need to decide whether the cylinder needs replacing.
- You do not need to buy valves, expansion parts, or thermostats before an engineer has checked the system.
- You do not need to drain the cylinder unless a competent professional tells you to.
- You do not need to keep watching the tundish once people are safe, the heat source is off, and help has been arranged.

## Important reassurance
A relief valve and discharge pipe are there to let water escape rather than allowing pressure or temperature to build unchecked. Seeing discharge is still a reason to act, but the safest first steps are simple: keep it unblocked, reduce heating, keep people clear, and get it checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and avoid dangerous mistakes. A competent engineer may need to test the safety controls, inlet pressure control, expansion arrangement, relief valve, thermostat, and discharge pipework before the system is used normally again.

## Important note
This guide is general information for an urgent home safety situation, not professional plumbing, heating, gas, electrical, legal, or tenancy advice. If you are unsure about any switch, valve, discharge point, or system type, keep clear and get qualified help rather than experimenting.
