# What to do if… you are inside a building and someone is lingering in the lobby or entry area watching residents (UK)

## Short answer
Place a locked door between yourself and the lobby and alert building security, the concierge, or the police immediately. Do not attempt to confront or move the individual yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Don't confront the person or ask them why they are there.
- Don't hold the secure door open or allow them to "tailgate" behind you.
- Don't enter small, enclosed communal spaces like bin stores, bike rooms, or lifts if the person is nearby.
- Don't reveal your flat number or use your keys/fob while they are watching you.
- Don't "test" the situation by walking past the person to see if they follow.

## What to do now
1. **Move immediately to a secure, locked space.** This is typically your own flat or a staffed concierge area. 
   - If you are already in your flat, keep the door locked and use the door chain or peephole only.
   - If you are in a corridor, move to the nearest secure exit or staffed area without running.
2. **Contact building authority figures to report the loitering.** Call the concierge, on-site security, or the building management company if they have a 24-hour line. 
   - Request that they check the lobby or communal CCTV.
   - Ask for a staff member to meet you if you need to pass through the lobby to reach a safer location.
3. **Notify the police based on the level of immediate threat.** 
   - **Call 999** if the person is trying flat doors, following residents, being aggressive, or if you believe a crime is in progress.
   - **Use the Silent Solution** if you cannot speak: dial **999**, listen for the automated message "You are through to the police," and press **55** when prompted.
   - **Call 101** if the person is suspicious but not causing an immediate threat (e.g., "casing" the building or loitering without clear intent).
4. **Note specific, observable details for the authorities.** Focus on physical markers and actions rather than guessing their motivation.
   - Observe clothing (colour, type), approximate height, build, and any specific items they are carrying (e.g., a delivery bag without a uniform).
   - Identify the specific location (e.g., "by the mailboxes" or "sitting on the internal stairs").
5. **Wait for a group or official escort if you must exit the building.** Do not walk through the entry area alone if the individual is still present. 
   - Wait for another resident you know to walk with you.
   - If no one is available and you feel unsafe, stay in your secure location until the police or building staff confirm the area is clear.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to make a formal complaint against the building management today.
- You do not need to check your own CCTV or doorbell footage immediately; this can be done once you are calm and the situation is resolved.
- You do not need to notify other residents via social media or WhatsApp groups until you are physically safe.
- You do not need to research the legal definitions of "trespass" or "loitering" right now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sudden spike in adrenaline or anxiety when a stranger is watching your home or communal area. These reactions are biological signals designed to keep you alert. Moving to a secure space and calling for help is a standard safety procedure, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate physical stabilization and reporting for the first few minutes of noticing a suspicious person. It does not cover long-term security upgrades or civil disputes with landlords.

## Important note
This guide provides general safety information for residents in the UK. It is not a substitute for professional security advice or legal counsel. If you believe there is a risk of harm, always contact emergency services first.
