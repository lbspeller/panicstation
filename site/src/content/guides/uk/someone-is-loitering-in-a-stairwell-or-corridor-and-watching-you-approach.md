# What to do if… someone is loitering in a stairwell or corridor and watching you approach (UK)

## Short answer
Do not enter an enclosed stairwell or corridor with them if you can avoid it. Create distance, move to a safer and more public place, and contact building staff, security, or the police if you feel threatened.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t walk past them just to avoid seeming rude.
- Don’t go straight to your flat or room door if they could follow you and learn where you live.
- Don’t confront them, argue, or try to test the situation by getting closer.
- Don’t let yourself get funnelled into a dead end, such as a stairwell, long corridor, basement, bin store, or car park, if you can avoid it.
- Don’t fumble with keys at your door while they are nearby.
- Don’t hold an access-controlled door open behind you.
- Don’t post about it in real time, because it can distract you and may reveal your location.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the approach and change the situation.** Turn around calmly and head back to a safer point: outside the building, a staffed reception, a concierge desk, a well-lit lobby, or an area with other people and more than one way out.
2. **Put distance and a barrier between you and them if you can.** Move behind a secure door, into a staffed area, or into a lobby where you can clearly leave again. Avoid a lift, stairwell, or narrow corridor if it would trap you.
3. **Make contact with a real person immediately.** Call a friend, housemate, concierge, or building security. Keep the call simple: say where you are, what you can see, and that you are not going past the person yet.
4. **Use other people without putting yourself in a dead end.** If there are nearby residents, staff, open businesses, or passers-by, move towards them. Knock or ask for help only from a place where you still have space to leave.
5. **If you feel in danger or think violence could happen soon, call 999.** Say your building name, road, postcode if you know it, floor or entrance, what the person is doing, and that you are keeping distance.
6. **If you cannot speak safely on a 999 call, use the silent call process.** If you are calling from a mobile, stay on the line, listen to the prompts, and press 55 when prompted to be put through to police. If you are calling from a landline, stay on the line and follow the operator’s prompts; the 55 option is for mobile calls. If you already use Relay UK or another emergency text route, use the method you already have set up.
7. **If it feels concerning but not immediately dangerous, log it after you are safe.** Call 101 or use your local police force’s online reporting route.
8. **Capture useful details without staring or escalating.** Note clothing, approximate age and height, distinguishing features, exact location, direction of travel, and any vehicle details if relevant.
9. **If this is your building, report the immediate access problem.** Contact the landlord, letting agent, housing association, concierge, or building manager. Ask whether relevant CCTV, door-entry logs, lighting faults, broken locks, or tailgating issues can be checked and preserved.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it counts as a crime.
- You do not need to identify them, confront them, or prove intent.
- You do not need to write a full report immediately.
- You do not need to decide long-term building complaints, tenancy issues, or neighbour disputes right now.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, doubting yourself, or worrying about overreacting is common in a moment like this. Treating it as a safety problem first is reasonable: distance, exits, other people, and help are enough for the next few minutes.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes to hours only. If this keeps happening, feels targeted, or involves your home address, later decisions may need specialist help from building management, the police, a housing adviser, or another appropriate support service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel you are in immediate danger, prioritise getting to a safer place and contacting emergency services.
