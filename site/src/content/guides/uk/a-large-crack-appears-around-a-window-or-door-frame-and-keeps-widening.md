# What to do if… a large crack appears around a window or door frame and keeps widening (UK)

## Short answer
Evacuate the immediate area and call 999 if the crack is visibly widening, the wall is bulging, or you hear active cracking sounds. If the movement is slower but persistent, contact your local council’s Building Control "Dangerous Structures" team and your home insurer immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to force open or close windows or doors that have become jammed or distorted by the movement.
- Do not remove plaster, bricks, or wallpaper to "inspect" the depth of the crack.
- Do not fill, paint, or patch the crack, as this hides the rate of movement from professional surveyors.
- Do not ignore the smell of gas or the sound of hissing, which can occur if structural movement ruptures pipes.
- Do not stay in the building if you see debris falling or if floors feel suddenly unstable or sloped.

## What to do now
1. **Evacuate the immediate area of the crack.** Move all people and pets to a different part of the property or outside. If the crack is on an upper floor, also clear the room directly beneath it.
2. **Assess for immediate collapse risk.** If any of the following are occurring, leave the building immediately and call **999** (or 112):
   - The crack is widening noticeably over minutes or hours.
   - You can hear ongoing cracking, grinding, or loud creaking sounds.
   - The wall is visibly bowing, bulging, or leaning.
   - Substantial amounts of dust or debris are falling from the crack.
3. **Check for secondary utility hazards.** Structural movement can damage gas, water, or electric lines.
   - If you smell gas or hear hissing, do not use light switches or naked flames. Open windows and doors, leave the property, and call the **National Gas Emergency Service** on **0800 111 999**.
   - If you see water leaking from the area of the crack, locate and turn off the internal stop tap if it is safe to do so.
4. **Notify the relevant authority for safety.** Contact your local council’s **Building Control** department and ask for the **Dangerous Structures** officer.
   - Most councils provide a 24-hour emergency contact number for structures that may pose a risk to occupants or the public.
5. **Contact your landlord or insurer.**
   - **If you rent:** Report the damage to your landlord or letting agent immediately as an emergency safety issue.
   - **If you own:** Call your home insurance provider’s 24-hour claims or emergency helpline. Sudden structural movement is typically a time-sensitive reporting requirement in most policies.
6. **Capture evidence only if the area is stable.** If it is safe to remain in the property, take photos of the crack from a distance and close-up with a ruler or coin next to it for scale. Note the exact time the photo was taken.
7. **Secure the site from entry.** Close and lock doors to the affected room to prevent children or others from wandering near the hazard until a professional arrives.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the cause (such as subsidence, heave, or lintel failure) yourself; this requires a professional survey.
- You do not need to obtain repair quotes or contact builders until the structure is stabilized and your insurer has provided guidance.
- You do not need to worry about cosmetic redecoration or "tidying up" the cracked area.

## Important reassurance
Discovering a widening crack is a high-stress event, and feeling a sense of urgency or alarm is a normal reaction to a threat to your home's stability. Taking immediate steps to move away and contact specialists is the most effective way to manage the situation and ensure the safety of everyone in the building.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and risk reduction during an active structural event. It does not replace a formal inspection by a structural engineer or the statutory requirements for reporting dangerous buildings.

## Important note
Structural movement can lead to sudden collapse. If you are in any doubt about the safety of the building, evacuate immediately. This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional engineering or legal advice.
