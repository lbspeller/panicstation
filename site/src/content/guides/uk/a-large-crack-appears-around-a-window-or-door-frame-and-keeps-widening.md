# What to do if… a large crack appears around a window or door frame and keeps widening (UK)

## Short answer
Treat a widening crack around a window or door frame as a possible structural safety issue. Move people away from that area now, and if you think there is any chance of collapse or falling debris, leave the building and call 999 or 112.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep testing a sticking door or window by forcing it open or shut.
- Do not drill, chisel, cut, remove plaster, or remove brickwork to “see how bad it is”.
- Do not fill, tape over, or decorate the crack as your first response.
- Do not stand under or beside a wall, ceiling, frame, chimney breast, or lintel that is cracking, bowing, sagging, or shedding debris.
- Do not keep using washing machines, heavy equipment, or anything else that shakes the affected area if you can stop it safely.
- Do not stay inside to wait for confirmation if the building feels unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Move everyone, including children and pets, away from the cracked wall, the window or door opening, and the room directly above or below it if possible. Keep clear of anything that could fall.

2. **Decide whether this is an emergency right now.** Leave the building and call 999 or 112 if any of these are happening:
   - The crack is widening over minutes or hours.
   - You hear ongoing cracking, creaking, popping, or falling debris.
   - The wall, ceiling, lintel, or frame is bowing, bulging, sagging, or visibly deforming.
   - A door or window frame is suddenly out of square, jammed, or pulling away from the wall.
   - A floor nearby suddenly slopes, bounces, dips, or feels unstable.

3. **If you smell gas, hear hissing, or suspect a gas leak, treat that as urgent.** Do not use electrical switches, flames, or appliances. Open doors and windows only if you can do it without delaying your exit or entering a risky area. Do not enter a cellar or basement to turn gas off. Leave and call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999.

4. **Reduce load and vibration without trying to repair it.** If it is safe to remain briefly, stop anything shaking the area, keep heavy items away from that wall, and do not lean furniture, ladders, or tools against the cracked section.

5. **Record only what helps.** Take a few clear photos or a short video from a safe distance, with a coin, ruler, or door handle in view for scale. Note the time and whether the crack is still changing. If it is safe, make a small pencil mark at each end of the crack, then stop checking repeatedly.

6. **Use the right urgent building-safety route.**
   - If you rent, report it immediately to your landlord or letting agent as an urgent safety repair, by phone and in writing.
   - If you own, consider contacting your home insurer before starting repairs and ask what evidence or inspection they need.
   - If the structure may be dangerous to occupants, neighbours, pedestrians, or the public, contact your local council or local authority dangerous-structure route. The service may be called Building Control, Building Standards, or a dangerous buildings service, depending on where you are in the UK.

7. **Arrange a professional assessment before repairs.** Ask for an independent structural engineer or a chartered building surveyor to assess the movement. Avoid agreeing to major repairs, underpinning, replacement windows, or cosmetic filling until the affected area has been properly assessed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the cause today, such as subsidence, lintel failure, moisture, settlement, or previous building work.
- You do not need to choose a repair method today.
- You do not need to contact multiple contractors before you know whether the structure is safe.
- You do not need to redecorate, fill, or hide the crack now.

## Important reassurance
A widening crack can feel frightening because it may signal real movement, and taking it seriously is sensible. Creating distance, avoiding DIY investigation, recording what you can safely see, and involving the right emergency or building-safety route are enough for the first stage.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about cause, repairs, insurance, liability, tenancy issues, or costs may need help from a structural engineer, chartered surveyor, insurer, landlord, local authority, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, insurance, engineering, building-control, or professional safety advice. If you think anyone is in immediate danger, leave the building and call emergency services.
