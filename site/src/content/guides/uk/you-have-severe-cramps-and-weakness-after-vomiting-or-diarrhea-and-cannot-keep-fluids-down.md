# What to do if… you have severe cramps and weakness after vomiting or diarrhea and cannot keep fluids down (UK)

## Short answer
Treat severe cramps and weakness after vomiting or diarrhoea as possible dehydration and salt loss. Call 999 now if you are confused, difficult to wake, struggling to breathe, have cold or blotchy skin, vomit blood, have bloody diarrhoea, or have sudden severe tummy pain; otherwise get urgent advice from your GP, 111 if available where you are, or your local urgent care route.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to sleep it off if you are getting weaker, confused, faint, or barely passing urine.
- Do not drive yourself to get help if you feel faint, confused, very weak, or close to collapse.
- Do not drink alcohol.
- Do not drink fruit juice or fizzy drinks while diarrhoea is continuing, as they can make diarrhoea worse.
- Do not gulp large amounts quickly if you are vomiting; use small, frequent sips instead.
- Do not take ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac, or similar anti-inflammatory painkillers while you may be dehydrated unless a clinician or pharmacist has told you it is safe for you.
- Do not use medicine to stop diarrhoea while you have blood in your poo, bleeding from your bottom, severe belly pain, fever, or you feel very unwell unless a pharmacist or clinician says it is suitable.
- Do not stop or change regular medicines by guessing; ask for urgent medicine or “sick day” advice if dehydration may affect your medicines.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether this is an emergency. Call 999 now if you or the person affected:**
   - is confused, not responding normally, unusually sleepy, or difficult to wake
   - has severe difficulty breathing, or is taking lots of quick, short breaths
   - has blue, grey, pale, blotchy, or cold skin, lips, or tongue; on brown or black skin, check the palms of the hands or soles of the feet
   - vomits blood, has vomit that looks like coffee grounds, has bloody diarrhoea, or has bleeding from the bottom
   - has sudden severe tummy pain, severe pain in one place, or pain that is getting worse
   - may have swallowed something poisonous

2. **If it is not a 999 emergency but you cannot rehydrate well, get urgent health advice now.**
   - Contact your GP practice urgently if it is open.
   - If your GP is closed or you cannot get through, use 111 if it is available where you are in the UK, or use your local GP out-of-hours or urgent care route.
   - Say clearly: “I have vomiting or diarrhoea, severe cramps and weakness, and I am struggling to keep fluids down.”

3. **Start small-sip rehydration while you arrange advice.**
   - Use oral rehydration solution sachets if you have them, mixed exactly as the packet says.
   - If you are vomiting, take teaspoons or very small sips often.
   - If oral rehydration solution will not stay down yet, take small sips of water or diluted squash until you can try oral rehydration solution again.

4. **Reduce strain while you wait.**
   - Sit or lie somewhere safe and cool.
   - Avoid exertion, heat, alcohol, and large meals.
   - Eat only if you feel able. Bland food is fine, but fluids matter more right now.

5. **Watch two simple worsening signs.**
   - **Urine:** very dark pee, strong-smelling pee, or peeing much less than usual.
   - **Standing:** dizziness, faintness, or vision greying out when you stand.
   If either is getting worse, update the urgent care service. Call 999 if the symptoms become severe or you feel close to collapse.

6. **Say early if you are higher risk.**
   Tell the GP, 111, pharmacist, or urgent care service if you are pregnant, over 65, have diabetes, kidney disease, inflammatory bowel disease, a weakened immune system, take diuretics or blood pressure medicines, recently took antibiotics, recently travelled, or have been in hospital recently.

7. **Keep your medicines nearby for advice.**
   Have the packets, bottles, or repeat prescription list with you. This helps a clinician or pharmacist check whether dehydration changes what you should do with your usual medicines.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now, such as virus, food poisoning, or something you ate.
- You do not need to force normal meals while you feel unstable.
- You do not need to decide about work, school, travel, or plans today.
- You do not need to try several over-the-counter medicines at once.
- You do not need to handle this alone if you feel faint, confused, very weak, or unable to keep fluids down.

## Important reassurance
Severe cramps and weakness after vomiting or diarrhoea can happen when the body is short of fluid and salts, and it can feel frightening. Getting urgent advice and taking small, steady sips is a sensible way to protect yourself while you find out whether you need more help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about tests, treatment, medicines, work, travel, or ongoing symptoms may need help from a clinician, pharmacist, or another appropriate professional.

## Important note
This is general information only and is not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If symptoms are severe, worsening, or you cannot keep fluids down, use urgent NHS, GP out-of-hours, local urgent care, or 999 services as appropriate.
