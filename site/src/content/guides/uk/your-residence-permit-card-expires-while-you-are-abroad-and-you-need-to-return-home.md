# What to do if… your residence permit card expires while you are abroad and you need to return home (UK)

## Short answer
Do not make an expired residence permit card your travel plan. First get into your UKVI account, check that your eVisa and current passport details match your booking, and ask the airline to confirm how they will check your status; if that cannot be fixed before travel, use the official route for a temporary visa or other official return route.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume an expired BRP will be accepted for boarding.
- Do not assume the date printed on the BRP is the end of your immigration permission; check your eVisa or official decision record.
- Do not change flights, routes, or accommodation in a rush before checking what your airline and UKVI account show.
- Do not rely on screenshots, forwarded images, or unofficial “verification letters” as your main proof.
- Do not pay unofficial agents who claim they can quickly fix your UK immigration status or documents.

## What to do now
1. Write down the details you will need for calls and forms:
   - your return travel date;
   - your BRP expiry date;
   - your passport number and expiry date;
   - the name, date of birth, and nationality on your passport;
   - the email address and phone number linked to your UKVI account;
   - a safe place where you can receive email, texts, and calls.

2. Sign in to your UKVI account and view your eVisa.
   - Check that your eVisa is visible.
   - Check that your name, date of birth, nationality, and immigration status look right.
   - Get a share code if the service lets you do that.
   - If you cannot sign in, use the official UKVI account recovery or webchat route.

3. Check the passport link before you focus on the flight.
   - Confirm that the passport or travel document you will use to travel is added to your UKVI account.
   - If you renewed your passport or your details changed while abroad, try to update the UKVI account first.
   - If the system says you cannot update the details from outside the UK, follow the official route it gives you for returning.

4. Contact the airline before airport check-in.
   - Ask: “I have UK immigration permission as an eVisa. Can you check my permission to travel to the UK using the passport details in your system?”
   - Ask them to record the answer on your booking if they can.
   - If they say they cannot check your eVisa, ask them to contact the UK Border Force carrier support hub.
   - Keep any written reply or chat transcript.

5. Build a two-track plan.
   - Track A: regain or confirm UKVI account access, make sure your passport details match, and get airline confirmation before you go to the airport.
   - Track B: if Track A will not work before travel, use the official online route for a temporary visa or other official return route, as directed by GOV.UK or the visa application service.

6. If Track B is needed, gather documents before you start the application.
   - Passport.
   - Your BRP or other residence-permit card, even if expired.
   - UKVI reference numbers, if you have them.
   - Home Office decision email or letter, if available.
   - Documents that may help explain that you normally live, work, or study in the UK, such as a tenancy document, employer letter, student letter, or recent UK address evidence.
   - Details of the nearest visa application centre in the country you are in, because you may need to give fingerprints and a photo.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now how to handle long-term document changes after you return.
- You do not need to explain every detail to work, university, or family until you know whether your return travel can go ahead.
- You do not need to argue at the airport; it is usually better to sort the UKVI account, passport match, airline check, or official fallback before you get there.
- You do not need to solve every immigration-record issue today if the immediate problem is return travel.

## Important reassurance
This is stressful because it combines documents, travel, and time pressure, but there are official steps for checking an eVisa, updating passport details, getting help with a UKVI account, and applying for a return route if needed. Work through one step at a time and avoid expensive rushed decisions until you know which track is possible.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and make a practical return plan. Later decisions, including correcting records or dealing with a delayed return, may need specialist immigration advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Immigration and travel checks can depend on your exact status, passport, airline, route, and country you are in, so use official UKVI processes and get specialist advice if you are unsure.
