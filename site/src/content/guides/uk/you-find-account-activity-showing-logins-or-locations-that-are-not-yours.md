# What to do if… you find account activity showing logins or locations that are not yours (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the account by ending all active sessions, changing the password to a unique phrase, and enabling two-step verification immediately. Secure your primary email account first, as it is the gateway to all other digital services.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links in "security alert" emails or texts to resolve the issue; navigate to the service's official website or app independently.
- Don’t reuse any current or previous password, even with minor variations or added symbols.
- Don’t assume a "strange location" is a technical error if it is accompanied by unfamiliar device names or browser types.
- Don’t ignore your recovery email settings; attackers often change these to regain access after you change your password.
- Don’t delay reporting financial theft to your bank if the compromised account contains stored payment methods.

## What to do now
1. **Log in to the affected account via the official website or app.**
   - Do not use the link provided in the notification email.
   - If you are locked out, use the official "Account Recovery" or "I've lost access" pathway provided by the service.

2. **End all active sessions and sign out of all devices.**
   - Look for "Security," "Sign-in activity," or "Manage devices" in your settings.
   - Select the option to "Sign out of all other sessions" to disconnect any unauthorized users immediately.

3. **Secure your primary email account regardless of which account was flagged.**
   - Change your email password to a unique string of three random words.
   - Check "Rules" or "Filters" in your email settings for any instructions to "forward" or "delete" incoming mail, which attackers use to hide password reset alerts.

4. **Update the compromised account's password and recovery details.**
   - Create a strong, unique password not used for any other service.
   - Verify that the recovery phone number and secondary email address are still yours.

5. **Enable two-step verification (2SV) or multi-factor authentication (MFA).**
   - Use an authenticator app (such as Google, Microsoft, or Authy) rather than SMS if the service allows it.
   - Download or record "Backup codes" and store them offline in a safe physical location.

6. **Review recent account changes and transactions.**
   - Check for new "trusted devices," added apps, or changes to your display name and contact information.
   - Undo any changes you do not recognise.

7. **Forward the original suspicious notification to the authorities.**
   - Forward suspicious emails to `report@phishing.gov.uk`.
   - Forward suspicious text messages to `7726`.

8. **Report the incident if personal data or money was stolen.**
   - For users in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report to **Report Fraud** at `reportfraud.police.uk` or call 0300 123 2040.
   - For users in Scotland, report to **Police Scotland** by calling 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific IP address or physical identity of the intruder right now.
- You do not need to delete the account entirely unless you cannot regain control and it contains sensitive data.
- You do not need to change every password for every account you own immediately; prioritize email, banking, and any accounts that shared the compromised password.
- You do not need to pay for credit monitoring services in the first few minutes; you can apply for **CIFAS Protective Registration** (£30 for 2 years) later if you believe your full identity is at risk.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a login alert for a location you haven't visited is a standard security feature designed to give you the upper hand. In most cases, taking immediate steps to end sessions and update your password stops the unauthorized access before any significant data or financial loss occurs. Confusion and a sense of urgency are normal reactions to digital intrusion.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for suspected account compromise. It does not cover long-term identity theft recovery, legal action against providers, or forensic device cleaning.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or technical advice. If you suspect your workplace accounts are affected, you must follow your organization’s internal IT security reporting procedures. If you are at immediate risk of financial loss, contact your bank using the number on the back of your card or their official app.
