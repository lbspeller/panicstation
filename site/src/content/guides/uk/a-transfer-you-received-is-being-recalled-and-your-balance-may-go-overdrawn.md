# What to do if… a transfer you received is being recalled and your balance may go overdrawn (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the funds as unavailable immediately and contact your bank using the number on your card or the 159 hotline to confirm the recall type. Do not send money back to the sender yourself, as this is often a scam; let the banks handle the reversal internally.

## Do not do these things
- Don't send any money back to the sender directly via bank transfer, cash, or crypto.
- Don't move your own money out of the account to "hide" it, as this can lead to a deeper negative balance if the recall is forced.
- Don't click links in texts or emails claiming to be from your bank regarding the recall.
- Don't ignore the notice; automated recalls can happen quickly, leaving you with failed Direct Debits.
- Don't share one-time passcodes (OTPs) with anyone, even if they claim to be from the bank's fraud team.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all non-essential spending from the affected account immediately.**  
   Assume the disputed funds will be removed within 24 to 48 hours and do not rely on the current displayed balance for new purchases.

2. **Dial 159 to speak with your bank's fraud or security department.**  
   159 is the secure UK-wide hotline that connects you directly to most major banks; use this if you suspect the original transfer or the recall request is part of a scam.

3. **Identify all essential payments due to leave your account in the next three days.**  
   Check your banking app for:
   - Mortgage or rent payments.
   - Utility Direct Debits.
   - Pending card transactions that have not yet "settled."
   If the recall will cause these to fail, move a small amount of your own money from a savings account into this one to cover the gap.

4. **Ask the bank specifically if this is a "Credit Payment Recovery" (CPR) or a fraud-related debit.**  
   If it is a CPR (mistaken payment), the bank generally has to give you 20 working days to respond before returning the funds, unless they suspect you are acting dishonestly. Ask the agent:
   - "Is this a request for me to consent to a return, or is the bank mandating the reversal?"
   - "Can you place a temporary 'fee block' on my account to prevent overdraft charges caused by this specific reversal?"

5. **Request a formal hold on any associated overdraft interest.**  
   Under the FCA Consumer Duty (2026), banks should act to prevent foreseeable harm; tell them that the sudden removal of funds will cause financial distress and ask for a temporary waiver of unarranged overdraft fees.

6. **Contact essential billers if a payment is likely to bounce.**  
   Use a clear script: "There is a temporary bank-side investigation affecting my available balance. I am working with my bank to resolve this, but my payment on [date] may be delayed. Can you note my account to avoid late fees?"

7. **Report the incident to the official "Report Fraud" service.**  
   If the sender is pressuring you or you believe the original payment was part of a "triangulation" or "overpayment" scam, file a report at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.

8. **Secure a copy of the bank's "Final Response" if they refuse to help with fees.**  
   If the bank allows the account to go negative and charges you fees for a mistake you did not make, start a formal complaint in-app immediately. You will need their final letter to escalate to the Financial Ombudsman Service later.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove the money is "yours" in the first hour; focusing on fee prevention is more urgent.
- You do not need to respond to messages from the sender; legal and procedural communication should go through the bank.
- You do not need to close the account; doing so during an active investigation can trigger a "CIFAS" marker or other fraud flags.

## Important reassurance
Bank errors and mistaken payments are common, and the recall process is a standard industry mechanism designed to fix them. Having your balance fluctuate is stressful, but by contacting your bank early and naming the "Credit Payment Recovery" process, you are taking the correct steps to protect your credit score and avoid unfair charges.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilisation only. It does not provide legal advice on the ownership of disputed funds or long-term debt management.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only. Bank policies regarding Faster Payments and Credit Payment Recovery (CPR) are subject to specific terms and conditions. Always follow the instructions provided through your bank’s official, verified secure channels.
