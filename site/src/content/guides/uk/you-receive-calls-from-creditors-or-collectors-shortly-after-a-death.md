# What to do if… you receive calls from creditors or collectors shortly after a death (UK)

## Short answer
State clearly that the person has died and that you will not discuss the matter by phone. Request that the creditor provides all details of the claim in writing to the estate and end the call immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay any money from your own personal bank account during the call.
- Don’t agree to a payment plan or "temporary arrangement" over the phone.
- Don’t provide your own personal details, such as your date of birth, National Insurance number, or workplace.
- Don’t admit liability for the debt if you are not a joint account holder or a guarantor.
- Don’t feel obligated to continue a conversation with an aggressive or pressuring caller.

## What to do now
1. **End the call immediately after notifying them of the death.** **State that the account holder is deceased and you require all future correspondence to be sent in writing to the personal representative.**
   - Do not wait for their response or answer "security questions" about yourself.
   - If they continue to speak, hang up the phone.
2. **Use the Death Notification Service for major financial institutions.** **Visit the Death Notification Service website to notify multiple banks and building societies at once.**
   - This service is free and covers most major UK lenders, which should stop automated calls from these firms.
3. **Keep a precise log of all contact.** **Write down the date, time, company name, and the name of the caller for every call received.**
   - Note the specific amount they claim is owed and the account number if they provide it.
4. **Identify the correct point of contact for the estate.** **Determine if there is a will and who the named executor is, as they are responsible for official creditor contact.**
   - If you are not the executor or administrator, simply state: "I am not the personal representative of the estate. Please send all letters to 'The Personal Representative of [Name]' at the deceased's last address."
5. **Request a formal statement of account in writing.** **Send a brief letter or email to the creditor asking for the total balance, the nature of the debt, and their bereavement team’s contact details.**
   - Ask them to "pause" the account for a reasonable period (usually 30 to 60 days) while probate is sought.
6. **Apply for 'Breathing Space' if the pressure is unmanageable.** **Contact a debt advice charity such as National Debtline or StepChange to see if the estate or you personally qualify for the Debt Respite Scheme.**
   - This can provide 60 days of legal protection from creditor action and contact.
7. **Protect the estate by placing a Deceased Estates notice.** **Place a notice in The Gazette and a local newspaper to notify potential creditors of the death and set a deadline for claims.**
   - This protects the executor or administrator from personal liability for debts they were unaware of when they eventually distribute the estate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove the estate is solvent or insolvent during the first few calls.
- You do not need to calculate the priority order of debts (e.g., funeral costs vs credit cards) today.
- You do not need to apply for Probate or Letters of Administration immediately just to satisfy a debt collector.
- You do not need to verify the accuracy of the debt amount until you have received written statements.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for automated debt collection systems to trigger calls before a company’s bereavement department has processed the notification of death. Receiving these calls does not mean you have done anything wrong, and you have a legal right to insist on written communication only.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling immediate creditor contact. It does not cover the legal process of probate, the complex rules for insolvent estates, or the distribution of assets.

## Important note
Individual liability for debt depends on whether the debt was held jointly or if a guarantee was signed. If you are being pressured to pay a debt you believe is not yours, seek independent advice from a regulated debt advisor before making any payments.
