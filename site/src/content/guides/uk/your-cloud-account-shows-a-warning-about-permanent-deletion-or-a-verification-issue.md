# What to do if… your cloud account shows a warning about permanent deletion or a verification issue (UK)

## Short answer
Go to the provider’s official website or app, not a link in a message, and use its recovery, appeal, or verification route. If you still have access, start exporting the most irreplaceable files now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click “verify” links from unexpected emails, texts, adverts, or pop-ups; deletion warnings are often copied by phishing scams.
- Don’t keep retrying logins or verification in a panic if the provider says to wait; repeated attempts can sometimes add more security checks.
- Don’t send passport or driving licence images by email, direct message, or social media; only use the provider’s official secure upload flow.
- Don’t sign out of a device where you are still logged in, unless the provider tells you to.
- Don’t pay anyone who claims they can restore the account through social media, direct messages, or unofficial support.

## What to do now
1. **Check the warning through a clean route.**  
   Open a fresh browser window or the official app, type the provider’s address yourself, and sign in from there. If the warning only appears after clicking a message link, treat the message as suspicious.

2. **Capture proof before anything changes.**  
   Screenshot the warning, any deadline shown, any case or ticket ID, the exact wording of the policy or verification reason, and the options offered. Keep the original message and do not delete it. If you already know how to save email headers, save them too.

3. **Use the provider’s official recovery, appeal, or verification flow.**  
   Follow the route shown inside the official website or app. Give consistent details that match the account, such as the recovery email, phone number, billing history, device names, or approximate last successful login. Avoid opening several separate tickets for the same issue unless the provider tells you to.

4. **Start an export or backup if you still have access.**  
   Prioritise files that are hardest to recreate: photos, original documents, account records, recovery codes, and files needed to keep work, study, benefits, bills, or other services running. Use the provider’s built-in export or download tool if available, and save the export somewhere you control.

5. **Keep the working session alive.**  
   If one trusted phone, tablet, or computer is still signed in, keep it powered, connected, and nearby. Do not revoke sessions, change recovery details, or reset security settings until you have a confirmed way back in, unless the provider’s official flow instructs you to do so.

6. **Reduce security flags while you recover.**  
   Use a familiar device and your normal internet connection. Avoid VPNs, proxies, rapid password guesses, and repeated failed verification attempts. If the page tells you to wait before retrying, follow that instruction.

7. **Use official support from inside the account or provider site.**  
   Send the ticket ID, screenshots, approximate last login, payment receipts or order IDs if relevant, and the device names you normally use. Upload identity documents only through the provider’s secure portal.

8. **If you suspect a scam or account takeover, secure the recovery email first.**  
   Change the recovery email password, turn on two-step verification if you can, and check for suspicious forwarding rules, filters, recovery numbers, or recovery email changes. If you have been affected by fraud or cyber crime in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud. If you are in Scotland, report fraud to Police Scotland via 101. If you saw a suspicious website, you can report the site to the National Cyber Security Centre.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to leave the provider permanently.
- You do not need a perfect full backup before acting; start with the files and account details you cannot easily recreate.
- You do not need to argue every policy detail right now; focus first on stopping deletion, exporting key data, and preserving proof.
- You do not need to buy recovery software or pay unofficial account-recovery services.

## Important reassurance
A deletion or verification warning is stressful, and that pressure can make unsafe links or rushed uploads feel tempting. Using only official routes, keeping your last working session open, and saving proof gives you the best chance of preventing avoidable loss.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation only. Later decisions about complaints, data rights, provider disputes, security hardening, or backup routines may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. Provider processes vary and can change. UK personal data rights, including access and data portability, may apply in some situations, but they do not guarantee that a provider can or will restore an account or stop deletion.
