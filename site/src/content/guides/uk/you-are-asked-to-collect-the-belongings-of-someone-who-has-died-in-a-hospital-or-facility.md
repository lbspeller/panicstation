# What to do if… you are asked to collect the belongings of someone who has died in a hospital or facility (UK)

## Short answer
Phone the hospital or facility before you go. Ask who is holding the belongings, what ID or authorisation they need, whether you need an appointment, and how you can get a written record of what is handed over.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t turn up without checking first; some places use appointments, office hours, or a named bereavement, patient affairs, ward, or property team.
- Don’t accept belongings informally from a busy ward if there is no clear record of what is being handed over.
- Don’t argue at the desk about who should collect; ask what proof or written authorisation their policy requires.
- Don’t assume everything is in one place; valuables, clothing, mobility aids, documents, and medical devices may be held separately.
- Don’t assume everything can be released immediately; some items may be delayed if a coroner or another investigating authority is involved.
- Don’t throw items away before you have made a simple list and checked for keys, phone, wallet or purse, documents, jewellery, glasses, hearing aids, dentures, and bank cards.
- Don’t give or take the person’s prescription medicines for anyone else to use.

## What to do now
1. **Call the ward, bereavement office, patient affairs team, mortuary team, care home, hospice, or facility reception.**  
   Ask:
   - who is holding the belongings,
   - whether collection is from the ward, an office, a safe, the mortuary area, or another location,
   - whether an appointment is needed,
   - what opening hours apply,
   - what ID or authorisation you need to bring.

2. **Ask who the facility is allowed to release items to.**  
   Say: “I’ve been asked to collect belongings. What does your policy require before you can release them?” If you are not the recorded contact, ask whether they can accept written authorisation from that person plus your ID.

3. **Take ID and any requested authorisation.**  
   Bring photo ID if you have it, such as a passport or driving licence. Also bring any written authorisation, reference number, contact details, or paperwork the facility has asked for.

4. **Ask for a property list, inventory, receipt, or written note of the handover.**  
   Before signing, or as soon as you safely can, check for the main high-risk items: phone, wallet or purse, bank cards, cash, keys, ID documents, jewellery, glasses, hearing aids, dentures, mobility aids, and important papers. If you are given sealed bags, ask how to record any concern after you open them.

5. **If something important is missing, pause and ask where else items may be held.**  
   Ask:
   - “Are any valuables held separately in a safe?”
   - “Was anything transferred from the ward to a bereavement office, mortuary, patient affairs office, or property store?”
   - “Could any items be held because another authority is involved?”
   - “Who should I contact if I notice something missing after I leave?”
   Write down the name of the team, the phone number, and what they said.

6. **For a care home, hospice, or other facility, ask about room clearance before removing things.**  
   Ask how long they can safely store items, whether you need a collection appointment, whether furniture or equipment is included, and whether anything belongs to the facility and must stay or be returned.

7. **Ask what to do with medicines and medical equipment.**  
   If medicines are handed to you, ask whether they should be left with staff or returned to a pharmacy for safe disposal. Ask whether any equipment, such as mobility aids, oxygen equipment, hearing support items, pressure cushions, or loaned NHS or care equipment, needs to be returned and to whom.

8. **If you cannot face collecting the belongings yourself, ask about an authorised alternative.**  
   Some facilities may allow another authorised person or a funeral director to collect items. Ask exactly what written permission and ID they need before arranging this.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today what to keep, donate, sell, or throw away.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements about the belongings at the collection point.
- You do not need to sort every bag immediately; first keep the items safe and keep the handover record.
- You do not need to deal with the estate, banks, insurance, or formal records before collecting ordinary personal effects unless the facility specifically asks for proof of authority.

## Important reassurance
This can feel like an unreal and painful errand. Asking for a careful handover, writing down what you receive, and pausing before sorting things is a reasonable way to prevent later confusion.

## Scope note
These are first steps for collecting belongings safely and avoiding irreversible mix-ups. Later decisions about the estate, disputes, complaints, insurance, access to records, or missing valuables may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Processes vary between hospitals, hospices, care homes, and UK nations, and some items may be handled differently if an investigating authority is involved.
