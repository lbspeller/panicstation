# What to do if… you are told your vehicle is flagged and you must attend an appointment to resolve it (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before paying, clicking links, or driving the vehicle. Verify the contact through an official police route, then prepare documents that show who you are, your connection to the vehicle, and whether it is insured, taxed, and correctly identified.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay a fee, fine, release charge, or “clearance” payment over the phone, by bank transfer, gift card, or crypto unless you have independently verified the route is official.
- Do not use links, QR codes, or direct numbers from a text or email as your only proof that the appointment is real.
- Do not ignore a verified police request; leaving it unresolved can increase the chance of further stops, delays, or escalation.
- Do not drive the vehicle until you have checked the issue if the concern may involve insurance, licence cover, cloned plates, incorrect plates, or a stolen-vehicle report.
- Do not alter, remove, or replace number plates before asking what the marker is about, unless police or another official body tells you to do so.
- Do not hand over original documents to anyone outside an official police station, police pound, solicitor’s office, insurer, or other verified official route.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the contact through an official route.**
   - If there is immediate danger, call 999.
   - If it claims to be from the police and it is not an emergency, use Police.uk, your local force website, or call 101.
   - Say you have been told your vehicle is flagged and ask them to confirm the appointment, station or unit, reference number, and what you should bring.
   - If someone gave you a direct mobile number, do not rely on it alone; ask the official switchboard or contact route to connect you.

2. **Ask what the marker or flag is believed to relate to.**
   - Ask whether it is about suspected cloned plates, a stolen-vehicle report, insurance, licence cover, keeper details, tax, MOT, or another enquiry.
   - Ask whether the vehicle itself must be brought, or whether you should attend without it.
   - Write down the name or collar number if given, the reference number, the date, the time, and the exact words used to describe the flag.

3. **Check the vehicle records you can check yourself.**
   - Check MOT status on GOV.UK using the registration number.
   - Check vehicle tax or SORN status on GOV.UK using the registration number.
   - Check your insurance with your insurer first: confirm the policy is active, the registration number is correct, and the named or permitted drivers are covered.
   - You can also use askMID or Navigate to check whether your own vehicle appears insured.

4. **Prepare a small proof pack.**
   - Bring photo ID, such as a driving licence or passport.
   - Bring proof of address if you have it.
   - Bring the V5C log book if you have it, or the new keeper slip, purchase invoice, lease agreement, company car letter, or other document showing your link to the vehicle.
   - Bring proof of insurance, such as the certificate, insurer email, or insurer app record.
   - Bring any notice, letter, text, email, or roadside paperwork you were given.
   - If cloned plates are mentioned, bring clear photos of your vehicle from the front, back, both sides, and any distinguishing marks.

5. **Plan the appointment so it does not create a roadside problem.**
   - If there is any doubt about insurance, licence cover, identity of the vehicle, or whether the car is reported stolen, arrange travel without driving the flagged vehicle.
   - If you are told the vehicle must be inspected, ask the police how they want it brought in before you drive it.
   - Tell someone you trust where you are going and keep your phone charged.

6. **Get legal advice before attending if the contact sounds like an interview or arrest risk.**
   - This matters if you are told you may be interviewed under caution, there may be a warrant or bail issue, or the vehicle is linked to an investigation.
   - A solicitor can help you attend prepared and can tell you whether to ask for legal advice at the station.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, claim compensation, contact the previous keeper, or argue about who caused the marker.
- You do not need to post about it online or share screenshots of police messages.
- You do not need to prove everything immediately; the first task is to verify the appointment, avoid scam payments, avoid unlawful driving, and bring the right documents.

## Important reassurance
A flagged vehicle can be caused by a record mismatch, a report connected to the number plate, an insurance or keeper-detail problem, or a precautionary marker. Being asked to attend does not by itself prove you have done anything wrong, and a careful verification-first approach can reduce the chance of a stressful stop or mistake.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the vehicle has been seized, is linked to suspected cloning, or is part of an active police investigation, later decisions may need help from a solicitor, your insurer, DVLA, or the relevant police force.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Police processes and documents can vary by force and by the reason for the marker. If you feel unsafe, suspect a scam, or are unsure whether driving the vehicle is lawful, use official contact routes and consider getting legal advice before attending.
