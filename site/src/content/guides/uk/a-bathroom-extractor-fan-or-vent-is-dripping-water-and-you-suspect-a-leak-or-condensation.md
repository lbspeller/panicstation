# What to do if… a bathroom extractor fan or vent is dripping water and you suspect a leak or condensation (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as a water-and-electricity risk. Stop using the fan, keep people away from the wet area, and isolate the fan or bathroom circuit if water is dripping onto or near any electrical fitting.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep running the fan to “dry it out” if water is dripping from it or from the ceiling around it.
- Don’t touch the fan, grille, light fitting, switches, or isolator with wet hands, or while standing on a wet floor.
- Don’t rely on the bathroom light switch alone to make the fan safe.
- Don’t open the fan housing, pull at the grille, or disturb wiring while the area is wet.
- Don’t go near exposed wiring, buzzing, crackling, smoke, sparks, or a burning smell.
- Don’t ignore a bulging, soft, or sagging ceiling around the fan.
- Don’t paint, seal, or cover the damp patch before the cause has been checked.

## What to do now
1. **Make the bathroom safe to stand near.** Put a bucket or towels under the drip if you can do that without touching the fan or wet electrics. Dry the floor and keep people and pets out until it is not slippery.

2. **Stop adding moisture.** Pause showers and baths for now. If it is safe and does not put you near the drip, open a window slightly. Keep the bathroom door mostly shut until the floor is dry so moist air does not spread through the home.

3. **Switch the fan off from a safe, dry place.** If there is a fan isolator switch outside or above the bathroom door, use that only if the switch and surrounding wall are dry. If the fan, light, switch, or ceiling around them is wet, turn off the relevant circuit at the consumer unit if you can reach it safely without crossing wet areas.

4. **If you cannot isolate it safely, step back.** Do not reach through water, touch wet switches, or open the fitting. Keep the room closed off and contact a qualified electrician, landlord, or building manager.

5. **If there are electrical warning signs, treat it as urgent.** If you notice smoke, sparks, a burning smell, buzzing, crackling, electric shock, or repeated circuit tripping, keep away from the bathroom and get urgent electrical help. If there is fire or immediate danger, leave the property and call 999.

6. **If the dripping is continuous or getting worse, reduce possible plumbing damage.** If you suspect a live water pipe leak and you can reach the internal stop tap safely, turn it clockwise slowly. Do not force it. This will not stop every type of leak, but it can reduce damage from many supply pipe leaks.

7. **Record what you can without dismantling anything.** Take photos or a short video of the drip, damp ring, staining, swelling, soft plaster, and the fan or vent location. Note whether it started after a shower, during rain or wind, or without an obvious trigger.

8. **Do a quick look-only check from the bathroom.** Look for damp rings, cracks, sagging, soft plaster, or staining spreading away from the fan. Drips mainly after showers can point towards condensation or duct problems; continuous or worsening dripping can point towards an active leak.

9. **Only check above the bathroom if it is safe and easy.** If you can safely access a loft or roof space without touching wet wiring or unstable surfaces, look only. Look for a wet or sagging extractor duct, a disconnected duct, a duct ending in the loft, wet insulation, wet timbers, dripping pipework, or signs of roof water. If you see water near wiring or junction boxes, leave the area and keep the power isolated.

10. **Report or arrange help now.**
    - If you rent or have building management, report it now and say: “Water is dripping from the bathroom extractor fan or vent, there may be contact with electrics, and the circuit or fan has been isolated if safe.” Send the photos.
    - If you own, contact a plumber if it looks like a pipe or drain leak, a roofer if it happens during rain or near roof/vent pipework, and a qualified electrician if any electrical fitting may have got wet.
    - If the ceiling is bulging, soft, or sagging, keep out of the room and tell the repair person or landlord that there may be trapped water above the ceiling.

11. **Once electrical risk is controlled, dry only what you can reach.** Wipe up surface water, keep the area ventilated if safe, and leave investigation or repair of the fan, duct, ceiling void, wiring, pipework, or roof to the right professional.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide right now whether it is condensation, a roof leak, or a plumbing leak.
- You don’t need to choose a new fan, ducting, insulation, humidistat, or bathroom upgrade today.
- You don’t need to dismantle the fan, cut holes, remove insulation, or inspect wiring yourself.
- Insurance, quotes, redecorating, and mould treatment can wait until the immediate water and electrical risk is controlled.

## Important reassurance
This situation is unsettling, but it is a known type of household problem. Warm, humid bathroom air can collect as water in cold or poorly routed ducting, and roof or pipe leaks can show up at the fan opening because it is a gap in the ceiling. Acting cautiously around the electrics is the right first step while the cause is checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The eventual fix may involve extractor duct routing, duct insulation, duct sealing, outside termination, roof repair, plumbing repair, ceiling repair, or electrical inspection.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, electrical, plumbing, building, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If water may have contacted electrical components, keep the affected circuit or fan off until a qualified professional confirms it is safe.
