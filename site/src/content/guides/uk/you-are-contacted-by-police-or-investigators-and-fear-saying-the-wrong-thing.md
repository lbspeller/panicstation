# What to do if… you are contacted by police or investigators and fear saying the wrong thing (UK)

## Short answer
Stop answering questions about the allegation and ask for a criminal defence solicitor before any interview or written account. UK police processes vary by nation, but asking for legal advice before speaking about the allegation is the safest first step.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t “just explain” informally on the phone, by text, email, or in person to “clear it up”.
- Don’t attend an interview, even a “voluntary” one, without arranging legal advice first.
- Don’t guess dates, fill gaps, or agree to summaries you have not read and understood.
- Don’t agree to provide your phone, accounts, passwords, documents, or downloads without asking what legal power is being used and getting legal advice, unless a lawful requirement is clearly explained.
- Don’t contact witnesses, complainants, colleagues, or other people involved to compare stories or “sort it out”.
- Don’t delete messages, files, call logs, social posts, browser history, or account content.
- Don’t sign any statement, written account, interview note, or consent form without legal advice.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the conversation, politely.** Use one line and repeat it if needed: “I want legal advice before I answer any questions about this.”
2. **Clarify what they are asking you to do, without discussing the allegation.** Ask: “Am I under arrest?”, “Is this a voluntary interview?”, “Is this an interview under caution?”, “What offence or allegation is being investigated?”, “When and where do you want me to attend?”
3. **Ask for a solicitor immediately.** If you are at or going to a police station, ask police to contact a solicitor, the duty solicitor, or the local equivalent. If you are being invited to attend, say you will arrange the time through a solicitor or after legal advice is booked.
4. **If questioning starts, stop it clearly.** Say: “I am not answering questions about the allegation until I have spoken privately with a solicitor.” Do not try to explain parts of the story while waiting.
5. **If you are unwell, panicking, unable to understand, or struggling to communicate, say so plainly.** Ask for a break, medical help if needed, an interpreter if you need one, communication support if you need it, and an appropriate adult if you are under 18 or may need one because of vulnerability.
6. **Write down only the contact details and logistics for your solicitor.** Note who contacted you, the force or department they gave, date and time, reference numbers, what interview they want, where it is meant to happen, and any deadline mentioned.
7. **Keep existing material safe and unchanged.** Do not delete, edit, forward, or organise messages and files to make your own case. Keep them as they are and ask your solicitor what to do next.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to give a full account, answer “no comment”, provide a prepared statement, or hand over material.
- You do not need to build a detailed timeline, contact other people, gather evidence from others, or investigate the allegation yourself.
- You do not need to argue about fairness, motives, or “why me” with the officer or investigator.
- You do not need to choose a long-term legal strategy in this first moment.

## Important reassurance
Feeling scared about saying the wrong thing is a normal reaction to being told you are under investigation. Asking for a solicitor is a standard safeguard, not an admission. Your job right now is to slow the situation down, keep the facts unchanged, and avoid rushed words or rushed consent.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and help you avoid irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about interview answers, written statements, evidence, devices, bail, court, work, immigration, or professional regulation may need advice from a solicitor or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Law and police processes vary by circumstances and by UK nation. If you are unsure what you are being asked to do, or you feel pressured, stop discussing the allegation and ask for legal advice.
