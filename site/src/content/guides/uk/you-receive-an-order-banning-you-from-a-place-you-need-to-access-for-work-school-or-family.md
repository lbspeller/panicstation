# What to do if… you receive an order banning you from a place you need to access for work, school, or family (UK)

## Short answer
Do not enter the restricted area until you have the specific terms in writing and a formal variation or permission is granted. Immediately request a written copy of the order from the issuer and contact a solicitor to apply for a variation if the ban prevents essential access to work, education, or childcare.

## Do not do these things
- Do not enter the restricted area for any reason, including brief visits to explain your situation or collect items.
- Do not rely on verbal permission from staff, managers, or friends at the location.
- Do not contact or use third parties to contact anyone protected by the order to negotiate access.
- Do not ignore the order even if you believe it was issued in error or is based on false information.
- Do not delete any official communications, emails, or text messages regarding the restriction.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific authority that issued the restriction.**
   - Check if the document was issued by a Court (Restraining Order, Injunction, Criminal Behaviour Order), the Police (Bail Conditions), or a private entity (Employer, School, or Venue).
   - Note the case reference number, the name of the issuing officer or judge, and the date the order expires.

2. **Obtain the full written wording and any attached maps.**
   - **Request a formal copy from the issuing body immediately if you only have a verbal notice or a photo.**
   - Determine the exact boundaries (e.g., "within 100 metres of the site" or "the building marked in red").
   - Assume the strictest possible interpretation of the boundary until you have legal clarification.

3. **Contact a solicitor to apply for a variation of a court order.**
   - **Inform the solicitor if the ban prevents you from attending work, taking exams, or meeting childcare obligations.**
   - Ask about applying to "vary or discharge" the order under the Protection from Harassment Act 1997 or the Sentencing Act 2020.
   - Note that under 2026 rules, courts must prioritize victim safety but can consider "proportionate" access for essential needs if risks can be managed.

4. **Request a variation from the Police if you are on pre-charge bail.**
   - **Write to the Officer in the Case (OIC) or the Custody Sergeant to request a formal variation for specific, time-limited access.**
   - Be aware that the police have a 2026 statutory duty to consult any alleged victims before agreeing to vary conditions.
   - If the police refuse, your solicitor can apply to a Magistrates' Court for a "reconsideration of bail," which the court should target to list within 5 business days.

5. **Formalize an interim plan for workplace or school access.**
   - **Notify HR or the school administration in writing that you have a restriction and are seeking to comply.**
   - Propose remote work, alternative campuses, or online learning as temporary measures.
   - If the exclusion is part of an employment dispute, reference the Fair Work Agency (operational as of April 2026) if you believe your statutory rights to work are being breached without due process.

6. **Arrange a controlled handover for essential items.**
   - **Request a specific time and neutral location for a third party or security officer to hand over medication, keys, or passports.**
   - Do not attend the handover yourself if it involves entering the exclusion zone or meeting a protected person.
   - Ask for "escorted access" only if the police or the court explicitly authorize it in writing.

7. **Maintain a contemporaneous compliance log.**
   - **Record every attempt you make to clarify the order or request access through official channels.**
   - Save all timestamps of emails sent to solicitors, the police, and employers.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove the underlying allegations are false today; focus only on stabilizing access and avoiding a breach.
- You do not need to provide a full defense statement or collect witness character references in the next few hours.
- You do not need to contact the people at the restricted location to apologize or explain your side of the story.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a sudden exclusion is a high-stress event that can feel like it is ending your career or education. This restriction is a legal status, not a final judgment on your character. By choosing to stay away and using formal legal channels to vary the order, you are protecting your future position and preventing an arrest for a breach.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only to prevent immediate arrest for breach of an order. It does not cover long-term legal defense or the merits of any underlying criminal or civil case.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and is not a substitute for legal advice. Breaching a court order or police bail is a criminal offense that can lead to immediate arrest and imprisonment. Under the Sentencing Act 2026, there is a presumption that sentences under 12 months will be suspended, but this does not apply to breaches of court orders.
