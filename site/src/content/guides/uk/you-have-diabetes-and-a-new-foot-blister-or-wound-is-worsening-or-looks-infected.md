# What to do if… you have diabetes and a new foot blister or wound is worsening or looks infected (UK)

## Short answer
Seek a medical assessment within **24 hours** by contacting your GP, podiatry team, or NHS 111. If you have signs of spreading infection, blue/black skin, or symptoms of sepsis (confusion, slurred speech, or mottled skin), **call 999 or go to A&E immediately**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait longer than 24 hours to speak to a clinician, even if the wound is painless.
- Do not burst, cut, or debride the blister or wound yourself.
- Do not soak the foot in water, salt baths, or foot spas as this softens and damages the skin further.
- Do not apply antiseptic creams, iodine, or home remedies unless specifically prescribed for this wound.
- Do not apply adhesive tape directly to the skin around the wound.
- Do not use hot water bottles or heating pads on the foot to "soothe" pain.
- Do not walk on the affected foot or wear the shoe that caused the injury.

## What to do now
1. **Remove all weight from the foot immediately.** Sit or lie down and remove your footwear and socks gently to inspect the area. 
   - Use a mirror to see the bottom of the foot if necessary.
   - Do not walk on the foot until it has been assessed by a professional.
2. **Conduct an immediate sensory check for emergency triggers.** Call 999 or go to A&E if you observe:
   - Skin that is turning blue, grey, or black (necrosis).
   - Redness or discolouration spreading rapidly up the foot or leg.
   - On brown or black skin, look for areas that are darker than surrounding skin, purple, or feel unusually hard/tight.
   - Systemic symptoms: slurred speech, confusion, extreme shivering, or mottled (blotchy) skin.
3. **Clean and protect the area without soaking.** If there is visible debris, gently rinse the area with clean, lukewarm running water.
   - Pat the skin dry with a clean towel; do not rub.
   - Cover the area with a sterile, non-adherent (non-sticky) dressing.
   - Secure the dressing with light bandage wrap rather than tape on the skin.
4. **Contact your specialist or urgent care pathway.** Be explicit about your diabetes diagnosis.
   - Call your **Multi-Disciplinary Foot Team (MDFT)** or podiatry clinic if you have their direct number.
   - Call your **GP surgery** and state: "I have diabetes and an active foot problem that looks infected; I require a referral or assessment within 24 hours."
   - Call **NHS 111** if it is out of hours or your GP is unavailable.
5. **Document the current state of the wound.** Take a clear photo of the wound next to a coin for scale.
   - Note the time you first noticed the change.
   - Record your current body temperature if a thermometer is available.
   - Check and record your blood glucose levels, as infection often causes unexpected spikes.
6. **Follow diabetes "sick day" protocols.** Increase the frequency of glucose monitoring while waiting for your appointment.
   - Stay hydrated with water.
   - Do not stop taking your insulin or diabetes medication unless instructed by a doctor.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific bacteria or the exact "grade" of the ulcer yourself.
- You do not need to purchase specialised orthopaedic footwear before your assessment.
- You do not need to decide on long-term treatment plans or surgical options today.
- You do not need to provide a life-long history of your HbA1c levels; your current glucose and medication list are the priority.

## Important reassurance
It is physiologically normal to feel a sense of urgency or hesitation when a new wound appears. Because diabetes can mask pain through neuropathy, your visual observation of redness, swelling, or heat is a reliable and sufficient reason to seek urgent help. Acting within the first 24 hours is the standard clinical recommendation to prevent complications.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute foot change. It is not a substitute for clinical debridement, antibiotic prescribing, or vascular assessment provided by the NHS.

## Important note
Diabetic foot infections can progress rapidly due to reduced circulation and nerve damage. If you cannot reach your GP or podiatrist, NHS 111 is the primary route for urgent triage. Do not ignore a wound simply because it does not hurt.
