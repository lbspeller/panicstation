# What to do if… you are copied into an internal email about you that suggests misconduct or dismissal (UK)

## Short answer
Do not enter an isolated or private location alone if you feel at risk. Formally propose moving the meeting to a visible area, a room with a glass wall, or a remote video call.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to an isolated location (such as a locked room, a car, or an offsite venue) to avoid "making a scene" if you feel unsafe.
- Do not leave your mobile phone, bag, or keys behind; keep your means of communication and exit on your person.
- Do not allow yourself to be rushed into a private space before you have set a boundary in writing.
- Do not agree to any documents or sign any statements while feeling physically or psychologically pressured.
- Do not delete any emails, calendar invites, or messages that show the meeting request or the location specified.
- Do not rely solely on secret recording as a safety plan, as this may breach workplace policies; focus on physical safety first.

## What to do now
1. **Create an immediate physical pause.** If you are being approached right now, stay in a populated area (such as an open-plan office or a reception area) and state that you need a moment to finish a task or collect your notebook before the meeting begins.
2. **State your safety boundary clearly and calmly.** Use a neutral statement: "I am not comfortable meeting in that private location. I am happy to have this discussion in [Public Meeting Room] or via a Teams/Zoom call."
3. **Send a follow-up message to confirm your boundary.** Immediately reply to the calendar invitation or send an email/message to the requester stating: "As discussed, I am available to meet at [Time] in [Visible Room] or via video link. I am not able to meet in [Private/Isolated Location]."
4. **Identify the nature of the meeting.** Ask for the purpose of the meeting in writing, specifically whether it is an informal catch-up or a formal meeting (such as a disciplinary investigation or grievance hearing) that could lead to formal action.
5. **Request a witness or companion be present.** If the meeting is a formal disciplinary or grievance hearing, you have a statutory right under the Employment Relations Act 1999 to be accompanied by a colleague or union representative.
   - For informal or investigation meetings, state that you would prefer a second manager or an HR representative to attend for transparency.
6. **Escalate to a neutral authority if the pressure continues.** If your manager persists in demanding a private meeting, contact Human Resources, a more senior manager, or your trade union representative immediately. State: "I have expressed safety concerns regarding the meeting location and these have not been addressed."
7. **Consult the Fair Work Agency or ACAS for guidance.** If your employer ignores your safety concerns or threatens you with "insubordination" for refusing an unsafe meeting, contact the ACAS helpline (0300 123 1100) or reference the Fair Work Agency enforcement rules regarding workplace rights.
8. **Document the incident while the details are fresh.** Record the date, time, specific location requested, exact words used by the requester, and any witnesses who saw you being pressured. Keep this record in a secure, personal location.
9. **Exit the building if you feel in immediate physical danger.** Move to a public space or leave the premises if you believe you are at risk of assault or harassment. Call **999** for emergencies or **101** for non-emergency police assistance.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you want to resign or file a formal legal claim.
- You do not need to justify or explain the details of your safety concerns to the person pressuring you; "I am not comfortable meeting privately" is a sufficient professional boundary.
- You do not need to have a perfect "case" ready; preserving the messages and your brief record is enough for the first few hours.

## Important reassurance
It is a normal and proportionate response to refuse a meeting in an isolated location if you feel unsafe. Workplace safety laws, including the Health and Safety at Work Act and the Worker Protection Act (2026 updates), require your employer to maintain a safe environment and take all reasonable steps to prevent harassment.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a workplace safety crisis. It does not cover long-term legal strategies, tribunal procedures, or detailed trade union negotiations which should be handled with a professional advisor once you are safe.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment laws and internal company policies vary; always check your specific employment contract and staff handbook. If you are a member of a trade union, contact them as soon as possible for specific representation and support.
