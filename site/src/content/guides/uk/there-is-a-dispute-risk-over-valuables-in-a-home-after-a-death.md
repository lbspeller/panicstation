# What to do if… there is a dispute risk over valuables in a home after a death (UK)

## Short answer
Pause all removals and focus on safeguarding: secure the property, notify the insurer of the unoccupancy, and create a documented photo inventory to prevent later allegations of theft or loss.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t remove any items "for safekeeping" without the presence of another party or written agreement from all beneficiaries.
- Don’t start dividing possessions, gifting items, or promising specific heirlooms until the legal executor or administrator is formally confirmed.
- Don’t change the locks unilaterally if you are not the named executor; if there are joint executors, they must usually agree on security changes together.
- Don’t discard, sell, or donate anything—even items that appear to be "rubbish"—as they may have sentimental or hidden financial value that becomes a point of legal contention.
- Don’t engage in room-by-room arguments; if tensions escalate, leave the property and continue the conversation in writing or via a third party.

## What to do now
1. **Declare a temporary "freeze" on all property removals.** **State clearly to all parties that nothing is to leave the house until a baseline inventory is completed and the legal representative confirms the process.**
   - Use a neutral, firm phrase: "We need to record everything for the estate's tax and probate records before anything is moved."
2. **Identify the person with current legal authority.** **Check the most recent will to identify the named executor(s) or determine if the estate is "intestate" (no will).**
   - In the UK, an executor’s authority begins at the moment of death, but an administrator (where there is no will) only gains formal authority once "Letters of Administration" are granted.
   - If there are multiple executors, remind them that they have a joint duty to safeguard assets and must act together.
3. **Secure the property and contact the insurer immediately.** **Call the deceased's home insurance provider to inform them of the death and ask specifically about "unoccupied property" conditions.**
   - Most UK policies become restricted or void if a house is empty for more than 30 or 60 days.
   - Ask for a list of security requirements (e.g., turning off water, specific lock types, or weekly inspections) and document who will carry these out.
4. **Create a "baseline" photographic record.** **Walk through every room and take timestamped photos or video of the contents, including inside drawers, wardrobes, and safes.**
   - Do this with at least one witness present if possible.
   - Focus on high-value areas: jewellery boxes, electronics, antique furniture, and any stashes of cash or documents.
5. **Implement a "buddy system" for property access.** **Agree that no one enters the home alone until the estate is settled.**
   - Keep a simple logbook at the front door or in a digital document noting the date, time, person entering, and the reason for the visit.
6. **Move high-risk items to a secure, documented location if necessary.** **If items like cash or high-value jewellery are visible and the house is insecure, place them in a sealed container or safe with two people present.**
   - Create a signed and dated list of what was moved, why it was moved, and exactly where it is now stored. 
   - Send this list to all executors and beneficiaries immediately to maintain transparency.
7. **Consult a professional if the dispute involves threats or physical blockades.** **If you are being physically prevented from fulfilling executor duties or if items are actively being stolen, contact a solicitor specializing in "contentious probate."**
   - If there is an immediate risk of violence or a crime is currently in progress, call **999**.
   - For non-emergency reports of suspected theft that has already occurred, call **101**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on the final distribution of sentimental items today.
- You do not need to value the entire estate for tax purposes (Inheritance Tax) in the first 48 hours.
- You do not need to clear the house or arrange for a professional house clearance immediately.
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family grievances or "who deserves what" arguments right now.

## Important reassurance
High emotions and suspicion are very common after a death, even in families that usually get along. Implementing strict, transparent recording and security measures is not an accusation of "distrust"—it is a standard procedure that protects everyone from future legal claims or misunderstandings.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization to prevent the physical loss of assets and the escalation of family conflict. It does not cover the formal probate application process, tax reporting, or the legal resolution of a contested will.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Estate law in the UK is complex; if there is a serious dispute, you should consult a qualified probate solicitor. Be aware that executors can be held personally and financially liable for failing to protect estate assets.
