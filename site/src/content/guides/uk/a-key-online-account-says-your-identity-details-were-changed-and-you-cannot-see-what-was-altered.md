# What to do if… a key online account says your identity details were changed and you cannot see what was altered (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible account takeover until you prove otherwise. Do not use links in the alert; go to the official app or type the website address yourself, secure your email account first, then lock down the affected account.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click “review changes” links in the alert email or text unless you reached the site or app yourself.
- Don’t keep trying passwords repeatedly if you are unsure; this can trigger lockouts while someone else may still be active.
- Don’t assume it is only this one account until you have checked the email account that controls password resets.
- Don’t rely on SMS-only codes where the account offers a stronger option such as an app, biometric check, security key, or other supported 2-step verification method.
- Don’t delete the alert message yet; keep the timestamp and wording in case support asks for it.
- Don’t send identity documents through a chat, email address, or form unless you are sure it is the provider’s official recovery route.

## What to do now
1. Use a device you trust and open the service safely.  
   Use the official app or type the website address yourself. If the device is behaving strangely, use another trusted device before signing in.

2. Secure your email account first.  
   Your email is often the route used to reset other accounts. Change the email password to a new, unique password. Turn on 2-step verification. In the email settings, check recent activity, forwarding, filter rules, recovery email, recovery phone, and connected apps. Remove anything you did not set. Sign out of other email sessions or devices.

3. Lock down the affected account.  
   In the affected account’s security, login, privacy, or account settings, change the password to a new, unique one. Sign out of all devices or sessions. Remove unknown devices, browsers, active sessions, and unfamiliar connected apps.

4. Rebuild the recovery routes.  
   Check the recovery email, recovery phone, backup codes, trusted devices, 2-step verification method, and recovery key if the service uses one. Replace anything you do not fully control. Reissue backup codes if you did not create them.

5. Check the identity details one page at a time.  
   Look at profile, personal details, account information, addresses, security, payment, verification, and contact preferences. Specifically check name, date of birth, address, username, recovery email, recovery phone, two-step verification method, contact email, linked accounts, and saved payment methods.

6. If you cannot get in, use the provider’s official compromised-account recovery route.  
   Tell them the approximate time of the alert, that it said identity details were changed, and that you cannot see what changed. Ask whether they can lock the account, review recent changes, revert unauthorised profile changes, and tell you which fields were altered.

7. If payment methods, money, or shopping accounts are involved, check them now.  
   Remove stored cards or payment methods if you can do this safely. Check your bank or card app for pending transactions. If you see unauthorised transactions, contact your bank or card provider using the number on your card, the official app, or the provider’s official website.

8. If you have lost money, data, or account access, report it through the UK route for where you live.  
   If you live in England, Wales or Northern Ireland, report cyber crime and fraud through Report Fraud online or by phone. If you live in Scotland, report fraud to Police Scotland by calling 101.

9. If inaccurate personal details remain on the account, ask the organisation to correct them.  
   You can ask for inaccurate personal data to be rectified. Make the request through the provider’s official support route, keep a copy, and say which details are wrong. If the organisation does not resolve it, you can use the ICO data protection complaint route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly how this happened right now.
- You do not need to change every password you have ever used; focus on your email, this account, and any account sharing the same password.
- You do not need to decide today whether to close the account permanently.
- You do not need to contact everyone yet unless messages, posts, payments, or suspicious activity came from the account.

## Important reassurance
This kind of alert is unsettling, especially when it does not say what changed. A careful sequence usually helps: secure the email account, remove other sessions, replace recovery details, then check the identity fields without guessing.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for stabilising the situation. If the account is financial, work-related, used for official identity checks, or linked to a wider data breach, later decisions may need the provider’s specialist support or another appropriate professional route.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and practical first steps only. It is not legal, financial, technical, cyber-security, or professional advice. Account labels and recovery processes vary by provider, so use the provider’s official recovery pages and support channels for account-specific instructions.
