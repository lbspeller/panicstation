# What to do if… you are told your paid sick leave will be stopped because paperwork is insufficient (UK)

## Short answer
Preserve visual evidence of the change immediately and notify your manager and payroll in writing to request a formal explanation and correction.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "change it back" within the system, as this can create a confusing audit trail that suggests you also tampered with records.
- Do not delete your own personal records, rotas, or original submission confirmation emails.
- Do not accuse specific individuals of fraud or theft in public forums or group chats while the investigation is ongoing.
- Do not sign or "finalise" any pay period documents if you know the underlying hours are incorrect.
- Do not wait until your next pay date to raise the issue if the discrepancy is already visible in the system.

## What to do now
1. **Capture visual evidence of the current state of the record before any further edits occur.**
   - Take screenshots of the modified entries, including any "last edited by" stamps or system history notes.
   - Download or print a copy of the timesheet as it appears now.
   - Locate and save your original submission confirmation or any photo you took of the paper timesheet.

2. **Send a factual written notification to your manager and the payroll department today.**
   - State the specific dates and hours that have been altered since your submission.
   - Attach or reference your evidence of the original hours worked (e.g., rota, swipe card logs, or GPS data).
   - Ask for a written explanation for the adjustment and a timeline for the correction.

3. **Request a copy of the electronic audit trail or system log for the affected entries.**
   - Ask specifically for the metadata showing the timestamp of the change and the user ID associated with the edit.
   - If the employer refuses, remind them of your Right of Access under the UK GDPR and the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025.

4. **Verify if the change has already impacted your upcoming or previous payslip.**
   - Compare the "hours worked" on your most recent payslip against your own records.
   - Calculate the exact gross shortfall in pay resulting from the unauthorised change.

5. **Maintain a private, contemporaneous log of all communications regarding this dispute.**
   - Record the date, time, and summary of any verbal conversations.
   - Save all email replies from HR or management in a folder outside of the company’s internal server if possible.

6. **Initiate an informal or formal grievance if the response is unsatisfactory or delayed.**
   - Follow your company's written grievance procedure exactly.
   - If you are a member of a trade union, contact your representative for support during any meetings.

7. **Escalate the matter to Acas if the internal process fails to resolve the pay discrepancy.**
   - Contact Acas for "Early Conciliation" if you believe an unlawful deduction from wages has occurred.
   - Note that for most pay claims, you must start the process within 3 months minus 1 day of the date the payment was due.

8. **Report suspected systemic pay record tampering to the Fair Work Agency (FWA).**
   - As of April 2026, the FWA oversees enforcement for holiday pay and record-keeping breaches.
   - Use this route if the employer refuses to provide records or if multiple staff members are affected.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to resign or take legal action in the first 24 hours.
- You do not need to have a perfect legal argument prepared; a simple factual statement of the error is sufficient for the first step.
- You can wait for the first formal response from payroll before involving external legal counsel.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of violation or panic when your work records are altered without your consent. In many cases, these changes are administrative errors or misunderstandings of break policies that can be resolved through a clear, written paper trail. By documenting the change now, you are taking the most effective step toward protecting your income and your professional reputation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for individual timesheet disputes in the UK. It does not cover collective bargaining negotiations or complex tax status disputes for self-employed contractors.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment laws, including the 2026 enforcement powers of the Fair Work Agency, are subject to specific eligibility criteria. If you are facing disciplinary action related to timesheet discrepancies or if your income is significantly withheld, seek advice from Acas or a qualified employment law solicitor immediately.
