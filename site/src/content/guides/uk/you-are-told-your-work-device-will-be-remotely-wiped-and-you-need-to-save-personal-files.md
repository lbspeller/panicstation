# What to do if… you are told your work device will be remotely wiped and you need to save personal files (UK)

## Short answer
Contact IT or the service desk immediately and ask for a short hold or supervised export window for clearly personal items only. Do not try to bypass security controls, block the wipe, or copy mixed work and personal material in a rush.

## Do not do these things
- Do not copy work files, confidential material, customer data, source code, client information, or internal messages “just in case”.
- Do not email files out, sync folders to personal cloud storage, or plug in a personal USB drive unless IT explicitly approves it.
- Do not disable device management, remove a management profile, tamper with security tools, or try to stop the wipe yourself.
- Do not wipe, reset, or factory-reset the device yourself.
- Do not delete logs, chats, emails, tickets, or access records to “tidy up”.
- Do not assume you can recover local items after the wipe; recovery may be difficult or impossible.

## What to do now
1. **Record the wipe notice.** Note the device, asset number if visible, the stated deadline, who sent the notice, and whether it says full device wipe, work-profile wipe, account removal, or managed-data wipe. Take a photo or screenshot of the notice if you can do that without breaching policy.

2. **Make a narrow list of personal items.** Write down only the personal items you are trying to save, such as personal photos, personal contacts stored locally, personal documents you created for yourself, personal certificates, or personal browser bookmarks. Leave out anything that could be work product or confidential business information.

3. **Ask IT for a compliant export route.** Call or message the service desk, then follow up in writing. Ask for a short hold or supervised export window, and ask what route they permit, such as an IT-approved transfer, an encrypted device they provide, or an export they perform for you.

4. **Use precise wording in the request.** Say that you are not asking to retain work data and that you want IT or HR to supervise or approve the export. Include the device details, the wipe deadline, and the exact personal folders, apps, or records you want checked.

5. **Secure personal accounts from another trusted device.** Where possible, use your own phone or computer to change passwords for any personal accounts used on the work device. Remove the work device from trusted-device lists, active sessions, password managers, authenticator apps, cloud storage, email accounts, and browser sync accounts.

6. **Protect two-factor access before the wipe happens.** If the work device receives codes or holds an authenticator app for personal accounts, add another trusted method, save backup codes, or move the authenticator from a personal device before access is lost.

7. **Download employment records from official systems where available.** If you still have access, use HR, payroll, pension, benefits, or learning portals rather than local device folders. Save only your own records, such as payslips, P60 or P45 where available, contract or offer letter, pension information, benefits documents, training certificates, and formal review documents.

8. **If IT refuses, ask where to send a personal-data request.** You can ask for a copy of your personal data that the organisation holds, often called a subject access request or SAR. Be specific about your name, employee number, date range, systems, and the records you want. Organisations usually have one month to respond to a SAR, though some requests can take longer. A SAR is not a device-recovery tool and may not bring back personal files stored only locally on a wiped device.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to raise a grievance, appeal a decision, resign, or take legal action.
- You do not need to explain your whole employment history.
- You do not need to organise every file perfectly.
- You do not need to argue about policy while the wipe deadline is approaching; first focus on a narrow, written, policy-compliant request for the personal items that matter most.

## Important reassurance
A small amount of personal material on a work device is a common problem. A calm request for a supervised, policy-compliant export of clearly personal items is a reasonable first step, even if the employer still has to protect its systems and data.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: they are meant to reduce the chance of irreversible data loss and avoid actions that could make the work situation worse. Later decisions about grievances, dismissal, disciplinary allegations, data protection complaints, or legal claims may need specialist employment, union, legal, or data protection advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, employment, cyber-security, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Workplace device rules vary by employer, contract, policy, and the reason for the wipe. Even if your intention is only to recover personal files, bypassing controls or copying mixed data can be treated as misconduct, so keep your request narrow, written, and routed through IT or HR.
