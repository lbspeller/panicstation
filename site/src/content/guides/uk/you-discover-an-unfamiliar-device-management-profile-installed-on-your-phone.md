# What to do if… you discover an unfamiliar “device management” profile installed on your phone (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the device by enabling Airplane Mode, capture screenshots of the profile name and listed permissions, and remove the profile immediately unless you can verify it was installed by a known employer or school.

## Do not do these things
- Don't enter passwords, bank details, or two-factor codes on the device until the profile is removed.
- Don't follow instructions from a pop-up or text message telling you to install a "security patch" or "certificate" to fix the issue.
- Don't factory reset the device immediately if you have not backed up personal photos or files, as removal of the profile is often possible without a full wipe.
- Don't ignore the profile if you bought the phone second-hand; "Remote Management" locks can reappear even after a reset if not cleared by the original owner.
- Don't trust "IT support" callers who ask you to install software or profiles to "verify" your identity.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the device from the network**
   - **Enable Airplane Mode** and manually ensure **Wi-Fi and Bluetooth are toggled off** to stop remote commands or data syncing.
   - If the phone belongs to an employer, use a different device to contact their IT department before taking further action.

2. **Document the management details**
   - **Take screenshots** of the profile name, the "Organisation" listed, and the "More Details" or "Rights" section which shows what the profile can control (e.g., "Erase all data," "Monitor network traffic").
   - **Note the date** you first noticed the profile or when you last installed a new app or clicked a suspicious link.

3. **Locate and inspect the profile on iPhone or iPad**
   - **Go to Settings > General > VPN & Device Management.**
   - **Check for "Configuration Profiles" or "Mobile Device Management"** entries.
   - **Tap on any unrecognised entry** to see if it allows for removal; if it says "This iPhone is supervised and managed," the profile may be locked by an organisation.

4. **Locate and inspect the profile or admin apps on Android**
   - **Check Settings > Accounts (or Users & Accounts)** for a "Work" tab or a briefcase icon which indicates a Work Profile.
   - **Check Settings > Security > Device Admin Apps** (or "Device Administrators") to see if any unknown apps have permission to lock or wipe the phone.

5. **Remove the unauthorized profile or account**
   - **On iPhone:** Tap the profile in VPN & Device Management and select **Remove Profile** or **Remove Management**, then restart the device.
   - **On Android:** Go to **Settings > Google > Set up & restore > Set up your work profile** to find removal options, or delete the suspicious account from **Settings > Accounts**.
   - **Restart the phone** once the removal is confirmed.

6. **Reset compromised account credentials**
   - From a **separate, known-safe computer or tablet**, change the passwords for your Apple ID or Google Account, primary email, and banking apps.
   - **Check for unrecognised "Trusted Devices"** in your Apple or Google security settings and remove them.

7. **Verify SIM and network security**
   - **Call your mobile provider** (e.g., EE, O2, Vodafone, Three) using the number from an official bill to check for recent unauthorized "SIM swap" requests or redirects.

8. **Report the incident to UK authorities**
   - If you have lost money or believe you are a victim of a cybercrime in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, contact **Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) at 0300 123 2040 or via reportfraud.police.uk.
   - If you are in Scotland, report the incident to **Police Scotland via 101**.
   - Forward any scam texts that led to this installation to **7726** and emails to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to replace the physical phone hardware unless a specialist confirms a persistent firmware-level compromise.
- You do not need to notify all contacts immediately unless you have evidence that messages were sent from your accounts without your knowledge.
- You do not need to buy "anti-virus" software for the phone, as removing the profile and admin permissions usually terminates the remote access.

## Important reassurance
Finding a management profile often means a scammer attempted to gain access, but it does not mean they have successfully stolen your entire identity. Most configuration profiles are designed to be removable by the user. Taking the phone offline and removing the profile stops the active management immediately.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for personal devices. It does not cover legal recovery of corporate-locked (DEP/ABM) devices purchased second-hand, which may require proof of purchase to be cleared by the manufacturer.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional IT security analysis. If your device is owned by an employer, removing management profiles may violate your employment contract or result in the loss of work-related data. Always verify the source of a profile before deletion if the device is used for official school or business purposes.
