# What to do if… you need to tell a child that someone close has died and you are in shock (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself into a safe, still pause and then tell the child simply and directly: "[Name] has died." Use clear, honest language and avoid euphemisms like "lost" or "asleep" which cause confusion.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use vague phrases like “went to sleep”, “passed away”, “lost”, or “gone” as young children take these literally.
- Do not invent stories or offer uncertain details to fill the silence.
- Do not deliver the news in a rushed or public setting if a private, familiar space is available.
- Do not promise outcomes you cannot guarantee, such as "nothing will change" or "we will never be sad again."
- Do not leave the child alone to overhear adult conversations containing graphic details or speculation.
- Do not wait for a "perfect moment" or for your own shock to disappear; clarity is more important than perfection.

## What to do now
1. **Get a support adult in place immediately if possible.** Ask a trusted friend or family member to be physically present or on a live phone call so you are not handling the child’s reaction while in acute shock.
2. **Choose the simplest, most familiar setting available.** Sit at the child’s eye level in a quiet room and ensure the TV and all notifications are silenced.
3. **Deliver the news in one clear, short sentence.** 
   - Say: “I have some very sad news. **[Name] has died.**” 
   Stop speaking after this sentence to allow the child time to hear and process the words.
4. **Provide a basic biological explanation of death if the child is young.** 
   - Explain: “Died means **their body has stopped working** and **they cannot breathe, eat, or talk anymore**.” 
   Indicate that the person can no longer feel pain, cold, or hunger.
5. **State the two most critical reassurances clearly.** 
   - Tell them: “**This is not your fault**” and “**You are safe right now, and I am here with you.**” 
   Repeat these as often as the child needs to hear them.
6. **Respond to questions with simple, truthful facts.** If you do not know the answer, say “I do not know yet, but I will tell you when I do.” Avoid graphic details unless they are necessary for the child to understand the cause of death.
7. **Switch to medical safety mode if you feel physically compromised.** If you experience chest pain, severe dizziness, or feel you may faint, sit on the floor, sip water, and call **NHS 111**. In a life-threatening emergency, call **999** or **112**.
8. **Contact a specialist bereavement helpline for immediate guidance.** 
   - Call **Winston’s Wish** on 08088 020 021 (8am–8pm weekdays) or the **Cruse Bereavement Support** helpline on 0808 808 1677.
9. **Notify the child’s school or nursery via a single message.** Inform the headteacher or pastoral lead that a death has occurred and refer to the 2026 statutory RSHE guidance regarding bereavement support. Ask them to monitor the child and provide a "safe space" if they attend school tomorrow.
10. **Establish a tiny, concrete plan for the next hour.** 
   - Decide who is staying in the house, what the next meal will be, and whether a comforting activity like quiet music or a familiar book is appropriate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain funeral arrangements or spiritual beliefs right now.
- You do not need to provide an exhaustive timeline of events or medical causes.
- You do not need to decide if the child will attend the funeral today.
- You do not need to manage or "fix" the child's emotional response.
- You do not need to notify the wider community or distant relatives immediately.

## Important reassurance
Being in shock can make you feel disconnected, "robotic," or intensely emotional. These are normal biological responses to crisis. Your child does not need you to be perfectly calm; they need you to be honest and present. It is common for children to ask the same question repeatedly as they process the permanence of death.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only for the initial notification and the immediate hours following a bereavement. Long-term grief support for children requires ongoing communication and potentially professional intervention.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and is not a substitute for professional medical, legal, or psychological advice. If you or the child are in immediate danger or at risk of harm, call 999 or 112 immediately.
