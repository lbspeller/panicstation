# What to do if… your laptop is stolen and it contains sensitive personal documents (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your mobile network provider immediately using an official number from a different phone to report the unauthorized change and block any pending number transfers or SIM replacements.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use the phone number in any suspicious alert text or email to call your provider; only use the number found on their official website or an old bill.
- Don’t share any "One Time Passwords" (OTPs) or Porting Authorisation Codes (PACs) with anyone who calls you, even if they claim to be from your network.
- Don’t attempt multiple PIN resets yourself, as this can trigger a security lockout that prevents the legitimate support team from helping you quickly.
- Don’t assume the issue is a temporary network glitch if you suddenly lose service after receiving a PIN change alert.
- Don’t ignore the notification even if you still have signal; a PIN change is often the first step in a "SIM swap" or "number porting" attack.

## What to do now
1. **Find a separate, trusted device to make calls (1 minute).** Use a landline or a different person's mobile phone to contact your provider so you are not reliant on a SIM card that may be in the process of being deactivated.
2. **Call your mobile network's official customer service line immediately.** Dial 150 from a working phone on the same network, or find the specific 0333 or 0800 number on their official website.
   - Tell them: "My account PIN and security code have been changed without my permission."
   - State clearly: "I suspect an unauthorized SIM swap or number transfer."
3. **Request an immediate block on all porting and SIM activities.** Ask the agent to:
   - Block any outgoing "PAC" (Porting Authorisation Code) or "STAC" (Service Termination Authorisation Code) requests.
   - Cancel any pending SIM or eSIM activation requests.
   - Place a "fraud marker" or "security note" on the account requiring in-person ID verification for any future changes.
4. **Verify if a 9-digit PAC has already been issued.** Ask for the exact time the PIN was changed and if any codes were sent via SMS.
   - If a PAC was issued, ask for it to be voided immediately.
   - Note that PACs are valid for 30 days and are the key to moving your number to a different network.
5. **Secure your primary email and banking accounts from a clean computer.** Access your email via a browser and:
   - Change the password to something unique.
   - Check "forwarding rules" to ensure your emails aren't being sent to a third party.
   - If your bank uses SMS for logins, call them to alert them that your mobile number is compromised and ask for alternative verification.
6. **Report the incident to the national fraud reporting service.**
   - In **England, Wales, or Northern Ireland**, report to **Report Fraud** (the successor to Action Fraud) via reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.
   - In **Scotland**, report to **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
7. **Forward any scam or suspicious notification texts to 7726.** This is a free service that alerts mobile providers to malicious numbers and helps them block future attacks.
8. **Begin a formal complaint if the provider fails to secure the account (6-week timeline).** Keep a record of all call times and reference numbers.
   - If the issue is not resolved within 6 weeks (for complaints raised after April 8, 2026), you can escalate to the **Communications Ombudsman** or **CISAS**.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to change your actual mobile number yet; focus on securing the current account first.
- You don’t need to notify all your social media contacts immediately unless you see evidence of those accounts being accessed.
- You don’t need to perform a factory reset on your phone unless you suspect the compromise came from a malicious app rather than a carrier-level breach.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a PIN change notification is a high-priority alert, but acting within the first few minutes significantly reduces the chance of a successful number hijack. Most networks have dedicated fraud teams specifically trained to roll back these unauthorized changes and restore account security.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and account recovery steps. It does not provide long-term identity theft monitoring or legal advice regarding financial losses.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and reflects UK procedures and regulations as of March 2026. If you have lost money or believe your identity has been stolen, contact your bank and the police immediately through official channels.
