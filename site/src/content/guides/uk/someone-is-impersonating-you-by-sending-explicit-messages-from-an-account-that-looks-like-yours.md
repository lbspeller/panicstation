# What to do if… someone is impersonating you by sending explicit messages from an account that looks like yours (UK)

## Short answer
Secure your real accounts first, then report the impersonating account in the app and warn a few key people not to engage with it. If there is immediate danger, threats of violence, or you feel at risk of harm now, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply to the impersonator to reason with them, negotiate, or prove who you are.
- Do not send personal photos, ID documents, passwords, codes, money, or more information to the impersonator.
- Do not make a rushed public post with identifying details while you are shocked; it can spread the account or messages further.
- Do not delete or deactivate your real accounts in panic if you can secure them instead.
- Do not screenshot, download, forward, or store any intimate image if the person in it may be under 18.
- Do not assume this is your fault. Impersonation and sexual harassment tactics are designed to shock and destabilise.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Put the phone down for a minute, move away from anyone pressuring you, and use one trusted device if you can. If someone is threatening violence, is nearby, or you feel at immediate risk, call 999.

2. **Secure your real accounts.**
   - Change the password for your main email account first, because it can reset other accounts.
   - Change passwords on the social or messaging accounts being copied.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication.
   - Check for unknown devices, new login alerts, recovery email changes, and email forwarding rules.
   - Sign out of other sessions if the account settings allow it.

3. **Report the impersonating account on the platform.**
   - Use the platform’s impersonation, pretending to be me, harassment, or sexual content report route.
   - Report the profile and the specific messages if the platform allows both.
   - Ask two or three trusted contacts who received messages to report the account too, without replying to it.

4. **Send a short warning to key people.**
   - Send a plain message such as: “Someone is impersonating me and sending explicit messages. Please do not reply, click links, send money, or share anything. Please report the account and check with me directly if you are unsure.”
   - Send it only to people who need to know right now, such as close friends, family, a manager, HR, or a safeguarding lead.

5. **Save only a minimal safe record.**
   - Save the username, profile URL, display name, platform, date, time, and who received messages.
   - If there are no intimate images of someone who is or may be under 18, you can save a small number of screenshots showing the fake profile and non-intimate context.
   - If an intimate image may involve someone under 18, do not screenshot, download, forward, or store it. Record only the account details and report through the platform, police, CEOP, or Childline routes.

6. **Get specialist help if sexual images, threats, or blackmail are involved.**
   - If you are 18 or over and intimate images or manipulated sexual images are involved, contact the Revenge Porn Helpline for confidential support and takedown help.
   - If you are 18 or over, still have the intimate image or video, and are worried it may be shared, StopNCII may help participating platforms detect and remove copies without uploading the image itself.
   - If you are under 18, or the image may involve someone under 18, use CEOP or Childline Report Remove and involve a trusted adult if it is safe to do so.
   - If someone is blackmailing you for money, images, silence, or more contact, stop communication where you safely can and consider reporting to police.

7. **Consider reporting to UK authorities.**
   - For threats, stalking, harassment, intimate image abuse, or sextortion, contact police on 101, or 999 in an emergency.
   - If the incident is cyber crime or fraud, especially if money, login theft, or scam links are involved, Report Fraud is the reporting route for England, Wales, and Northern Ireland.
   - If you are in Scotland, report cyber crime or fraud to Police Scotland on 101, unless it is an emergency.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to go public, confront anyone, or prove your innocence to everyone.
- You do not need to read every message, gather a perfect evidence file, or work out who did it today.
- You do not need to decide today whether this is a police matter, a workplace matter, or a platform matter. Lockdown, reporting, containment, and support come first.

## Important reassurance
Feeling sick, shaky, furious, frozen, or ashamed can happen after sexualised impersonation. This situation is designed to hijack your attention and push you into rushed decisions. Slow, contained steps are enough for now: secure accounts, report the fake account, warn key people, and get support.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation, reduce spread, and buy time. Later decisions may need help from the platform, a specialist helpline, police, safeguarding staff, or another appropriate professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If you are under 18, use child-safeguarding routes and involve a trusted adult if it is safe to do so.
