# What to do if… water is leaking in around a window or door during a storm (UK)

## Short answer
Keep people away from wet electrics first, then contain the water from inside with towels, bowls, buckets, or plastic sheeting if you can do so safely. Do not go outside or climb up to repair the window, door, gutter, or roof while the storm is active.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t touch switches, sockets, extension leads, chargers, or appliances if water is reaching them or you are standing on a wet floor.
- Don’t go outside, lean out of a window, climb a ladder, or try to seal the outside of the frame during wind, darkness, lightning, or heavy rain.
- Don’t use a hairdryer, fan heater, wet vacuum, or mains-powered tool near the leak or wet flooring.
- Don’t stay in a room with a bulging ceiling, bulging wall, spreading cracks, or falling plaster; move everyone out of that room and call 999 if there is immediate danger.
- Don’t let children or pets play in storm or flood water, especially if it may have come from outside, drains, or the ground.
- Don’t switch power back on, plug items back in, or reuse wet appliances until you have had appropriate professional advice.

## What to do now
1. **Move people and pets away from the wet area.** Use a drier room if possible, and close internal doors to slow the spread of water.

2. **Make the electrics safer before handling the leak.**
   - If water is near sockets, extension leads, chargers, appliances, or the consumer unit, keep away from them.
   - Switch off electricity at the consumer unit only if you can reach it without stepping in water and the consumer unit area is dry.
   - Do not touch the consumer unit if it looks wet or damaged, or if there is buzzing, sparking, smoke, heat, or a burning smell.
   - In England, Wales or Scotland, call 105 if there is a power cut, electrical network emergency, or damage to outside electrical equipment.
   - In Northern Ireland, contact NIE Networks for power cuts or hazardous outside electrical equipment.
   - If anyone is in immediate danger, call 999.

3. **Catch and divert the water from inside.**
   - Put bowls, buckets, or trays where water is dripping or running.
   - Lay towels in a U shape to slow the spread across the floor.
   - If water is tracking around the edge of a window or door frame, press towels or cloths into the wet corner to slow it.
   - Tape a plastic bag or plastic sheet on the inside of the frame only if you can do it from a dry, safe position and away from sockets. Angle it so water runs into a container.

4. **Move the highest-risk items first.**
   - Move electronics, chargers, extension leads, power strips, and anything plugged in away from the water.
   - Move medicines, keys, important documents, and essential bags next.
   - Put items on a table, worktop, bed, or upstairs only if the route is dry and safe.

5. **Stop fabric from pulling water further in.**
   - Lift curtains, blinds, cushions, rugs, and trailing cables away from the wet sill, threshold, and floor.
   - If a carpet edge is soaking up water, press towels along the wet edge rather than rubbing or spreading the water.

6. **Treat outside water as not clean.**
   - Avoid splashing it into your eyes or mouth.
   - Wash hands after handling wet towels, buckets, or items.
   - Bag very wet, contaminated, or dirty soft items separately if they are safe to move.

7. **Take simple evidence only when it is safe.**
   - Take a few photos or a short video of the leak, the wet area, and any damaged items.
   - Do not delay moving away from electrics or a possible structural hazard to take photos.

8. **Tell the right person once the immediate area is safer.**
   - If you rent, contact your landlord, letting agent, housing association, or building manager.
   - If water is entering from a shared hallway, balcony, roof, external wall, or another flat, tell the building manager or freeholder if you have their emergency contact route.
   - If you own the home, check your insurer’s emergency contact route before authorising non-urgent repairs.

9. **Escalate if the situation is becoming unsafe.**
   - If water is rising quickly, spreading into several rooms, affecting electrics, or you are told to evacuate, follow emergency services and local council advice.
   - If there is immediate danger to life, electrical arcing, fire, suspected collapse, or someone is trapped, call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the cause during the storm, such as a failed seal, blocked gutter, loose flashing, wind-driven rain, or a door threshold problem.
- You do not need to fully dry the room, lift flooring, remove skirting, or start repairs right now.
- You do not need to decide tonight who is at fault or whether the repair is an insurance, landlord, builder, or freeholder issue.
- You do not need to throw away wet items immediately unless they are unsafe to keep near people, pets, or electrics.

## Important reassurance
A storm leak around a window or door can look alarming, but the first useful aim is limited: keep away from wet electrics, slow the spread, and protect essentials. You can deal with the cause and repairs after the weather and the immediate risk have eased.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation during the storm. Later decisions may need help from a landlord, building manager, insurer, contractor, flood specialist, or registered electrician.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, insurance, engineering, electrical, or other professional advice. If water has touched electrical systems, gas appliances, structural areas, or shared building services, get appropriate professional help before switching things back on or making repairs.
