# What to do if… you cannot find the important documents or will of a person who has died (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and secure what you can first: the home, keys, post, and any obvious documents. Then make a short list and work outward from the home, to close contacts, to professionals, to official records.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t throw anything away to tidy up; plain-looking letters can contain account numbers, policy details, or solicitor names.
- Don’t share scans or photos of the death certificate, passport, bank letters, or identity documents widely.
- Don’t guess what the will says or start distributing possessions informally.
- Don’t try to access accounts by guessing passwords or using someone else’s login without authority.
- Don’t assume there must be one master folder; papers may be spread across several places.
- Don’t let a pressured relative, neighbour, or acquaintance take documents away unless you know they have authority and you keep a record of what was taken.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the basics first.**  
   Collect keys if you are allowed to have them, lock the home, and put obvious important papers into one safe place, such as a labelled envelope or box. If you take photos of where things were found, keep them private and do not share images of sensitive documents.

2. **Start a simple tracking list.**  
   Make three headings: **Found**, **Searched**, and **Need to request**. Write down each room, drawer, box, bag, and folder as you check it so you do not have to keep re-checking the same places.

3. **Look for the minimum set first.**  
   Prioritise the death certificate or certified copies, any original will or codicil, bank and building society details, pension or benefit letters, mortgage or tenancy paperwork, insurance policies, property papers, vehicle papers, and contact details for a solicitor, accountant, financial adviser, employer, or pension provider.

4. **Search likely places, not the whole home at once.**  
   Check filing cabinets, desk drawers, bedside tables, document boxes, kitchen life-admin drawers, labelled folders, bags near the door, bookshelves, wallets, purses, calendars, address books, and the area around the computer or printer.

5. **Use clues from letters and emails without breaking into accounts.**  
   Look for letterheads, envelopes, appointment cards, old invoices, cheque book stubs, bank statements, pension statements, insurance renewal letters, and solicitor or accountant details. If you find a solicitor’s name, contact the firm and ask whether they hold a will or documents; they may need proof of death and evidence of your authority before discussing details.

6. **Keep post from going missing.**  
   If the deceased person’s address will not be reliably monitored, use Royal Mail’s redirection information and the Special Circumstances route for someone who has died. Expect identity checks and evidence that you are authorised to deal with the deceased person’s mail.

7. **If you think probate may already have happened in England or Wales, search the official records.**  
   The official probate search can show grants of representation and, where a will has become part of the probate record, a copy of the will. It will not find every will, and not every probate record contains a will.

8. **If property is involved in England or Wales, check the land register before hunting for title deeds.**  
   For registered property, HM Land Registry can usually provide the title register and title plan. If the property is not registered, or if the register shows filed deeds may exist, you may still need to ask a solicitor, lender, or HM Land Registry what documents are available. Scotland and Northern Ireland use different systems.

9. **Keep government-death paperwork together.**  
   If you have a Tell Us Once reference, put it with your core documents. Tell Us Once is normally explained after registering the death and can notify many government organisations where the person was living in England, Scotland or Wales. Northern Ireland uses a different process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find every paper today.
- You do not need to decide about selling property, closing every account, or distributing possessions until you know whether there is a will and who has authority to act.
- You do not need to sort digital access immediately unless it is needed to keep essentials running or to find contact details for a bank, insurer, pension provider, solicitor, or accountant.
- You do not need to solve probate, tax, inheritance, or family disagreements in this first search.

## Important reassurance
It is common not to know where someone kept documents, especially if they were private, unwell, or handled life admin alone. A calm, recorded search and a few official requests can usually replace or confirm many missing details.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and avoid irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about probate, inheritance, property, tax, disputes, or authority to act may need help from a solicitor, probate specialist, accountant, or other appropriate professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If there is conflict, uncertainty about who may act, pressure to hand over documents or property, or concern about fraud, slow down and seek professional advice before doing anything irreversible.
