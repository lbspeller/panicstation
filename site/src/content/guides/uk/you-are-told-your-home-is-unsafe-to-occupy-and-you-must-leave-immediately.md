# What to do if… you are told your home is unsafe to occupy and you must leave immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a safety issue first: get people and pets to a safer place, then get clear written confirmation of who is telling you to leave and why.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore the instruction if there may be immediate danger, such as fire, gas, carbon monoxide, structural movement, flooding, or electrical hazards.
- Don’t keep going back inside for belongings if you have been told the building is unsafe or entry is restricted.
- Don’t turn electrical switches on or off if you suspect gas, carbon monoxide, or electrical danger from water.
- Don’t argue while you are still inside the risk area; step away first, then clarify.
- Don’t sign anything you have not read or do not understand while you are panicked.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Move outside the risk area with anyone in the home, including pets if you can safely take them. Take your phone, keys, and any essential medicine that is immediately to hand. If there is immediate danger to life, call **999**.
2. **Find out who is giving the instruction.** From a safe place, ask: “Which organisation are you from?”, “What is the hazard?”, “Can anyone go back in?”, and “Is entry banned or only restricted?” If there is a notice on the door, wall, or letterbox, photograph it clearly, including any reference number.
3. **If you suspect gas or carbon monoxide, leave first and call the emergency gas route.** In Great Britain, call the National Gas Emergency Service on **0800 111 999**. In Northern Ireland, call the Gas Emergency Service on **0800 002 001**. Do this from outside or from a neighbour’s home, not from inside the affected property. If anyone may have carbon monoxide symptoms, call **NHS 111** for medical advice; call **999** for breathing difficulty, sudden confusion, collapse, severe weakness, chest pain, or muscle pain.
4. **If you rent, tell your landlord, agent, council landlord, or housing association immediately in writing.** Send a short message: “I have been told the property is unsafe to occupy. Here are photos of the notice or damage. Please confirm the safety status, access restrictions, and any emergency or temporary accommodation arrangements.” Keep a copy.
5. **If you own the home, contact your buildings insurer or emergency assistance line once people are safe.** Say: “I have been told the property is unsafe to occupy and may need emergency accommodation or urgent assessment.” Do not stay on hold if you still have nowhere safe to sleep.
6. **If you have nowhere safe to stay tonight, contact the urgent homelessness route for where you are.** Use your local council or local authority out-of-hours service, or the Northern Ireland Housing Executive route if you are in Northern Ireland. Say: “I cannot occupy my home because of a safety risk and I need emergency accommodation tonight.”
7. **Only collect a very small grab list if someone responsible says it is safe and allowed.** Prioritise ID, bank cards, keys, phone and charger, essential medicines or devices, glasses or hearing aids, baby supplies, pet lead or carrier, and a warm layer. If you cannot safely collect them, leave them.
8. **Make a simple record from a safe place.** Write down the time, who told you to leave, what they said, and where you are staying tonight. Keep photos of notices or visible damage and receipts for emergency costs.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to repair, move permanently, or make a full claim.
- You do not need to list every item you own in the first hours.
- You do not need to resolve who is legally or financially responsible right now.
- You do not need to return for non-essential belongings while access is unsafe or unclear.

## Important reassurance
Being told your home may not be safe can feel shocking and unreal. The first goal is narrow: get out of danger, find out who made the decision, and make sure you have somewhere safe to be tonight.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repairs, rehousing, insurance, rent, access, or legal rights may need specialist housing, insurance, building safety, or legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, insurance, housing, or professional safety advice. If there may be immediate danger, leave the risk area and contact emergency services.
