# What to do if… you receive a letter saying you may owe a penalty for an official filing you did not submit (UK)

## Short answer
Do not use any contact details or QR codes provided in the letter. Verify the claim by logging into your official government portal (HMRC Personal Tax Account or Companies House Service) or by using independently sourced contact details from GOV.UK.

## Do not do these things
- Do not call any phone number printed on the letter until it is verified.
- Do not scan any QR codes or click links included in the physical letter.
- Do not provide bank details, National Insurance numbers, or passwords to "confirm your identity" to the sender.
- Do not pay the penalty under pressure of "immediate enforcement" or "legal action" until the notice is confirmed as genuine.
- Do not discard the letter or the envelope, as they contain timestamps and barcodes needed for fraud investigations.

## What to do now
1. **Check your official digital records first.** Access your account via a known, secure route to see if a penalty or outstanding filing is listed there.
   - For tax or personal penalties, log into your HMRC Personal Tax Account or Business Tax Account via GOV.UK.
   - For company filing penalties, check the "Filing History" of your company on the official Companies House Service website.
2. **Verify the sender’s instructions against official scam lists.**
   - Visit the "Check if a letter you've received from HMRC is genuine" page on GOV.UK to compare the letter's content and reference numbers.
   - Search "Companies House scam guidance" on GOV.UK to check for known fraudulent letter templates.
3. **Capture evidence of the communication.** Take clear photographs of the letter and the envelope. Note specific details including the Return Address, the Department Name, and any "Our Reference" numbers provided.
4. **Contact the agency using a verified number from GOV.UK.** 
   - For HMRC, call the relevant general helpline (e.g., Self Assessment or Corporation Tax) found on the official GOV.UK contact pages.
   - For Companies House, if the letter is suspicious, call 0303 1234 500 or email phishing@companieshouse.gov.uk.
   - Use a script: "I have received a letter with reference [Number] claiming a penalty for [Filing Type]. I am calling to verify if this notice is genuine and issued by your department."
5. **Report the letter to the national fraud authority.**
   - If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report the incident to Report Fraud (the service that replaced Action Fraud) online or by calling 0300 123 2040.
   - If you are in Scotland, report the matter to Police Scotland by calling 101.
6. **Notify your bank if any financial data was shared.** If you have already interacted with a link or provided bank details, call your bank immediately using the number on the back of your card or via their official app.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a formal appeal or "Notice of Objection" until the agency confirms the letter is genuine.
- You do not need to hire a solicitor or accountant to dispute the debt until its authenticity is established.
- You do not need to correct your records or resubmit any filings until you have verified that the "missing" filing is a real requirement.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a demand for money is a high-stress event, but official agencies are aware that scammers frequently use their branding to cause panic. A genuine administrative error or a scam attempt is more likely than immediate legal enforcement. Taking 15 minutes to verify the source through an official portal is the most effective way to protect yourself.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and verification procedures for suspicious official correspondence. It does not cover the legal process for appealing a confirmed, genuine penalty notice.

## Important note
All contact with government agencies should be initiated through the official GOV.UK domain. Be cautious of "sponsored" search results when looking for helplines, as these may lead to high-cost call forwarding services or fraudulent sites.
