# What to do if… you are stopped for a document check abroad and do not have your passport with you (UK)

## Short answer
Stay calm, cooperate, and clearly say that your passport is at your accommodation or another safe place. Offer any other ID you have, ask what they need to complete the check, and ask whether your passport can be brought to you or shown through an official process.

## Do not do these things
- Do not run, argue, push past anyone, or physically resist.
- Do not offer money, a gift, or “cash to make it go away”.
- Do not treat a requested bribe as the same as an official fine; if a fine is being issued, ask for official paperwork or a receipt where possible.
- Do not sign anything you cannot read or do not understand; ask for an interpreter or translation first.
- Do not unlock or hand over your phone more broadly than needed if all that is being requested is a passport image; ask what they need to see and ask for an interpreter if you are unsure.
- Do not say you have “no ID” if you have another document, a passport photo, a residence card, a driving licence, or a local registration document.

## What to do now
1. **Use a simple, steady explanation.** Say: “I am British. My passport is at my accommodation. I can help you check my identity.” Keep your hands visible and follow clear safety instructions.

2. **Ask what would resolve the check.** Use short questions:
   - “Can I show another ID?”
   - “Can my passport be brought here?”
   - “Is there an official way to show it later?”
   - “Can I have an interpreter, please?”

3. **Show what you do have.** Offer another photo ID, a residence card, a driving licence, a local registration slip, or a photo or scan of your passport. Treat a passport photo as supporting information only, not as a guaranteed substitute.

4. **Arrange safe retrieval if the passport is nearby.** Call your hotel, host, travel companion, or accommodation reception. Ask them to confirm the passport is there or bring it to a safe agreed place if the officer allows that.

5. **Keep the focus narrow.** Give your name, where you are staying, and where the passport is. You do not need to explain your whole trip unless you are asked a direct, relevant question.

6. **If you are taken to a police station or office, stay calm and ask for basics.** Ask where you are being taken, why, whether you are free to contact someone, and whether the local British embassy, high commission, or consulate can be notified if you are detained and want consular assistance.

7. **If the passport is lost, stolen, retained, or impossible to access, switch to document-recovery steps.** Ask local police how to report a stolen passport and request a written record if available. If you need to travel urgently and cannot use your UK passport, check whether a UK emergency travel document is appropriate.

8. **Once you are safe, write down the details.** Note the date, time, place, station or unit name, names or badge numbers if visible, what you were asked for, what you showed, and any paperwork or receipt you were given.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, change accommodation, claim on insurance, or alter your whole trip.
- You do not need to apply for an emergency travel document if your passport is simply at your accommodation and can be retrieved.
- You do not need to solve future passport-carrying habits during the stop; focus only on staying safe and completing the check.

## Important reassurance
A missing passport in the moment can feel frightening, but it is usually a practical document problem first. Calmly showing what you have, explaining where the passport is, and asking for the next official step gives you the best chance of resolving the immediate situation.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate stop and the next short period. If you are detained, fined, threatened, injured, or your passport is taken, later decisions may need country-specific legal advice, help from your insurer, or consular support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, travel, immigration, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Local laws on carrying ID and police powers vary by country, and the British embassy, high commission, or consulate cannot give legal advice, represent you, or override local authorities.
