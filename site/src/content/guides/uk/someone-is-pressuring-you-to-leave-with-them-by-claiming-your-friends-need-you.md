# What to do if… someone is pressuring you to leave with them by claiming your friends need you (UK)

## Short answer
Do not go anywhere with them. Stay where there are other people, contact your friends directly yourself, and get staff or police help if the person will not back off.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t “just quickly” step outside, around a corner, into a stairwell, lift, car park, alley, or quiet place with them.
- Don’t hand them your phone or let them dial numbers for you.
- Don’t let politeness override safety; you do not need to explain or justify your refusal.
- Don’t follow them to prove you are being reasonable or to check their story.
- Don’t accept a lift, taxi, or ride they arranged, or get into any vehicle with them.
- Don’t separate from your bag, coat, keys, phone, money, or anything else you need to leave safely.

## What to do now
1. **Stop moving and create a safer pause.** Stay near other people, such as a queue, reception desk, shop counter, bar, security point, or busier part of the street.
2. **Use one clear line, then repeat it.** Say: “I’m not going anywhere. I’m calling my friends now.” Say it loudly enough for nearby people to hear.
3. **Contact your friends yourself.** Call them directly or message: “Are you looking for me? Where are you right now?” Do not rely on the person’s phone, message, or explanation.
4. **Move to staff or security and be plain.** Say: “Someone is trying to get me to leave with them. I don’t know them. Please stay with me and help me get safe.”
5. **At a participating venue, you can try Ask for Angela.** If staff do not recognise it straight away, switch to plain language: “I feel unsafe. I need help and I need to stay here.”
6. **Create witnesses and distance.** Stand beside a specific person or group and say: “Can I stand with you? I’m not safe.” If you are outside, move toward light, cameras, open businesses, or staffed transport points.
7. **Call 999 or 112 if you feel in immediate danger.** This includes being blocked from leaving, grabbed, followed aggressively, threatened, or pressured toward an exit or vehicle.
8. **If you cannot speak safely on a 999 call from a mobile, use the Silent Solution.** Call 999, stay on the line, listen to the operator, and try to cough, tap, or make another safe sound if you can. If prompted, press 55 to show it is a real emergency and to be put through to police.
9. **If you are in a venue, ask staff to note what happened.** Once you are away from the person, ask a manager or security staff to record the incident and preserve relevant CCTV if they can.
10. **When you are safe, write down what you remember.** Note the time, place, exact words used, description of the person, direction they went, and any vehicle details.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it was definitely dangerous or just strange.
- You do not need to confront them, prove they are lying, or get their name.
- You do not need to continue your plans tonight.
- You do not need to report it before you are safe, unless there is immediate danger now.
- If it is not an emergency but you want to tell police later, you can call 101 or report online when you are calm and safe.

## Important reassurance
This can be a manipulation pattern: urgency, social pressure, and a reason to move you away from other people. Feeling unsure, frozen, or overly polite in the moment is normal; your job is simply to slow things down and stay around other people.

## Scope note
These are first steps to avoid being isolated and to get immediate help. Later decisions, including whether to report it, speak to venue management, or change travel plans, may need specialist help or support from someone you trust.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal advice, medical advice, therapeutic advice, or professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 999 or 112 right away.
