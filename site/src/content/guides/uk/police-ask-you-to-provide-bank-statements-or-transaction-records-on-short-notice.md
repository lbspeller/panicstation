# What to do if… police ask you to provide bank statements or transaction records on short notice (UK)

## Short answer
Pause, ask for the request in writing, and get legal advice before providing anything voluntarily. If you have been served with paperwork that looks like a court order or production order, do not ignore it; treat it as urgent and speak to a solicitor before responding.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t give your online-banking login, passcodes, one-time codes, recovery codes, or password-reset links.
- Don’t voluntarily hand over your phone, laptop, banking app, or payment app “just for a minute” without legal advice.
- Don’t send screenshots, full exports, or “everything I can find” when the request is vague.
- Don’t alter, delete, rename, hide, or “tidy up” transactions, messages, receipts, accounts, or files.
- Don’t guess what the police mean by “records”; ask them to put the accounts, dates, and document types in writing.
- Don’t rush into explanations about transactions while you are still trying to understand the request.
- Don’t contact other involved people to discuss the facts; speak to a solicitor and, if needed, a trusted person for practical support.

## What to do now
1. **Work out whether this is voluntary or formal paperwork.** Ask calmly:
   - “Is this a voluntary request, or have I been served with an order?”
   - “What power or order are you relying on?”
   - “What is the deadline, and where is it written?”
   - “Does this apply in the part of the UK where this is being handled?”

2. **Get the request in writing before you provide anything voluntarily.** Ask for:
   - the officer’s name, collar or warrant number, unit, and contact details
   - the incident, crime, case, or reference number
   - an email from an official address, a letter, or the full paperwork
   - the exact account names, account numbers or identifiers, platforms, date range, and record types requested
   - whether they want statements, a transaction export, payment-app records, crypto-exchange records, or something else

3. **Use one calm holding sentence.**
   - “I’m not refusing, but I need to speak to a solicitor before I provide any financial records voluntarily.”

4. **If you are at a police station, under arrest, or being asked to attend an interview, ask for legal advice immediately.**
   - Say: “I want free independent legal advice before I answer questions or provide records.”
   - If you do not have a solicitor, ask custody staff to arrange the local duty or legal aid solicitor route.
   - Do not discuss the substance of the case while legal advice is being arranged.

5. **If you have paperwork that looks like a court order, production order, notice, warrant, or summons, treat it as urgent.**
   - Photograph or scan every page, including the envelope or covering email.
   - Note when and how you received it.
   - Check the named person, named accounts, dates, deadline, court or authority, and what it says you must do.
   - Contact a criminal solicitor and send them the full paperwork before producing records.
   - If the deadline is very close, ask the solicitor about clarification, narrowing, or an extension.

6. **If police mention PACE, Proceeds of Crime Act, a production order, a court order, or local equivalent wording, do not rely on memory or summaries.**
   - Ask them to identify the exact written power or document.
   - Ask where the order says the records must be sent.
   - Ask whether the request is limited to specific accounts, dates, platforms, or transaction types.

7. **Keep the scope controlled if records are eventually provided.**
   - Provide only what the written request or order covers, after legal advice where possible.
   - Prefer bank-generated or platform-generated statements and exports over cropped screenshots.
   - Do not add extra accounts, longer date ranges, explanations, messages, receipts, or device access unless advised or clearly required.
   - Keep an identical copy of what you send.

8. **Make a simple disclosure log.** Record:
   - what you provided
   - the account or platform covered
   - the date range
   - the file format
   - the date and time sent
   - who it was sent to
   - the written request or order it relates to

9. **Use a safer delivery route.**
   - Ask for an official secure upload route, official email address, or solicitor-to-police route.
   - Avoid sending sensitive financial records through personal messaging apps unless your solicitor advises that route and you keep a full record.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain every transaction right now.
- You do not need to search years of records unless a written request or order specifies that period.
- You do not need to provide extra context documents, receipts, messages, photos, or spreadsheets unless your solicitor advises it or the written order clearly requires it.
- You do not need to decide today whether to provide anything beyond what is clearly requested in writing.
- You do not need to solve the whole investigation; your immediate task is to avoid unsafe over-disclosure, missed formal paperwork, and accidental deletion.

## Important reassurance
A short-notice request for account statements or transaction records can feel intimidating, especially when it comes from police. Slowing down to confirm what is being asked, getting it in writing, and taking legal advice is a normal way to prevent mistakes; it is not the same as refusing to cooperate.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, aimed at stabilising the moment and preventing avoidable harm. Anything involving interview, arrest, court paperwork, production orders, device access, passwords, or deadlines may need case-specific advice from a criminal solicitor.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Police powers, voluntary requests, court orders, deadlines, and rights can depend on the document used, the investigation, and where in the UK the matter is being handled.
