# What to do if… your HVAC unit is leaking from the condensate line and water is collecting nearby (UK)

## Short answer
Turn the system off immediately and keep all water away from electrical components, sockets, or the consumer unit. If water has entered electrical fittings, do not touch the area and isolate the power at the fuse box if it is safe to do so.

## Do not do these things
- Don't leave the system running to see if the leak slows down or clears itself.
- Don't touch any electrical switches, plugs, or the unit itself with wet hands or while standing in water.
- Don't attempt to dismantle the unit, remove covers, or poke objects into the drain line.
- Don't use a vacuum cleaner to clear water unless it is a specialist wet-vac and the power is isolated.
- Don't ignore small drips on walls or ceilings, as water can track behind surfaces into hidden wiring.

## What to do now
1. **Power down the system via the controller.** Turn the HVAC or air conditioning unit to the **off** or **standby** setting using the thermostat or remote.
2. **Isolate the local power supply if the area is dry.** Locate the local isolator switch or fused spur (usually a white switch near the indoor unit) and turn it **off**.
   - If the switch is wet or surrounded by water, **do not touch it**.
   - If water is dripping into a socket or consumer unit, go to the main fuse box and turn off the relevant circuit breaker if you can do so while standing on a dry surface.
3. **Clear the immediate area of hazards.** Move rugs, furniture, and electronic devices away from the water.
   - Use towels to create a barrier to stop water spreading toward other rooms or electrical floor sockets.
4. **Contain the leak at the source.** Place a bucket or shallow tray directly under the drip point to catch falling water.
5. **Check the external discharge point if accessible.** Look at the end of the condensate pipe (usually outside or over a internal drain) to see if there is a visible blockage like leaves or debris.
   - **Do not** climb ladders or attempt any high-level inspection.
6. **Request an emergency repair.**
   - **Private or Social Tenants:** Report the leak immediately as an emergency under the **Renters' Rights Act 2026**. State that there is a "water leak with electrical risk" to trigger the mandatory 24-hour response window for hazardous conditions.
   - **Homeowners:** Contact a F-Gas registered HVAC engineer or a specialist drainage contractor. If water has contacted any electrical accessories, book a registered electrician to verify safety before restoring power.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify whether the cause is a cracked pan, a failed pump, or a frozen coil.
- You do not need to clean the internal filters or the evaporator coil right now.
- You do not need to contact your buildings insurer until the immediate leak is contained and the electrical safety is confirmed.

## Important reassurance
Condensate leaks are a standard maintenance issue caused by the natural moisture the system pulls from the air. While the water pooling can be startling, turning the system off prevents the production of more water immediately. Once the system is off and the area is contained, the risk of further damage is significantly reduced.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization only. Professional intervention is required to clear blockages, replace pumps, or repair the condensate line.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace professional electrical or mechanical advice. If you suspect an immediate fire risk or major electrical arcing, evacuate the area and call 999.
