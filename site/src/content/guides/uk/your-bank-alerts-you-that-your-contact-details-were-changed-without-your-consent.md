# What to do if… your bank alerts you that your contact details were changed without your consent (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately using a trusted route, such as the number on the back of your card, the bank’s official website, or the app you normally use. Say your contact details may have been changed without consent and ask them to lock down the account, check recent activity, and stop any unauthorised payments they can still stop.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links or call numbers from the alert message.
- Do not share one-time passcodes, card PINs, passwords, or security codes with anyone who contacts you.
- Do not approve any sign-in, card, payee, or payment request you do not recognise.
- Do not rely on SMS password resets until your mobile network has checked for a SIM swap, eSIM activation, or number port.
- Do not delete alerts, emails, texts, or app notifications, even if they are upsetting.
- Do not let a caller rush you into moving money, installing software, or keeping the call secret.

## What to do now
1. **Contact your bank through a trusted route.** Ask for the fraud team and say: “My phone number or email may have been changed without my consent.” Ask them to temporarily block online and telephone banking, undo the contact-detail change, confirm the contact details they currently hold, and tell you the safe way they will contact you.

2. **Ask the bank to check account-takeover changes.** Ask them to review recent payments, pending payments, card use, cash withdrawals, new payees, linked accounts, new devices, new cards, address changes, statement settings, and paperless settings. Ask them to remove anything you did not authorise and give you a case or reference number.

3. **Use your banking app only through your normal trusted route if you can still access it safely.** Freeze your card if the app offers this, sign out of other devices if shown, and check profile settings for unknown phone numbers, email addresses, devices, payees, or delivery addresses. If anything looks wrong, stop using the app and continue through the bank’s fraud team.

4. **Secure the email account linked to your bank.** From a device you trust, change the email password, turn on two-step verification, check recovery email and phone details, remove mail-forwarding rules you did not set, and look for recent sign-ins you do not recognise.

5. **Contact your mobile network provider through a trusted route.** Ask whether there has been a SIM swap, eSIM activation, PAC request, number port, or account-security change. Ask them to secure your mobile account with any stronger checks they offer, such as an account PIN or passphrase, and reset your voicemail PIN if you use voicemail.

6. **When the bank says the account is back under your control, refresh banking security.** Change your banking password from a device you trust, remove unknown devices or sessions if the bank shows them, and use non-SMS approval methods if your bank offers them.

7. **Write down a clean timeline.** Record the alert time and date, what changed, who you spoke to, the bank reference number, what was blocked or reversed, and any payments or payees you do not recognise. Keep screenshots of alerts, emails, texts, app messages, and account-change notifications.

8. **Report wider fraud or identity misuse if needed.** If money has gone, someone has used your identity, or you suspect wider fraud, report it to Report Fraud if you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland. If you are in Scotland, report it to Police Scotland. Consider Cifas Protective Registration if you are worried someone may use your identity to open or take over accounts; it may make genuine applications take longer while extra checks are done.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to switch banks, close every account, replace your phone, or buy identity-monitoring services.
- You do not need to change every password you have ever used; focus first on banking, the linked email account, and your mobile number.
- You do not need to make a formal complaint while the immediate lock-down is still happening. If the bank does not put things right, you can use the bank’s complaints process and then ask the Financial Ombudsman Service to look at it after a final response, usually within six months of that final response, or if the bank has not responded within eight weeks.

## Important reassurance
This kind of alert can be frightening because it suggests someone may be trying to take control of your account. The useful first move is not to solve everything at once; it is to get the bank, email account, and mobile number locked back to you step by step.

## Scope note
These are first steps to regain control and reduce further loss. Later decisions about refunds, complaints, identity protection, credit files, or specialist support depend on what the bank, email provider, and mobile network find.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 999. For refunds, fraud claims, and formal disputes, follow your bank’s fraud and complaints process and keep records of everything you report.
