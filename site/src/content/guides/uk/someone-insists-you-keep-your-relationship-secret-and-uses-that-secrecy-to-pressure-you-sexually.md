# What to do if… someone insists you keep your relationship secret and uses that secrecy to pressure you sexually (UK)

## Short answer
You do not owe anyone secrecy or sexual access. Move to a physical space where you are not alone with them and contact a specialist support service to break the isolation.

## Do not do these things
- Don't agree to sexual contact as a way to "manage" their mood or prevent them from exposing the relationship.
- Don't accept the framing that secrecy is a "test of loyalty" or a requirement of the relationship.
- Don't meet them in a private or isolated location to discuss these boundaries if you feel unsafe or pressured.
- Don't send intimate images or messages as a "compromise" to stop their demands.
- Don't delete evidence of threats or pressure if you feel safe keeping it, as it may be needed for support or reporting later.
- Don't blame yourself for "going along with it" or freezing; these are common responses to coercion and control.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritise immediate physical distance.** If you are currently with the person, leave the room or building using a neutral excuse if necessary. If you are not with them, do not agree to meet in private.
2. **Contact a specialist support service for an objective perspective.** You can speak to these services without involving the police.
   - **England and Wales (16+): Rape Crisis 24/7 Support Line 0808 500 2222** or use their 24/7 online chat.
   - **Scotland (13+): Rape Crisis Scotland 08088 01 03 02** (daily 5pm–midnight).
   - **Northern Ireland (18+): Rape Crisis NI 0800 0246 991** (check website for hours) or **Nexus (16+) 028 9032 6803**.
   - **Any UK location: Victim Support 0808 168 9111** (24/7).
3. **Check your digital safety settings.** If the person has had access to your devices, they may be monitoring you.
   - Use the "Safety Check" feature (on iOS) or "Security Checkup" (on Google) to see who has access to your location and accounts.
   - Change your primary email password first, then other key accounts, using a device they have never touched (such as a library computer or a friend’s phone).
4. **Access medical or forensic support if sexual contact occurred without your full consent.** You do not need to report to the police to receive healthcare.
   - Visit **nhs.uk/sarcs** to find your nearest Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) in England.
   - Call **NHS 111** for guidance on urgent medical care in any part of the UK.
5. **Set a written boundary only if it is safe to do so.** A single, clear message establishes your position: "I am not comfortable with the secrecy of this relationship being used to pressure me. I do not consent to sexual contact. Do not contact me about this further."
6. **Secure evidence of coercion.** Save screenshots of messages where they use the secret as leverage or ignore your "no." Move these to a secure, password-protected cloud folder or email them to a new account that only you can access.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to end the relationship permanently right this second.
- You do not need to decide whether to make a formal police report (999 for emergencies; 101 for non-emergencies).
- You do not need to "prove" that what happened was a crime to receive support from a helpline or SARC.

## Important reassurance
Using secrecy to isolate someone and pressure them into sex is a known tactic of domestic abuse and sexual coercion. Feeling trapped, confused, or unable to speak up is a natural reaction to this dynamic. You are not "at fault" for the secrecy, and you are not responsible for the other person's actions or threats.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute situation involving sexual coercion and secrecy. It is not a substitute for legal advice, medical diagnosis, or long-term therapeutic support.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance. If you are under 18, you can contact **Childline at 0800 1111** for confidential help. If you believe you are in immediate physical danger, call 999. As of April 2026, new public sexual harassment laws provide additional protections against intentional intimidation in public spaces.
