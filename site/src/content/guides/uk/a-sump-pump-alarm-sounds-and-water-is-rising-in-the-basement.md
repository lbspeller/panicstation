# What to do if… a sump pump alarm sounds and water is rising in the basement (UK)

## Short answer
Avoid entering the water until you are certain the electricity is isolated, as standing water near sockets or appliances creates a high risk of electric shock. If safe and dry to do so, turn off power at the consumer unit and contact an emergency plumber or pump specialist immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not step into standing water if it has reached electrical sockets, extension leads, or the consumer unit (fuse box).
- Do not touch the consumer unit or any electrical switches if your hands are wet or if you are standing on a damp floor.
- Do not ignore the alarm even if the water level appears to be rising slowly.
- Do not let children or pets enter the affected area.
- Do not attempt to dismantle the pump or electrical control panel while the power is connected.
- Do not use a petrol or diesel pump or generator inside the house or any enclosed space due to carbon monoxide risks.
- Do not assume the floodwater is clean; it may contain sewage or chemical runoff.

## What to do now
1. **Move everyone to a safe, dry area of the property immediately.** If water is rising rapidly, stay on the ground floor or above and do not attempt to salvage items from the basement until you have confirmed the area is electrically safe.
2. **Isolate the electricity only if the consumer unit is in a dry, accessible location.** **If you can reach the main switch without entering water or touching damp surfaces, turn it off.** 
   - If the consumer unit is in the basement or the area around it is wet, stay away.
   - Call **105** to report the risk to the electricity network operator (England, Scotland, and Wales). 
   - In Northern Ireland, call **NIE Networks** on **03457 643 643**.
3. **Turn off the gas supply at the meter if there is a risk of deep flooding or structural damage.** **Only do this if the meter is located in a safe, dry place away from the rising water.**
4. **Identify the alarm type from a safe distance.** **Check if the alarm is a "High Water" alert or a "Power Failure" alert on the control panel.**
   - If it is a power failure and the rest of the house has electricity, check the circuit breakers (only if dry).
   - If it is a high water alarm, the pump may be blocked or the float switch may be stuck.
5. **Check the pump's discharge point outside the building.** **Look for signs of a blocked or frozen pipe where the water should be exiting.**
   - If you see water backing up or a kinked hose, and it is safe to reach, try to clear the obstruction without entering the basement.
6. **Contact a specialist pump engineer or emergency plumber.** **State clearly that you have an active basement flood and a high-level sump alarm.**
   - If you are a tenant, notify your landlord or managing agent immediately as they may have a preferred contractor.
7. **Move high-value portable items to upper floors if the water is not yet near electrics.** **Focus on documents, electronics, and sentimental items that can be carried safely.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the mechanical cause of the pump failure right now.
- You do not need to begin cleaning or disinfecting the area while water is still rising.
- You do not need to contact your insurance company in the first few minutes; focus on safety and stopping the water first.
- You do not need to decide on permanent drainage upgrades or replacement pumps today.

## Important reassurance
Basement flooding is a high-stress event, and the sound of a sump alarm is designed to cause urgency. It is normal to feel a need to rush, but taking a moment to ensure the area is electrically safe prevents the situation from becoming a life-threatening emergency. Professional help is available 24/7 for these specific utility failures.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active sump pump failure. It does not cover long-term drying, mold remediation, or technical repair of pumping systems.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only and does not replace professional electrical or plumbing advice. If water has reached any electrical installations, do not enter the area until a qualified electrician or your network operator has confirmed it is safe.
