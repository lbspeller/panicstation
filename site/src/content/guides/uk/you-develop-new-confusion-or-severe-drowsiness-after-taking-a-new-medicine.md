# What to do if… you develop new confusion or severe drowsiness after taking a new medicine (UK)

## Short answer
**Call 999 immediately if the person is unresponsive, struggling to breathe, or cannot be woken up.** Otherwise, contact NHS 111 (call 111) for urgent clinical advice before taking any further doses.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive, cycle, or operate any machinery if you feel even slightly drowsy or less alert than usual.
- Do not consume alcohol, as it can dangerously increase the sedating effects of many medications.
- Do not take another dose of the new medicine until a healthcare professional tells you it is safe to do so.
- Do not stop any long-term, regular medications abruptly without medical advice, as this can cause withdrawal or a relapse of your condition.
- Do not take any "extra" sedating substances like over-the-counter sleep aids, strong painkillers, or sedating antihistamines.
- Do not eat or drink if you are struggling to stay awake or feel very confused, due to the risk of choking.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency red flags and call 999 if they are present.** You must seek immediate emergency help if the person:
   - Is difficult to wake up or completely unresponsive.
   - Has slow, shallow, or noisy breathing, or their lips/fingernails appear blue or grey.
   - Is having a seizure or has collapsed.
   - Shows signs of a stroke (facial drooping, arm weakness, or slurred speech).
2. **Contact NHS 111 urgently if there are no immediate life-threatening signs.** You can call 111 from any phone; if the person is under 5 years old, you must call 111 rather than using the online service.
3. **Gather all medication packaging and leaflets to have ready for the clinician.** You will need to provide:
   - The exact name and strength of the new medicine.
   - The exact time and amount of the last dose taken.
   - A list of all other medicines, supplements, or herbal remedies taken in the last 24 hours.
4. **Position yourself or the person safely to prevent falls or airway obstruction.** If you are feeling very sleepy:
   - Lie on your side (the recovery position) rather than flat on your back.
   - Stay on the ground floor if possible to avoid stairs.
   - Keep your phone within reach and unlocked.
5. **Ask someone to stay with you until you have spoken to a professional and your symptoms improve.** If you are alone, call a friend or family member and ask them to keep you on the line or come to your location.
6. **Check the medication packaging for a "Black Triangle" symbol (▼).** This inverted black triangle means the medicine is being intensively monitored; tell the 111 advisor if you see this symbol on the box or leaflet.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you will stop this treatment permanently today; focus only on the immediate dose.
- You do not need to research side effects on internet forums, as this can be misleading and delays professional assessment.
- You do not need to file a formal side-effect report (Yellow Card) until you are medically stable.

## Important reassurance
It is common for the body to react unexpectedly to a new chemical substance, and feeling "spaced out," heavily sedated, or confused can be frightening. These reactions are recognized medical events, and seeking help via 111 or 999 is the standard, appropriate response to ensure your safety.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Decisions regarding dose adjustments, tapering, or switching to alternative medications must be made by a prescribing clinician or pharmacist.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not replace professional medical judgment. If symptoms worsen rapidly or the person becomes unresponsive, call 999. Side effects of medicines in the UK should eventually be reported to the MHRA Yellow Card scheme (online or via 0800 731 6789).
