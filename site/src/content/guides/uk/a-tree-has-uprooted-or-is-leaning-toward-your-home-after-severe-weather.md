# What to do if… a tree has uprooted or is leaning toward your home after severe weather (UK)

## Short answer
Get everyone away from the side the tree is leaning toward, and keep well clear of any overhead lines or fallen cables. If there is immediate danger to life or serious injury, call 999; otherwise arrange urgent help through the landowner, road authority, landlord, building manager, or an insured tree professional.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t stand under the leaning side of the tree or near the lifted root plate.
- Don’t touch, approach, or try to move anything near overhead lines or fallen cables.
- Don’t try to cut, pull, brace, or tie back a storm-damaged tree yourself.
- Don’t use ladders or go onto the roof near the likely fall path.
- Don’t let children or pets into the garden, driveway, pavement, or room nearest the tree.
- Don’t assume the tree is safe because it has stopped moving; windblown and damaged trees can shift unexpectedly.

## What to do now
1. **Move people out of the likely fall path.**  
   Move everyone to a part of the home away from the tree, or leave the building if the safest rooms are still in the likely fall path. Stay away from hanging branches, lifted roots, cracked trunks, and walls or windows the tree could hit.

2. **Check for electrical hazards from a distance.**  
   If the tree is touching lines, there are cables on the ground, or you see or hear sparking:
   - Call 999 if there is immediate danger to life or serious injury.
   - In England, Scotland or Wales, call 105 to report the electricity network hazard.
   - In Northern Ireland, call NIE Networks on 03457 643 643.
   Keep everyone away until the electricity network operator or emergency services say it is safe.

3. **If it affects a road, pavement, public right of way, or public land, report it.**  
   If it is an immediate danger, call 999. Otherwise, report it to the local council or road authority. For major roads, use the relevant route: National Highways in England, Traffic Scotland in Scotland, Traffic Wales in Wales, or nidirect in Northern Ireland.

4. **If you rent or live in a managed building, contact the responsible person early.**  
   Call your landlord, letting agent, housing association, factor, freeholder, or building manager using their emergency or out-of-hours contact route. Ask them to arrange urgent make-safe work. Do not wait for permission before moving away from danger.

5. **If it is on private land, arrange a competent tree professional.**  
   Contact an insured tree surgeon or arborist for an urgent make-safe visit, for example through a recognised professional directory. Say: “A tree has uprooted or is leaning toward the house after severe weather.”

6. **Reduce harm only from a safe place.**  
   If you can do it without going near the tree or any cables, move cars, bins, garden furniture, and people’s routes away from the likely fall path. Keep exits clear.

7. **Take simple records from a safe distance.**  
   Take photos or video showing the lean, exposed roots, cracked trunk, damaged branches, and distance from the roof, windows, road, or cables. Contact your home insurer if the tree may damage the building or if emergency work is needed.

8. **If the building has been hit or looks structurally unsafe, leave it.**  
   Leave if you see major cracking, sagging, sudden movement, a damaged roof, or a tree pressing on the building. Call 999 if there is immediate danger. Only shut off utilities if you can do so without entering the risk area.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide liability or whose tree it is right now.
- You do not need to clear debris immediately.
- You do not need to use ladders, ropes, chainsaws, or your car to move the tree.
- You do not need full repair quotes before the immediate hazard is controlled.
- You can wait to write formal complaints, claims, or neighbour letters until people and the building are safe.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to want to fix the problem quickly. With a storm-damaged tree, the safer first step is usually to slow down, keep distance, and bring in the right service, especially if electricity, a road, or the building itself may be involved.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation. Later decisions may need help from your insurer, landlord, council, road authority, electricity network operator, structural professional, or qualified tree professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, engineering, arboricultural, or other professional advice. If you think anyone is in immediate danger, call 999. If you cannot confirm something is safe, especially around electricity or structural damage, treat it as unsafe and wait for professionals.
