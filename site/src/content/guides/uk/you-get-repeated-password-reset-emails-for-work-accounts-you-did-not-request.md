# What to do if… you get repeated password reset emails for work accounts you did not request (UK)

## Short answer
State one clear boundary, pause the interaction, and immediately involve a manager or security. Under 2026 UK law, your employer must take all reasonable steps to prevent third-party harassment and support your withdrawal from unsafe situations.

## Do not do these things
- Do not match the customer's volume, tone, or use of aggressive language.
- Do not attempt to resolve the underlying complaint while the abuse is ongoing.
- Do not stay in an isolated area; do not allow the customer to stand between you and an exit.
- Do not offer unauthorized refunds or policy exceptions to "quieten" the situation.
- Do not minimize the incident or assume it is "just part of the job."
- Do not delete recordings, call logs, or digital transcripts of the interaction.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the interaction immediately.** Stop speaking for several seconds to break the cycle of shouting. Focus on creating physical or digital distance (stepping back from a counter or placing a caller on a brief hold).
2. **State one boundary line once.** Use a neutral, scripted phrase to define what is unacceptable.
   - “I am happy to help you, but I will not continue this conversation if you use that language.”
   - “I need to pause this interaction until we can speak without shouting.”
3. **Escalate to a supervisor or manager immediately.** Do not wait for the customer to "calm down." Use your workplace’s internal alert system (panic button, code word, or radio) to bring a second staff member to the location.
4. **Request a handover or two-person support.** If your employer expects you to continue the task, state that you require a colleague or manager to be present for the remainder of the interaction.
   - For physical sites: move to a well-lit, CCTV-monitored, or public-facing area.
   - For digital/phone: ask for a supervisor to join the line or move the case to a monitored ticket system.
5. **Identify specific safety triggers for total disengagement.** Stop the interaction entirely and move to a secure area if you observe:
   - Personal threats or threats against your family/home.
   - Looming, blocking of exits, or physical contact.
   - Hate speech or harassment related to protected characteristics (e.g., race, sex, disability).
6. **Contact emergency services if danger is imminent.** Dial **999** if the person refuses to leave, makes credible threats of violence, or displays a weapon. For non-imminent threats that still require a record, call **101**.
7. **Document the interaction within two hours.** Write down the exact phrases used, the time of the incident, and the names of any witnesses. Under April 2026 rules, reports of third-party sexual harassment are explicitly protected under whistleblowing laws.
8. **Request an entry in the workplace violence/aggression log.** Ensure your manager records the incident formally. This is essential for proving your employer is meeting their "all reasonable steps" legal duty to prevent third-party harassment.
9. **Use "Section 44" terminology if pressured to stay in danger.** If a manager insists you continue an interaction where you reasonably believe there is serious and imminent danger, state: "I am exercising my right to withdraw from a dangerous situation under Section 44 of the Employment Rights Act."

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to file a formal grievance against your employer.
- Researching long-term legal claims for harassment or stress.
- Reviewing CCTV footage (your employer or the police will handle this later).
- Responding to follow-up emails or social media messages from the customer.

## Important reassurance
Experiencing verbal abuse often triggers a physical "fight or flight" response, including shaking or a racing heart; these are normal physiological reactions to hostility. You are not "failing" at customer service by prioritizing your safety over a transaction.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and harm prevention during an active or recent abusive encounter. It does not cover long-term tribunal strategies or complex HR negotiations.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. While UK law requires employers to protect staff from third-party harassment as of 2026, individual workplace policies vary. If you are in immediate physical danger, contact the police on 999.
