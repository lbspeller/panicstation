# What to do if… you notice a painful swelling near your eye or nose that seems to be spreading (UK)

## Short answer
Seek an **immediate medical assessment** (A&E or an emergency GP appointment) because infections near the eye can spread rapidly to deeper tissues. If you experience vision changes, pain when moving your eye, or feel systemically unwell, **call 999 or attend A&E now**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for the swelling to "go down" on its own if it is spreading or causing significant pain.
- Do not squeeze, poke, or attempt to drain the swollen area or any visible spots.
- Do not apply any pressure to the eyeball itself when checking the swelling.
- Do not use old or leftover antibiotic creams or tablets.
- Do not wear contact lenses or apply eye makeup until a clinician has cleared you.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if your vision is affected or you feel dizzy.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency "red flag" symptoms and call 999 if any are present.**
   - **Any change in vision**, such as blurred vision, double vision, or colors appearing "washed out" or dull.
   - **Pain when moving your eye** or a feeling that the eye is restricted or "stuck."
   - **The eyeball appearing to bulge** or being pushed forward compared to the other side.
   - **Uneven pupils** or a change in how your pupils react to light.
   - **Signs of severe illness**, including a very high temperature, confusion, drowsiness, or a severe headache and stiff neck.
2. **Contact your GP or NHS 111 immediately if red flags are absent.**
   - **Request a same-day urgent appointment** and explicitly state: "I have painful, spreading swelling near my eye and nose."
   - **Use NHS 111 online or call 111** if your GP is closed; they can arrange an out-of-hours assessment or direct you to an Urgent Treatment Centre.
3. **Document the spread of the swelling to assist the clinician.**
   - **Take a clear photo** of your face in good light to show the current extent.
   - **Note the exact time** you noticed the swelling and how quickly it has changed.
   - **Mark the outer edge of the redness** on your cheek or nose with a pen if the skin is not too tender (do not do this on the eyelid).
4. **Isolate the area and minimize irritation.**
   - **Keep your head elevated** using extra pillows to help reduce fluid build-up.
   - **Avoid touching or rubbing the eye**, as this can introduce more bacteria or cause trauma to the infected tissue.
5. **Gather your medical history for the assessment.**
   - **List any recent infections**, such as a cold, sinus pain, a dental abscess, or an insect bite near the face.
   - **Note your current medications** and any known allergies, especially to antibiotics like penicillin.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific type of bacteria or the exact name of the infection (e.g., preseptal vs. orbital cellulitis) before seeking help.
- You do not need to apply heat or home remedies, as these can sometimes mask symptoms or encourage bacterial growth.
- You do not need to worry about long-term vision impact right now; prompt medical treatment is designed to prevent complications.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel concerned when swelling appears near the eye. Medical professionals treat facial swelling with high priority because the area is sensitive. Acting quickly by seeking an assessment is the standard and most effective way to manage these symptoms and ensure the correct treatment is started.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and urgent navigation only. A physical examination by a healthcare professional is required to differentiate between surface-level skin infections and deeper orbital infections.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. If your symptoms worsen rapidly while waiting for an appointment, go directly to your nearest A&E department.
