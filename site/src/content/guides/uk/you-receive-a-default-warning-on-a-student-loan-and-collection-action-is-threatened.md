# What to do if… you receive a default warning on a student loan and collection action is threatened (UK)

## Short answer
Do not make an immediate payment under pressure, but do not ignore the notice; contact the Student Loans Company (SLC) or the named agency via verified official channels to confirm the debt status and request an immediate pause while you provide evidence or seek advice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay immediately just because the notice uses high-pressure language like "final demand" or "immediate enforcement."
- Do not use telephone numbers or links provided in a suspicious text or email if you cannot verify them on a .gov.uk website.
- Do not share one-time passcodes (OTPs), full bank passwords, or login credentials with anyone calling you to "help" with the debt.
- Do not agree to a monthly repayment figure that exceeds your surplus income just to end a stressful conversation.
- Do not assume the debt is an error if you are living abroad; 2026-27 overseas threshold cuts have caused many accounts to fall into technical arrears.
- Do not rely on the Breathing Space scheme to pause standard SLC loans, as they are legally excluded from this specific protection.

## What to do now
1. **Gather the physical notice and identify the specific entity demanding payment.** **Look for the organisation's name (e.g., Student Loans Company, TDX Group, or Pastdue Credit Solutions) and any unique reference numbers.**
2. **Verify the legitimacy of the contact using an independent official source.** **Visit the official GOV.UK "Contact Student Loans Company" page to find a verified phone number rather than calling the number on the letter immediately.**
3. **Determine if the "debt" is actually a penalty for missing an overseas income assessment.** **Check if you have failed to provide SLC with your current employment status or income evidence while living outside the UK, as this triggers a high "fixed monthly repayment" penalty.**
4. **Compare the demand against your recent employment records and payslips.** **Confirm whether student loan deductions are already being taken via PAYE (Pay As You Earn) or if your Self Assessment tax return for the previous year included these payments.**
5. **Call the verified organisation and ask for the specific status of the account.** **Use the exact phrase "I need to know if this account is in arrears, in formal default, or has been passed to a third-party collector."**
6. **Request a temporary hold on collection activity while you gather evidence or consult a debt adviser.** **Ask them to confirm in writing how long they will pause escalation (e.g., 14 or 30 days) while you verify the balance or submit an Overseas Assessment Form.**
7. **Submit any missing income evidence or employment details via the official SLC online portal immediately.** **Provide recent payslips, tax returns, or bank statements if you are currently earning below the relevant 2026-27 repayment threshold.**
8. **Contact a free, accredited UK debt advice service if the amount demanded is unaffordable.** **Reach out to National Debtline or Citizens Advice to discuss your options, especially if you have other debts or the loan is a private (non-SLC) agreement.**
9. **Log all interactions in a dedicated file or digital document.** **Record the date, time, name of the person you spoke to, and exactly what was agreed regarding the pause or next steps.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to calculate the total interest or dispute the lifetime balance of the loan today.
- You do not need to decide if you want to make a "full and final settlement" offer during the first call.
- You do not need to provide your full updated budget or "Statement of Means" until you have spoken with a free debt adviser.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a notice about "collection action" or "default" is designed to be alarming, but it is often a procedural trigger caused by a documentation gap—such as a missed annual overseas update—rather than a permanent legal judgment. Contacting the correct department and providing the requested evidence usually stops the escalation process immediately.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for UK student loan repayment crises only. It does not provide long-term financial planning or legal defense for court proceedings.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Student loan regulations, specifically overseas thresholds, are subject to annual changes and vary by individual loan plan (e.g., Plan 1, 2, 4, or 5).
