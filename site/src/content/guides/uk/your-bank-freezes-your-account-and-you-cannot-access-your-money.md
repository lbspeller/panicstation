# What to do if… your bank freezes your account and you cannot access your money (UK)

## Short answer
Contact your bank through an official route and ask what category of restriction is on the account and what they need from you. At the same time, stop new income being trapped and arrange a temporary way to cover food, travel, medication, housing, and other essentials.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use phone numbers or links from unexpected texts, emails, or social media messages about the freeze; use the number on your card, the bank’s official app, or the bank’s official website.
- Don’t pay a “fee” to anyone promising to unfreeze the account.
- Don’t move money through a stranger’s account, a “helper” account, or a cash-out offer.
- Don’t keep sending wages, benefits, pension payments, or refunds into the frozen account until you know whether you can use incoming money.
- Don’t ignore secure requests from the bank for ID, address, or source-of-funds evidence; delays can make the restriction last longer.
- Don’t give anyone your banking password, one-time passcodes, card PIN, or full security answers.

## What to do now
1. **Make a 48-hour essentials list.** Write down what must be covered in the next 2–3 days: food, travel to work, medication, childcare, rent or mortgage, utilities, and any urgent care costs. Use this list when you ask the bank or creditors for urgent help.

2. **Contact the bank through an official route and ask what kind of freeze it is.** Ask:
   - Is this a fraud or identity check, a compliance review, a blocked payment, or a court or legal order?
   - Are incoming payments being accepted or returned?
   - If incoming payments are accepted, will you be able to use them?
   - What exact documents or actions are needed to restore access?
   - How should you submit documents securely?
   - Can the bank allow any temporary access for essentials, such as a supervised cash withdrawal or specific essential payments?

   Ask for a reference number and a secure written summary of anything the bank can tell you.

3. **Send documents only through secure channels.** Use the bank’s app upload, secure message system, branch, or another route the bank confirms as secure. Keep copies of what you send and note the date, time, and reference number.

4. **Stop new money getting trapped.** Try to open an alternative account, such as a basic bank account if suitable. Ask your employer, benefits office, pension provider, clients, or anyone else who pays you regularly whether they can redirect payments to the alternative account or hold the next payment briefly while you set it up.

5. **If you have court or debt paperwork, identify the process and act quickly.**
   - **England and Wales:** a creditor may use an interim third party debt order to freeze money before a court hearing. Look for the hearing date, court name, claim number, and instructions for responding.
   - **Scotland:** a creditor may use bank arrestment. A protected balance usually applies, and some funds may need urgent advice if benefits or essential money are affected.

   Use the court name, case number, or creditor name from the paperwork when asking for help from the court or a debt adviser.

6. **Protect priority bills before they snowball.** Contact your landlord or agent, mortgage provider, energy supplier, council tax office, and any priority creditors. Say: “My bank account is temporarily restricted. I’m arranging an alternative way to pay. Please note this on my account and pause late action while I sort access.” Ask for confirmation in writing.

7. **Make a formal complaint if the bank is not giving a clear route forward.** Tell the bank: “I want to make a formal complaint about my account being frozen and the impact on essentials.” For many payment-service complaints, firms usually have 15 business days to send a final response, or up to 35 business days in exceptional circumstances. Other financial complaints can usually take up to 8 weeks. If you are not satisfied with the final response, or the firm does not respond in time, you can take the complaint to the Financial Ombudsman Service.

8. **If you suspect fraud, report it through the right UK route and secure your access.**
   - **England, Wales, and Northern Ireland:** report fraud and cyber crime through Report Fraud.
   - **Scotland:** report fraud to Police Scotland, usually through 101 for non-emergencies.

   Change your banking and email passwords, turn on 2-step verification where available, and check your account for unknown payees, devices, or contact details.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to switch banks permanently.
- Arguing about compensation before you have access, paperwork, and the bank’s position in writing.
- Deep-checking your credit file unless there are clear signs of identity theft now.
- Moving savings, closing accounts, or making large financial decisions before you know why the restriction happened.
- Writing a long complaint history before you have covered food, travel, housing, medication, and income redirection.

## Important reassurance
A frozen account can feel frightening because it blocks ordinary life tasks. Many restrictions are temporary and linked to fraud checks, identity checks, compliance duties, or court orders. The stabilising order is: confirm the type of freeze, use secure routes only, protect essentials, and redirect new income while the issue is reviewed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce harm and buy time. If the freeze involves suspected fraud, financial crime checks, benefits, business funds, or a court order, later decisions may need specialist debt, legal, or financial help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, debt, or professional advice. Banks may be limited in what they can explain while certain reviews are ongoing. If anyone pressures you to unlock funds quickly, asks for codes or passwords, or tells you to move money through another person’s account, treat it as a red flag and use only trusted contact routes.
