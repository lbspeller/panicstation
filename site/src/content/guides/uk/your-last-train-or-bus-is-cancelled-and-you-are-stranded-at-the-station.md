# What to do if… your last train or bus is cancelled and you are stranded at the station (UK)

## Short answer
Move into the safest staffed or well-lit place you can, then use official live information and ask transport staff if there are any. The safest reroute is usually the next official service, or an operator-authorised alternative, not an unsolicited lift or an unverified taxi.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t rush outside to “figure it out” if you can check it from a safer place first.
- Don’t accept unsolicited lifts or “cash taxis” offered in the station, stop, or forecourt.
- Don’t split your group up unless there is a clear safety reason.
- Don’t assume you can use any operator or route; check first, especially with an Advance rail ticket.
- Don’t let your phone battery run down while deciding; turn on low power mode and keep enough charge for calls, maps, tickets, and proof of disruption.

## What to do now
1. **Move to the safest waiting point before you plan anything.**  
   Go to a ticket office, customer service desk, help point, staffed platform, or a busy, well-lit area under CCTV. If the station or stop is closing and there is no safe staffed area, move to a nearby staffed public place if one is available, such as a hotel reception, open shop, or cafe, rather than waiting alone outside.

2. **Confirm what is actually running using official information.**  
   Check departure boards, the operator’s live updates, National Rail for trains, or the local operator or transport authority for buses and local transport. If staff are present, ask them to confirm the last viable route, including whether there is a rail-replacement bus, ticket acceptance on another route, or a safer interchange.

3. **Clarify your ticket situation in one sentence.**  
   Tell staff which of these applies:
   - **“I missed a connection because my earlier service was delayed or cancelled.”** You are generally allowed to continue on the next permitted train or trains to complete a rail journey, but the operator, route, and ticket restrictions may still matter, so ask staff to confirm.
   - **“I missed my first booked train before I had started travelling.”** With an Advance rail ticket, you may usually need a new ticket unless the operator tells you otherwise.

4. **Ask for an operator-authorised reroute rather than inventing one yourself.**  
   Say: “My last connection isn’t running. What is the safest way you can get me to [destination] tonight on this ticket?”  
   Ask for one of these, depending on what is available:
   - permission to take the next permitted service
   - permission to use a different route or operator, and how to show that permission
   - operator-arranged replacement transport, such as a rail-replacement bus
   - a safer staffed place or interchange to wait if the next option is much later

5. **Choose a safer pause point if the only route involves isolation.**  
   If the remaining option means a long wait at an empty stop, an unstaffed station, or a quiet interchange late at night, ask staff whether there is a safer staffed place to wait or a safer route via a busier hub, even if it takes longer.

6. **If you must use a taxi or private hire vehicle, make it verifiable.**  
   Use an official taxi rank, or book through a known provider, station staff, hotel reception, or a reputable app. For a private hire vehicle, such as a pre-booked minicab, check that the car, number plate, and driver details match the booking before you get in. If you are alone, sit in the rear seat and share your trip details with someone you trust.

7. **If you feel unsafe or are being harassed, escalate early.**  
   Move towards staff, other passengers, or a staffed public place. For non-emergency incidents on the rail network, you can text British Transport Police on 61016. Do not use 61016 for an emergency; if a crime is happening, someone is injured, threatened, or in danger, call 999.

8. **Keep proof now, so you are not scrambling later.**  
   Screenshot disruption information, keep your tickets, and keep receipts for anything you pay for because staff told you to, without assuming it will be reimbursed. Write down the time, station or stop, operator, and what staff told you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to claim compensation, request a refund, or complain.
- You do not need to optimise the cheapest route; focus on the safest workable route that actually runs.
- You do not need to argue about ticket rules on the spot; get staff to confirm what you are allowed to do and get to a safer place.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel suddenly panicky when the last connection disappears. Slowing down, getting into a safer place, and making the operator or official information confirm the next move is a sensible way to regain control.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next couple of hours: staying safer, getting reliable information, and choosing a safe reroute. Later decisions about refunds, compensation, complaints, insurance, or repeated disruption may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, safety, or professional advice. What you are allowed to do can vary by operator, route, ticket type, location, and the cause of disruption. If you feel unsafe at any point, prioritise immediate safety and contact emergency services.
