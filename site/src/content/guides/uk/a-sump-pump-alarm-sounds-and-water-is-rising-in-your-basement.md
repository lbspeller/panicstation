# What to do if… a sump pump alarm sounds and water is rising in your basement (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as an electric shock risk first: keep out of the water, keep children and pets away, and only switch off electricity if you can do it from a dry, safe place. If water is near electrics, or you cannot reach the pump and consumer unit safely, call for electrical or emergency plumbing help rather than wading in.

## Do not do these things
- Do not step into basement or cellar water if it could be touching sockets, appliances, extension leads, the pump plug, or the consumer unit area.
- Do not touch the consumer unit, plugs, appliances, pump, cables, or extension leads if you are wet, standing near water, or the area looks damp.
- Do not reset breakers repeatedly, unplug wet equipment, or use mains-powered tools in the flooded area.
- Do not go near exposed wiring, buzzing, burning smells, sparks, or equipment that has been under water.
- Do not let children or pets into the basement or cellar.
- Do not run a generator indoors, in a basement, in an attached garage, or near open doors or windows.
- Do not assume the water is clean; avoid direct contact where possible and wash hands after handling wet items.

## What to do now
1. **Make a safer pause.** Keep everyone upstairs or away from the basement entrance. Use a torch if lighting is poor, and do not use candles.
2. **Decide whether electricity could be involved.** If water may have reached sockets, plugs, appliances, the pump power supply, the meter, or the consumer unit, keep out of the water.
3. **Switch off only if it is clearly safe.** If you can reach the main switch, isolator, or consumer unit without stepping in water and the area is dry, switch off electricity to the affected area, or to the whole property if you are unsure. If you would need to enter water, or if the consumer unit, meter, or isolator area is wet, do not touch it.
4. **Call the right help for an electrical hazard.** For an electrical hazard or power cut, call your electricity network operator on 105 in England, Scotland and Wales. In Northern Ireland, call Northern Ireland Electricity Networks on 03457 643 643. If there is immediate danger to life, call 999.
5. **Check for a simple pump cause only from a dry, safe position.** If there is no standing water where you must stand and no wet electrics nearby, look for an obvious issue: a tripped RCD or breaker, a battery backup alarm, a stuck float switch, a kinked discharge hose, or a disconnected outlet pipe. Do not touch wet plugs, wet cables, or equipment that has been under water.
6. **If the pump can be restarted safely, do only the obvious reset.** Free a visibly stuck float, restore a dry switch, or activate a battery backup only if you can do it without entering water or touching wet equipment. Stop if the alarm continues, the pump hums without moving water, or water keeps rising.
7. **Call for practical flood help early.** Call an emergency plumber, drainage contractor, or flood and water-damage service and say: “Sump pump alarm, water rising in the basement or cellar, possible electrical hazard.” If you rent, or the basement is in a block, contact the landlord, freeholder, or managing agent as soon as you can.
8. **Limit damage only where it is dry and safe.** Move documents, electronics, medicines, and absorbent items upstairs or onto high shelves. From dry ground only, use towels or temporary barriers to slow water spreading to other rooms.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact pump fault right now.
- You do not need to choose a replacement pump, drainage upgrade, battery backup, or waterproofing work during the first hour.
- You do not need to start stripping carpets, cutting plasterboard, disinfecting, or using dehumidifiers until the electrics are safe and the water level is controlled.
- You do not need to complete a full insurance claim right now. Once it is safe, photos from a dry place and a brief note of what happened may help later.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel rushed when an alarm is sounding and water is rising. Slowing down around electricity is the safest move: many serious mistakes happen when someone tries to quickly unplug, reset, or wade in.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate risk and limit damage. Drying, mould prevention, electrical inspection, repairs, insurance, landlord issues, and pump replacement may need qualified or specialist help after the area is safe.

## Important note
This is general first-step information, not legal, medical, financial, electrical, insurance, or other professional advice. If electrics may have been affected by water, treat the area as unsafe until the supply and equipment have been checked by a qualified professional or your electricity network operator.
