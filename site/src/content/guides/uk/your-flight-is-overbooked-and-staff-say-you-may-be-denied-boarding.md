# What to do if… your flight is overbooked and staff say you may be denied boarding (UK)

## Short answer
Ask the gate staff immediately if you are being asked to volunteer or if you are being denied boarding involuntarily. This distinction determines whether you can negotiate your own compensation or if you are entitled to fixed statutory amounts up to £520.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t accept a voucher or cash offer to "volunteer" unless it explicitly includes confirmed rerouting and a written guarantee of duty of care (meals/hotels).
- Don’t leave the gate area or the immediate vicinity of the customer service desk until you have received a written statement of your rights and a new booking confirmation.
- Don’t assume "extraordinary circumstances" applies to overbooking; overbooking is almost always within the airline's control and qualifies for compensation.
- Don’t cancel your booking yourself in an airline app while standing at the gate, as this can be interpreted as a voluntary cancellation and may forfeit your right to assistance.
- Don’t engage in verbal confrontation; request all communication regarding the denial in writing or via email.

## What to do now
1. **Retain all travel documents and evidence of your presence at the gate.**  
   - Keep your original boarding pass and booking reference.  
   - Note the exact time you arrived at the gate and the names of the staff members you spoke with.  
   - Take a photograph of the gate screen if it shows the flight is overbooked or "oversold."

2. **Establish if the denial is voluntary or involuntary.**  
   - If you **volunteer** to stay behind, you must agree on the compensation (cash/vouchers) with the airline before giving up your seat.  
   - If you are **denied boarding against your will**, you are legally entitled to fixed compensation based on flight distance: £220 (up to 1,500km), £350 (1,500km to 3,500km), or £520 (over 3,500km).

3. **Choose between a full refund or rerouting to your destination.**  
   - You have the right to a refund of the unused parts of your ticket within 7 days.  
   - Alternatively, you can choose to be rerouted as soon as possible or at a later date that suits you.  
   - If you are mid-journey and choose a refund, you are also entitled to a flight back to your original point of departure.

4. **Request immediate "care and assistance" while you wait for a replacement flight.**  
   - If your delay exceeds 2 hours (short-haul), 3 hours (medium-haul), or 4 hours (long-haul), the airline must provide:  
     - Vouchers for a reasonable amount of food and drink.  
     - Two free phone calls or emails.  
   - If rerouting requires an overnight stay, the airline must provide hotel accommodation and transport to and from the airport.

5. **Obtain a written statement of the reason for denial.**  
   - Ask for a "denied boarding" certificate or a letter stating the flight was overbooked.  
   - Ensure the document includes your new flight details and any vouchers issued at the gate.

6. **Self-fund only as a last resort and keep every receipt.**  
   - If staff are unable to provide vouchers or book a hotel, you may pay for "reasonable" expenses yourself.  
   - Avoid "luxury" spending (e.g., five-star hotels or alcohol) as these are unlikely to be reimbursed.  
   - Keep all itemized receipts; credit card statements are often insufficient for claims.

7. **Verify baggage status for your new itinerary.**  
   - Ask if your checked bags will be automatically transferred to the new flight or if you must collect them from the arrivals hall and re-check them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file the formal compensation claim while at the airport; you generally have up to 6 years to claim in the UK.
- You do not need to calculate the exact mileage of your flight to determine the compensation bracket right now.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to accept a voucher for future travel if you prefer a cash payment later.

## Important reassurance
Being denied boarding due to overbooking is a procedural event covered by robust consumer laws. Your travel rights are protected under UK261 regulations, and the airline has a strict duty of care to ensure you are either sent home, refunded, or housed while you wait for your next flight.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for airport-based disruptions. It does not cover the specifics of code-share agreements, non-UK/EU carriers arriving from outside the UK, or long-term legal litigation against airlines.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Your eligibility for compensation depends on meeting check-in deadlines and holding valid travel documents. The cause of the denial (e.g., safety, security, or documentation issues) can change your entitlements. If the airline refuses assistance, document the refusal and seek help from the Civil Aviation Authority (CAA) or an approved Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) body.
