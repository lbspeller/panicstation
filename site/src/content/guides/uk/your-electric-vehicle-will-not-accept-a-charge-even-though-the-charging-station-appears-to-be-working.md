# What to do if… your electric vehicle will not accept a charge even though the charging station appears to be working (UK)

## Short answer
End the session, unplug the connector, wait 30 seconds, then re-insert it firmly and try an alternative activation method (RFID or app) if the first failed. If it still fails, call the 24/7 operator helpline number printed on the unit to request a remote reset.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t force a connector that is resisting or attempt to wedge objects into the port to improve contact.
- Don’t repeatedly attempt the "handshake" more than three times; some vehicles will lock the charging port after multiple failed communication attempts.
- Don’t touch any part of the charger or cable if you see smoke, sparks, or melted plastic.
- Don’t leave your vehicle in a position that blocks other bays if you have concluded the unit is faulty.
- Don’t use a domestic 3-pin "granny" cable if the socket looks scorched or the wiring appears substandard.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause if you are in active traffic.** If the charger is in a high-traffic area or poorly lit corner, ensure you are safely parked and the handbrake is on before troubleshooting.
2. **Identify immediate electrical hazards.** **If there is smoke, fire, or a loud crackling sound from the charger or your car, move away immediately and call 999.** If the charger unit has been physically smashed or has exposed wiring but is not currently sparking, call the local network operator on **105** only if the damage affects the street's electrical safety; otherwise, report it to the operator via the helpline on the unit.
3. **Perform a clean "handshake" reset.** **End any active session in your app or on the charger screen, unplug from the car, and wait at least 30 seconds for the system to clear.** Re-insert the connector firmly until it clicks, and hold the weight of the cable upward for 10 seconds while the vehicle and charger communicate (this prevents "plug sag" which causes many handshake timeouts).
4. **Check for a depressed Emergency Stop button.** **Look for a large red button on the front or side of the charger unit.** If it has been pressed in, the unit will appear "Ready" but will not deliver power; if safe to do so, twist it to release it, then wait for the charger to reboot.
5. **Rule out vehicle-side software blockers.** **Ensure the car is fully in "Park" and turned off, as many UK EVs will not initiate a charge if the ignition is still in the "on" or "accessory" position.** Check your dashboard or app for active "Charge Timers," "Departure Preconditioning," or "Battery Limit" settings that may be pausing the session.
6. **Verify the payment and network status.** **If your app or RFID card failed, try using a contactless credit/debit card if the unit is 50kW or above (as required by 2023 regulations).** Confirm the charger ID on the screen matches the one you have selected in your app.
7. **Call the 24/7 staffed helpline shown on the unit.** **UK law requires public charge point operators to provide a free, 24/7 helpline; call them and provide the unit ID and the specific error code shown.** Ask the operator to perform a "Remote Start" or "Hard Reset" of the bay, which often clears internal software glitches.
8. **Move to a different bay or charging site.** **If one bay fails, try the adjacent bay if available, as faults are frequently isolated to a single connector or power module.** If your battery is critically low, use an aggregator app (like Zap-Map or WattsUp) to find the nearest alternative network to avoid being stranded.
9. **Contact breakdown recovery if the car continues to refuse all chargers.** **If you have tried two different charging networks and the vehicle displays a "Charging System Fault," call your breakdown provider (AA, RAC, or manufacturer assistance).** Inform them you are at a public charger and are unable to take on energy, as this may require a flatbed recovery to a service centre.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to determine if the fault is with the charger’s firmware or your car's Battery Management System (BMS) right now.
- You don’t need to file a formal complaint or request a refund for a failed pre-authorisation hold while you are still on-site.
- You don’t need to check your home charging cable if you are using a tethered public rapid charger.

## Important reassurance
Charging failures are frequently caused by minor communication "handshake" errors or payment processing timeouts rather than permanent hardware damage. Most issues are resolved by switching bays or having the operator reset the unit remotely.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a failed charging session. It does not cover technical repairs, internal vehicle diagnostics, or long-term consumer rights regarding faulty infrastructure.

## Important note
This information is for guidance in a non-emergency troubleshooting scenario. If you suspect an electrical fire or see visible arcing, stay away from the vehicle and the charger and contact emergency services. EV charging involves high-voltage systems; never attempt to open a charger cabinet or repair a damaged cable yourself.
