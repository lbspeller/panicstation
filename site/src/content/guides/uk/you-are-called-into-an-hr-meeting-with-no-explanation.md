# What to do if… you are called into an HR meeting with no explanation (UK)

## Short answer
Calmly ask what the meeting is for, what process it is part of, and whether it could lead to formal disciplinary action. If it is a formal disciplinary hearing, or a grievance meeting where the right to be accompanied may apply, ask for time to prepare and to arrange a companion.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign “to make it go away” or agree to “leave today” on the spot.
- Do not sign anything you have not read and understood, including outcome letters, settlement documents, or “I agree” statements.
- Do not guess what the issue is or fill silences with speculation.
- Do not admit to things you have not been clearly told you are being asked about.
- Do not log into personal accounts, share personal passwords, or hand over personal devices just because you feel pressured. Ask what is being requested, why, and under what policy.
- Do not secretly record the meeting without thinking it through first. Workplace policies and consequences vary. If you want an accurate record, ask whether notes will be taken, whether you can have a copy, or whether everyone agrees to a recording.

## What to do now
1. **Take a short reset before you go in.** Slow down, lower your voice, and get a notebook or personal notes app ready. Avoid using a work-managed device for your own notes if you can.
2. **Make a quick timestamped note for yourself.** Record the date, time, who called you, what they said, where you were asked to go, and whether any agenda was given.
3. **At the start, ask three clarifying questions and write the answers down:**
   - “What is the purpose of this meeting?”
   - “Is this an investigation meeting, a disciplinary hearing, a grievance meeting, or something else?”
   - “Could this meeting lead to a formal warning, dismissal, or other disciplinary action?”
4. **Ask who is present and how the meeting will be recorded.** Ask whether someone will take notes or minutes, whether you can have a copy afterwards, and whether any documents being discussed can be shared with you.
5. **If it is a disciplinary hearing that could lead to formal action, ask to be accompanied.** Say: “I am making a reasonable request to be accompanied. I would like time to arrange my companion and prepare.” Your companion is usually a colleague, a workplace trade union representative who is certified by their union to act as a companion, or an official employed by a trade union.
6. **If the right to be accompanied applies and your companion cannot attend the proposed time, ask to rearrange it.** Suggest a reasonable alternative time when everyone is usually at work and available, normally within 5 working days of the original meeting.
7. **If it is a grievance meeting, ask about accompaniment clearly.** Say: “I would like to be accompanied. Please confirm whether the right to be accompanied applies here, or whether you will allow it as good practice.” This is especially important if the grievance concerns a legal or contractual duty.
8. **If it is an investigation meeting, ask anyway but do not assume the same right applies.** Say: “I would like to have a colleague or union rep present. Is that allowed under our policy?” At a disciplinary investigation meeting, there is generally no legal right to be accompanied, but employers may allow it and should explain the purpose of the meeting.
9. **Get the concern stated plainly.** Ask: “What exactly is the concern, and what dates, events, messages, or documents does it relate to?” If they only say it aloud, repeat it back and ask whether you have understood correctly.
10. **Keep your answers short and factual.** Useful phrases are:
   - “I want to cooperate, but I need the specific concern first.”
   - “I would like time to review the documents and policy before answering.”
   - “I do not recall. I would need to check records before answering.”
   - “I need a short break before I respond.”
11. **If they push you to sign something, slow it down.** Ask for a copy to take away and read. If they only want acknowledgement of receipt, write “Received on [date and time]” rather than “agreed”, and keep your own copy or photo where permitted.
12. **Before you leave, confirm next steps.** Ask: “What happens next, when will I hear back, who do I contact, and what deadline do you say applies?”
13. **Immediately afterwards, make a contemporaneous record.** Write down who was there, what was said, what you were asked, what you answered, documents shown, breaks requested, and any deadlines. Save it somewhere safe and timestamped.

## What can wait
- Deciding your long-term legal strategy, unless you are being asked to resign, sign a settlement document, leave immediately, or accept an outcome today.
- Writing a full statement before you know the exact concern and have seen the documents or policy being relied on.
- Deciding whether to raise a grievance or appeal.
- Debating motives, blame, or worst-case outcomes.

## Important reassurance
An unexplained HR meeting feels alarming because it is formal and unclear. Your aim right now is not to solve everything in the room; it is to slow the process down enough to understand what is happening, avoid irreversible mistakes, and keep a clear record.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a trade union representative, Acas, an employment adviser, or a qualified employment solicitor, depending on your contract, workplace policies, employment status, and what the meeting turns into.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, medical, or other professional advice. Workplace procedures and what is reasonable can depend on your role, contract, employer policies, employment status, and the exact process being used.
