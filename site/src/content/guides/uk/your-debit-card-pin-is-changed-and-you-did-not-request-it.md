# What to do if… your debit card PIN is changed and you did not request it (UK)

## Short answer
Freeze or block your card immediately via your banking app and contact your bank using the secure **159** hotline or the number on the back of your card. Treat any unauthorized PIN change as a potential account takeover until a bank security specialist confirms otherwise.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to use the "new PIN" at an ATM or in a shop to verify if it works.
- Do not click on links in any text or email notification regarding the PIN change.
- Do not provide one-time passcodes (OTPs) or approve app notifications to anyone claiming to be from the bank.
- Do not use a phone number provided in a suspicious text message to call your bank.
- Do not move money to a "safe account" if instructed by a caller; banks will never ask you to do this.

## What to do now
1. **Freeze your debit card immediately using your official banking app.** If you cannot access the app, proceed directly to calling your bank to request a hard block on the card and all cash withdrawals.
2. **Contact your bank safely by dialing 159 from your phone.** This secure short-code connects you to the fraud departments of over 99% of UK retail banks; if your provider is not part of the scheme, use the specific fraud number found on the back of your physical card or a recent statement.
3. **Inform the bank specialist that your PIN was changed without your authorisation.** State clearly: "I have received a notification that my PIN was changed, but I did not request this and I suspect my account is compromised."
4. **Instruct the bank to check for and remove any unrecognised devices or digital wallets.** Ask them specifically to:
   - Rescind any recent changes to your registered mobile number, email, or home address.
   - Remove your card from any digital wallets (like Apple Pay or Google Pay) that you did not personally set up.
   - De-register any new mobile devices linked to your online banking in the last 48 hours.
5. **Request a replacement card with a completely new card number.** Ensure the bank cancels the existing card permanently rather than just issuing a new PIN for the old card number.
6. **Review your transaction history for any unrecognised activity.** Look for small "test" payments, cash withdrawals, or new payees; if you find any, request that the bank opens a formal fraud investigation for reimbursement.
7. **Secure your associated email account and mobile service.** Change your email password immediately and contact your mobile network provider if you have experienced a loss of signal, as this may indicate a "SIM swap" scam used to intercept bank codes.
8. **Check your credit report for any unauthorised applications.** Use a free service to ensure no new credit accounts or "hard searches" have been performed in your name following the suspected account takeover.
9. **Report the incident to the national fraud reporting service.** 
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland: Report to **Report Fraud** at reportfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040.
   - In Scotland: Contact **Police Scotland** by calling 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to switch banks or close the entire account until the immediate fraud threat is neutralised.
- You do not need to gather physical evidence or "proof" before calling the bank; speed of blocking access is more important than documentation.
- You do not need to notify the Financial Ombudsman Service immediately; you must first allow the bank to complete their internal investigation.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an unrequested PIN change notification is a high-priority security indicator, but taking immediate action to freeze the card usually prevents further financial loss. UK banks have robust protocols for "stopping the bleeding" in these scenarios, and you are generally protected against losses from unauthorised transactions provided you act promptly.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilisation for a suspected unauthorised PIN change. It does not cover long-term identity restoration or legal proceedings.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute financial or legal advice. Regulations regarding fraud reimbursement (FCA/PSR) are subject to specific criteria; always follow the direct instructions provided by your bank's official fraud department.
