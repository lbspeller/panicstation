# What to do if… you suspect you may have a serious side effect from a vaccine or injection (UK)

## Short answer
Call **999** immediately if you experience chest pain, difficulty breathing, or sudden swelling of the face, tongue, or throat. For non-life-threatening but worsening symptoms, call **NHS 111** or use 111 online to be assessed by a clinician.

## Do not do these things
- Do not **drive yourself** to a hospital if you are feeling dizzy, faint, short of breath, or confused.
- Do not **delay seeking help** if you notice "ABC" symptoms: Airway swelling, Breathing difficulty, or Circulation issues (fainting / collapse).
- Do not take **extra doses** of medications like antihistamines or painkillers to "see if it stops" without clinical advice.
- Do not **assume the reaction is over** if symptoms settle briefly; bi-phasic reactions can occur several hours later.
- Do not **ignore chest pain or heart palpitations** following a vaccination, even if they feel mild.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate emergency triggers (ABC symptoms).**  
   **Call 999 if you experience any of the following: difficulty swallowing, a hoarse voice, swelling of the throat or tongue, gasping for air, wheezing, or persistent coughing.** These indicate a potential airway or breathing emergency (anaphylaxis).
2. **Check for cardiovascular or neurological red flags.**  
   **Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if you have chest pain, a feeling of tightness in the chest, a fast-beating or fluttering heart (palpitations), sudden confusion, or if you feel you are about to faint.** Tell the operator exactly when you received the injection and what type it was.
3. **Use your adrenaline auto-injector if you have one.**  
   **If you have been prescribed an EpiPen, Jext, or Emerade for allergies, use it immediately in the outer thigh and call 999.** Inform the dispatcher that adrenaline has been administered.
4. **Contact NHS 111 for non-emergency but concerning symptoms.**  
   **Call 111 if you have a worsening rash, a high fever (above 39°C), a severe headache that does not respond to painkillers, or significant swelling at the injection site.** Keep your vaccination record or "batch number" card nearby to provide details to the clinician.
5. **Document the reaction for medical review.**  
   **Take photos of any visible rashes or swelling and note the exact time symptoms started.** Record the site of the injection (e.g., left arm) and any other medicines you took in the 24 hours prior.
6. **Lie down or sit in a safe position.**  
   **If you feel faint, lie flat with your legs raised; if you have breathing difficulties, sit up slightly to help your airways stay open.** Do not stand up or walk around until you have been assessed.
7. **Report the incident once you are stable.**  
   **Once a clinician has seen you, report the suspected side effect to the MHRA via the Yellow Card Scheme website, the app, or by calling 0800 731 6789 (Mon-Fri, 9am-5pm).** You do not need to be certain the vaccine caused the reaction to report it.

## What can wait
- Determining the exact cause of the reaction; medical stabilization is the only priority.
- Deciding on future doses or boosters; this will be managed by a specialist later.
- Filing for the Vaccine Damage Payment Scheme (VDPS); eligibility requires a long-term assessment (at least 6 months) and can be handled later.
- Researching the batch number online or looking for similar anecdotes on social media.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel anxious when your body reacts unexpectedly to a medical treatment. Symptoms like localized swelling, mild fever, and muscle aches are common and usually resolve within 48 hours, but your decision to seek professional advice for more intense symptoms is a correct and safe response.

## Scope note
This guide is for immediate first-step stabilization and triage only. It does not replace the diagnosis of a healthcare professional or the long-term management of allergies or adverse events.

## Important note
Emergency services (999) and NHS 111 are the primary authorities for assessing vaccine reactions. Do not use this guide to "wait and see" if you are experiencing difficulty breathing or chest pain.
