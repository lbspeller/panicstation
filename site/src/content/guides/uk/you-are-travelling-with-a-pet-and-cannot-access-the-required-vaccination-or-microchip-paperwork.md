# What to do if… you are travelling with a pet and cannot access the required vaccination or microchip paperwork (UK)

## Short answer
Ask the carrier exactly which proof is missing for this route, then try to get an emailed copy from your vet or the issuing vet while you are still at the desk. Do not invent, edit, or guess any microchip, rabies, stamp, or certificate details.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess, edit, or fabricate dates, microchip numbers, stamps, or certificates.
- Do not hand over your only original paperwork out of sight unless you have taken a clear photo and noted who has it.
- Do not keep arguing at the counter before asking what exact proof, if any, they can accept right now.
- Do not assume a vaccination record alone is enough if the carrier has asked for a pet passport, animal health certificate, or other route-specific document.
- If you are not sure whether the rabies vaccination was given the same day as or after the microchip was implanted, treat that as something to verify before travelling.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the exact missing item.**  
   Ask the carrier: “Which exact document or detail is missing for this route: the microchip number, rabies vaccination entry, pet passport, animal health certificate, GB pet health certificate, or another document?” Ask whether they can accept a clear scan or direct email from the issuing vet.

2. **Get the refusal point in writing if possible.**  
   Write down the staff member’s name, the time, the route, and the exact missing proof. If they cannot board your pet, ask them to write or email the reason and the document that would fix it.

3. **Ask for a short hold while you retrieve proof.**  
   Ask whether they can put a temporary note on your booking while you contact the vet. If there is a connection or closing check-in time, ask for the latest time by which they need the proof.

4. **Contact the vet practice urgently.**  
   Ask your vet, the issuing vet, or the practice group to email the relevant record directly to you and, if the carrier agrees, directly to the carrier. Ask for the record to show the pet’s microchip number, rabies vaccination date, vaccine details, validity or booster status, and the vet or practice details. A copy may help the carrier decide, but the carrier may still require the correct original or official travel document.

5. **If you do not know the microchip number, get the pet scanned.**  
   Ask whether the port, airport, carrier, or a nearby vet can scan your pet. The scan can help match your pet to the document or vet record. Do not rely on a microchip database record alone unless the carrier or a vet says it is enough for this immediate check.

6. **Search only the places most likely to hold the proof.**
   - Search your email for the vet’s name, “rabies”, “microchip”, “AHC”, “animal health certificate”, “pet passport”, and “health certificate”.
   - Check your vet app or online portal.
   - Check pet insurance documents, as they may list the chip number.
   - Ask someone at home to photograph or scan the document and email it to you.
   - Check cloud photos, downloads, messaging apps, and shared folders for previous scans.

7. **If the carrier says only a specific original or official document is acceptable, pause the journey.**  
   Ask about rebooking, a pet-safe waiting place, and whether your pet can remain with you while you sort the documents. This is usually safer than reaching the destination or next check and being refused later.

8. **If you are entering or returning to Great Britain, assume an unresolved document problem can stop travel.**  
   Carriers and pet checkers generally have to check that pet travel rules are met. If they cannot verify the required information, treat it as a “stop and fix the paperwork” moment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to change vets, change insurers, or reorganise all pet records.
- You do not need to research every country’s pet rules right now. Focus on this route, this carrier, and the single missing proof.
- You can set up longer-term backups, printed copies, shared folders, and spare logins after your pet is safe and the immediate travel decision is made.

## Important reassurance
This is a common travel problem when records are on a phone you cannot access, in an email account you cannot open, or in paperwork left elsewhere. The safest move is to slow the situation down and obtain the exact proof being requested, rather than trying to talk your way past a document check.

## Scope note
These are first steps for being blocked at check-in, boarding, or a pet travel document check. Pet travel rules vary by route, carrier, destination, species, and your pet’s vaccination history, and later decisions may need help from a vet, the carrier, or an official pet travel service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, veterinary, travel, or professional advice. Requirements can change and can differ by destination, carrier, route, and your pet’s history. When in doubt, prioritise your pet’s welfare and get route-specific written confirmation from the carrier, a vet, or current government guidance.
