# What to do if… you receive a threat that someone will send police or emergency services to your address as a prank (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the police immediately on the non-emergency number **101** to report the threat and request that an information note be placed on your address for control room staff. This ensures that if a hoax call is made, responders have prior context about a potential "swatting" incident.

## Do not do these things
- Do not call **999** to report a threat that has not yet resulted in an arrival, as this ties up emergency lines.
- Do not engage with, taunt, or attempt to negotiate with the person making the threat.
- Do not delete any digital evidence, including messages, usernames, or timestamps.
- Do not post details of the threat or your address on social media or live streams.
- Do not run toward the door or hold any objects (including phones or controllers) in a way that could be mistaken for a weapon if police arrive.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a secure area of your property and ensure all external doors are locked.** This provides a safe pause to gather information without being immediately visible from the street.
2. **Preserve all evidence of the threat immediately.** Take screenshots of messages, including the sender's username, profile details, and the platform used. 
   - Note the exact time and date the threat was received.
   - Do not block the user until you have secured the evidence.
3. **Call the police on 101 or use the official online reporting tool for your local force.** State clearly that you have received a "swatting threat" or a threat of a "malicious hoax emergency call."
   - Provide your full address, including any building access codes or flat numbers.
   - Ask the operator to "place a flag or an information note on the CAD (Computer Aided Dispatch) system for this address."
   - Provide the names of everyone currently in the house.
4. **Inform other members of your household about the threat in a single, factual sentence.** Tell them that if emergency services arrive, they must remain calm, keep their hands visible, and follow all instructions without argument.
5. **Contact your building security or concierge if you live in managed housing.** Ask them to notify you immediately if emergency vehicles arrive, but instruct them not to interfere with any police activity.
6. **Prepare for a potential arrival by keeping your hands empty and your phone accessible but not in your hand.** If you hear responders at the door, speak through the door first to identify yourself before opening it.
7. **Follow all police instructions exactly if they arrive at your property.** 
   - Keep your hands visible at all times.
   - Say: "I am the resident. I believe this is a hoax call. I reported a threat of this on 101 earlier."
   - Do not make sudden movements or reach into pockets.
8. **Call 999 only if you see emergency responders approaching and believe there is an imminent risk of a dangerous misunderstanding.** Tell the operator: "I am at [Address], police are arriving for what I believe is a hoax call; please notify the units on the scene that I reported this threat on 101."

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the real-world identity of the person threatening you right now.
- You do not need to file platform harassment reports until after the police have taken your initial statement.
- You do not need to change your locks or install new security systems in the next few minutes.
- You do not need to decide whether you want to "press charges" during the initial 101 call.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a threat of this nature is a targeted attempt to cause panic and a loss of control. By notifying the police via 101, you are taking the most effective step to neutralise the threat's impact. The police are trained to handle hoax reports, and providing them with early information is the standard procedure for managing these incidents safely.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for receiving a threat of a hoax emergency call. It does not cover long-term cyber-security, legal action against harassers, or post-incident trauma support.

## Important note
This information is for guidance purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Police response protocols may vary by force. Always follow the direct instructions of uniformed officers on the scene. If you are in immediate danger or a crime is in progress, call 999.
