# What to do if… someone you are dating keeps pressuring you for sex after you have said no (UK)

## Short answer
Get yourself to a safer pause, away from them and preferably near other people. Repeated pressure after a clear “no” is a safety issue, not something you need to keep debating.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep negotiating, explaining, or debating your no to make them stop.
- Do not agree to sexual contact just to end the pressure, calm them down, or avoid conflict.
- Do not go somewhere private with them, such as their home, your home, a car, or a quiet room, to “talk it out”.
- Do not let them take your phone, block exits, control transport, or isolate you “for a minute”.
- Do not blame yourself for freezing, fawning, going quiet, laughing it off, or changing the subject to stay safe.
- Do not confront them in person if you feel at risk, especially if they have escalated before.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** If you are with them now, move towards other people, staff, security, a public place, or an exit. Use a plain line if you need one: “I’m leaving now.” Then leave.
2. **Get support in the moment.** Call or message someone you trust and say: “I need you to stay on the phone or help me get home. They won’t accept my no.” If you are in a venue, tell staff or security that you need help leaving safely.
3. **Use emergency help if you feel in danger.** Call **999** if a crime is in progress, you are in immediate danger, or you need urgent medical help. Call **101** if you are safe but want non-emergency police help, information, or to make an enquiry.
4. **Reduce contact immediately.** If you are not with them now, do not meet them alone to “clear the air”. Keep replies minimal, or do not reply. If you need one message, keep it short: “I said no. Do not ask again. I am not meeting you.” Then stop engaging.
5. **Keep what you already have, if it feels safe.** If you may want support or to report later, keep texts, DMs, voicemails, call logs, or screenshots that show the pressure or your no. Do not get drawn into a long exchange to create more proof.
6. **If anything sexual happened that you did not want, or you are unsure, contact specialist support.** A sexual assault referral centre or local sexual assault service can offer medical, practical, and emotional support. In England, you can use the NHS SARC finder. You do not have to report to the police to ask for help.
7. **Use a confidential support line if talking feels possible.** In England and Wales, the 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line is free and confidential for people aged 16+ on **0808 500 2222** or via online chat. In Scotland, Rape Crisis Scotland can support people affected by sexual violence. In Northern Ireland, the Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline is available 24/7. These are not emergency services; call 999 if you are in immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it “counts”, what to call it, or whether you will report anything.
- You do not need to write a perfect message, explain your reasons, or convince them to understand.
- You do not need to confront them, meet them again, or give “one last chance” to be polite.
- You do not need to make long-term relationship decisions before you are physically safer and supported.

## Important reassurance
Being pressured after you clearly said no is not a misunderstanding you caused. Many people freeze, go quiet, laugh it off, or try to appease someone to stay safe. That is a normal response to pressure or threat, not consent.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce risk and stabilise the situation. Later decisions, including safety planning, medical choices, reporting, blocking contact, housing, work, or study concerns, may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If you have urgent medical concerns, seek urgent care through 999, NHS 111 where available, or a sexual assault referral centre or local sexual assault service.
