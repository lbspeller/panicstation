# What to do if… a new authorised user appears on one of your credit accounts (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the card provider’s fraud department immediately to remove the unauthorised user, cancel any associated cards, and secure the account access credentials.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for a new statement or transaction to appear before reporting the change.
- Do not use the same password for your banking and the email address linked to that account.
- Do not contact the individual named as the new user via personal social media or phone.
- Do not close the account through an automated app feature before speaking to a fraud agent.
- Do not assume a notification about a new user is a technical glitch without verification.

## What to do now
1. **Secure your digital access immediately.**
   - **Log out** of the credit account on all devices.
   - **Change your password** for both the credit provider’s portal and your primary email account using a trusted device.
   - **Enable two-step verification (2FA)** if it is not already active on these accounts.
   - **Check your email settings** for any new "auto-forwarding" rules or "reply-to" addresses you did not set.

2. **Call the lender’s fraud department.**
   - **Dial the number on the back of your card**, the official website, or **call 159** (the secure shortcut to most UK bank fraud teams).
   - **Instruct the agent to remove the unauthorised user** and cancel any additional cards issued in that name.
   - **Request a full card replacement** with a new card number, rather than just a duplicate of the existing card.
   - **Ask for the audit trail** of the change, specifically the date, time, and method (app, phone, or post) used to add the user.
   - **Verify your contact details** on file, including your mobile number, postal address, and email.

3. **Isolate and dispute unauthorised activity.**
   - **Review all transactions** from the last 30 days and identify any items you did not personally authorise.
   - **Ask the agent to place a block** on any pending transactions or continuous payment authorities linked to the new user.
   - **Record the crime reference number** or internal case ID provided by the bank for your records.

4. **Review your credit reports for further indicators of fraud.**
   - **Access your statutory credit report** from Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion.
   - **Check the 'Financial Associations' section** for any new names linked to your profile.
   - **Search for 'Hard Searches'** or applications for credit that you did not initiate.
   - **Dispute any inaccuracies** directly with the credit reference agency and the lender involved.

5. **Report the incident to the national authorities.**
   - **If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland**, report the identity fraud to **Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) via their website or by calling 0300 123 2040.
   - **If you are in Scotland**, report the matter to **Police Scotland** by calling 101 or using their online reporting form for fraud.

6. **Apply for additional identity protection.**
   - **Register for Cifas Protective Registration** via the Cifas website; this costs £30 and places a flag on your file for 2 years, requiring lenders to perform extra verification for new applications.

## What can wait
- You do not need to close all other bank accounts immediately if there is no evidence of their compromise.
- You do not need to pay for premium credit monitoring subscriptions; statutory reports are available for free.
- You do not need to decide on long-term legal action or formal complaints in the first 24 hours.

## Important reassurance
The appearance of an unknown user on an account is a known tactic used in "account takeover" fraud. Banks have established protocols to reverse these changes and protect you from liability for transactions made by unauthorised parties. Acting quickly to notify the lender creates a legal record that protects your rights.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a financial security breach. It does not cover long-term credit repair, legal disputes over liability, or debt management strategies.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute financial or legal advice. If you are experiencing an active loss of funds, prioritise immediate contact with your financial institution and the police.
