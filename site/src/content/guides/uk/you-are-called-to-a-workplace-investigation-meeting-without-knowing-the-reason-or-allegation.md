# What to do if… you are called to a workplace investigation meeting without knowing the reason or allegation (UK)

## Short answer
Reply in writing asking what the meeting is about, whether it is an investigation meeting or a disciplinary hearing, and what information you will be given before it. If it is a disciplinary hearing, ask for the allegation, evidence, possible outcomes, and your right to be accompanied to be confirmed in writing in good time.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess what they mean or fill in the blanks; keep to what you actually know.
- Do not admit something just to make the meeting end.
- Do not sign minutes, notes, or a statement on the spot if you have not read them carefully or they are inaccurate.
- Do not delete or alter emails, messages, files, notes, rotas, or records.
- Do not secretly record the meeting. If you want an accurate record, ask to take notes, ask for a copy of the notes, or ask whether an agreed recording is possible.
- Do not discuss it widely at work. Keep it to your representative or companion, if any, and necessary support outside work.

## What to do now
1. **Email back and ask for the basics in writing.** Keep it brief and neutral. Ask:
   - what the meeting is about
   - whether it is an investigation meeting, a disciplinary hearing, a grievance meeting, or something else
   - the date, time, location, and who will attend
   - who is leading the meeting and who is taking notes
   - whether you can be accompanied and how to arrange this
   - whether they will share any documents, messages, statements, or records they want to discuss
   - whether the meeting can be rearranged if you have not been given reasonable notice to take part properly
2. **Check your workplace policy quickly.** Look at your handbook, contract, intranet, or disciplinary and investigation procedure. Note:
   - what the policy says they should provide before an investigation meeting
   - what the policy says about companions at investigation meetings
   - what the policy says about recording, confidentiality, and note-taking
3. **Sort out support based on what type of meeting it is.**
   - **Investigation meeting about a possible disciplinary issue:** there is usually no legal right to be accompanied, but it is still reasonable to ask, and many employers allow it.
   - **Disciplinary hearing:** if the meeting could give or confirm a formal warning or other disciplinary action, you generally have a legal right to be accompanied by a trade union representative, a certified union companion, or a work colleague.
   - **Grievance meeting:** if the employer says it is a grievance meeting, ask what companion rights apply under the policy and the Acas Code.
   - If you are in a union, contact your rep and forward the invite.
4. **Write a one-page facts-only timeline for yourself.** Include:
   - dates and times you remember
   - who was present
   - what was said or done
   - relevant emails, messages, rotas, instructions, documents, or policies you can point to
   - questions you need answered before you can respond properly
5. **At the start of the meeting, ask them to define the concern.** If they still will not tell you what it is about, say calmly:
   - you want to cooperate
   - you cannot respond meaningfully without knowing the concern or allegation
   - you are asking for the meeting to be paused and rearranged once you have a written summary and reasonable notice
6. **Answer carefully and keep options open.**
   - Use plain phrases such as: “I do not recall,” “I would like to check my records,” “Can you clarify the timeframe?” and “Can you show me the policy or document you mean?”
   - Take your own notes.
   - Ask for a break if you feel rushed, confused, or need to check something.
7. **Control the paper trail after the meeting.**
   - Ask for the notes or any witness statement you are expected to confirm.
   - Ask for time to check it before signing or agreeing it is accurate.
   - As soon as you reasonably can, send a short email confirming what you understood the concern to be, what information you requested, and any important corrections to the notes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, raise a grievance, or get a solicitor.
- You do not need to write a long defence statement before you know the allegation or concern and what process they are using.
- You do not need to decide your whole strategy before you have the meeting invite, the basic details in writing, and any documents they say they will rely on.

## Important reassurance
Being called to a meeting without clear information is unsettling. Asking for the reason in writing, reasonable notice, and any companion rights is a normal way to slow the process down and reduce misunderstandings.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation, protect the record, and reduce avoidable mistakes in the initial meeting or meetings. Later decisions may need specialist help based on your contract, workplace policy, employment status, and what the allegation actually is.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Workplace procedures and contracts vary. If you are unsure about the process or your rights, your union, Acas, or a UK workplace advice service can help you interpret your specific documents and situation.
