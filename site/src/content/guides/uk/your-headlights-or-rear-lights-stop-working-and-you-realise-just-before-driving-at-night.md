# What to do if… your headlights or rear lights stop working and you realise just before driving at night (UK)

## Short answer
Do not drive on public roads during the "hours of darkness" (half an hour after sunset to half an hour before sunrise) if any obligatory lamp is non-functional. Park in a safe location, manually verify all light settings, and use an alternative method of transport if the issue is not a simple bulb or fuse.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume daytime running lights (DRLs) provide sufficient rear visibility; they typically do not activate rear lamps.
- Do not attempt to drive using only sidelights (parking lights), even in well-lit 30 mph zones; while technically the legal minimum in some specific lit areas, it is unsafe and often results in a police stop for lack of roadworthiness.
- Do not drive "just a short distance" to get home; a single failed headlight or tail light makes you less visible and is a primary cause for Fixed Penalty Notices.
- Do not toggle light switches repeatedly while the vehicle is in motion to try and "shock" the lights on.
- Do not allow a passenger to stand in the road or behind the vehicle to check lights if you are near moving traffic.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the vehicle in a safe, stationary position.**  
   **Ensure you are parked off the main carriageway or in a well-lit parking area before investigating.** If you are forced to stop in a precarious position, turn on your hazard warning lights and exit the vehicle toward a safe verge or pavement if visibility is low.
2. **Manually override the "Auto" lighting setting.**  
   **Turn the lighting dial or stalk to the "On" or "Dipped Beam" position rather than relying on sensors.** Walk around the vehicle to check the following:
   - Both front dipped beams.
   - Both front sidelights.
   - Both rear tail lights.
   - The rear number plate light.
3. **Clean all light lenses with a cloth or tissue.**  
   **Wipe away road grime, salt, or mud that may be obscuring the bulb's output.** In some cases, a "failed" light is simply a lamp so dirty it appears unlit from a distance.
4. **Determine if the failure is isolated or systemic.**  
   **Identify if only one bulb is out or if an entire circuit (e.g., both headlights) has failed.** 
   - One bulb out: Usually a simple bulb failure; do not drive at night until replaced.
   - Both bulbs out simultaneously: Likely a fuse, relay, or switch failure; this is a major safety defect.
5. **Check the relevant fuse only if you have the tools and a spare.**  
   **Consult the vehicle owner's manual to locate the lighting fuse box and identification diagram.** Only replace a fuse with one of the exact same Ampere (A) rating; if the new fuse blows immediately, stop and do not attempt further electrical work.
6. **Execute a "safe plan" for the journey.**  
   **Abandon the drive and use a taxi, public transport, or a lift from a friend.** If you are away from home, contact a breakdown recovery service; many policies cover "lighting failure at night" as a safety-critical breakdown.
7. **Secure the vehicle for the night.**  
   **If the vehicle must be left on a public road with a speed limit over 30 mph, you must ensure your sidelights remain on while parked.** If you are in a safe driveway or car park, turn all lights off to avoid draining the battery.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific root cause (e.g., alternator vs. wiring) right now.
- You do not need to buy specialized bulbs or tools tonight if a taxi or alternative is available.
- You do not need to report the failure to any authority; it is a maintenance issue, not a crime, provided you do not drive the vehicle.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for bulbs to fail upon startup due to the initial voltage surge. While it is frustrating to cancel plans, noticing the failure now has prevented a potentially dangerous encounter with unlit obstacles or a £100 Fixed Penalty Notice from the police.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a vehicle lighting failure discovered while stationary. It does not cover in-depth electrical diagnostics or roadside mechanical repairs which require professional certification.

## Important note
Under the Road Vehicles Lighting Regulations 1989, all obligatory lamps must be kept in good working order and clean when a vehicle is used on a road at night. Failure to comply can result in a Fixed Penalty Notice, penalty points, or a court fine of up to £1,000 for cars. If you are in doubt about the legality or safety of your vehicle's lighting, do not drive it until it has been inspected by a qualified technician.
