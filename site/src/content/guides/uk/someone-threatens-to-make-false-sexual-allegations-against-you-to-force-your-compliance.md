# What to do if… someone threatens to make false sexual allegations against you to force your compliance (UK)

## Short answer

Treat this as a threat and a pressure tactic, not something you need to solve by obeying. Put immediate safety first, stop negotiating if you safely can, and preserve the messages exactly as they are.

## Do not do these things

- Do not send money, sexual images, passwords, account access, or a rushed apology just to make the threat stop.
- Do not delete messages, voicemails, call logs, emails, social media chats, usernames, or profile links.
- Do not alter screenshots, crop out context, or tidy your phone before preserving what is there.
- Do not meet them alone to “sort it out”.
- Do not retaliate, threaten back, or post about them publicly.
- Do not give a long defensive explanation to police, work, university, or another organisation before you have had a moment to steady yourself and get advice.

## What to do now

1. Get to a safer pause. If you feel at risk of immediate harm, or the threat is happening now and you need urgent police help, call 999. If you call 999 from a mobile and cannot speak, listen to the operator and press 55 or tap when prompted.

2. Preserve the threat as it is. Take screenshots showing dates, times, usernames, phone numbers, profile links, and the full demand. Save voicemails and emails. Keep payment requests, account names, and any message where they connect the demand to the threatened allegation.

3. Back up a copy somewhere the other person cannot access. Send copies to a trusted person or save them to an account, device, or storage location that the person does not know about and cannot sign into.

4. Stop bargaining if you safely can. Do not argue about whether the threatened allegation is true or false. Do not agree to demands. If you need to send one message for immediate safety, keep it short and practical, such as saying you are not discussing this further by message.

5. Tell one trusted person now. Ask them to keep a copy of the messages and stay available in case the person contacts your workplace, university, family, friends, or the police.

6. Report the threat if there is a demand, ongoing harassment, stalking, pressure to do something against your will, or fear that the person may act on it. For non-emergencies in the UK, you can usually call 101 or use your local police force’s online reporting route. If it is happening now or you are in immediate danger, use 999.

7. If the threat is online, cyber-enabled, or tied to money, Report Fraud may also be relevant in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland. If you are in Scotland, use Police Scotland through 101 for non-emergency reporting.

8. If the threat is tied to sexual images or videos, do not send more images and do not pay to stop sharing. Report the account on the platform, keep the report confirmation, and use a specialist intimate-image abuse service for help with takedowns and account safety.

9. If police, your employer, university, regulator, or another institution contacts you about an allegation, keep your first response short. Say you need a short opportunity to get advice before giving a detailed statement, and that you are preserving relevant messages.

10. If this is connected to a partner, ex-partner, family member, sexual coercion, or controlling behaviour, contact a domestic abuse service for support with safety planning. You do not have to decide today whether to make any wider report.

## What can wait

You do not need to decide today whether to make a full complaint, confront them, explain everything to everyone, or work out how this ends. You also do not need to build a perfect timeline right now. First preserve what exists, reduce the pressure, and get support around you.

## Important reassurance

Threats like this are often designed to create panic, shame, and rushed compliance. Feeling shocked, frozen, or afraid of not being believed does not mean you have done anything wrong. Slowing the situation down is a sensible first move.

## Scope note

These are first steps only. Later decisions about reporting, workplace or university processes, online takedown requests, and legal advice may need specialist help.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Exact options can differ depending on where in the UK you are, whether the threat is from a partner or ex-partner, whether it is online, and whether sexual images, stalking, or immediate safety risks are involved.
