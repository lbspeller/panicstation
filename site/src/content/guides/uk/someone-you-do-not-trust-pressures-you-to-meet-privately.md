# What to do if… someone you do not trust pressures you to meet privately (UK)

## Short answer
You do not have to agree to a private meeting. Create time and distance now: refuse the private meet, move the conversation into writing, and tell someone you trust what is happening.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree “just to end the conversation” or to calm them down.
- Don’t meet them alone, in a private place, in a car, or at either of your homes.
- Don’t let them control your transport, pick you up, give you a lift, or move you “somewhere else”.
- Don’t share your address, routine, availability, travel plans, or real-time location to reassure them.
- Don’t get pulled into long calls where you feel cornered; don’t argue your reasons.
- Don’t delete messages, voicemails, call logs, or screenshots that show pressure, threats, or repeated contact.

## What to do now
1. **Send one line that blocks the private meeting and buys time.**  
   Example: “I’m not meeting privately. If it’s important, put it in writing.”

2. **Move the conversation to writing and stop live negotiation.**  
   If they call, you can let it go to voicemail and reply once in writing: “I can’t talk by phone. Message me.”

3. **Tell a trusted person now so you are not handling it alone.**  
   Message or call someone you trust. Share who it is, what they asked, where and when they wanted to meet, and why you feel pressured. Send screenshots so your support person has the details.

4. **Reduce the information you give them.**  
   Don’t give your location, availability, home address, workplace pattern, or travel details. If they keep pushing, repeat the same sentence without adding reasons: “I’m not meeting privately.”

5. **If you choose any meeting at all, set conditions you control, or do not meet.**  
   - **Place:** a busy public location you choose, not “somewhere quiet”.
   - **Time:** daytime and time-limited, such as “15 minutes”.
   - **People:** bring someone, or have someone nearby and expecting you.
   - **Transport:** arrive and leave independently.
   - **Check-in:** give a trusted person the venue address and time, and agree when you will message or call them.

6. **Start a simple record while it is fresh.**  
   Save screenshots, voicemails, call logs, usernames, and messages. Note dates, times, and a short factual summary of what was said, especially threats, implied consequences, secrecy, or repeated pressure.

7. **If you feel unsafe, contact the police.**  
   - **Immediate danger:** call **999**.
   - **Not an emergency:** call **101** or use an online police reporting route.
   - **If you are in danger but cannot speak:** call **999** if you can. From a mobile, press **55** or tap when prompted if you cannot speak.

8. **If this is connected to work, education, housing, volunteering, or another organisation, use a formal route instead of a private meet.**  
   Ask to communicate through a manager, HR, student services, building security, reception, or a safeguarding lead if there is one. Keep it in writing. Do not let the person redirect that process back into a private meeting.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to confront them, explain properly, or meet to “clear the air”.
- You do not need perfect wording; one repeatable sentence is enough.
- You do not need to decide right now whether to block them. Sometimes it is safer to pause, limit contact, and keep records first.
- You do not need to decide about making a formal report unless you want to or you feel at risk; focus first on distance, witnesses, and documentation.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressured is a useful warning signal. It is allowed to refuse, change your mind, keep things public, and keep things documented without proving your reasons.

## Scope note
This guide covers first steps to reduce immediate risk and prevent a pressured private meeting. Later decisions, such as formal complaints, ongoing safety planning, legal options, or specialist support, can come later if needed.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you believe you are at risk of harm, prioritise getting to a safer situation and contacting emergency services.
