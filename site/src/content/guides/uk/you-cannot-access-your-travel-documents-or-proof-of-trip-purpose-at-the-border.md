# What to do if… you cannot access your travel documents or proof of trip purpose at the border (UK)

## Short answer
Stay calm, answer truthfully, and ask if you may take a short moment to retrieve your documents, such as by charging your phone, connecting to Wi-Fi, or logging in. If that is not possible quickly, offer any alternative evidence or verifiable details you can access and ask what evidence they need from you first.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t guess, exaggerate, or improvise details to fill gaps; inconsistent answers can make things harder.
- Don’t show altered, borrowed, or unverifiable documents, including screenshots you cannot explain.
- Don’t argue, joke, or get sarcastic about checks.
- Don’t delete emails, messages, bookings, or files while you are being questioned.
- Don’t sign anything you do not understand in a rush; ask what it is, read it, and ask for clarification before you decide.

## What to do now
1. **Say clearly what has happened in one sentence.**  
   Example: “My supporting documents are in my email or cloud storage, but I cannot access them because my phone has no charge, I am locked out, or there is no signal.”

2. **Ask politely if you may try one quick fix.**  
   Ask if you may connect to airport Wi-Fi, use a charging point, open your email, or step aside briefly to log in. If the officer says no, do not argue; move to what you can provide now.

3. **Give the main trip details you know without guessing.**  
   Keep your answers simple:
   - why you are coming to the UK
   - where you plan to stay
   - how long you plan to stay
   - how you will pay for the trip
   - when and how you plan to leave

4. **Offer alternative proof that can be checked.**  
   Give details you can provide from memory or from anything still accessible:
   - accommodation name, address, booking name, and dates
   - host, employer, event organiser, school, or meeting contact name and phone number
   - return or onward travel airline, flight number, date, or booking reference
   - bank card, travel money, or a way to show funds if you can access banking
   - invitation, event, course, meeting, or itinerary details you can describe accurately

5. **Suggest a verification route that does not depend only on your phone.**  
   If the officer allows it, ask whether your hotel, host, employer, travel organiser, or school can confirm your plans by email or phone. If you are travelling with someone, ask whether you may use their device to access the same booking or confirmation.

6. **Slow down if stress or language could make you inaccurate.**  
   Ask for the question to be repeated, ask for a moment to think, or ask whether an interpreter or language support is available. It is better to pause than to guess.

7. **If you are given paperwork, keep it.**  
   Ask what the document means, ask for time to read it, and keep any written decision, refusal notice, detention notice, or other papers with your travel records.

## What can wait
- You do not need to search every app or account at once; start with the core points: purpose, accommodation, funds, and leaving the UK.
- You do not need to make big travel decisions while you are being questioned, such as cancelling the trip, changing hotels, or rebooking flights, unless an officer or your airline tells you it is necessary.
- You do not need to give long explanations unless you are asked for them.
- You do not need to contact everyone at once; choose the person or organisation most likely to confirm your trip quickly.

## Important reassurance
Access problems can happen at the worst moment. Calm, consistent answers and a practical route to retrieve or verify your documents are more useful than frantic searching or over-explaining.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment you are being questioned at the UK border. If you are refused entry, detained, or your situation is complex, you may need specialist immigration advice afterwards.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Border decisions can be discretionary and may depend on factors such as your nationality, immigration status, travel history, documents, answers, and the officer’s assessment. If you do not understand what you are being asked to do or sign, it is reasonable to ask for clarification and time to read it.
