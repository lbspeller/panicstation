# What to do if… a last-minute reroute adds a transit stop and you are unsure whether extra documents are required (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the transit country's entry rules for your specific passport and obtain confirmation from the airline that you meet all requirements to board the new itinerary.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume that staying in the airport exempts you from document requirements; many countries require a visa or electronic authorization for airside transit.
- Do not rely on verbal assurances from other passengers or unofficial travel forums.
- Do not pay for new visas or flights until you have confirmed the exact requirements for the current reroute.
- Do not hand your passport to unauthorized individuals offering to "fix" your documents outside of official airline or government desks.
- Do not attempt to board if the airline has flagged a document discrepancy, as this may lead to being stranded at an intermediate stop without support.

## What to do now
1. **Document the full details of the new itinerary immediately.** Write down every stop, the specific airport and terminal names, connection times, and whether the flight numbers have changed.
2. **Determine if the transit is airside or landside.**
   - **Airside transit:** You stay behind passport control and do not collect checked baggage. This is only possible at specific airports like London Heathrow or Manchester.
   - **Landside transit:** You must pass through passport control to change terminals, collect/re-check baggage, or stay in a hotel. Treat all Gatwick or overnight connections as landside by default.
3. **Use the GOV.UK "Check if you need a UK visa" tool if the stop is in the UK.**
   - **Select "Transit"** as the reason for your visit.
   - **Confirm your nationality** and whether you will pass through passport control.
   - **Note if you require a Direct Airside Transit Visa (DATV)** for stays under 24 hours or a Visitor in Transit visa for stays up to 48 hours.
4. **Check the status of your Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA) if you are a visa-exempt national.** As of 2026, most non-visa nationals (including those from the US, Australia, and the EU) require a valid ETA to transit the UK, even if remaining airside.
5. **Contact the airline to confirm your "Clearance to Board."** Use the airline's official app or service desk and ask specifically:
   - "Does my passport nationality require a transit visa or ETA for this specific airport and terminal?"
   - "Are my bags checked through to the final destination, or must I clear passport control to retrieve them?"
6. **Request a different reroute if document requirements cannot be met in time.** If you cannot obtain a required visa or ETA before departure, ask the airline to find a route that avoids that specific transit country.
7. **Keep digital and physical copies of your essential documents accessible.** Ensure you have your passport bio-page, onward flight ticket, and any existing visas for your final destination ready for inspection at the boarding gate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to research the general tourist rules for the transit country unless you intend to leave the airport.
- You do not need to settle compensation claims for the reroute until you have reached your final destination.
- You do not need to update your travel insurance immediately, provided the reroute is handled by the airline.

## Important reassurance
Rerouting is a standard operational procedure for airlines. While the sudden change in document requirements is stressful, most transit questions can be resolved by checking official government tools or speaking with airline transit desks. Passport and gate agents deal with these queries daily.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for urgent transit document uncertainty. It does not cover long-term visa applications, complex dual-nationality issues, or travel on refugee documents, which may require consultation with an embassy.

## Important note
Transit and entry regulations are subject to change without notice and vary based on nationality, terminal facilities, and local laws. Airlines have the final authority to refuse boarding if they determine a passenger does not meet the entry or transit requirements of any country on the itinerary.
