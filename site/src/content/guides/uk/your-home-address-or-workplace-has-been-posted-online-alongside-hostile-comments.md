# What to do if… your home address or workplace has been posted online alongside hostile comments (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a real-world safety issue first: move to a safer pause, tell someone nearby or at work, and contact police on 999 if there is immediate danger or 101 if it is not an emergency but you feel threatened.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t reply publicly, argue with the poster, or invite people to debate it.
- Don’t correct the post by sharing more details about yourself, your home, your workplace, your routine, or your family.
- Don’t meet, confront, threaten, or try to identify the person yourself.
- Don’t delay calling 999 because you are collecting screenshots.
- Don’t delete everything in a panic if it is the only record of what happened.
- Don’t treat it as “just online” when your home address or workplace has been exposed.

## What to do now
1. **Check immediate danger first.**  
   If someone is nearby, a threat is happening now, the post is calling for people to come to your home or workplace, or you think the situation could become violent soon, call **999**. Move away from doors, windows, reception areas, or predictable entry and exit points if you can do that safely.

2. **Tell one real-world person now.**  
   Ask a trusted person to stay with you, meet you, or stay on the phone while you make the next steps. If you are at work, tell your manager, HR, security, or reception that your workplace has been posted online with hostile comments.

3. **Ask for practical workplace safety changes.**  
   If your workplace is involved, ask reception, security, or your manager not to confirm your schedule, contact details, or whether you are on site to unknown callers or visitors. Ask them to follow visitor procedures strictly and to help you avoid predictable arrival or leaving routines for now.

4. **Make a short record if it is safe to do so.**  
   Take screenshots showing the post, the address or workplace detail, the hostile comments, the account name, the platform or website, the URL, and the date and time you saw it. Stop after you have the essentials; do not keep scrolling through hostile comments.

5. **Report the post on the platform or website.**  
   Use the platform’s reporting tools for harassment, threats, and sharing personal information. If it is on a forum or standalone website, use its contact or abuse route and ask for the home address, workplace details, or other personal information to be removed.

6. **Report to police if you feel threatened, targeted, or unsafe.**  
   If it is not an emergency, call **101** or use your local police force’s online reporting route. Say that your home address or workplace has been posted publicly with hostile comments, and describe any threats, repeated contact, calls to harass you, or people contacting your workplace.

7. **Reduce visibility without trying to fix the whole internet today.**  
   You can ask search engines to remove results that expose personal data in some cases, but this usually reduces search visibility rather than removing the page itself. You can also ask organisations that hold your personal data to delete it in some circumstances, but this is not automatic and can wait until the immediate safety steps are done.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to take legal action.
- You do not need to write a full public statement.
- You do not need to remove every trace from the internet in one sitting.
- You do not need to change your phone number, move home, or make major life changes unless police, workplace security, or another appropriate specialist advises it for a specific risk.
- You do not need to investigate who posted it before asking for help.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaken is a normal response when your address or workplace is exposed alongside hostility. The useful first move is not to win the argument online; it is to reduce the chance of real-world contact, preserve the basic record, and get support around you.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours or day. If the targeting continues, later decisions may need help from police, workplace security or HR, a solicitor, a data protection adviser, or a specialist victim support service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call **999**; if it is not an emergency but you feel threatened or targeted, contact police on **101** or through your local police force’s online reporting route.
