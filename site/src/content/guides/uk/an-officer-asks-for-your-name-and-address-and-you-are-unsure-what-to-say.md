# What to do if… an officer asks for your name and address and you are unsure what to say (UK)

## Short answer
Slow the moment down and ask: “Am I being detained, or am I free to go?” If they say they require your name and address, ask what power they are using; if they confirm it is required, give correct details and do not guess or make anything up.

## Do not do these things
- Do not give a false name or address to make the situation end faster.
- Do not guess an old address, a nickname, or details you are not sure are correct.
- Do not volunteer extra details beyond what is being required, such as where you are going, who you are meeting, or where you are staying tonight.
- Do not argue about legal technicalities on the street.
- Do not walk away if you have been clearly told you are detained.
- Do not sign something you do not understand just to end the moment; ask what it is, whether signing is required, and ask for a copy.

## What to do now
1. Ask the key question first: “Am I being detained, or am I free to go?”

2. If they say you are free to go, you can leave calmly. If you choose to stay and talk, keep your answers short.

3. If they say you are detained, ask: “Are you requiring my name and address? What power are you using?”

4. If the officer or PCSO gives a named power, ask the narrow follow-up: “Does that power require me to give my name and address?”

5. If the situation is about driving, a vehicle, insurance, MOT, or a driving licence, give your correct name and address when required and follow instructions about producing documents.

6. If they mention anti-social behaviour and Section 50 in England or Wales, ask them to confirm that they are requiring your name and address under that power. If they confirm this, give your correct name and address.

7. If it is a stop and account or stop and search, you may be asked for your details, but you are generally not required to give your name and address just for the search record unless another lawful power or suspected offence is being relied on. Ask: “Am I legally required to give this, or am I free to go?”

8. If you give details, keep it minimal and accurate: “My name is ____. My address is ____.” Then stop.

9. If you are told you are not required to provide details and you do not want to, say: “If I am not legally required to give my details, I would prefer not to. Am I free to leave?”

10. If you are arrested or taken to a police station, say: “I want legal advice.” Then avoid answering questions about what happened until you have had legal advice.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain or challenge the stop.
- You do not need to prove your rights on the street.
- You do not need to explain your full situation to make the interaction end.
- You do not need to remember every legal rule; the immediate task is to ask whether you are detained, ask what power is being used, and avoid giving false details.

## Important reassurance
Going blank, shaking, or feeling pressured is a normal stress response. You are allowed to pause, ask simple questions, and repeat the same calm sentence instead of filling the silence.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the moment you are asked for your name and address. Police powers and duties can vary by situation and across England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, so later decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. If you are detained, arrested, or worried that you may be suspected of an offence, ask for legal advice as soon as you can.
