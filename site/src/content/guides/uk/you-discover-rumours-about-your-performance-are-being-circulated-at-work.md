# What to do if… you discover rumours about your performance are being circulated at work (UK)

## Short answer
Move to a safe location, put your complaint and the evidence of escalation in writing immediately, and formally request interim safety measures to isolate you from the source of the behaviour while the investigation proceeds.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to confront the individual alone or resolve the matter in a private or unstaffed setting.
- Do not post details of the incident or the HR delay on social media or send group emails to colleagues.
- Do not delete any evidence, including messages, call logs, emails, or handwritten notes.
- Do not resign or sign any informal agreements or "settlements" while the situation is active and escalating.
- Do not record conversations without first checking your specific workplace policy on covert recordings.
- Do not assume that an HR delay means the behaviour is acceptable or that you have no further options.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritise your immediate physical safety if the behaviour involves threats or physical proximity.**  
   Move to a public or well-staffed area of the workplace or leave the premises if you believe you are at risk.
   - If there is an immediate threat of violence, call **999**.
   - For non-emergency criminal behaviour such as stalking or historical assault, call **101**.

2. **Update your contemporaneous incident log with the most recent escalations.**  
   Record every instance of unwanted behaviour, including the specific date, time, location, and any witnesses present.
   - Describe exactly what was said or done using factual language.
   - Note your response and how the behaviour has changed or worsened since your last report to HR.
   - Identify any digital evidence such as Slack messages, emails, or CCTV locations.

3. **Send a formal written escalation to HR and a senior manager today.**  
   Title the email "Urgent Escalation: Repeated Unwanted Behaviour and Request for Interim Protections" to ensure it is flagged as a priority.
   - State clearly that the behaviour is repeated and is now escalating.
   - List 3 to 5 specific examples of the most recent incidents with dates.
   - Reference your previous reports and note the length of the current delay in action.
   - As of April 2026, if the behaviour involves sexual harassment, explicitly state that you are making a "protected disclosure" (whistleblowing).

4. **Request specific interim measures to ensure your safety during the investigation.**  
   Do not wait for HR to suggest solutions; propose clear boundaries that can be implemented immediately.
   - Request a "no-contact" instruction between you and the other party.
   - Ask for a temporary change in shift patterns, desk location, or reporting lines.
   - Request that any necessary interactions are moderated by a third party.

5. **Trigger a formal grievance if you have previously only used informal channels.**  
   Submit a formal letter of grievance and state that you wish for it to be handled under the organisation’s formal grievance procedure in line with the ACAS Code of Practice.
   - Ask for written confirmation of receipt and a timeline for the investigation meeting.
   - If the employer fails to follow the ACAS Code, any future tribunal award could be increased by up to 25 percent.

6. **Exercise your legal right to be accompanied if a meeting is scheduled.**  
   Notify HR in writing that you wish to bring a companion to any formal grievance or investigation meetings.
   - You have a statutory right to be accompanied by a colleague or a trade union representative.
   - If you have a disability, request the right to bring a support worker or family member as a reasonable adjustment.

7. **Contact external support services for guidance on your specific rights.**  
   - **Acas Helpline**: Call **0300 123 1100** for free advice on grievance procedures and employer obligations.
   - **Equality Advisory and Support Service (EASS)**: Call **0808 800 0082** if the behaviour involves discrimination or harassment related to a protected characteristic (e.g., sex, race, disability).
   - **Trade Union**: Contact your local representative or head office if you are a member.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to take the case to an Employment Tribunal today; the focus is on immediate stabilization.
- You do not need to have a perfectly drafted legal statement ready right now; a factual list of incidents is sufficient for the initial escalation.
- You do not need to determine the "motive" or "intent" of the person engaging in the behaviour; focus on the impact and the facts of the conduct.
- You do not need to respond to "informal" settlement offers or "quiet chats" intended to bypass the formal process.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard and expected reaction to feel a sense of heightened alertness or distress when workplace boundaries are repeatedly ignored. Taking steps to document the escalation and requesting formal protection is a constructive and necessary response to ensure your safety and professional standing.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an escalating workplace crisis in the UK. It does not cover long-term legal strategy, constructive dismissal claims, or specific tribunal procedures, which require professional legal or union advice.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment laws, including the Worker Protection Act and the Employment Rights Act 2025, impose specific duties on employers to prevent harassment. If you feel your physical safety is at risk, prioritise your personal security and contact emergency services before following internal workplace procedures.
