# What to do if… someone is trying to follow you or others into your secure building (UK)

## Short answer
Do not let them in. Step back, let the communal door close and lock, and move to a safer position; if you feel threatened, they force entry, or a crime is happening now, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hold the door open “to be polite” or use your fob, key, or access code for them.
- Do not go into a stairwell, lift, bin store, or other enclosed area with them.
- Do not physically block, grab, chase, or try to restrain them.
- Do not argue at close range or keep replying once you have set a boundary.
- Do not follow them deeper into the building if they slip in.
- Do not share door codes, fob details, or resident information.

## What to do now
1. **Create space and let the door secure.** Step back, keep the door between you and them if you can, and let the communal door close and lock behind you.
2. **Move to a safer position.** Go outside, behind a locked private door, near concierge or staff, or into a nearby open shop or public place if you feel unsafe.
3. **Use one firm, neutral line if it is safe.** Say: “Sorry, I can’t let anyone in. Please use your own key or buzz the flat you’re visiting.” Say it once, then stop engaging.
4. **If another resident is holding the door, speak briefly from a safe distance.** Say: “Please don’t hold it for someone you don’t know.” Then move away rather than debating it.
5. **Redirect them to the proper access route.** Point to the intercom, entryphone, concierge, security desk, or the resident they say they are visiting, without granting access yourself.
6. **Call 999 if there is immediate danger.** This includes violence or threats, a forced entry, someone trying doors, a crime happening now, or someone being in immediate danger. Give the exact address, entrance, floor if known, description, direction of travel, and what they are doing.
7. **Use a non-emergency route if there is no immediate danger.** Call 101 or use the relevant police online reporting route if the incident has passed but should still be reported.
8. **Record only safe details.** Note the time, door used, clothing or appearance, what was said, and where they went. Do not take photos or video if that would keep you close to them or escalate the situation.
9. **Report any access fault urgently.** Tell the managing agent, landlord, concierge, or building management if the communal door did not latch, the lock failed, the intercom failed, or a door code may have been shared.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether this was definitely criminal, suspicious, confused, or harmless.
- You do not need to confront them, prove their intent, or investigate inside the building.
- You do not need to solve building-wide security today.
- You do not need to write a perfect report before you are safe.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel awkward refusing someone at a shared entrance. In a controlled-access building, letting the door close and asking people to use their own key, fob, intercom, or host is a reasonable safety boundary.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment you notice a possible unauthorised entry. Repeated incidents, faulty access systems, or disputes with building management may need later help from the managing agent, landlord, freeholder, police, or another specialist service.
