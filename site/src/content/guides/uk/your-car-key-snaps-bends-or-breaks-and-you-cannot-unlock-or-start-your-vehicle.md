# What to do if… your car key snaps, bends, or breaks and you cannot unlock or start your vehicle (UK)

## Short answer
Stop forcing the key. Make the place safe first, then use a spare key, your breakdown provider, or an auto locksmith so the lock or ignition is not damaged further.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep twisting, yanking, or “trying a bit harder” with a bent or broken key.
- Don’t push a broken fragment deeper into the door lock or ignition with tools or another key.
- Don’t put household oil, grease, glue, or random sprays into the lock or ignition.
- Don’t work on the key while standing close to moving traffic.
- Don’t attempt repairs on a motorway or other high-speed road.
- Don’t let someone force, drill, or dismantle the lock or ignition unless you understand what they are doing and you trust them.

## What to do now
1. **Make the situation safe first.**
   - If you are near moving traffic, switch on hazard lights if you can and move yourself and passengers to a safer place before focusing on the key.
   - If you are on a motorway or high-speed road, get away from the vehicle and traffic if it is safe to leave the vehicle.
   - If you cannot get to a safer place, cannot leave the vehicle safely, are in a live lane, or feel at immediate risk, call 999 and ask for the police.

2. **Stop using the damaged key blade.**
   - If the remote or fob still unlocks the vehicle, use the fob and avoid putting the damaged blade into the lock.
   - If the vehicle is keyless or push-to-start and does not detect the fob, check the owner’s manual for the emergency start method. Some vehicles have a specific place to hold the fob.
   - If the blade is bent, do not try to straighten it while it is in the lock or ignition.

3. **If a broken piece is visible, only try one gentle removal.**
   - If the broken piece is clearly sticking out and you can grip it without pushing it further in, try a careful straight pull with fine pliers.
   - Stop immediately if it does not come out easily, if it turns at an angle, or if you feel resistance.
   - Keep both pieces of the key if you have them.

4. **Use a spare key if it can reach you safely.**
   - Call someone you trust who can bring the spare key.
   - If you are roadside or somewhere unsafe, do not spend time searching bags or online fixes unless you are already in a safe place.
   - If the spare key works, avoid using the broken key again until a professional checks or replaces it.

5. **Contact the right help for extraction or replacement.**
   - If you have breakdown cover, contact your provider and say: “My car key has snapped, bent, or broken, and there may be a fragment in the door lock or ignition.”
   - If you do not have breakdown cover, contact an auto locksmith who deals with vehicle keys, immobiliser keys, and key extraction.
   - If the vehicle cannot be locked, cannot be moved, or is unsafe to leave, ask the breakdown provider or locksmith whether recovery to a safer place is needed.

6. **Have the basic details ready.**
   - Give your exact location, including motorway marker posts or nearby signs if relevant.
   - Have the vehicle make, model, year, and registration ready.
   - Be ready to show ID and proof that you own or are allowed to use the vehicle before a key is cut or programmed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether a dealer, locksmith, or breakdown provider is the best long-term option.
- You do not need to diagnose whether the cause is key wear, lock wear, ignition wear, or a fob problem while you are stranded.
- You do not need to try online extraction tricks or buy tools before you have made the vehicle and your location safe.
- You do not need to decide now whether to replace all keys or change locks unless a professional tells you there is a security risk.

## Important reassurance
A broken or bent vehicle key is stressful, but it is usually a practical access and replacement problem. Stopping early and avoiding force can help prevent extra damage.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to make the situation safer and avoid making the key, lock, ignition, or security system worse. Later decisions about replacement keys, programming, lock wear, or recovery may need help from a breakdown provider, auto locksmith, dealer, or mechanic.

## Important note
This is general information, not mechanical, legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Vehicle locks, ignitions, fobs, and immobilisers vary, so follow your owner’s manual and use emergency help if your location or personal safety is at risk.
