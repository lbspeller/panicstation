# What to do if… you return home and find a window screen removed or a window left open (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible break-in. Do not go inside; move to a safer place and call 999 if someone may still be inside, the break-in may be happening now, or you feel in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t step inside “just to check” or call out into the home.
- Don’t try to reattach the screen, close the window, or touch the frame or handles if you may report it.
- Don’t confront anyone, follow anyone, or try to block exits.
- Don’t search room-to-room, including a quick check upstairs.
- Don’t circle the property if it puts you in a vulnerable spot, such as a narrow alley, back garden, or unlit side path.
- Don’t post details or your location publicly while you are outside the property.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and create distance.** Step back from the window or door. Move somewhere safer, such as a neighbour’s home, a nearby shop or café, or inside your locked car if that is safer than staying by the property.
2. **Choose 999 or 101. If you are unsure, choose 999.**
   - Call **999** if you think someone may still be inside, you saw or heard anything worrying, you feel threatened, or the break-in may be happening now.
   - Call **101**, or make an online report through Police.uk if available for your local force, if you are confident it is not happening now and you are safe.
3. **If you need 999 but cannot speak safely, still call 999.** Listen to the questions, cough or tap if you can, and press **55** when prompted from a mobile so the call can be transferred to the police.
4. **Observe only from a safe distance.** Without approaching, note anything useful for police, such as:
   - any person seen leaving and their direction of travel
   - any vehicle details, such as colour, make, or part of a number plate
   - which window is affected
   - what looks moved or removed, such as a screen, latch, curtain, blind, or frame
5. **If someone else might be inside, contact them from where you are safe.** Call or text a housemate, family member, cleaner, or visitor. Do not enter to look for them. If you cannot confirm they are safe, tell the police.
6. **Use remote information only if you can do it from safety.** You can check a doorbell camera, alarm app, or smart lock status from where you are, but do not approach the property to do this and do not rely on remote actions to make it safe.
7. **If police advise you to enter, follow their instructions.** Avoid touching the window area, handles, disturbed surfaces, or anything that seems out of place. Do not tidy up.
8. **Once the immediate risk is dealt with, note the report details.** If a crime is reported, ask for or write down the crime reference number.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it was definitely a burglary.
- You do not need to fix the window or screen, deep-clean, or make a full inventory while you may still be unsafe.
- You do not need to contact insurance, a landlord or agent, or arrange lock changes until you are safe and, if relevant, police have advised on next steps.

## Important reassurance
Finding a screen removed or a window unexpectedly open is a reasonable reason to feel alarmed. Treating it as a safety issue first is not overreacting; it is a way to protect yourself while the situation is still unclear.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance for the first minutes after noticing that a possible entry point has been disturbed. Follow-on decisions about repairs, security upgrades, insurance, and longer-term safety planning can come later and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, or other professional advice. Follow instructions from police call handlers and attending officers, and prioritise your personal safety over property.
