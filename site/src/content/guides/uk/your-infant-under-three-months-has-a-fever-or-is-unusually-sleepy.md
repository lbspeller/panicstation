# What to do if… your infant under three months has a fever or is unusually sleepy (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as urgent. If your baby is under 3 months and has a temperature of 38°C or higher, or you think they have a fever, call NHS 111 or your GP urgently; if they will not wake up normally, are very floppy, or breathing is a struggle, call 999 or go to A&E now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “wait it out” because the fever seems mild; babies under 3 months need urgent advice for a fever.
- Do not rely on touch alone to decide it is safe; check a temperature if you can.
- Do not delay 999 or A&E to take a temperature if your baby looks very unwell.
- Do not cool them with cold baths, ice packs, or rubbing alcohol.
- Do not overdress them or cover them in too many blankets.
- Do not give paracetamol to a baby under 2 months unless a clinician tells you to.
- Do not give ibuprofen to a baby under 3 months unless a doctor has prescribed it.
- Do not give aspirin.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel you cannot do it safely or your baby seems to be getting worse quickly; use 999.

## What to do now
1. Put your baby somewhere safe for a moment.
   - Hold them securely, or place them on a firm, flat surface.
   - Look at whether they wake, respond, breathe comfortably, and feed in their usual way.

2. If any of these are happening, treat it as an emergency: call 999 or go to A&E now.
   - They will not wake up normally, are very floppy or limp, or are not responding in their usual way.
   - Breathing looks like hard work, including grunting, very fast breathing, long pauses, or the chest or tummy sucking in.
   - Skin, lips, or tongue look blue, grey, very pale, or blotchy, or they feel unusually cold as well as unwell.
   - They have a spotty purple or red rash that does not fade when you press the side of a clear glass against it.
   - They have a seizure or fit.
   - They are under 8 weeks old and you are very worried.

3. Check a temperature if you can, but do not delay emergency help if they look very unwell.
   - Use a digital thermometer in the armpit.
   - Write down the number and the time you took it.
   - A temperature of 38°C or higher is a fever in this situation.

4. If the temperature is 38°C or higher, or you think they have a fever, call NHS 111 or your GP urgently.
   - Say: “My baby is under 3 months and has a temperature of 38 or above,” or “My baby is under 3 months and I think they have a fever.”
   - Follow the advice you are given. They may tell you to go to an urgent assessment service or A&E.

5. If the main worry is unusual sleepiness, treat that as urgent even without a confirmed temperature.
   - Call NHS 111 or your GP urgently if they are much harder to wake than usual, too sleepy to feed normally, or not themselves.
   - Use 999 or A&E if they will not wake up normally or you are seriously worried by how different they seem.

6. While you are getting help, keep care simple.
   - Offer feeds as normal; if breastfed, offer feeds more often if they will take them.
   - Do not force feeds.
   - Do not cover them in too many clothes or blankets.
   - Keep watching breathing, colour, alertness, rash, vomiting, and wet nappies.

7. Write down a few facts to tell the clinician.
   - Age in weeks.
   - Highest temperature and when you took it.
   - How alert they are and whether they are feeding.
   - Wet nappies today.
   - Any rash, breathing change, vomiting, or seizure.
   - Any recent vaccinations and when they happened.
   - Any medicine already given, including name, dose, and time.

8. Be ready to leave if you are told to.
   - Take the red book if you have it nearby.
   - Take nappies, wipes, a blanket, and your notes.
   - Do not delay leaving to find non-essential items.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause right now.
- You do not need to try cooling tricks such as sponging, cold baths, or over-undressing.
- You do not need to decide what tests or treatment are needed.
- You do not need to decide whether this is because of vaccinations; mention any recent vaccinations to the clinician and follow their advice.
- Focus only on getting urgent clinical advice or emergency help.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to feel frightened when a very young baby seems hot, unusually sleepy, or different from normal. Acting quickly is the right kind of caution, and clinicians would rather assess a young baby early.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: checking for emergency signs, taking a temperature if it is safe to do so, and getting the right UK help quickly. Later decisions about tests, medicines, or treatment need a clinician.
