# What to do if… you receive an official request to update your address urgently and you are unsure what happens if you do not (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the request independently via official GOV.UK portals or known switchboards before sharing any data. If it is genuine, provide only the required details through these secured channels to avoid fines or legal missed deadlines.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, scan QR codes, or call phone numbers contained within the suspicious letter, text, or email.
- Don’t provide scans of identity documents or bank details to any third-party "address update" services that charge a fee.
- Don’t ignore mail from the DVLA, HMRC, or the courts simply because you are worried about the contents; delays can trigger automatic fines or enforcement.
- Don’t assume that updating your address with one government department (like HMRC) automatically updates it with others (like the DVLA).
- Don’t open mail that is clearly addressed to a previous tenant or someone you do not know.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the purported sender and any reference numbers provided on the envelope or letter.** Look for specific department names such as HMRC, DVLA, or HMCTS (courts), but do not use the contact details printed on the document yet.
2. **Determine if the mail is intended for you or a previous occupant.**
   - If the name is incorrect, write "Not known at this address" or "No longer lives here" on the unopened envelope.
   - Cross out the address but leave the name and return address visible.
   - Place the item in any Royal Mail postbox; no additional postage is required.
3. **Verify the request using an independent official channel.** 
   - Access the GOV.UK website directly to find the official "change of address" service for the specific department mentioned.
   - For DVLA matters, check both your driving licence and your vehicle log book (V5C) status separately.
   - For HMRC matters, sign in to your Personal Tax Account via the official GOV.UK gateway.
4. **Respond only through the verified official portal if the request is confirmed as genuine.**
   - Update your records immediately to ensure future correspondence reaches you.
   - Note that as of 2026, the DVLA requires address updates within 14 days of a move to avoid a potential £1,000 fine.
5. **Report the communication if you suspect it is a scam or phishing attempt.**
   - Forward suspicious text messages to 7726.
   - Forward suspicious emails to report@phishing.gov.uk.
   - If you have already shared data or lost money, contact Action Fraud (or Police Scotland via 101) and alert your bank immediately.
6. **Check your registration on the electoral roll to ensure your legal and credit records are consistent.**
   - Use the "Register to vote" service on GOV.UK to update your details for your current local authority.
   - This prevents issues with identity verification and ensures you receive official polling information.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain why the address was not updated sooner in your initial response.
- You do not need to contact every company you do business with in the first hour; focus on the "Big Three" (DVLA, HMRC, and Local Council/Electoral Roll) first.
- You do not need to provide a full history of your previous addresses unless specifically requested by a verified official during a formal application.

## Important reassurance
Receiving urgent official mail can be startling, especially if you are concerned about missed deadlines. Most administrative errors regarding addresses can be resolved by providing the correct information through the proper channels. Taking a moment to verify the sender protects you from fraud and ensures your records are corrected legally.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling urgent address update requests. It does not cover specific legal defenses for missed court dates or detailed appeals against existing fines. If you are involved in active criminal proceedings or immigration matters, consult a specialist solicitor after verifying the source of the contact.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Requirements for updating addresses and the penalties for failing to do so vary between government departments. Always use official GOV.UK resources to confirm the current regulations and deadlines applicable to your specific situation.
