# What to do if… an airbag or SRS warning light comes on before you drive (UK)

## Short answer
Do not start the journey as normal. Treat a continuing airbag or SRS warning as a sign that part of the restraint system may not protect you properly in a crash, and arrange prompt diagnostic help before driving far.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore it and set off for a normal trip.
- Do not try to reset the airbag system by disconnecting the battery or pulling fuses.
- Do not poke, unplug, or test wiring or connectors under seats, in the steering wheel, dashboard, pillars, or around airbags.
- Do not assume it is only the passenger airbag being switched off unless the display clearly says that.
- Do not put a rear-facing baby seat in the front passenger seat unless you are sure the front passenger airbag is deactivated and your vehicle manual allows that setup.

## What to do now
1. **Pause before setting off.**  
   Park safely, keep the vehicle still, and give yourself a moment to check what the warning is actually showing.

2. **Do one safe re-check without touching airbag parts.**  
   Switch the engine off, wait briefly, then switch the ignition on and watch the airbag or SRS warning. Many cars show warning lights briefly during start-up. If the airbag or SRS light stays on, comes back on, or the display shows an airbag or restraint fault, treat it as an active fault.

3. **Check whether it is a separate passenger airbag OFF light.**  
   A clearly labelled passenger airbag OFF indicator can mean the passenger airbag has been intentionally disabled, which is different from an SRS malfunction warning. If that setting is not what you intended, check your vehicle handbook for the correct switch or menu. Do not guess with child seats or front-seat passengers.

4. **Remove obvious obstructions without disturbing connectors.**  
   If you have just moved a front seat, return it gently to a normal driving position. Remove bags, bottles, tools, or other items jammed under the front seats that may be pressing on seat rails or sensors. Do not unplug or reconnect anything.

5. **Choose the lowest-risk transport option.**  
   If you can avoid driving, use another vehicle, public transport, a taxi, or call your breakdown provider or a garage for advice. If you must move the car, keep it to the shortest practical journey, avoid higher-speed roads if there is a practical alternative, and go directly to a garage or safe place.

6. **Book an SRS or airbag diagnostic check.**  
   Ask the garage to read the SRS fault codes and check whether the fault affects airbags, seat-belt pretensioners, seat sensors, or related wiring.

7. **Reduce passenger risk until it is checked.**  
   Postpone the trip if you can. If you cannot, keep children in the rear seats where possible, avoid using a rear-facing baby seat in the front unless the passenger airbag is definitely deactivated, and avoid using the front passenger seat if the airbag status is unclear.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact faulty part right now.
- You do not need to compare repair quotes today.
- You do not need to decide whether to use a dealer or independent garage before the fault is confirmed.
- You do not need to clear the warning light yourself.

## Important reassurance
An airbag warning light usually means the vehicle has detected a fault or an unexpected reading, not that you have done something wrong. Stopping before the journey and arranging a proper check is a sensible safety step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce immediate risk and avoid making the problem worse. Diagnosis and repair depend on the vehicle and may need a qualified technician.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not vehicle-specific mechanical, legal, or professional advice. Airbag and SRS systems are safety-critical; if the warning persists, it is generally safest to avoid normal driving and get professional diagnostics promptly.
