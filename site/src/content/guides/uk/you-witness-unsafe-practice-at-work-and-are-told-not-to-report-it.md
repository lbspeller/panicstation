# What to do if… you witness unsafe practice at work and are told not to report it (UK)

## Short answer
If someone could be harmed, prioritise immediate safety and make a clear, factual report through a route that creates a record. If your workplace blocks you, you may be able to raise the concern as whistleblowing and report it to the correct health and safety enforcing authority.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore a real risk of injury or serious harm because someone told you to stay quiet.
- Do not argue about blame or motives; stick to what you saw, where it happened, who may be at risk, and what was said.
- Do not post about it on social media or share it widely at work.
- Do not destroy, edit, or delete records, messages, photos, incident forms, or notes out of fear.
- Do not secretly record people without getting advice first, because it can create extra risk.
- Do not sign anything saying the issue is resolved if you do not genuinely believe the risk has been controlled.

## What to do now
1. **Make the situation safer first.**  
   If there is an immediate risk, such as faulty equipment in use, unsafe work at height, chemical exposure, violence, fire, or a moving vehicle risk, move to a safer position and alert the person in charge of the work. If there is imminent danger to life, call **999**.

2. **Write a short factual note while it is fresh.**  
   Record the date, time, exact location, what you saw, who was present, who may be harmed, and what you were told about not reporting it. Keep it factual, not emotional or accusatory.

3. **Use the workplace safety reporting route.**  
   Use the incident system, near-miss system, health and safety mailbox, supervisor route, or written procedure if one exists. If you were verbally told not to report it, follow up in writing with a simple message such as:  
   “I am raising a health and safety concern about [short facts]. I am concerned that [who] could be harmed. Please confirm what control measures are in place.”

4. **Keep copies somewhere appropriate.**  
   Keep copies of your report, replies, rota changes, warnings, or messages about the concern. Do not take confidential files, personal data, or employer property unless you have advice that it is safe and lawful to do so.

5. **Escalate away from the person blocking the report.**  
   If your line manager is stopping the report, raise it with a more senior manager, a health and safety lead, HR, a whistleblowing contact, or a recognised trade union safety representative if you have one. Ask for confidentiality if you need it, while understanding it may not always be possible to keep your identity hidden.

6. **Frame the concern as a risk to others, not a personal dispute.**  
   Health and safety danger, cover-ups, and some legal breaches can be qualifying whistleblowing concerns if the disclosure meets the legal tests. Keep the message focused on the risk, who could be affected, and the fact you were told not to report it.

7. **Use the correct external route if the risk is serious and internal action is blocked.**  
   For England, Scotland, and Wales, check whether the Health and Safety Executive, a local authority, or another body enforces the workplace. For Northern Ireland, check the Health and Safety Executive for Northern Ireland. Use the route that matches the workplace and the type of risk.

8. **Get early employment guidance if you are threatened or punished.**  
   Keep a simple timeline of what changed after you raised the concern, such as hours, duties, warnings, treatment, or messages. Contact Acas, a union, or an employment adviser before resigning, signing anything, or making a formal claim.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, go public, or start a legal claim.
- You do not need to prove intent, prove a cover-up, or gather perfect evidence before raising a real safety concern.
- You do not need to confront the person who told you to stay quiet beyond making a clear report and escalating it through a safer route.
- You do not need to know the exact legal label before writing down the facts and protecting immediate safety.

## Important reassurance
Being told not to report unsafe practice can make you feel exposed, guilty, or frightened. A calm factual record, a clear safety report, and careful escalation are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about whistleblowing, grievances, resignation, tribunal deadlines, or legal claims may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. The correct route can vary by workplace, sector, role, and location, so prioritise immediate safety and get appropriate advice where needed.
