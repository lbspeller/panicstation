# What to do if… someone tries to follow you into an elevator and you do not feel safe letting them in (UK)

## Short answer
Do not enter the lift with the person. Immediately step back, allow the doors to close without you, and move toward a public or staffed area where you are visible to others.

## Do not do these things
- Don't prioritize politeness over your personal safety or intuition.
- Don't physically block the door or engage in a confrontation to stop them entering.
- Don't step into the lift to avoid an awkward social interaction if you feel uneasy.
- Don't lead the person to your specific flat floor or a quiet residential corridor.
- Don't use your keys or fobs in front of them if you think they are tracking your destination.
- Don't hold the lift doors open while you decide what to do.

## What to do now
1. **Create physical distance immediately by stepping away from the lift doors.** Do not enter the car; if you are already inside and the person enters, step out before the doors close.
2. **Move toward a populated or staffed area of the building.** Go directly to a concierge desk, security station, or a busy lobby area where there is CCTV and other people.
3. **Make a visible and audible phone call to signal you are not alone.** Call a friend or family member and clearly state your location, such as "I am in the lobby of [Building Name] and will be up in a minute."
4. **If you are stuck inside the lift with them, press the button for the very next floor or the lobby.** Do not go to your home floor; exit as soon as the doors open at any level that offers a quicker route back to a public space.
5. **Use the lift alarm or help button only if you are in immediate physical danger or being prevented from exiting.** Be aware that in some systems, this may stop the lift, so prioritize exiting at the next available floor if possible.
6. **Call 999 if the person continues to follow you or becomes aggressive.** If you cannot speak safely, use the "Silent Solution" by dialing 999 and then pressing 55 when prompted to alert the operator that you are in a genuine emergency.
7. **Report the incident to building management or security as soon as you are in a secure location.** Provide a description of the person and the time of the incident so they can review CCTV and address any breaches in access control or tailgating.

## What can wait
- You do not need to justify your decision to step away to the other person.
- You do not need to determine if the person "actually" meant harm before seeking help.
- You do not need to file a full police report (101) until you have reached a safe, locked environment.

## Important reassurance
Feeling startled or anxious when someone follows you into a confined space is a natural safety response. You have a right to choose who you share a lift with, and taking precautions to remain in public view is a sensible and standard safety practice.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate personal safety at a lift or elevator. It does not cover long-term stalking investigations or building-wide security audits.

## Important note
This information is for immediate safety guidance and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. In any situation involving an active threat or crime in progress, dial 999 immediately.
