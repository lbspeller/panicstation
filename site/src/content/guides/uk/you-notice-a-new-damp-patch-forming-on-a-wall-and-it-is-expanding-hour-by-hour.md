# What to do if… you notice a new damp patch forming on a wall and it is expanding hour by hour (UK)

## Short answer
Treat an expanding patch as an active high-volume leak: isolate any nearby electricity immediately, then shut off the internal water stopcock to stop the flow.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the patch because there is no standing water; hour-by-hour growth indicates a pressurized or high-volume active entry.
- Do not touch any sockets, switches, or appliances that are in or directly below the damp area.
- Do not use a ladder to inspect a ceiling patch if the floor is wet, due to slip and shock risks.
- Do not drill or cut into the wall to "drain" the water, as you may hit live wiring or the leaking pipe itself.
- Do not apply heat or use a hairdryer to dry the wall while the water is still entering or near electrics.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate electrical hazards and create distance.**  
   **If you see water dripping from a light fitting, hear crackling from a socket, or see smoke, keep everyone away and do not touch any switches.**  
   - Call **999** only if there is an active fire or an immediate risk of person-to-water electrical contact that you cannot safely stop.

2. **Isolate the power to the affected area at the consumer unit.**  
   **Go to your fuse box (consumer unit) and switch off the circuit breaker for the affected room if it is safe to do so.**  
   - If the damp is spreading toward the consumer unit itself or you are unsure which circuit to flip, turn off the **Main Switch** (usually a large red switch) to kill all power to the property.  
   - Use a torch if it is dark; do not use candles near a suspected leak.

3. **Shut off the internal stopcock (stop tap) completely.**  
   **Locate your internal stopcock—usually found under the kitchen sink, in a hallway cupboard, or under the stairs—and turn it fully clockwise.**  
   - If it is seized, do not force it with tools; try to locate the **external stopcock** (usually under a small cover in the pavement at your property boundary).  
   - Once turned off, open the cold kitchen tap to drain the remaining water from the pipes.

4. **Triage the likely source based on the patch location.**  
   - **Internal wall near a bathroom or kitchen:** Assume a plumbing leak or waste pipe failure.  
   - **External wall during heavy rain:** Assume guttering, roof, or masonry failure (ingress).  
   - **Ceiling below a bathroom:** Assume a bath/shower seal failure or an upstairs appliance leak.

5. **Contact the relevant repair service based on your housing status.**  
   **If you are a social housing or private tenant, contact your landlord or agent immediately and state that the repair is an "Emergency Hazard."**  
   - Under **Awaab's Law (2026)**, social landlords must investigate emergency hazards (like leaks near electrics) within **24 hours**.  
   - If you are a homeowner, call an **emergency plumber** or check your home emergency insurance for a 24/7 call-out number.

6. **Mitigate further damage only once the area is safe.**  
   **Move furniture, rugs, and electronics away from the expanding patch.**  
   - Place buckets or heavy towels at the base of the wall to catch runoff.  
   - Take timestamped photos and videos of the patch every 30 minutes to document the speed of expansion for insurance or landlord evidence.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact pipe or hole responsible; that is the role of the professional.
- You do not need to clean the damp or treat for mould today; focus entirely on stopping the liquid entry.
- You do not need to file a full insurance claim immediately; a "notification of loss" or early phone call is sufficient once the site is stable.

## Important reassurance
A spreading damp patch can feel like the house is being destroyed, but water damage is manageable once the pressure is removed. By turning off the stopcock and the power, you have already stopped the primary drivers of damage and danger.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for acute, expanding dampness only. It does not cover long-term rising damp, condensation, or permanent structural repairs.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and does not replace professional plumbing, electrical, or structural advice. If water has entered electrical fittings, do not restore power until a qualified electrician has issued an EICR or safety certificate.
