# What to do if… your motorcycle chain looks loose or damaged and you are deciding whether it is safe to ride (UK)

## Short answer
Do not ride until the chain, sprockets, chain guard, and rear wheel alignment have been checked against your bike’s handbook or by a qualified mechanic. A loose, tight, worn, or damaged chain may come off, jam, or fail.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take a “slow test ride” to see if it feels okay.
- Do not tighten the chain by guesswork if you do not know the correct slack range and measuring point for your bike.
- Do not loosen the rear axle or adjust the chain unless you can correctly secure everything afterwards.
- Do not adjust the chain without re-checking rear wheel alignment and chain slack after tightening.
- Do not put fingers, clothing, tools, or cleaning rags near the chain or sprockets while the engine is running.
- Do not ignore cracked plates, missing parts, seized links, a loose joining link, badly hooked sprocket teeth, or a chain guard that is loose or rubbing.

## What to do now
1. **Choose the safer default: do not ride.** If you are unsure, treat the bike as not safe to move under engine power.
2. **Make the bike safe to inspect.** Park on level ground if you can, switch the engine off, remove the key, let hot parts cool, and keep your hands clear of the chain run.
3. **Look for stop-ride signs.** Do not ride if you see any of these:
   - a cracked, bent, split, or separating chain link;
   - stiff or kinked links that do not move freely by hand;
   - anything loose, missing, or wrongly seated around a joining link or clip, if your chain has one;
   - sharp, hooked, uneven, chipped, or damaged sprocket teeth;
   - a chain guard that is loose, damaged, or rubbing the chain;
   - the chain running visibly off-line;
   - the chain pulling noticeably away from the back of the rear sprocket.
4. **Check the correct slack range.** Use your owner’s handbook or the swingarm sticker, if fitted, for the exact slack range, bike position, and measuring point. Check slack at several points by turning the rear wheel by hand; if it is outside the handbook range at any point, do not ride.
5. **If the chain is out of spec but not obviously damaged, choose one safe option.**
   - Arrange a garage, mobile mechanic, or recovery service to inspect and adjust it.
   - If you are already competent and have the right tools, adjust only to the handbook spec, then re-check slack, rear wheel alignment, and axle fasteners exactly as the manual says.
   - If you cannot get it into spec, the adjusters are near their limit, or anything still looks wrong, stop and arrange inspection or recovery.
6. **If you need to get home, do not ride the bike home unless it passes the checks.** Use breakdown cover, a local recovery service, or transport by van or trailer. Tell them it is a possible drivetrain or chain issue.
7. **Take clear photos before moving anything.** Photograph the chain on the rear sprocket, any damaged link, the sprocket teeth, the chain guard, and both rear wheel adjusters.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to replace just the chain or a full chain-and-sprocket set.
- You do not need to deep-clean or lubricate the chain before deciding whether it is safe to ride.
- You do not need to work out the cause today, such as wear, age, poor adjustment, corrosion, or lack of lubrication.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to second-guess this because motorcycle chains are meant to have some slack. Choosing not to ride until the chain and sprockets are confirmed safe is a calm, sensible decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to help you avoid an unsafe ride and get the bike checked. Chain slack ranges, adjustment methods, axle tightening, and joining-link details vary by model, so use your handbook or a qualified mechanic for the full procedure.

## Important note
This is general information, not mechanical, legal, financial, or professional advice. MOT guidance referenced here is for Great Britain; Northern Ireland has separate MOT arrangements.
