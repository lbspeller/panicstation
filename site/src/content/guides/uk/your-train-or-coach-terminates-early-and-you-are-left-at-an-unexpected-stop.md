# What to do if… your train or coach terminates early and you are left at an unexpected stop (UK)

## Short answer
Do not rush away from the station or coach stop. Confirm the exact place you must get off, move to the safest staffed or well-lit area, and get the operator’s official onward advice before buying anything new.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t get off and walk away immediately to “figure it out later”; you may lose access to staff, safer waiting areas, and live information.
- Don’t assume you must buy a brand-new ticket straight away; during disruption, there may be ticket acceptance, re-routing, replacement transport, or operator advice to follow.
- Don’t rely on a rumour in the crowd or a random social post; use staff, the driver, station boards, the operator’s app or website, or official live updates.
- Don’t accept a pressured lift or unlicensed taxi offer from someone approaching you outside a station or coach stop.
- Don’t delete booking emails, QR codes, screenshots, or receipts; you may need them for re-routing, refunds, compensation, or a complaint.
- Don’t argue about compensation while you are still stranded; getting to a safe place and confirming the next official step matters more.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the basics before you move away.**
   - Ask staff, the guard, or the driver: “Where exactly are we terminating, and is everyone required to get off?”
   - Ask: “What is the official onward option for passengers going to my destination?”
   - Note the time of the announcement and the service number, train number, coach number, or booking reference if you can see it.

2. **Make the safest information point your first destination.**
   - At a railway station, go to the concourse, ticket office, help point, staffed barrier, or the best-lit busy area.
   - At a coach station or roadside stop, stay near the driver, staff, a staffed building, a well-lit public area, or other passengers while you check information.
   - If there is immediate danger, call 999. At a railway station in Great Britain, British Transport Police can take non-emergency texts on 61016.

3. **Get the onward plan for your exact destination.**
   - For a train, ask whether ticket acceptance, a different permitted route, another train company, or rail-replacement transport is being offered.
   - For a coach, ask the driver or operator whether another coach, a transfer, a rebooked journey, or a named waiting point is being arranged.
   - Write down the instruction you are given, including who gave it and the time.

4. **Before spending money, ask what the operator is advising or authorising.**
   - Say: “I cannot complete my journey from here as planned. What is the official advice for getting to my destination?”
   - If it is late, the last practical service, or you are stuck somewhere unfamiliar, ask whether staff can help you contact the operator, arrange onward travel, or identify a safe place to wait.
   - If you decide you must pay for transport or somewhere safe to wait, keep receipts. Reimbursement can vary, so avoid spending money you cannot afford to lose unless safety makes it necessary.

5. **Save proof while it is easy to get.**
   - Take a photo of the departure board, coach display, notice, cancellation message, or app update showing the disruption.
   - Keep your ticket, QR code, booking confirmation, and any messages from the operator.
   - Put one note in your phone: “Service terminated early at [place] at [time]; advised [instruction].”

6. **Choose the next safe step, not the perfect plan.**
   - If there is a clear official onward route, take that route.
   - If information is changing, stay in the staffed or safest waiting area and recheck official updates.
   - If a safe person can collect you, send them the exact stop or station name, your location within it, and your phone battery level.

7. **If you stop travelling, protect your refund position.**
   - For train tickets, if your train is delayed, cancelled, rescheduled, or you cannot complete the journey because of disruption, you can usually claim a refund for the unused ticket from the original retailer without an admin fee.
   - For coach tickets, check the operator’s own disruption instructions and keep proof of what happened before making a refund or complaint request.
   - Later coach complaint or reporting routes can vary by where the journey was, so keep the date, time, route, operator, where you were told to get off, and what staff or the driver told you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a formal complaint.
- You do not need to calculate compensation while you are stranded.
- You do not need to prove who is at fault.
- You do not need to sort every screenshot and receipt immediately; just keep them.
- You can deal with refund, compensation, and complaint forms once you are somewhere safe and calm.

## Important reassurance
This is unsettling because the route you were relying on has changed without warning. A safe place, one official source of information, and a short written note of what happened are enough for the next few minutes.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about refunds, compensation, complaints, missed connections, split tickets, or extra costs may need help from the operator, retailer, passenger body, or another specialist source.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, safety, transport, or professional advice. What is offered can vary by operator, ticket type, location, time of day, and the cause of the disruption, so check official instructions before making irreversible decisions.
