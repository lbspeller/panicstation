# What to do if… you find signs of sexual activity in your room and do not remember consenting (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safer place first. If you are in danger, badly hurt, very unwell, or think someone may still be nearby, call 999; otherwise contact a sexual assault healthcare service, SARC, SARCS, or specialist sexual violence support before deciding what to do next.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront anyone you suspect or invite them back to explain.
- Do not blame yourself or force yourself to work out exactly what happened right now.
- Do not post publicly or message lots of people for answers while you feel shocked.
- Do not clean the room, wash bedding, or throw away items you think may matter while you are still deciding what you want to do.
- If you might want evidence considered later, and it feels manageable, avoid showering and avoid washing the clothes you were wearing for now.
- Do not let anyone pressure you into reporting, not reporting, forgiving, staying silent, or making a statement before you feel ready.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.**  
   Leave the room if you feel unsafe. Go to a trusted person, a neighbour, a public place, or somewhere you can lock a door. Call 999 if you think someone may still be nearby, you are being threatened, or you need urgent help.

2. **Bring one trusted person in.**  
   Call or message one person you trust and ask them to stay with you or stay on the phone. You can say: “I’m not sure what happened. I don’t feel safe and I need you with me.”

3. **Get sexual assault healthcare support, even if you are unsure.**  
   Contact the sexual assault healthcare service for your part of the UK. It may be called a Sexual Assault Referral Centre, SARC, or SARCS. Ask: “Can I self-refer, can I speak to someone confidentially, and what are my options if I do not want police involvement right now?”

4. **Treat illness, injury, or suspected spiking as urgent.**  
   Go to A&E or call 999 if you have severe symptoms such as trouble breathing, chest pain, fainting, serious injury, heavy bleeding, or confusion that is getting worse. Tell staff you have a memory gap and are worried about possible sexual assault or spiking.

5. **Keep the next few choices small.**  
   Put on comfortable clothes if you can. Drink water if you can tolerate it. Write down or voice-note only what you clearly remember, such as when you last felt normal, who was nearby, when you woke up, and what you noticed, without trying to fill in gaps.

6. **Use confidential specialist support without committing to a report.**  
   If you are in England or Wales and aged 16 or over, you can contact the 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line by phone or online chat. In Scotland and Northern Ireland, use the nation-specific support links below. These services are not emergency services, so use 999 for immediate danger or urgent medical help.

7. **If you think your room or home was entered, focus on safety first.**  
   Move away from the room if you can. If you feel unsafe or think the person may return, call 999. If it is not urgent but you want police advice about a possible crime, call 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether it counts as assault right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to report to the police today.
- You do not need to contact a landlord, employer, university, family member, or anyone you do not trust yet.
- You do not need to clean, reorganise, replace bedding, or make the room look normal immediately.
- You do not need to explain gaps in your memory before asking for help.

## Important reassurance
Not remembering, freezing, sleeping, being intoxicated, or not fighting back does not mean you agreed. You deserve care and support even if you feel confused, numb, uncertain, or worried that you cannot prove what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, urgent health needs, specialist support, and keeping choices open. Later decisions about reporting, housing, security, healthcare follow-up, or formal statements may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, therapeutic, forensic, or professional advice. UK services and referral routes differ between England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, so use the resource that fits where you are. If you feel in immediate danger or seriously unwell, call 999.
