# What to do if… you are contacted by a regulator about possible violations and you are unsure how to respond (UK)

## Short answer
Pause, preserve all relevant records immediately, and obtain legal advice before providing any substantive response or explanation. Your first priority is to acknowledge receipt and request the specific legal basis and scope of the inquiry in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the contact, miss deadlines, or assume the matter will resolve itself without action.
- Do not destroy, delete, edit, or "tidy up" any records, including emails, chat logs, physical files, or calendar entries.
- Do not attempt to "align stories" or coordinate narratives with colleagues or staff.
- Do not provide off-the-cuff explanations or "helpful" context during an initial phone call or visit.
- Do not volunteer information or documents that have not been explicitly requested.
- Do not hand over devices, sign statements, or agree to immediate interviews without verifying the regulator's statutory powers.
- Do not discuss the details of the investigation on internal open channels; keep communications restricted to a small "client group" to protect legal privilege.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the identity of the contact using independent official channels.**  
   **Do not use the contact details provided in the message; instead, find the regulator's official switchboard number on their .gov.uk or official website to confirm the case officer and reference number exist.**
2. **Issue an immediate internal "litigation hold" or preservation notice.**  
   **Direct all relevant staff and IT departments to cease any routine deletion, archiving, or "housekeeping" of data related to the topics mentioned in the regulator's contact.**
   - Include emails, instant messages (Teams/Slack/WhatsApp), and physical documents.
   - Disable auto-delete functions on relevant accounts immediately.
3. **Identify the specific statutory power the regulator is citing.**  
   **Check the document for references to specific Acts (e.g., Financial Services and Markets Act, Competition Act, Health and Safety at Work Act) or "Statutory Notices."**
   - If no power is cited, treat the request as "voluntary" but still formal.
4. **Establish a restricted "client group" for all investigation-related communications.**  
   **Identify the small group of senior individuals who will manage the response and ensure all their communications with lawyers are clearly marked "Confidential – Subject to Legal Professional Privilege."**
   - This prevents the accidental loss of privilege that occurs when sensitive legal discussions are forwarded to wider staff.
5. **Request the scope, legal basis, and deadline in writing.**  
   **Send a brief acknowledgement stating that you have received the contact and are seeking advice, then request a clear list of the information required and the statutory deadline for the response.**
6. **Notify your professional indemnity insurers or in-house compliance team.**  
   **Check your insurance policy requirements; many policies require "notification of circumstances" as soon as you become aware of a potential regulatory issue to maintain coverage.**
7. **Appoint a single point of contact for the regulator.**  
   **Instruct all other staff that they must not respond to the regulator directly and must forward all inquiries to the designated person without comment.**
8. **If officials arrive on-site, verify their warrant or entry powers before allowing access.**  
   **Ask for identification and a copy of the written authority (warrant or notice) that permits their entry, then contact a solicitor immediately while they wait.**
   - Under 2026 PACE updates, an increasing number of enforcement officers have powers of search and seizure; do not obstruct, but ensure you record exactly what is accessed or taken.
9. **Clarify the status of any requested interview.**  
   **Ask if the interview is voluntary or compulsory, and whether it will be conducted "under caution" (meaning what you say can be used as evidence in criminal proceedings).**
   - Do not attend any interview, including "voluntary" ones, without legal representation.
10. **Prepare a factual timeline of events for your legal advisor.**  
    **List the key dates, people involved, and locations of relevant records without adding speculation, opinions, or admissions of fault.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to admit fault or provide an apology in your initial response.
- You do not need to conduct a full internal investigation or "solve" the problem today.
- You do not need to decide on a final defense strategy or public relations response immediately.
- You do not need to confront or discipline staff members mentioned in the inquiry until you have received legal advice.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a notice from a regulator is a procedural event, not a final judgement. Regulators frequently conduct inquiries as part of routine monitoring or fact-finding. By pausing and preserving evidence, you are protecting your ability to provide an accurate, legal, and controlled response later.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate stabilization steps for the first minutes and hours after contact. It does not cover the long-term management of a regulatory defense, which requires specialized legal counsel.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. Regulatory powers and duties (such as mandatory self-reporting for SRA or FCA firms) vary significantly by sector. Always consult a UK-qualified solicitor specializing in regulatory law.
