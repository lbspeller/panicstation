# What to do if… you are asked to show proof of an airport departure fee or tax and cannot find a receipt (UK)

## Short answer
Ask exactly what proof they will accept right now, such as a receipt, e-ticket, invoice, card transaction, QR code, or booking record. Try to recreate proof from your booking or payment record before paying again. If you must pay again to avoid missing your flight, pay only through an official counter or airline desk and get a fresh official receipt.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hand over your passport, phone, wallet, or card to someone walking around the airport.
- Don’t pay a “helper” or anyone who approaches you away from an official desk, cashier, airline counter, or clearly marked airport office.
- Don’t argue about the rule in the moment; focus on what proof they need and what you can show within minutes.
- Don’t pay twice unless you have no practical alternative to continue your journey.
- Don’t pay again without asking for a receipt that shows what the payment was for.
- Don’t delete emails, app notifications, booking messages, or bank alerts while searching.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the exact acceptable proof.** Say: “What exact document or record will you accept as proof that this fee or tax has been paid?”
2. **Move the conversation to an official point.** Use the airline check-in desk, gate desk, marked airport authority counter, finance office, cashier, or information desk. Avoid anyone who is not behind an official desk or clearly identifiable as airport or airline staff.
3. **Check your booking for possible proof.** Open the airline app, travel agent app, or “manage booking” page and look for words such as **receipt**, **invoice**, **payment confirmation**, **e-ticket**, **taxes**, **fees**, or **charges**.
4. **Search your email quickly.** Search the airline or travel agent name plus **receipt**, **invoice**, **itinerary**, **e-ticket**, **tax**, **fee**, **departure**, and your booking reference.
5. **Show the payment trail if they will accept it.**
   - For card or mobile wallet payments, show the transaction details with date, merchant name, amount, and currency.
   - For cash payments, ask whether the official desk can check the payment against your booking reference, passenger name, flight number, or any receipt number you remember.
6. **Ask for reissue or verification.** Say: “Can you reprint the receipt, reissue proof of payment, or confirm in your system whether this passenger or booking has paid?”
7. **If the fee may already be in your ticket, show the ticket breakdown.** Airlines selling flight tickets in the UK generally show taxes, fees, and charges as part of the ticket price breakdown. This may help if the question is whether a ticket tax was already included, but it may not prove a separate local airport fee.
8. **If you have to pay again, make it traceable.** Pay only at an official desk or counter, preferably by card if available. Ask for a receipt showing the fee or tax name, date, time, amount, currency, desk or location, and any booking or passenger identifier. Photograph the receipt immediately.
9. **Make a brief note before you move on.** Record the airport, date, time, desk or location, staff name or ID if visible, what you were asked for, what you showed, and whether you paid again.

## What can wait
- You do not need to research the whole rule at the airport.
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, dispute the charge, or claim a refund.
- You do not need to contact the nearest British embassy, high commission or consulate, or the FCDO consular contact route, unless something more serious is happening, such as being detained, threatened, unable to access essential documents, or unable to leave a place safely.
- You do not need to sort every receipt in your email account; find enough proof for the desk in front of you, then preserve the rest for later.

## Important reassurance
This can happen where a fee is separate from the ticket, a system has not updated, a receipt was issued only once, or staff need a specific document before boarding. Keeping the question narrow — “what proof will you accept right now?” — gives you the best chance of resolving it without extra stress.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation at the airport, reduce the risk of being scammed, and preserve proof if you have to pay again. Later complaints, charge disputes, travel insurance questions, or refund requests may need help from the airline, travel agent, card provider, insurer, airport authority, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, travel, or professional advice. Airport fees and departure taxes vary by country, airport, airline, ticket type, and local procedure. UK consular services can sometimes help British nationals with serious problems abroad, but they generally cannot pay airport fees, travel costs, or other bills for you.
