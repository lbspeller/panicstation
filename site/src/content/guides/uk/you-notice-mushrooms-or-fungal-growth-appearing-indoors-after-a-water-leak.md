# What to do if… you notice mushrooms or fungal growth appearing indoors after a water leak (UK)

## Short answer
Keep people away from the growth, check for immediate hazards, and stop the water source if you can do that safely. Then document it and report it for urgent drying, inspection, and repair of the cause.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore visible mushrooms, mould, or fungal growth after a leak; it can mean moisture is still present.
- Do not dry-sweep, vacuum, scrape, or brush the growth, because this can spread material into the air.
- Do not mix cleaning chemicals, especially bleach with anything containing ammonia.
- Do not paint, seal, or cover the area as a first fix; it can hide moisture and delay proper repair.
- Do not pull down plaster, boards, insulation, or flooring if the area is wet, sagging, mouldy, or contaminated.
- Do not use mains-powered electrical items in the wet area unless you are sure it is safe.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safe before anything else.** Keep people out if there is water near sockets, switches, appliances, light fittings, a consumer unit, or a sagging or bulging ceiling. If you can safely switch off power at the consumer unit without standing in water or touching wet equipment, do that; otherwise keep away and get urgent electrical or emergency repair help.

2. **Stop the water source if you can.** If it is a plumbing leak, turn off the relevant isolation valve or the main stop tap if you can find it safely. If the leak is from a flat above, a roof, a communal pipe, or a managed building area, contact the neighbour, managing agent, freeholder, concierge, emergency repairs line, or building insurer contact as appropriate.

3. **Separate the affected space.** Close the door to the room or area. Keep children, pets, older adults, and anyone with asthma, a lung condition, allergies, or a weakened immune system away from the growth and damp materials.

4. **Limit your own exposure if you have to enter.** Wear gloves, use eye protection if available, and use a well-fitting mask if you have one. Move slowly and avoid touching or disturbing the mushrooms, mould, damp plaster, wet insulation, or contaminated soft furnishings.

5. **Start safe drying without spreading the problem.** Open windows in the affected room if safe. Keep the affected room separated from the rest of the home where you can. Use heating or a dehumidifier only if the electrics and appliance location are dry and safe. Move dry belongings away from the damp area; keep wet, musty items separate from dry rooms.

6. **Do not try to identify the species.** Take clear photos or video of the mushrooms or fungal growth, damp patches, the leak source, damaged flooring, ceilings, walls, furniture, and any visible water. Write down the date, the room, what leaked, what still feels wet, and any musty smell.

7. **Report it in writing if you rent or live in managed housing.** Tell the landlord, agent, housing provider, or building manager that there has been a leak and there is now visible fungal growth indoors. Ask for the cause of the leak to be fixed, the area inspected, and wet or mouldy materials dried, cleaned, removed, or replaced as needed. Keep copies of messages and photos.

8. **Get extra help if it is not a small, dry, surface patch.** Arrange a professional inspection or urgent repair help if growth is spreading, more than one area is affected, a ceiling or wall has been soaked, the smell remains after ventilation, insulation or flooring may be wet, there is sewage or floodwater, or anyone’s breathing symptoms are worse.

9. **Escalate if the home is not being made safe.** If a serious rented-home damp or mould problem is not being addressed, contact your local council or local authority housing or environmental health team and ask how to request an inspection or support.

## What can wait
- You do not need to know the exact species of mushroom, mould, or fungus right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether every damaged item can be saved.
- You do not need to prove whether the cause is condensation, a plumbing leak, roof water, or a neighbour’s leak before reporting what you can see.
- You do not need to deep-clean the whole room before the leak source and damp materials have been dealt with.

## Important reassurance
Finding mushrooms or fungal growth indoors can feel alarming, but the first steps are practical: keep people away, stop more water getting in, document the damage, and get the damp materials dealt with. The problem is usually much easier to control once the moisture source is fixed and the wet area is properly dried or removed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Hidden damp inside walls, ceilings, insulation, flooring, or shared building areas may need specialist inspection, repair, drying, cleaning, or replacement.

## Important note
This is general information, not building, legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Call 999 if someone has severe breathing difficulty, chest pain, collapse, or another medical emergency. For urgent non-emergency health symptoms, use the urgent health advice route for your part of the UK, contact a GP, or another appropriate health professional. If there may be electrical danger, structural damage, sewage, or contaminated water, keep out of the area and use appropriate emergency or repair services.
