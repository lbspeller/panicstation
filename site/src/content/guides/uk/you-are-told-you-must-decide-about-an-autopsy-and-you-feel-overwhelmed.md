# What to do if… you are told you must decide about an autopsy and you feel overwhelmed (UK)

## Short answer
Identify if the post-mortem is legally required by a Coroner or Procurator Fiscal, or if it is a voluntary hospital request. For voluntary requests, you have the right to a 24-hour pause before deciding, while for legal investigations, you can still formally record objections or requests for non-invasive methods.

## Do not do these things
- Don't sign any consent or authorisation forms until you have read them in full and had your questions answered.
- Don't assume a Coroner or Procurator Fiscal investigation requires your permission; they have the legal right to proceed, but they must still listen to your concerns.
- Don't agree to the retention of organs or tissue for research or teaching unless you explicitly want to support those activities.
- Don't feel pressured to resolve family disagreements immediately; ask the authorities to wait while you appoint one lead contact person.
- Don't ignore religious or cultural requirements; state these immediately as they may trigger an urgent review or a less invasive examination.

## What to do now
1. **Ask exactly which authority is requesting the examination and why.** 
   - In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland, ask: "Is this a coroner’s post-mortem or a hospital-consented post-mortem?"
   - In Scotland, ask: "Is this a Procurator Fiscal instruction or a hospital authorisation request?"
   - **Confirm why the cause of death cannot be established without this step.**

2. **Speak to the Medical Examiner’s office if the death was in England or Wales.** 
   - **Ask the Medical Examiner (ME) or their officer to explain the clinical reasons for the post-mortem.** 
   - By law, all deaths not investigated by a coroner must now be reviewed by an independent Medical Examiner. They are there to explain the process and listen to your concerns.

3. **Request a 24-hour pause for any "consented" or "hospital" post-mortem.** 
   - **State clearly: "The Human Tissue Authority recommends a 24-hour period for families to consider this. I would like to use that time."**
   - Use this window to speak with other relatives or your religious community.

4. **Formally record any objections based on faith or culture immediately.** 
   - **Tell the Coroner’s Officer or Procurator Fiscal’s office: "We have religious / cultural objections to an invasive examination and request a non-invasive approach."**
   - Ask if Digital Autopsy (CT scanning) or a limited external examination is possible in your specific area.

5. **Clarify who is the legally "appropriate person" to make the decision.** 
   - **Do not assume "next of kin" is the legal term used; ask the bereavement officer to check the hierarchy under the Human Tissue Act 2004 (or 2006 in Scotland).**
   - There is a strict legal list of who can sign (e.g., spouse, then children, then parents).

6. **Ask for a single point of contact for all future updates.** 
   - **Get the direct name and telephone number of the Coroner's Officer or the Fiscal's bereavement liaison.**
   - Request that they do not call multiple family members, as this leads to conflicting information.

7. **Ask about the practical timeline for the funeral.** 
   - **Ask: "When do you expect the body to be released to the funeral director?"**
   - Knowing the likely day of release can reduce the panic regarding funeral bookings and viewing arrangements.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on the long-term storage of tissue samples today.
- You do not need to read the full pathology report immediately; it can take weeks or months to be finalized.
- You do not need to decide which funeral director to use before the post-mortem is even scheduled.
- You do not need to provide a life history of the deceased during the initial "overwhelmed" phone call.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel "frozen" or unable to process information when asked about an autopsy. These systems are designed to move quickly, but the staff are trained to handle your hesitation. Slowing the conversation down by asking for things in writing is a valid and helpful way to regain control of the situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the initial hours after a post-mortem is requested. It does not cover legal challenges, inquest procedures, or long-term bereavement counseling.

## Important note
Post-mortem procedures and the legal rights of the bereaved differ significantly between England / Wales / Northern Ireland (Coroner system) and Scotland (Procurator Fiscal system). This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice.
