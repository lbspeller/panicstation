# What to do if… police ask you to come to the station to collect property and you are unsure why (UK)

## Short answer
Verify the request through an official police 101 number first, and clarify if a voluntary interview or witness statement is also being requested. If you are asked any questions about an incident or offence, exercise your right to free legal advice before answering.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attend the station without confirming the specific officer’s name and the reference number for the property.
- Do not enter an interview room or answer questions about an offence "just to clear things up" without a solicitor.
- Do not volunteer your phone, passwords, or cloud account access as a condition of receiving your property back.
- Do not sign any document that includes a statement of facts or an admission of guilt if you only intended to sign a property receipt.
- Do not assume that being asked to "come in voluntarily" means you cannot be arrested if you refuse to answer questions or try to leave.

## What to do now
1. **Call the police force directly via 101 to verify the request.**  
   **Ask for the officer’s name, collar number, and the incident reference number (CAD or OIC reference).** If the officer is unavailable, ask the desk staff to verify if there is an active note on the system regarding property release for your name.

2. **Confirm the exact purpose of the attendance over the phone.**  
   **Ask specifically: "Is this visit only for property collection, or am I being asked to provide a statement or undergo a voluntary interview?"** Write down the name of the person who gives you this answer and the time of the call.

3. **Request a specific appointment time for the front desk.**  
   **Ask if the property can be left at the front counter for collection rather than meeting an officer in a private room.** If you are told you must meet an officer, treat the meeting as a potential voluntary interview.

4. **Arrange for legal advice if an interview is mentioned.**  
   **Tell the officer: "I am happy to collect my property, but if you wish to interview me, I require a solicitor to be present."** 
   - In England and Wales, you have a right to the duty solicitor for a voluntary interview at the station.
   - In Scotland, ask for the "Solicitor Contact Line" or your own lawyer before any questioning starts.

5. **Prepare your identification and documentation.**  
   **Gather a valid photo ID (passport or driving licence) and any original property seizure receipts (Exhibit forms) you were issued.** Bring a charged phone but keep it locked and in your pocket while inside the station.

6. **Identify "interview cues" upon arrival at the station.**  
   **Notice if you are led away from the public front desk into a restricted area, a room with fixed microphones/cameras, or a room with a "Notice to Detained Persons" on the wall.** If this happens, immediately ask: "Am I being interviewed under caution?"

7. **Isolate the property collection from the conversation.**  
   **State clearly: "I am here to collect my property [Reference Number]; I am not prepared to discuss the investigation without legal representation."** If they insist on a "quick chat" before handing over items, repeat that you require a solicitor.

8. **Inspect all items before signing the release form.**  
   **Check for damage, missing components, or items listed on the receipt that are not present.** If there is a discrepancy, ask the officer to note it on the form before you sign, or ask for a copy of the discrepancy report.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide an immediate explanation for why the property was in your possession or how it was used.
- You do not need to decide whether to file a complaint about the seizure or the condition of the property while you are still at the station.
- You do not need to agree to "further assistance" or "follow-up calls" during the collection process.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a call from the police is a high-stress event that often triggers a "fight or flight" response. It is normal to feel suspicious or anxious about their motives. Collecting property is a standard administrative procedure, and by asking for clarity and legal representation, you are following established legal protections, not "looking guilty."

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate steps to verify police contact and manage the physical collection of property while protecting your legal rights. It does not cover legal defense strategies, property law disputes, or long-term investigation procedures.

## Important note
Police procedures vary between England/Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. This information is for general stabilization and is not legal advice. If you are suspected of an offence, always consult a qualified solicitor.
