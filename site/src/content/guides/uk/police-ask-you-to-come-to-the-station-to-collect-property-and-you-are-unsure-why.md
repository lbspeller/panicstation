# What to do if… police ask you to come to the station to collect property and you are unsure why (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as two separate issues: collecting property and possible questioning. Before you go, ask whether any interview, statement, or questions about an offence are planned; if questioning starts, ask for legal advice before answering.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t “pop in quickly” without first asking whether you are expected to be interviewed, questioned, or asked to give a statement.
- Don’t try to “clear it up” by chatting informally at the front desk or on the phone about events, messages, other people, or why the property was taken.
- Don’t unlock, scroll through, or show your phone voluntarily just because you are there for property collection.
- Don’t sign anything you do not understand, especially anything that looks like a statement, interview record, consent form, disclaimer, or property release form.
- Don’t assume “voluntary” means casual or consequence-free if the police start asking about a suspected offence.
- Don’t rely only on a number in a text message if you are unsure the request is genuine.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the request through an official route.**  
   Use the police force website, 101, or another official contact route to confirm the request is real. Ask to be put through to the officer, property office, or station desk dealing with the property.

2. **Ask what the visit is for before agreeing a time.**  
   Ask:
   - “Is this only to collect property?”
   - “Is any interview, statement, or questioning planned?”
   - “Am I being asked to attend voluntarily?”
   - “What is the incident or property reference number?”
   - “Who is dealing with the release of the property?”

3. **Ask exactly what property is being released.**  
   Ask for a plain description of the item or items, whether anything is still being kept, and whether you need to bring ID, proof of ownership, a property receipt, a case reference, or written authority from someone else.

4. **If they will not clearly say it is only property collection, treat it as possible questioning.**  
   Say: “I am willing to arrange collection of property, but I want legal advice before answering any questions about an incident or offence.”  
   Legal advice arrangements and police terminology differ across the UK, so ask the station how to speak to a solicitor before any interview or questioning.

5. **Try to keep the appointment limited to property release.**  
   Ask for a time to collect the property at the front counter or property office. When you arrive, say: “I am here to collect property only. Is any interview or statement being requested?”

6. **Prepare a minimal-information visit.**  
   Bring any ID, property receipt, proof of ownership, or reference number the police have asked for, and ask in advance what they will accept if you do not have photo ID. Keep your phone locked. If anyone asks to see or search it, ask what legal power or consent form they are relying on and ask for legal advice before doing anything.

7. **Have support nearby if that helps you stay calm.**  
   You can arrange for a trusted person to travel with you, wait nearby, or check in with you afterwards. Do not assume they will be allowed into private police areas, interviews, or legal consultations.

8. **If questioning starts, pause immediately.**  
   If anyone starts asking about what happened, who was involved, your messages, your movements, or an alleged offence, say: “Am I being interviewed or questioned about an offence? I want legal advice before answering.” Then stop discussing the incident.

9. **Before leaving with the property, check the handover paperwork.**  
   Ask for a receipt, inventory, or release record showing what has been returned. Check the item, obvious damage, missing parts, SIM cards, chargers, keys, documents, or storage media before you leave. If something is missing or damaged, say so at the time and ask who to contact.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain the incident today to be polite or cooperative.
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, seek compensation, challenge the seizure, or “set the record straight.”
- You do not need to agree on the spot to a phone search, account access, statement, or interview.
- You do not need to remember every legal rule before you go; the immediate job is to confirm the purpose, avoid unplanned questioning, and get legal advice if questioning begins.

## Important reassurance
Property returns can be routine, including recovered property, items no longer needed, or items authorised for release. It is still sensible to slow the situation down, ask clear questions, and avoid being pulled into an unplanned conversation about an investigation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: confirming the request, collecting property safely, and avoiding irreversible mistakes in the moment. If there is an investigation behind the request, later decisions may need help from a criminal defence solicitor or another specialist adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Police powers, legal advice arrangements, and terminology can differ across England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, and can also depend on the facts of the situation.
