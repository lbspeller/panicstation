# What to do if… you receive an email saying your “case number” requires urgent action but you cannot verify the sender (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the email as a phishing attempt and **do not click any links or open any attachments**. Verify the claim independently by contacting the relevant department using a known official phone number or website.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, open PDF/ZIP attachments, or scan QR codes within the email.
- Don’t provide personal information such as your National Insurance number, date of birth, or bank details to "confirm your identity".
- Don’t use any contact details provided in the email, including phone numbers or "live chat" links.
- Don’t pay any "fines", "late fees", or "settlements" requested via the email, especially those demanding bank transfers, gift cards, or cryptocurrency.
- Don’t assume formal language, official-looking logos, or a specific "case reference" proves the email is genuine.
- Don’t reply to the email to "unsubscribe" or "ask for more information", as this confirms your email address is active.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the communication and stop interacting with the message.** **Focus on preventing any data transfer or financial loss by closing the email and not clicking further.**
2. **Check for immediate technical exposure if you have already interacted.**
   - If you clicked a link or opened an attachment, disconnect the device from the internet (Wi-Fi and mobile data) immediately.
   - If you entered a password, change that password on a separate, secure device.
3. **Capture evidence for reporting without further engagement.**
   - Take a screenshot of the email showing the sender’s full email address, the subject line, and the "case number".
   - Keep the original email in your inbox (do not delete it yet) so it can be forwarded to authorities.
4. **Verify the claim through a trusted, independent route.**
   - **If it claims to be from the Police:** call **101** from a different phone line and ask to verify the identity of the officer or the existence of the case number.
   - **If it involves money or banks:** call **159** (the secure break-the-spell hotline) to be connected safely to your bank’s fraud department.
   - **If it claims to be from HM Courts & Tribunals Service (HMCTS):** find the official contact for the specific court or the "Civil National Business Centre" via GOV.UK and call them directly.
5. **Report the phishing attempt to the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC).**
   - Forward the suspicious email to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.
   - If you received a related text message, forward it to **7726** (it is free to do so).
6. **Report fraud or attempted fraud to the national service.**
   - If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report to **Report Fraud** (which replaced Action Fraud in 2026) via `reportfraud.police.uk` or by calling **0300 123 2040**.
   - If you are in Scotland, call **101** to report the matter to Police Scotland.
7. **Secure your financial accounts if you shared details or paid money.**
   - Call your bank immediately using the number on the back of your card.
   - Inform them that you have been targeted by a "case number" phishing scam.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to determine if the "case number" is in a valid format or matches local records yourself.
- You do **not** need to justify why you didn't respond to the "urgent" deadline mentioned in the email.
- You do **not** need to seek legal representation until you have confirmed the communication is legitimate.
- You do **not** need to worry about "contempt of court" or "arrest warrants" mentioned in an unverified email.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of panic when receiving messages that threaten legal action or arrest. These emails are intentionally designed by professionals to trigger a "fight or flight" response, making it harder to think clearly. Taking a few minutes to verify the sender through a different channel is a safe and standard procedure that official bodies expect and support.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying and reporting phishing. It does not provide legal advice or cover the management of genuine ongoing court proceedings.

## Important note
Official UK government departments, the police, and courts rarely initiate contact regarding "urgent" legal action or payments via unsolicited emails containing attachments. If you believe there is an immediate risk of physical harm, call 999.
