# What to do if… police ask to search your car and you want to know your options (UK)

## Short answer
Stay calm, keep your hands visible, and do not physically block the search. Ask whether they are asking you to cooperate or saying they have a legal power to search; if you do not agree, calmly say: “I do not consent to a search.”

## Do not do these things
- Do not physically resist, block doors, close the boot on an officer, or interfere with the search.
- Do not argue about the law at the roadside.
- Do not say you agree just because you feel rushed, if you do not want to agree.
- Do not make sudden movements towards pockets, bags, the glovebox, or the boot without saying what you are doing first.
- Do not guess, lie, or give long explanations to fill the silence.
- Do not tell passengers to argue, crowd the officers, or interrupt the search.

## What to do now
1. **Make the stop calmer.** Pull over safely if you have not already, switch off the engine, keep your hands visible, and speak slowly. If it is dark, consider turning on the interior light.
2. **Ask what is happening.** Say: “Are you asking me to cooperate, or are you saying you have a legal power to search this car?”
3. **Ask whether you are free to go.** Say: “Am I being detained, or am I free to leave?” If they say you are not free to leave, stay calm and do not try to leave.
4. **If they are not giving a legal power, state your choice clearly.** If you do not want the car searched, say: “I do not consent to a search.” If they still intend to search, ask: “What power are you using?”
5. **If they say they have a power to search, ask for the basics.** Ask:
   - “What power are you using?”
   - “What are you looking for?”
   - “What are your grounds for searching this car?”
   - “What is your name or number, and what station or police service are you attached to?”
6. **Ask about the search record.** Say: “How can I get the search record or reference number?” If they cannot give it there and then, ask how to get it later.
7. **Do not handle items unless told to.** If they ask you to open the boot, glovebox, bags, or a compartment, move slowly and say what you are about to do before moving.
8. **Keep passengers quiet and visible.** Say calmly: “Please keep your hands visible and do not interrupt.” If someone records, they should keep distance, avoid blocking anyone, and follow lawful instructions.
9. **Make a brief note as soon as you safely can.** Write down the time, location, officer details if given, police service, what power or reason was stated, what was searched, what was taken, and whether you received a record or reference number.
10. **If you feel unsafe, focus on getting through the stop safely.** You can ask questions and make a complaint later. The immediate aim is to avoid escalation and reach a safer pause.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide at the roadside whether the search was lawful.
- You do not need to make a complaint during the stop.
- You do not need to explain your whole journey, personal life, or plans just because you feel nervous.
- You do not need to decide now whether to get legal advice, unless you are arrested, something is seized, or the situation becomes more serious.

## Important reassurance
It is common to freeze, comply automatically, or feel intimidated when police ask to search a car. Calmly asking what power they are using can help you keep your options open without escalating the stop.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: staying safe, avoiding irreversible mistakes, and keeping a clear record of what happened. Later decisions, including complaints, seized property, arrest, or possible unlawful treatment, may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Stop and search rules, search records, and complaint routes can depend on where in the UK the stop happened and which power was used. In an emergency call 999; for non-emergency police contact, 101 is commonly used.
