# What to do if… you receive a plea offer or case resolution proposal with a short deadline to decide (UK)

## Short answer
Request an immediate pause and contact your criminal defence solicitor (or the duty solicitor) to review the written terms before accepting or signing anything. Do not feel compelled to provide an answer before your legal representative has confirmed they have received all relevant prosecution evidence.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree to any terms verbally over the phone or in a holding cell without a solicitor present.
- Don’t sign any "basis of plea" or "agreed facts" documents that contain details you know to be inaccurate.
- Don’t assume a short deadline is legally binding if the prosecution has not yet met their statutory disclosure obligations.
- Don’t discuss the merits of the offer with other defendants, prison staff, or police officers.
- Don’t ignore the deadline entirely; failing to respond via a solicitor can result in the loss of sentence "credit" for an early plea.

## What to do now
1. **Contact your solicitor immediately and state that you have a time-sensitive resolution proposal.** **Tell them the exact time and date the offer is set to expire according to the prosecution.** If you do not have a solicitor, ask the court or police station staff to contact the duty solicitor or provide details for legal aid applications.
2. **Request a full copy of the written offer including the proposed "basis of plea."** **Ensure you have the specific wording of the charges you would be pleading to and which charges the prosecution intends to drop or "leave on file."** If the offer was made verbally, write down the name of the person who made it and the exact time it was communicated.
3. **Ask your solicitor to confirm if "Initial Details of the Prosecution Case" (IDPC) or full disclosure has been received.** **Verify whether key evidence such as CCTV, forensic reports, or witness statements are still missing before you decide to waive your right to a trial.** Under 2026 rules, specific time limits apply to the service of indictments and evidence; your solicitor can check if these have been met.
4. **Instruct your solicitor to formally request an extension of the deadline if you are in physical or mental distress.** **Ask them to send a written request to the prosecutor and, if a hearing is imminent, to notify the court that the deadline provided is insufficient for proper legal advice.**
5. **Identify the specific court procedure relevant to your jurisdiction.** 
   - In England and Wales, ask if the decision can be deferred to the Plea and Trial Preparation Hearing (PTPH).
   - In Scotland, ask how the offer affects a potential Section 76 expedited procedure or the Preliminary Hearing (High Court) / First Diet (Sheriff Court).
   - In Northern Ireland, ask how the 2026 Sentencing Bill provisions, such as mandatory aggravators or disclosure rules, affect the proposal.
6. **Request a "sanity check" of the immediate non-custodial consequences.** **Ask your solicitor to list the specific impact the plea will have on your immigration status, professional registrations, and any mandatory orders such as driving bans or restraining orders.**
7. **Document any pressure or coercion you feel regarding the timeline.** **If you are told you "must" decide today or lose all benefits, ask your solicitor to place this on the court record.** This ensures that any later challenge to the "voluntariness" of the plea is supported by a contemporary record.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your long-term mitigation strategy or gather character references today.
- You do not need to prepare a personal statement for the sentencing judge while the plea is still being negotiated.
- You do not need to determine how you will pay any potential fines or compensation until the plea is finalized.
- You do not need to contact the prosecution or police yourself to "explain" your decision.

## Important reassurance
Short deadlines are a common procedural tactic used to manage court backlogs, but they do not override your right to competent legal advice. Taking a few hours or an extra day to consult with a solicitor is a standard part of the process and is understood by the court.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling rushed legal proposals. It does not replace the specific advice of a qualified solicitor who has access to your case file and the specific evidence against you.

## Important note
Legal procedures regarding pleas and sentencing discounts changed in 2026, particularly in Scotland regarding jury majorities and in Northern Ireland regarding sentencing principles. Ensure your solicitor is using the most current jurisdictional guidelines. If you are in custody and feel unable to cope with the pressure, alert the medical wing or a custodial officer immediately.
