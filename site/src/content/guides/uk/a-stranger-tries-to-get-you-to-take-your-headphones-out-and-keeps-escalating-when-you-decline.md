# What to do if… a stranger tries to get you to take your headphones out and keeps escalating when you decline (UK)

## Short answer
Create physical distance immediately and move toward a staffed, well-lit, or busy location such as a shop, station office, or café. If the person continues to follow you, blocks your path, or you feel in immediate danger, call **999**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take your headphones out or turn off your music if you feel unsafe.
- Do not apologize for not wanting to talk or engage.
- Do not move into quieter areas, alleyways, or doorways to "hear them better".
- Do not stop moving in areas where you can be boxed in between walls, parked cars, or platform edges.
- Do not unlock your phone or open your bag while they are within reaching distance.
- Do not lead the person toward your home or workplace—stay in public areas until the situation is resolved.

## What to do now
1. **Change the geometry of the interaction immediately.** **Step sideways and back to create a physical gap, then continue moving toward a populated area with staff and cameras.** Aim for a shop counter, hotel reception, or a busier train carriage rather than staying in a static or isolated spot.
2. **Use a brief, neutral "boring" line to set a boundary.** **State "No," "Stop," or "I am not talking" once in a steady voice without providing justifications or entering a debate.** If they continue to speak, do not respond again; focus entirely on moving toward safety.
3. **Signal for bystander or staff assistance.** **Move into the line of sight of other people or staff and state clearly: "I am being followed/harassed; can I stand with you?"** In venues like pubs or bars, you can ask staff for "Angela" to signal you need discreet help under the national Ask for Angela scheme.
4. **Utilize the 61016 text service on the rail network.** **If you are on a train or at a station and the situation is not an immediate emergency, text your location and a description of the person to 61016.** British Transport Police monitor this 24/7; if you are being prevented from leaving or the threat escalates, call **999** instead.
5. **Set up an active safety line.** **Call a friend or family member, put them on speakerphone if you are in a populated area, and state your exact location and what is happening.** For example: "I am on [Street Name] walking toward [Shop Name]. A stranger is bothering me and following me. Stay on the line."
6. **Trigger an emergency response if the threat escalates.** **Call 999 immediately if they touch you, block your exit, make specific threats, or refuse to leave you alone after you have reached a staffed location.** Tell the operator you are being harassed, give your current location (use shop names or street signs), and describe the person's appearance and actions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if their behaviour is "serious enough" to be a crime before seeking help.
- You do not need to confront the person or explain why their behaviour is wrong.
- You do not need to provide your personal details or contact info to "calm them down".
- You do not need to review CCTV or write a formal statement until you are in a secure, private location.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a "freeze" response or to worry about being perceived as rude when someone is pushing your boundaries. Escalating behaviour after a polite "no" is a significant warning sign, and your priority is your own physical safety. Trusting your instinct to move away is a valid and protective response.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active public interaction. It does not cover long-term reporting or legal proceedings for stalking or harassment, which may require follow-up with local police once you are safe.

## Important note
Public harassment is a criminal offence in the UK under the Protection from Sex-Based Harassment in Public Act 2026 and the Public Order Act. This guide is for immediate safety only and does not constitute legal or security advice. Always follow the directions of emergency dispatchers.
