# What to do if… a medical provider says services will be paused unless an unexpected balance is paid immediately (UK)

## Short answer
Request a written, itemised statement of the balance and ask a clinician to confirm if the paused care is "urgent or immediately necessary." Under UK rules, urgent care must not be delayed for payment, though you remain liable for any legitimate charges later.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay a large sum immediately if you do not recognise the charges or have not received an itemised breakdown.
- Do not provide credit card details over the phone to an unexpected caller without verifying their identity through a trusted hospital or clinic switchboard.
- Do not sign new financial agreements or "repayment contracts" while in physical distress or under immediate pressure at a reception desk.
- Do not assume a high bill is correct; overseas visitors in England are often charged 150% of the standard NHS rate, which can lead to calculation errors.
- Do not ignore the demand; if care is non-urgent (elective), providers in England have a legal requirement to secure payment upfront.

## What to do now
1. **Request an immediate itemised statement and a written explanation of the "pause" in care.**  
   **Ask the billing staff or receptionist to provide:**  
   - A line-by-line breakdown of dates, services, and individual costs.  
   - The name of the specific department or clinician stating that care will be paused.  
   - A copy of any original price estimate or terms and conditions you previously signed.

2. **Ask a clinician to formally categorize the care as "urgent," "immediately necessary," or "non-urgent."**  
   **This distinction is critical because:**  
   - If care is "immediately necessary" (e.g., maternity care, life-saving surgery), it must proceed even if you cannot pay upfront.  
   - If care is "urgent" (cannot wait until you leave the UK), it must proceed without delay.  
   - Only "non-urgent" (routine or elective) care can be legally withheld pending payment for chargeable patients.

3. **Check if the specific service is exempt from all charges regardless of your residency status.**  
   **In the NHS, you cannot be charged for:**  
   - Accident and Emergency (A&E) services (until you are admitted as an inpatient).  
   - Diagnosis and treatment of specific infectious diseases (e.g., TB, food poisoning).  
   - Family planning services (excluding abortions or fertility treatment).  
   - Treatment for conditions caused by domestic or sexual violence.

4. **Contact the specific patient support body for your UK nation immediately.**  
   - **England:** Ask for the **Patient Advice and Liaison Service (PALS)** or the hospital's **Overseas Visitor Manager**.  
   - **Scotland:** Contact the **Patient Advice and Support Service (PASS)** via Citizens Advice Scotland (0800 917 2127).  
   - **Wales:** Contact the **People’s Enquiry and Resolution Service** or seek a "listening discussion" under the **Listening to People** framework (0808 801 0102).  
   - **Northern Ireland:** Contact the **Patient and Client Council (PCC)** support service (0800 917 0222).

5. **Request a formal "account hold" while the dispute is investigated.**  
   **State clearly:** "I dispute this balance and am seeking assistance from [PALS / PASS / Llais / PCC]. Please place a hold on this account and confirm in writing that no further administrative or recovery action will be taken until the itemised breakdown is reviewed."

6. **Verify the subscriber status if the provider is a private clinic or hospital.**  
   **Ask the billing department:**  
   - "Are you a subscriber to the **Independent Sector Complaints Adjudication Service (ISCAS)**?"  
   - "Can you provide your three-stage written complaints procedure now?"  
   If you have private insurance, call your insurer immediately to see if they have already authorized the payment or if the provider is billing you incorrectly for a "shortfall."

7. **Label any "goodwill" or partial payment as "paid under protest" if you must pay to continue routine care.**  
   **If you decide to pay a portion to avoid a pause in non-urgent care:**  
   - Get a printed receipt immediately.  
   - Ensure the receipt specifies which service/date the payment applies to.  
   - State in writing (email or letter) that the payment does not constitute an admission of the full debt.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle the entire balance today to begin a dispute process.
- You do not need to determine your "ordinary residence" status immediately if the care required is clinically urgent.
- You do not need to provide a formal "letter of complaint" in the first hour; a verbal request for a "listening discussion" (Wales) or PALS intervention (England) is sufficient.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when financial demands intersect with medical needs. UK healthcare systems have built-in advocacy services (PALS, PASS, Llais, PCC) specifically designed to step in when administrative or billing errors threaten patient care. Asking for a pause to verify a bill is a standard and protected right.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for billing disputes at the point of care. It does not cover long-term debt management, clinical negligence claims, or immigration appeals related to NHS debt.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Rules regarding NHS charging (especially for overseas visitors) and private healthcare contracts are complex and vary significantly between England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland. If your medical condition worsens, prioritize seeking emergency care via 999 or A&E regardless of billing status.
