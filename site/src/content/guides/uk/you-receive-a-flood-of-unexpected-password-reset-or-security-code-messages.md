# What to do if… you receive a flood of unexpected password reset or security code messages (UK)

## Short answer
Do not click links in the messages or share any codes. Secure your email account first, then look for one real security alert, purchase, or password-change message hiding in the flood.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click “reset password”, “verify”, “cancel”, or “unsubscribe” links inside the messages.
- Do not reply to the messages or call phone numbers shown in them.
- Do not bulk-delete everything immediately if you can avoid it; a real password change, purchase, or new-device alert may be buried in the noise.
- Do not share any one-time passcode, login code, or approval prompt with anyone.
- Do not turn off 2-step verification to “make it stop”.
- Do not use the same password on your email and other accounts.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safe pause**
   - Silence notifications briefly if they are making you rush.
   - Open a new browser tab and type your email provider’s address yourself, or use the official email app.
   - Do not use any links from the reset messages.

2. **Secure your email account first**
   - Change your email password to a long, unique password that you do not use anywhere else.
   - Turn on 2-step verification if it is not already on, and choose the strongest method the service offers.
   - In your email security settings, sign out of other devices or sessions if that option is available.
   - Review recent sign-ins, devices, recovery email addresses, and recovery phone numbers for anything you do not recognise.

3. **Check for hidden mailbox changes**
   - Look for forwarding settings you did not add.
   - Look for mail rules or filters that mark messages as read, archive them, delete them, or move them out of your inbox.
   - Remove any unknown forwarding address, rule, filter, recovery detail, or connected app.

4. **Search for the message that matters**
   - Search your mailbox for: `password changed`, `new sign-in`, `security alert`, `purchase`, `order`, `new device`, `two-factor`, `login code`, `recovery email`, `payment`.
   - Check spam, junk, archive, deleted items, and any folder that rules may have moved messages into.
   - Prioritise email, banking, payment apps, your mobile network, Apple, Google, Microsoft, and any account with payment details attached.

5. **Check important accounts the safe way**
   - For each important service, open the official app or type the service address yourself.
   - Check recent logins, devices, active sessions, recovery details, and security settings.
   - If anything looks wrong, change that account’s password, remove unknown sessions or devices, and turn on 2-step verification.

6. **Report suspicious emails**
   - Forward suspicious emails to report@phishing.gov.uk.
   - Mark the messages as spam or junk in your email app.
   - If you clicked a link or entered details, change the affected password from the official site or app, and check that account’s recovery details and active sessions.

7. **If money moved or an account was taken over**
   - Contact the affected bank, payment provider, or account provider using its official app, website, or the number on your card.
   - If the crime is in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report it to Report Fraud.
   - If you are in Scotland, or the crime happened there, contact Police Scotland on 101.

## What can wait
- You do not need to clean up every message right now.
- You do not need to close every account you do not recognise today.
- You do not need to work out exactly how your email address was targeted before securing your email and important accounts.
- You do not need to reply to senders to ask them to stop.

## Important reassurance
A password reset message often only means someone knows your email address. The immediate risk is clicking a bad link, approving a login, sharing a code, or missing a real alert hidden in the flood. Slowing down and using official apps or typed addresses is the safer move.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate harm and prevent lockouts. If you confirm an account takeover, fraud, or money loss, later decisions may need help from the provider, your bank, Report Fraud, Police Scotland, or a qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or other professional advice. If you are unsure whether a message is genuine, treat it as suspicious and access accounts only through official apps or addresses you type yourself.
