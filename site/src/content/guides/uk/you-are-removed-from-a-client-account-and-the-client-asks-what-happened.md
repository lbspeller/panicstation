# What to do if… you are removed from a client account and the client asks what happened (UK)

## Short answer
Do not guess, defend yourself, or explain internal matters to the client. Pause, keep any response neutral and brief, and ask your manager or the account owner for the exact approved wording and the new client contact.

## Do not do these things
- Do not tell the client you were removed, investigated, disciplined, sidelined, or treated unfairly unless you have written authorisation to say that.
- Do not blame a colleague, your employer, or the client.
- Do not share internal staffing decisions, disputes, access issues, security concerns, performance concerns, or personal information about anyone.
- Do not try to get back into client systems using another person’s login, shared passwords, screenshots, copied files, or informal workarounds.
- Do not forward client material to a personal account or download extra copies.
- Do not send a long emotional message to the client or post about the situation publicly.
- Do not resign, threaten legal action, or raise a formal complaint in the heat of the first message.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether you have been told not to contact the client.** If you have been told not to contact them, or you are unsure whether you may reply, do not send a client message. Forward the client’s message to your manager or the account owner and ask them to handle it.

2. **If a reply is expected and you are allowed to send one, use a neutral holding line only.** For example: “Thanks for your message. The account is being handled by our team, and I have asked the account owner to follow up with the right contact details.” Do not add a reason.

3. **Ask for the new contact and approved wording in writing.** Send your manager or the account owner three questions:
   - “Who is the named contact for this client now?”
   - “What exact wording am I authorised to use if the client asks what happened?”
   - “Should I stop all direct contact with this client?”

4. **Make a short factual timeline in approved work systems.** Note when access changed, what you were told, what the client asked, who you forwarded it to, and what you replied, if anything. Do not move work material into personal storage.

5. **Secure the client handover.** Put any unfinished client tasks, open risks, deadlines, promised follow-ups, and key documents into the normal handover route your workplace uses. Keep it factual and useful, not defensive.

6. **Keep client materials where they belong.** Do not take screenshots, copy files, export CRM records, or ask colleagues to pull information for you unless your manager has authorised it and it is part of the handover.

7. **If you are invited to a meeting about this, clarify what kind of meeting it is.** Ask: “Is this informal fact-finding or a formal process?” and “What concern or decision is being discussed?” If a formal disciplinary hearing or formal grievance meeting is arranged, ask whether you may be accompanied by a colleague or trade union representative.

8. **If you are being pushed to say something untrue or misleading to the client, pause.** Ask for the instruction in writing before sending anything. If needed, ask HR, your manager’s manager, or your trade union representative for help before replying.

9. **If you think this may relate to discrimination, victimisation, whistleblowing, or another protected concern, keep a separate factual note.** Record dates, words used, people involved, and documents that already exist. Do not turn that note into a client message.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, raise a formal grievance, threaten a claim, or try to clear your name with the client.
- You do not need to give the client a detailed explanation right now.
- You do not need to solve the whole workplace issue before making a safe handover.
- You do not need to argue about motive until you know whether this is a routine account change, an access control issue, or a formal employment process.

## Important reassurance
Being removed from a client account suddenly can feel exposing, especially if the client contacts you directly. A short neutral handoff, written instructions, and a clean factual record reduce the chance of making the situation worse while you find out what is happening.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, mainly for the first hours or day. If this becomes a formal disciplinary, grievance, discrimination, whistleblowing, data protection, regulated-sector, or dismissal issue, later decisions may need specialist help based on your contract, workplace policies, and the facts.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, employment, data protection, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. What you can say to a client depends on your role, contract, workplace policies, regulator rules if any, and the instructions you have been given.
