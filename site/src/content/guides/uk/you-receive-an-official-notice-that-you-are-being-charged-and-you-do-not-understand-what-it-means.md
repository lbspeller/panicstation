# What to do if… you receive an official notice that you are being charged and you do not understand what it means (UK)

## Short answer
Do not ignore the document. Locate the response deadline (usually 21 days) and any hearing date, then verify the notice through the official "Find a court or tribunal" service before contacting a criminal defence solicitor.

## Do not do these things
- Do not discard the envelope or the letter, even if you believe it is sent to the wrong person or address.
- Do not enter a plea, make a payment, or send a written explanation until you have confirmed the notice is genuine and sought legal advice.
- Do not use the phone numbers, QR codes, or web links provided in the letter until you have verified them independently via a gov.uk website.
- Do not contact any witnesses or other parties named in the paperwork.
- Do not post images of the charge sheet or notice on social media, as this may violate privacy rules or court restrictions.

## What to do now
1. **Locate the specific document name and any listed deadline immediately.** 
   - Identify if the title is a Single Justice Procedure Notice (SJPN), a Postal Requisition, a Summons, or a Notice of Charge.
   - Look for the "date of notice" and the "response required by" date; for an SJPN, this is strictly 21 days from the date shown on the notice.

2. **Note any specific court date, time, and location provided in the text.** 
   - If the document is a Postal Requisition or Summons, it will specify a date and time you must attend a Magistrates' Court.
   - Write these details down in a separate place and set a calendar reminder immediately.

3. **Verify the authenticity of the notice using an independent government directory.** 
   - Search for "Find a court or tribunal" on gov.uk to find the official contact details for the court named on the letter.
   - Call the court’s general enquiries line to confirm that the case reference number and the charges against you are legitimate.

4. **Contact a criminal defence solicitor to review the documents with you.** 
   - Search the Law Society directory for a solicitor specializing in "Criminal Litigation" in your local area.
   - Ask the solicitor if you are eligible for legal aid, which may cover the cost of your defence depending on your income and the nature of the charge.

5. **Notify the court immediately if you believe you have been charged in error.** 
   - If the name, address, or details do not match your identity, inform the court staff during your verification call.
   - Follow their instructions for returning the notice, but do not simply ignore it, as a warrant could still be issued in your name.

6. **Check for any immediate bail conditions or restrictions listed in the paperwork.** 
   - Look for phrases like "bail conditions," "do not contact," or "reside at" which may be in effect even before your first court date.
   - If you cannot comply with a condition (e.g., if you have moved house), tell your solicitor immediately so they can apply to vary the conditions.

7. **Request professional assistance if you have communication or accessibility needs.** 
   - Inform the court and your solicitor immediately if you require an interpreter, documents in an accessible format, or a hearing loop for court appearances.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to plead guilty or not guilty in the first few minutes of receiving the letter.
- You do not need to prepare your full defence or gather witness statements today.
- You do not need to notify your employer or family until you have confirmed the charge is genuine and understood the implications.

## Important reassurance
Legal documents are purposefully written in technical language that is difficult for most people to interpret. Feeling overwhelmed or confused by a charge notice is a common response and does not mean you have already been found guilty. There are established procedures to help you understand the charges and access legal representation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for receiving a criminal charge notice in the UK. It is not a substitute for legal advice and does not cover specific defence strategies or the details of the trial process.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. If you have been charged with a crime, you should consult a qualified criminal defence solicitor as soon as possible to protect your legal rights and ensure you meet all court deadlines.
