# What to do if… a partner threatens to accuse you of cheating to pressure you into sex (UK)

## Short answer
You do not have to agree to sex to stop a threat. Get to a safer pause, away from them if you can, and contact a confidential sexual violence or domestic abuse support service for help with the next safe step.

## Do not do these things
- Do not treat agreeing to sex as the only way to stay safe; try to create distance and get support instead.
- Do not argue your innocence or try to prove anything while they are pressuring you.
- Do not agree to meet them privately to talk it out if you feel pressured, intimidated, or unsafe.
- Do not send long explanations or emotional messages in the moment; keep any necessary communication brief and practical.
- Do not delete messages, call logs, or voicemails in a panic if you might later want help understanding what happened.
- Do not assume you must report to the police right now; you can get support without reporting.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** If you can, end the interaction: go to a different room, leave the home, or get to somewhere with other people nearby, such as a shop, café, friend, relative, or neighbour. If you feel in immediate danger, call **999**.
2. **Use one clear line and stop negotiating.** If you need words, use: “I’m not having sex. Do not threaten me. I’m ending this conversation now.” Then disengage rather than debating.
3. **Keep any necessary communication brief and safer.** If you must communicate, use short written messages where possible. Only turn off read receipts, location sharing, or shared-device access if it is safe and unlikely to be noticed immediately.
4. **Preserve the threat only if safe.** If you may want support later, keep any messages, voicemails, or call logs somewhere they cannot access, but do not do this if it could put you at risk.
5. **Contact specialist sexual violence support without pressure to report.**
   - **England and Wales:** Rape Crisis England & Wales run the free, confidential 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line by phone and online chat for people aged 16+.
   - **Scotland:** Rape Crisis Scotland offer a free national helpline, text, and email support.
   - **Northern Ireland:** The Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline offers 24/7 support by phone and email.
6. **Use a sexual assault referral route if you want medical or practical help.** In England and Wales, a Sexual Assault Referral Centre can offer medical, practical, and emotional support. In Scotland, NHS SARCS can help arrange care. In Northern Ireland, The Rowan is the regional Sexual Assault Referral Centre. These services can usually explain options without forcing you to report.
7. **Get domestic abuse support if you live together or fear escalation.** A domestic abuse helpline can help you think through safer ways to leave, stay elsewhere, remain safer at home, or manage phone and location risks.
8. **Tell one safe person what is happening.** A short message is enough: “My partner is pressuring me for sex with threats. Can I come over, or can you stay on the phone while I leave?” Having someone nearby can reduce isolation and risk.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this was “serious enough” or what label fits.
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report to police or make a formal complaint.
- You do not need to write the perfect message, explanation, timeline, or evidence file tonight.
- You do not need to make big relationship decisions in this moment; focus on safety and support first.

## Important reassurance
Consent means agreeing by choice, with the freedom and capacity to make that choice. Threats, pressure, and manipulation can mean real choice is not present. You deserve support even if no physical force was used, even if you froze, and even if you are not sure what happened “counts.”

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance for stabilising and reducing harm. Ongoing coercion, threats, or sexual pressure may need tailored safety planning with a specialist service.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If you are worried someone may monitor your device, consider using a safer phone or computer, or getting help in person.
