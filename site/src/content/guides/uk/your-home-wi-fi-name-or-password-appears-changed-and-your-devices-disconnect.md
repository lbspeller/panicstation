# What to do if… your home Wi-Fi name or password appears changed and your devices disconnect (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible router control problem, not just a Wi-Fi nuisance. Regain control of the router, change the router admin password, update it if possible, then create a new Wi-Fi password before reconnecting everything.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep retrying old Wi-Fi passwords over and over; it wastes time and may keep devices stuck in a connection loop.
- Don’t use banking, email, work, or other sensitive accounts over that Wi-Fi until the router is re-secured. Use mobile data or a trusted connection if something is urgent.
- Don’t assume it was your provider if the Wi-Fi name, password, or security setting changed without warning.
- Don’t use a simple or reused replacement Wi-Fi password.
- Don’t turn on remote administration or remote management as a quick fix unless your ISP specifically tells you how to secure it.
- Don’t factory reset every phone, laptop, or smart device right now; start with the router.

## What to do now
1. **Check you are dealing with your own router.**  
   Look at the router label, your ISP app, or your ISP account page for the expected Wi-Fi name. If you have an Ethernet cable, connect one computer directly to the router so you are not relying on the changed Wi-Fi to fix the changed Wi-Fi.

2. **Restart the router once.**  
   Turn it off at the plug, wait about 30 seconds, then turn it back on. If the Wi-Fi name or password still looks wrong, carry on as if the settings may have been changed.

3. **Use your ISP or router instructions to regain control.**  
   If you cannot log in, follow the reset instructions for your exact router model or ask your ISP to talk you through it. A factory reset usually clears unknown settings, but it can also remove your saved Wi-Fi name, password, and any custom setup.

4. **Change the router admin password first.**  
   This is different from the Wi-Fi password. Use a strong, unique admin password. If the router lets you change the admin username, do that too. If it offers multi-factor authentication or extra login checks, turn them on.

5. **Update the router before reconnecting everything.**  
   In the router admin page, ISP app, or ISP account portal, check for firmware or software updates and enable automatic updates if that option exists. If the router is no longer supported, ask your ISP or the manufacturer about replacement.

6. **Undo unfamiliar settings that could keep the problem going.**  
   Check these router settings and set them back to normal unless you deliberately use them:
   - DNS settings: set to automatic or ISP default unless you intentionally use a trusted custom DNS.
   - Port forwarding: remove rules you did not create.
   - UPnP or automatic port opening: disable it if you do not need it.
   - Remote administration or remote management: turn it off unless you have a specific, secured reason to use it.

7. **Create the Wi-Fi again safely.**  
   Use WPA2 or WPA3 security, not WEP, old WPA, or an open network. Set a new long Wi-Fi passphrase that you do not use anywhere else. Three random words can work well if they are not obvious personal words. Choose a Wi-Fi name that does not reveal your surname, flat number, or router model.

8. **Reconnect devices gradually.**  
   Start with your phone or computer, then add household devices one by one. Watch the router’s connected-devices list. If a device name is unclear, compare it with your own devices first. If it is still unknown, block or remove it and change the Wi-Fi password again.

9. **Secure accounts that could change the router.**  
   Change the password for your ISP account or router app. Turn on multi-factor authentication if the ISP offers it. Update the phone or computer you used to manage the router, and run a malware scan if you suspect that device may have been compromised.

10. **Report it if it connects to fraud, a scam, or money loss.**  
   If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, use Report Fraud for cyber crime or fraud. If you are in Scotland, contact Police Scotland; use 101 for non-emergencies and 999 if there is an immediate danger or crime in progress.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out who did it right now.
- You do not need to replace every device today.
- You do not need to decide today whether to change ISP.
- You can tidy smart home settings, guest networks, and device names later, once the router is under your control again.

## Important reassurance
A sudden Wi-Fi name or password change is unsettling, but the stabilising move is clear: regain router control first. Many home-network problems can be contained by resetting the router, changing the admin password, updating it, and reconnecting devices carefully.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If you use a mesh system, work VPN equipment, smart alarms, or a complex home setup, later decisions may need help from your ISP, device maker, employer IT team, or a trusted technical specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. It cannot confirm whether hacking happened or guarantee that a router is secure.
