# What to do if… police arrive at your home asking to speak with you and you are unsure why (UK)

## Short answer
Keep the door closed or on a chain, speak through the door to verify their identity and purpose, and do not let them inside unless they have a warrant or are acting to prevent immediate harm.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t open the door fully until you have confirmed who they are and why they are there.
- Don’t invite officers inside for a "chat" or out of politeness; once inside, they may observe items in plain view.
- Don’t hand over or unlock your mobile phone or computer for them to "check" something.
- Don’t physically resist or block the doorway if an officer states they are entering under a specific legal power.
- Don’t offer a timeline of your day or provide "clarifications" on the doorstep without legal advice.
- Don’t sign any paperwork, notebooks, or forms unless you have read them and understand the legal implications.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the entrance immediately.** If the door is open, close it or use a door chain. Speak through the door or a nearby window. 
2. **Request to see their warrant card.** Ask the officer to hold their identification up to a window or the door gap. 
   - Check for a photograph, the name of the police force, and a holographic emblem.
   - Note the **name** and **collar number** of every officer present.
3. **Verify their identity independently if you are suspicious.** Call **101** and ask the operator to confirm that the officers with those collar numbers are currently on duty and have been dispatched to your address.
   - If they are behaving aggressively or attempting to force entry without explaining why, call **999** immediately.
4. **Ask for the specific reason for the visit.** Use direct questions:
   - "**What is the purpose of this visit?**"
   - "**Am I a suspect in an investigation?**"
   - "**Am I under arrest or being detained?**"
5. **Establish the legal basis for entry if they ask to come inside.** If they do not have a warrant, ask: "**Under what power are you seeking entry?**"
   - If they are not using a power (such as Section 17 of PACE to save life or limb) and you do not want them inside, state clearly: "**I do not consent to you entering my home.**"
   - If they claim they have a warrant, ask them to pass a copy through the letterbox or hold it against the glass so you can read the address and the signature.
6. **Limit your responses to the caution.** If they begin to "caution" you (the "You do not have to say anything..." speech), stop speaking immediately.
   - Say: "**I will not answer any questions without a solicitor present.**"
   - If they offer a "voluntary interview" at your home, decline and ask for the request in writing so you can arrange for legal representation.
7. **Address any immediate safety or dependency issues.** If you are being arrested or must leave with them, state clearly if there are children, vulnerable adults, or pets in the house that require care.
   - Do not leave the house until you have confirmed who will be notified to take over these responsibilities.
8. **Record the interaction accurately.** If you have a phone or camera, you may record the interaction from inside your home. 
   - Note the date, time, vehicle registration numbers, and exactly what was said by the officers regarding their "powers" or "reasons."

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a statement or an "explanation" today.
- You do not need to decide whether to attend a voluntary interview while standing at the door.
- You do not need to justify why you are refusing entry; it is your right to do so unless a specific power applies.
- You do not need to look for or provide "evidence" to "prove your innocence" on the spot.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel an immediate sense of panic, guilt, or confusion when police arrive at your door, regardless of whether you have done anything wrong. This reaction is a standard physiological response to an unexpected encounter with authority. Taking time to verify their identity and legal standing is a protective measure, not a sign of obstruction.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an initial doorstep encounter. It does not cover long-term legal strategy, court procedures, or specific advice for Scotland or Northern Ireland where legal terminology and police powers differ.

## Important note
This information applies to England and Wales. Police have broad powers to enter without a warrant in emergencies (to save life, prevent serious damage, or pursuit of a suspect for an indictable offence). If officers enter your home, do not use force to stop them; instead, state your lack of consent clearly and seek legal advice immediately after the encounter.
