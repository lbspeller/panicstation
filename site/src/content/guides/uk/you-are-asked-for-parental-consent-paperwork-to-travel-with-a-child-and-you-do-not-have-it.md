# What to do if… you are asked for parental consent paperwork to travel with a child and you do not have it (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and ask exactly what document they will accept. If staff will accept an email, scan, or photo, try to get written permission from everyone with parental responsibility sent to you immediately. If you cannot get permission, or you are not sure you have it, do not try to push through; switch to delaying travel safely.

## Do not do these things
- Do not argue your way past check-in, gate, or border staff, or keep re-joining queues hoping for a different answer.
- Do not present altered, backdated, or template paperwork signed by someone who has not actually agreed.
- Do not claim you have permission if you do not.
- Do not put the child on a flight with another adult unless the airline has confirmed it is acceptable for that route and destination.
- Do not let time pressure push you into an escalated dispute in front of the child.

## What to do now
1. **Ask what is missing and what they will accept.**  
   Say: “What exact document are you missing, and what would you accept instead today, such as an email, scan, signed letter, or court order?” Note the staff member’s name, desk, and wording.

2. **Check whether an order already covers travel.**  
   In England and Wales, if a Child Arrangements Order says the child lives with you, you can generally take the child abroad for up to 28 days without getting the other parent’s permission, unless that order or another court order says you cannot. Read the wording carefully and do not rely on a summary or memory of the order. If you have any order, find the clearest photo or PDF of the whole order now and show it. If the order is from Scotland or Northern Ireland, or you are not sure what it allows, pause and verify before relying on it.

3. **Ask who is requiring the paperwork.**  
   Ask: “Is this your airline policy, or a destination or transit-country rule you are applying?” This matters because an airline may refuse boarding if it is not satisfied with documents for the route.

4. **Try to obtain fast written consent from everyone with parental responsibility.**  
   Call or text calmly and ask for a signed and dated letter, sent as a photo or scan if staff say they will accept that format. Ask the signer to include their full name, contact details, the child’s full name, the travel dates, destination, address of stay if known, and a clear sentence saying they give permission for this trip. Add passport numbers and dates of birth if available and useful.

5. **Gather documents that show the relationship and reduce doubt.**  
   Have ready, on paper or as clear phone photos, the child’s birth or adoption certificate, any court order, and any document explaining a different surname, such as a marriage certificate or change-of-name document.

6. **Ask for a document-check supervisor early.**  
   Ask the airline or border staff to review what you have and tell you the least they will accept today. If they will accept an emailed or photographed letter, ask the person giving consent to send it while you are still with staff.

7. **If you cannot obtain consent or there is a dispute, stop the journey for now.**  
   Ask the airline about rebooking options, fees, and luggage retrieval. Move to a quieter place with power and Wi-Fi, keep the child away from the dispute as much as possible, and get advice before attempting to travel again.

## What can wait
- You do not need to solve who is right today or explain the whole family history at the airport.
- You do not need to make long-term decisions about parenting arrangements or future travel in this moment.
- You do not need to argue legal points with frontline staff if they are not satisfied with the documents.
- You can deal with complaints, refunds, or formal applications after the immediate travel pressure has reduced.

## Important reassurance
This situation is common, especially for separated families, single parents, and families where surnames differ. Being asked for extra proof usually reflects safeguarding checks and travel-document rules, not an accusation.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation, avoid escalation, and buy time. If there is uncertainty, disagreement, or a court order you do not fully understand, later decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional travel advice. Requirements vary by airline, route, destination, and court order, and staff can refuse boarding if they are not satisfied with documentation.
