# What to do if… two relatives both claim legal authority to make decisions after a death (UK)

## Short answer
Pause all irreversible actions and request the original court-certified proof of authority. Legal authority rests with the person named as executor in the latest valid will or the person entitled to be the administrator under intestacy rules; organisations will generally not act until a Grant of Probate, Grant of Confirmation, or Letters of Administration is presented.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t release keys, property, cash, or sensitive documents to any individual based on verbal claims or family status alone.
- Don’t attempt to settle the dispute by "splitting" authority or allowing one person to handle specific accounts while the other handles the house.
- Don’t rely on photocopies, digital images, or screenshots of a will as definitive proof of current authority.
- Don’t sign any indemnity forms or instructions for banks, insurers, or pension providers until a single representative is verified.
- Don’t write on, staple, or alter any original testamentary documents, as this can invalidate them in court.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the correct court jurisdiction based on where the deceased lived:**
   - In England and Wales, authority is confirmed by the **HMCTS Probate Registry**.
   - In Scotland, authority is confirmed via **Confirmation** through the local **Sheriff Court**.
   - In Northern Ireland, authority is confirmed by the **Probate Office** (Belfast) within the **Northern Ireland Courts and Tribunals Service (NICTS)**.
2. **Require see-and-record proof from both claimants:**
   - **Ask for the original latest Will and Testament** (or a solicitor’s certified copy).
   - **Verify the "Executor" or "Personal Representative" clause** to see exactly who is named.
   - **Check if multiple executors are named**, as they must generally act jointly unless one "renounces" or "power is reserved."
3. **Notify financial and utility institutions of a formal dispute:**
   - **Contact the deceased’s banks and insurers** to state that legal authority is currently contested.
   - **Request that accounts remain frozen** (except for funeral expenses) until a Grant of Probate or Confirmation is produced.
4. **Secure physical assets and documentation immediately:**
   - **Change locks on the property** if there is a risk of unauthorised entry, but only if you have a neutral right to do so (e.g., as a co-habitant or named executor).
   - **Create an inventory of valuables** with a neutral witness present and take dated photographs of all rooms.
5. **Implement a legal "stop" if an unauthorised application is suspected:**
   - **In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland:** apply to enter a **Caveat** at the Probate Registry or Probate Office to prevent a Grant from being issued for 6 months.
   - **In Scotland:** if an application for Confirmation is made by the "wrong" person, you must lodge an **Objection** in the Sheriff Court.
6. **Address urgent funeral arrangements via neutral channels:**
   - **Inform the funeral director** in writing that there is a dispute regarding who has the legal right to instruct.
   - **Check the Will for funeral wishes**, though these are technically not legally binding on the executor, who has the final "right of disposal" of the body.
7. **Maintain a strict log of all communications:**
   - **Record every request for assets** and every attempt by relatives to assert control.
   - **Save all emails and letters** regarding the dispute, as these may be required by a court or solicitor later.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide who is "morally" entitled to the estate right now.
- You do not need to clear the house, sell furniture, or distribute heirlooms today.
- You do not need to respond to personal insults or family pressure regarding "fairness."
- You do not need to pay off the deceased’s debts immediately; these are paid from the estate once authority is clear.

## Important reassurance
Family conflict following a bereavement is a common reaction to grief and stress. Pausing the process to verify legal standing is not an act of aggression; it is a necessary step to protect the deceased's wishes and ensure the estate is handled lawfully. Taking this time prevents the person acting from becoming personally liable for mistakes.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and the prevention of unauthorized asset handling. Resolving disputes over will validity, "undue influence," or complex intestacy requires formal legal intervention in the specific UK jurisdiction involved.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. In Northern Ireland, be aware that new probate fee structures took effect in April 2026. If you suspect fraud, theft of estate assets, or feel physically threatened, contact the police immediately.
