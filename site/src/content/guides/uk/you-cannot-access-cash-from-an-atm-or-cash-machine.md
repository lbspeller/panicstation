# What to do if… you cannot access cash from an ATM or cash machine (UK)

## Short answer
Stop retrying the same cash machine. Use another safe cash route, such as cash at the till, a Post Office counter if your bank supports it, a Banking Hub if available, or your own bank or building society branch.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep trying the same withdrawal in a panic; repeated attempts can hit limits or trigger security checks.
- Don’t use a cash machine that looks tampered with, is poorly lit, or shows a fee you do not want to pay.
- Don’t accept “help” from strangers near a cash machine or let anyone watch you enter your PIN.
- Don’t hand your card to anyone offering to “run it for you”.
- Don’t assume it is only you, but don’t assume it is a national crisis either; check quickly and move to another option.

## What to do now
1. **Work out whether the problem is the cash machine or your card.**
   - Check your banking app, if you can, for balance, card blocks, declined-payment alerts, or outage messages.
   - If it feels safe and you can do it without pressure, try one small card payment in a normal shop, preferably chip-and-PIN if offered.
   - Try one other cash machine in a different place, ideally inside or beside a bank, supermarket, Post Office, or busy shop. If it fails in the same way, stop repeated attempts.

2. **Use the LINK Cash Locator to find nearby cash options.**
   - Search by postcode or town.
   - Look for ATMs, Post Offices, Banking Hubs, cash at the till, banks, and free-to-use locations.
   - Prioritise places that are staffed, well lit, and easy to leave if you feel uncomfortable.

3. **Ask about cash at the till.**
   - In a participating shop, ask whether cash at the till is available.
   - You may not always need to buy something, but the shop must have cash available.
   - Check any fee, limit, and amount on the screen before you approve the transaction.

4. **Try a Post Office counter if your bank supports Everyday Banking there.**
   - Ask whether your bank allows cash withdrawals at that branch.
   - You will usually need your debit card and PIN for a withdrawal.
   - If the branch is busy, closed, or low on cash, use the locator to find another nearby branch or another cash route.

5. **Use a Banking Hub if there is one nearby.**
   - A Banking Hub has a shared counter service operated by the Post Office for everyday cash transactions.
   - It may also have a community banker from different banks on different days, but you do not need to wait for that if you only need the counter cash service.
   - Check opening times before travelling.

6. **Go to your own bank or building society branch if one is open.**
   - A staffed branch may be able to explain local ATM problems, card blocks, withdrawal limits, or safe alternatives.
   - Check opening times and whether the branch has counter service before travelling.

7. **Contact your bank if the problem looks account-related.**
   - Call or message your bank if your card is declined in several places, your balance looks wrong, your card may be blocked, or you see ATM attempts you do not recognise.
   - Ask what they can see, whether your card is blocked, and what safe cash-access options they support today.
   - Use the phone number in your banking app, on your card, or on your bank’s official website; do not use a number from a message you did not expect.

8. **Use a non-cash fallback if you only need to pay someone today.**
   - Ask whether they can accept card, bank transfer, or a later cash payment.
   - If it is a bill, check whether you can pay directly through your bank’s app, online banking, or the bill provider’s usual payment route.

9. **Keep brief notes if the outage costs you money.**
   - Note the time, location, machine message, and what you tried.
   - Keep receipts, screenshots, or messages if they show fees, failed payments, missed payments, or bank updates.
   - This can help later if you need to ask the bank to put things right or make a complaint.

## What can wait
- You do not need to change banks, cancel your card, or move all your money today unless your bank tells you there is a security reason.
- You do not need to withdraw a large amount as soon as one machine works; focus on the cash you need for the next day or two.
- You do not need to decide about a formal complaint now. Brief notes are enough for the moment.
- You do not need to solve every future cash-access problem today.

## Important reassurance
Cash machines can run out or go offline, especially when an area is busy or there is a technical issue. This is stressful, but one working alternative, such as cash at the till, a Post Office counter, a Banking Hub, or a branch, may be enough to get you through the immediate problem.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you access cash safely and avoid panic decisions. If the problem continues, money is missing, or charges start building up, later decisions may need help from your bank, a complaints route, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not financial, legal, professional, or security advice. Services vary by bank, retailer, branch, location, and time of day. If you feel unsafe while trying to get cash, leave the area and use another option.
