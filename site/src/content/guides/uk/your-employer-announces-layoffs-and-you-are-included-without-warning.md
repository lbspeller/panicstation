# What to do if… your employer announces layoffs and you are included without warning (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all further personal spending for work purposes immediately and submit a formal, written demand for the outstanding balance including copies of previous approvals.

## Do not do these things
- Do not continue to pay out-of-pocket for business costs while previous claims are unpaid.
- Do not rely on verbal promises or informal chat messages for future payment dates.
- Do not delete receipts, approval emails, or bank statements showing the original payments.
- Do not resign or stop working your contracted hours as a protest without legal advice.
- Do not assume the non-payment is a simple error if the company has ignored multiple inquiries.

## What to do now
1. **Cease all out-of-pocket expenditure immediately.**  
   Inform your manager in writing that you are unable to incur further personal costs until the outstanding reimbursements are settled. Request alternative payment methods for any essential upcoming work costs:
   - A corporate credit or debit card.
   - Use of a central company booking system.
   - A cash advance to cover anticipated costs.

2. **Assemble a formal evidence file.**  
   Collect all documentation related to the unpaid claims into a single chronological record. Ensure you have:
   - The original approval for the expense (email, message, or signed form).
   - The specific expense policy that was in place when the cost was incurred.
   - Proof of payment (receipts and bank/credit card statements).
   - Proof of submission (copies of the claims sent to finance or payroll).

3. **Submit a formal written request for payment.**  
   Send an email to your manager and the finance department. State the total amount owed and provide an itemised breakdown. Ask specifically:
   - On what date will the payment be processed?
   - If payment is being withheld, what is the specific contractual or policy reason?
   - Will the payment be made as a special "off-cycle" run or with the next payroll?

4. **Request an emergency payment if the delay causes hardship.**  
   If the missing funds are causing personal financial distress, such as potential bank charges or missed personal bills, state this clearly. Ask for an immediate BACS transfer or an advance rather than waiting for the next monthly pay cycle.

5. **Initiate a formal grievance if the response is unsatisfactory.**  
   If the employer refuses to pay or fails to provide a date, follow your company's formal grievance procedure. Put the complaint in writing, titled "Formal Grievance," and include your evidence file.
   - **You have a legal right to be accompanied** to any formal grievance meeting by a work colleague or a trade union representative.

6. **Monitor legal time limits.**  
   If you intend to take legal action for "unlawful deduction from wages" or "breach of contract," you must act quickly.
   - **The current Tribunal time limit** is 3 months minus 1 day from the date the payment was due.
   - **Note for late 2026:** Under the Employment Rights Act 2025, this limit is scheduled to increase to 6 months starting in October 2026.
   - **Notify Acas for Early Conciliation** before filing a claim; as of late 2025, this process can now last up to 12 weeks.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to leave your job or change your career path today.
- You do not need to hire a private solicitor to send the initial internal requests.
- You do not need to identify the "root cause" (such as company insolvency or payroll error) before protecting your own finances.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of urgency or instability when personal funds used for work are not returned as agreed. This situation is a contractual dispute, not a personal reflection of your value. By pausing spending and documenting the debt, you are taking the standard professional steps required to resolve the issue and protect your household budget.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate stabilization of personal finances and internal escalation procedures within the UK. It does not cover the technical process of filing an ET1 form or the specific rules for self-employed contractors.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Employment laws, particularly those regarding tribunal limits and "unlawful deductions," are subject to change following the Employment Rights Act 2025. Consult Acas or a qualified legal professional for advice specific to your contract and circumstances.
