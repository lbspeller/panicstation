# What to do if… a job audition or photoshoot introduces unexpected nudity or sexual expectations (UK)

## Short answer
Get out of the interaction and move to a safer place or person. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go along with nudity, sexual contact, sexual images, or a private meeting because you feel shocked, trapped, or worried about losing the opportunity.
- Don’t stay alone with the person for “one last explanation” or a private discussion.
- Don’t send nude or sexual images to prove you are serious, complete an audition, or secure the job.
- Don’t pay money or share bank, passport, driving licence, National Insurance, or other ID details after sexual or nude demands appear.
- Don’t keep negotiating while panicked; you can stop replying.
- Don’t arrange another meeting, call, or video chat to get proof.
- Don’t delete messages, emails, DMs, contracts, payment requests, or profile details if keeping them is safe for you.

## What to do now
1. Create space and safety. Leave the room or location if you can, move into a public place, call someone you trust, or ask staff, security, reception, a neighbour, or a nearby business for help. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.

2. Use a short stop line, then disengage. You can say, “No, I’m leaving now,” or “This is not what was agreed.” You do not need to justify, debate, persuade them, or finish the meeting.

3. Tell one safe person what happened. Send a simple message such as: “Something sexual was introduced into a job, audition, or photoshoot and I feel unsafe. Can you stay on the phone or help me get away?” Ask them not to contact the person for you unless you want that and it feels safe.

4. Get specialist support, even if you are unsure what to call it.
   - England and Wales, age 16 or over: Rape Crisis England & Wales, 0808 500 2222.
   - Scotland: Rape Crisis Scotland, 08088 010302.
   - Northern Ireland: Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline, 0808 802 1414.
   - Children and young people, UK-wide: Childline, 0800 1111.
   These services can help you steady yourself and think through options without pressure.

5. If you were touched, forced, threatened, trapped, or you cannot safely leave, treat it as urgent. Call 999 if you are in danger or need urgent help. If you want medical care, help with injuries, sexual health support, emergency contraception, or just to be checked, you can contact or ask about a Sexual Assault Referral Centre. You can also seek help from a GP surgery, A&E, or a sexual health clinic.

6. Preserve what already exists if you can do it safely. Screenshot messages, profiles, adverts, contracts, usernames, phone numbers, payment requests, call logs, and the address or venue. Note the date, time, and who was there. Do not reply, meet them, or send anything else to get more proof.

7. If they threaten to share images, demand money, or demand sexual acts, treat it as sextortion or blackmail. Do not pay. Do not send more images. If you are in danger, call 999. Otherwise, you can contact police on 101 or report online through Report Fraud. If intimate images are involved, adults can use StopNCII to help prevent sharing on participating platforms; if you are under 18, use Report Remove through Childline and the Internet Watch Foundation.

8. If a real company, venue, agency, theatre, studio, or employer seems connected, create a safety buffer only if it feels safe. You can block the individual, ask a trusted person to help you document what happened, and send one brief message to a general company contact saying you are withdrawing and do not consent to further contact from that person.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to report to police, an employer, a venue, a union, or an industry body.
- You do not need to write a detailed statement tonight.
- You do not need to confront the person or warn others publicly while you are shaken.
- You do not need to decide whether it was a scam, harassment, assault, exploitation, or a misunderstanding before getting support.
- You do not need to reply to every message.

## Important reassurance
People who introduce sexual demands after offering work often rely on shock, confusion, embarrassment, and the fear of losing an opportunity. Freezing, going quiet, appeasing, or doubting yourself can happen under stress. You are allowed to stop at any point, and work does not require giving someone access to your body.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you get safer and supported in the moment. Later decisions about reporting, work consequences, legal options, medical care, or image takedowns may need specialist help when you are steadier.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
