# What to do if… you develop rapid weight gain and new widespread swelling over a day or two with shortness of breath (UK)

## Short answer
Treat this as a medical emergency if you have severe difficulty breathing or chest pain. Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if you cannot speak in full sentences, are coughing up pink frothy phlegm, or feel suddenly confused.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "sleep off" breathlessness if you are unable to lie flat or are breathless while sitting still.
- Do not take extra doses of diuretics (water tablets) or laxatives unless specifically instructed by your clinical team today.
- Do not drink excessive fluids to "flush the system" or stop drinking fluids entirely.
- Do not start new anti-inflammatory painkillers like ibuprofen, as these can worsen fluid retention.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel dizzy, faint, or are struggling to breathe.

## What to do now
1. **Call 999 or go to A&E immediately if you meet emergency criteria.** 
   - You are gasping for air or cannot finish a short sentence.
   - You have chest pain, tightness, or a heavy feeling in the chest.
   - Your skin, lips, or tongue look pale, blue, or grey (on brown/black skin, check palms or soles).
   - You are coughing up pink, frothy, or bubbly sputum.
   - You feel suddenly confused, drowsy, or faint.
2. **Contact NHS 111 urgently if you are not in immediate danger but symptoms are new or worsening.**
   - Use 111 online or call 111 and state that you have "rapid weight gain, widespread swelling, and new shortness of breath."
   - Request a same-day assessment at an Urgent Treatment Centre or with a GP.
3. **Sit upright and remain still while waiting for help or transport.**
   - Use pillows to prop yourself up; do not lie flat if it makes breathing harder.
   - Avoid any physical exertion, including walking up stairs or packing a bag.
4. **Record your specific symptoms and recent weight changes to tell the clinician.**
   - Note if the swelling is in your ankles, legs, abdomen, hands, or face.
   - Note if you have been passing less urine than usual.
   - Note exactly how much weight you have gained and over what time frame (e.g., 2kg in 24 hours).
5. **Gather your current medications and medical history into one place.**
   - Include any recent changes to your prescriptions or missed doses.
   - Mention if you have a history of heart, kidney, or liver problems.
   - Mention if you are pregnant or have given birth within the last 6 months.
6. **Contact your specialist heart failure or renal team immediately if you are already under their care.**
   - Follow the specific "red flag" instructions provided in your existing care plan.
   - Inform them of your current symptoms even if you have already called 111 or 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the underlying cause (such as heart or kidney function) yourself.
- You do not need to implement strict salt or fluid restrictions before being seen by a clinician.
- You do not need to find historical weight records if you do not have them to hand; current symptoms are the priority.

## Important reassurance
Sudden swelling accompanied by breathing changes is a recognized clinical trigger for urgent review. It is a common reason for emergency assessment, and seeking help immediately is the standard procedure to ensure fluid levels are managed safely.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only. Subsequent diagnosis and treatment, such as blood tests (NT-proBNP), scans, or medication adjustments, require a formal clinical assessment.

## Important note
This information is for stabilization only and does not replace professional medical advice. If your condition changes or worsens while waiting, call 999 immediately.
