# What to do if… you leave a child car seat or booster seat in a taxi or rideshare and you need it to travel (UK)

## Short answer
Start the official lost-item process for that exact trip now, and make a backup plan that keeps your child out of car travel unless you have a suitable seat or you are using a limited legal exception.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t take another car journey with a child who still needs a seat unless you can do it safely and lawfully.
- Don’t post your child’s name, location, school, hotel room, or travel plans publicly to find the driver.
- Don’t arrange a rushed private handover with someone claiming to be the driver unless it is through the app, taxi operator, or booking company.
- Don’t use an unknown spare seat unless you can check it is the right size, complete, undamaged, suitable for the vehicle, and fitted according to its instructions.
- Don’t assume a taxi or app driver will have a child seat available.

## What to do now
1. **Capture the trip details while they are fresh.**  
   Write down the pickup point, drop-off point, time, route, driver name if shown, vehicle registration, taxi number if visible, booking reference, and receipt. Screenshot the trip or receipt before the app view changes.

2. **Use the official lost-item route for that exact booking.**  
   For an app-based private hire trip, open the trip in the app and use the lost-item or contact-driver option. For a minicab or private hire vehicle booked through a local operator, call the operator and give the booking details so they can contact the driver.

3. **Use the London route only if it fits the vehicle.**  
   For a London black cab, make a TfL lost property enquiry. For a London minicab or other private hire vehicle, contact the operator or app you booked through, because TfL directs non-TfL lost property such as licensed minicabs back to the service operator.

4. **Ask for a clear return plan.**  
   When you reach the driver, app support, or operator, ask them to confirm whether the seat is in the vehicle, where it is now, and how it can be handed back. Use a safe, public handover point such as a staffed reception desk, station information point, or airport assistance desk.

5. **Make a safe travel backup at the same time.**  
   Check whether you can buy a suitable replacement nearby, use public transport such as rail, Underground, tram, or bus, or ask a hotel, airport help desk, or local family service where the nearest reputable child-seat retailer or hire option is.

6. **Be careful with the taxi and minicab exception.**  
   UK rules generally say that if a taxi or minicab driver does not provide the correct child car seat, a child may travel without one only on a rear seat. A child aged 3 or older must use an adult seat belt; a child under 3 may travel without a seat belt. This is a legal exception, not a safer choice, so avoid car travel if you can wait for a suitable seat.

7. **Check the seat before using it again.**  
   If the seat is returned, check that it is the same seat, has all parts, has no visible cracks, stress marks, broken buckles, cut straps, missing padding, or damaged ISOFIX arms or base. Do not use it if you cannot make it fit securely or you are not confident it is complete and undamaged.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, leave a review, or ask for compensation.
- You do not need to prove who made the mistake before sorting safe onward travel.
- You do not need to compare every car-seat model in detail right now; a suitable, approved, available seat that fits your child and can be installed correctly is enough for the immediate problem.

## Important reassurance
This is a common travel mistake, especially when you are managing bags, tickets, and a child at the same time. The next useful step is not blame; it is getting the trip details into the right lost-property route and choosing the safest available onward travel.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about complaints, charges, replacement costs, insurance, or detailed fitting advice may need help from the operator, retailer, insurer, or a child-seat specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, safety, transport, or other professional advice. If you are unsure what is safe for your child right now, choose the option that avoids car travel until you have a suitable child car seat or booster seat.
