# What to do if… an official asks you to provide next-of-kin contact details and you are not sure who qualifies (UK)

## Short answer
Clarify if they need a practical emergency contact or the person with formal legal authority, then provide the details of the most reliable and reachable person while explicitly stating your uncertainty.

## Do not do these things
- Don't guess a specific relationship status if you are uncertain of the legal hierarchy.
- Don't assume that being named as next of kin (NoK) automatically grants a person the right to make medical or financial decisions.
- Don't feel pressured to resolve long-standing family disputes or estrangements on the spot.
- Don't provide personal data over the phone until you have verified the identity and department of the official.
- Don't delay providing a reliable contact for the sake of finding a "perfect" legal relative; communication is the priority.

## What to do now
1. **Ask the official to define exactly what they need the contact details for.** **The requirements for an "Emergency Contact" (updates and messages) are different from the "Nearest Relative" (Mental Health Act) or "Personal Representative" (legal estate matters).**
   - If they need someone to receive updates, offer the person most likely to answer their phone.
   - If they need someone for legal consent, ask for their specific departmental guidance on the hierarchy they follow.
2. **Verify the requester's credentials before sharing sensitive contact information.** **Ask for the official’s name, job title, and the switchboard number for their organisation so you can call them back to confirm they are legitimate.**
3. **Provide the most reliable person’s details with a clear disclaimer about their status.** **State clearly: "I am providing [Name] as the most reachable contact, but I am not certain if they meet your formal definition of next of kin."**
   - Ensure the official records this caveat in their notes or on the system.
4. **Offer a secondary contact if the family structure is complex or there is an estrangement.** **Providing two contacts (such as a long-term partner and a sibling) ensures the organisation can reach someone if the first person is unavailable or lacks authority.**
5. **Request to check existing records for a nominated contact if the person is currently in a healthcare setting.** **Hospitals often have a "Nominated Contact" or "Emergency Contact" listed on admission paperwork which may supersede general next-of-kin assumptions.**
6. **Follow jurisdiction-specific terminology for death-related inquiries.**
   - **In England and Wales:** **Ask to speak with the Medical Examiner’s Officer or the Coroner’s Officer to clarify who they have recorded as the "Primary Family Contact" for the investigation.**
   - **In Scotland:** **Contact the Scottish Fatalities Investigation Unit (SFIU) to ask about the "Nearest Relative" requirements for the Procurator Fiscal’s records.**
7. **Document exactly what information you have shared and with whom.** **Keep a record of the date, time, the name of the official, and the specific contact details you provided to ensure consistency in future calls.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the final legal hierarchy of the family today.
- You do not need to locate every distant relative before providing an initial point of contact.
- You do not need to prove relationships with certificates (like marriage or birth certificates) during the initial notification phase.
- You do not need to resolve disagreements between family members about who should be the "lead" contact.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed or confused when asked for these details, especially in a crisis. The term "next of kin" is widely used by the police and NHS but has no single, fixed legal definition across all scenarios. Officials are used to dealing with complex family trees and uncertainties; being honest about your doubt helps them record information more accurately.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate steps for stabilizing communication with officials. It does not cover the long-term legal processes of probate, estate administration, or formal guardianship, which may require specialist legal advice.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Rules regarding "Nearest Relatives" under the Mental Health Act and "Personal Representatives" under intestacy law are specific and vary between England/Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. If a dispute arises over legal authority, consult a solicitor or Citizens Advice.
