# What to do if… you are told an investigation is happening at work and you may be interviewed as a witness (UK)

## Short answer
Identify the meeting type immediately; you have a statutory right to be accompanied by a colleague or trade union representative at any formal disciplinary or grievance hearing. If they refuse a reasonable request, do not resign, but submit a written request to postpone the meeting for up to five working days to allow your companion to attend.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume "informal chats" are safe if the employer mentions performance or conduct issues.
- Do not record the meeting covertly unless you have checked your specific company policy, as this can be grounds for separate disciplinary action.
- Do not resign or walk out mid-meeting without advice, as this may jeopardize a future constructive dismissal claim.
- Do not bring a friend, family member, or lawyer unless your contract specifically allows it or you are requesting a "reasonable adjustment" for a disability.
- Do not ignore the meeting invitation; failing to attend without a valid reason can allow the employer to proceed and make decisions in your absence.

## What to do now
1. **Request the meeting's formal status and potential outcomes in writing.**
   **Ask the employer to confirm if the meeting is an investigation, a disciplinary hearing, or a grievance meeting.**
   - Confirm if a formal warning or dismissal is a possible outcome of this specific meeting.
   - If the answer is yes, the statutory right to be accompanied under Section 10 of the Employment Relations Act 1999 applies.

2. **Verify if you are requesting a companion from an approved category.**
   **Ensure your chosen companion is a fellow work colleague, a full-time trade union official, or a "lay" union representative.**
   - If using a lay union representative (a volunteer), check they have written certification from their union stating they have received training/experience to act as a companion.

3. **Submit a formal written request to be accompanied at least 24 hours before the meeting.**
   **Provide the name of the companion and their status (colleague or union rep) to the person holding the meeting.**
   - State: "Under Section 10 of the Employment Relations Act 1999, I am making a reasonable request to be accompanied by [Name]."

4. **Propose a postponement if your companion is unavailable at the scheduled time.**
   **Suggest an alternative time that is within five working days of the original date.**
   - The five-day period starts the day after the original meeting date.
   - The employer must agree to this postponement if the alternative time is reasonable.

5. **Request a reasonable adjustment if you have a disability.**
   **Ask to bring a support worker, carer, or family member if your disability makes it difficult to participate with only a colleague or union rep.**
   - Explain that this is a "reasonable adjustment" required under the Equality Act 2010 to ensure a fair process.

6. **Confirm if an "investigatory" meeting is being used to bypass your rights.**
   **Ask for a written guarantee that no disciplinary decision or warning will be issued at the end of an investigatory meeting.**
   - While there is no statutory right to a companion at a pure "fact-finding" investigation, many employers allow it as good practice if you ask.

7. **State your objection for the record if the meeting proceeds without a companion.**
   **Read a prepared statement at the start of the meeting noting that your request for a companion was denied.**
   - Ask for this objection to be recorded in the formal meeting minutes.
   - Request frequent breaks to take your own notes if you are unsupported.

8. **Secure a copy of the meeting notes before leaving or immediately after.**
   **Check the notes for accuracy and provide written corrections if they do not reflect what was said.**
   - Do not feel pressured to sign the notes "as a true record" if you disagree with them; sign only "to acknowledge receipt."

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to appeal a decision until you have received the outcome in writing.
- You do not need to respond to new evidence presented for the first time in the meeting; you can ask for an adjournment to review it.
- You do not need to initiate an Employment Tribunal claim immediately; the deadline is usually three months minus one day from the date of the breach.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel intimidated when denied support in a high-stakes meeting. The "right to be accompanied" is a fundamental protection in UK law designed to balance the power dynamic. Refusing it is a procedural error by the employer that an Employment Tribunal can penalize, often resulting in an award of up to two weeks' pay.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate steps to take when a companion is refused for a formal meeting in the UK. It does not provide strategy for the underlying disciplinary or grievance case.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. Employment rights vary based on the specific type of worker status (employee vs. worker) and individual contracts. If you face dismissal or discrimination, contact Acas or a qualified employment solicitor immediately.
