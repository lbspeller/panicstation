# What to do if… you find an outdoor tap or hose connection leaking into an interior wall (UK)

## Short answer
Stop the water flow first if you can do so safely, usually by using the inside stop valve, stopcock, or stop tap. Treat nearby sockets, switches, appliances, and the consumer unit as unsafe if water may have reached them.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using the outdoor tap or hose to finish a job.
- Don’t touch or test wet sockets, switches, extension leads, appliances, exposed wiring, or a wet consumer unit.
- Don’t force a stiff stop valve, stopcock, or stop tap.
- Don’t start opening the wall in panic if there may be wiring, pipes, or live electrics behind it.
- Don’t run heaters hard against a wet wall before the leak has been stopped.
- Don’t restore power to the affected area until it has been checked if water may have reached electrics.

## What to do now
1. **Shut off the water inside the property if you can.**
   - Use the inside stop valve, stopcock, or stop tap if you can reach it safely.
   - It usually turns clockwise to close.
   - Turn it slowly and stop if it will not move without force.
   - Open a cold tap briefly to reduce pressure and check whether the flow has slowed or stopped.

2. **Stop using the outdoor tap and hose connection.**
   - Leave the outside fitting alone once the water is off.
   - If a hose is attached, only disconnect it if the flow has stopped and the fitting is safe to handle.
   - Don’t keep tightening, loosening, or twisting the tap, hose connector, or wall plate.

3. **Reduce electrical risk near the wet wall.**
   - Keep people and pets away from the damp area.
   - If it is safe and dry to reach, switch off the affected circuit at the consumer unit or fuse box.
   - If the consumer unit, fuse box, floor, or your hands are wet, do not touch it.
   - Arrange a registered electrician if water may have reached sockets, switches, wiring, appliances, or the consumer unit.

4. **Limit the spread of water.**
   - Move belongings away from the wall.
   - Catch drips in a bucket or tray.
   - Blot standing water with towels.
   - Use towels at skirting boards or floor edges to slow spread.

5. **Get the right help.**
   - If you rent, tell your landlord, letting agent, housing association, or council landlord immediately that water is leaking into an interior wall from an outside tap or hose connection.
   - If you own the property, contact a plumber and say the leak appears to be from an outside tap or hose connection entering the wall.
   - If the inside stop valve will not stop the leak, if you cannot find it, or if the supply pipe may be involved, contact your water company for guidance.
   - If an outside stop valve may be needed, try the inside stop valve first where possible. If you may share a supply, warn affected neighbours if it is safe and practical.

6. **Record what happened without delaying safety steps.**
   - Take photos or video of the outdoor tap, hose connection, wet interior wall, floor, skirting, sockets nearby, and affected belongings.
   - Note the time you found the leak, when you shut off the water, who you contacted, and what they said.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to redecorate, replace plasterboard, or make an insurance claim.
- You do not need to diagnose whether the failed part is the tap, washer, hose connector, wall plate, pipe, or joint.
- You do not need to open the wall yourself right now.
- You do not need to decide on drying equipment until the leak is stopped and electrical safety has been considered.

## Important reassurance
A small leak at an outside tap or hose connection can travel into a wall before it is obvious indoors. Shutting off the water, avoiding wet electrics, and getting practical help are the right first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Repairs, electrical checks, drying, insurance decisions, and tenancy responsibilities may need help from a plumber, registered electrician, landlord, water company, insurer, or housing adviser.

## Important note
This guide is general information for urgent first steps, not professional plumbing, electrical, legal, insurance, tenancy, or safety advice. If water may have reached electrics, keep clear and get professional advice before using the affected area.
