# What to do if… you receive a demand to return company equipment with threats of action (UK)

## Short answer
Reply calmly in writing, confirm what you have, and arrange a return that gives you proof. Do not accept blame, agree to pay money, or agree to a deduction from wages on the spot.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the message, even if it feels aggressive.
- Do not hand anything over without written proof of what was returned, ideally including serial numbers or asset tags.
- Do not agree on the spot to pay fees, replacement costs, or deductions from wages unless they have clearly explained what authorises it.
- Do not argue by phone or in person if you feel panicked; keep the practical return arrangements in writing.
- Do not wipe, reset, delete, or alter work devices unless your employer instructs you in writing. You can sign out of clearly personal accounts if you can do that without deleting company information.
- Do not send equipment to an address you have not verified.
- Do not ship equipment without tracked delivery, proof of posting, and a record of what was inside the parcel.

## What to do now
1. **Pause before replying.** Read the demand once, then put it down for a few minutes. Your immediate aim is to return any company property, keep evidence, and avoid rushed admissions.

2. **Make a simple inventory.** List each item you have, such as a laptop, phone, charger, badge, keys, headset, screen, dock, security token, or documents. Add any serial number, asset tag, visible damage, and whether you are unsure about ownership.

3. **Photograph the evidence.** Take clear photos of each item, serial number, asset tag, accessories, and the packed box before sealing it. Keep the photos somewhere you can still access after returning the equipment.

4. **Reply once in writing with a return plan.** Ask them to confirm:
   - the exact items they believe you still have;
   - any asset tags or serial numbers;
   - the return address or collection method;
   - whether they will provide a prepaid tracked courier label or booked collection;
   - who will confirm receipt.

5. **Use a return method that creates proof.**
   - If they arrange a courier, keep the booking confirmation, tracking number, and collection receipt.
   - If you return items in person, take your inventory and ask the receiver to sign and date it, or email confirmation before you leave.
   - If you must post the items yourself, use tracked delivery, keep proof of posting, keep the receipt, and email the tracking number and inventory the same day.
   - If postage cost is a problem, ask in writing whether they will arrange collection or reimburse reasonable return costs under company policy.

6. **Keep your property separate.** Remove only items that are clearly yours, such as your own case, personal SIM card, or personal cable. If ownership is unclear, list the item and ask them to confirm whether they say it belongs to the company.

7. **If they threaten deductions from wages, ask for the basis in writing.** Ask them to identify the contract term, written agreement, legal requirement, overpayment, or other reason they rely on. Say you do not agree to any unexplained deduction and that you are arranging documented return of the equipment.

8. **If they threaten legal action or official involvement, do not match the tone.** Keep replying only about the return arrangements: the inventory, collection, delivery address, tracking, and receipt confirmation. Save emails, letters, texts, screenshots, call logs, courier receipts, and photos.

9. **If police contact you, stay calm and keep it factual.** Verify who is contacting you, be polite, and get independent legal advice before giving a detailed statement. Do not destroy, hide, sell, or alter any item they say is company property.

10. **Get UK employment help if pay is withheld or threatened.** Contact Acas for guidance about deductions from wages. If you are in a union, send your rep the demand, your inventory, and any message about deductions or withheld final pay.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a formal complaint.
- You do not need to write a long explanation of your employment history.
- You do not need to threaten legal action back.
- You do not need to accept a settlement, sign a new document, or agree to deductions immediately.
- You do not need to resolve every disagreement before arranging a safe, documented return.

## Important reassurance
Threatening wording can make a practical return feel urgent and frightening. A calm written record, clear inventory, and proof of return can reduce the risk of avoidable disputes and give you time to get advice if money is withheld.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the situation, return property safely, keep proof, and avoid rushed commitments. If wages are withheld, a deduction is made, court papers arrive, or there is a formal pre-action letter, later decisions may need specialist employment or legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, employment, therapeutic, or professional advice. What an employer can deduct or claim depends on your contract, any written agreement, the condition and ownership of the items, and the facts of the dispute.
