# What to do if… you receive a demand to return company equipment with threats of action (UK)

## Short answer
Request the specific attendance data and evidence relied upon in writing and formally dispute the accuracy before the warning is finalized. Ensure you meet any appeal deadlines, typically five working days, even if you are still waiting for a response to your data request.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any document that acknowledges "agreement" or "admission" of the attendance breach if the data is incorrect.
- Do not rely on verbal explanations alone, as these are often misrecorded or omitted from the official file.
- Do not miss the deadline for an appeal or a meeting response while waiting for HR to find "missing" clock-in records.
- Do not delete or alter personal digital logs, messages, or calendar entries that could serve as evidence.
- Do not attempt to "backfill" or create new entries in timekeeping systems to fix an old error, as this may be treated as fraud.
- Do not resign immediately; a resignation may limit your ability to challenge the warning or claim unfair treatment later.

## What to do now
1. **Request the specific details of the allegation in writing.** Send a formal email to your manager or HR department asking for:
   - The exact dates and times of the alleged absences or lateness.
   - A copy of the attendance policy or threshold you are accused of breaching.
   - Copies of the evidence used to trigger the warning, such as clock-in reports, door badge logs, or rota "exception" reports.
   - Confirmation of whether this is an informal "improvement note" or a formal disciplinary warning.

2. **Secure your right to be accompanied if a meeting is scheduled.** If the meeting could result in a formal warning or dismissal, you have a statutory right to bring a companion.
   - Identify a colleague or a trade union representative to attend with you.
   - Request a postponement of up to five working days if your chosen companion is unavailable on the proposed date.
   - Ask for all evidence to be provided at least 24 hours before the meeting.

3. **Compile a "verification pack" of your own existing evidence.** Gather data that shows you were working or authorized to be absent on the disputed dates.
   - Screenshots of the rota or schedule you were originally issued.
   - Sent emails, login timestamps, or digital handovers from the disputed times.
   - Messages from managers approving shift swaps or leave.
   - Fit notes or medical evidence if the dispute relates to how sickness was coded.

4. **Submit a formal written correction of the record.** Keep this document focused on the data rather than personal grievances.
   - State clearly that you believe the warning is based on inaccurate data for specific dates.
   - Attach your verification pack as supporting evidence.
   - Formally request that the records are updated and the warning withdrawn.

5. **Submit a formal appeal within five working days if a warning is issued.** Do not wait for a "corrected" version to be sent before appealing.
   - Use the employer's official appeal procedure.
   - Focus the grounds of appeal on "procedural error" or "inaccurate evidence."
   - Request that a manager not previously involved in the case hears the appeal.

6. **Submit a Subject Access Request (SAR) if data is being withheld.** If the employer refuses to show you the attendance logs they are using against you, use your data rights.
   - Request "all attendance, time-keeping, and rota records relating to [your name] for the period of [dates]."
   - Be aware that under the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025, the employer can "stop the clock" on the one-month response deadline if they need to clarify your request.

7. **Contact your union or legal representative immediately.** Professional support can ensure that the employer follows the ACAS Code of Practice, which is mandatory for fair disciplinary procedures.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file an Employment Tribunal claim today; focus on the internal appeal first.
- You do not need to provide a complete history of your performance or work ethic; focus only on the specific dates in the warning.
- You do not need to resolve how the "system error" happened; you only need to prove that an error exists.
- You do not need to confront the manager who logged the data incorrectly.

## Important reassurance
Attendance warnings are frequently the result of automated system errors, such as clocking-in machines failing to sync or "coding errors" where a manager forgets to log a pre-approved shift swap. These are often treated as administrative issues once the evidence is presented. Your reaction of concern is normal, but the process is designed to allow for the correction of factual errors.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for disputed attendance records in the UK. It does not cover long-term legal strategy, redundancy procedures, or performance-related capability dismissals.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. UK employment law changed significantly in 2025 and 2026; employees now have unfair dismissal protection from day one of employment, meaning your employer must follow a fair procedure regardless of your length of service. If you are a member of a trade union, you should follow their specific protocols for workplace disputes.
