# What to do if… a new crack appears in a wall or ceiling and seems to be widening (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the area immediately and seek a professional structural assessment through your local council's Building Control department or a chartered structural engineer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stand, sit, or sleep directly beneath a widening ceiling crack or a sagging ceiling.
- Do not attempt to fill, plaster over, or hide the crack before a professional inspection has taken place.
- Do not investigate the crack by removing plaster, drilling into the wall, or applying pressure to the area.
- Do not ignore associated signs of movement such as doors that no longer close or windows that have suddenly jammed.
- Do not allow heavy furniture or loads to remain in a room where the floor or ceiling shows signs of bowing.

## What to do now
1. **Evacuate the immediate area if collapse appears imminent.** If you hear loud cracking noises, see debris falling, or observe a significant bulge or sag in a structural element, **leave the room and call 999 immediately**.
2. **Contact Building Control for a dangerous structure assessment.** If the building feels unsafe but there is no immediate collapse, **call your local council and ask for the Building Control Dangerous Structures team**.
   - Most UK councils operate a 24-hour emergency line for structural safety reports.
3. **Address potential utility ruptures.** 
   - **If you smell gas:** Leave the building immediately and call the **National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999** from outside. 
   - **Do not** operate light switches or use naked flames if a gas leak is suspected.
4. **Notify your landlord or Building Safety Regulator (if applicable).**
   - **If you rent:** Send an **urgent written notice** to your landlord. Under 2026 standards, landlords must investigate structural hazards within mandated timeframes.
   - **If you live in a high-rise (over 11m/18m):** Contact your Principal Accountable Person or report the concern to the **Building Safety Regulator (BSR)**.
5. **Document the movement without touching the crack.**
   - Take clear photographs of the crack from a distance and close-up.
   - Place a common object (like a 50p coin) next to the crack for scale in photos.
   - Note the exact time and date the movement was first observed.
6. **Measure and monitor the width.** Use a ruler to measure the widest point of the crack in millimetres and record the measurement.
   - Look for "stair-step" patterns in brickwork or diagonal cracks over 3mm wide.
7. **Secure professional expertise.**
   - **Homeowners:** Hire a **Chartered Structural Engineer** or a **Chartered Building Surveyor** (RICS) to perform a structural survey.
   - Ensure the professional is specifically qualified to assess building movement or subsidence.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the root cause (such as subsidence, heave, or thermal movement) yourself.
- You do not need to contact your insurance company until you have the initial professional assessment or council report.
- You do not need to plan or price repairs until the structure has been stabilized and the movement has been monitored for a sufficient period.

## Important reassurance
Visible cracks are common in UK properties due to seasonal changes and settling. While a widening crack requires urgent professional verification, it does not always indicate an immediate total collapse. Following the safety protocol of isolating the area and involving Building Control ensures the situation is managed by experts.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization only. Structural movement requires long-term monitoring and specialist engineering solutions that are outside the scope of immediate crisis management.

## Important note
This guide provides general safety information and is not a substitute for an on-site professional structural assessment. Structural integrity can only be verified by a qualified engineer or building control officer.
