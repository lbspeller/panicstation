# What to do if… police ask to enter your home and you are unsure if you must let them in (UK)

## Short answer
Keep a closed-door pause if you can, and ask whether they are asking for your consent or saying they have a legal power to enter. If they say they have a warrant or a power to enter, do not physically resist; calmly ask what they are relying on and keep a simple record.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t open the door wide or step back in a way that could look like you are inviting them in if you do not want to.
- Don’t physically block the doorway or try to stop officers if they say they are entering under a legal power.
- Don’t lie about who is inside.
- Don’t move, hide, delete, or throw away anything while they are waiting.
- Don’t agree to a “quick look around” if you are unsure; ask the basis first.
- Don’t get drawn into a doorstep argument; keep your words short and procedural.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause at the door.** Speak through the closed door, a window, a door viewer, or a door chain if you can do that safely.
2. **Check who they are.** Ask: “Please show your warrant card or ID,” “What is your name or identification number?” and “Which police force or station are you from?” If you are worried they may not be genuine, use an official police contact route to check.
3. **Ask the key question.** Say: “Are you asking for my consent to come in, or are you saying you have a legal power to enter?”
4. **Ask the reason and basis.** Say: “What is the purpose of coming in?” and “What warrant, order, law, or power are you relying on?”
5. **If they say it is voluntary, be clear.** If you do not want them inside, say: “I do not consent to entry or a search. I am willing to speak here.” If you step outside to speak, close the door behind you.
6. **If they say they have a warrant, ask to see it.** Ask them to hold it up, pass it through safely, or otherwise let you read it. Check the address, the date, what it authorises, and whether it appears to cover entry, search, seizure, or people being searched.
7. **If they say they can enter without consent, do not fight at the door.** Say once: “I understand you are saying you have a power to enter. I am not resisting. I do not consent.” Then move out of the doorway and observe from somewhere safe.
8. **If a search happens, ask for the paperwork trail.** Ask for the warrant or power relied on, a search reference or record number, and a written list or receipt for anything taken.
9. **Make a simple record if it is safe.** Note the time, the officers’ names or identification numbers, what they said the reason was, what rooms were entered, and what was taken. Do not record in a way that gets in the way or escalates the situation.
10. **If you are arrested, told you must go to a station, or asked detailed questions about an offence, ask for legal advice.** Say: “I want legal advice,” or “I want a solicitor.” Avoid discussing the incident in detail until you have had advice.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide your whole legal position at the door. Complaints, challenges to a search, and longer-term decisions can wait until the immediate situation is calmer, but ask for legal advice straight away if you are arrested, searched, or questioned about an offence.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel intimidated when police are at your door. Short phrases such as “Are you asking, or requiring entry?” and “I do not consent” can help you stay clear without turning the moment into a confrontation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the moment police ask to come inside. Powers and wording differ across England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, and later decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Police powers can depend on the exact location, the reason for entry, the warrant or legal power used, and whether there is an urgent safety risk. Prioritise safety, do not physically resist, and ask for legal advice as soon as you can.
