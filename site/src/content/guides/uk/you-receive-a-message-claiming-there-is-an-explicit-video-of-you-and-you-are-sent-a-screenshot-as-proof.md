# What to do if… you receive a message claiming there is an explicit video of you and you are sent a screenshot as “proof (UK)

## Short answer
Stop replying, do not pay, and do not send anything else. Keep what you already have without creating risky extra copies, secure your accounts, then use the platform, police, and specialist UK reporting routes that fit your age and situation.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay, send more images, or agree to another demand.
- Don’t argue, insult, threaten, or try to outsmart them in chat; it can keep the pressure going and give them more material.
- Don’t click links, open files, scan QR codes, or install anything they send.
- Don’t delete the message thread or your account in panic; if you need the account removed, deactivate rather than delete it.
- Don’t post public explanations or screenshots with their username, number, or your personal details.
- Don’t assume the screenshot proves the video is real; screenshots can be faked, edited, stolen, or AI-generated.
- Don’t screenshot, download, forward, or share any sexual image if you are under 18, if the person shown may be under 18, or if you are unsure.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the conversation.** Do not reply again. Keep the existing thread visible if you safely can, then use the platform’s report and block tools.

2. **Do a quick account lockdown.**
   - Change the password for your email first, then any social accounts connected to it.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication for email and social accounts.
   - Check for unfamiliar logins, devices, recovery emails, recovery phone numbers, and linked apps; remove anything you do not recognise.

3. **Preserve only what is already there.**
   - Keep the message thread, account handle, phone number, email address, payment demand, and the date and time.
   - Do not create extra copies of intimate images or videos.
   - If any sexual image may involve someone under 18, do not screenshot, record, download, forward, or share it with anyone.

4. **Report it on the platform where it happened.**
   - Use the in-app route for blackmail, sextortion, intimate image abuse, impersonation, or non-consensual intimate images.
   - Set accounts to private, restrict direct messages to contacts, review followers or friends, and remove accounts you do not know.

5. **Use the right UK reporting route.**
   - If you are under 18, or the image may involve someone under 18, use the CEOP Safety Centre. You can also use Report Remove for sexual images or videos of yourself.
   - If you are 18 or over and it is not an emergency, report it to your local police force by calling 101.
   - If you are 18 or over in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland and this involves fraud, cyber crime, payment demands, cryptocurrency, gift cards, or money already paid, Report Fraud may also be appropriate.
   - If you are in Scotland, report to Police Scotland by calling 101 unless there is immediate danger.
   - If you feel in immediate danger, someone is threatening violence, or someone is trying to meet you in person, call 999.

6. **If it came by threatening email, treat it as suspicious email too.**
   - Forward the email to report@phishing.gov.uk without clicking links or opening attachments.
   - This is not a crime report, so still use police or Report Fraud routes if there is a threat, payment demand, or ongoing harm.

7. **If an intimate image or video of you may be shared, check whether StopNCII.org fits.**
   - It may help if you are an adult, the image or video is of you, you have access to it, and it was not taken when anyone shown was under 18.
   - It creates a digital fingerprint of your image or video and shares that fingerprint with participating companies to help detect and stop sharing.
   - It does not cover every site, every platform, or most encrypted messaging services.

8. **If you are 18 or over and want specialist support, contact the Revenge Porn Helpline.**
   - They support adults in the UK with intimate image abuse, threats to share intimate images, webcam blackmail, reporting, and removal guidance.

9. **Ask one safe person to sit with you while you do the steps.**
   - You can say: “I’m being blackmailed online with a sexual threat. I don’t want to explain everything right now. Can you stay with me while I secure accounts and report it?”

## What can wait
- Deciding whether the screenshot is real or fake.
- Writing a perfect report.
- Gathering lots of extra evidence.
- Finding out who the sender really is.
- Warning them, confronting them, or tracking them.
- Deleting all social media, changing your phone number, or telling lots of people.

## Important reassurance
This is a coercion tactic designed to make you feel ashamed and rushed. Being targeted does not mean you did anything wrong, and the safest first move is usually to stop feeding the threat, secure access, and use established reporting routes.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce harm, stop escalation, and buy time. Later decisions about takedowns, police contact, account recovery, or ongoing safety may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are under 18, treat any sexual image or video involving you as a child-safety issue and use child-specific support routes. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
