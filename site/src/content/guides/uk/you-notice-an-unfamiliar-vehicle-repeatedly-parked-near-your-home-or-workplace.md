# What to do if… you notice an unfamiliar vehicle repeatedly parked near your home or workplace (UK)

## Short answer
Move to a safer pause indoors or into a staffed public place, keep distance, and do not approach the vehicle. If you feel in immediate danger or think a crime is happening now, call 999; otherwise report the repeated pattern through 101 or online police reporting.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the driver or walk up to the vehicle to check.
- Do not block the vehicle in, stand in its path, or try to force the driver to explain themselves.
- Do not post the number plate, photos, or accusations publicly.
- Do not assume it is definitely nothing if the same pattern keeps happening.
- Do not make sudden isolated changes that leave you more exposed; make small, calm safety adjustments instead.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Go indoors or into a staffed public place, such as work reception, a shop, or a café. Lock doors if you are at home. Keep distance and avoid standing at a window where you are easy to spot.
2. **Bring another person into the situation.**
   - At home: tell someone you live with, or call or message a trusted person and stay connected.
   - In a block or office: tell reception, concierge, facilities, or security so they can monitor entrances and note the vehicle.
   - At work: alert your manager, reception, or security and ask them to keep an eye out.
3. **Record the minimum useful details from a safe place.** Write down:
   - exact location, street, nearby landmarks, dates, times, and how long it stayed
   - make, model, colour, number plate, and distinguishing marks such as stickers or dents
   - what you observed, such as waiting, watching entrances, following someone, taking photos, or trying doors
   If it is safe, take a photo or video discreetly from indoors or from a safe distance; do not move closer to get it.
4. **Preserve any relevant footage you already have.** If you have a doorbell camera or CCTV, save clips for the times the vehicle appears. If this is at work, ask security or facilities to retain the relevant timestamps.
5. **Choose the right contact route for the risk right now.**
   - **Call 999** if you feel threatened, the person is approaching you, trying doors, following you, making threats, or you believe a crime is in progress.
   - **Call 101 or report online** if it is concerning but not an immediate threat. Give the repeated pattern and the details you recorded.
   - **If you need 999 but cannot speak safely:** call 999, listen to the operator, and respond by coughing, tapping, or pressing keys if you can. If calling from a mobile, press 55 if prompted so the call can be transferred to police. If calling from a landline and you cannot speak, staying silent while the operator can hear background noise may lead to the call being transferred to police.
6. **Ask for a reference or incident number.** Keep later updates connected to the same report where possible, because a clear timeline can show the pattern.
7. **Make a small safety plan for the next 24 hours.**
   - Leave or arrive with someone where possible.
   - Use well-lit routes and staffed entrances.
   - Have your keys and phone ready before stepping outside.
   - If you park, choose a well-lit spot near people or cameras rather than an isolated convenient spot.
8. **Reduce predictable visibility at home without trapping yourself.** Close curtains at night, switch on external lights when arriving, and avoid spending time right by front windows while the vehicle is present.
9. **If it seems targeted at you, widen support carefully.** Tell one trusted neighbour, colleague, or building staff member what the vehicle looks like and ask them to contact you or police if they see concerning behaviour. Ask them not to confront the driver.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify who owns the vehicle right now.
- You do not need to confront anyone, gather proof, or make a social media post.
- You do not need to buy new security equipment immediately; preserving existing footage and reporting the pattern matters more in the first hours.
- You do not need to decide whether this is stalking or harassment yet; focus on safety, distance, and a clear record.

## Important reassurance
Noticing a repeated odd-hours pattern and taking it seriously is a normal safety response. Many unfamiliar vehicles have harmless explanations, but you do not need certainty before taking calm steps that keep you safer and make it easier to get help if the behaviour continues.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the situation and reduce immediate risk. Later decisions may need specialist help from local police, workplace security, housing staff, or a stalking support service if the pattern continues or feels targeted.

## Important note
This guide is general information for immediate safety and decision support. It is not legal, security, investigative, therapeutic, or professional advice.
