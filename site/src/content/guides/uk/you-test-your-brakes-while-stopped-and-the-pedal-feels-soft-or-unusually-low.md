# What to do if… you test your brakes while stopped and the pedal feels soft or unusually low (UK)

## Short answer
Do not move the vehicle. Keep the car stationary, apply the parking brake, and arrange for a professional inspection or recovery before attempting to drive.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to drive the car to a garage or "just around the block" to see if the brakes still work.
- Do not pump the pedal repeatedly to "build pressure" and then assume the car is safe to drive once it feels firmer.
- Do not ignore the issue if the pedal feel returns to normal temporarily; hydraulic faults often manifest intermittently before total failure.
- Do not top up the brake fluid reservoir and drive off; a low fluid level usually indicates a leak or significant component wear that requires inspection.
- Do not assume it is safe just because there is no fluid visible on the ground.
- Do not allow anyone to perform high-speed "test stops" in a public area.

## What to do now
1. **Apply the parking brake and switch off the engine.**  
   **Ensure the vehicle is fully secured using the handbrake or electronic parking brake (EPB).** Use hazard warning lights if the vehicle is in a position where it may be a hazard to other road users or if you are on a public highway.
2. **Clear the driver footwell of any physical obstructions.**  
   **Check that the floor mat is not bunched up behind the brake pedal or that an object has rolled under it.** If a physical obstruction was present, remove it and re-test the pedal feel while stationary.
3. **Check the dashboard for red warning lights.**  
   **Look for the red brake warning light (usually a circle with an exclamation mark).** If this light is illuminated, the hydraulic system is likely compromised or the fluid is critically low; do not attempt to move the vehicle.
4. **Perform a visual external scan for leaks.**  
   **Walk around the vehicle and look for wet patches or puddles on the ground near the wheels or under the engine area.** Brake fluid is typically clear, light yellow, or brown and feels oily or slippery to the touch. 
5. **Verify the brake fluid level only if safe to do so.**  
   **Open the bonnet and locate the brake fluid reservoir to check if the level is below the "MIN" marker.** If the level is low or the reservoir is empty, do not drive the vehicle under any circumstances.
6. **Call for breakdown assistance or a mobile mechanic.**  
   **Inform the provider that the brake pedal is sinking or feels soft while stationary and request a recovery to a garage.** State clearly that the vehicle is unsafe to drive due to a suspected hydraulic failure.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific mechanical cause (such as a master cylinder fault or air in the lines) right now.
- You do not need to decide on repair options or costs while the vehicle is being recovered.
- You do not need to check the brake pads or discs yourself; these require a professional inspection.

## Important reassurance
Identifying a brake fault while the vehicle is stationary prevents a high-risk situation on the road. It is normal to feel stressed when a safety-critical system fails, but by staying parked, you have already eliminated the primary danger.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate stabilization of a vehicle when a soft or low brake pedal is detected at a standstill. It does not provide instructions for moving a vehicle with failing brakes or performing DIY hydraulic repairs.

## Important note
Brake systems are safety-critical components. This information is for general guidance and does not replace a professional mechanical assessment. If you are in an unsafe location on a public road, contact emergency services or your breakdown provider immediately.
