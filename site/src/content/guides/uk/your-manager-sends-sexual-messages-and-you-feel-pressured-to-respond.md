# What to do if… your manager sends sexual messages and you feel pressured to respond (UK)

## Short answer
Create a safety buffer: stop engaging with the sexual content, save the messages, and bring in a safer person or service so you are not handling this alone. You do not have to decide right now whether to make a formal complaint.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel you have to “play along” to stay safe at work; you can keep replies neutral or stop replying.
- Do not delete the messages, screenshots, or your own replies in a panic.
- Do not confront the manager alone in a private place if you feel intimidated.
- Do not resign, send a long explanation, or agree to a private meeting while you are panicked, unless leaving is needed for immediate safety.
- Do not assume you must report to the police to be taken seriously; reporting is your choice.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause and reduce exposure.** If you are at work, move to somewhere public or near other people, such as a break area, reception area, corridor near the toilets, or beside a trusted colleague. Take a minute before replying.

2. **Stop the sexual thread without escalating it.**
   - If you need to reply, use one short work-focused line, such as: “I’m not comfortable with personal messages. Please keep communication work-related.”
   - If not replying feels safer, you can stop responding.
   - Do not explain, apologise, send extra personal messages to soften it, or debate whether they “meant it”.

3. **Save what you have in a simple, accurate way.**
   - Screenshot the messages showing the sender, date, time, and content.
   - Save any work messages, emails, rota changes, meeting requests, or voice notes connected to it.
   - Write a brief note for yourself with what happened, when, where you were, and how you replied or did not reply.
   - If this is on a work device or work account, avoid secret recording, mass forwarding, or downloading large amounts of workplace data. Keep preservation simple and ask for advice if you are unsure.

4. **Tell one safer person what is happening.**
   - This could be a trusted colleague, union rep, HR contact, a named harassment or dignity-at-work contact, or a senior manager who is not connected to the manager.
   - Use plain wording: “My manager has sent sexual messages and I feel pressured to respond. I have saved them and need help keeping work communication safe.”
   - If you are in a union, ask whether a rep can help you prepare what to say and attend any meeting with you.

5. **Move future contact into documented work channels where possible.**
   - Keep replies about work only.
   - Use email or an official messaging system rather than private messages if you can.
   - Ask for no one-to-one meetings with the manager, another person present, or a temporary different reporting line while you decide what to do next.

6. **If you feel able, make a short written record with a safer workplace contact.**
   - You can write: “I am receiving sexual messages from my manager. I feel uncomfortable and pressured to respond. I have screenshots. Please tell me the next steps under the harassment or dignity-at-work policy and what temporary protections can be put in place.”
   - Ask how they will protect you from victimisation or being treated worse because you raised it.
   - You can ask what will happen before you decide whether to make a formal grievance.

7. **Start a simple changes log.** Note any sudden changes after this, such as rota shifts, duties removed, criticism, isolation, threats, or pressure to meet privately. Record the date, what happened, who was involved, and any witnesses.

8. **If you fear immediate harm, widen your safety net.**
   - Tell one trusted person where you are and how you are getting home.
   - Avoid being alone with the manager if you can.
   - If you feel unsafe, receive threats, or need emergency help, call 999 or 112.

9. **Get specialist support if you feel shaken, frozen, or frightened.**
   - You can contact a sexual violence support service even if it was “only messages” or you are unsure what label fits.
   - You can contact ACAS for workplace guidance about options without committing to a formal step.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a tribunal claim, accept a settlement, leave your job, or report to the police.
- You do not need to write a perfect statement right now; saving the messages and making a basic note is enough for the first step.
- You do not need to confront the manager face-to-face to prove that you objected.
- You do not need to work out the legal label before asking for support.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressured, conflicted, frozen, or worried about consequences is a common response when the person sending the messages has power over your work. You are allowed to keep this simple, prioritise safety, and bring in support rather than carrying it alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation, preserve options, and reduce immediate risk. Later decisions about a formal grievance, external routes, legal advice, or leaving the role may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice, medical advice, therapy, or a substitute for professional support. If you are in immediate danger, call 999 or 112. If you are unsure what to call what is happening, it is still valid to seek help and ask your employer or a specialist service what support is available.
