# What to do if… your computer displays a ransom note and you have not paid or contacted anyone yet (UK)

## Short answer
Disconnect the affected computer from Wi-Fi, Ethernet, Bluetooth, and any shared network now. Do not pay, reply, click the ransom note, or plug in backup drives.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay, negotiate, or message the attacker “just to ask”.
- Don’t click links, QR codes, “support chat” buttons, attachments, or payment instructions in the ransom note.
- Don’t plug in backup drives, USB sticks, phones, or external hard drives.
- Don’t log in to banking, email, cloud storage, work systems, or a password manager on the affected computer.
- Don’t run random “ransomware removal” or “decryptor” tools from search results.
- Don’t factory reset, reinstall, or repeatedly restart the computer unless your IT team or a trusted professional tells you to.
- Don’t accept unsolicited calls, messages, or adverts from people claiming they can fix it for a fee.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the device first.**
   - Turn off Wi-Fi.
   - Unplug the Ethernet cable if there is one.
   - Turn off Bluetooth.
   - Disconnect it from any dock, office network, shared drive, or home network storage.
   - If you cannot disconnect it from the network without clicking through the ransom note, leave the note alone and get help from a known-clean phone or computer.

2. **Keep backups and other devices away from it.**
   - Do not connect backup drives or USB sticks.
   - Do not sign in from another device to sync files with the affected computer.
   - If other computers share the same network or shared folders, avoid opening shared drives until someone has checked whether they are affected.

3. **Record what is visible without exploring the computer.**
   - Take photos of the ransom note, any countdown timer, payment demand, contact address, file extension, and victim ID shown on screen.
   - Note the approximate time you first saw it.
   - Write down which places look affected, such as Desktop, Documents, a shared drive, or an external drive that was already connected.

4. **If it is a work, school, charity, or organisation device, stop and escalate.**
   - Contact IT, the helpdesk, your manager, or the security team using a different phone or computer.
   - Say: “Ransom note on screen; device isolated from network; I have not paid or contacted anyone.”
   - Do not try to clean, decrypt, restore, or investigate the device yourself unless they tell you to.

5. **From a known-clean device, protect the accounts most likely to be abused.**
   - Start with email, banking, cloud storage, remote access, and any password manager account.
   - Change passwords only from a device that is not affected.
   - Turn on two-step verification where available.
   - Check for new forwarding rules, password reset emails, new devices, or new payees.

6. **Report it through the right UK route.**
   - If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report cyber crime or fraud through Report Fraud.
   - If you are in Scotland, report it to Police Scotland online, by calling 101 for a non-emergency, or by calling 999 if there is an immediate emergency.
   - If this involves an organisation, the organisation can also use the NCSC cyber incident reporting route.

7. **If money or identity details may be exposed, add friction now.**
   - Contact your bank’s fraud team using the number in your banking app, on your card, or on the bank’s official website.
   - Watch for password reset emails you did not request, new devices on accounts, new payees, or unusual login alerts.
   - Do not use any phone number, link, or chat contact shown in the ransom note.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you would ever pay.
- You do not need to contact the attacker to “buy time”.
- You do not need to find a decryptor immediately.
- You do not need to restore from backups until the affected device and any shared storage have been checked.
- You do not need to write a full incident report before the device is isolated and the right people are told.

## Important reassurance
A ransom note is designed to make you rush. Not paying, not replying, and isolating the computer are already protective steps that reduce the chance of making the situation worse.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Recovery, file restoration, device rebuilding, malware removal, data-theft checks, and decisions about affected personal data may need trusted technical, organisational, legal, or data-protection help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. If the device belongs to an employer, school, charity, or other organisation, follow their incident process and ask the responsible person to check any data-protection reporting duties.
