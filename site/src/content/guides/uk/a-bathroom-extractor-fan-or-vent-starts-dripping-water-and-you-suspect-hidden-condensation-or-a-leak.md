# What to do if… a bathroom extractor fan or vent starts dripping water and you suspect hidden condensation or a leak (UK)

## Short answer
Isolate the power to the fan circuit immediately, stop using the bathroom for steam-generating activities, and place a container to catch the water. Do not touch the fitting or internal wiring while water is present.

## Do not do these things
- Don't use a hair dryer or heater to try and "dry out" the vent while it is still dripping.
- Don't touch the fan, pull-cord, or light switch with wet hands or while standing on a wet floor.
- Don't poke tools or fingers into the fan grille to "see where it's coming from."
- Don't ignore the drip even if it stops when the shower is off; it indicates a structural or ventilation failure.
- Don't reset a tripped RCD or circuit breaker if you suspect water has entered the electrical housing.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the electrical supply to the fan.** 
   - Locate the **fan isolator switch**, typically a 3-pole switch located high up on the wall outside the bathroom or above the door. 
   - If water is dripping directly from or onto the fan housing or light, **turn off the "Lights" or "Bathroom" circuit at the consumer unit (fuse box)**.
2. **Check the consumer unit for tripped switches.** 
   - Look for an **RCD (Residual Current Device)** or a specific circuit breaker that has flipped down. If it has tripped, **do not attempt to flip it back up** until a professional has inspected the fan.
3. **Contain the water and secure the room.** 
   - Place a bucket or towels on the floor directly under the drip to prevent slips. 
   - **Mop up any standing water on the floor** immediately and keep pets and children out of the room.
4. **Stop the source of moisture.** 
   - Do not use the shower, bath, or any hot water in that room. 
   - If the weather is dry, **open the bathroom window fully** to allow humid air to escape naturally. Keep the bathroom door closed to prevent moist air from traveling to other cold parts of the house.
5. **Document the damage for repair or insurance.** 
   - Take photos of the **drip point**, any **yellow or brown staining** on the ceiling, and any **swelling/bulging** of the plasterboard. 
   - Note if the dripping is constant (suggests a pipe/roof leak) or only occurs after a shower (suggests duct condensation).
6. **Notify the responsible party immediately.** 
   - **If you rent:** Report this as an "Emergency Hazard" to your landlord or agent. Under the Renters' Rights Act 2026, landlords must respond to emergency hazards affecting safety (water/electrics) within 24 hours.
   - **If you own:** Contact a NAPIT or NICEIC registered electrician if the water hit electrical components. Contact a plumber if the drip is constant or a roofer if it occurs during rain.
7. **Conduct a visual-only check of the loft space.** 
   - If you have safe access to the loft area above the bathroom, look for a **sagging flexible duct** that may be holding a pool of water ("water-trapping"). 
   - Look for **missing insulation** around the ducting or a **disconnected pipe** where moist air is being dumped into the loft instead of outside. **Do not touch any wet electrical junctions.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause (condensation vs. roof leak) before seeking professional help.
- You do not need to replace the fan or ducting today; the priority is making the electrical system safe.
- You do not need to clean or paint over water stains until the area is confirmed dry and the leak source is fixed.

## Important reassurance
Water dripping from a fan is a frequent occurrence in UK homes, often caused by warm air condensing in uninsulated ducts during cold weather. While it looks alarming, the risk is manageable as long as the power is isolated and you do not interact with the wet components.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and hazard isolation. Permanent repairs may involve installing insulated ducting, a condensation trap, or repairing external roof flashing.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and is not a substitute for a professional survey. If water has entered electrical fittings, the circuit must remain off until certified safe by a qualified electrician. If a landlord fails to address a significant hazard, contact your local council’s Environmental Health department.
