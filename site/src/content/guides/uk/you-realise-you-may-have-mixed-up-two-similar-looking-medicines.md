# What to do if… you realise you may have mixed up two similar-looking medicines (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and do not take any more doses until you have checked what happened. Get urgent advice from a pharmacist or NHS 111; call 999 now if there are emergency symptoms such as severe breathing difficulty, seizure, collapse, unconsciousness, or the person cannot be kept awake.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to “cancel it out” by taking the other medicine or an extra dose.
- Do not take your next scheduled dose until a pharmacist, NHS 111, or another clinician has told you what to do.
- Do not throw away packaging, empty blister strips, bottles, labels, or the pill organiser involved.
- Do not make yourself or someone else vomit.
- Do not give food, drink, alcohol, or “detox” remedies unless a clinician tells you to.
- Do not drive yourself to A&E or anywhere else if you feel unwell, drowsy, dizzy, confused, faint, or impaired.

## What to do now
1. Stop taking medicines for the moment. Put the two similar-looking medicines, and any other possible medicine involved, in one clear place where they cannot be mixed further.
2. Keep everything in its original packaging if you can: boxes, blister packs, bottles, pharmacy labels, patient leaflets, and any pill organiser involved.
3. Check for emergency signs now. Call 999 if the person has severe difficulty breathing, has stopped breathing, has had a seizure, has collapsed, is unconscious, cannot be kept awake, or you think the situation is immediately life-threatening.
4. Work out only the facts you can check. Look at the medicine names, strengths, dose instructions, how many tablets or capsules may be missing, and the time the dose may have been taken. Do not guess to fill gaps.
5. Get urgent advice using the fastest route available:
   - If a pharmacy is open and easy to reach, call or go in and say, “I may have taken the wrong medicine or wrong dose.” Take the packaging with you if you go.
   - If you cannot reach a pharmacist quickly, or you are not sure it is safe to wait, call NHS 111.
   - Use 111 online if it is available where you are; call 111 instead if the person is under 5, has complex health problems, or you are unsure which route to use.
6. Have this information ready for the pharmacist, NHS 111, ambulance crew, or clinician:
   - the exact medicine names and strengths from the labels
   - the dose instructions on each medicine
   - how much may have been taken
   - when it may have been taken
   - the person’s age
   - pregnancy, kidney or liver disease, diabetes, heart conditions, blood-thinning medicine, sedatives, strong pain medicines, or any other major medical condition or regular medicine
7. Follow the advice you are given exactly. If you are told to go to A&E or urgent care, take all the packets, bottles, labels, leaflets, and the pill organiser if it was involved.
8. While waiting for advice, stay where you can be observed if drowsiness, dizziness, faintness, confusion, vomiting, rash, swelling, chest pain, or breathing changes start. Call 999 for emergency signs; otherwise use NHS 111 or a pharmacist urgently if symptoms change.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this was “serious”. Get professional triage first.
- You do not need to solve your whole medicines routine today.
- You do not need to report packaging, labelling, side effects, or a near miss until the immediate safety question has been dealt with.
- You do not need to work out blame. The useful thing now is to keep the packaging and get the right advice.

## Important reassurance
Medicine mix-ups can happen, especially when names, packaging, tablets, or routines look similar. Pausing, keeping the evidence of what was taken, and asking for urgent advice are practical steps that help reduce risk.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes to hours. Later decisions about dose changes, replacement medicines, side effects, reporting, storage, or safer labelling may need help from a pharmacist, NHS 111, your GP surgery, or another clinician.

## Important note
This is general information, not a diagnosis or personalised medical, legal, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you think someone is in immediate danger, call 999.
