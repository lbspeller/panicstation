# What to do if… you realise you may have mixed up two similar-looking medicines (UK)

## Short answer
Stop taking any further medication and keep all original packaging in front of you for identification. Contact a pharmacist immediately if you feel well, or call NHS 111 if the pharmacy is closed or if you are unsure what you have taken.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take any "compensatory" doses or try to cancel the medicine out with another pill.
- Do not wait for symptoms to appear before seeking professional advice.
- Do not throw away the blister packs, boxes, or labels even if they are empty.
- Do not attempt to induce vomiting or use home remedies to "flush" your system.
- Do not drive or operate machinery until a clinician confirms it is safe to do so.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the suspected medicines and their packaging immediately.** **Place all boxes, blister strips, and any loose tablets on a clear surface to prevent further mix-ups.**
   - Keep the items in the containers they came in.
   - Do not tip tablets out of their strips to compare them.
2. **Check the person for life-threatening symptoms that require an immediate 999 call.** **Look for signs of an acute reaction or overdose, including:**
   - Difficulty breathing, gasping, or being unable to speak in full sentences.
   - Blue or purple tint to the lips, tongue, or fingernails.
   - Unusual snoring or gurgling sounds while breathing.
   - Sudden swelling of the face, lips, or throat.
   - Seizures or uncontrollable shaking.
   - Being unable to wake the person up or keep them awake.
3. **Identify the exact names and strengths of the potential medicines involved.** **Write down the name of the medicine, the strength (e.g., 5mg or 500mg), and the time the dose was taken.**
   - If using a pill organiser (dosette box), check which compartments are empty compared to your usual routine.
4. **Access professional clinical triage based on the person's age and symptoms.** **Contact the correct service immediately to explain that a medication error has occurred:**
   - If the person feels unwell or has any symptoms: Call 111 or 999 based on severity.
   - If the person feels well and a pharmacy is open: Call your local pharmacist for urgent advice.
   - If using digital help: Use 111 online (for those aged 5 and over only).
   - If the person is under 5 years old: Call 111 directly rather than using the website.
5. **Provide the clinician with the patient's specific health details.** **Be ready to state the person's age, weight, and any known kidney or liver problems.**
   - Mention if the person is pregnant or breastfeeding.
   - List any other regular medications being taken to check for interactions.
6. **Follow the instructions given by the pharmacist or 111 advisor exactly.** **If you are told to attend A&E or an Urgent Treatment Centre, take every piece of packaging and the suspected medicines with you.**
7. **Ensure the person is monitored by another adult for the next few hours.** **Stay with the person and watch for delayed symptoms such as extreme drowsiness, dizziness, or vomiting.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to figure out how the mistake happened while you are in the crisis phase.
- You do not need to reorganise your entire medicine cabinet or pill routine today.
- You do not need to worry about "wasting NHS time"; medication mix-ups are a standard reason for triage.

## Important reassurance
Medication errors are common and often happen due to "Look-Alike Sound-Alike" (LASA) packaging or simple distraction. Most mix-ups are successfully managed when caught early. Taking the step to stop and ask for help is the correct safety action.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the minutes following a suspected medication mix-up. It does not replace the specific clinical advice of a doctor or pharmacist.

## Important note
Always follow the advice of the medical professional you speak to on the phone or in person. This information is for general guidance and does not account for specific drug toxicities.
