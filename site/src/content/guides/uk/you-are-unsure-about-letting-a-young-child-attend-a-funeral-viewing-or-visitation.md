# What to do if… you are unsure about letting a young child attend a funeral viewing or visitation (UK)

## Short answer
You do not have to decide on the spot. Slow it down, get clear on what the viewing will be like, and, if appropriate, offer the child a real choice with simple, honest preparation and a clear “leave anytime” plan.

## Do not do these things
- Do not present it as a test, a duty, or something the child is doing to help adults feel better.
- Do not use euphemisms like “asleep” if the child is likely to take that literally.
- Do not force a yes or no immediately, or pressure them with “you’ll regret it if you don’t.”
- Do not let adults argue about it in front of the child.
- Do not take them in without a named adult whose only job is to watch the child and step out with them.
- Do not assume “young” automatically means “too young” or “fine”; the child’s temperament and understanding matter.

## What to do now
1. **Buy time and reduce pressure.** Say to the organiser or family: “I can’t decide this minute. I need the details and a plan for the children.” Ask whether there is another time slot or the option to arrive early when it is quieter.
2. **Get the concrete facts so you are not imagining worst-case.** Ask the funeral director or the person arranging the viewing:
   - where it happens, for example a chapel of rest,
   - whether the coffin is open or closed and what will be visible,
   - how long you can stay and whether you can step out easily,
   - what the room is like, including lighting, smells, and how many people may be present.
   If the viewing is at a hospital mortuary, ask hospital staff what the process looks like and where you can wait or step out.
3. **If the death was sudden or traumatic, ask before offering the choice.** Speak to hospital staff or the funeral director first about what viewing may involve, whether preparation is possible, and whether there are gentler alternatives.
4. **Decide who the choice is for.** If the child is old enough to understand a basic explanation, aim for their informed choice. If they are very young or easily overwhelmed, you can still plan a gentle optional approach, such as coming to the building but not entering the viewing room.
5. **Give the child a simple, honest preview.** Use plain words:
   - “We can go to a room where we can see [name]’s body. Their body has stopped working and they can’t feel anything.”
   - “They may look different from when they were alive.”
   - “You can choose not to go in, and you can change your mind at any time.”
6. **Offer safe options, not a single cliff-edge yes or no.** For example:
   - “We can come with me to the door and decide there.”
   - “You can stand far back.”
   - “You can come in briefly and then leave.”
   - “You can stay with [trusted adult] in the waiting area instead.”
7. **Make an exit plan the child can trust.** Agree a quiet signal, such as a hand squeeze or one word. Tell them: “If you use the signal, we leave straight away.”
8. **Assign roles before you arrive.** One adult stays calm and focuses on the child. A second adult handles other relatives and logistics. If you are the bereaved parent and may be overwhelmed, ask another trusted adult to be the child’s anchor for the visit.
9. **Bring one grounding item and set expectations.** A small comfort item, such as a toy or blanket, is OK. Explain that people may cry, the room may be quiet, and they will not be in trouble for having feelings or for not having strong feelings.
10. **If family members disagree, use a neutral rule.** Say: “No pressure, no forcing. We will prepare the child and follow the plan to leave if they want.” Repeat it and end the debate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether this will help their grief long-term.
- You do not need to choose the perfect wording; simple and honest is enough.
- You do not need to resolve family conflict about what is appropriate right now.
- You do not need to decide whether the child will attend the funeral or service as well. Treat that as a separate decision.

## Important reassurance
Feeling torn is normal. Wanting to protect children and also wanting them included can both be true. A calm, informed choice with a clear exit plan can make the moment feel less pressured and more manageable.

## Scope note
This is first-steps guidance for the immediate decision and the next practical actions. Later decisions may need help from a funeral director, hospital staff, school pastoral staff, or a specialist child bereavement service, especially if the death was sudden, traumatic, or the child is already highly anxious.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel unable to keep the child emotionally safe during a viewing, it is OK to choose an alternative way to say goodbye and seek support from a child bereavement service, school pastoral lead, health visitor, or GP if you are worried about their wellbeing.
