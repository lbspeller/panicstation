# What to do if… your wheelchair caster or wheel starts wobbling and you need to keep moving safely (UK)

## Short answer
Slow down straight away and steer to the safest flat place you can reach without sharp turns. Stop, put the brakes on if fitted, and treat the wobble as a safety fault until it has been checked.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep your usual speed to “get it over with”; wobble or pulling can get harder to control.
- Don’t take ramps, cambers, kerbs, rough paving, or tight turns while it is wobbling if you can avoid them.
- Don’t lean far forward or sideways to inspect the wheel while still moving.
- Don’t let anyone push you quickly from behind unless you have agreed a slow, straight, flat route.
- Don’t travel any distance if a wheel looks loose, rubs the frame, shifts on its axle, or braking feels uneven.
- Don’t try major repairs, bearing adjustments, locknut adjustments, or caster alignment in the street.

## What to do now
1. **Slow down and keep the chair’s path simple.** Roll straight, slow, and steady. Avoid quick steering, sudden braking, slopes, and rough surfaces.

2. **Move to a safe pause point if you can.** Aim for flat ground away from traffic, such as a wide pavement area, shop entrance, lobby, station concourse, hospital reception, or level landing. Do not stop on a slope if you can reach level ground safely.

3. **Stop and stabilise the chair.** Apply both parking brakes if fitted. If you are using a powered wheelchair, stop driving and switch the chair off if it feels unstable.

4. **Do a quick no-tools check from a stable position.**
   - Look for hair, string, stones, mud, or other debris caught in or around the caster, castor fork, or wheel. Remove only what you can reach safely.
   - Check whether the wheel is rubbing the frame, wobbling side to side, or sitting at an unusual angle.
   - If your chair has quick-release axles, check whether the wheel appears fully seated. Do not force it; if it does not look or feel secure, stop using the chair and get help.
   - If you have pneumatic tyres and one feels very soft, avoid travelling any distance until it has been dealt with.

5. **Choose between a short slow move and stopping fully.**
   - **Stop and get help now** if the wobble is strong, the chair veers, a wheel looks loose, the wheel rubs the frame, braking feels uneven, or you are near traffic, slopes, kerbs, or a ramp.
   - If the wobble is mild and you must move, only roll slowly on flat ground to the nearest safer indoor space or staffed help point. Avoid ramps, kerbs, road crossings, rough paving, and distance.

6. **Ask for practical help where you are.**
   - In a station, shopping centre, hospital, campus, workplace, or venue, ask staff or security for a safe waiting space and help arranging onward travel.
   - Ask someone nearby to walk beside you or behind you only if they can keep the chair slow and steady and follow your instructions.
   - If you are stranded somewhere unsafe, explain clearly: “My wheelchair wheel is wobbling and I need help getting to a safe flat place.”

7. **Use emergency or urgent health routes only when they fit the risk.**
   - Call **999** if there is immediate danger to life or risk of serious injury, for example you are stuck in a road, about to fall, have fallen and may be seriously hurt, or cannot move away from danger.
   - Use **NHS 111** if you are injured, unwell, or unsure whether symptoms after the incident need emergency care.

8. **Arrange repair once you are stable.**
   - If the wheelchair was issued or maintained by the NHS, contact your local NHS wheelchair service if you have their contact details, or ask your GP, physiotherapist, occupational therapist, or hospital staff how to reach the right wheelchair service.
   - If the wheelchair was privately bought, leased, or supplied by a retailer, contact the supplier, manufacturer, or an approved repairer and avoid further use until it has been assessed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact mechanical cause right now.
- You do not need to adjust bearings, locknuts, caster angle, wheel alignment, or spokes while stranded.
- You do not need to decide today about parts, replacement equipment, or long-term mobility plans.
- You do not need to continue the journey just because it feels awkward to stop; the priority is safe, stable mobility.

## Important reassurance
A wobbling caster or wheel is a valid safety warning. It is reasonable to slow down, stop, ask for help, and get the chair checked before travelling further.

## Scope note
These are first steps to help you reach a safer pause point and the right repair route. Later decisions about repairs, adjustments, replacement parts, or alternative mobility support may need help from a wheelchair technician, NHS wheelchair service, Occupational Therapy service, supplier, or manufacturer.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not professional repair, medical, legal, financial, or transport advice. If you feel unsafe continuing, stop and seek help; if there is immediate danger to life or risk of serious injury, call 999.
