# What to do if… your charge port will not close after charging and you are unsure it is safe to drive (UK)

## Short answer
Ensure the inner protective caps or doors covering the electrical pins are fully seated and flush. If the outer flap still will not latch, do not drive the vehicle if the flap obstructs your view, creates a snagging hazard, or if the dashboard displays a red warning light.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force or slam the flap closed as this can snap the plastic hinges or damage the locking actuator.
- Do not use metal tools, screwdrivers, or wire brushes to poke or clean the electrical inlet.
- Do not spray WD-40, water, or cleaning chemicals directly into the electrical pin holes.
- Do not drive on high-speed roads (motorways / dual carriageways) if the flap is unlatched and could swing open.
- Do not ignore red warning symbols or messages stating "Electrical System Fault" or "High Voltage Hazard".

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe, level location and apply the electronic parking brake.** Ensure the vehicle is in Park (P) and fully powered down before inspecting the exterior.
2. **Verify that all inner protective covers are completely shut.** Most "stuck flap" issues are caused by the inner plastic DC or AC dust caps being slightly askew, which prevents the outer door from meeting the latch.
   - Remove any grit, snow, or debris from the rubber seals of these inner caps.
   - Ensure the tether (the plastic string holding the cap) is not caught in the hinge.
3. **Inspect the inlet for moisture or foreign objects without touching the pins.** If you see water sitting inside the inlet, do not attempt to dry it with a finger or tool; use a dry, lint-free cloth to dab the surrounding plastic housing only.
   - Look for "pushed back" pins or bent plastic that may indicate the charging cable was removed at a sharp angle.
4. **Perform a vehicle lock / unlock cycle to reset the latching actuator.** Many modern EVs use an electronic solenoid to lock the flap; locking the car with the key fob, waiting 30 seconds, and then unlocking can reset the mechanism.
5. **Check for ice or physical obstructions in the hinge area.** If the weather is below freezing, use the warmth of your hands or a lukewarm (not hot) damp cloth on the *outer* plastic to melt ice; never pour boiling water near electrical components.
6. **Secure the flap temporarily only if the vehicle is safe to move.** If the inner caps are secure and there are no electrical warnings, but the outer flap is loose, you may use a small piece of low-residue tape (such as electrical or painter's tape) to hold the flap flush against the bodywork.
   - Stop immediately if the tape fails or the flap begins to vibrate.
7. **Contact emergency road services if you are on a motorway or feel unsafe.** If the car will not "start" due to a charge-port-open sensor, or if you are in a live lane, seek professional recovery.
   - **In England:** Call National Highways on 0300 123 5000.
   - **In Scotland:** Call Traffic Scotland on 0800 028 1414.
   - **In Wales:** Call Traffic Wales on 0300 123 1213.
   - **In Northern Ireland:** Call 999 for motorway assistance (PSNI).

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the specific failure (e.g., solenoid vs hinge) while at the roadside.
- You do not need to arrange a permanent repair until the vehicle is in a safe, non-obstructing location.
- You do not need to clean or detail the charge port area beyond removing immediate obstructions.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel anxious when an EV component does not behave as expected, especially involving the high-voltage system. Charge port latches are mechanical wear items and frequently experience minor issues due to road grit or cold weather; taking a moment to check the inner caps usually resolves the problem.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a mechanical flap failure only. It does not cover electrical diagnostic procedures or internal high-voltage repairs, which must be performed by a qualified EV technician.

## Important note
Driving a vehicle with an unsecured exterior component may be considered a roadworthiness violation if it poses a risk to other road users or obstructs the driver's view. If the dashboard displays a red warning light, the vehicle should not be driven; contact your breakdown provider or the manufacturer's roadside assistance immediately.
