# What to do if… your minimum credit card payment jumps far higher than usual (UK)

## Short answer
Check your latest statement or app to find what changed, then contact your card provider before the due date if paying the new minimum would put essential bills at risk.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore it or assume it will sort itself out next month; charges, extra interest, and credit-file damage may follow.
- Don’t pay the new minimum if it means missing priority bills such as rent or mortgage, council tax, energy, essential food, or medicine.
- Don’t take high-cost short-term credit just to cover the minimum.
- Don’t cancel a direct debit in a panic unless you have made another clear plan to avoid missing the due date.
- Don’t assume the figure is correct if a transaction, fee, rate change, or returned payment looks unfamiliar.

## What to do now
1. **Open the latest statement or app and find the exact minimum payment and due date.**  
   Save a screenshot or PDF showing the minimum payment, due date, balance, interest, fees, and any new instalment or plan amount.

2. **Look for what changed this month.**  
   Check for:
   - A 0% or promotional rate ending
   - Interest added for the first time or at a higher rate
   - A late fee, overlimit fee, returned payment, or cash-like transaction fee
   - New spending, a reversed refund, or a higher balance
   - A message or notice saying the provider changed the minimum-payment calculation or account terms

3. **Protect priority bills before unsecured credit-card payments.**  
   Make sure rent or mortgage, council tax, energy, essential food, medicine, and essential living costs are covered before deciding what you can safely pay to the card.

4. **If you can pay the new minimum without harming essentials, schedule it now and stop using the card.**  
   Pay from an account that will not be pushed into an unaffordable overdraft. Keep the payment confirmation.

5. **If you cannot afford the new minimum, contact the card provider before the due date.**  
   Ask for:
   - A clear explanation of what changed and how the minimum was calculated
   - A temporary reduced payment arrangement you can actually meet
   - A pause, reduction, or freeze on interest and charges if they can offer it
   - A review of any fee that seems wrong or unusual

   You can say: “My minimum payment has jumped to £__ and I cannot afford that this month without missing essential bills. Please explain what changed and help me set an affordable temporary arrangement before the due date.”

6. **If a charge, transaction, or fee looks wrong, raise it in writing.**  
   Send a short message saying what the item is, why you think it is wrong, and what correction or refund you are asking for. Save copies, dates, and any reply.

7. **Get free debt advice quickly if this is not a one-off.**  
   A free adviser can help you work out priority bills, prepare a realistic offer to the card provider, and check which debt options fit where you live.
   - If you live in England or Wales, ask whether Breathing Space is suitable.
   - If you live in Scotland or Northern Ireland, ask about the local debt options instead, because schemes differ.

8. **If the provider will not help or you think they have treated you unfairly, complain to the provider.**  
   Keep your complaint short and factual: what happened, when you contacted them, what you asked for, and what would put things right. If it is not resolved through the provider’s complaint process, you can ask the Financial Ombudsman Service to look at it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to close the card, consolidate debt, switch provider, or use a formal debt solution.
- You do not need a perfect long-term repayment plan before contacting the provider.
- You do not need to challenge every line of the statement at once; focus first on the due date, the reason for the jump, and any obvious error.

## Important reassurance
A sudden minimum-payment jump usually has a traceable cause, such as interest, fees, a higher balance, a promotional rate ending, or a changed repayment calculation. One steady sequence is enough for now: check the statement, protect essentials, and contact the provider early.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the next payment and reduce avoidable harm. Later choices about repayment strategy, refinancing, complaints, or formal debt solutions may need help from a free debt adviser or another suitable specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not financial, legal, or professional advice. Credit-card terms and support options vary by provider and by where you live in the UK. If you are at risk of missing priority bills or several debt payments, get free UK debt advice as soon as you can.
