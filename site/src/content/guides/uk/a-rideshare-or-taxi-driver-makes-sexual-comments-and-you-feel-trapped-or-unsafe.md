# What to do if… a rideshare or taxi driver makes sexual comments and you feel trapped or unsafe (UK)

## Short answer
Your priority is to get to a safer, public place as soon as you can. If you feel in immediate danger, call **999**.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t argue, explain, or try to “teach a lesson” if that might escalate the driver.
- Don’t feel you have to be polite, laugh, or play along to keep things calm.
- Don’t accept a detour, quick stop, or offer to be dropped somewhere more private.
- Don’t share personal details, such as whether you live alone, where you are going next, or your relationship status, if you can avoid it.
- Don’t wait to see if it gets worse if you already feel unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Switch to “get out safely” mode.** Use a neutral, practical reason to end or pause the trip:
   - “I feel sick. Please pull in at the next petrol station or busy shop.”
   - “I need to take a call. Please stop at the next safe place.”
   - “I’m changing my drop-off. Please stop at that supermarket entrance.”
   Aim for a staffed, well-lit, busy location, such as a petrol station, supermarket entrance, hotel lobby, transport hub, or open shop.

2. **Make it easier for someone to find you.**
   - If you are in a rideshare app, share your trip status or live location with a trusted person if the app allows it.
   - If you cannot do that, send a short message such as: “In a taxi now. I feel unsafe. Heading to [busy place].”
   - If it feels safer, call someone and keep the call simple: “I’m in the car now. Stay on the phone with me.”

3. **Use emergency options if you feel at risk.**
   - If you feel in immediate danger, call **999**.
   - If you cannot speak safely on a mobile, call **999**, listen to the operator, make a sound if you can, and press **55** if prompted so the call can be transferred to police.

4. **Create a low-conflict exit at the stop.**
   - Keep your reason short and practical: “This is fine, I’m getting out here.”
   - Take your belongings before you end the conversation.
   - Step out and move toward people or staff, not to a quiet pavement corner.
   - If the driver challenges you, repeat the same short line rather than debating.

5. **Once you are away from the vehicle, do not re-engage.**
   - Go inside the busy place if you can.
   - Ask staff for help arranging another way home or calling someone.
   - If the driver follows, blocks you, threatens you, or will not leave, ask staff to call **999**.

6. **Capture only what is easy and safe.**
   If it will not put you at risk, note or screenshot the time, pickup and drop-off area, car registration, driver name, badge or licence number, route, and trip receipt. Do not do this while it could escalate the situation.

7. **After you are safe, use support or reporting routes only if you choose.**
   - You can speak to a specialist sexual violence support service without deciding whether to report.
   - Rideshare app: report it in-app as a safety concern if you want to.
   - Taxi or private hire booked through an operator: report it to the operator if you want to.
   - Taxi or private hire licence issue: report it to the local council licensing team, or to TfL if it was in London, if you want to.
   - Police: use **101** or online reporting if it is not an emergency.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report to police, the company, or the licensing authority.
- You do not need to write a perfect account immediately; getting safe first is enough.
- You do not need to answer messages from the driver, operator, or platform while you are still shaken.
- You do not need to decide whether what happened was “serious enough” before seeking support.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, going quiet, laughing nervously, or being overly polite can be a normal safety response. Feeling trapped can make sexual comments or questions feel frightening, and you are allowed to prioritise getting away safely.

## Scope note
These are first steps to help you reach a safer pause and stabilise the situation. Later decisions, including reporting, complaints, or support, may need specialist help if you choose to seek it.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call **999**. If you are safe now but want police advice or to report, you can contact police via **101** or online.
