# What to do if… you discover an adult is sending sexual messages to your child in a game or app (UK)

## Short answer

Get your child away from the conversation, stay calm with them, and treat this as a safeguarding concern rather than something they should handle alone. If your child is at immediate risk, or the adult is trying to meet them, threatening them, or blackmailing them, call 999.

## Do not do these things

- Do not tell your child to keep replying so you can see what happens.
- Do not confront the adult through your child’s account.
- Do not ask your child to retell everything repeatedly right now.
- Do not blame your child for replying, playing along, hiding it, or not spotting it sooner.
- Do not delete the chat immediately if you may need to make a report.
- Do not share screenshots with friends, group chats, or other parents.
- Do not arrange your own call, meeting, or sting.

## What to do now

1. Move your child to a calmer, safer pause and stop contact with that person now. Close the chat, take the device out of active use for the moment, and stay with your child while you decide the next step.

2. Tell your child clearly that they are not in trouble and this is not their fault. Keep questions simple and practical, such as where the chat happened, what username was used, and whether the person asked for secrecy, photos, live video, money, threats, or a meetup.

3. If you may report it and the messages are visible, keep only a brief record before anything disappears, such as screenshots showing the username, platform, date if visible, and the concerning messages. Do not investigate further or ask your child to keep engaging.

4. Use the game or app’s reporting tools after you have made any brief record you need. Report the account for sexual contact with a child, grooming, or inappropriate sexual communication, then block the account so they cannot keep contacting your child there.

5. Report the concern to CEOP if an adult has been asking your child for sexual chat, sexual images, sexual activity on webcam or livestream, secrecy, or a meetup after online contact. You do not need to know everything before reporting.

6. Call 999 if your child is at immediate risk, the adult is trying to meet them, knows where they are and may come there, or is making threats or blackmail demands. If it is not an emergency but you need police involvement, call 101 or report the crime online.

7. If you are worried that your child is at risk or being abused, contact children’s social care at your local council. A trusted safeguarding adult, such as the school safeguarding lead when appropriate, can also help with immediate support and next steps.

8. If a sexual image or video of your child has already been shared online, do not negotiate with the adult. Focus on safety and reporting; Childline and the Internet Watch Foundation’s Report Remove tool may help under-18s in the UK ask for a sexual image or video to be removed.

9. If you need help thinking through the next immediate steps, contact the NSPCC Helpline. Your child can also contact Childline if they want private support from someone outside the family.

## What can wait

You do not need to decide today whether your child can ever use that game or app again, change every privacy setting, or have a big internet safety talk. You also do not need to work out who the adult really is, prove their age, or run your own investigation before reporting.

## Important reassurance

Children are often manipulated in ways that are confusing, flattering, secretive, or frightening. A calm response from you now can reduce shame and make it more likely your child will keep telling you important things.

## Scope note

These are first steps only. Later decisions about safeguarding, school support, platform safety, police follow-up, or emotional support may need specialist help.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, investigative, or other professional advice. In highly sensitive situations like this, the safest first priority is your child’s immediate emotional and physical safety, then using the appropriate reporting route.
