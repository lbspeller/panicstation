# What to do if… police contact you and ask for your side of the story (UK)

## Short answer
Do not give your side of the story immediately. First check the contact is genuine, then say you want legal advice before answering questions or agreeing to an interview.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to “clear it up” on the phone or in an unplanned chat.
- Do not guess, fill gaps, speculate, or explain other people’s motives.
- Do not give an informal statement, written timeline, screenshots, passwords, phone access, or messages before legal advice.
- Do not delete, edit, hide, or forward messages or files in a panic.
- Do not contact anyone involved to “sort it out” or ask what they have said.
- Do not pay money, share bank details, or use a link sent by the caller to “close the report”.
- Do not attend an interview without asking for legal advice first, unless you are arrested or clearly told you are legally required to attend.

## What to do now
1. **Pause before answering.** Your first job is not to persuade anyone. It is to avoid giving a rushed account before you understand what is happening.
2. **Check the contact is genuine.** Ask for the person’s name, role, police force or station, and reference number. End the call, then contact the police through 101 or a number from the police force’s official website. Do not rely on a number, link, or payment instruction supplied only by the caller.
3. **Ask only process questions.** Keep it short:
   - “Am I under arrest?”
   - “Am I free to leave or end this conversation?”
   - “Is this a request for a voluntary interview or formal interview under caution?”
   - “What offence or allegation is being investigated, in brief?”
4. **Use one calm sentence.** Say: “I am willing to cooperate, but I will not answer questions without legal advice. Please arrange contact through a solicitor or give me time to arrange one.”
5. **Ask for legal advice before any interview.** If police want a formal interview, including a voluntary interview, ask for free independent legal advice. You can ask for a solicitor you know, or ask police to arrange the available police station legal advice scheme. Asking for a solicitor does not mean you have done anything wrong.
6. **If they say it must happen now, repeat the request.** Say: “I want legal advice before any questions.” If they say they have a legal power, notice, or warrant, ask them to explain it clearly and ask for legal advice immediately.
7. **Write down the contact details.** Record the date, time, caller name, force or station, reference number, what they asked you to do, and any deadline or pressure used.
8. **If officers are at your door, keep it brief.** Be polite and say: “I am not answering questions without legal advice. Am I under arrest?” If you are not under arrest and they are asking for a voluntary interview, ask to arrange it through a solicitor.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to give a full account.
- You do not need to write a statement, timeline, apology, defence, or explanation tonight.
- You do not need to gather every message or document before speaking to a solicitor.
- You do not need to accept the first interview time suggested if you are not under arrest and have not had time to arrange legal advice.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel that explaining immediately will stop things getting worse. In police contact, a rushed explanation can be incomplete, misunderstood, or used later. Slowing down and getting legal advice is a normal protective step.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hour after unexpected police contact. Police wording and procedure can vary across the UK, and later decisions may need help from a criminal defence solicitor who deals with the process where you are.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. If someone is in immediate danger, call 999. If you are arrested or police are present and you cannot safely pause, ask for legal advice as soon as you can.
