# What to do if… a garage ceiling or roof structure is sagging and you are unsure if it is safe (UK)

## Short answer
Treat it as unsafe until proven otherwise. Get everyone out from under the sagging ceiling or roof, keep the garage clear, and call 999 if anyone may be in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay underneath it to watch, measure, film, or finish a task.
- Do not go back in for tools, vehicles, stored items, or sentimental items if you would need to stand under the sagging area.
- Do not try to prop it up with timber, jacks, ladders, shelves, or a car jack unless a competent professional is directing the work.
- Do not open or close an electric garage door if the frame, tracks, roof, or ceiling look twisted, scraping, or strained.
- Do not climb into a roof space, loft hatch, or storage area above the garage.
- Do not ignore new cracking, creaking, popping, falling plaster, falling boards, or a sag that is getting worse.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** Get people and pets out of the garage and away from the door line. If the garage is attached to the house, and you can do it without entering the garage, keep people away from rooms directly beside or above it until you have advice.
2. **Create a no-go zone.** Shut any internal door, keep children and pets away, and put something obvious in the way, such as bins, tape, or a chair, so nobody walks in by habit.
3. **Check for red flags only from a safe distance.** Treat it as urgent if you notice a rapidly worsening sag, falling plaster or boards, new large cracks, doors suddenly jamming, roof timbers bending, water bulging in the ceiling, or repeated cracking or creaking.
4. **Call 999 if there may be immediate danger.** Say there is a possible structural collapse risk in a garage, whether anyone is injured or trapped, and that you are keeping people away.
5. **Report it through your local council or local authority dangerous-structure route.** Search your council website for “dangerous structure”, “dangerous building”, “building control”, or “building standards”. If you cannot find the right page and the risk feels urgent, phone the council’s main number or out-of-hours emergency number.
6. **Tell the responsible person immediately.**
   - If you rent, contact your landlord or letting agent and say the garage is a no-go area because the ceiling or roof structure is sagging.
   - If it is leasehold, shared, or on an estate, contact the freeholder, managing agent, housing association, or building manager.
   - If you own it, contact your buildings insurer and ask what they need before inspection or repair work starts.
7. **Arrange a structural safety assessment.** Ask for a structural engineer, or a suitably competent building surveyor who can assess structural safety, before normal use or re-entry under the sagging area.
8. **Treat water and electrics as a hazard.** If water may be involved and you can do it safely without entering the garage or standing beneath the sagging area, switch off the garage circuit or supply at the consumer unit or fuse box. If water has reached the fuse box, there are sparks, buzzing, burning smells, exposed wiring, or you are unsure, stay clear and ask for urgent professional electrical advice.
9. **Do not add weight or disturb the structure.** Do not move storage around, climb on the roof, open roof storage, drain a bulging ceiling from underneath, or pull down wet boards.
10. **Record only what you can see safely.** Take photos or video from outside or from a doorway, note when it started, and write down recent triggers such as heavy rain, snow, impact, leaks, building work, or new storage above the garage.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the cause is rot, water weight, overload, roof spread, failed fixings, or damaged timbers right now.
- You do not need to choose a repair method today.
- You do not need to clear the garage contents if doing that would put you under the sagging area.
- You do not need to argue with yourself about whether you are overreacting; the first decision is simply to keep people out.

## Important reassurance
Leaving the space and keeping others out is a sensible first response, not an overreaction. A sagging ceiling or roof can sometimes turn out to be repairable, but it should be treated as unsafe until someone competent has assessed it.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce harm and get the right help. Later decisions about access, repair, insurance, responsibility, and building work may need advice from a structural professional, electrician, insurer, landlord, council, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, engineering, electrical, surveying, insurance, or other professional advice. If you believe anyone is in immediate danger, call 999.
