# What to do if… you develop a sudden high fever after returning from travel (UK)

## Short answer
Treat a sudden high fever after travel as urgent and get medical advice today. Tell the service or clinician exactly where you travelled, when you returned, and whether you were in a malaria-risk area.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if it passes if the fever is sudden, high, or comes with chills after travel.
- Do not sit in a GP, urgent-care, or emergency department waiting area without immediately telling reception or triage: “I have a fever after travel.”
- Do not take leftover antibiotics, antimalarials, or someone else’s medicine.
- Do not take ibuprofen or aspirin for this fever unless a clinician or pharmacist says it is suitable, especially if dengue could be possible.
- Do not travel alone by public transport if you feel faint, confused, very weak, or likely to collapse.
- Do not hide travel details because they feel unimportant; stopovers, rural stays, mosquito bites, animal contact, and freshwater swimming can matter.

## What to do now
1. Call 999 now if you have severe difficulty breathing, chest pain, new confusion, a seizure, collapse, blue or grey lips, severe drowsiness, uncontrolled bleeding, a purple rash that does not fade when pressed, or a very severe headache with stiff neck or bright-light sensitivity.

2. If it is not immediately life-threatening, use the urgent medical advice route for where you are in the UK. In England, use 111 online or call 111. In Scotland or Wales, phone 111 or use the local NHS 111 service. In Northern Ireland, use Phone First or the urgent-care route listed for your area. Say clearly: “I have a sudden high fever after travel.”

3. If you were in a malaria-risk country in the last 12 months, or you are not sure, say this early and plainly: “I have been in a possible malaria area within the last year and now I have a fever.” Ask what you should do today and where you should be assessed.

4. Write a short travel timeline before you speak to a clinician or while waiting for a call back. Include countries, regions, stopovers, dates, return date, rural or city stays, mosquito bites, freshwater swimming, animal bites or scratches, sick contacts, vaccines, malaria tablets, missed doses, and when the fever started.

5. Keep the fever information easy to share. Note your highest temperature if you have measured it, plus chills, sweats, rash, headache, cough, diarrhoea, vomiting, yellow eyes or skin, confusion, unusual bleeding, or worsening weakness.

6. Reduce close contact until you have advice. Stay in one room if practical, wash hands often, avoid sharing towels and cutlery, ventilate the room, and keep some distance from babies, pregnant people, older people, and anyone with a weakened immune system.

7. Support yourself safely while you are waiting for advice. Sip fluids regularly, rest, avoid alcohol and heavy exercise, and use paracetamol for fever or pain if you can take it safely.

8. If you are told to attend in person, take your travel timeline, medicines list, travel-clinic paperwork, and any malaria tablet packaging. On arrival, tell reception or triage before sitting down that you have fever after travel.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out which infection it is right now.
- You do not need to decide whether the trip caused it.
- You do not need to deep-clean luggage or your whole home.
- You do not need to contact airlines, hotels, insurers, or employers before getting medical advice.
- You do not need to decide when to return to work or school until you have been advised.

## Important reassurance
Fever after travel feels frightening because the possibilities are unfamiliar. The useful next step is simple: get urgent advice, say “fever after travel,” and make sure malaria risk is not missed.

## Scope note
This guide covers first steps only for the first hours after a sudden high fever after travel. Testing, diagnosis, treatment, isolation advice, and follow-up should come from NHS or urgent-care clinicians.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, diagnosis, or a substitute for urgent professional assessment. If you feel rapidly worse, develop severe symptoms, or cannot stay safely at home, call 999 or go to A&E.
