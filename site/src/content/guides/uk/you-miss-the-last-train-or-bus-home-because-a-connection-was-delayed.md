# What to do if… you miss the last train or bus home because a connection was delayed (UK)

## Short answer
Move to the safest staffed or well-lit place you can, then ask railway staff or the train operator for safe onward travel tonight. Focus on safety and a clear plan before refunds, compensation, or complaints.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave the station for a dark or isolated place if you can stay near staff, other passengers, CCTV, a ticket hall, or a Help Point.
- Do not accept lifts from strangers or get into an unbooked or unlicensed car.
- Do not wait in an unsafe place just to avoid paying for transport.
- Do not assume you are stuck because the delay was described as outside the operator’s control.
- Do not get pulled into an argument; repeat the practical request: “I missed the last service because my connection was delayed. What can you do to help me get to my destination safely tonight?”
- Do not throw away your ticket, booking confirmation, screenshots, or receipts.

## What to do now
1. Move to the safest available place and stay visible. Use the staffed ticket office, gateline, concourse, waiting room, Help Point, or a well-lit area near other people.

2. Speak to railway staff as soon as you can. If you are still on the delayed train, ask the guard, conductor, or onboard staff before you get off. Say: “My connection was delayed and I have missed the last service to my destination. I need safe onward travel tonight.”

3. Ask staff to confirm your next valid way to continue the journey. If you have one ticket, one booking, or an itinerary showing the connection, say so and ask them to confirm the next available train, ticket acceptance, or re-routing. If you used separate tickets, still explain that the delay caused the missed connection and ask what help is available.

4. Ask for a clear option in this order:
   - the next available service, including ticket acceptance on another operator if needed
   - a rail replacement bus or coach if one is running
   - a taxi or other alternative transport arranged by the operator, if available and appropriate
   - overnight accommodation if there is no safe or practical way to continue until morning

5. If there are no staff nearby, contact the train operator or National Rail Enquiries and state the same facts: delayed connection, missed last service, current station, destination, ticket type, and whether you feel unsafe waiting.

6. If you feel unsafe, treat that as the priority. In immediate danger, call 999. For non-emergency railway police issues, text British Transport Police on 61016 or call 0800 40 50 40.

7. Save simple evidence now. Take screenshots or photos of the departure board, journey planner, delay messages, cancellation messages, your ticket or booking, and the time you missed the connection. Note who you spoke to, what they said, and the time.

8. If you have to pay to get somewhere safe, keep it reasonable and easy to explain. Use a licensed taxi or reputable booked car, avoid premium options where possible, keep itemised receipts, and write one sentence explaining why you could not safely wait.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to complain or escalate the issue.
- You do not need to calculate compensation or reimbursement tonight.
- You do not need to know the exact rule for every ticket type before asking for help.
- You can make any Delay Repay, refund, complaint, or expense claim later, once you are safe and rested.

## Important reassurance
This is a recognised disruption situation, and it is reasonable to ask for help completing the journey safely. Keep the next hour simple: stay somewhere safer, contact the railway, get a clear plan, and keep evidence.

## Scope note
These are first steps for being stranded after a delayed connection makes you miss the last service home. Later decisions about compensation, refunds, complaints, or disputed expenses may need the train operator, retailer, Rail Ombudsman, or other specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Policies, staff availability, and the help available can vary by operator, station, ticket, and circumstances.
