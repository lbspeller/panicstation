# What to do if… your vehicle is blocked in and you cannot drive out (UK)

## Short answer
Do not force your way out or move the other vehicle yourself. Get to a safe pause, try the fastest safe way to find the driver through staff, security, reception, neighbours, or nearby premises, and use 999 only for immediate danger; otherwise use the site manager, local council/local authority, or police non-emergency route depending on where the vehicle is and what risk it is causing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not squeeze through, mount kerbs, push the other vehicle, or try to “make space”.
- Do not threaten the driver, block them in, damage the vehicle, or leave an angry note.
- Do not clamp, tow, immobilise, or otherwise restrict the other vehicle yourself.
- Do not pay an unknown person who offers to “sort it” at the scene.
- Do not leave your full name, home address, or other unnecessary personal details on a note.
- Do not call 999 unless there is immediate danger, urgent medical need, fire risk, violence, or emergency access is being blocked.

## What to do now
1. **Move yourself to a safer pause.**
   - Stay in a public, well-lit place if you can.
   - If you feel unsafe, go back into the shop, workplace, reception area, or other staffed building and ask staff or security to stay involved.

2. **Check whether this is an emergency.**
   - Call 999 if someone is in immediate danger, emergency access is blocked, there is violence or a threat of violence, or there is an urgent medical or fire risk.
   - If it is not an immediate emergency, keep 999 clear and use the steps below.

3. **Record the situation before it changes.**
   - Take clear photos of your vehicle, the blocking vehicle, number plate, position of both vehicles, bay markings, signs, entrance or exit, and wider location.
   - Note the time, exact location, car park name or street, and anything the driver has left on display that may help staff identify them.

4. **Try the quickest safe way to find the driver.**
   - In a supermarket, retail park, hospital, station, leisure venue, or similar site, ask customer services or security to make an announcement using the vehicle details.
   - At work, in a block of flats, or on managed land, contact reception, concierge, facilities, security, the building manager, or the managing agent.
   - On a residential street, ask nearby neighbours only if you feel safe doing so; otherwise skip this and use the council or police non-emergency route.

5. **Use the right reporting route for the place.**
   - **Private car park, workplace site, residential parking area, or other managed private land:** ask the landowner, site manager, security team, managing agent, or parking operator to contact the driver and log the incident.
   - **Public road, dropped kerb, pavement, crossing, junction, marked bay, or other highway location:** contact the local council or local authority parking enforcement team if it appears to be a parking contravention.
   - **Dangerous obstruction or you are prevented from leaving and cannot resolve it through staff, neighbours, or the council route:** contact your local police force online or by calling 101 and explain that your vehicle is blocked in and cannot be driven out.

6. **Give only the details needed.**
   - Provide the location, vehicle registration, make, model, colour, how it is blocking you, whether anyone is in danger, and whether you have already tried staff, security, neighbours, or the council.
   - Keep your own contact details limited to what the official route, site manager, or police force needs to respond.

7. **Make a practical fallback plan without escalating the scene.**
   - If you need to leave before the vehicle is moved, arrange another safe way to travel.
   - Send a simple update to anyone waiting for you: “My vehicle is blocked in. I’m safe and I’m dealing with it through the site, council, or police non-emergency route.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, claim costs, contact a solicitor, or pursue the driver.
- You do not need to argue about fault with the driver if they return.
- You do not need to contact your insurer unless there is damage or a collision.
- You do not need to solve repeat parking problems tonight; a log and formal complaint can wait until you are away from the immediate situation.

## Important reassurance
Being blocked in can feel trapping and infuriating, but slowing the situation down usually protects you. Photos, calm reporting, and using the site, council, or police non-emergency route are more likely to help than confrontation or trying to move the other vehicle yourself.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repeated obstruction, complaints, costs, damage, or legal options may need specialist help from the site operator, council, police, insurer, Citizens Advice, or a solicitor.

## Important note
This is general information for immediate first actions, not legal, insurance, transport, or professional advice. Parking and obstruction responsibility can vary by location, type of land, and local enforcement arrangements, so check the official route for the place where the vehicle is blocked.
