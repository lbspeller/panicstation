# What to do if… your webcam light turns on unexpectedly (UK)

## Short answer
Cover the camera lens, close anything that could be using the camera, and take the device offline while you check what triggered it.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume it is definitely hacked and start deleting things in a panic; you may lose useful clues and still not fix the cause.
- Don’t keep logging into important accounts on that device, such as email, banking, work tools, or admin accounts, until you are more confident it is safe.
- Don’t install a “cleanup”, “driver”, or “security” tool from a pop-up, advert, unsolicited message, or unknown website.
- Don’t leave the camera uncovered to see what happens.
- Don’t ignore repeated, unexplained camera activation, especially if it happens when no video app, browser tab, or camera feature is open.

## What to do now
1. **Block the camera immediately.** Slide a webcam cover over it, close a built-in shutter if there is one, or tape a small piece of paper over the lens.

2. **Take the device offline for the first check.** Turn off Wi-Fi, unplug Ethernet, and turn off mobile data or tethering if the device uses it. Keep the lens covered.

3. **Close likely camera sources.**
   - Quit video meeting apps such as Teams, Zoom, Google Meet, FaceTime, or Skype.
   - Close messaging apps with video features.
   - Close browser tabs that could use the camera, such as meeting rooms, online proctoring pages, recording tools, chat sites, or web apps.
   - If the light stays on after closing them, restart the device while keeping the lens covered.

4. **Write down a quick record.** Note the time, what apps and browser tabs were open, whether you were in or near a video call, and anything installed or updated recently, including browser extensions.

5. **Check camera permissions.**
   - **Windows:** Start → Settings → **Privacy & security** → **Camera**. Turn off camera access for apps you do not recognise or do not need. You can also temporarily turn off **Let desktop apps access your camera**, but this may affect some legitimate apps.
   - **macOS:** Apple menu → System Settings → **Privacy & Security** → **Camera**. Turn off access for any app you do not recognise or do not want using the camera.
   - **Browser:** Open the browser’s settings for site permissions, camera, or privacy, then remove camera permission from any site you do not recognise or no longer need.

6. **Look for the specific app using the camera.**
   - On Windows, check the camera privacy page for recent camera activity if it is shown.
   - On macOS, quit all apps that can use the camera; the green light should normally turn off when all camera-using apps are closed.
   - If an app name looks unfamiliar, do not open it again to test it. Leave it closed and continue with the checks below.

7. **Run trusted security checks.**
   - Use the device’s built-in security tools or antivirus you already trust.
   - Update the security tool from its official update route, then run a full scan.
   - Do not use scanner links from pop-ups, emails, adverts, or messages.
   - If the scan finds malware, follow the security tool’s advice and consider using NCSC recovery guidance before logging back into important accounts on that device.

8. **If this is a work, school, or managed device, report it now.** Contact the IT helpdesk or security team and say: “The webcam light turned on unexpectedly; the camera is covered; the device has been taken offline; the time and open apps are noted.”

9. **Escalate if you think this is targeted access, blackmail, fraud, or a crime.**
   - If you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, you can report cyber crime or fraud through Report Fraud.
   - If you are in Scotland, contact Police Scotland on 101 for non-emergency reporting.
   - If someone is in immediate danger, or a serious offence is happening now, call 999.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it was malware, a browser permission, a meeting app, or a normal background process.
- You do not need to factory-reset the device immediately.
- You do not need to confront anyone, post about it online, or keep testing the camera.
- You do not need to change every password in the first few minutes unless you have already found signs of account compromise; focus first on covering the lens, taking the device offline, and checking permissions.

## Important reassurance
A webcam light can come on for ordinary reasons, such as a meeting app opening, a browser tab requesting access, or a permission you forgot you granted. It is still sensible to treat it carefully: covering the lens, closing camera apps, checking permissions, and scanning the device are calm protective steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stop possible camera access, preserve useful clues, and avoid rushed mistakes. If it keeps happening, if malware is found, or if you think someone is targeting you, later decisions may need specialist IT, workplace security, or police support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, technical, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe, are being threatened or blackmailed, or your employer requires a specific incident process, prioritise immediate safety and follow the appropriate official or organisational reporting route.
