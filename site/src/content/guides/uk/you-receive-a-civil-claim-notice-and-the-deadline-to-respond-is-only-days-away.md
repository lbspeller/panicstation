# What to do if… you receive a civil claim notice and the deadline to respond is only days away (UK)

## Short answer
File an **Acknowledgment of Service** (AoS) immediately to extend your response window from 14 to 28 days. This prevents the claimant from applying for a default judgment while you prepare your defense or seek advice.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore the notice because the deadline is close; a late response is better than none.
- Don't assume that speaking to the claimant on the phone pauses the legal deadline.
- Don't send your completed Admission form (N9A) to the court if it is a specified money claim; it must go to the claimant.
- Don't miss the deadline while waiting for a return call from a solicitor or advice agency.
- Don't dispose of the envelope; the postmark may be needed to prove the date of service.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the claim type and response method immediately.**  
   Check the Claim Form (N1) to see if it was issued via Money Claim Online (8-digit number), Online Civil Money Claims (starts with 'MC'), or by post.
   - For online claims, log in to the specified portal using the claim number and security code provided.
   - For paper claims, locate the "Response Pack" (Forms N9, N9A, N9B, or N9C/D).

2. **Calculate your exact 'Deemed Service' date.**  
   The deadline is calculated from the date the claim is "served," not the date it was written.
   - For post, service is deemed to occur on the second business day after the court posted it.
   - Record this date as "Day 0."

3. **File an Acknowledgment of Service (Form N9) to gain 14 extra days.**  
   If you intend to defend the claim or need more time to calculate what you owe, submit the Acknowledgment of Service within 14 days of deemed service.
   - This formally extends the deadline to file a full Defence to 28 days from the date of service.
   - You can file this online via the portal or by posting Form N9 to the Civil National Business Centre (CNBC).

4. **Select the correct response form based on your position.**  
   - If you agree you owe the full amount (Specified): Use Form N9A.
   - If you dispute the claim (Specified): Use Form N9B.
   - If the claim is for an "Unspecified" amount (e.g., damages): Use Form N9C (Admission) or N9D (Defence).

5. **Send the completed forms to the correct recipient.**  
   - **Admission (N9A):** Send this directly to the claimant or their solicitor at the "Address for Documents" on the N1 form.
   - **Defence (N9B/D):** Send this to the court (CNBC or the address on the portal).
   - **Acknowledgment of Service (N9):** Send this to the court.

6. **Request a further 28-day extension from the claimant if necessary.**  
   Under Civil Procedure Rule 15.5, you and the claimant can agree in writing to extend the defence deadline by up to an additional 28 days.
   - You must notify the court in writing of any such agreement.
   - This agreement must be made before the current deadline expires.

7. **Contact the Civil National Business Centre for procedural guidance.**  
   If you are unsure where to send a form or if a judgment has already been requested, call 0300 123 1056 (8:30 am to 5:00 pm, Monday to Friday).

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide your full evidence or witness statements today.
- You do not need to have a solicitor appointed to file the initial Acknowledgment of Service.
- You do not need to finalize your repayment plan if you are only filing the Acknowledgment of Service to buy time.

## Important reassurance
Receiving court papers is a procedural event, not an immediate conviction or debt collection. By filing the Acknowledgment of Service, you take control of the timeline and prevent an automatic "Default Judgment," which is the primary risk when deadlines are close.

## Scope note
This guide covers the first-step stabilization of a County Court civil money claim response in England and Wales. It does not cover High Court proceedings, family court, or criminal matters.

## Important note
This information is based on the Civil Procedure Rules (CPR) and 2026 guidance. Legal deadlines are strict; if you miss a deadline, the claimant can seek a CCJ against you immediately. For specific legal advice, contact a qualified solicitor or a free advice service like Citizens Advice or National Debtline.
