# What to do if… a co-parent has died and you need to manage an immediate child handover (UK)

## Short answer
Make the next handover safe before trying to solve the whole arrangement. Keep the child in a known safe place, pause informal changes, and confirm who is allowed to collect while you check parental responsibility, any court order, and school or childcare pickup instructions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the old routine still works exactly as before.
- Do not hand the child to a relative, friend, or new collector just because they are upset or insistent.
- Do not decide long-term living arrangements by text, argument, or pressure at the door.
- Do not involve the child in adult conflict or ask the child to choose sides.
- Do not post details about the death, the child’s location, or handover plans on social media.
- Do not ignore an existing court order, but do not guess what it means if the death has made the next handover unclear.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the next 24 hours only.**  
   Record where the child is now, where they are sleeping tonight, who is physically with them, the next school or childcare run, and the next planned handover time.

2. **Find the most recent paperwork.**  
   Look for any Child Arrangements Order or equivalent child contact, residence, parental responsibility, or guardianship order. Also check any written agreement, school pickup list, birth certificate details, parental responsibility paperwork, or solicitor letter. Photograph or scan what you find so the adults and any adviser are working from the same document.

3. **Treat authority as unconfirmed until it is clear.**  
   If you are not sure who has parental responsibility, who the order names, or whether a guardian named in a will can act now, do not guess. UK family law and court routes vary, and a will or informal family statement should not be treated as controlling for an immediate handover without urgent legal advice.

4. **Send one calm logistics message to the surviving parent, person with parental responsibility, or their representative if known.**  
   Keep it short: say the child is safe, acknowledge the death, state the next handover or school-run issue, propose a temporary plan for the next day or two, and ask for a copy of any current order or pickup authority if you do not have it.

5. **Update school, nursery, or childcare collection instructions today.**  
   Ask them to check their current authorised-collector list, record that arrangements are temporarily unclear because of a bereavement, require ID from any unfamiliar adult, and call the usual safeguarding lead if someone not on the list tries to collect.

6. **Use the lowest-conflict temporary plan for any handover due within hours.**  
   This may mean keeping the child with the current safe caregiver until the documented position is checked, arranging collection only by someone already authorised, or moving communication to email so there is a clear record. Do not use extended family as go-betweens unless everyone with authority agrees and the school or childcare setting can safely follow it.

7. **Act first if there is an immediate safety risk.**  
   If the child is in immediate danger, call 999. If there is an urgent concern but no immediate danger, contact 101 or the local authority children’s services team for safeguarding guidance.

8. **Get urgent family-law help if adults disagree or an order cannot safely be followed.**  
   Ask a family-law solicitor or an official legal advice service what urgent options exist in your part of the UK. If the matter reaches court, safeguarding checks or child-focused enquiries may be part of the process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the child’s long-term living pattern today.
- You do not need to resolve the estate, the will, inheritance issues, or longer-term guardianship immediately.
- You do not need to write a perfect parenting plan before the next school run.
- You do not need to prove every legal point before taking sensible steps to keep the child safe and collected only by authorised adults.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for handovers to become confused after a death, especially where grief, fear, and previous conflict are all present. A short written stopgap, clear pickup controls, and calm factual messages can protect the child while the legal position is checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilise the next handover, reduce conflict, and avoid unsafe or irreversible decisions. Later decisions may need specialist family-law advice, court guidance, mediation where safe and appropriate, or support from children’s services.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Family situations vary, and urgent child arrangements should be checked with an appropriate professional or official service where there is disagreement, uncertainty, or risk.
