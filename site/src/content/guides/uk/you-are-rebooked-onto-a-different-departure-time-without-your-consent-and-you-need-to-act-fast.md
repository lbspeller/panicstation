# What to do if… you are rebooked onto a different departure time without your consent and you need to act fast (UK)

## Short answer
Do not click any "accept" or "confirm" buttons in the app or email; instead, immediately contact the operator to state you have not consented to the change and require either reinstatement of the original time, a workable alternative, or a full refund.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click "Accept", "Confirm", or "Check-in" on the new itinerary if the time is unworkable, as this may be treated as voluntary acceptance of the change.
- Do not cancel the booking yourself via the app or website unless instructed by staff to do so as part of a refund process, as standard cancellation fees may apply.
- Do not book a replacement ticket with a different company before securing evidence that the original operator cannot or will not provide a workable alternative.
- Do not rely on verbal assurances from station or airport staff; always request a written confirmation or a case reference number for any promises made.
- Do not assume a "schedule change" is different from a "cancellation" regarding your rights; significant time moves often trigger the same legal protections.

## What to do now
1. **Capture evidence of the original and new departure times immediately.**
   - Screenshot your original booking confirmation showing the reference number, price, and scheduled time.
   - Screenshot the notification of the change and the new itinerary provided by the operator.
   - Save any email or SMS that states the change was made by the operator rather than by your request.

2. **Verify if the change meets the legal threshold for a "cancellation" or significant disruption.**
   - For flights (UK261), a departure moved more than 1 hour earlier or an arrival moved more than 2 hours later is generally treated as a cancellation.
   - For trains (National Rail), any change that makes your ticket unusable for your intended journey allows for a full refund or rebooking.
   - For ferries, a delay or change of more than 90 minutes triggers rights to care, assistance, and rerouting.

3. **Contact the operator via their fastest live channel and state your non-consent.**
   - Use live chat, social media messaging, or the emergency phone line rather than email for immediate departures.
   - Use these exact words: "I have been rebooked onto a different departure time without my consent. I have **not accepted** this change. I require you to **reinstate my original departure** or **provide a workable alternative** for today. If you cannot, I require a **full refund to my original payment method**."

4. **Request a specific alternative or a "duty of care" if you are at the terminal.**
   - Go to the staffed service desk and ask for a supervisor if the first agent cannot override the system.
   - If the operator cannot move you back to your original time, ask them to book you onto the next available service, even if it is with a different operator (standard for flights and rail during major disruption).
   - If you are delayed at the airport or port, ask for vouchers for food, drink, and communication.

5. **Adhere to the 2026 National Rail refund deadlines if you choose not to travel.**
   - From 1 April 2026, for Anytime or Off-Peak tickets, you must request a refund by **23:59 on the day before the ticket becomes valid**.
   - If the service is cancelled or significantly rescheduled on the day of travel, you remain entitled to a refund even after this deadline, but you must keep evidence of the disruption.
   - Contact the original retailer (e.g., Trainline, operator app) rather than the station staff to process the refund.

6. **Create a timestamped written record of the interaction.**
   - Note the name of any staff member you speak to and the time of the call or chat.
   - Send a brief follow-up message through the operator's official contact form or email stating that you are acting under protest because the new time is unworkable.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a formal compensation claim (e.g., for "Delay Repay" or UK261 fixed sums) while still at the terminal; focus on getting rebooked or refunded first.
- You do not need to argue about whether the change was "extraordinary" or "within the operator's control" in the first few minutes; these are legal arguments for later.
- You do not need to decide right now whether to accept vouchers instead of a cash refund; you are legally entitled to cash for involuntary changes.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel pressured to click "confirm" when a notification appears on your phone. However, the travel operator is legally required to provide you with options when they change your departure time involuntarily. Pausing to secure proof and state your requirements is the most effective way to protect your rights.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for involuntary rebooking in the UK. It does not cover long-term legal claims, insurance disputes, or international travel outside UK/EU jurisdictions.

## Important note
Travel rights depend on specific ticket conditions, the "Conditions of Carriage," and the nature of the transport (air, rail, or sea). While UK law provides baseline protections for involuntary changes, operators may have different internal procedures for rebooking. Always prioritize obtaining written evidence of any changes made by the operator.
