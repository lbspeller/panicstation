# What to do if… you receive an urgent official request to update your address and are unsure if it is real (UK)

## Short answer
Pause and verify the request using a trusted route, such as GOV.UK or the organisation’s official switchboard. Then respond only through that official route, giving the minimum needed: usually your current address or a request for exactly what they need.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, scan QR codes, or call numbers printed in the letter, text, or email until you have independently checked the organisation.
- Don’t share scans or photos of passports, bank cards, or a full set of identity documents just because the message sounds urgent.
- Don’t ignore something that might be from a court, police, HMRC, DVLA, probation, prison, fines office, or enforcement body because it feels stressful.
- Don’t pay address-update fees to random third parties.
- Don’t guess which address they have on file and hope it sorts itself out.

## What to do now
1. **Put the request in front of you without replying yet.** Note the sender name, reference number, what address they want updated, what documents they ask for, and any deadline.
2. **Check whether the request is about you or misdelivered.**
   - If it is clearly for someone else at your address, keep the envelope unopened, cross out the address, write “Not known at this address” on it, and put it back in a post box.
   - If it might be for you, such as your name, your old address, a vehicle you own, or a reference you recognise, treat it as potentially real and continue.
3. **Verify it using an independent route, not the message.**
   - For a DVLA driving licence address change, use GOV.UK to reach the DVLA service.
   - For DVLA vehicle records, use GOV.UK to check which vehicle documents may need updating.
   - For personal HMRC address details, use GOV.UK’s HMRC change-address service.
   - For anything claiming “court”, “fine”, “warrant”, “bailiffs”, “police”, “probation”, “licence”, or “prison”, look up the organisation’s official contact details yourself and quote the reference number.
4. **If you confirm it is genuine, respond with the minimum needed today.**
   - Give your current address only through the official channel you found independently.
   - If documents are requested, ask exactly which documents are acceptable and whether you can cover non-essential information, such as account numbers, where they only need a name and address match.
5. **If you cannot quickly verify it, treat it as suspicious while still containing the risk.**
   - Do not use the original link, QR code, payment page, or phone number.
   - Forward suspicious emails to report@phishing.gov.uk.
   - Forward suspicious texts to 7726.
   - If you think you have shared details, lost money, or been targeted for fraud, report it through Report Fraud if you are in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland. If you are in Scotland and have lost money, report it to Police Scotland.
6. **Reduce the chance of missing important letters next.**
   - If you drive or keep a vehicle, check whether your DVLA driving licence, vehicle log book, vehicle tax Direct Debit, or other DVLA records need updating.
   - If HMRC may contact you, update your personal HMRC address through GOV.UK.
   - If you have moved, update the electoral register by registering again with your new address.
   - Consider Royal Mail Redirection if important post may still be going to an old address.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out every possible consequence right now.
- You do not need to send multiple identity documents today unless an official channel confirms they are required.
- You do not need to write a long explanation or argue the whole situation today. First verify the sender, then update the address in the right place.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to freeze when something looks official and urgent. The safest first move is not to panic-reply and not to ignore it: check the sender through an official route, then take one small address-update step if it is genuine.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the request is tied to an active case, court deadline, bail, licence condition, probation, prison contact, immigration status, safeguarding, debt enforcement, or a fraud report, later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, immigration, fraud, or professional advice. Processes and consequences depend on which body is contacting you and why, so use official contact routes and ask what deadline applies to you.
