# What to do if… you receive an unsolicited meet me now message from an unfamiliar or spoofed account (UK)

## Short answer
Do not go to meet them. Stay where you are, or move to a safer public place, and verify the sender using a trusted channel you already know.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to the location “just to check” or “to get it over with”, especially alone.
- Do not click links, open attachments, scan QR codes, install apps, or share login codes they send “to prove who they are”.
- Do not share your live location, address, workplace, routine, travel plans, or who you are with.
- Do not get pulled into arguing, explaining yourself, or proving anything.
- Do not post identifying screenshots publicly. Save them privately in case you need to show police, the platform, or someone helping you.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safety buffer.** Stay where you are if it is safe, or move to a busier public place. If you were about to leave, pause and do not go to the suggested meeting place.
2. **Treat the urgency as a warning sign.** A “meet me now” message from a spoofed, new, or unfamiliar account is enough to treat it as unverified until proven otherwise.
3. **Verify through a channel you already trust.** If the message claims to be someone you know, contact that person using a saved number, an existing chat thread you have used before, or another known method. Ask: “Did you just message me from a new account asking me to meet right now?”
4. **Do not confirm useful details to the sender.** Do not tell them where you are, whether you are alone, whether you are leaving, or what route you might take.
5. **Use police routes if safety is at risk.**
   - If a crime is in progress, someone is in immediate danger, or you feel in immediate danger, call **999**.
   - If it feels threatening, targeted, stalking-like, or harassing but is not an emergency, call **101** or use your local police non-emergency reporting route.
6. **If money, hacking, or sensitive details are involved, use the fraud route as well.**
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report cyber crime or fraud to **Report Fraud**.
   - In Scotland, report cybercrime to **Police Scotland** by calling **101**, or **999** in an emergency.
7. **Preserve evidence without engaging.** Screenshot the message, username, profile page, phone number if shown, and any links. Note the time and date. Keep the thread intact and do not forward it widely.
8. **Report the contact through the relevant channel, then mute or block.**
   - For a suspicious text message, forward it to **7726** if available through your provider.
   - For a suspicious email, forward it to **report@phishing.gov.uk**.
   - For an app or social media account, use the in-app report option for impersonation, harassment, scam, or suspicious contact.
9. **Check immediate privacy settings.** Turn off live location sharing you do not fully trust, including phone-level sharing and app-level sharing. If you clicked a link, shared a code, or gave sensitive information, change the affected password and turn on two-step verification where available.
10. **Tell one trusted person.** Say that you received a suspicious “meet me now” message, that you are not meeting anyone, and that you would like a simple check-in.

## What can wait
- Working out who it “really” was.
- Trying to catch them out, test them, or keep the conversation going.
- Deciding whether to make a wider report beyond the immediate safety, platform, police, or fraud routes above.
- Explaining yourself to the sender.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaken or unsure is normal. Urgent messages can be designed to make you act before you think, and pausing, verifying, and refusing to meet is a sensible first response.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes and hours only. If the contact continues, escalates, or feels targeted, later decisions may need specialist help from police, platform support, cybercrime support, or safeguarding services.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe, prioritise immediate safety and official help.
