# What to do if… your sent folder shows emails you do not recognise sent to many recipients (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the email account as compromised until you have checked it. First regain control of the account, remove forwarding or rules you did not create, change the password, and sign out other sessions.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t reply from the affected account to the suspicious sent emails; use another channel if you need to warn someone.
- Don’t click links, open attachments, or use “unsubscribe” links in the suspicious emails or related bounce-back messages.
- Don’t only change the password and stop; attackers can sometimes stay in through forwarding, mailbox rules, connected apps, or active sessions.
- Don’t send sensitive documents, codes, or passwords from the account to test it.
- Don’t delete everything in panic; keep enough details, such as dates, subjects, and recipients, in case your provider, employer, bank, or a fraud report needs them.

## What to do now
1. **Use the provider’s recovery or security page if you can’t trust the login.** Go directly to your email provider’s official website or app, not through a link in a message. If you cannot log in, start the provider’s account recovery process.
2. **Check forwarding and mailbox rules before relying on the account again.**
   - Remove any forwarding address you do not recognise.
   - Delete any rule or filter you did not create, especially one that forwards, deletes, hides, or moves messages.
   - Check blocked addresses and safe sender lists for anything unfamiliar.
3. **Change the email password from a device you trust.** Use a new, unique password that you do not use anywhere else.
4. **Sign out other sessions and connected devices.** Use the account’s security settings to sign out other devices, browsers, and apps where possible.
5. **Turn on 2-step verification.** Use the strongest option your provider offers, such as an authenticator app or security key if available.
6. **Secure the recovery routes.**
   - Confirm the recovery email address and phone number are yours.
   - Remove any recovery option you do not recognise.
   - Regenerate backup or recovery codes if the provider offers them.
7. **Remove unfamiliar third-party access.** Check connected apps, app passwords, and “sign in with” access. Revoke anything you do not recognise.
8. **Look for signs that other accounts were targeted.** Search for terms such as “password reset”, “security alert”, “new sign-in”, “forwarding”, and “rule”, plus names of banks, payment services, shopping accounts, cloud storage, and social accounts you use. Change passwords and add 2-step verification on any affected accounts, starting with banking, payments, shopping, and cloud accounts.
9. **Warn the people most likely to be harmed, using a safe channel.** Text or call close contacts, colleagues, or anyone who might act on payment requests, gift card requests, invoices, links, or attachments. Ask them to ignore recent messages from the account unless you confirm by phone.
10. **Report phishing or fraud where relevant.**
    - If you have suspicious emails, forward them to report@phishing.gov.uk.
    - If you lost money, shared financial details, or the account takeover led to fraud, report it to Report Fraud. If you live in Scotland, Report Fraud says to report via 101.
11. **If this is a work, school, or shared organisation account, contact IT or the helpdesk now.** Ask them to check sign-in logs, forwarding, mailbox rules, third-party access, and whether any data or other accounts may be affected.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out who did it right now.
- You do not need to message every contact immediately; prioritise people who might lose money, open files, or act on urgent requests.
- You do not need to wipe devices today unless there are clear signs of malware or your IT/security support tells you to.
- You do not need to decide long-term changes to your email address until the account is stable.

## Important reassurance
Unknown mass-sent emails are a common sign of account compromise, and it does not mean you did anything careless. Regaining control, removing hidden access, and warning the most exposed contacts are the main first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to stabilise the situation and prevent further harm. Later decisions may need help from your email provider, workplace IT, bank, cyber security support, or fraud reporting services.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cyber security, or other professional advice. For urgent account loss, financial loss, workplace systems, or ongoing fraud, use your provider’s official support route, your bank, your organisation’s IT team, or the relevant UK reporting channel.
