# What to do if… you think a friend may be in a sexually coercive situation and they suddenly stop responding (UK)

## Short answer
Assess the immediate risk: if you believe they are in active danger or a crime is occurring, call **999** immediately; if not, initiate a low-profile contact check and be prepared to report a **missing person** or a **concern for safety** based on specific vulnerability factors.

## Do not do these things
- Don't contact the person you suspect is coercive (this can alert them, increase the friend's risk, or cause the friend to be further isolated).
- Don't send multiple, emotionally charged messages (if the phone is monitored, this confirms the friend has outside help, which may escalate control).
- Don't post their photo or "missing" status on social media (this violates their privacy and can alert a perpetrator of your exact concerns).
- Don't attempt to "rescue" them or enter a private property yourself.
- Don't use third-party tracking apps to confront anyone at a specific address.

## What to do now
1. **Document the last known facts immediately.**
   - Write down their full name, appearance, clothes, and **last confirmed location**.
   - Note the exact time they stopped responding and any specific details about the person they were with.
   - Save screenshots of the final messages or any shared location data before it expires.
2. **Send a single, neutral "check-in" message.**
   - Use a pre-arranged code word if you have one.
   - If not, use a "yes / no" question that is easy to answer silently: "**Are you safe to chat? Just send an emoji if you can't talk.**"
   - Avoid asking "where are you?" or "is he hurting you?" as these are dangerous if read by a third party.
3. **Attempt one voice call then stop.**
   - If it goes to voicemail, do not leave a message describing your fears.
   - Say: "**Give me a ring when you're free to catch up.**"
4. **Contact the venue if they are in a public space.**
   - If they are at a bar, club, or hotel, call the front desk or security.
   - Ask if the venue uses the "**Ask for Angela**" protocol.
   - Request that they look out for your friend (provide a description) but do not ask them to "intervene" unless they see an immediate crime.
5. **Call 999 if you suspect an immediate "risk to life or serious harm."**
   - Under 2026 **Right Care, Right Person (RCRP)** rules, UK police will only deploy for welfare checks if there is an immediate threat of death or serious injury, or a crime in progress.
   - When calling, state: "**I am reporting a concern for safety with an immediate risk of sexual violence/coercion.**"
   - Emphasize the specific "vulnerability factors" (e.g., they stated they felt unsafe, the person is known to be dangerous, or they are in a remote location).
6. **Call 101 to report them as a Missing Person if the immediate threat is unclear.**
   - You **do not** have to wait 24 hours to report someone missing in the UK.
   - Tell the operator: "**I want to report a missing person who I believe is in a sexually coercive or exploitative situation.**"
   - This triggers a risk assessment (High, Medium, or Low) based on the coercion you have observed.
7. **Contact a specialist support service for guidance on the next 60 minutes.**
   - Call the **Rape Crisis England & Wales 24/7 Support Line** on **0808 500 2222**.
   - They can help you stay grounded and advise on safe wording for any further messages.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove that a crime has been committed yet.
- You do not need to decide if your friend wants to press charges or involve the police long-term.
- You do not need to notify their entire family or social circle until the risk is assessed.
- You do not need to verify the identity of the person they were with before seeking help.

## Important reassurance
Sudden silence in a high-stakes situation is frightening, and your instinct to check on their safety is a valid protective response. Panic can make it hard to think, but following a structured contact plan is the most effective way to help your friend without unintentionally increasing the danger they are in.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a friend or witness. It focuses on immediate safety and initial police contact. It does not cover forensic evidence preservation, long-term legal options, or therapeutic recovery.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. In any emergency where you believe someone is at risk of immediate harm, always dial **999**. Police response times and deployment criteria may vary by local force area under RCRP protocols.
