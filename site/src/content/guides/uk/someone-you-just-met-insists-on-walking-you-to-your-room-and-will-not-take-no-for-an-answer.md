# What to do if… someone you just met insists on walking you to your room and will not take no for an answer (UK)

## Short answer
Do **not** enter your room or use your key while they are present; immediately move to a staffed or public area like a reception desk or a busy bar and state clearly that you are being followed.

## Do not do these things
- Don't "just let them" reach your door to avoid a scene; the risk of them forcing entry or noting your room number is higher than the social cost of a scene.
- Don't go into confined, unstaffed spaces like lifts, stairwells, or laundry rooms with them.
- Don't unlock your door or show them which room is yours.
- Don't try to "reason" with them or explain why you want to be alone; "no" is a complete statement.
- Don't worry about being rude or "overreacting"; persistent following after a refusal is a criminal offense in the UK as of 2026.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a staffed or high-traffic area immediately.** If you are in a hotel, return to the main lobby or reception; if in student halls, go to the security lodge or a common room with other students present.
2. **Use the "Ask for Angela" protocol if in a licensed venue.** Approach staff at the bar or reception and ask "Is Angela in?"; this is a widely recognised UK safety code that alerts staff you need help leaving a situation discreetly.
3. **Approach a third party and provide a direct instruction.** Say to a staff member or a group of people: "I do not know this person and they are following me to my room after I told them to stop. Please stay with me while I call security."
4. **Utilise the 999 "Silent Solution" if you cannot speak safely.** If the person is too close for you to talk to emergency services, **dial 999 and press 55** when prompted; this alerts the UK police that it is a genuine emergency and you are unable to talk.
5. **Request a professional escort from accommodation staff.** Ask the hotel manager, hall warden, or Senior Resident Assistant (SRA) to **physically escort you** to your room and ensure the other person is removed from your floor or the building.
6. **Name the behavior out loud if in a public space.** Say clearly and loudly: "You are following me. I have said no. I am going to the police/security now." This draws attention and can deter a perpetrator who relies on social compliance.
7. **Contact the 24-hour security line for your specific building.**
   - **Student Halls:** Call the security or "Hall Team" number usually found on the back of your room door or key card.
   - **Hotels:** Use a house phone or your mobile to call the front desk directly.
8. **Record the incident with staff before going to sleep.** Ensure the venue or accommodation provider logs the date, time, and a physical description of the person to prevent them from targeting you or others again.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to press charges or "report" them to the police right this second.
- You do not need to figure out how to "explain" your behavior to mutual acquaintances.
- You do not need to check your DMs or respond to any "apology" texts they might send.
- You can wait until the morning to decide if you want to request a permanent room change.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel "frozen" or to worry about being "dramatic." Persistent following after you have expressed a lack of interest is a significant boundary violation. In the UK, the Protection from Sex-based Harassment in Public Act 2023 (effective April 2026) specifically criminalises intentional harassment or intimidation in public and semi-public spaces like hotels and halls. Your reaction is a valid response to a threat.

## Scope note
This guide focuses on immediate physical separation and safety. It does not cover long-term reporting or the legal intricacies of harassment claims, which may require specialist legal or support services.

## Important note
As of April 1, 2026, sex-based harassment in public spaces—including hospitality venues and common areas of accommodation—is a specific criminal offense in England and Wales. This includes threatening or intimidating invasion of personal space. If you are in immediate danger, always dial 999.
