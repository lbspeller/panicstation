# What to do if… you notice a rash with fever and you are unsure if it could be serious (UK)

## Short answer
If the person has a rash that does not fade under pressure (non-blanching) or appears very unwell with symptoms like confusion or difficulty breathing, call **999** immediately. Otherwise, call **NHS 111** or your GP for urgent same-day advice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait and see if the person seems drowsy, confused, or is deteriorating quickly.
- Do not use ibuprofen if you suspect chickenpox (it can increase the risk of serious skin infections).
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel faint, dizzy, or short of breath.
- Do not walk into a GP surgery or A&E without calling ahead if you suspect measles (to prevent spreading it to vulnerable patients).
- Do not apply new creams or makeup to the area as this can hide the rash's appearance from clinicians.
- Do not ignore a fever in a baby under 3 months old even if there is no rash.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate 999 red flags and call for an ambulance if any are present.**
   - A rash that looks like small red/purple "pin-pricks" or bruises that do not fade when a glass is pressed against them.
   - Blue, grey, pale, or blotchy skin, lips, or tongue (on dark skin, check the palms, soles, or inside the eyelids).
   - Difficulty breathing, gasping, or very fast breathing (in babies, look for "grunting" or the stomach sucking in under the ribs).
   - Severe drowsiness, being hard to wake, or acting confused.
   - A stiff neck, severe headache, or an extreme dislike of bright lights.
2. **Perform the "glass test" on the rash, but do not let this delay calling 999 if the person is very ill.**
   - Press the side of a clear glass firmly against the spots or rash.
   - **If the spots stay visible through the glass:** treat this as a medical emergency and **call 999**.
   - **If the spots fade or disappear:** it could still be serious; continue to monitor for worsening symptoms and re-test if the rash changes.
3. **Contact the correct service if no immediate 999 red flags are present.**
   - **Call your GP surgery** for an urgent same-day appointment if you suspect measles (fever, runny nose, cough, and sore red eyes followed by a rash).
   - **Call NHS 111** (or use 111 online) if you are worried but there are no 999 red flags.
   - **Act urgently** if a baby under 3 months has a temperature of 38°C or higher, or a baby aged 3–6 months has a temperature of 39°C or higher.
4. **Isolate the person while waiting for advice if an infectious disease like measles or chickenpox is suspected.**
   - Keep them away from others, especially pregnant people, infants, and those with weakened immune systems.
   - Check the mouth for small white or greyish-white spots (Koplik spots), which can be an early sign of measles.
5. **Gather vital information for the medical call.**
   - **Note the exact time the fever and rash started** and the highest temperature recorded.
   - **Check for "amber" signs** like cold hands and feet, a high-pitched cry in babies, or a fever that has lasted more than 5 days.
   - **Identify any new medications** started in the last two weeks or any recent travel outside the UK.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific type of rash yourself.
- You do not need to notify school, nursery, or work until you have received a clinical assessment.
- You do not need to monitor the person's temperature every few minutes; once every few hours is sufficient unless they are deteriorating.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when a rash and fever appear together, especially in a child. Choosing to seek urgent medical advice when a rash does not fade or a person looks unwell is a responsible safety action, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage guidance only. It is not a substitute for clinical diagnosis or professional medical advice.

## Important note
Health conditions can escalate rapidly. If your instinct tells you something is seriously wrong, or if a person's condition worsens after initial advice, trust that feeling and seek emergency help immediately by calling **999**.
