# What to do if… you receive a repossession warning on a financed vehicle with a near deadline (UK)

## Short answer
Contact the finance provider immediately to confirm if a Default Notice has been issued and the exact deadline to remedy the breach. If you have paid more than one third of the total price (including deposit), the vehicle is "protected goods" and usually cannot be repossessed without a court order.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore the letter; once a Default Notice deadline passes (usually 14 days), the lender can terminate the agreement and escalate to recovery.
- Don't hide the vehicle in a way that breaches your contract terms, as this can be used as grounds for the lender to seek an immediate court order.
- Don't pay money to third-party "repossession stoppers" or unverified callers; only deal with the lender using the number on their official website or statement.
- Don't sign a "Voluntary Surrender" form under pressure, as this waives your right to a court hearing and the "one third" protection.
- Don't engage in physical or verbal confrontation with recovery agents; record the interaction from a distance if necessary.
- Don't leave essential items in the vehicle, including house keys, medications, child seats, or work tools.

## What to do now
1. **Locate your original agreement and calculate the "one third" figure.**  
   **Check the "Repossession: your rights" box in your contract to find the specific amount that constitutes one third of the total price.** 
   - Add up all payments made to date, including your initial deposit and any part-exchange value.
   - If your total payments equal or exceed this figure, the lender must get a court order to take the car from anywhere.

2. **Verify the status of the "Default Notice" (2 minutes).**  
   **Call the lender's arrears department using the number on your latest statement and ask if a formal Section 87(1) Default Notice has been served.**
   - Confirm the exact date the notice expires.
   - Ask if the account has already been passed to an external recovery or repossession agent.

3. **Establish if the vehicle is on "private land" or "public highway."**  
   **If you have paid less than one third, the lender can technically "snatch back" the vehicle without a court order if it is on a public road.**
   - In England and Wales, they generally still need a court order or your consent to enter a private driveway or garage.
   - In Scotland, a court order is generally required to repossess from any private property, regardless of the amount paid.

4. **Request an "FCA Information Sheet" if it was missing from the notice.**  
   **Lenders are legally required to include a standard Financial Conduct Authority information sheet on arrears/defaults with their warning.**
   - If you did not receive this, inform the lender; it may affect the validity of their notice period.

5. **Ask for a "hold" on recovery action while you seek debt advice.**  
   **Explicitly state: "I am experiencing financial difficulty and intend to seek regulated debt advice; please place a 30-day hold on recovery action."**
   - Most regulated lenders will grant a short hold if you demonstrate active engagement with a service like National Debtline or StepChange.

6. **Apply for the "Debt Respite Scheme" (Breathing Space) or DAS Moratorium.**  
   **Contact a regulated debt adviser today to start a formal Breathing Space application.**
   - In England and Wales: This provides 60 days of legal protection from enforcement and interest.
   - In Scotland: Ask about a "Statutory Moratorium" via the Debt Arrangement Scheme (DAS) which provides similar protection for 6 months.

7. **Record the "recovery agent" details if they are already on-site.**  
   **If an agent is at your door, stay inside, speak through the door or window, and ask for their name, company, and proof of court order.**
   - Without a court order, you can simply say: "I do not consent to the repossession or to you entering my property. Please leave."

## What can wait
- You do not need to figure out how you will commute or get to work long-term today.
- You do not need to decide whether to "Voluntary Terminate" the agreement (the 50% rule) until the immediate threat of repossession is paused.
- You do not need to provide a full monthly budget to the lender on the first phone call; "I am seeking professional advice" is a valid initial response.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a repossession notice is a high-stress event, but the UK has strict consumer protections under the Consumer Credit Act 1974. If you have paid a significant portion of the loan or if the car is on your driveway, the lender cannot simply take it without following a specific legal process. Taking the first step to call them or a debt adviser often stops the clock.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization for urgent vehicle repossession warnings in the UK under regulated Hire Purchase (HP), Conditional Sale (CS), and Personal Contract Purchase (PCP) agreements. It does not cover logbook loans or business-use-only contracts.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Jurisdictional rules regarding "snatch back" and private land vary between England/Wales and Scotland. If you are facing immediate repossession, contact a free, regulated debt advice service such as National Debtline, StepChange, or Citizens Advice immediately.
