# What to do if… police ask to search your car and you are unsure what your options are (UK)

## Short answer
State "I do not consent to a search" if you are being asked for permission, but do not physically resist if the officer identifies a specific legal power they are using to proceed. Ask for the grounds, the object of the search, and the officer’s details before the search begins.

## Do not do these things
- Do not physically block the officer from accessing the doors, boot, or engine bay.
- Do not reach into the glove box, center console, or under seats without explaining your movement first.
- Do not give consent in Scotland, as "voluntary" searches are no longer legal there; officers must have a statutory power.
- Do not argue about the validity of the "reasonable grounds" at the roadside.
- Do not hand over items "voluntarily" to speed up the process if you are unsure of your rights.

## What to do now
1. **Pull over safely and turn off the engine.** Keep your hands visible on the steering wheel and wait for the officer to approach the window.
2. **Clarify the nature of the stop.** Ask: "Am I being detained for a search, or is this a routine traffic stop?"
   - Under Section 163 of the Road Traffic Act, you must stop when signaled by a police officer in uniform, but this power does not automatically include a search.
3. **Determine if the search is consensual or mandatory.** Ask: "Are you asking for my permission, or are you searching me under a legal power?"
   - If in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland and they are asking for permission, you may say: "I do not consent to this search." 
   - If in Scotland, police cannot perform consensual searches; they must state a specific statutory power (such as the Misuse of Drugs Act).
4. **Request the mandatory GOWISELY information.** Before any search begins, the officer must provide:
   - **G**rounds for the search (why they suspect you).
   - **O**bject of the search (what they are looking for).
   - **W**arrant card (if they are not in uniform).
   - **I**dentity (the officer’s name or collar number).
   - **S**tation (where they are based).
   - **E**ntitlement to a record of the search.
   - **L**egal power being used.
   - **Y**ou are detained for the purpose of a search.
5. **Observe the search from a safe distance.** You and your passengers have the right to film the search as long as you do not obstruct the officers or get in their physical way.
6. **Request a search record immediately.** Ask for a written record or a receipt; if it is not possible to provide one at the roadside, they must tell you which station you can visit to collect it.
   - You generally have 3 months to request a copy of the search record from a police station (England and Wales).
7. **Document the encounter independently.** As soon as the stop concludes, write down the time, exact location, police vehicle registration, and the sequence of events while they are fresh in your memory.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to lodge a formal complaint while still at the roadside.
- You do not need to justify why you did not consent to the search if it was a request.
- You do not need to explain your full itinerary or the contents of your car beyond what is required by the specific search power used.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard reaction to feel a sense of pressure or confusion during a police interaction. Stating that you do not consent to a search is a legal choice and does not constitute an admission of guilt or a crime in itself. Understanding the difference between a request and a legal requirement helps provide a clear framework for your actions.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and rights-clarification during a roadside stop. It does not cover legal defense strategies if items are seized or if an arrest occurs.

## Important note
Police powers vary between England and Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. In Scotland, the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Act 2016 prohibits consensual searches. In all jurisdictions, if an officer has "reasonable grounds," they can search without your permission. Failure to stop for a uniformed officer is an offense. Use 101 for non-emergency follow-ups or to verify an officer's identity if you are concerned they are not genuine.
