# What to do if… you are asked to arrange a funeral but cannot find a will or instructions (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before making irreversible funeral choices. Try to find any will, funeral plan, or named executor, and only make the minimum arrangements needed to keep the situation calm.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign funeral contracts or pay large deposits until you have checked for a will, a prepaid funeral plan, insurance linked to funeral costs, or someone else who should be leading, such as a willing named executor.
- Do not assume “next of kin” automatically settles the decision if there may be a will, an executor, or a family dispute.
- Do not empty accounts, sell property, or distribute belongings to cover costs before you know who has authority to deal with the estate.
- Do not let pressure force fast choices about burial, cremation, service type, or spending if you are unsure.
- Do not throw papers away while searching. Put anything possibly legal, financial, or funeral-related in one safe place.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a stable paperwork pause.** If you are at a hospital, hospice, care home, or with emergency staff, ask what happens next and whether the death is being referred to a coroner, procurator fiscal, or other relevant authority.
2. **Check whether a funeral can be scheduled yet.** A funeral can usually only take place after the death is registered, unless the death has been reported to a coroner or equivalent authority. Ask what documents are needed before the funeral director, crematorium, or burial authority can proceed.
3. **Do a focused will and instructions search, then stop before you get overwhelmed.**
   - Check obvious places first: folders marked “will”, “solicitor”, “funeral plan”, “insurance”, a home safe, desk drawers, filing cabinets, and recent post.
   - Search email and messages for terms such as “will”, “funeral”, “plan”, “solicitor”, “cremation”, “burial”, and “insurance”.
   - Ask close family or trusted friends one clear question: “Do you know which solicitor or will writing service they used, or where they kept their will?”
   - Call any solicitor, will writing service, bank, or funeral director named in their papers and ask whether they hold a will, funeral instructions, or plan details.
   - Consider an optional National Will Register search if you have no leads.
4. **Check for prepaid funeral arrangements.**
   - Look for plan documents, funeral-plan direct debits, insurance specifically linked to funeral costs, or letters from funeral providers.
   - Ask local funeral directors whether they hold a plan or record for the person who died. You can ask this without committing to use them.
5. **Identify who should be giving instructions, and slow down if there is conflict.**
   - If a will is found and names an executor who is willing and able to act, that person will generally be the right person to lead funeral decisions.
   - If there is no will, or it cannot be found yet, a close relative may usually arrange the funeral, but this can become difficult if relatives disagree.
   - If there are several close relatives, ask for a simple written agreement by email or message before anyone commits to burial, cremation, a service, or significant costs.
   - If there is serious disagreement, pause and get legal advice before making irreversible arrangements.
6. **Make only limited, reversible arrangements with a funeral director.**
   - Say: “We cannot locate the will or instructions yet. We need a few days to confirm wishes and who should lead.”
   - Ask what they can do without commitment, such as explaining the likely timeline, collecting information, and giving written price options.
   - Ask them to separate unavoidable costs from optional choices so you do not agree to more than you can afford.
7. **Protect yourself financially.**
   - If you pay anything personally, keep receipts and write down why the payment was needed.
   - Do not pay more than you can afford to be without, even if you expect the estate may reimburse funeral costs later.
   - If money is a concern, ask about a simple funeral and check whether Funeral Expenses Payment, Bereavement Support Payment for an eligible partner, or local support may apply to your situation.
8. **Use the backstop if nobody can arrange or pay.**
   - If there is genuinely no one willing or able to arrange the funeral, contact the local council or relevant public authority and ask what happens locally.
   - This may lead to a simple public-health or equivalent funeral arrangement. It is a backstop to prevent panic decisions, not something you need to decide alone in the moment.
9. **Start only the minimum official steps.**
   - Register the death when you are able and when the required medical or official process allows it.
   - After registration, use Tell Us Once where available, or ask the registrar which organisations must be told separately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the perfect funeral, music, readings, flowers, order of service, guest list, or wake today.
- You do not need to settle every family argument today. The immediate task is to prevent irreversible decisions until wishes and authority are clearer.
- You do not need to deal with probate, confirmation, inheritance, or distributing belongings right now.
- You do not need to notify every bank, insurer, pension provider, utility, or subscription immediately unless there is an urgent risk or payment problem.

## Important reassurance
It is common for wills, funeral plans, and wishes to be hard to find at first. Taking a short pause to check papers, ask the right people, and avoid rushed spending is responsible, not a failure.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about probate, confirmation, estate debts, disputes, funeral costs, or who has legal authority may need specialist legal or bereavement support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes can vary across the UK, especially where a coroner, procurator fiscal, registrar, local council, or dispute is involved.
