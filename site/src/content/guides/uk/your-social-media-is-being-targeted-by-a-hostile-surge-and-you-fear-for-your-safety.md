# What to do if… your social media is being targeted by a hostile surge and you fear for your safety (UK)

## Short answer
Stop engaging, restrict access to your accounts, remove location clues, and tell a trusted person what is happening. If you are in immediate danger, call 999; if it is not an emergency but you need police advice or want to report threats or harassment, use 101 or the appropriate online police reporting route.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t argue, “clap back”, or post emotional updates while the surge is active.
- Don’t share where you are, where you are going, or anything that shows a routine, landmark, doorway, window view, car registration, workplace, or school.
- Don’t delete serious threats, doxxing, or abusive messages before saving evidence if you can do that safely.
- Don’t move the conversation to DMs with strangers who say they want to “clear it up”.
- Don’t post threats, dares, callouts, or “bring it on” messages.
- Don’t meet anyone who contacts you through the surge and says they want to “talk” or “sort it out”.
- Don’t dismiss credible threats, doxxing, or someone turning up as “just online”.

## What to do now
1. **Stop replying and make a safer pause.**  
   Put the app down for a moment and decide not to respond while you are stressed. If you are out and feel watched, followed, or exposed, move into a busier place or a staffed building.

2. **Tell one trusted person now.**  
   Message or call a friend, partner, family member, housemate, neighbour, or colleague. Say that hostile online attention is escalating, tell them where you are, and agree a check-in time.

3. **Lock down your social accounts.**  
   Set accounts to private where possible. Restrict DMs, replies, mentions, tagging, comments, duets, stitches, remixes, and reposts to people you know or follow. Remove public contact buttons, public email addresses, phone numbers, and location details from your profile.

4. **Reduce what strangers can connect to real life.**  
   Remove or hide recent posts and stories that show your home, street, commute, car registration, children’s school clues, workplace entrance, regular venues, or live location. Turn off location sharing for social apps and camera uploads. Ask close friends not to tag you, share your location, or defend you publicly.

5. **Secure the accounts that matter most.**  
   Change passwords for your social media and main email if you think anyone may try to get in. Turn on two-step verification where available, especially for your email and the account receiving the hostile attention.

6. **Save the most serious evidence, then take a break.**  
   Make a folder and save screenshots that show usernames, dates, times, links, direct threats, address leaks, impersonation, stalking indicators, and attempts to contact your workplace or family. Prioritise serious content rather than trying to capture every comment.

7. **Report, mute, and block.**  
   Report specific posts or accounts for harassment, threats, doxxing, impersonation, or private information. After reporting, mute or block to reduce incoming contact. If platform reporting is confusing or not working, use Report Harmful Content for UK help with harmful online content reporting routes.

8. **Contact police if there are threats or real-life risk.**  
   Call 999 if you are in immediate danger, someone is outside your home or workplace, or there is a credible imminent threat. Use 101 or online police reporting for non-emergency threats, harassment, stalking, doxxing, malicious messages, or repeated unwanted contact. Be ready to summarise what changed, what was said, whether your address or workplace is involved, and what evidence you have saved.

9. **Put a real-life buffer around the next day.**  
   If your home address may be circulating, keep doors and windows locked, do not buzz in strangers, and consider delaying non-essential outings. If your workplace, school, or regular venue could be targeted, discreetly tell reception, security, a manager, or another responsible person not to confirm your details to callers or visitors.

## What can wait
- Writing a public statement, apology, “final word”, or explainer thread.
- Reading every reply, quote-post, comment, or repost.
- Working out who started it.
- Deciding whether to delete your account, change jobs, move home, or take legal action.
- Debating whether you “deserve” the attention while you are under stress.

## Important reassurance
A sudden surge of hostile attention can make your body react as if danger is close, even before you know how serious it is. You do not have to win the argument or fix the story right now. The priority is to reduce access to you, save serious evidence, and get another person aware of what is happening.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the initial hours or days of hostile online attention that may spill into real life. If threats continue, your address is shared, people contact your workplace or family, or you are followed or repeatedly contacted, you may need specialist help with safety planning, reporting, legal options, or platform escalation.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. If you believe a crime is happening or you are in danger, contact the police using the appropriate emergency or non-emergency route.
