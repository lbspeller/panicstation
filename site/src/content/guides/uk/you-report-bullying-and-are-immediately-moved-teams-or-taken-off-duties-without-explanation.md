# What to do if… you report bullying and are immediately moved teams or taken off duties without explanation (UK)

## Short answer
Ask for the move or duty change, the reason, and whether it is temporary to be confirmed in writing. Keep your bullying report active, preserve evidence, and avoid resigning or signing a role change while you are in shock.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign, walk out, or send an ultimatum email while you feel panicked.
- Do not assume the move means your bullying complaint is closed, accepted, rejected, or sorted.
- Do not rely on a vague verbal explanation as the only record.
- Do not sign anything changing your role, hours, pay, duties, reporting line, or workplace before you understand what it says.
- Do not delete messages, notes, calendar entries, rota records, HR emails, or chat logs.
- Do not confront the person you reported alone or in private.
- Do not post about it on public social media or large work group chats.

## What to do now
1. **Make a short timeline.**  
   Write down when you reported the bullying, who you told, how you reported it, and exactly when you were moved teams or taken off duties. Save rota changes, duty lists, emails, messages, meeting invites, HR ticket numbers, and screenshots showing the change.

2. **Ask for the change to be confirmed in writing.**  
   Send a calm email to your manager or HR asking them to confirm:
   - what has changed, including team, duties, reporting line, location, shifts, access, or workload
   - whether the change is temporary or permanent
   - who authorised it
   - the reason, or as much of the reason as they can share
   - whether your pay, grade, hours, objectives, bonus, appraisal, or performance record are affected
   - whether the change is not disciplinary and will not be treated as a performance issue

3. **Ask for clear working instructions.**  
   Ask what you should do each day until the matter is reviewed, including what tasks you should do, what tasks you must not do, who approves your work, who you report to, and how your work will be assessed.

4. **Confirm that your bullying report is still being handled.**  
   Ask for written acknowledgement of your report and the next step. Ask who is handling it, what process they are using, when they expect to speak to you, and where you can find the bullying, harassment, grievance, or dignity at work policy. If you want it treated as a formal grievance, say that plainly.

5. **Use careful wording if discrimination or harassment may be involved.**  
   If the bullying report involved discrimination or harassment linked to a protected characteristic, say this factually and say you are concerned the team move or duty removal may be connected to that complaint. If it was not about discrimination or harassment, say you are concerned the change may be connected to you raising a bullying complaint and ask for the reason and safeguards.

6. **If your report may also be whistleblowing, flag that separately.**  
   If you reported possible wrongdoing in the public interest, such as safety risks, fraud, regulatory breaches, or legal breaches, ask for it to be logged under your workplace whistleblowing or protected disclosure process. A move, duty removal, or sidelining after a protected disclosure may be relevant to detriment, so keep careful notes of what changed and when.

7. **Bring support into the process.**  
   If a formal grievance meeting is arranged, ask to be accompanied by a trade union representative or a work colleague. If you are in a union, contact your rep and send them the key emails, the timeline, and the written change request.

8. **Protect your records without taking confidential business data.**  
   Keep copies of records you are entitled to keep, such as your complaint email, acknowledgements, meeting invites, your contract or job description, relevant policies, and your own timeline. Keep your timeline somewhere safe that you can access if your work account or device access changes.

9. **Consider a narrow Subject Access Request if you need your personal data.**  
   You can ask for personal data held about you, such as emails or notes that discuss you. Keep the request focused by using dates, teams, names, and keywords. The organisation generally must respond without undue delay and within one month, but this can be extended for complex or multiple requests, and timing can be affected if they need ID or clarification. Some information may be withheld or redacted, including third-party data or legally privileged material.

10. **Get neutral guidance before making a major decision.**  
   Contact Acas for guidance on bullying, grievance handling, discrimination, victimisation, whistleblowing, and when early conciliation might become relevant later. Use the guidance to understand your next step before resigning, agreeing a permanent move, or sending a long complaint email.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to make a tribunal claim.
- Deciding whether to resign.
- Writing a full case file tonight; a short timeline and saved records are enough for now.
- Demanding an apology or confronting anyone.
- Agreeing to mediation, settlement discussions, or a permanent role change before you have support or advice.
- Working out every legal label today.

## Important reassurance
Being moved teams or taken off duties right after reporting bullying can feel like punishment. It can also be a rushed attempt to separate people while the situation is reviewed. Either way, you are allowed to ask what has changed, get the reason recorded, and ask for your complaint to stay in a fair process.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, including grievances, whistleblowing steps, discrimination issues, settlement discussions, resignation, or tribunal action, may need specialist employment advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, medical, or professional advice. Workplace rights depend on your contract, workplace policies, employment status, and what your report was about. If you feel in immediate danger at work, move to a safer place and contact a trusted manager, HR, your union rep, security, or emergency services.
