# What to do if… you have severe constipation with increasing abdominal swelling and nausea (UK)

## Short answer
Treat these symptoms as a medical emergency. Severe constipation combined with a visibly swelling abdomen and nausea often indicates a bowel obstruction or faecal impaction, which requires immediate clinical assessment at an Accident and Emergency (A&E) department or by calling 999.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take any more laxatives, suppositories, or enemas, as these can increase pressure behind a potential blockage and cause further pain or injury.
- Do not attempt to eat or drink large amounts to force a bowel movement.
- Do not take opioid-based painkillers such as codeine or co-codamol, as these significantly slow down the bowel and worsen the underlying issue.
- Do not wait for your GP surgery to open if you are unable to pass wind or are vomiting.
- Do not ignore the symptoms if they seem to "come and go," as bowel obstruction pain often occurs in waves.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency red flags that require an immediate 999 call or A&E visit.** Seek emergency help now if:
   - You are unable to pass any wind (fart) at all.
   - You are vomiting repeatedly or your vomit has a very strong, unpleasant, or faecal-like smell.
   - Your abdomen is significantly distended, feels very hard, or is tender to the touch.
   - You have severe, cramping abdominal pain that is worsening.
   - You feel dizzy, confused, or have a high temperature.
2. **Contact NHS 111 immediately if you do not have the emergency signs above but cannot pass stool.** Tell the operator clearly: "I have severe constipation with increasing abdominal swelling and nausea." Be prepared to provide:
   - The date and time of your last bowel movement.
   - The last time you were able to pass wind.
   - Any other symptoms such as a lack of appetite or a feeling of extreme fullness.
3. **Stop all intake of solid food and limit fluids to small sips only.** If you are feeling nauseated, do not drink large volumes of water, as this may trigger vomiting and lead to aspiration.
4. **Remain in an upright or side-lying position if you feel sick.** This helps protect your airway if you begin to vomit suddenly.
5. **Gather your current medications and a brief medical history.** Clinicians will need to know if you take iron supplements, opioids, or have a history of abdominal surgery, hernias, or inflammatory bowel disease.
6. **Alert a friend, family member, or neighbour to your situation.** If your condition worsens quickly, you may need someone to assist with transport or to speak with emergency services on your behalf.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause of the blockage or "fix" the constipation yourself.
- You do not need to plan dietary changes or high-fibre menus until after you have been medically cleared.
- You do not need to keep a stool diary or monitor the situation overnight if symptoms are present now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when experiencing significant physical discomfort and visible changes like swelling. These symptoms are recognized as urgent by the NHS, and seeking a professional assessment is the standard procedure to ensure your safety.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions for acute symptoms only. It does not replace professional diagnosis or long-term management of chronic constipation.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute medical advice. If you cannot pass wind, are vomiting, or have a swollen, painful abdomen, you must seek professional medical assessment immediately via 999, A&E, or NHS 111.
