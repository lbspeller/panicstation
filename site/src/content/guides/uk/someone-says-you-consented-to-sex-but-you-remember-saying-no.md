# What to do if… someone says you consented to sex but you remember saying no (UK)

## Short answer
Get to a safe location and contact a specialist support service or Sexual Assault Referral Centre (SARC) before responding to the other person or attempting to prove what happened.

## Do not do these things
- Do not engage in arguments via text, social media, or phone to "prove" you did not consent.
- Do not meet the person to "talk it out" or accept a private apology.
- Do not share screenshots or details of the event publicly on social media.
- Do not pressure yourself to decide immediately if you want to report this to the police.
- Do not wash, shower, or brush your teeth if you think you may want a forensic medical exam (this can wait until you speak to a professional).
- Do not delete messages, call logs, or photos even if looking at them is distressing.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause immediately.** Move to a public place, a trusted friend's home, or a locked room where you are not alone with the person involved. If you are in immediate danger, call **999**.
2. **Stop all contact with the other person.** Silence their notifications or block them if it is safe to do so. This prevents further pressure while you assess your options.
3. **Contact a specialist support line for confidential advice.** These services are independent of the police and can talk through your options:
   - **England & Wales (16+):** Rape Crisis 24/7 Support Line on **0808 500 2222**.
   - **Scotland (13+):** Rape Crisis Scotland helpline on **08088 01 03 02** (daily, 5pm – midnight).
   - **Northern Ireland:** Domestic & Sexual Abuse Helpline on **0808 802 1414** (24/7).
4. **Access medical care and forensic options via a SARC.** Sexual Assault Referral Centres provide healthcare, emergency contraception, and can collect forensic evidence even if you do not report to the police.
   - **England:** Search "NHS SARC" or go to the NHS website to find your local centre.
   - **Scotland:** Call the NHS Scotland SARC self-referral line on **0800 148 88 88** (24/7).
   - **Northern Ireland:** Contact "The Rowan" (SARC) on **0800 389 4424**.
5. **Prioritise time-sensitive medical needs.** If you have injuries, pain, or require emergency contraception, go to **A&E** or contact **NHS 111**. Emergency contraception is most effective when taken as soon as possible.
6. **Preserve existing digital and physical evidence without reviewing it.** Keep your clothes in a clean paper bag if you have already changed. Leave messages and call logs as they are.
7. **Tell one trusted person that you need support.** Use a direct request: "Something happened and I am not okay; can you help me get home / stay with me / help me call a support line."
8. **Make a brief, private note of the basic facts.** Write down the date, time, location, and the specific words or actions you remember using to say "no" or "stop." Keep this note in a secure, private place.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to file a formal police report.
- Responding to threats or "gaslighting" from the other person.
- Explaining the situation to family, colleagues, or mutual friends.
- Reviewing digital evidence or messages in detail.
- Determining exactly how to label the experience.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel confused or to doubt your memory when someone insists that you agreed to something you remember refusing. Refusal can be verbal, but it can also be shown through your body language, freezing, or simply not participating. None of these reactions constitute consent. Specialist professionals are trained to help you navigate this exact situation without judgment.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety information. It does not provide legal or medical diagnoses. Subsequent decisions regarding reporting or recovery should be made with the assistance of specialist advocates or legal professionals.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional legal or medical advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999. If you are struggling to keep yourself safe or feel at risk of self-harm, go to A&E or contact a crisis service immediately.
