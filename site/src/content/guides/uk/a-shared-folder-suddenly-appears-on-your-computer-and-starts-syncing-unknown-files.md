# What to do if… a shared folder suddenly appears on your computer and starts syncing unknown files (UK)

## Short answer
Immediately pause the sync application or disconnect from the internet to stop further downloads. Identify the linked account and revoke access through the cloud service's web portal before opening any files.

## Do not do these things
- Don't open any of the unknown files to "check" them, as they may contain malware, ransomware, or malicious macros.
- Don't delete, move, or rename the files within the folder while the sync is active, as this may trigger notifications to the sender or delete data on other linked devices.
- Don't click any "View Folder" or "Accept Share" links in emails that arrived around the same time.
- Don't sign out of the sync application until you have noted the exact email address and service name associated with the activity.
- Don't attempt to "clean" the folder by dragging suspicious files into your personal directories.

## What to do now
1. **Interrupt the data transfer immediately.**
   - Locate the sync icon in your Windows taskbar (bottom right) or macOS menu bar (top right).
   - Right-click the icon (e.g., OneDrive cloud, Dropbox box, Google Drive triangle) and select **Pause syncing** or **Quit**.
   - If the application is unresponsive, **turn off your Wi-Fi** or unplug your Ethernet cable to physically cut the connection.

2. **Verify the source of the sync.**
   - Open the sync application's **Settings** or **Preferences**.
   - Note the **Account** or **User Email** currently signed in; confirm if it is your personal account, a work/school account, or an unrecognised address.
   - Look for a section labeled **Manage Backup**, **Choose Folders**, or **Synced Libraries** to see the name of the unrecognised folder and who owns it.

3. **Secure the account via a web browser.**
   - Using a different, trusted device (like a smartphone), log in to the web version of the service (e.g., onedrive.live.com, dropbox.com).
   - Navigate to **Security** or **Recent Activity** to check for sign-ins from unrecognised locations or IP addresses.
   - Go to the **Sharing** or **Shared with me** section, locate the unknown folder, and select **Remove from shared list** or **Stop syncing**.

4. **Change credentials from a clean device.**
   - Change your password for the affected cloud service immediately.
   - Enable **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** or Two-Step Verification if it is not already active.
   - Use the "Sign out of all sessions" or "Remove trusted devices" option if the service provides it.

5. **Scan the local machine for changes.**
   - Ensure your internet remains disconnected.
   - Run a full scan using **Windows Security** (Microsoft Defender) or your installed reputable antivirus software.
   - Look for changed file extensions (e.g., files ending in .locked, .crypted) which may indicate ransomware activity.

6. **Report the incident (UK specific).**
   - If this is a work-issued device or contains work data, **notify your IT department or Data Protection Officer (DPO) immediately**. Under 2026 regulations, they may have a 24-hour window to report a potential breach.
   - For personal incidents in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland, report to the **Report Fraud** service (formerly Action Fraud) online or via 0300 123 2040.
   - If in Scotland, report the digital crime to **Police Scotland** by calling **101**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to delete the local copies of the files until you have confirmed they are not part of a legitimate workplace update.
- You do not need to perform a factory reset of the computer immediately unless a security professional confirms a deep compromise.
- You do not need to notify your bank unless you find evidence that financial details were stored in the affected cloud account.

## Important reassurance
In many cases, an unknown folder appears due to a "Shared" invitation from a colleague or a legitimate administrative change in a SharePoint environment. Seeing files appear does not always mean your hardware has been "hacked," but pausing the sync is the correct first step to prevent any potential risks from escalating.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for unrecognised file syncing. It does not cover deep forensic analysis, data recovery for ransomware, or legal disputes regarding data ownership.

This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional IT or legal advice.
