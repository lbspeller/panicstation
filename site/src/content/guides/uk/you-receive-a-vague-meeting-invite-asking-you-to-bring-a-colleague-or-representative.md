# What to do if… you receive a vague meeting invite asking you to bring a colleague or representative (UK)

## Short answer
Ask for the meeting type, topic, attendees, evidence, and possible outcomes in writing before you attend. If you are an employee or worker and it is a qualifying disciplinary or grievance meeting, make a reasonable written request to be accompanied.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess what the meeting is about and try to “wing it”.
- Do not go alone just because it feels awkward to ask questions.
- Do not assume you automatically have a legal right to be accompanied at every workplace meeting.
- Do not sign anything you have not had time to read and understand.
- Do not hand over passwords, personal devices, or personal accounts on the spot without a clear written reason and time to consider it.
- Do not vent, speculate, or accuse in writing while you still do not know the purpose of the meeting.

## What to do now
1. **Reply briefly and ask what the meeting is.**  
   Ask them to confirm whether it is an investigation meeting, a disciplinary hearing, a grievance meeting or hearing, or something else. Ask for:
   - the specific issue, allegation, complaint, or topic
   - who will attend, with names and roles
   - who will chair the meeting
   - the expected duration and format
   - the possible outcomes, including whether formal action is being considered

2. **Ask for what you are expected to respond to before the meeting.**  
   Request copies of any evidence or information they plan to rely on, such as emails, reports, policy sections, complaint summaries, or notes. If they will not share everything, ask for a written outline of the topics and the timeframe being discussed.

3. **If it is a disciplinary hearing, request to be accompanied in writing.**  
   Write: “I am making a reasonable request to be accompanied.” For a qualifying disciplinary hearing, a worker or employee can usually choose:
   - someone they work with
   - a workplace trade union representative certified by their union to act as a companion
   - an official employed by a trade union

4. **If it is a grievance meeting, ask for accompaniment and check the basis.**  
   The legal right to be accompanied generally applies where the grievance is about the employer breaching a legal or contractual duty, including implied duties. If this is unclear, ask them to allow accompaniment as good practice and check your workplace policy.

5. **If it is an investigatory or fact-finding meeting, ask what support is allowed.**  
   In a disciplinary investigation meeting, there is usually no statutory right to be accompanied, but your employer’s policy may allow it and Acas treats accompaniment as good practice. Ask in writing to bring a companion, note-taker, or support person so you can keep an accurate record. If they say no, ask for:
   - written questions or topics in advance
   - permission to take your own notes
   - a short reschedule so you can prepare
   - any reasonable adjustment you need if disability, health, language, or communication affects the meeting

6. **If your chosen companion cannot attend a qualifying disciplinary or grievance hearing, propose another time promptly.**  
   Put the alternative date and time in writing. The statutory rearrangement rule can apply if your proposed time is reasonable and not more than five working days after the original proposed date.

7. **Do a short stability prep before replying again.**
   - Write down what you know, what you do not know, and three questions to ask at the start.
   - Decide who you want to ask as your companion or note-taker.
   - Save the invite, your reply, any policies, and any documents they send.
   - Agree with your companion that they take notes, you pause before answering, and either of you can ask for a break if new information appears.

8. **If you end up in the meeting without clarity, slow it down at the start.**  
   Say: “Before we begin, please confirm what type of meeting this is, what it is about, and the possible outcomes.” If new allegations or evidence appear for the first time, say: “I need time to review this. I am requesting a short adjournment and a follow-up meeting.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare a full response until you know what the meeting is and what the issue is.
- You do not need to decide today whether to raise a grievance, submit a counter-complaint, appeal anything, or contact a solicitor.
- You do not need to argue the facts immediately if you have not been given the evidence they are relying on.
- You can ask to pause if “informal” questioning starts before the meeting type, topics, and support position are clear.

## Important reassurance
A vague invite plus “bring a colleague” or “bring a representative” can feel alarming, but asking for clarity is normal. Written questions, a support person, and time to read documents can reduce the chance of being rushed into avoidable mistakes.

## Scope note
These are first steps to slow things down, get clarity, and protect you from avoidable errors. Later decisions may need specialist help and will depend on whether this is investigatory, disciplinary, or grievance-related, and what your employer’s own policy says.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Your contract and workplace policies may give you more, or clearer, process rights than the legal minimum. Keep requests calm and in writing, and prioritise clarity and support before engaging with allegations.
