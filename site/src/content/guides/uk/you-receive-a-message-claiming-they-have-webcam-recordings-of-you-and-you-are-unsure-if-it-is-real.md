# What to do if… you receive a message claiming they have webcam recordings of you and you are unsure if it is real (UK)

## Short answer
Stop all contact immediately, do not pay any money, and secure your email account using two-factor authentication. Report the threat to the police via the national reporting gateway (Report Fraud) or 101.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply to the message, negotiate with the sender, or attempt to plead with them.
- Do not pay any money or send cryptocurrency; doing so identifies you as a "payer" and often results in higher demands.
- Do not click any links or download attachments in the message, as these may contain malware to actually compromise your device.
- Do not delete the original message or email until you have saved a record and reported it.
- Do not share more images or "verification" videos to prove you are the person in the recording.
- Do not reset your device to factory settings yet, as this may destroy evidence needed by the police.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all engagement with the sender immediately.** **Block the sender on the platform where the message was received and do not respond to any further attempts to contact you.**
2. **Secure your primary email account.** **Change your email password to a strong, unique phrase and enable two-factor authentication (2FA) immediately, as email is the primary way hackers reset other account passwords.**
3. **Save a record of the threat.** **Take screenshots of the message, the sender’s details (email address, username, or phone number), and any payment instructions provided (such as a Bitcoin wallet address).**
   - Keep the original email in your inbox or "junk" folder; do not delete it yet.
4. **Report the message to the National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC).** **If the threat arrived via email, forward it to report@phishing.gov.uk to help authorities track the scam infrastructure.**
5. **Report the crime to the appropriate UK police gateway.** **Follow the specific route for your location:**
   - In England, Wales, or Northern Ireland: report to **Report Fraud** (formerly Action Fraud) online at reportfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040.
   - In Scotland: report directly to **Police Scotland** by calling 101.
   - If you are in immediate physical danger or being actively coerced into self-harm, call 999.
6. **Check your password security.** **If the scammer included a password you recognize, visit "Have I Been Pwned" to see if your data was leaked in a past breach, and change that password on every site where it is used.**
7. **Contact specialist support if intimate images actually exist.** **If you believe a recording was genuinely taken, use these services:**
   - **StopNCII.org:** A tool for adults to help prevent the sharing of intimate images on participating social media platforms.
   - **Revenge Porn Helpline:** Provides free, confidential advice for UK adults (18+) on image removal and legal rights.
   - **CEOP:** If you or the person involved is under 18, report directly to the Child Exploitation and Online Protection Command.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to tell your family, partner, or employer right now.
- You do not need to replace your hardware or "wipe" your computer immediately.
- You do not need to determine with 100% certainty if the recording is real before reporting it; the police handle "bluff" threats regularly.
- You do not need to respond to "deadlines" set by the blackmailer; these are designed to force a panicked decision.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a threat of this nature is a high-stress event designed to trigger shame and urgency. These messages are frequently automated "scattergun" scams sent to thousands of people using old leaked passwords to appear legitimate. Taking a moment to secure your accounts and report the incident is a standard, effective response that stops the vast majority of these attempts.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for receiving a blackmail threat. It does not replace a full police investigation or professional cybersecurity audit. If images are actively being shared or if you have already paid money, you will require direct assistance from the police and specialist support services.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. Reporting to the police is the primary method for investigating crimes in the UK. Under the 2026 Online Safety Act rules, major platforms are required to remove non-consensual intimate content within 48 hours once reported through their internal tools.
