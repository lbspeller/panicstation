# What to do if… you receive a legal threat from a former employer about contacting clients or colleagues (UK)

## Short answer
Do not buy gift cards, share codes, or make the payment. Verify the request through a separate, trusted communication channel and report it to your employer's security team immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not purchase gift cards for a work request, regardless of promises of reimbursement.
- Do not share photographs of gift card backs, redemption codes, or receipts with the requester.
- Do not click links in the message that claim to lead to invoices, payment portals, or shared files.
- Do not use the "Reply" button or call the number that sent the message to verify it.
- Do not follow instructions to keep the request "confidential" or bypass normal finance procedures.
- Do not delete the message until it has been safely captured for internal security reporting.

## What to do now
1. **Interrupt any active transaction immediately.** If you are currently at a retail till or on a banking website, close the screen or walk away from the checkout without completing the purchase.
2. **Verify the request using a known, independent contact method.**
   - Call the person on their official internal extension or a mobile number you have used previously.
   - Use the official company directory or internal chat (Teams / Slack) rather than clicking links in the suspicious message.
3. **Notify your internal IT or Security department.** 
   - Forward the suspicious email as an attachment (to preserve "header" data) or provide screenshots of SMS / WhatsApp messages.
   - Inform the finance or payroll department so they can alert others to potential business email compromise (BEC) attempts.
4. **Report the message to national UK security services.**
   - **Forward emails** to **report@phishing.gov.uk** (NCSC).
   - **Forward text messages** to **7726** (free service for mobile providers).
5. **Contact your bank immediately if you have already transferred money.**
   - Call the **159** hotline to reach your bank safely and ask them to stop or recall the payment.
   - Ask for a reference number and note the exact time of the call.
6. **Alert the gift card issuer if codes were shared.**
   - Contact the brand (e.g., Amazon, Apple, Google Play) immediately to see if the balance can be frozen or cancelled.
   - Keep the physical cards and original receipts as evidence.
7. **Submit an official fraud report.**
   - In England, Wales, and Northern Ireland: Use the **Report Fraud** online portal (the 2026 replacement for Action Fraud) or call **0300 123 2040**.
   - In Scotland: Call **Police Scotland via 101** to report the financial crime.
8. **Document the incident for your records.** Save a copy of the original request, the sender's details, any account numbers provided, and a timeline of your response.

## What can wait
- You do not need to initiate a disciplinary or formal policy review yourself.
- You do not need to perform a full malware scan of your personal device unless instructed by IT.
- You do not need to contact the police in person; the 101 or Report Fraud channels are the correct starting points.

## Important reassurance
Scammers use psychological pressure, including simulated urgency and authority, to bypass normal logic. Identifying a suspicious request and stopping to verify is the correct professional response, even if the message appeared highly personalized or credible.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and risk-mitigation steps for workplace payment scams. It is not a substitute for your employer’s specific internal security policies or legal advice regarding liability.

## Important note
Actions taken by banks or gift card issuers to recover funds are not guaranteed and depend on the speed of reporting. Reporting to national bodies helps map criminal activity but may not result in an immediate police investigation. Contact the Citizens Advice consumer service at 0808 223 1133 for additional procedural support.
