# What to do if… you are asked to complete an official form or declaration in a language you cannot read (UK)

## Short answer
Pause before signing or submitting. Ask for an English version, another version you can read, an interpreter, or for an official to read each question and your answers back in a language you understand.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess answers just to move the queue along if the form is an entry, customs, immigration, health, police, fine, or legal declaration.
- Do not sign a form you cannot read or do not understand.
- Do not let another traveller, taxi driver, tout, or stranger fill it in for you away from an official desk.
- Do not hand your passport, phone, cards, or documents to an unofficial person offering help.
- Do not argue about legal rights at the desk if it is making things worse; keep repeating that you need to understand the form before answering.

## What to do now
1. Say clearly: “I cannot read this language. I need an English version, another version I can read, or an interpreter before I can complete or sign it.”
2. Ask the official, airline desk, hotel desk, border officer, or customs officer for an official alternative if one is available.
3. If the first person says no, calmly ask whether a supervisor, interpreter, or language assistance point is available.
4. If the form must be completed at the desk, ask the official or interpreter to read one question at a time, wait for your answer, then repeat back exactly what has been written before moving on.
5. If you are using a phone translation app, treat it as a rough support only. Ask an official or interpreter to confirm any entry, customs, health, police, or money declaration before you sign or submit it.
6. If you are being rushed, say: “I am not refusing. I am asking for time to understand it properly.” Ask to step aside so the queue can continue while you get language help.
7. If staff allow it, keep a simple record of the time, place, desk, form name, and what help you asked for. Only take photos or use your phone where staff say it is allowed.
8. If you are told to sign despite not understanding, ask where you can note that you could not read the form and asked for language help. If staff say not to write on the form, do not write on it.
9. If the situation escalates into threats, detention, confiscation, or police involvement, ask the local authorities to contact the nearest British embassy, high commission, or consulate. You can also contact FCDO consular assistance through the nearest British embassy, high commission, or consulate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain the whole situation in detail right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, challenge the process, or take formal action.
- You do not need to solve the full legal or travel problem at the desk; the immediate goal is to avoid making a declaration you cannot verify.

## Important reassurance
Needing language help with an official travel form is a practical problem, not a personal failure. Slowing down is a reasonable way to avoid giving a wrong answer or signing something you did not understand.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the form is connected to refusal of entry, fines, police action, detention, or a legal case, later decisions may need help from consular staff, a local lawyer, or a qualified interpreter.

## Important note
This is general information for immediate harm-prevention, not legal, immigration, travel, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Local rules and official processes vary by country and agency.
