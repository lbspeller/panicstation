# What to do if… someone you met online pressures you to meet in private and refuses public places (UK)

## Short answer
Do not meet them in private. End the contact, or stop replying, and call 999 if you are in immediate danger or a crime is happening now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree to a quick meet in a car, home, hotel, workplace, car park, or secluded place.
- Don’t let them pick you up, book your transport, or learn where you live, work, study, or regularly go.
- Don’t share your address, routine, workplace, school, live location, or nearby landmarks.
- Don’t keep negotiating your boundary to prove you are serious.
- Don’t send intimate images or do anything on camera because they are pressuring you.
- Don’t meet them because they are angry, guilty-making, persistent, or threatening to disappear.
- Don’t meet while they are refusing public, safer options.
- If you already shared something personal or intimate, don’t blame yourself. You can still block, report, and get support.

## What to do now
1. **Treat private-only pressure as a stop sign.** You can send one short message such as: “I’m only comfortable meeting in a public place. If that’s not okay, I’m ending this.” If replying feels unsafe, you can block without another message.
2. **Block and report them in the app or platform.** Use the platform’s report option for harassment, coercion, sexual pressure, grooming concerns, threats, or unsafe behaviour. You can keep the report brief.
3. **Save key messages if it is safe to do so.** Take screenshots of threats, pressure, usernames, profiles, phone numbers, photos, payment requests, or attempts to get your address. Then mute or block so you are not pulled back into the conversation.
4. **Reduce what they can find out about you now.**
   - Turn off location sharing for the app and check phone location permissions.
   - Remove or hide public details that show your workplace, school, regular places, town, check-ins, or live location.
   - If they have your number, silence unknown callers and check messaging privacy settings.
5. **Tell one real person.** Send a trusted friend, family member, housemate, or colleague a short note with the person’s name, username, app, and what happened. Ask them to stay reachable for now if you are worried.
6. **Use police help if there are threats, stalking, harassment, blackmail, or forced contact.**
   - Call **999** if they are nearby, you are in immediate danger, violence is threatened, or a crime is happening now.
   - Call **101** or use online police reporting if it is not an emergency but you want to report threats, harassment, stalking, sexual offences, or coercive behaviour.
7. **If you are under 18, or worried about a child or young person, use child-protection support.** CEOP can receive reports about online sexual abuse, grooming, pressure to meet face-to-face, sexual messages, or pressure to do things you do not want to do. If there is immediate danger, call 999 and tell a trusted adult if you can.
8. **If sexual pressure, threats, intimate images, harassment, assault, or uncertainty about consent is involved, contact specialist support.** In England and Wales, Rape Crisis England & Wales and the 24/7 Rape and Sexual Abuse Support Line can support people aged 16 or over affected by sexual violence, abuse, assault, or harassment. In Scotland, Rape Crisis Scotland supports people aged 13 or over affected by sexual violence. In Northern Ireland, Nexus NI runs the Domestic and Sexual Abuse Helpline. These are not emergency services; call 999 for immediate danger or urgent medical help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this counts as abuse, grooming, stalking, harassment, or a crime.
- You do not need to explain yourself in detail to them.
- You do not need to convince them that your boundary is reasonable.
- You do not need to warn them, confront them, or teach them a lesson.
- You do not need to make a formal report today unless you want to, or unless you are in immediate danger.

## Important reassurance
Someone who insists on a private meeting and refuses safer public options is not respecting your safety. You are allowed to stop contact without further explanation. Feeling alarmed, frozen, guilty, or unsure does not mean you are overreacting.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: reduce immediate risk, stop escalation, preserve your options, and connect you with support. If there are ongoing threats, stalking, harassment, blackmail, sexual pressure, or grooming concerns, later decisions may need specialist help and a tailored safety plan.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, safeguarding, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 999.
