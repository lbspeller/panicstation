# What to do if… you receive a last-minute notice to provide evidence or documents before a hearing (UK)

## Short answer
Read the notice or order carefully, keep a copy of it, and contact the court or tribunal using the contact details on the notice to ask exactly what is required, how to send it, and what to do if you cannot meet the deadline. Assume the hearing is still going ahead unless you receive written confirmation that the court or tribunal has changed it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice or assume the court or tribunal will know why you did not respond.
- Do not send original documents you cannot replace unless the notice clearly says originals are required.
- Do not alter documents, change dates, or recreate records from memory as if they were originals.
- Do not send sensitive documents to an address found by a general web search; use the notice, the court or tribunal finder, or another official route.
- Do not assume that asking for more time changes the deadline or hearing date unless the court or tribunal confirms this.
- Do not send the other side private contact details, medical records, bank records, or child-related material unless the notice, order, rules, or legal adviser tells you it is required.

## What to do now
1. **Capture the essentials in one place.**  
   Write down the case number, parties’ names, hearing date and time, the exact documents or evidence requested, the deadline, and the method for sending them.

2. **Identify the forum named on the notice.**  
   Check whether it is a civil court, family court, criminal court, Employment Tribunal, or another tribunal. Also note the location or part of the UK named on the notice, because the correct process can differ.

3. **Check whether you must file, serve, or both.**  
   “File” usually means send to the court or tribunal. “Serve” usually means send to the other party or their representative. If the notice is unclear, ask the court or tribunal office what the notice requires and record the answer.

4. **Contact the court or tribunal office today using the notice details.**  
   Ask:
   - what exactly must be provided;
   - whether copies, scans, screenshots, or originals are needed;
   - whether the documents must go by email, portal, post, upload, or in person;
   - whether there are file size, format, bundle, or page limits;
   - what written request or application is needed if you cannot meet the deadline.

5. **If emailing documents, make the subject line easy to match to your case.**  
   For civil court email filing, the subject line should include the case number, parties’ names, and hearing date and time. For other courts or tribunals, follow the notice or the official instructions for that forum.

6. **Send what you can safely send now.**  
   Attach or upload the documents you already have, unless the notice tells you to wait or use a different route. Add a short cover note saying:
   - “Documents provided in response to the notice/order dated [date].”
   - “Document 1: [name]. Document 2: [name].”
   - “The following documents are still outstanding: [short list].”
   - “I have requested the outstanding documents from [person or organisation], if true.”

7. **If you cannot meet the deadline, make a same-day written request for more time.**  
   Send a short email or letter to the court or tribunal office and, if required, the other side. Include:
   - when you received the notice;
   - the deadline you were given;
   - what you have sent already;
   - what is still missing;
   - the practical reason you cannot get it in time;
   - the extra time you are asking for;
   - whether you are asking for an extension of time, a postponement, or an adjournment.  
   In a civil court case, if you have already missed a deadline or an order mentions sanctions, ask whether you need to apply for relief from sanctions. In other cases, ask what the correct request is called.

8. **Keep proof of every attempt.**  
   Save sent emails, upload confirmations, delivery receipts, screenshots, postal receipts, and notes of phone calls. Keep a simple list of what you sent, when you sent it, and where you sent it.

9. **Use any urgent legal support route you already have.**  
   If you have a solicitor, barrister, union representative, duty solicitor, support worker, or legal adviser, forward the notice and your draft response to them. If this is criminal or custody-related, ask custody staff, your representative, or the court office for the correct urgent route for time-critical papers.

10. **Prepare as if the hearing is still happening.**  
   Attend or join the hearing as directed unless the court or tribunal confirms otherwise. Have your documents, your written request for more time, and your proof of sending ready to show or explain.

## What can wait
- You do not need to perfect your whole case today.
- You do not need to decide today whether to settle, plead, appeal, complain, or make a long-term legal plan.
- You can sort longer statements, chronologies, and extra evidence after you have confirmed what the notice actually requires.
- You can tidy the folder later; the immediate job is to respond safely, clearly, and with proof.

## Important reassurance
A last-minute document request can feel overwhelming, especially if you are unrepresented. The safest first step is to be visible and organised: ask what is needed, send what you can, explain what you cannot do yet, and keep proof of every attempt.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for handling an urgent evidence or document deadline before a hearing. Court and tribunal rules differ by case type, by the wording of the notice or order, and by the part of the UK or tribunal system named on the notice, so later decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you may lose your liberty, your home, contact with children, immigration status, employment, or face a serious penalty, try to get urgent specialist advice while still responding to the notice as safely as you can.
