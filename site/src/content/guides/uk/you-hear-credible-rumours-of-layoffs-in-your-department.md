# What to do if… you hear credible rumours of layoffs in your department (UK)

## Short answer
Switch to a formal, written paper-trail: send one final reminder with a clear deadline, then escalate to a formal Letter Before Action (for companies) or a Letter of Claim (for individuals) to preserve your right to a court claim.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send repeated, informal messages across different social media channels or messaging apps.
- Don’t contact the client’s family members or multiple unrelated employees at the company.
- Don’t publicly name the client on review sites or social media while the debt is outstanding.
- Don’t threaten legal action unless you are prepared to follow through with a court claim.
- Don’t use aggressive or emotional language in your correspondence.
- Don’t continue providing new work or services if the payment remains unresolved.
- Don’t rely on verbal agreements; if a phone call occurs, always follow up with a written summary.

## What to do now
1. **Gather a complete evidence pack for the debt.**  
   - Save the signed contract or agreed statement of work.
   - Collate the purchase order (if provided) and the specific invoice.
   - Archive the email thread showing the invoice delivery and any evidence of service completion (e.g., file transfers, sign-off emails).
2. **Verify the correct legal entity and service address.**  
   - If the client is a **limited company or LLP**, find their registered office address via Companies House.
   - If the client is a **sole trader or individual**, use the address provided in the contract or their last known trading address.
3. **Send one final "Late Payment" reminder with a 7-day deadline.**  
   Keep this short and factual. State the invoice number, amount, and due date. Ask: "Is there an issue with the invoice format or a missing PO number preventing payment?" Clearly state that if payment is not received within 7 days, you will initiate a formal pre-action process.
4. **Calculate statutory interest and compensation for business-to-business debts.**  
   If the client is another business (or public body), you are legally entitled to add:
   - **Statutory Interest:** 8% above the Bank of England base rate (calculated daily).
   - **Compensation:** A fixed fee per invoice (£40 for debts under £1,000; £70 for £1,000–£9,999; £100 for £10,000+).
   - Note: If the client is a consumer, these commercial rates do not automatically apply unless specified in your contract.
5. **Issue a formal Letter Before Action to a company or LLP.**  
   **The letter must be sent by post to the registered office and should provide a 30-day response window.**
   - State the basis of the claim (the contract and the work completed).
   - List the total amount including any interest and compensation.
   - Attach copies of the relevant invoices.
   - Explicitly state that you will issue a court claim if the debt is not settled.
6. **Follow the Pre-Action Protocol for Debt Claims for individuals or sole traders.**  
   **If the debtor is an individual, you must send a formal "Letter of Claim" by post.**
   - You must include the mandatory Information Sheet, the Reply Form, and a Financial Statement form.
   - You must allow the individual **30 days** to respond before taking any further action.
7. **Pause all ongoing work to prevent further exposure.**  
   Stop delivering services or providing access to digital assets unless your contract explicitly forbids suspension. If you must suspend access, provide the client with written notice of the suspension date and the specific unpaid invoices triggering the action.
8. **Submit a claim via the Online Civil Money Claims service if the deadline passes.**  
   If the total claim (debt + interest + costs) is under £10,000, you can use the Government’s online service.
   - Ensure the total does not exceed £10,000 to remain in the "Small Claims" track.
   - Be aware that you will need to pay an upfront court fee based on the claim value.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to hire a solicitor or debt collection agency in the first 30 days.
- You do **not** need to investigate the client’s financial health or "ghosting" reasons today.
- You do **not** need to decide on a court hearing date; most claims are settled or handled via mediation once the claim is issued.

## Important reassurance
Non-responsiveness is often a failure of administrative systems or internal approvals rather than a deliberate attempt to avoid payment. Following a structured, professional protocol protects your legal standing and often prompts a response from the client's finance department.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and legal positioning for unpaid invoices in the UK. It does not cover complex cross-border litigation, insolvency proceedings, or disputed professional negligence claims.

## Important note
This guide provides general information on UK debt recovery procedures and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding interest rates and pre-action protocols are subject to change. If you suspect your client is entering insolvency or if the debt is significantly large, you should consult a legal professional or the Office of the Small Business Commissioner.
