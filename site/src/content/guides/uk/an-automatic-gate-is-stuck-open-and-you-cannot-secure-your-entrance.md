# What to do if… an automatic gate is stuck open and you cannot secure your entrance (UK)

## Short answer
Treat the open gate as a safety and security problem, not as something to force shut. Keep people away from the moving parts, secure the building, improve visibility, and arrange urgent help from a competent gate engineer.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t put your body in the gate’s path or push it while the motor is engaged.
- Don’t bypass, tape over, or disable safety devices, for example photocells or safety edges.
- Don’t climb through the gate or squeeze through gaps where it could move unexpectedly.
- Don’t open control boxes or attempt electrical repairs unless you are trained and it is safe to do so.
- Don’t leave ladders, tools, bins, garden furniture, or vehicles where they make entry easier.
- Don’t create a barrier that blocks a public road, footpath, shared access, or emergency access.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Stop pressing remotes or buttons. Keep children, pets, and bystanders away from the gate, hinges, rollers, track, closing edge, and any gaps where trapping could happen.
2. **Secure the building first.** Lock external doors and windows, lock the garage, and lock any internal door between the garage and the house. Set any alarm you have.
3. **Make the entrance more visible.** Turn on front and exterior lighting. Turn on a couple of interior lights that can be seen from the front. If you have dusk-to-dawn lighting, enable it.
4. **Remove easy entry aids.** Move bins, ladders, tools, bikes, and garden furniture away from walls, fences, windows, doors, and the open entrance.
5. **Look for an obvious obstruction only while the gate is stopped.** From a safe position, check for debris in a sliding gate track, something touching a safety edge, or anything blocking a photocell line of sight. Clear only what you can reach without entering hinge, track, roller, or closing gaps.
6. **Use safer temporary deterrents.** Lock any separate pedestrian or side gate. Move vehicles, bikes, and tools into a locked garage or behind locked doors if you can.
7. **Only park across your own driveway if it stays safe and lawful.** You can use a vehicle inside your driveway to reduce easy access if it does not block a public road, pavement, shared access, or emergency escape route. Keep the keys accessible.
8. **Try one cautious reset only if the isolator is obvious.** If there is a clearly labelled gate isolator or fused spur that you normally use, stand clear of the gate and switch it off briefly, then back on once. If you are unsure what it is, skip this step.
9. **Use manual release only if you already know the system.** Some gates have a key release to disengage the motor. Use it only if you can positively identify it, keep everyone clear, and move the gate without putting yourself near trapping points. If not, stop and wait for help.
10. **Arrange urgent repair.** Call the gate installer, maintenance company, or the number on the service label. If you are in a managed building or estate, contact the managing agent’s out-of-hours line.
11. **Get police help if the security risk becomes immediate.** If someone is trying to enter, a crime is happening now, or anyone is in immediate danger, call 999. If you want to report suspicious activity that is not an emergency, use 101 or your local police online reporting route.

## What can wait
- Diagnosing whether the fault is the motor, controller, photocells, safety edges, remote, or power supply.
- Buying CCTV, a new operator, new access control, or other upgrades.
- Deciding who is at fault or whether the whole gate system needs replacing.
- Cleaning, lubricating, adjusting, or testing the gate beyond a simple safe obstruction check.

## Important reassurance
A gate stuck open can feel exposing, but you do not need to solve the machinery fault tonight to reduce risk. Locking the building, improving visibility, removing easy entry aids, and arranging proper repair are practical steps that buy time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Powered gates can cause serious injury if they move unexpectedly, and later decisions about repair, safety testing, insurance, tenancy duties, or shared access may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, electrical, engineering, security, insurance, or professional advice. If there is immediate danger or a crime is happening now, contact emergency services.
