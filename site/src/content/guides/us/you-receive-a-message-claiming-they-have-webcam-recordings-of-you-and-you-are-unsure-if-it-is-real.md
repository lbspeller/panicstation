# What to do if… you receive a message claiming they have webcam recordings of you and you are unsure if it is real (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay, do not respond, and do not provide any further information. Secure your accounts immediately and report the threat to the FBI and FTC through their official 2026 reporting portals.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money, gift cards, or cryptocurrency (this confirms you are a "payer" and leads to higher demands).
- Do not reply to the message, even to argue or ask for proof (this confirms your account is active).
- Do not click links or download any "evidence" files they send (these often contain actual malware).
- Do not share any "verification" or two-factor authentication (2FA) codes with anyone.
- Do not delete the message until you have taken a secure screenshot of the threat and payment details.
- Do not use "recovery services" found on social media; these are almost always secondary scams.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause by putting the device down for a moment.** These messages are specifically designed to provoke a "fight or flight" response to force a fast payment before you can think clearly.
2. **Save a screenshot of the demand and the sender's details before taking any other action.** Capture the email address or profile name, the specific threat made, any payment addresses (like Bitcoin wallets), and any "proof" they claim to have (like a password).
3. **Stop all contact and block the sender on the platform where the message was received.** Use the platform's internal reporting tools to flag the message as "Extortion" or "Harassment" so the account can be suspended.
4. **Change your email password immediately and enable an authenticator-based two-factor authentication (2FA).** If the scammer included an old password as "proof," it likely came from an old data breach; changing it now prevents them from actually accessing your accounts.
5. **Secure your social media privacy settings to prevent the scammer from seeing your friends or followers.** Set your lists to "Private" or "Friends Only" and restrict who can tag you or comment on your posts.
6. **File a report with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) at ic3.gov.** This is the primary U.S. database for tracking these criminal networks and is essential for official documentation.
7. **Submit a fraud report to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.** The FTC uses these reports to identify and warn the public about emerging 2026 scam variations.
8. **Contact the NCMEC CyberTipline at cybertip.org if you are under 18 or if the threat involves a minor.** For those under 18, NCMEC provides specialized support and can assist in removing images from the internet using the "Take It Down" tool.
9. **Place a physical cover over your webcam and run a security scan on your device.** While most of these mass-emails are fake, a scan provides peace of mind that no unauthorized remote-access tools are active.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you should tell your family, partner, or employer right now.
- You do not need to figure out exactly how they got your information or password tonight.
- You do not need to delete your entire social media presence or deactivate all your accounts immediately.
- You do not need to engage with local police in person unless you feel there is an immediate physical threat to your safety.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a threat like this is a high-stress event, and feeling a sense of panic or shame is a completely natural reaction. In the vast majority of cases in 2026, these are automated "blanket" scams sent to thousands of people using data from old breaches. Taking the steps to secure your accounts and stop contact is the most effective way to make the scammer move on to a easier target.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and harm reduction for individuals receiving digital extortion threats. It does not replace the need for professional legal advice or ongoing support from victim services if the situation escalates.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and reflects standard 2026 safety protocols. If you are in immediate physical danger or are being coerced into self-harm, call 911 or contact the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline immediately by dialing 988.
