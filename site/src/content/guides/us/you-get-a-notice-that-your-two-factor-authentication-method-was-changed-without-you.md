# What to do if… you get a notice that your two-factor authentication method was changed without you (USA)

## Short answer
Assume an active account takeover attempt: navigate directly to the service website (do not click the alert link), initiate official account recovery, and revoke all active sessions immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click any "Secure Account" or "Review Activity" links inside the email or text notification; these are often phishing traps.
- Don’t enter your credentials on any page reached via a link in a suspicious message.
- Don’t ignore the alert even if you still have access; attackers often change 2FA methods before changing the password to lock you out later.
- Don’t use the same password for your email account as you do for the compromised service.
- Don’t share any "one-time codes" or "verification pins" with anyone claiming to be "support" over the phone or via chat.

## What to do now
1. **Access the service through a known-safe channel:** Type the official URL directly into your browser or use the verified mobile app, then navigate to the **Security** or **Sign-in Settings** menu.
2. **Execute the official recovery process:** 
   - If you are locked out: Select **“I can’t access my account”** or **“Forgot password”** to reach the identity verification stage.
   - If you still have access: Change your password immediately to a unique phrase of at least 15 characters.
3. **Terminate all active sessions:** Locate the **“Sign out of all devices”** or **“Remove trusted devices”** option and activate it to kick the unauthorized user off the account.
4. **Purge the attacker's recovery methods:** 
   - Remove any unfamiliar phone numbers, email addresses, or authenticator apps from the security settings.
   - Generate **new backup codes** and save them in a physical location or encrypted vault; this invalidates any old codes the attacker may have stolen.
5. **Set up phishing-resistant MFA:** Replace SMS-based 2FA with a **Passkey** or a hardware security key (FIDO2) if the service supports it, as these cannot be easily intercepted by SIM swapping.
6. **Secure your primary email account:** Check your email settings for new **auto-forwarding rules** or **filters** that might be hiding security alerts from you; change the email password and sign out of all sessions.
7. **Contact your mobile carrier for a "Number Lock":** If you suspect your phone number is involved, call your carrier and ask for a **Number Lock** or **Port-Out PIN** to prevent unauthorized SIM swaps under the Safe Connections Act.
8. **Contact financial fraud departments:** If the account is linked to a bank or credit card, call their fraud line and specifically request a **“Hold Harmless Letter”** or **“Letter of Indemnity”** regarding any unauthorized transactions.
9. **Place a fraud alert on your credit file:** Visit **IdentityTheft.gov** to file an official report and contact one of the three bureaus—Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion—to place a free one-year fraud alert.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to identify the specific malware or phishing site that caused the breach right now.
- You don’t need to notify your entire contact list until you have verified your email and primary accounts are secure.
- You don’t need to replace your physical hardware unless a technician confirms a local device infection.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a security notification is a standard part of modern digital defense. It is common to feel startled or rushed, but the alert itself is a tool designed to give you a head start. Following a structured recovery process—focusing on session revocation and recovery method cleanup—is the standard and effective way to regain control.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for digital account takeover. It does not cover long-term forensic investigation or complex legal recovery for corporate-level data breaches.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or technical advice. Account recovery procedures vary significantly between providers like Google, Microsoft, and financial institutions. Always prioritize the specific instructions provided by the official support channels of the service in question.
