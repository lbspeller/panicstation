# What to do if… you are refused entry at a border and are waiting in a controlled area (USA)

## Short answer
Stay calm, ask what happens next, do not sign anything you do not understand, and use any allowed phone access to tell one trusted person where you are.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t argue, joke about security, or make threats; keep your words simple and calm.
- Don’t sign forms you do not understand. Ask for an interpreter or clearer explanation first.
- Don’t unlock your phone or give a passcode impulsively just to end the situation. If asked, calmly ask what is being requested, whether refusal has consequences, and whether the request can be noted.
- Don’t delete messages, photos, travel records, or apps while you are in the controlled area.
- Don’t assume documents will be returned automatically. Calmly ask what is being kept, why, and how it will be returned.
- Don’t rely on memory alone if you are allowed to write things down.

## What to do now
1. **Clarify the immediate outcome.** Ask: “Am I being refused entry? What happens next? Am I waiting here, being moved, or being put on a return flight?”
2. **Ask who is arranging the next movement.** Ask whether border officials, the airline, or you need to take the next step, and ask for the flight number if a return flight has been arranged.
3. **Ask for an interpreter if you need one.** Say: “I need an interpreter before I answer more questions or sign anything.”
4. **Ask for copies or photos of paperwork.** If you are given a refusal notice, written reason, interview record, baggage form, or document to sign, ask for a copy. If allowed, take clear photos of each page.
5. **Write a short record while you still can.** Note the date and time, airport or border post, officer names or badge numbers if visible, what you were told, what documents were taken, and where your bags are.
6. **Send one short update if phone use is allowed.** Message one trusted person: your location, that entry was refused, whether you are waiting in a controlled area, your flight details if known, and whether your passport or documents are being held.
7. **Preserve phone power and access.** Use low power mode, dim the screen, close background apps, and ask calmly whether you may charge your phone.
8. **If you are a U.S. citizen and are detained or cannot communicate freely, ask to contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate.** Say: “I am a U.S. citizen. I want the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate notified, and I want to communicate with consular staff if possible.”
9. **If you are not a U.S. citizen, ask for your own embassy or consulate.** Say the country on your passport and ask for that embassy or consulate to be notified.
10. **Ask for essential needs clearly.** If you need medication, medical equipment, food, water, bathroom access, or urgent medical help, say it plainly and ask for it to be recorded.
11. **If you feel unsafe or unwell, keep the statement factual.** Say: “I feel unwell,” “I need medication,” or “I do not feel safe,” and ask who you can speak to next.

## What can wait
- Refunds, insurance claims, rebooking strategy, and future travel plans do not need to be solved right now.
- You do not need to write a long defence of your trip while you are in the controlled area.
- Complaints, appeals, legal advice, and future visa planning can wait until you are out of the controlled area and have your basic facts recorded.

## Important reassurance
Being refused entry and held in a controlled area can feel frightening and disorienting. The safest first step is to slow the situation down, stay factual, protect your documents and phone access where you can, and make sure someone outside knows where you are.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the controlled-area phase only. Border procedures vary by country and airport, and later decisions may need specialist immigration or legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Local law and border authority discretion apply. U.S. consular staff may be able to help with welfare, communication, family contact, and general information, but they generally cannot override another country’s immigration decision, force entry, provide legal advice, act as your lawyer, or get you released from detention.
