# What to do if… your key fob stops working and you can’t unlock or start your vehicle (USA)

## Short answer
Use the physical emergency key hidden inside the fob to unlock the driver’s door, then depress the brake pedal and hold the fob directly against the start button or in the designated backup slot to start the engine. If the vehicle has no electrical power at all, request a jump-start for the 12V vehicle battery rather than a locksmith.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay in an unlit or isolated area to troubleshoot; move to a staffed business or safe public space first.
- Do not use metal tools or screwdrivers to pry open door handle covers; use the plastic end of the fob or the emergency key blade to prevent paint damage.
- Do not repeatedly press the fob buttons; if the first three attempts fail, the battery is likely too weak for standard range and requires physical contact.
- Do not attempt to force the ignition or "hotwire" a modern vehicle; smart key systems use encrypted RFID chips that cannot be bypassed with physical force.
- Do not accept "starting at" quotes from mobile locksmiths; insist on a total final price including the service call and labor before they arrive.

## What to do now
1. **Relocate to a safe, well-lit environment and verify your surroundings.** If you are in a location where you feel physically threatened, enter a nearby business or call 911 for a safety standby.
2. **Extract the mechanical emergency key from the fob housing.**
   - Locate the small slide switch or release button on the side or back of the key fob.
   - Pull the metal key blade out of the plastic casing.
3. **Unlock the driver door manually using the hidden key cylinder.**
   - If no keyhole is visible, look at the underside of the driver's door handle for a small slot. 
   - Insert the tip of the emergency key into that slot and gently pop off the plastic cap to reveal the lock cylinder.
   - Insert the key and turn it to unlock the door.
4. **Attempt the backup start procedure for push-button ignitions.**
   - **Firmly depress the brake pedal.** 
   - Hold the key fob directly against the "Start / Stop" button.
   - Press the button using the fob itself; the vehicle’s short-range RFID reader can often detect the chip even with a dead fob battery.
5. **Check for alternative backup transmitter locations if the button-touch fails.**
   - Center console: Look for a key-shaped icon or a small pocket inside the armrest storage or under the cup holder inserts.
   - Steering column: Check the side of the column for a plastic slot or a marked area where the fob can be held.
   - Glove box: Some manufacturers place a dedicated key slot on the left side of the glove compartment interior.
6. **Determine if the fault is the key fob or the vehicle battery.**
   - If the dashboard, dome lights, and headlights do not respond when you open the door, the 12V vehicle battery is likely dead.
   - Request "Roadside Assistance" for a jump-start rather than a locksmith.
7. **Contact verified roadside services for professional entry.**
   - Call your insurer's roadside program, AAA, or the vehicle manufacturer’s emergency line.
   - Explicitly state if you have the physical key but the vehicle will not start, as this may require a tow rather than a lockout service.

## What can wait
- Replacing the CR2032 or CR2025 coin-cell battery in the fob can wait until you reach a pharmacy or hardware store.
- Investigating potential radio frequency interference (RFI) from nearby towers or devices.
- Reprogramming the smart key system or ordering a permanent replacement fob.
- Assessing long-term 12V battery health or alternator function.

## Important reassurance
Key fob battery failure is a common maintenance issue and does not mean your vehicle is permanently disabled. Most modern cars are engineered with passive RFID technology specifically to ensure you can start the engine even when the fob’s internal battery is completely exhausted.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for accessing and starting a vehicle with a non-responsive smart key. It does not cover technical repairs, ECU programming, or troubleshooting for mechanical ignition locks that require a traditional turned key.

## Important note
Procedures for emergency entry vary significantly by vehicle make, model, and year. Always consult the digital version of your owner's manual via the manufacturer's app or website if available. If a component does not move with light pressure, do not apply force, as this may damage proximity sensors or anti-theft hardware.
