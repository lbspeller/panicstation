# What to do if… you have a new episode of unusual sensations and confusion and worry it could be a seizure (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer place, sit or lie down, and have someone stay with you if possible. Call 911 now if this may be a first seizure, you are not returning to normal, there is trouble breathing, injury, water exposure, or back-to-back episodes; otherwise get same-day medical advice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive, cycle in traffic, or operate machinery until you have been medically assessed.
- Do not go near stairs, roads, water, baths, cooking heat, open flames, sharp tools, or heights while you feel confused or not right.
- Do not let anyone hold you down or physically restrain you if your body starts to jerk or you lose awareness.
- Do not let anyone put anything in your mouth.
- Do not force food, drink, or pills while you are confused, drowsy, or not fully alert.
- Do not use alcohol or recreational drugs to try to settle the episode.

## What to do now
1. **Make the space safer immediately.**  
   Sit or lie down away from hard edges, heat, water, and traffic. If you can, lie on your side and put something soft and flat under your head.

2. **Bring in another person if you can.**  
   Call or text someone nearby and say: “I’m having unusual sensations and confusion, and I’m worried it could be seizure-related. Please stay with me and watch for changes.”

3. **Start timing the episode.**  
   Note the time it started, what you felt, and whether your awareness changed. Useful details include an odd smell or taste, a rising feeling in your stomach, tingling, staring, lip-smacking, one-sided jerking, or not being able to respond normally. If someone is with you, ask them to write down what they see.

4. **Call 911 now if any of these apply:**  
   - This may be the first time you have had a seizure, or you are not sure.
   - Shaking, jerking, loss of awareness, or inability to respond lasts about 5 minutes or longer.
   - Another episode starts before you fully return to normal.
   - You have trouble breathing or trouble waking up afterwards.
   - The episode happened in water.
   - You are injured, pregnant, have diabetes with loss of consciousness, or anything feels life-threatening.
   - You are not returning to your normal self afterwards.

5. **If 911 is not needed, get same-day medical advice.**  
   Call your primary care office, a nurse line, or urgent care. Say: “I had a new episode of unusual sensations and confusion, and I’m worried it could be seizure-related.” Have someone else drive you if you need to be seen in person.

6. **Do a no-driving reset right now.**  
   Put your keys away, cancel driving plans, and arrange a ride. Driving rules after a seizure or altered-awareness episode vary by state; the immediate safety step is not to drive until you have been evaluated and told what applies.

7. **If you are alone while arranging help, reduce risk around you.**  
   Keep your phone charged and nearby. Stay seated or lying down somewhere safe. Consider making it easier for help to reach you, such as unlocking a door only if that is safe for you, and call 911 if symptoms worsen or return.

8. **Only if it is safe and not delaying help, capture a brief record.**  
   Write a short note or ask someone to record a short video of what is happening. Stop if this makes you less safe or delays calling for help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it definitely counts as a seizure.
- You do not need to research causes online or compare every possible diagnosis.
- You do not need to sort out DMV details today.
- You do not need to make work, school, travel, or long-term medication decisions in this moment.

## Important reassurance
A strange episode with confusion can feel frightening, and it is understandable to doubt yourself afterwards. Treating it as worth checking is a calm safety step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about testing, neurology referral, treatment, work or school notes, and driving restrictions may need help from a medical professional or the relevant state licensing authority.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical advice, diagnosis, legal advice, or professional advice. If symptoms worsen, you are not returning to normal, or you are unsure whether it is an emergency, call 911.
