# What to do if… you receive a notice that your communication with someone is restricted by an official order (USA)

## Short answer
Cease all forms of direct and indirect communication immediately and contact a qualified attorney or public defender to interpret the specific terms of the order before taking any other action.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send a final message to explain, apologize, or say goodbye, as this constitutes a violation.
- Do not ask third parties, such as friends, family members, or colleagues, to deliver messages or check on the protected person.
- Do not respond if the protected person contacts you first; their "permission" does not override a court order.
- Do not interact with the protected person on social media through likes, tags, comments, or private messages.
- Do not delete any existing communication logs, messages, or the digital notification of the order, as these may be evidence.
- Do not attempt to "test" the order by going to places where the person might be located or by using anonymous accounts.

## What to do now
1. **Terminate all contact across every digital and physical channel immediately.** 
   This includes phone calls, text messages, emails, social media interactions, letters, and in-person visits. 
   - Block the protected person's number and profiles if doing so prevents accidental outgoing contact.
   - Deactivate or log out of shared accounts or apps where communication might occur.

2. **Read the entire order to identify specific prohibited actions and mandatory requirements.** 
   Locate the stay-away distance (often 100 yards or 300 feet), specific locations you must avoid (homes, workplaces, schools), and the expiration date. 
   - Note if the order requires the immediate surrender of firearms or ammunition to local law enforcement.
   - Identify the date, time, and location of the next court hearing.

3. **Secure a physical and digital copy of the paperwork and record the time of service.** 
   Keep the original documents in a safe location and save a PDF or photograph to a secure cloud drive. 
   - Document the exact time and method by which you were notified (e.g., served by a sheriff, received via certified mail).

4. **Inform household members and close associates that they must not contact the person on your behalf.** 
   Explicitly state that "helping" by sending a message or checking in can result in your arrest for a third-party contact violation.

5. **Consult an attorney or public defender before filing any response or attending the hearing.** 
   **Anything you say in a civil protective order hearing can be used against you in a concurrent or future criminal case.** 
   - Legal counsel should draft your official response to avoid self-incrimination.
   - Ask your lawyer to request a "conduct-only" or "limited" modification if you have unavoidable needs, such as child custody exchanges.

6. **Follow the firearms and ammunition surrender protocols if the order includes them.** 
   Most states and federal conditions require you to relinquish all firearms to a designated law enforcement agency or a licensed dealer within a very short window (often 24–48 hours). 
   - Obtain and keep the official receipt for any surrendered items to prove compliance to the court.

7. **Notify your probation or supervised release officer if the restriction is a condition of your supervision.** 
   Failure to report a new no-contact order or a contact-related incident to your officer is often a separate violation of your release terms.

8. **Use only court-approved methods for essential logistics like property retrieval or child visitation.** 
   If the order does not explicitly allow a specific method of contact for these needs, do not improvise. 
   - Request that your attorney arrange for a "civil standby," where a police officer monitors you while you retrieve essential belongings.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve the underlying relationship dispute or "clear your name" today.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether you will fight the order permanently; focus only on current compliance.
- You do not need to gather a complete lifetime of evidence tonight; preserve what is currently on your devices and wait for legal guidance.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a restrictive order is often a high-stress event that can cause panic or a desire to fix the situation immediately. It is common to feel that a quick explanation will resolve the issue, but in the legal system, silence and distance are your most effective tools for stabilization. Following the order strictly now does not mean you agree with the allegations; it means you are protecting your legal standing for the future.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for individuals served with a civil or criminal no-contact order, restraining order, or protective order in the United States. It does not cover international law or long-term legal defense strategies.

## Important note
This information is not legal advice. Restraining orders and no-contact conditions carry criminal penalties for violations, including mandatory jail time in many jurisdictions. Rules vary significantly by state and between civil and criminal courts. If you are unsure of a specific term, assume the strictest possible interpretation until a lawyer confirms otherwise.
