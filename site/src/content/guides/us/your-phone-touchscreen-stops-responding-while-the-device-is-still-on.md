# What to do if… your phone touchscreen stops responding while the device is still on (USA)

## Short answer
Do not factory reset the phone while access matters. First use another device to reach the account, ticket, code, or file you need; then try only low-risk phone steps such as a force restart or non-touch input.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t factory reset the phone to “fix it” while you still need data, codes, tickets, photos, or app access from it.
- Don’t keep guessing passcodes or repeatedly tapping at random; you may trigger lockouts, emergency shortcuts, or more failed sign-in attempts.
- Don’t sign out of Apple, Google, email, banking, or password-manager accounts on other devices unless you know you can sign back in without this phone.
- Don’t approve repairs, trade-in, recycling, or replacement until you have written down what you still need from the phone and what accounts depend on it.
- Don’t press hard on the screen, heat it, charge it unattended, or keep using it if the battery looks swollen, hot, leaking, or physically damaged.

## What to do now
1. **Name the single urgent access problem.**
   - Examples: a 2FA code, an authenticator prompt, a boarding pass, a bank approval, a contact, a work login, a photo, or a document.
   - Deal with that one item first instead of trying to solve the whole phone problem at once.

2. **Move the urgent task to another device.**
   - On a laptop, tablet, or borrowed phone, sign in to your primary email, password manager, cloud storage, bank website, airline or ticket account, or work portal.
   - Use web versions where possible instead of the broken phone app.
   - Check for backup codes, a trusted second device already signed in, a security key, email verification, or account recovery options you already set up.

3. **Keep your main sign-in routes working.**
   - Confirm that you can still access your primary email and password manager from another device.
   - If you suspect someone else has access to your accounts, change the email password from a working device and update recovery options there first.
   - Do not remove this phone from Apple, Google, banking, work, or authenticator settings until you have another working sign-in method.

4. **Use lost-device tools only if there is a loss or access risk.**
   - For an iPhone, use iCloud Find Devices from another device if the phone is lost, stolen, or at risk; Lost Mode and erase are separate options.
   - For Android, Google Find Hub may be able to find, secure, or erase the phone if it has power, is online, is signed in to the Google Account, and Find Hub is enabled.
   - Do not erase the phone unless protecting the data is more important than losing local data and possibly losing access paths.

5. **Try a force restart.**
   - For most recent iPhones: quickly press Volume Up, quickly press Volume Down, then hold the Side button until the Apple logo appears.
   - For Android: use the official restart or force-restart instructions for your exact model. A common pattern is holding Power and Volume Down, but button combinations vary.
   - After restart, stop and extract the urgent item first if touch works even briefly.

6. **Try non-touch control only if your setup supports it.**
   - Android phones may accept a USB mouse through a USB-OTG adapter; if a pointer appears, use it to unlock, copy the urgent item, or change sign-in settings.
   - If you already enabled voice control, switch access, an external keyboard, or other accessibility control, use that already-configured method.
   - Do not spend a long time buying adapters or testing accessories before you have checked web, email, cloud, carrier, and backup-code routes.

7. **If SMS codes matter, contact your carrier from another device.**
   - Ask about moving your number to a working SIM or eSIM.
   - Ask how to protect the account with a PIN, passcode, number lock, or port-out protection if available.
   - After your number works on another device, update important accounts so they do not depend only on SMS to the broken phone.

8. **If you get access for even a minute, copy only what matters first.**
   - Save the ticket, code, contact, photo, document, or approval you need to email, cloud storage, or another trusted device.
   - Check that contacts, photos, notes, and documents are syncing before the screen fails again.
   - Add at least one backup sign-in route where the account supports it, such as backup codes, another trusted device, an authenticator on a second device, or a security key.

9. **Prepare for repair or replacement without giving away access.**
   - Keep proof of purchase, warranty information, and a short symptom note: the phone is on, buttons work, touch does not respond, and access matters.
   - Ask the manufacturer, retailer, carrier, or repair shop what they will do before you hand over the phone, especially whether they may erase it.
   - In the USA, written warranty terms and state implied-warranty rules may matter, but the exact remedy can vary by state and seller.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to switch phone brands, change carriers, or rebuild your whole security setup.
- You do not need to diagnose whether the failure is hardware, software, water damage, or a screen connector before you stabilise access.
- You do not need to erase, trade in, or replace the phone until you have protected accounts and copied the urgent item where possible.

## Important reassurance
The phone screen is not the only path to many accounts and files. Once your email, password manager, cloud storage, carrier number, or backup codes work from another device, the repair decision becomes less urgent.

## Scope note
These are first steps to preserve access, avoid lockouts, and reduce data loss. Later repair, warranty, carrier, workplace, banking, or account-recovery decisions may need help from the relevant provider or a qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical diagnostic, security, or professional advice. Device models, account settings, repair practices, warranties, and carrier processes vary; pause before any step that may erase data or remove a sign-in method.
