# What to do if… a manager or supervisor makes you feel uncomfortable or unsafe during one-to-one meetings (USA)

## Short answer
For the next meeting request, move toward a safer, more work-focused setup: ask for an agenda, suggest a visible workplace setting or remote call, and consider involving another person. You do not have to decide today whether to make a formal report.

## Do not do these things
- Do not meet alone in a secluded place just to avoid awkwardness.
- Do not agree to an after-hours or personal meeting if you feel pressured or unsafe.
- Do not confront the manager alone behind closed doors if that feels unsafe.
- Do not delete existing messages or invitations in a rush if keeping them is safe.
- Do not make a rushed job decision while panicked unless leaving is necessary for immediate safety.
- Do not keep sensitive notes only on a work device or work account if that could reduce your privacy.

## What to do now
1. **Change the setup for the next one-to-one.**
   - Ask for a written agenda.
   - Suggest a visible workplace setting, a remote call, or another arrangement that feels safer.
   - Ask to keep the meeting within your usual work hours.

2. **If it feels safe, send one brief, work-focused message.**
   - Example: “Please send an agenda for the one-to-one. I would like to keep the meeting focused on work priorities and use [meeting room or video call] during my usual work hours.”
   - If personal messages continue, you can say: “Please keep messages to work channels and work topics.”
   - If contacting the manager directly does not feel safe, skip this step and use a support route instead.

3. **Consider adding a buffer.**
   - Ask whether another colleague, representative, or support person can join.
   - If you need to reschedule while you arrange this, keep the message brief.

4. **Choose one support route if that feels safe.**
   - Check whether your employer has an anti-harassment policy.
   - Consider contacting one person other than the manager, such as another supervisor, HR, a union representative if applicable, or a designated workplace contact.
   - You can ask for information about options without deciding immediately whether to make a formal complaint.

5. **Keep only a simple private note if you may want it later.**
   - If it is safe, consider keeping existing messages and a brief factual note of what happened somewhere private.

6. **Use specialist support if the situation involves sexual coercion, threats, assault, or abuse.**
   - RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline offers 24/7 confidential support by phone at **800-656-HOPE** and through online chat. Its official hotline page explains privacy details.

7. **Get outside information if you need it.**
   - The U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission can provide information about workplace harassment and possible next steps.
   - You do not need to decide immediately whether to file anything, but filing deadlines can apply, so getting information promptly may matter.

8. **Treat immediate danger as urgent.**
   - Go to a safer place if you can.
   - Call **911** if you need immediate police, fire department, or ambulance assistance.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to make a formal workplace complaint.
- Deciding whether to contact an outside agency.
- Writing a long account of everything that happened.
- Telling multiple coworkers.
- Making a final decision about leaving the job, unless leaving is needed for immediate safety.

## Important reassurance
Feeling uneasy is enough reason to slow the situation down and change the meeting conditions. You do not need to prove what the manager intended before asking for a safer, more professional setup or seeking support.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about workplace reporting, outside processes, or legal advice may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Prioritize immediate safety and choose the support options that feel safest for you. You do not have to confront anyone today. If you may want to contact an outside agency, getting information promptly can matter because filing deadlines may apply.
