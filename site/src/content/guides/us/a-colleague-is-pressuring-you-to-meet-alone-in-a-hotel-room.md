# What to do if… a colleague is pressuring you to meet alone in a hotel room (USA)

## Short answer
Do not go to the hotel room. Move toward a public, staffed place, such as the lobby or front desk, or keep your room door locked if you are already inside. Contact hotel staff, a trusted person, or a workplace contact when it feels safe to do so.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to a private room meeting because you feel you need to be polite or protect your career.
- Do not keep explaining or replying. Use a short refusal only if it feels safe.
- Do not open your room door if you do not want to.
- Do not confront the person alone.
- Do not feel pressured to decide tonight whether to report the situation formally.
- Do not delete messages in the moment if keeping them feels safe and manageable.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause.** Go to the hotel lobby, the front desk, or another public, staffed area. If you are already in your room, lock and latch the door and call the front desk.
2. **End the private-meeting request.** A short message is enough if replying feels safe: “I am not meeting in a hotel room.” You can stop responding after that.
3. **Ask hotel staff for practical help.** Say: “Another guest is pressuring me to meet privately. I want help staying separate.” Ask what options are available, such as a room change, help with unwanted calls, staff awareness, or an escort to a public area.
4. **Contact someone you trust.** Tell them where you are and ask them to stay on the phone, meet you in the lobby, or check in with you.
5. **Contact a workplace route when you are ready.** You can choose a manager, HR contact, travel contact, security team, or ethics line that feels usable. Say: “A colleague is pressuring me to meet alone in a hotel room. I said no. I need help staying separate for the rest of the trip.”
6. **Keep necessary contact public.** For the rest of the trip, ask for group settings, public meeting areas, separate transport, or another person present.
7. **Save only what feels manageable.** If you want to, keep the messages or make a brief note of what happened. You do not need to build a case tonight.
8. **Use emergency help if needed.** If you are in immediate danger or need urgent police, fire-department, or ambulance assistance, call **911**. You can also ask hotel staff to call.
9. **Use confidential specialist support if it would help.** If sexual contact happened without your consent or you are unsure what happened, contact RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline at **800-656-4673**, text **HOPE** to **64673**, or use its online chat.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to make a formal workplace complaint or contact an outside agency.
- You do not need to confront the person or prove what they intended.
- You do not need to gather perfect evidence.
- You do not need to continue the trip exactly as planned.
- You do not need to make career decisions right now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling frozen, uncertain, polite, or worried about work consequences is understandable. You do not owe anyone a private meeting, a detailed explanation, or another reply. The immediate priority is a safer pause and support around you.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about workplace processes, medical care, specialist support, or reporting may need help from an appropriate professional or support service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Available workplace routes and hotel procedures can vary. If you are in immediate danger or need urgent police, fire-department, or ambulance assistance, call 911.
