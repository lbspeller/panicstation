# What to do if… you are told you must attend a first court appearance and you cannot get legal advice in time (USA)

## Short answer
Attend the hearing as scheduled and request court-appointed counsel immediately upon your case being called. If a lawyer is not provided on the spot, ask for a "continuance" to obtain counsel and enter a "Not Guilty" plea to preserve your rights if the court requires a response.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t skip the court date because you lack a lawyer; this typically results in a bench warrant for your arrest.
- Don’t speak to the prosecutor or District Attorney (DA) about the facts of your case, even if they approach you in the hallway.
- Don’t plead "Guilty" or "No Contest" just to get the hearing over with or to get out of custody faster.
- Don’t explain your side of the story or argue evidence to the judge; first appearances are for rights and release, not trial.
- Don’t waive your right to a lawyer or sign "waiver of counsel" forms unless a lawyer has reviewed them with you.
- Don’t assume the "court clerk" or "bailiff" can give you legal advice; they are prohibited from doing so.

## What to do now
1. **Bring all paperwork and physical identification to the courthouse early.**  
   Pack your summons, citation, bond paperwork, and a photo ID, and ensure you know the exact courtroom or department number.

2. **Check in with the courtroom deputy or clerk and state that you are unrepresented.**  
   **Say:** "I am here for my appearance, I do not have a lawyer, and I am requesting court-appointed counsel." Ask if there is a "Financial Affidavit" or "Indigency Form" you must fill out right now to qualify for a Public Defender.

3. **Request a "Continuance to Seek Counsel" the moment the judge calls your name.**  
   **Say:** "Your Honor, I am requesting a continuance so I can obtain legal advice, and I am requesting the court appoint counsel to represent me." 

4. **Enter a plea of "Not Guilty" if the judge insists the hearing proceed.**  
   **This is a procedural placeholder that allows your future lawyer to review evidence; you can always change this plea later after receiving advice.**

5. **Provide only basic biographical information for bail or release considerations.**  
   **If the judge asks about your background to determine release, stick to facts: your address, how long you have lived there, your employment status, and any caregiving duties.** Do not mention anything about the alleged crime during this discussion.

6. **State clearly if you need an interpreter or a disability accommodation.**  
   **Say:** "I need an interpreter for [Language]" or "I have a disability and require a [specific accommodation] to participate." In federal court, ask for the "Access Coordinator."

7. **Request a copy of the "Complaint" or "Information" and your next court date before leaving.**  
   **Ensure you have a physical or digital record of the next appearance time and the specific charges filed against you.**

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to prepare a defense, witnesses, or evidence for this hearing.
- You do **not** need to decide if you want a trial or a plea deal today.
- You do **not** need to have the money for a private lawyer if you are applying for a Public Defender.
- You do **not** need to respond to the "merits" or "truth" of the police report right now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed by the speed and terminology of a courtroom. Thousands of people attend first appearances without lawyers every day. The system is designed to handle this by appointing counsel; your only job is to make that request clearly on the record and avoid making statements about the case.

## Scope note
This guide addresses the immediate stabilization of a first court appearance (arraignment or initial appearance) where counsel is not yet present. It does not cover trial strategy, sentencing, or long-term legal planning.

## Important note
Procedures vary by state and between state and federal systems. In some jurisdictions, a lawyer may be provided for the bail hearing only; in others, you may have to wait for a subsequent date. If you are confused by a judge's question, it is acceptable to say: "I don't understand the legal consequence of that question and would like to speak to an attorney before answering."
