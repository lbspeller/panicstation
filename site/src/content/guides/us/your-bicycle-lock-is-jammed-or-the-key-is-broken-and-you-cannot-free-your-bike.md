# What to do if… your bicycle lock is jammed or the key is broken and you cannot free your bike (USA)

## Short answer
Get yourself to a safer pause point first, then gather proof that the bike is yours and ask the site owner, security, campus police, or a legitimate mobile locksmith about a documented lock-cut.

## Do not do these things
- Do not start cutting, grinding, or breaking the lock in public without permission; it can injure you and can look like theft.
- Do not keep forcing the key or dials once they bind; a bent key, snapped key, or damaged cylinder can make the lock harder to remove.
- Do not leave the bike unsecured while you go looking for tools.
- Do not accept pressured help from strangers with tools if you feel unsure or unsafe.
- Do not post private information publicly, such as your full phone number, if you leave a note.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause point.** Stand somewhere well lit and visible, such as a station lobby, security desk, staffed building entrance, or busy public area. If you feel threatened or there is immediate danger, call **911**.

2. **Try only gentle, quick checks for 2–3 minutes.**
   - **Combination lock:** apply light tension to the shackle or chain while turning each dial slowly; check that every dial is fully seated on a number.
   - **Keyed lock:** make sure the key is fully inserted; use only a gentle wiggle and stop before the key bends.
   - **Frozen or dirty lock:** warm it with your hand or shelter it from wind if you can do that safely; do not use flame.

3. **Create quick proof that the bike is yours.**
   - Take photos of the full bicycle, the lock, what it is attached to, and any unique features.
   - Record the exact location and time.
   - Look for the serial or frame number if you can reach it safely; it is often under the bottom bracket, but it may be elsewhere. Do not flip the bike over if that would damage it or draw concern.
   - Pull up anything you already have: a purchase receipt, registration record, Bike Index entry, campus registration, repair invoice, insurance record, or photos of you with the bike.

4. **Contact the site owner before arranging a cut.**
   - **Campus or university property:** call campus police, transportation services, facilities, or the posted bike-program contact. Many campus programs require photo ID and proof of ownership or registration before cutting a lock.
   - **Transit station or managed bike parking:** go to the station agent, customer service desk, parking operator, or security desk; check posted signs for the bike-parking contact.
   - **Private property:** ask building security, property management, the store manager, or workplace facilities where lock removal is allowed and what proof they need.

5. **Use a documented lock-cut option if the lock cannot be opened.**
   - If the site has a lock-cut process, follow their ID, proof, location, and presence rules.
   - If the site will not help and local rules allow it, call a mobile locksmith and ask before they come what proof of ownership and site permission they need for a bike lock in public.
   - Ask for the locksmith’s business name, price range, and payment method before agreeing.

6. **If you must leave the bike temporarily, reduce misunderstanding.**
   - Tell staff, security, or the property owner that the lock failed and that you are arranging help.
   - Consider leaving a short note such as “Owner returning today for lock issue” with only the contact detail you are comfortable sharing.
   - Take a final photo showing the bike, lock, note if used, and location.

7. **Choose a safe way home now.**
   - Use public transit, rideshare, taxi, a friend, or another safe ride.
   - If it is late, isolated, or you are tired, it is usually safer to go home and return with proper help in daylight than to struggle with the lock in public.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to replace the lock, upgrade security, or buy new tools.
- You do not need to diagnose the lock brand or model tonight.
- You do not need to file a theft report unless the bike is missing, damaged by someone else, or you believe a crime happened.
- You do not need to prove anything to bystanders; let staff, security, campus police, or the locksmith handle legitimacy checks.

## Important reassurance
This is a stressful transport failure, not a personal failure. The safest path is usually to document the bike, use the property or campus process, and get home another way rather than turning a stuck lock into a public confrontation or injury risk.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, such as replacement keys, lock replacement, bike registration, insurance, or a police report if something was stolen, can be handled after you are safe and calmer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, safety, transportation, locksmith, insurance, or professional advice. Rules and lock-cut policies vary by city, campus, transit agency, property owner, and locksmith.
