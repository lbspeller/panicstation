# What to do if… someone claims they are underage after sending explicit images and now demands money or favours (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all contact immediately, do not pay anything, and do not provide any further images or information. Secure your accounts, preserve evidence of the threat, and use official reporting tools like IC3 or NCMEC to address the extortion.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money, gift cards, cryptocurrency, or additional images to the person.
- Do not attempt to negotiate or argue about the person's age in messages.
- Do not delete the message history or account details until you have captured screenshots.
- Do not forward or share the explicit images with anyone else, including friends or family, to "verify" them.
- Do not call any phone number provided by the person claiming to be a parent, lawyer, or police officer.
- Do not agree to any "last favour" or meet-up in exchange for the images being deleted.

## What to do now
1. **Cease all communication with the individual immediately without a final warning.**
   - Do not explain why you are stopping.
   - Do not respond to threats of exposure or police involvement.
2. **Capture screenshots of the entire conversation, the offender’s profile URL, and any payment instructions.**
   - Ensure the screenshots show the account name, date, and specific threats made.
   - Save these to a secure location that is not the app where the conversation occurred.
3. **Report the account and the messages directly to the platform where the incident happened.**
   - Use the specific "Sextortion," "Blackmail," or "Non-consensual sexual imagery" reporting options if available.
   - Most major platforms are now required under the Take It Down Act to process removal requests within 48 hours.
4. **Secure your social media and email accounts by changing passwords and enabling multi-factor authentication.**
   - Set all profiles to private to prevent the person from accessing your friend list or contact information.
   - Review your "logged in devices" list and log out of any sessions you do not recognize.
5. **Contact your bank or payment provider immediately if you have already sent money or shared financial details.**
   - Tell them you are the victim of a fraud or extortion scheme.
   - Request a stop-payment or account freeze to prevent further unauthorized transactions.
6. **Report the extortion to the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) or the CyberTipline if a minor is involved.**
   - If the images involve a minor (or someone claiming to be one), file a report at report.cybertip.org.
   - If you are an adult being extorted, file a complaint at www.ic3.gov.
7. **Use the Take It Down tool if you are under 18 or the images depict you as a minor.**
   - Visit takeitdown.ncmec.org to create a "hash" of the image, which helps platforms identify and block it without you having to upload the actual photo to a person.
   - Adults can use StopNCII.org for similar hashing and removal services for non-consensual intimate imagery.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the person's claim of being underage is true right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to tell your employer or family today.
- You do not need to have a complete file of evidence before contacting a support service.

## Important reassurance
Sextortion and "minor-claim" scams are designed to trigger intense shame and panic to force you to act quickly. This reaction is a normal response to a high-pressure threat, but it does not mean the threats will be carried out if you stop responding. Most scammers move on once they realize no payment or further engagement is possible.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate stabilization steps for handling online extortion and underage claims. It does not provide legal defense or long-term privacy management.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Federal and state laws regarding non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) and extortion are complex and updated frequently, including the enforcement of the Take It Down Act in 2026. If you feel you are in immediate physical danger, call 911.
