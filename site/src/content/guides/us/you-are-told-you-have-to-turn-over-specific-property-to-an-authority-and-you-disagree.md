# What to do if… you are told you have to turn over specific property to an authority and you disagree (USA)

## Short answer
Do not physically resist the handover, but explicitly state that you do not consent to a voluntary search or seizure. Ask for the specific legal authority (such as a warrant, subpoena, or court order) and demand a detailed written receipt for every item taken.

## Do not do these things
- Do not physically struggle, hide the property, or attempt to pull items back once an official has asserted authority.
- Do not delete, wipe, or alter any data on electronic devices if a seizure is imminent.
- Do not volunteer explanations, passwords, or PINs without consulting a lawyer first.
- Do not sign any document that describes the handover as "voluntary" or "consensual" if you do not agree.
- Do not assume a verbal promise to return the property is legally binding.
- Do not argue about the underlying merits of the case with the officer on the scene.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the official and their agency.**  
   **Ask for the name, badge number, and agency of the individual demanding the property.** Record this information immediately along with any case or incident number provided.

2. **Clarify the legal basis for the demand.**  
   **Ask if the official has a warrant, a subpoena, or a court order that requires the handover.** If they state the action is voluntary, clearly say: "I do not consent to this search or seizure."

3. **Request to view and document any legal paperwork.**  
   **Read the warrant or order to see which specific items are listed and if there are geographic or time limits.** If it is safe to do so, take a photograph of the document or write down the issuing court and the date.

4. **Insist on a comprehensive written receipt.**  
   **Demand a property seizure receipt that lists each item with unique identifiers such as serial numbers, IMEI numbers for phones, and a description of the item's current physical condition.** Ensure the receipt is signed and dated by the seizing official.

5. **State your lack of consent for the record.**  
   **Clearly state, "I am providing this property under protest and I do not consent to this seizure or any subsequent search of the items."** Do not elaborate further or answer questions about the property's contents.

6. **Protect digital privacy by invoking the Fifth Amendment.**  
   **If asked for a password or PIN to a device, state that you wish to remain silent and want to speak to an attorney before providing any access credentials.** Do not attempt to guess or explain why you are refusing.

7. **Follow facility-specific protocols if in a secure setting.**  
   **If the seizure occurs in a jail or prison, ask for a copy of the formal inventory and the facility policy governing property confiscation.** Request the necessary forms to file an administrative grievance or remedy regarding the seized items.

8. **Note the exact time and location of the seizure.**  
   **Write down the circumstances of the handover, including what was said by the official and whether anyone else witnessed the interaction.** This documentation is vital for a future legal challenge.

9. **Monitor deadlines on any forfeiture notices.**  
   **Keep any document titled "Notice of Seizure" or "Notice of Intent to Forfeit" and highlight the specific deadline for filing a claim.** Missing this window often leads to permanent loss of the property.

10. **Consult a lawyer regarding a motion for return of property.**  
   **Provide all documentation to an attorney to determine if a motion under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 41(g) or a similar state-level process is appropriate for your jurisdiction.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove the property is legal or yours during the interaction.
- You do not need to decide whether to file a lawsuit in the next few minutes.
- You can wait to provide a detailed history of the property until you have legal representation.

## Important reassurance
The moment of seizure is often the most stressful part of a legal process, and feeling overwhelmed is a standard response. By securing a receipt and clearly stating your lack of consent, you are creating the necessary paper trail that allows the legal system to address the disagreement later.

## Scope note
This guide covers initial stabilization and documentation steps during an unwanted property turnover. It does not provide legal strategy for long-term recovery or address specific state-by-state variations in forfeiture law.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and is not legal advice. The rights and procedures involving property seizure are subject to complex federal and state statutes. If you are facing a demand for property, prioritize your physical safety and seek professional legal counsel immediately.
