# What to do if… you hit a pothole and the steering starts shaking or pulling after you have pulled over safely (USA)

## Short answer
Do not merge back into traffic or attempt to drive at normal speeds. Shaking or pulling indicates mechanical damage to tires, wheels, or suspension that can lead to a sudden loss of control; arrange a professional tow or roadside inspection immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don't increase speed to see if the vibration smooths out.
- Don't drive if you see a bulge, bubble, or cut on the tire sidewall.
- Don't drive if the wheel rim appears bent, flattened, or cracked.
- Don't continue if the steering wheel is no longer centered when driving straight.
- Don't ignore new clunking, grinding, or metallic snapping noises.
- Don't attempt to inspect the underside of the car while parked on a narrow or active highway shoulder.

## What to do now
1. **Activate your hazard flashers and keep them on.**
2. **Prioritize your physical safety from passing traffic.**
   - If you are on a high-speed road or narrow shoulder, stay in the vehicle with your seatbelt on if the shoulder is too narrow to safely exit.
   - If there is a barrier or safe ground well away from the road, exit through the passenger side and move to that area.
3. **Perform a visual check of the tires and wheels from a safe position.**
   - Look for tires that appear lower than the others or completely flat.
   - Look for "bubbles" or bulges on the tire sidewalls (inner or outer).
   - Look for visible bends in the metal or alloy rim of the wheel.
   - Check for any fresh fluid (oil or coolant) dripping from the front area.
4. **Call 911 if you are stranded in a hazardous position.**
   - If your vehicle has stalled in a live lane or you are stopped in a "blind spot" where you are at risk of being struck, request emergency assistance for traffic control.
5. **Contact roadside assistance or a towing service.**
   - State clearly that the vehicle has "sustained impact damage from a pothole" and is "exhibiting steering instability."
   - Request transport to a certified repair facility or tire specialist for a steering and suspension safety inspection.
6. **Note the location and time for potential claims.**
   - Identify the street name, nearest intersection, or mile marker.
   - Take photos of the damage and the pothole only if it is physically safe to do so without entering the roadway.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the issue is alignment, a bent rim, or a broken suspension component at the roadside.
- You do not need to file a report with the city, county, or state road department until you are in a safe, permanent location.
- You do not need to decide on an insurance claim today; wait for a professional repair estimate first.

## Important reassurance
Potholes can exert thousands of pounds of force on a vehicle's front end. Shaking or pulling is a common and serious symptom of internal tire failure or suspension damage that is not always visible to the naked eye. Choosing to tow the vehicle rather than "limping it home" is the standard safety protocol to prevent a secondary accident.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for drivers who have already pulled over. It does not cover repair procedures, insurance litigation, or long-term maintenance.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance only and does not replace the judgment of emergency responders or certified mechanics. Vehicle steering and suspension systems are safety-critical; if any doubt exists regarding the structural integrity of the vehicle, do not drive it.
