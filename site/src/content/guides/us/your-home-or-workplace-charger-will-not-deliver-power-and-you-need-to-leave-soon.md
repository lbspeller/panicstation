# What to do if… your home or workplace charger will not deliver power and you need to leave soon (USA)

## Short answer
Inspect the equipment for heat or damage, then switch to a known-safe secondary charger or outlet while enabling your device's maximum power-saving mode.

## Do not do these things
- Do not continue using any charger, cable, or outlet that is hot to the touch, sparking, buzzing, or emitting a burning smell.
- Do not attempt to force a plug into a loose or resistant outlet.
- Do not use metal tools (like needles or paperclips) to clean out a charging port.
- Do not use a hairdryer or external heat source to dry a device displaying a moisture warning.
- Do not attempt to reset a circuit breaker more than once if it immediately trips again.
- Do not dispose of lithium-ion batteries or power banks in standard trash or recycling bins.

## What to do now
1. **Unplug the equipment and check for physical hazards (10 seconds).** Inspect the charger body, cable, and wall outlet for discoloration, melting, frayed wires, or a burnt odor. **If any signs of heat or damage are present, stop using the equipment immediately.**
2. **Enable maximum battery-saving settings on your device.** Turn on "Battery Saver" or "Low Power Mode" to restrict background activity. **Reduce screen brightness to the lowest usable level and disable GPS, Bluetooth, and Wi-Fi if they are not immediately required for your departure.**
3. **Isolate the failure by swapping one component at a time.**
   - Plug a known-good cable into the charger.
   - Plug the charger into a different wall outlet (avoid using the same power strip or surge protector).
   - Use a different manufacturer-approved or safety-certified (UL, ETL, or CSA) power adapter.
4. **Check the device charging port for debris.** Use a bright light to look for lint or dust inside the port. **If debris is visible, gently remove it using a soft, non-conductive tool such as a wooden or plastic toothpick.**
5. **Reset GFCI outlets in wet-prone areas (bathrooms, kitchens, or outdoors).** Look for a "RESET" button on the outlet itself or an upstream outlet in the same room. **Ensure your hands are dry and the area is free of moisture before firmly pressing the RESET button until it clicks.**
6. **Address dead outlets at home via the circuit breaker panel.** If multiple outlets are dead, locate your electrical panel and look for a breaker in the "OFF" or "TRIPPED" (middle) position. **Flip the breaker fully to OFF and then back to ON one time; if it trips again, leave it off and seek a different power source.**
7. **Isolate and report faulty equipment at a workplace.** Move to a different workstation or communal charging area immediately. **Affix a "DO NOT USE" label to the suspected faulty outlet or charger and notify your Facilities or IT department according to your company’s 2026 safety reporting protocols.**
8. **Activate a secondary power plan for your transit.**
   - Connect to a trusted portable power bank.
   - Use a vehicle-based 12V charger once you begin your journey.
   - If using a power bank, monitor it for unusual heat or swelling; **if it feels hot or looks distorted, disconnect it and move it away from flammable materials.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine why the original charger failed before leaving.
- You do not need to purchase a permanent replacement charger until you are in a stable location.
- You do not need to perform a full technical diagnostic of your device's battery health.

## Important reassurance
Electrical components like cables and adapters can fail without warning due to internal wear. Switching to a secondary, certified power source is a standard safety procedure and does not mean your device is permanently broken. Panic is a common response when a critical tool fails before a deadline; focusing on the next safe charging step will resolve the immediate power need.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for charging failures and power preservation. It does not cover internal device repairs, electrical panel wiring, or long-term battery maintenance.

## Important note
This information is for general safety and stabilization only and does not constitute professional electrical advice. Always prioritize OSHA workplace safety standards and manufacturer-specific instructions. If you encounter evidence of an electrical fire or smoke, evacuate the area and call 911. Lithium-ion batteries must be disposed of at designated hazardous waste collection sites or through certified programs like Call2Recycle, as they pose a fire risk in standard waste streams.
