# What to do if… you notice someone taking close-up photos of your car’s plates or your home and you feel targeted (USA)

## Short answer
Create immediate physical distance by moving to a secure, locked location and call **911** if you feel an active threat exists or the person is approaching you. For non-immediate concerns, contact your local police department's 10-digit non-emergency line to file a suspicious activity report.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the individual or block their path.
- Do not exit your home or vehicle to "get a better look" if you are currently unobserved.
- Do not post the footage to public social media groups with your specific address or full plate number visible.
- Do not touch or remove any unfamiliar objects found on your vehicle, such as tags or tracking devices; wait for police guidance.
- Do not ignore the situation if the behavior is repetitive or if the individual follows you when you leave.

## What to do now
1. **Secure your immediate surroundings and create distance.** If you are outside, go into your home, a shop, or a well-lit public building and lock the doors immediately. If you are in your vehicle, keep the windows up and doors locked, and drive to a police station or a busy gas station rather than your own driveway.
2. **Determine if the situation requires 911 or a non-emergency report.** **Call 911 immediately if the person is actively following you, attempting to open your doors, or acting in a threatening manner.** If the person has left the scene or is keeping their distance, use your local police department’s 10-digit non-emergency number or 311 if your city uses it specifically for police reports.
3. **Document the incident from a safe, obscured position.** **Capture a description of the individual and any vehicle they are using, including the make, model, color, and license plate number.** Do not let the person see you taking photos or videos, as this may escalate the interaction.
4. **Preserve electronic evidence immediately.** **Download and save any footage from doorbell cameras, dash cams, or CCTV systems covering the time of the event.** Store these files in a separate folder or cloud drive to prevent them from being overwritten by the system's loop recording.
5. **Request a specific incident or case number from law enforcement.** **Ensure the dispatcher records the event as "suspicious surveillance" or "casing" so that a paper trail is established.** Ask for the officer's name or badge number if they respond to your location.
6. **Limit external visibility and access to your property.** **Close all blinds, curtains, and garage doors, and ensure all secondary entries like gates or sheds are locked.** If possible, move your vehicle into a garage or a spot visible from a well-lit window.
7. **Notify other members of your household or a trusted neighbor.** **Instruct everyone in the home not to answer the door to unknown individuals for the remainder of the day.** If a neighbor has cameras, ask them to check their footage for the individual's arrival or departure route.

## What can wait
- Determining the specific motive or identity of the person.
- Installing new permanent security cameras or alarm systems.
- Contacting your insurance provider, unless physical damage is discovered.
- Reviewing 2026 real estate photography laws (such as CA AB 723) to see if the activity was a legitimate professional survey.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard physiological response to feel a heightened sense of alertness or fear when your privacy is interrupted. Not all photography indicates a criminal threat—insurance adjusters, real estate agents, and local government surveyors often document properties—but your priority is safety until the situation is verified. Taking these steps creates a record that protects you regardless of the person's intent.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute encounter with suspicious surveillance. It does not cover long-term stalking investigations or legal actions for harassment.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Jurisdictional laws regarding photography in public spaces vary, but your right to report felt threats to law enforcement is universal across the USA.
