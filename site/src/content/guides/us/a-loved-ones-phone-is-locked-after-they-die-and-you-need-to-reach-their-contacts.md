# What to do if… a loved one’s phone is locked after they die and you need to reach their contacts (USA)

## Short answer
Do not keep guessing the passcode or reset the phone. Keep the device and mobile line intact where practical, then use visible lock-screen information, incoming caller details, the carrier’s deceased-account process, and official Apple or Google account-data request routes where available.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep guessing the passcode. Repeated attempts can cause longer lockouts and may lead to data loss on some devices or configurations.
- Do not factory reset, restore, or erase the phone to try to unlock it. Data stored only on the device may be lost.
- Do not use bypass tools or paid unlocking services. They may be scams, compromise privacy, or damage data.
- Do not cancel or transfer the mobile line before asking the carrier what will happen to the number and voicemail.
- Do not post the person’s private details publicly while trying to find contacts.

## What to do now
1. Keep the phone safe and charged. Avoid changing settings, guessing passcodes, or beginning any reset process.

2. Check the lock screen without trying to unlock the phone.
   - Note any visible incoming caller names, missed-call details, or notification sender names.
   - On an iPhone, check whether Medical ID is available from the lock screen and whether it lists an emergency contact.
   - On some Android phones, emergency information or emergency contacts may be visible from the lock screen if the owner enabled that feature.
   - Use your own phone to contact any trusted person you identify.

3. Keep the mobile line active where practical while you contact the carrier.
   - Note visible incoming caller names or numbers.
   - Ask the carrier whether voicemail should be saved before any transfer or cancellation.
   - Do not open messages, change settings, or try to access voicemail unless you are authorised and already have the required details.

4. Contact the carrier and explain that the account holder has died.
   - Ask what options are available to keep the number active temporarily, transfer the line, preserve voicemail, or avoid losing the number.
   - Ask what documents are needed before making any change.
   - Keep a note of the date, the representative’s name, and any case number.

5. Use official account-data request routes where relevant.
   - For an iPhone, Apple cannot remove the device passcode without erasing the device. If the person named a Legacy Contact, that person can request access to certain Apple Account data using the access key and death certificate. Other Apple Account data requests in the USA may require verified legal documentation or a court order.
   - For a Google Account, immediate family members or representatives can submit a deceased-user request. Google may provide account content in some circumstances after review, but it does not provide passwords or login details.

6. Gather documents without trying to solve every estate issue now.
   - Keep the death certificate and any documents showing your authority available.
   - Ask the carrier or account provider exactly what it requires before sending personal documents.

7. Start a small contact chain without waiting for full phone access.
   - Contact any trusted person identified through visible lock-screen information, incoming calls, paper address books, mail, or an authorised already-signed-in account on another device.
   - Ask one or two trusted people to relay the news to others who knew the person.
   - Keep a simple list of who has been told.

## What can wait
- You do not need to unlock the phone immediately to begin notifying people.
- You do not need to decide today whether to close every account, transfer every service, or complete long-term digital-estate work.
- You do not need to solve every court or estate step before making the first calls, although some official requests may require paperwork later.

## Important reassurance
The locked phone does not need to be fixed immediately. Preserving the device and line while you make a few careful calls is enough for the first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about estate administration, account access, disputes, or valuable digital assets may need specialist help in your state.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Requirements vary by provider, device, account status, and state. Use official carrier and account-provider routes rather than workarounds.
