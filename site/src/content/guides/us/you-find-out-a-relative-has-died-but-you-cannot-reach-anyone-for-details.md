# What to do if… you find out a relative has died but you cannot reach anyone for details (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the news through an independent official source—such as the county coroner, medical examiner, or local police non-emergency line—before taking any financial or travel actions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not post news of the death on social media or notify extended networks until you have official confirmation.
- Do not send money, wire transfers, cryptocurrency, or gift card codes to anyone claiming to handle "immediate arrangements" or "unpaid debts."
- Do not use contact information or links provided in an unsolicited message; look up official agency numbers yourself.
- Do not assume you can receive medical details over the phone; HIPAA regulations allow hospitals to confirm a death but may restrict detailed health history to a verified next-of-kin.
- Do not cash or deposit any Social Security checks or direct deposits that arrive for the month of the death or later.

## What to do now
1. **Record the exact details of the notification.** 
   - Note the name, phone number, or account of the person who told you.
   - Write down the specific location (city and county) where the death is alleged to have occurred.
   - Note any specific facility names mentioned, such as a hospital or nursing home.

2. **Verify the caller's identity if you are being pressured to pay for anything.**
   - Recognize that legitimate government agencies, coroners, and hospitals will not ask for payment via gift cards or wire transfers.
   - Hang up and call the official publicly listed number for the organization they claim to represent.

3. **Call 911 if you believe the person is currently in life-threatening danger.**
   - Use emergency services only if you suspect a medical emergency or welfare crisis is actively occurring right now.

4. **Request a welfare check through the local police non-emergency number.**
   - Call the police department in the city where your relative lived.
   - Explain that you received a report of their death but cannot reach any family at the residence for confirmation.
   - Provide the relative's full name, address, and your relationship.

5. **Contact the County Medical Examiner or Coroner’s Office in the relevant county.**
   - Search online for the "[County Name] Medical Examiner" or "[County Name] Coroner."
   - Ask if they have a record of your relative; they handle cases involving sudden, accidental, or unattended deaths.
   - Ask for the name of the funeral home if the body has already been released.

6. **Call the hospital switchboard in the likely area of the death.**
   - Ask for the "Admitting Office" or "Patient Information."
   - Inquire if the relative is currently a patient or if they have a record of a recent expiration (death) for that individual.
   - If they confirm a death, ask for the contact information of the "Social Services" or "Decedent Affairs" department.

7. **Notify the Social Security Administration (SSA) to stop payments.**
   - Call **1-800-772-1213** (TTY **1-800-325-0778**) between 8:00 a.m. and 7:00 p.m. local time.
   - Provide the deceased person’s Social Security number if you have it; you do not need a death certificate to begin this initial notification.

8. **Identify the funeral home if one has been selected.**
   - Call the funeral home directly using an independently verified number.
   - Ask who is listed as the "authorizing agent" or "legal next-of-kin" so you can coordinate communication with the correct family member.

## What can wait
- You do not need to order certified death copies immediately; the funeral home or the state vital records office will handle this once the death is registered.
- You do not need to make travel arrangements until the location and custody of the body are confirmed.
- You do not need to notify banks, insurers, or the IRS today.
- You do not need to resolve questions about the will or inheritance until a legal death certificate is issued.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of extreme urgency or panic when you cannot reach family members after hearing bad news. Delays in communication are common in the first 24 hours as people process shock or manage logistics. Taking a few minutes to verify facts through official channels prevents the spread of misinformation and protects you from potential scams.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and verification only. It does not cover the legalities of probate, estate administration, or funeral planning, which require a certified death certificate.

## Important note
Procedures for death investigation and record-keeping vary significantly by state and county. If you suspect foul play or a scam, report the incident to the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) or your local law enforcement.
