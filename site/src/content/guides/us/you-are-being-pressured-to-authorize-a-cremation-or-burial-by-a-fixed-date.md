# What to do if… you are being pressured to authorize a cremation or burial by a fixed date (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the funeral home or other provider in writing to pause the planned cremation or burial while it confirms the basis for proceeding. Ask what is scheduled, what authorization it is relying on, and what it needs from the person who may be authorized under the applicable state rules or documents.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign a cremation authorization, burial instruction, or contract if you are unsure whether you are authorized or whether you agree with it.
- Don’t assume that being described as “next of kin” automatically settles who can give instructions. State rules and any relevant documents may matter.
- Don’t rely only on a phone conversation. Follow up using an available written channel and keep a copy.
- Don’t make package, service, or payment decisions under pressure while the authority issue is still unclear.
- Don’t get pulled into a family argument with the provider. State clearly that there is a dispute and ask what process applies.

## What to do now
1. **Send a written request to pause the planned step.**  
   Write: “I am asking you to pause the planned cremation or burial while the person authorized to give instructions is confirmed. Please tell me in writing what is scheduled, the date and time, what authorization or instruction you are relying on, and what you require to pause the process.”

2. **Ask what has already been received or signed.**  
   Ask for copies of any documents you signed. Also ask what authorization, instruction, permit, or release the provider is relying on and what information it can share about the basis for treating someone as authorized.

3. **Ask what documents are needed to clarify authority.**  
   Tell the provider your relationship to the person who died and ask whether it needs identification, proof of relationship, a designated-agent document, written instructions, or another document. Send what you already have, but do not delay the written pause request while gathering paperwork.

4. **If there is disagreement, use the word “dispute.”**  
   Write: “There is a dispute about who is authorized to give disposition instructions. Please tell me what is required to pause the process while this is addressed, and who I should contact urgently if the scheduled date is close.”

5. **If a medical examiner or coroner is involved, ask about release status.**  
   Ask the provider whether the remains have been released, by which office, and whether any further authorization is still needed. If you have the office’s contact details, ask whether it can confirm the release status and the receiving provider.

6. **If the provider says the remains are unclaimed, ask for the specific process in writing.**  
   Ask which agency or rule applies, who set the deadline, what documents are required, and where they must be sent. Write that the family is actively trying to identify the authorized person and ask for confirmation that your request has been logged.

7. **Use funeral-purchasing protections if prices or arrangements are being discussed.**  
   You can ask for price information by telephone. If you visit a funeral home in person, ask for its General Price List. After arrangements are selected, ask for the written itemized statement before paying. A funeral home that offers cremations cannot require you to buy a casket for direct cremation and must make an alternative container available.

8. **Get urgent local help if the provider says it will proceed before the dispute can be addressed.**  
   Contact a local attorney or legal-aid organization and give them the scheduled date and time, the provider’s written response, and any documents you have. You can also contact your state attorney general’s office and ask for the appropriate state consumer-protection or funeral-industry contact.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide service details, obituary wording, flowers, catering, or a package right now.
- You do not need to settle every family disagreement right now.
- You do not need to make non-urgent purchases while you are asking the provider to clarify the basis for proceeding.
- Focus first on the written pause request, the scheduled date and time, and the documents or rules the provider says it is relying on.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to ask for written clarity before an irreversible step. You do not need to solve the whole situation at once: start by asking what is scheduled, what the provider is relying on, and what is required to pause the process.

## Scope note
These are first steps for an urgent deadline. Rules about who may give disposition instructions, how disputes are handled, and how unclaimed remains are treated can vary by state and circumstances, so later decisions may need local legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If cremation or burial may happen imminently despite a genuine dispute, seek urgent local legal help.
