# What to do if… you get a flood of password reset messages for accounts you did not request (USA)

## Short answer
Avoid clicking any links in the messages and secure your primary email account immediately. Use a search within your inbox to locate any legitimate security alerts or transaction confirmations that may be hidden within the flood of automated messages.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click "verify," "unsubscribe," or "reset password" links within any of the suspicious emails.
- Do not approve multi-factor authentication (MFA) push notifications that you did not trigger.
- Do not provide one-time passwords (OTP) or security codes to anyone contacting you by phone or text, even if they claim to be from a security department.
- Do not delete the entire flood of messages instantly without checking for one or two legitimate alerts.
- Do not lower your security settings or disable MFA to stop the notification noise.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate your device from immediate notification pressure.** Silence your phone or close your email tab for 60 seconds to stop the sensory overload.
2. **Secure the primary email account associated with these alerts.** Change your email password to a unique, long phrase that you have never used elsewhere.
3. **Verify and update your email multi-factor authentication (MFA).** Ensure your MFA is set to an authenticator app or a physical security key rather than SMS, and use the "sign out of all other sessions" feature in your security settings.
4. **Audit your email "Rules," "Filters," or "Forwarding" settings.** Look for any new rules that automatically "Mark as Read," "Archive," or "Move to Trash" messages containing words like "confirm," "order," "purchase," or "security."
   - Delete any filter or forwarding address you did not create.
5. **Search the inbox for critical keywords to find the "needle in the haystack."** Use the search bar to look for: `purchase`, `order`, `shipped`, `changed`, `authorized`, `bank`, or `wire`.
   - Focus on financial accounts, primary ID accounts (Apple/Google/Microsoft), and high-value services like PayPal.
6. **Access important accounts directly through official apps or manually typed URLs.** Do not use the links in the reset emails to check your status; go to the service provider's official site yourself.
7. **Report the phishing attempt to federal authorities.** Forward a sample of the reset emails to **reportphishing@apwg.org**.
   - File a report at **ReportFraud.ftc.gov** to help the FTC track the campaign.
8. **Contact your financial institutions if you find evidence of an unauthorized transaction.** Call the number on the back of your physical card or use the official banking app's "Contact Us" feature.
9. **File a report with the FBI if you have lost money or access to a critical account.** Use the Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) at **ic3.gov** for cyber-enabled fraud or theft.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific leak or "data breach" that exposed your email address right now.
- You do not need to respond to every individual reset email or attempt to "fix" every account mentioned in the flood.
- You do not need to close old, unused accounts today; focus only on high-priority accounts (finance, email, primary identity).

## Important reassurance
Receiving a flood of reset messages—often called "subscription bombing"—is a known tactic used to distract users from a single real alert. It usually means your email address is known, not necessarily that your accounts have been breached. By securing your email first and ignoring the links, you maintain control of the situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for digital distraction attacks and potential account takeover attempts. It does not cover long-term identity theft recovery or the forensic investigation of specific data breaches.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal, financial, or professional cybersecurity advice. Digital threats evolve quickly; always prioritize the security instructions provided by your specific service providers (e.g., your bank or email host) and access your accounts only through verified, official channels.
