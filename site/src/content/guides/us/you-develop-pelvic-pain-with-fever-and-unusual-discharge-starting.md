# What to do if… you develop pelvic pain with fever and unusual discharge starting (USA)

## Short answer
Seek immediate medical evaluation at an emergency department or call 911 if you have severe pain, a temperature over 101°F, or might be pregnant. For milder symptoms, obtain a same-day medical appointment to prevent complications.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delay seeking care for more than a few hours once fever and pelvic pain occur together.
- Do not have sexual contact until a clinician has completed an evaluation and provided treatment.
- Do not douche or use internal cleansing products.
- Do not use tampons or menstrual cups; use external pads if needed.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics or medication prescribed for someone else.
- Do not assume you are not pregnant based on your cycle or contraceptive use.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel faint, dizzy, or if the pain is severe.

## What to do now
1. **Assess for emergency symptoms that require an immediate 911 call or ER visit.**
   - Severe or sudden pelvic or lower abdominal pain.
   - Pain that is significantly worse on one side.
   - Pain at the very tip of your shoulder.
   - Oral temperature higher than 101°F (38.3°C).
   - Fainting, extreme lightheadedness, or dizziness.
   - Repeated vomiting or inability to keep fluids down.
   - Heavy vaginal bleeding.
2. **Access same-day medical care if you do not meet emergency criteria.**
   - Contact your OB-GYN or primary care provider for an urgent appointment.
   - Visit an urgent care center or a sexual health / STI clinic.
   - Confirm the facility can perform a pelvic exam and diagnostic testing today.
3. **Take a pregnancy test immediately.**
   - Provide the result to the medical staff.
   - Note that pelvic pain and fever in pregnancy require emergency-level assessment.
4. **Document your symptoms for the clinician.**
   - When the pain and fever began.
   - Your highest recorded temperature.
   - Sensory details of the discharge (color, odor, consistency).
   - Date of your last menstrual period and current birth control method (including IUD).
   - Recent history of pelvic procedures, new sexual partners, or STI exposure.
5. **Isolate the hazard by avoiding sexual contact.**
   - Stop all sexual activity to prevent further irritation or transmission.
   - If you have a regular partner, inform them that you are being evaluated for a possible infection so they can seek testing.
6. **Limit physical activity and monitor vitals.**
   - Rest in a comfortable position.
   - Track your temperature every hour while waiting for care.
   - Stay hydrated with small sips of water.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific cause (such as PID, STI, or other infection) before arriving at the clinic.
- You do not need to contact previous sexual partners until you have a confirmed diagnosis and instructions from your provider.
- You do not need to bring a sample of discharge; the clinician will collect necessary swabs during the exam.

## Important reassurance
Pelvic pain accompanied by fever and unusual discharge is a common reason for urgent medical visits. While these symptoms can be distressing, they are manageable when addressed promptly by medical professionals. Seeking care now is the standard way to resolve the issue and protect your health.

## Scope note
This guide focuses on the first actions for stabilization and accessing appropriate care in the initial hours of symptoms. It does not cover long-term treatment plans, partner notification procedures, or medication management.

## Important note
This information is for first-step stabilization only and is not a substitute for professional medical diagnosis or treatment. Pelvic pain with fever may indicate a serious infection or an ectopic pregnancy, both of which require direct clinical assessment.
