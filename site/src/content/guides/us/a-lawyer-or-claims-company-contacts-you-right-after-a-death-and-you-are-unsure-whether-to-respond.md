# What to do if… a lawyer or claims company contacts you right after a death and you are unsure whether to respond (USA)

## Short answer
Do not share personal data or sign any documents during the initial contact. End the conversation immediately, request all information in writing, and verify the professional’s license through official state directories before responding.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide your Social Security number, bank account details, or copies of identification to "confirm your identity as an heir".
- Do not sign any retainer agreements, fee contracts, or "authorization for representation" forms while feeling pressured.
- Do not pay upfront "processing fees", "inheritance taxes", or "release costs" via wire transfer, Zelle, or gift cards.
- Do not assume a caller is legitimate because they know the deceased person's name, date of death, or obituary details.
- Do not trust the caller ID name alone, as scammers use "spoofing" technology to display the names of real law firms or funeral homes.
- Do not click links or download attachments in unexpected emails regarding a "claim" or "inheritance".

## What to do now
1. **Terminate the live contact immediately.** If on the phone, state: “I am not discussing this matter at this time; please send your credentials and the details of your inquiry in writing to my address.”
2. **Document the contact details.** Record the name of the individual, the firm or company they represent, their stated phone number, their physical address, and the specific reason for their contact.
3. **Verify the attorney’s license through the State Bar.** Use the American Bar Association (ABA) website to find the specific state bar directory for the state where the lawyer claims to be licensed.
   - Search for the lawyer by name to ensure they are "Active" and "In Good Standing".
   - Note that ABA Model Rule 7.3 generally prohibits lawyers from soliciting professional employment via live telephone or real-time electronic contact for financial gain.
4. **Verify funeral home requests via a known number.** If the caller claims to be from a funeral home asking for immediate payment, hang up and call the funeral home using a number you have independently verified (e.g., from a previous contract or their official website).
5. **Check the licensing of insurance adjusters or "claims helpers".** If they are not lawyers, use your state’s Department of Insurance website to verify that the individual or company holds a valid license to handle claims in your jurisdiction.
6. **Route all further communication to a trusted third party.** If you are overwhelmed, tell the contact: “All further correspondence regarding this estate must be sent to my designated representative/attorney.”
7. **Report suspected fraud to the FTC.** Submit a report at **ReportFraud.ftc.gov** if you receive suspicious inheritance offers or impersonation attempts.
8. **Contact the National Elder Fraud Hotline if applicable.** If the person being contacted is 60 or older, call **833-372-8311** (833-FRAUD-11) for assistance with reporting and next steps.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on legal representation today.
- You do not need to "secure your spot" or "claim the funds" within a few hours; legitimate legal processes take weeks or months.
- You do not need to respond to every marketing letter or solicitation you receive following an obituary publication.
- You do not need to finalize "fees" or "commissions" during a first conversation.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed or suspicious when contacted by strangers following a loss. Professional scammers and aggressive marketers frequently target the recently bereaved because they know the situation is disorienting. Taking days or weeks to verify a claim is a standard, responsible protective measure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling unsolicited contact. It does not address the long-term management of legitimate probate or estate litigation.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Attorney solicitation rules and licensing requirements vary significantly by state jurisdiction. Always verify professionals through official government or bar association channels before sharing sensitive information.
