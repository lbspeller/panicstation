# What to do if… you suspect someone is following you on public transport (USA)

## Short answer
Move toward people, light, and transit staff. If you feel in immediate danger, call 911 now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the person, argue with them, or try to prove they are following you.
- Do not move into an empty train car, a quiet platform end, an isolated stairwell, a parking lot, or a deserted stop.
- Do not get off somewhere secluded to try to lose them.
- Do not go home, to work, to school, or to a routine destination while you are still worried they may be following.
- Do not accept help from someone who wants to move you somewhere private or out of public view.
- Do not risk missing doors, crossing tracks, stepping into traffic, or rushing between cars to get away.

## What to do now
1. **Move to safety density.**  
   Go toward other riders, bright areas, open businesses inside the station if there are any, a station booth, a staffed area, a clearly marked help point, or a police or security presence. On a bus, move close to the driver.

2. **Change position calmly.**  
   Move to a busier part of the platform, vehicle, or station. If changing train cars feels safer, do it at a stop and in a calm way, such as walking along the platform to a busier car.

3. **Use an official help route.**  
   If you feel threatened right now, call 911. If a transit employee, police officer, driver, help point, emergency intercom, or posted safety text line is immediately available, use it. Do not spend time searching online for the perfect local contact while you feel unsafe.

4. **Tell staff or 911 what is happening in plain words.**  
   Say: “I think someone may be following me after I changed platforms or stops. I am staying in a public area and need help.” Give your route or line, direction of travel, current station or stop, next stop if you are moving, platform, vehicle number if visible, and a short description of the person.

5. **Choose the safest next pause.**  
   If you exit, do it at a busy, well-lit, staffed station or stop. Stay in the public area while getting help. Avoid side streets, parking lots, isolated stairwells, and quiet exits.

6. **Create a witness anchor.**  
   Stand near a group, staff area, driver, or open business. If you can, call or text someone you trust with your location and say you are staying in public while you get help.

7. **Use visible details only.**  
   Notice clothing, bag, direction of travel, and vehicle or car number only if you can do it without moving closer, staring, filming, or escalating.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether they definitely followed you.
- You do not need to collect evidence, film them, post online, or explain the whole situation perfectly.
- You do not need to finish the planned trip.
- You do not need to choose a long-term plan right now; the next goal is a safer public pause with official help.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to act on a repeated pattern that makes you feel unsafe, even if you are not certain. Moving toward people, staff, light, and 911 when needed is a safety step, not an accusation.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate moment only. Later decisions, such as changing routines, making a report, or getting specialist support, can wait until you are somewhere safer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger in the USA, call 911. Transit systems vary, so when you are unsure, prioritize 911, visible staff, police, a driver, or a posted emergency help point.
