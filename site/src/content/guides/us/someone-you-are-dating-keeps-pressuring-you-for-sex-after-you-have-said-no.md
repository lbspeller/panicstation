# What to do if… someone you are dating keeps pressuring you for sex after you have said no (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause, away from them and preferably around other people. Repeated pressure after a clear “no” is a safety issue, and you can reach out for support without deciding what to call it or whether to report anything.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel that you owe them more explanations, negotiation, or a perfect response.
- Do not blame yourself if you freeze, appease them, go quiet, or change tactics to reduce risk.
- Do not go somewhere private with them to talk if you feel unsafe or unsure.
- Do not meet them alone to confront them if you feel unsafe or unsure.
- Do not make yourself decide the future of the relationship while you are trying to get somewhere safer.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** If you are with them now, move toward other people, such as staff, friends, or a busier area, and leave when you safely can.
2. **Bring in another person.** Call or text someone you trust: “I need help getting home. They will not accept my no.” At a venue, tell staff or security that you need help leaving safely.
3. **Keep access to help where possible.** Keep your phone, transportation, and a route away from them accessible when it is safe to do so.
4. **Call 911 if you feel in immediate danger.** If you cannot safely make the call yourself, ask staff or a bystander to call.
5. **Reduce contact in the way that feels safest.** You do not owe further replies. If you choose to send one message, keep it short: “I said no. Do not ask again. I am not meeting you.” You can mute, block, or stop responding when doing so feels safer.
6. **Keep existing messages if you may want them later.** If it feels manageable, avoid deleting texts, direct messages, or voicemails that show the pressure.
7. **Consider medical support if sexual contact happened that you did not want, or if you are unsure.** You can ask a hospital emergency department or local sexual assault service provider about care and whether a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) or Sexual Assault Forensic Exam (SAFE) is available. Availability varies by location and facility.
8. **Contact specialist support.** RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline offers 24/7 support at 800-656-HOPE (4673) and through online chat. You do not need to label what happened before reaching out. The hotline page explains privacy details and limited reporting exceptions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now what to call the situation or whether it “counts”.
- You do not need to write a perfect explanation or persuade them to understand.
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report anything.
- You do not need to decide the future of the relationship today.

## Important reassurance
A clear no does not require further explanation. People may freeze, appease, or go quiet under pressure. Those responses do not equal consent, and they do not make the pressure your fault.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce risk and stabilize the moment. If the pressure is part of a larger pattern, such as threats, intimidation, monitoring, isolation, or retaliation when you refuse, specialist support may help you plan safer next steps.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If you have urgent medical concerns, seek urgent medical care. A confidential hotline can help you consider options without requiring you to decide immediately whether to report anything.
