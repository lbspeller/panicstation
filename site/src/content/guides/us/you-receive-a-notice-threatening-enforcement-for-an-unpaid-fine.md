# What to do if… you receive a notice threatening enforcement for an unpaid fine (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay, click, call, or scan anything from the notice yet. Verify the fine directly with the court, agency, or collector using contact details you find yourself from an official source.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links, scan QR codes, open attachments, or log into a payment portal from the notice.
- Do not call the phone number on the notice until you have checked it independently.
- Do not pay by gift card, cryptocurrency, wire transfer, payment app to an individual, or any method that feels unusual for a court or government agency.
- Do not share your Social Security number, bank login, card security code, date of birth, or one-time passcodes because someone says they need them to confirm a fine.
- Do not stay on a call with someone threatening immediate arrest, a warrant, license suspension, or extra fees unless you pay now.
- Do not ignore the notice completely until you have checked whether a real ticket, fine, case, or debt exists.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and keep the notice.** Screenshot or photograph the message, letter, envelope, sender details, phone number, QR code, and full link text. Do not click the link or scan the QR code.
2. **Check for scam red flags.** Treat it as suspicious if it demands instant payment, secrecy, gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfer, a payment app, personal information, or says you will be arrested unless you pay during the call.
3. **Verify the court or agency yourself.** Type the court, DMV, toll agency, city, county, or state website address yourself, or search for the official site. Do not use links or numbers from the notice.
4. **Ask the official office specific questions.** Ask whether there is a ticket, fine, case, citation, toll, or unpaid balance in your name; what the case or ticket number is; the exact amount; the due date; and the official payment methods.
5. **If it claims to be from a debt collector, ask for validation information before paying.** A real debt collector generally must give written validation information about the debt. After you receive that validation information, you generally have 30 days to dispute the debt in writing if you think it is wrong or not yours.
6. **If the notice names a court date, warrant, license suspension, or collection action, verify that point directly.** Ask the official court or agency whether any deadline, hearing, warrant, suspension, or collection referral is actually on record.
7. **If it is confirmed fake, report it.** Report the scam to the FTC. If you lost money, clicked a link, installed anything, or shared sensitive information, also contact your bank or card issuer immediately and consider reporting to IC3 and your state Attorney General.
8. **If it is confirmed real but you cannot pay immediately, ask about official options.** Ask the court, agency, or collector about the due date, payment plan, dispute process, fee breakdown, and where to send documents. Write down the date, time, phone number, case or ticket number, and the name or title of the person you spoke with.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to fight the fine; first confirm whether it exists and who issued it.
- You do not need to explain your full situation to an unverified caller, texter, or email sender.
- You do not need to pay during the same call or through the same link just because the notice says “immediately.”
- You do not need to choose a long-term legal strategy before you have the official case, ticket, or debt information.

## Important reassurance
Scammers use urgency and official-sounding words to make people skip verification. Slowing down and checking directly with the real court, agency, or collector is a protective step, not a refusal to deal with the issue.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the fine, ticket, case, debt, warrant, license issue, or collection action is real, later decisions may need help from the court clerk, the issuing agency, a consumer law attorney, a legal aid organization, or another qualified specialist in your state.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, identity-theft, cybersecurity, or other professional advice. USA procedures vary by state, local court, agency, type of fine, and type of collector.
