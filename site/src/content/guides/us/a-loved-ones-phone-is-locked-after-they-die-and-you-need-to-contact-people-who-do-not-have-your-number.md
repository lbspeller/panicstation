# What to do if… a loved one’s phone is locked after they die and you need to contact people who do not have your number (USA)

## Short answer
Do not attempt to guess the passcode; keep the device powered on and charged while using lock-screen "Emergency" buttons or carrier "Deceased Account" protocols to identify contacts and preserve the phone number.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to guess the passcode multiple times (repeated failures can trigger permanent lockout or data erasure).
- Do not attempt to use the deceased person's biometrics (Face ID or Touch ID) to unlock the device.
- Do not factory reset the device (this permanently deletes the contact list and messages you are trying to reach).
- Do not disconnect the mobile service immediately (the active number is often required for receiving notification calls or two-factor codes).
- Do not pay "third-party unlocking" services (these are frequently scams and can cause hardware damage).
- Do not power off or restart the device if it is currently on (restarting often moves the device into a higher security state that hides notifications).

## What to do now
1. **Check for lock-screen emergency information**
   - **On iPhone: Swipe up or press the home button, tap "Emergency," then tap "Medical ID" to see designated emergency contacts.**
   - **On Android: Swipe up, tap "Emergency call," then tap "Emergency info" (you may need to tap it twice) to view saved contacts.**
   - **Look for a "Lock Screen Message" which some users set with an alternative contact number or name.**

2. **Secure the device and power supply**
   - **Plug the phone into a charger and keep it in a safe, cool location.**
   - **Avoid letting the battery die, as a reboot may prevent incoming notifications from displaying names or message snippets.**

3. **Monitor incoming calls and messages**
   - **Note down the names and numbers of people who call or text the device.**
   - **Check for notifications that appear on the screen; even if the content is hidden, the name of the sender may be visible.**

4. **Contact the cellular service provider**
   - **Inform the carrier (e.g., Verizon, AT&T, T-Mobile) that the account holder is deceased and ask for their "Deceased Account" or "Bereavement" department.**
   - **Ask to keep the line active temporarily or to "Transfer Billing Responsibility" to your name so you can manage the number.**
      - Be prepared to provide the account owner's Social Security Number (SSN) and Date of Birth.
      - Ask if you can access the voicemail remotely or if they can assist in retrieving contact names.

5. **Initiate official digital legacy requests**
   - **For Apple: Visit the Apple Digital Legacy portal (digital-legacy.apple.com) if you have an Access Key provided by the deceased.**
   - **For Google: Use the "Submit a request regarding a deceased user's account" form on the Google Help Center.**
      - Note that Google typically requires a US court order to release specific data content if an Inactive Account Manager was not previously configured.

6. **Gather required legal documentation**
   - **Obtain multiple certified copies of the Death Certificate.**
   - **Locate your "Letters Testamentary" or "Letters of Administration" if you have been appointed as the executor or personal representative.**
   - **Keep these documents ready for verification at carrier retail stores or for uploading to tech support portals.**

7. **Search for secondary contact sources**
   - **Check physical address books, mail, or planners found in the deceased person's home.**
   - **Search for "signed-in" sessions on the deceased person’s home computer or tablet, which may allow access to email or social media contact lists without a phone passcode.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle the final phone bill or close the account today.
- You do not need to decide on a permanent "Legacy" status for social media profiles immediately.
- You do not need to unlock the physical hardware if you can successfully port the number to a new device.

## Important reassurance
It is common for families to be locked out of devices following a death. Most people who are trying to reach the deceased will continue to call or text the number over the coming days, giving you opportunities to identify and speak with them. Technical barriers are a standard part of modern estate handling and do not mean the data is lost forever.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and initial contact retrieval only. Permanent data recovery or hardware unlocking may involve lengthy legal processes with specific manufacturers.

## Important note
Requirements for account access vary significantly by service provider and state law. Possession of a device does not grant legal right to the data inside. Consult with an estate attorney if you encounter resistance from carriers or technology companies regarding "Letters Testamentary."
