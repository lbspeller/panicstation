# What to do if… your phone screen is broken and you cannot enter your passcode to unlock your device (USA)

## Short answer
Use a trusted computer or spare device to protect the accounts that control recovery: email, Apple or Google, banking, and your mobile carrier. Avoid erasing the phone until you have checked whether you can still sign in to those accounts and receive recovery codes.

## Do not do these things
- Do not factory reset or erase the phone immediately unless it is lost, stolen, or clearly compromised.
- Do not keep guessing passcodes; repeated attempts can slow you down or trigger lockouts.
- Do not hand your phone and passcode to an unknown repair counter, caller, chat account, or “support” person who contacted you first.
- Do not click text or email links claiming to be urgent Apple, Google, bank, carrier, or wallet verification.
- Do not remove two-factor authentication methods until another verified sign-in method is working.
- Do not rely on one app notification, one authenticator app, or one broken phone as your only recovery route if another safe option is available.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm whether the phone is with you**
   - If the phone is lost or stolen, move to remote lock steps first, then contact your carrier and bank.
   - If the phone is physically with you, keep it powered on, charged, and somewhere safe.
   - If the screen is cracked, avoid pressing broken glass; use tape or a screen protector only if it helps you handle it safely.

2. **Use a trusted computer or spare device to check your recovery accounts**
   - Sign in to your primary email account from a browser you trust.
   - Check recovery email, recovery phone, recent security activity, and active sessions.
   - Sign in to your Apple Account or Google Account from the same trusted device.
   - Change passwords only if you suspect compromise or you are confident you can receive recovery prompts after the change.
   - Sign out of unfamiliar sessions if the account security page lets you do that safely.

3. **Protect money access while the banking app is unreachable**
   - Use online banking from a trusted computer, or call the bank using the number on the back of your card or the bank’s official website.
   - Review recent transactions.
   - Lock or freeze cards if you see anything suspicious or cannot safely monitor the account.
   - Set alerts for transfers, card use, and new payees if your bank offers them.
   - If your phone is your only banking two-factor method, ask the bank what temporary verified alternatives it supports, such as in-branch identity checks or another approved recovery route.

4. **Use the official remote lock tool if the phone may be lost, stolen, or exposed**
   - For Android, use Google Find Hub from a browser to locate, secure, or erase the device.
   - For iPhone, use iCloud Find Devices or the Find My app on another Apple device to locate it, mark it as lost, or erase it.
   - Use erase only when the phone is lost, stolen, or clearly unsafe, or when you have confirmed you can sign back in afterward.

5. **Try non-destructive access before resetting**
   - If it is an Android phone and you have a USB adapter that fits your phone, hub, mouse, or keyboard, try using it to enter the known passcode.
   - If the phone supports external display output, try a monitor with a hub, mouse, and keyboard.
   - If it is an iPhone and touch input will not work, do not assume a mouse or keyboard will unlock it. Use Apple’s official passcode and Find My options, or ask Apple Support or an Apple Authorized Service Provider about repair before erasing.
   - If the screen is visible but touch does not work, ask a reputable repair provider whether a temporary screen replacement can allow unlock and backup before a full repair decision.

6. **Protect your phone number**
   - Contact your mobile carrier using its official website, app on another device, or published customer service number.
   - Ask for an account PIN or passcode if you do not already have one.
   - Ask for any available SIM-swap lock, port-out lock, number-transfer lock, or extra verification on account changes.
   - If you received messages about a SIM change or number transfer that you did not request, treat it as urgent: contact the carrier first, then your bank and email provider.

7. **If you cannot unlock the phone and reset becomes unavoidable**
   - For Android, official guidance says that if you cannot unlock the phone, you may need to erase it and set it up again.
   - Before an Android reset, confirm you know the Google Account and password used on the phone.
   - For iPhone, Apple’s supported passcode reset or restore process erases the current data on the iPhone.
   - Before an iPhone reset, confirm you can sign in to your Apple Account and reach the recovery email or phone number.
   - Do not reset just to “try something” if banking, email, Apple, Google, or carrier recovery is still unstable.

8. **If account takeover or identity theft may be involved**
   - Use IdentityTheft.gov to record what happened and create recovery steps.
   - Consider a fraud alert or credit freeze if someone may have enough information to open accounts in your name.
   - Keep notes of dates, carrier messages, bank calls, and suspicious transactions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to replace the phone, upgrade your plan, or change every password.
- Shopping, entertainment, social media, and non-essential app recovery can wait until email, Apple or Google, banking, and carrier access are stable.
- Data recovery, insurance claims, and long-term security cleanup can wait until the immediate lockout is contained.

## Important reassurance
A broken screen can make it feel as if every account is suddenly out of reach. The safest first move is usually to protect the accounts that control recovery, avoid a panic reset, and use official routes one at a time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Repair decisions, insurance, data recovery, fraud disputes, and longer-term account security may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, technical repair, or professional advice. Use official account, bank, carrier, Apple, Google, FTC, or regulator resources for decisions that affect money, identity, or data loss.
