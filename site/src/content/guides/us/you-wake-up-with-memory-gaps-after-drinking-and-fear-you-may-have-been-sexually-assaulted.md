# What to do if… you wake up with memory gaps after drinking and fear you may have been sexually assaulted (USA)

## Short answer
Reach a safe location, then contact a confidential advocate or go to a medical facility for a free forensic exam (SAFE/SANE) to preserve evidence and receive care, even if you are unsure what happened.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wash, shower, bathe, or brush your teeth if you intend to seek a medical forensic exam, as this can destroy DNA evidence.
- Do not change your clothes or discard the clothing you were wearing; if you must change, place the original clothes in a paper bag (not plastic).
- Do not force yourself to remember details or confront potential suspects while you are in a state of shock or panic.
- Do not blame yourself for the consumption of alcohol or for the physiological responses of your body, such as "freezing" or memory loss.
- Do not wait for "proof" before seeking medical attention or support; exams are time-sensitive.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe pause.** If you are currently with someone you do not trust or in an unfamiliar location, go to a public area, a friend's home, or a trusted facility.
   - If you are in immediate physical danger, call **911**.
2. **Contact a confidential advocate immediately.** Call the **National Sexual Assault Hotline** at **800-656-HOPE (4673)** or use their online chat service.
   - Advocates can help you locate the nearest hospital with a SANE (Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner) on duty.
   - They provide confidential support regardless of whether you choose to involve the police.
3. **Seek a medical forensic exam as soon as possible.** Go to a hospital emergency room or a specialized sexual assault clinic and request a **SAFE/SANE exam**.
   - These exams are most effective within **72 to 120 hours** of the incident.
   - Federal law (VAWA) requires that the forensic portion of the exam be provided **free of charge**.
   - You have the right to have a sexual assault advocate present during the exam.
4. **Disclose concerns about drugging or spiking.** If you suspect your drink was tampered with, tell the medical staff immediately so they can collect appropriate urine and blood samples for toxicology.
   - Many "date rape" drugs leave the system quickly, making early collection critical.
5. **Address immediate health needs.** Ask the clinician about prophylactic treatment for STIs (sexually transmitted infections) and emergency contraception (Plan B), which are standard parts of post-assault care.
6. **Preserve physical evidence.** If you have not yet washed, try to avoid doing so until the exam is complete. 
   - If you have already washed, you can still have an exam; evidence may still be present.
7. **Document what you can observe.** Use a notes app to write down the last thing you remember, the time you woke up, your location, and any physical sensations or injuries.
   - Keep this note private and do not share it on social media.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a police report today; evidence can be collected and stored (in many states) while you consider your options.
- You do not need to define what happened or use specific labels (like "assault" or "rape") to be eligible for care.
- You do not need to respond to messages or calls from people involved in the night's events.
- You do not need to explain your situation to employers, family, or friends until you feel ready.

## Important reassurance
Waking up with memory gaps is a common indicator of drug-facilitated incidents or alcohol-induced blackouts, and the confusion or panic you feel is a normal response to trauma. You do not need to have a perfect memory of the event to deserve medical care, safety, and professional support.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and evidence preservation instructions only. Long-term recovery, legal decisions, and counseling should be handled through specialized advocates and clinicians.

## Important note
This information is for general stabilization and is not legal or medical advice. Forensic protocols and evidence storage laws vary by state. If you are experiencing a medical emergency, such as difficulty breathing or loss of consciousness, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room immediately.
