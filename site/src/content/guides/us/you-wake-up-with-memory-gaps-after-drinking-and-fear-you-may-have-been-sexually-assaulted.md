# What to do if… you wake up with memory gaps after drinking and fear you may have been sexually assaulted (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safe place first, then contact confidential sexual assault support or medical care as soon as you can, even if you are not sure what happened.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force yourself to “solve it” right now by replaying memories, searching your phone for hours, or questioning people while you are panicked.
- Do not confront or meet anyone who was with you, or go back to where you were, unless you are with someone you trust and it feels safe.
- Do not post details publicly or message lots of people for “answers” while you are shaken.
- Do not blame yourself for drinking, not remembering, freezing, or not reacting the way you think you should.
- If you may want the option of a medical forensic exam, try, if bearable, not to shower, wash, brush your teeth, use the bathroom, or change clothes until you have spoken to a clinician or advocate.
- If you already changed clothes, do not panic; place the clothes you were wearing in a clean paper bag if you have one, or keep them somewhere separate.

## What to do now
1. **Get safe first.** If you are in immediate danger or seriously unwell, call **911**. If you are with someone you do not trust, move to a safer public place or somewhere you trust, such as a friend’s home, hotel lobby, hospital, or staffed 24-hour location.
2. **Bring one safe person in, if you can.** Call or text someone you trust to be with you, take you to care, or stay on the phone. If you do not have someone, you can still contact confidential support.
3. **Contact confidential sexual assault support.** Contact **RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline** at **800-656-HOPE (4673)**, use its online chat, or text **HOPE** to **64673**. Ask for help finding local sexual assault care near you.
4. **Ask for medical care and sexual assault care.** Go to an ER, hospital, or clinic and say: “I have memory gaps after drinking and I am worried I may have been sexually assaulted.” Ask whether they can provide or connect you with a **SAFE/SANE exam**.
5. **Ask for an advocate.** At the hospital or clinic, ask whether a sexual assault advocate can be present or contacted. You can also ask RAINN to help find a local sexual assault service provider.
6. **Say clearly if you suspect drugging or drink spiking.** Tell the clinician when you last remember feeling normal, what you drank or took, where you woke up, and any pain, injuries, vomiting, confusion, or missing time. They can advise what testing, treatment, or documentation may still be possible.
7. **You usually do not have to decide about police immediately.** Tell the clinician or advocate if you are unsure about reporting. Reporting rules and medical options vary by state and by circumstances, especially for minors, vulnerable adults, serious injuries, or immediate danger.
8. **Write one brief factual note for yourself.** Record only what you know: where you were, who you were with, when you last remember feeling okay, where you woke up, what you noticed on your body or clothes, and any messages or location records that already exist.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today what to call it or whether to report.
- You do not need a perfect timeline or proof before getting care and support.
- You do not need to contact people from last night, confront anyone, or explain anything to others.
- You do not need to make big decisions about work, school, relationships, or social media while you are in shock.

## Important reassurance
Feeling confused, numb, panicked, ashamed, or uncertain after memory gaps can happen after a frightening situation. Uncertainty does not mean you are overreacting. You deserve support and medical care, and you can take this one step at a time.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the moment, protect your safety, and keep options open. Later decisions may need help from a sexual assault advocate, clinician, attorney, campus advocate, or other specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, forensic, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe or seriously unwell, call 911 or go to the nearest ER. Services, reporting rules, exams, and confidentiality protections vary by state; a hotline advocate or clinician can help you find local options.
