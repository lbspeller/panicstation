# What to do if… someone threatens to show up where you are and gives details that suggest they can find you (USA)

## Short answer
Move immediately to a secure, populated, or access-controlled location and call **911** if you believe the person is nearby or arriving soon. If you are currently in transit and suspect you are being followed, do not go home; instead, head to the nearest police or fire station.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go home or to a friend's house if you suspect you are being followed or tracked.
- Do not go outside or "scout" the area alone to see if the person has arrived.
- Do not engage with the threat by arguing, bargaining, or meeting them to "settle it."
- Do not delete any evidence including texts, call logs, DMs, or voicemails.
- Do not post your current location or safety concerns on public social media.
- Do not open the door for any unexpected person, even if they claim to be a delivery driver or service worker.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause immediately.** **If you are at home, lock all doors and windows, close the blinds, and stay away from entry points.** If you are in public or in transit, move to a "staffed" location such as a hospital lobby, a busy store, or a police station.
2. **Determine the appropriate level of emergency response.** **Call 911 immediately if there is a threat of violence, if the person is nearby, or if you feel you are in imminent physical danger.** 
   - If you cannot safely speak, text 911 (available in most US jurisdictions).
   - If the threat is credible but not immediate (e.g., they threatened to show up tomorrow), call your local police department’s non-emergency number.
3. **Provide specific identifiers to the dispatcher.** **State your exact address or current location, the nature of the threat, and the specific details that indicate the person knows your whereabouts.** 
   - Provide a description of the person (name, appearance, clothing).
   - Provide vehicle details if known (make, model, color, license plate).
4. **Alert those who control access to your building.** **Notify security, a front desk manager, or your supervisor that you have received a credible threat and that this person is not permitted on the premises.** Provide them with a photo or description of the individual if available.
5. **Preserve every piece of digital and physical evidence.** **Take screenshots of all messages and call logs, ensuring the sender’s name, number, and timestamps are visible.** Start a simple log noting the date, time, and exact wording of the threat, but do not prioritize a detailed narrative over your immediate safety.
6. **Disable all active location-sharing features on your devices.** **Turn off "Find My," Google Location Sharing, and any social media "live" location tags.** If you suspect your vehicle is being tracked, ask the police to perform a quick visual inspection for unauthorized devices once you are in a safe location.
7. **Notify one trusted contact of your situation and location.** **Send a brief message stating that you have received a credible threat, that you are contacting the police, and where you are currently located.** Stay on the line with them or the dispatcher until help arrives or you are in a secured area.
8. **Follow the "Run, Hide, Fight" protocol if the threat becomes an active physical confrontation.** **Your first priority is to escape (Run); if escape is impossible, find a room that locks and silence your phone (Hide); as a final resort for your life, defend yourself with total aggression (Fight).**

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to block the person (retaining the ability to see incoming threats can provide early warning for law enforcement).
- Filing for a formal Order of Protection or Restraining Order (this happens after the immediate crisis is stabilized).
- Drafting a comprehensive history of your relationship or past interactions.
- Making long-term changes like moving house or changing jobs.

## Important reassurance
Feeling startled, hyper-vigilant, or physically shaky is a standard physiological response to a credible threat. Taking immediate action to secure your physical environment is a practical step that many people take when faced with unexpected or escalating harassment.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Stalking and credible threats often require long-term safety planning and legal intervention involving local victim services or law enforcement professionals.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and is not legal advice. If you are in immediate danger, dial **911**. For ongoing support and safety planning, you may contact the National Domestic Violence Hotline at 800-799-7233 or text "START" to 88788.
