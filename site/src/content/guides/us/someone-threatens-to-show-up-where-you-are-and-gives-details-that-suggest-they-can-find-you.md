# What to do if… someone threatens to show up where you are and gives details that suggest they can find you (USA)

## Short answer
Create a safer pause. If you are in immediate danger, the person may arrive soon, or you can see them nearby, call **911** now; move only if you can do so safely. Once you are in a safer pause, keep the messages and avoid deleting evidence.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go outside or check alone to see whether the person is nearby.
- Do not agree to meet them to resolve the situation or stop the messages.
- Avoid arguing, bargaining, or sending threatening replies unless emergency dispatch or law enforcement advises otherwise.
- Do not post your location, landmarks, or real-time updates where the person may see them.
- Do not delete texts, direct messages, emails, photos, or voicemails.
- Do not open the door to an unexpected visitor while the threat is unresolved.
- Do not make unfamiliar changes to a phone, tracker, or shared account if doing so could alert the person before you have reached a safer pause.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.**  
   If you can move safely, go to a staffed, busy, or access-controlled place such as a front desk, store, lobby, security desk, or trusted nearby location. If you are at home or in another secure place, lock doors and windows if you can do so safely, stay away from windows, and keep your phone with you.

2. **Call for immediate help when needed.**  
   Call **911** if you feel at risk now, the person may arrive soon, violence has been threatened, or you can see the person nearby. Follow the call-taker’s instructions. If the situation is not immediate, use the local law-enforcement agency’s non-emergency phone or reporting route for the place where you are.

3. **Be ready to share the key details.**  
   Tell the call-taker or officer your exact location, your callback number, what was said, and the details suggesting the person can find you, such as an address, workplace, school, vehicle, routine, or nearby landmark. Share the person’s name, phone number, usernames, description, or vehicle details if you have them.

4. **Tell people who can control access.**  
   If you are at a workplace, school, hotel, hospital, apartment building, or other staffed location, tell security, reception, a manager, or another responsible staff member. Ask them not to let the person in for you and to call **911** if the person appears.

5. **Keep the evidence.**  
   Save the messages, voicemails, emails, and direct messages. If it feels safe and the app will not alert the sender, take screenshots showing the sender, date, time, and wording. If you are unsure, leave the message in place and use another device to photograph the screen if one is safely available, or wait until you can get help. Make a brief note of what the person appeared to know and whether the messages are becoming more specific or urgent.

6. **Reduce visible location sharing only when it feels safe.**  
   Stop real-time public posting. If you know that you can safely turn off a live-location share without alerting the person or increasing risk, do that. Leave unfamiliar tracker, device, and account changes until you are safe enough to get help, because some changes may be noticeable to the person monitoring them.

7. **Bring in one trusted person.**  
   Tell one trusted person: “I received a threat with location details. I am at [place]. I am calling for help.” Ask them to stay on the phone or meet you at the safer location if that does not increase risk.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to block the person after the messages are saved.
- Writing a complete timeline; a brief note is enough for now.
- Trying to work out exactly how the person found the location.
- Changing every password, checking every device, or removing a suspected tracker before you are in a safer pause.
- Protective-order or restraining-order questions, which vary by state.
- Reporting to the FBI unless the threat may be connected to a federal crime or local law enforcement advises it. Immediate danger still goes to **911** first.
- Big decisions such as moving, changing jobs, or changing your phone number.

## Important reassurance
You do not have to decide whether the person is bluffing before asking for help. A threat that includes location details is a reasonable reason to create distance, contact emergency services when needed, and ask other people to help control access.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need support from local law enforcement, a victim-support service, a safety-planning specialist, or another appropriate professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. For immediate danger in the USA, dial **911**.
