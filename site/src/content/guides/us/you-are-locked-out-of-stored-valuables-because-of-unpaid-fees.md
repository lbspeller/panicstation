# What to do if… you are locked out of stored valuables because of unpaid fees (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the facility or bank immediately to request a formal "payoff amount" to stop a lien sale or escheatment, and confirm whether a specific auction date has already been set.

## Do not do these things
- Don't attempt to cut an "overlock" or force entry into a unit or box, as this constitutes a criminal offense and voids your right to negotiate.
- Don't assume you have a grace period of several months; some states allow the lien process to begin as few as 14 to 30 days after a missed payment.
- Don't rely on a verbal "we won't sell it yet" promise from a manager; only a written receipt or a formal "Notice of Lien Cancellation" is legally binding.
- Don't ignore mail marked "Notice of Lien Sale" or "Notice of Default," as these are often the final legal requirements before a permanent sale.
- Don't wait until the day of the auction to pay; many facilities have a "cutoff time" 24–48 hours before the sale where they stop accepting payments.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the physical status of the lockout to determine the urgency.**
   - For self-storage: Check if there is a "red lock" or "overlock" on your unit or if your gate code has been deactivated.
   - For safe deposit boxes: Confirm if your access card is suspended at the branch counter.
2. **Determine if you are protected by the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA).**
   - **Inform the facility immediately in writing if the account holder is on active military duty.**
   - Federal law generally prohibits storage lien sales against active-duty personnel without a specific court order.
3. **Call the facility manager and ask for the "Total Amount to Cure" the default.**
   - **Ask for an itemized breakdown including the base rent, late fees, and any "lien fees" or "auction advertising fees" added to the balance.**
   - Request that this statement be emailed to you immediately so you have a dated record of the payoff demand.
4. **Identify the exact date and time of any scheduled lien sale or auction.**
   - **Ask specifically: "Has a public auction date been set for this unit/box?"**
   - If a date is set, ask for the "final payment deadline" to stop the process, which is often earlier than the auction time.
5. **Request a one-time "supervised access" appointment if you have life-critical items inside.**
   - **Note that facilities are not legally required to grant this, but may allow you to retrieve medications, passports, or legal IDs under staff supervision.**
   - Clearly state you are only seeking "essential health and identity documents" rather than moving large items.
6. **For bank safe deposit boxes, confirm if the box has already been "drilled" for non-payment.**
   - **If the box was drilled, ask for the date of the inventory and whether the contents are still in the bank vault or have been sent to the State Treasurer.**
   - If the contents were escheated (sent to the state), obtain the "Report Number" or "Holder ID" to use when filing a claim with the State Unclaimed Property Office.
7. **Initiate a formal dispute if you believe the fees are inaccurate or notices weren't sent.**
   - **Send a written "Notice of Dispute" via certified mail if the facility claims they sent notices to an old address despite you providing an update.**
   - For storage units, contact your State Attorney General's consumer protection division.
   - For national banks, file a complaint with the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) via HelpWithMyBank.gov.

## What can wait
- You don't need to decide whether to move out or keep the unit long-term today; the priority is stopping the permanent loss of items.
- You don't need to pay the full multi-month balance if you can negotiate a "partial payment to stay the sale" (though this is at the facility's discretion).
- You don't need to hire a lawyer immediately unless the value of the items exceeds small claims limits and a sale is imminent (within 24 hours).

## Important reassurance
Being locked out is a standard part of the debt collection process for storage and is not a permanent loss of ownership yet. In almost every state, you retain the legal right to "redeem" your property by paying the owed amount at any point until the moment the auctioneer's hammer falls.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for domestic storage lockouts in the USA. It does not cover commercial warehouse liens, vehicle impound lots, or international storage laws.

## Important note
Procedures for safe deposit boxes and self-storage units are governed by different sets of state and federal laws. Self-storage is primarily governed by state-specific "Self-Service Storage Facility" acts, while safe deposit boxes are governed by banking regulations and state escheatment (unclaimed property) laws.
