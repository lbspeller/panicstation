# What to do if… you receive an urgent penalty notice or fine demanding immediate payment and threatening enforcement action (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay through the notice yet. First verify the penalty directly through the real agency, court, clerk, toll authority, or other official channel using contact details you find yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click a payment link in a text or email that says you must pay now to avoid enforcement.
- Do not assume caller ID, a logo, or an official-looking seal proves the notice is real.
- Do not call a phone number on the notice until you have independently checked that it belongs to the real office.
- Do not pay with gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfer, or payment apps while someone is threatening you.
- Do not share your Social Security number, banking login, one-time code, or photo ID to “clear it up”.
- Do not stay on the phone with anyone who is pressuring you to pay, log in, or keep the call secret.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and save the evidence.** Screenshot the message or website, or photograph the letter and envelope. Write down the date, time, amount demanded, payment deadline, and any case, ticket, citation, account, or reference number.
2. **Check for strong scam signs.** Treat the notice as suspicious if it threatens immediate arrest or a warrant unless you pay right now, demands gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfer, or secrecy, or sends you to a shortened or oddly spelled website.
3. **Verify through an official route you start yourself.** Do not use links or phone numbers from the notice. Search for the official website of the named city, county, court, clerk, parking authority, toll agency, transportation department, state vehicle agency, or other issuing office, then use its case lookup, citation lookup, violation lookup, or main public contact number.
4. **If it claims to be about a local ticket or citation, check the exact issuing office.** Look for the citation number, vehicle plate, date, location, and issuing agency. If you cannot find the record, call the official public number for the court, clerk, parking office, or agency and ask whether the notice exists.
5. **If it claims to be about a toll or vehicle violation, use the official toll or state vehicle route.** Go directly to the official toll agency, transportation department, or state vehicle agency site and look for violations or notice lookup. Do not enter card details on a site reached from the message.
6. **If it claims to be IRS-related, verify through IRS guidance.** The IRS generally first contacts taxpayers by mail. Use official IRS guidance to check whether the notice looks real and how to respond; do not pay an IRS-themed demand by gift card, cryptocurrency, wire transfer, or a link from a text.
7. **If it claims to be Social Security-related, treat threats as a warning sign.** Social Security scam messages often threaten arrest, legal action, or immediate payment. Use official Social Security scam guidance and do not pay or give personal information to the caller or sender.
8. **If it may be a debt collector rather than a government fine, keep it in writing.** Debt collectors generally must provide validation information about the debt. Ask for the written validation notice and review it before paying or admitting anything.
9. **If you already paid or entered financial details, protect the account now.** Contact your bank or card issuer using the number on the back of your card or the official app. Report fraud, ask about stopping or reversing charges, and change passwords for any account you entered.
10. **If you shared sensitive identity information, reduce the damage.** Consider a fraud alert or credit freeze, and use official identity-theft recovery guidance if your Social Security number, photo ID, or banking details were exposed.
11. **Report the suspicious contact.** Report government impersonation or payment-demand scams to the FTC. For IRS-themed or Social Security-themed scams, also use the official IRS or Social Security reporting route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to argue the ticket, citation, or penalty right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to contest it until you confirm it is real and know the actual deadline from the official office.
- You do not need to keep talking to the person who contacted you to “buy time”.
- You do not need to send a long explanation before you have verified the notice.

## Important reassurance
A real deadline is easier to handle after you know the notice is genuine. Scammers rely on pressure and confusion; taking a few minutes to verify through an official route is a protective step, not a refusal to deal with the issue.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent panic payment, preserve information, and verify whether the notice is real. Later decisions about paying, disputing, appealing, or responding may need help from the issuing office, a consumer-protection agency, or a qualified legal professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Processes and deadlines vary by state, county, court, agency, and type of notice.
