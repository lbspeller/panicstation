# What to do if… you missed a court response deadline and fear a default judgment (USA)

## Short answer
Act today: check the court docket or call the clerk to find out whether there is no default, an entry of default, or a default judgment. Then ask what filing the court will accept today, such as an answer, a request for permission to file late, or a motion to set aside or vacate.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume nothing happens until a hearing; some defaults can be entered from paperwork.
- Do not rely only on a phone call with the other side; you generally need something filed with the court.
- Do not ignore the case because you think service was wrong; service problems usually need to be raised through the court process.
- Do not pay someone who contacts you claiming they can clear the judgment immediately; verify everything through the official court docket or clerk.
- Do not message the judge directly unless the court’s rules specifically tell you to; use the court’s filing process.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the court and case number.** Use the summons, complaint, notice, envelope, text from the court, or online case search. Write down the court name, case number, plaintiff, defendant name used, and any deadline shown.

2. **Find the case status.** Check the online docket or call the clerk and ask whether the docket shows:
   - no default entered yet;
   - an entry of default;
   - a default judgment;
   - any hearing, garnishment, levy, eviction lockout, or other enforcement date.

3. **Ask the clerk for filing procedure, not legal advice.** Ask what forms, filing methods, fees, fee waiver forms, self-help center, and local rules apply. Ask how to file today by e-filing, in person, drop box, or other method the court accepts.

4. **If no default has been entered yet, ask how to file the fastest accepted response.** This may be an answer, a late answer with permission from the court, or another response allowed by that court. Keep proof of filing, such as a stamped copy, confirmation email, or e-filing receipt.

5. **If an entry of default or default judgment is already entered, ask how to request set-aside or vacatur.** The name varies, but common words are set aside, vacate, relief from judgment, or relief from default. Ask whether the court expects a proposed answer or defense statement to be filed with the request.

6. **If enforcement may happen soon, ask about a separate pause request.** A motion to set aside may not automatically stop collection or enforcement. If there is a wage garnishment, bank levy, eviction lockout, repossession, or other enforcement step pending, ask what form or motion is used to request a stay, temporary pause, or emergency review.

7. **Make a short factual timeline.** Keep it to dates and facts:
   - when you were allegedly served;
   - when you actually learned about the lawsuit or default;
   - why the deadline was missed, such as old address, papers not received, illness, confusion, or mistake;
   - what you did as soon as you learned;
   - the basic defense or reason you dispute the claim.

8. **Save proof without arguing with anyone.** Keep envelopes, tracking information, address-change records, medical notes, screenshots, emails, payment records, and docket printouts. Put them in date order.

9. **Try rapid local help.** Contact your court’s self-help center, local legal aid, or a consumer, housing, or small-claims clinic if one fits your case. If money is tight, prioritize legal aid or court self-help over debt-settlement or judgment-removal pitches.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to settle the whole case.
- You do not need to write a long story today.
- You do not need to fight every detail today; first find out the case status and get the right filing into the court system.
- You do not need to hire the first person who contacts you about the judgment.

## Important reassurance
Missing a lawsuit deadline can happen for ordinary reasons, including moving, illness, not receiving papers, or misunderstanding court forms. A missed deadline is serious, but it is not the same as having no options; acting quickly and using the court’s process gives you the best chance to stabilize the situation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Default, default judgment, set-aside, vacatur, enforcement, and deadline rules vary by state, county, court type, and whether the case is in state or federal court, so later decisions may need help from a court self-help center, legal aid, or a lawyer.

## Important note
This is general legal information, not legal advice or professional representation. Court staff can usually explain filing procedures, but they cannot choose your strategy or tell you what arguments to make.
