# What to do if… someone claims to be a worker or official at your door but you cannot verify it quickly (USA)

## Short answer
Do not open the door or provide entry; instead, keep the door locked and verify the visitor's identity through an official, self-sourced phone number. If they refuse to leave or you feel unsafe, call 911 immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door just because the person is wearing a uniform, carrying a clipboard, or wearing a badge.
- Do not let them inside for a "quick check" or "to use the phone," regardless of the claimed urgency.
- Do not call any phone number the visitor provides or use a number from a business card they hand you.
- Do not share account numbers, Social Security numbers, or any payment information at the doorstep.
- Do not step outside to "walk with them" to a utility meter or vehicle if you are uncertain.
- Do not ignore the presence of a lone individual claiming to be a police officer serving a warrant; warrants are almost always served by multiple officers.

## What to do now
1. **Establish a physical barrier and keep the door locked.** Communicate only through the closed door, a security camera, or a nearby window. 
2. **Issue a clear boundary statement.** Tell the individual, "I cannot open the door until I have verified your identity with your office; please wait outside."
3. **Inspect visual credentials from behind glass.** Ask them to hold their photo ID and any work orders up to your window or camera. 
   - Note the agency name, the person's full name, and any vehicle details.
   - Look for permanent professional markings on their vehicle; magnetic "slap-on" signs are a frequent red flag for impersonators.
4. **Call the official organization using a number you find independently.** 
   - For utility workers: use the customer service number found on your most recent paper or digital bill.
   - For city or county employees: call the municipal switchboard or the department's public directory number.
   - For law enforcement: call 911 and tell the dispatcher you have someone at your door claiming to be an officer and you need to verify their identity and purpose. 
5. **Request a marked unit if a "plainclothes" officer is present.** If an individual claims to be a detective or officer but is in civilian clothes or an unmarked car, ask the 911 dispatcher to send a uniformed officer in a marked patrol car to your location to confirm.
6. **Prioritize 911 if they attempt to force entry or become aggressive.** If the person begins rattling the handle, shouting, or threatening you, stop all communication and call 911 immediately.
7. **Report the incident to the FTC if it appears to be a scam.** If the visitor leaves after you refuse entry or if they were unable to provide verification, report the details to ReportFraud.ftc.gov.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if they are "guilty" or "innocent" right now; focus only on maintaining the physical barrier.
- You do not need to apologize for being cautious; legitimate professionals are trained to expect and respect identity verification.
- You do not need to worry about missing an "emergency" inspection; real utility emergencies will involve multiple vehicles and obvious local activity.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel pressured or flustered when someone appears in an official capacity and demands immediate action. Legitimate government and utility workers are required to carry ID and are instructed to wait while you verify their credentials for your own safety.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for doorstep interactions only. If you have already shared personal data or paid money to an unverified individual, you will need to contact your financial institutions and local law enforcement for fraud recovery.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and does not constitute legal advice. If you believe a crime is in progress or you are in immediate danger, call 911.
