# What to do if… you get a call saying a family member has been detained and you need to act quickly (USA)

## Short answer
Hang up immediately and call the family member's known personal phone number to verify their safety. If you cannot reach them, contact the local jail or police department using an official number you find independently through a search engine or government directory.

## Do not do these things
- Don't trust the voice on the phone even if it sounds exactly like your loved one; scammers use AI voice cloning to mimic family members.
- Don't pay anyone using gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfers, payment apps, or by handing cash to a courier.
- Don't keep the call a secret, even if the caller tells you that telling others will "make things worse" or "put them in danger."
- Don't share your Social Security number, bank account details, or any passwords with the caller.
- Don't stay on the line while you go to a bank or a store to get funds.

## What to do now
1. **End the suspicious call immediately.** **Hanging up is the fastest way to stop the high-pressure tactics and regain clarity.**
2. **Call the family member directly on their known personal number.** **Verify their location by speaking to them or another trusted family member who is physically with them.**
3. **Document the details of the call.** **Write down the caller's name, their claimed agency or law office, the booking number provided, and the specific amount of money requested.**
4. **Search for the official number of the detention facility.** **Look up the "county jail inmate locator" or "sheriff's department" for the city where the arrest allegedly occurred.**
   - Use the VINElink (vinelink.com) website or app to search for the person's name across multiple state and local databases.
   - Call the jail's booking desk directly and ask to speak with a duty officer to confirm if the person is in custody.
5. **Contact the local Public Defender's office if the arrest is real.** **In most jurisdictions, a lawyer will only be appointed by a judge at the first court appearance (arraignment), but the office can sometimes provide general procedural information.**
6. **Report the scam attempt.** **If you suspect fraud, file a report with the Federal Trade Commission (ReportFraud.ftc.gov) or the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (ic3.gov).**
7. **Contact your bank immediately if you have already sent money.** **Ask them to stop the transaction or flag the account for fraud, though funds sent via crypto or gift cards are rarely recoverable.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide between a private attorney and a public defender in the first hour.
- You do not need to provide the person's full history or medical details to the jail staff right now.
- You do not need to pay the full bail amount immediately; most people wait until the first appearance to see if the judge lowers the bail or releases the person on their own recognizance.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel an intense sense of urgency and fear when told a loved one is in trouble. Scammers rely on this specific reaction to bypass your logic. Taking five minutes to verify the story through a known, official number is the most helpful thing you can do for your family member and your own security.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate response to a high-pressure phone call alleging an arrest. It does not provide legal defense strategies or long-term guidance for navigating the criminal justice system.

## Important note
Procedures for arrest, booking, and bail vary significantly by state and county. Jail staff may be restricted in what information they can release over the phone. This guide is for informational purposes and is not a substitute for professional legal advice.
