# What to do if… you are worried a friend is in danger and they have suddenly stopped responding (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as an urgent safety concern. If you think harm could be happening now, call 911. If it is concerning but not immediate, contact the local police or sheriff non-emergency number where your friend likely is and ask how to request a welfare check or safety check.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront or message the person you are worried about; it may increase danger or alert them.
- Do not send long, emotional, or accusing texts to your friend; their phone may be monitored.
- Do not post the situation on social media.
- Do not go to a private location, hotel room, home, or vehicle to check on them yourself.
- Do not try to track, hack, or log in to their phone or accounts.
- Do not pressure your friend to report, explain, or prove anything if they do reply.

## What to do now
1. Write down the few safety facts you already know:
   - Your friend’s full name, phone number, age if known, last known location, last contact time, who they may be with, vehicle details if relevant, and any health, medication, accessibility, or communication needs that responders may need to know.
2. Send one short, low-risk message:
   - “Quick check-in. Are you safe to reply yes or no?”
   - If you already share a code word or simple check-in signal, use that instead.
3. Try one call or one neutral voicemail if that feels safe:
   - “Call me when you can.”
   - Avoid describing your fears in the message.
   - Avoid repeated calls or messages unless new information changes the safety picture.
4. Try one safer outside check:
   - Ask a trusted mutual friend or family member whether they have heard from your friend.
   - If your friend was going to a public business or public-facing place, such as a bar, venue, restaurant, campus safety office, campus building, or hotel lobby, you can ask staff whether they can pass on a neutral message. Do not share accusations or details if that could alert the person you are worried about.
5. Decide whether emergency help is needed now:
   - If your friend said they were scared, threatened, trapped, injured, intoxicated, being followed, being forced somewhere, or you know the location and think harm may be happening now, call 911.
   - Tell the dispatcher you are asking for help with an urgent safety concern. Give the facts you wrote down and say what made you worried.
   - If you are worried but do not think it is immediate, call the local police or sheriff non-emergency number for the city or county where your friend likely is. Ask how to request a welfare check, safety check, or missing-person concern report. Local wording varies.
6. If you believe your friend may be missing, ask law enforcement how to make a missing-person or concern-for-safety report:
   - Start with local law enforcement rather than an online database if the concern is urgent.
   - You do not need to prove that a crime happened before asking for help.
   - Give the last known location, last contact time, and why the silence is unusual or worrying.
7. Contact specialist support while you are doing this:
   - A sexual violence hotline can help you choose safer wording and think through options without pressuring your friend.
   - A domestic violence hotline can help if the situation may involve a partner, dating violence, stalking, or coercive control.
   - A trafficking hotline may be relevant if there are signs of force, fraud, coercion, or commercial sexual exploitation.
   - If you are also worried about self-harm and it is not an immediate emergency, 988 can provide crisis support. If you think there is immediate danger, call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide right now whether this is sexual assault, trafficking, dating violence, coercive control, or something else.
- You do not have to gather evidence.
- You do not have to decide whether your friend should report to police.
- You do not have to explain everything perfectly.
- You do not have to confront anyone.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to take sudden silence seriously when the last contact or context felt unsafe. A calm, brief safety check and a clear decision about 911 or local law enforcement is a practical way to act without escalating the situation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, careful communication, and getting appropriate help involved. Later decisions may need specialist advocates, local services, legal support, or medical support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, investigative, or professional advice. If you think someone may be in immediate danger, call 911.
