# What to do if… you develop blistering or peeling skin after starting a new medication (USA)

## Short answer
Blistering or peeling skin after starting a new medication is a medical emergency: **go to the nearest Emergency Room immediately or call 911**. Do not take another dose of the suspected medication.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for a primary care appointment if skin is blistering, peeling, or if you have sores in your mouth, eyes, or genitals.
- Do not take another dose of the suspected medication to see if it happens again.
- Do not apply adhesive bandages or tapes directly to skin that appears fragile, red, or blistering.
- Do not apply medicated creams, steroids, or ointments to the area until assessed by an emergency clinician.
- Do not attempt to pop blisters or peel away skin yourself.

## What to do now
1. **Seek emergency medical care immediately.**
   - **Call 911** if you have difficulty breathing, swelling of the face or tongue, or if the rash is spreading rapidly across your body.
   - **Go to the nearest Emergency Room** if you notice skin pain, "target-like" red spots, or blistering on the skin or mucous membranes (mouth, eyes, genitals).
2. **Stop taking the suspected new medication.**
   - **Identify any medication started or increased in dose** within the last 8 weeks. 
   - **Do not take the next dose** unless specifically told to do so by an emergency physician.
   - **Alert the medical team** if the medication is one that requires tapering (such as certain seizure or psychiatric medications) so they can manage the cessation safely.
3. **Bring all current medications with you to the hospital.**
   - **Pack all prescription bottles, over-the-counter drugs, and supplements** in a bag to show the medical team.
   - **Include the start date** for each medication and the time of your most recent dose.
4. **Identify accompanying symptoms for the medical team.**
   - **Note if you have had flu-like symptoms** (fever, sore throat, cough, or body aches) in the 1 to 3 days before the rash appeared.
   - **Check for eye symptoms**, such as burning, stinging, or redness, which are common in serious drug reactions.
5. **Request a specialist evaluation at the hospital.**
   - **Ask if a dermatologist or burn specialist is available** to assess the skin, as conditions like Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) often require specialized unit care.
6. **Protect the affected skin during transport.**
   - **Wear loose-fitting, soft cotton clothing** to minimize friction.
   - **Cover raw or oozing areas** with a clean, dry, non-stick dressing if available, but do not use tape on the skin itself.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the specific medical name of the reaction (e.g., SJS vs. TEN) before seeking help.
- You do not need to notify your insurance company or pharmacist before going to the ER.
- You do not need to decide on long-term medication alternatives right now.
- Reporting the reaction to the FDA MedWatch program (1-888-FDA-1088) can wait until you are medically stable.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when a physical reaction occurs suddenly. Blistering and peeling are distinct signals from the body that require professional intervention. Going to the Emergency Room is the standard, safest protocol for these symptoms and is the most effective way to prevent further complications.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency actions for suspected serious cutaneous drug reactions. It does not provide medical diagnosis or long-term treatment plans.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only and is not medical advice. Serious drug reactions can be life-threatening and require immediate professional medical assessment in a hospital setting.
