# What to do if… you develop blistering or peeling skin after starting a new medication (USA)

## Short answer
Blistering or peeling skin after starting a new medication can be a medical emergency. Go to an emergency department now, or call 911 if you need emergency assistance or cannot get there safely.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for a routine clinic appointment if your skin is blistering, peeling, very painful, or you have sores in your mouth, eyes, or genitals.
- Do not take another dose of the suspected new medication before you have urgent instructions from the emergency team.
- Do not try another dose to see whether the reaction happens again.
- Do not self-treat with leftover antibiotics, steroids, or someone else’s prescription medication.
- Do not apply harsh creams or chemicals to blistered or peeling skin.
- Do not start new nonprescription medicines or supplements unless a clinician advises it.

## What to do now
1. **Get emergency care now.**
   - Call 911 if you have trouble breathing, swelling of your lips, face, or throat, fainting, confusion, rapidly worsening symptoms, or you feel too unwell to travel safely.
   - Otherwise, go to the nearest emergency department now. Do not drive yourself if you feel unwell.

2. **Do not take another dose of the suspected new medication before urgent instructions from the emergency team.**
   - Tell the emergency team what you started, when you started it, and whether the dose recently changed.
   - Tell them immediately if missing a dose could be risky, including if the medication is used to control seizures or another serious condition.
   - Do not delay leaving for emergency care while seeking instructions.

3. **Bring your medication packaging if practical.**
   - Bring bottles, boxes, or blister packs for medicines taken in the last three weeks, including prescription medicines, nonprescription medicines, vitamins, herbal products, and supplements.
   - Note the medication name, dose, start date, last dose time, and any recent changes.

4. **Make a brief record only if this does not delay leaving or calling 911.**
   - Take a few photos in good light.
   - Note when the symptoms began, whether you have fever, eye burning, or mouth sores, and how quickly the symptoms are spreading.

5. **Avoid rubbing the affected skin while you get help.**
   - Wear loose, soft clothing if practical.
   - Do not delay emergency care to treat or dress the skin yourself.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact diagnosis now.
- You do not need to decide whether you will ever take that medication again.
- You do not need to create a perfect timeline. The medication name, start date, last dose time, and first symptoms are enough for now.
- Once you are stable, you can ask the clinician how the suspected medication and reaction should be recorded.
- Reporting the reaction to FDA MedWatch can wait until after urgent care.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to manage this alone or identify the cause yourself. Blistering or peeling skin after a new medication needs prompt emergency assessment, and getting assessed now is the appropriate next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Further treatment and medication decisions may need help from the emergency team, your prescriber, and other specialists.
