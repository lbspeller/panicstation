# What to do if… you are told life insurance paperwork is time-sensitive and you cannot find the policy (USA)

## Short answer
Verify any alleged "deadline" by contacting the insurer through their official public website rather than using provided callback numbers. You do not need the physical policy to start a search; you can initiate a claim using the deceased person’s Social Security Number and a death certificate through the NAIC Life Insurance Policy Locator.

## Do not do these things
- Don't provide a Social Security Number, bank details, or death certificate scans to anyone who contacted you via text, social media, or an unsolicited "cold call."
- Don't pay a fee to anyone claiming they can "find" a policy or "expedite" your payout; official state and NAIC search tools are free.
- Don't assume the loss of the physical policy document prevents you from claiming the money; insurers maintain digital records of all active policies.
- Don't rely on the "deadline" provided by an unknown caller to skip safety steps; legitimate insurers will put requirements in writing.
- Don't click on links in emails that claim to be "urgent policy updates" or "beneficiary notifications."

## What to do now
1. **Request the full details of the request in writing.** **If someone has contacted you claiming there is a deadline, ask them to provide the insurer’s full legal name, the claim number, and the specific policy number.**
   - If they refuse or insist on immediate payment to "hold" the funds, stop the conversation.
2. **Contact the most recent employer’s benefits department.** **Ask if the deceased person had group life insurance or a supplemental policy through work.**
   - These policies are common and often have simpler search processes.
   - Request the name of the insurance carrier (e.g., MetLife, Prudential, NY Life) and their group policy number.
3. **Submit a request through the NAIC Life Insurance Policy Locator.** **This free tool notifies all participating US insurers to search their records for the deceased person's details.**
   - You will need the deceased person's full legal name, Social Security Number (SSN), date of birth, and date of death.
   - If they had an ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) instead of an SSN, use that (it always starts with the number 9).
   - Note: It typically takes up to 90 days for insurers to respond if a match is found.
4. **Locate a certified copy of the death certificate.** **Most insurers and the NAIC tool require this to finalize any search or claim.**
   - You can obtain this from the state or county Vital Records office where the death occurred.
5. **Search for premium payments in bank records.** **Check the last 12 months of bank statements or canceled checks for payments to any insurance company.**
   - Look for terms like "Life Prem," "Ins Service," or names of known insurance providers.
6. **Find your state’s Department of Insurance.** **Use the NAIC Map tool to find the official contact information for the insurance regulator in the state where the deceased lived.**
   - They can verify if an insurance company is licensed to do business and can help if you believe you are being treated unfairly.
7. **Check official unclaimed property databases.** **Search the name of the deceased on missingmoney.com or the state treasurer’s website.**
   - Be aware that life insurance funds may not appear here until 3 to 5 years after the death (the "dormancy period").

## What can wait
- You don't need to hire a private investigator or a "policy finding service" right now.
- You don't need to have the physical, original paper policy to receive a payout.
- You don't need to settle the entire estate or finish probate before starting the search.
- You don't need to respond to "urgent" demands for information within minutes or hours.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for families to be unable to find original life insurance paperwork. Insurers are legally required to maintain these records, and there are established state-level processes to help you find them. If you feel a "deadline" is being used to scare you into acting quickly, take a breath; legitimate financial processes allow for a safe pause to verify identity.

## Scope note
This guide is for the immediate stabilization of a missing-paperwork crisis and the prevention of scams. It does not cover legal disputes over beneficiaries, contested deaths, or tax implications of insurance payouts.

## Important note
This guide provides general procedural information and is not legal or financial advice. Life insurance laws and "contestability periods" (usually 2 years from policy start) vary by state. If the policy was purchased very recently, the insurer may require more time for investigation.
