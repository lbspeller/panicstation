# What to do if… you receive a letter about an unpaid fine and potential driving restrictions you did not know about (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the letter as time-sensitive, but do not pay through links, QR codes, or phone numbers on the letter until you verify it through official state driver-licensing and court channels you find yourself. Until you confirm your current license status, avoid driving if the letter says your license may already be suspended, restricted, or not eligible.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click payment links, scan QR codes, or call a number printed in the letter until you have checked the agency or court independently.
- Do not keep driving “until it is sorted” if the letter suggests your license may already be suspended or restricted; depending on the state and facts, driving while suspended may lead to citations, towing, arrest, or new penalties.
- Do not ignore it because you never received the original ticket; missed mail, an old address, or a missed court date can still create a license hold or court problem.
- Do not assume every state handles this the same way; driver-licensing agencies, courts, fees, hearings, and reinstatement steps vary by state and county.
- Do not give your Social Security number, bank details, card details, or photo ID to an unverified caller, texter, email sender, or payment page.
- Do not pay “just to be safe” until you know whether paying closes the case, counts as admitting the violation, or is only one part of clearing the license hold.

## What to do now
1. **Get the letter under control.** Take clear photos of every page, including the envelope if you still have it. Write down the issuing agency, court name, county, case or citation number, deadline, claimed fine amount, and any stated suspension or restriction date.

2. **Find your official state driver-licensing agency yourself.** Use your state government website or the USA.gov state motor vehicle services finder. Your agency may be called driver services, department of licensing, department of public safety, motor vehicle division, or another state name.

3. **Check your license status with the official driver-licensing agency, not through the letter.** Ask:
   - Is my license currently valid, suspended, restricted, or pending action?
   - What case, citation, court, or reason code is listed?
   - What is the effective date?
   - What must happen before my record shows eligible or clear?
   - Is there a reinstatement fee, separate court requirement, or waiting period?

4. **Verify the court case with the court clerk listed on the official court website.** Do not rely on a collection number or payment link until the court confirms it. Ask:
   - Is this citation or case real?
   - What address were notices sent to?
   - Is the problem failure to pay, failure to appear, failure to comply, or something else?
   - Does the court require payment, a court appearance, a hearing request, proof of correction, or another step?
   - How does the court report clearance to the driver-licensing agency?

5. **If you did not know about the fine, ask about options before paying.** Use plain words: “I did not receive the original notice. What can I do before I pay?” Ask whether there is a way to reopen the matter, recall a failure-to-appear action, request a hearing, explain lack of notice, or set a payment plan if one is available.

6. **Ask what payment would and would not do.** Before paying, ask the court or licensing agency:
   - Does payment close the case or count as admitting the violation?
   - Will payment alone clear the hold or suspension?
   - Do I also need a court clearance, reinstatement fee, proof of compliance, or updated driver record?
   - How will I know the license record has changed?

7. **If you need to drive for essential reasons, ask only about legal options.** Some states may have restricted, hardship, occupational, or temporary driving options in limited situations. Ask the driver-licensing agency what exists in your state and do not drive unless the agency confirms you are currently allowed.

8. **Check for identity or record mix-ups.** If the name, date of birth, vehicle, address, location, or ticket details do not match you, tell both the court and driver-licensing agency that you believe this may be a misidentification, clerical error, or identity-theft issue. Ask what documents they need to review or correct the record.

9. **Make a short “do not make it worse” plan.** Arrange alternate transport if suspension or restriction is possible, save screenshots or confirmation numbers from official sites, keep a call log with dates and names, and set reminders for any deadline in the letter.

10. **Update your address through official channels.** If you moved or mail was unreliable, update your address with the driver-licensing agency and any court handling the case. Keep proof of the update.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to fight the ticket on the facts; first confirm whether the letter is real, whether your license is affected, and which court controls the case.
- You do not need to write a long explanation right now; get the case number, current license status, deadline, and required next step first.
- You do not need to pay immediately through the letter to stop panic; verification through official channels comes first.
- You do not need to solve transport, court strategy, fees, and identity concerns all at once; start with license status, court status, and safe transport for the next day or two.

## Important reassurance
This kind of notice can happen after a move, missed mail, old citation, court reporting issue, or data mismatch. Verifying first protects you from scams and helps you find the real agency, real case, and safest next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: confirm the notice, confirm your license status, identify the court case, avoid unsafe driving, and prevent escalation. Later decisions may need local legal aid, a traffic attorney, identity-theft support, or direct help from the court or driver-licensing agency.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Driver-license and traffic-court rules vary by state and county. If you are not sure whether you are currently allowed to drive, do not drive until you have confirmed your status through the official state driver-licensing agency.
