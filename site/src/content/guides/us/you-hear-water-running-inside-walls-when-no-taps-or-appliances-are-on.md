# What to do if… you hear water running inside walls when no taps or appliances are on (USA)

## Short answer
Assume there may be a hidden leak. Shut off the main water valve if you can do it safely, then contact a plumber, landlord, or building management right away.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore it or wait until morning if you can safely shut off the water now.
- Don’t cut drywall, drill, or open walls in a rush.
- Don’t touch outlets, switches, cords, or a breaker panel if you are wet, standing in water, or the area is damp.
- Don’t force a stuck valve hard enough that it could break.
- Don’t run faucets, showers, or appliances to “test” the noise while you suspect an active leak.
- Don’t stand under a sagging, bulging, or actively dripping ceiling.

## What to do now
1. Take a quick safety scan. Look for fresh wet spots, dripping, pooling water, a sagging ceiling, water near outlets or lights, or any sparking or burning smell. Keep people and pets away from wet or sagging areas.

2. Shut off the main water valve to your home if you can reach it safely. Common locations include where the water line enters the house, near the water heater, in a basement, utility room, garage, or at the meter outside. Turn the valve fully closed without forcing it.

3. Confirm whether the water is isolated. Open one cold faucet briefly at the lowest safe sink or tub and see whether flow slows or stops. Listen again for the wall sound and check whether visible dripping slows.

4. Check the water meter if you can access it safely. With all water use stopped, a moving leak indicator or changing meter reading may suggest a leak. If the meter still appears to move after the main valve is closed, tell the plumber or water utility because the valve may not be fully closed or the leak may be near the service line.

5. Treat water near electrical systems as urgent. Do not touch a breaker panel if you would need to stand in water or reach through a damp area. If the panel and your path to it are clearly dry, you may shut off power to the affected circuit or main breaker; otherwise, stay away and get urgent help. If there is arcing, sparking, smoke, rapid flooding, or a ceiling that may collapse, move to safety and call 911.

6. Contact the right responsible person now.
   - Homeowner: call a plumber and say you hear water running inside a wall, no taps or appliances are on, and the main water valve is off or unreachable.
   - Renter: call your landlord or property manager’s emergency maintenance line and say there may be an active hidden leak.
   - Condo or HOA building: notify building management or the HOA, because a leak can affect other units.

7. Limit damage only where it is safe. Put a bucket or towels under visible drips, move electronics, papers, and valuables away from wet areas, and keep people away from water near outlets, cords, lights, or appliances.

8. Document the situation quickly. Take photos or video of wet spots, ceiling or wall changes, the water meter, the shutoff valve position, and any visible dripping. Note the time you first heard the sound and whether it changed after the valve was closed.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide right now how repairs will be paid for.
- You don’t need to decide right now whether to file an insurance claim.
- You don’t need to open walls, remove flooring, or start major cleanup beyond safe, basic containment.
- You don’t need to identify the exact pipe or cause before calling for help.

## Important reassurance
A hidden water sound is unsettling because you cannot see the problem. The key first steps are simple: stop the water if you safely can, stay away from electrical or sagging areas, and get the responsible repair help involved.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilize a suspected hidden leak. Repairs, drying, mold prevention, insurance decisions, and responsibility for damage may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This guide is general information and not legal, medical, financial, insurance, plumbing, electrical, or other professional advice. If you cannot safely shut off the water, or there are signs of electrical danger, rapid flooding, or structural sagging, prioritize safety and get urgent professional help.
