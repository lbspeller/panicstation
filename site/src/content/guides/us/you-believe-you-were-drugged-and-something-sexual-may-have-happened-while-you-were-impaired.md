# What to do if… you believe you were drugged and something sexual may have happened while you were impaired (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safe location immediately and seek medical care at an emergency department—requesting a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) or Sexual Assault Forensic Examiner (SAFE) if you want the option of evidence collection.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to reconstruct the timeline or "figure it out" by yourself while in the immediate aftermath.
- Do not shower, bathe, brush your teeth, or change clothes before seeking medical care if you want the option of forensic evidence collection.
- Do not discard the clothing you were wearing; place it in a paper bag (not plastic) if you must change.
- Do not confront or message the person you suspect of drugging or assaulting you right now.
- Do not assume a lack of memory or the presence of alcohol/drugs means an assault did not occur or cannot be reported.
- Do not drive if you are still feeling sedated, dizzy, or uncoordinated.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause immediately.** Go to a trusted friend’s home or a staffed public location like a hospital or police station.
2. **Seek medical care as soon as possible, ideally within the first 72 to 120 hours.** The window for certain medications (like HIV prevention) is 72 hours, and forensic evidence is typically collected within 120 hours.
3. **Ask the hospital staff specifically for a SANE or SAFE exam.** This is a specialized medical-forensic exam performed by a trained nurse.
   - You can receive medical treatment for injuries or drugging without consenting to a forensic kit.
   - In most US jurisdictions, the forensic exam is free of charge under the Violence Against Women Act (VAWA).
4. **Request a toxicology screen specifically for "drug-facilitated sexual assault."** Many "date rape" drugs leave the system within 6 to 12 hours, making early testing critical for detection.
5. **Contact the National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-HOPE (4673) for confidential support.** They can help you identify hospitals with SANE programs and provide an advocate to meet you at the hospital.
6. **Ask for a sexual assault advocate to be present during your hospital visit.** Advocates provide emotional support and help explain your rights and options without pressuring you to report to police.
7. **Record essential details in a secure location if you feel able to do so.**
   - Location and names of people you were with.
   - Approximate times of last memory and waking up.
   - Symptoms you experienced (extreme drowsiness, nausea, loss of motor control).
   - Screenshots of ride-share apps, texts, or digital payments.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you want to file a formal police report or pursue a criminal case.
- You do not need to have a clear memory of the events to receive medical care or a forensic exam.
- You do not need to contact the venue, mutual friends, or the suspected individual.
- You do not need to worry about medical bills for the forensic portion of the exam, as federal law requires this to be provided at no cost to the survivor.

## Important reassurance
Feeling confused, numb, or having gaps in your memory is a common reaction to both trauma and sedating substances. A patchy memory does not mean your experience is invalid, and you are not responsible for the actions of others while you were impaired.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm reduction for the immediate aftermath of a suspected drugging or assault. It does not cover long-term legal strategies or psychological recovery.

## Important note
This guide contains general procedural information and is not a substitute for professional medical or legal advice. If you are experiencing a medical emergency or are in immediate physical danger, call 911.
