# What to do if… you receive a message claiming you must pay a fine or fee immediately to avoid enforcement (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the message as a scam and do not click any links or provide information. Verify the claim by contacting the relevant agency directly using a phone number or website you locate independently through an official .gov portal.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links, scan QR codes, or download attachments provided in the message.
- Do not call the phone number that sent the text or any number listed within the message.
- Do not pay using gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfers, or peer-to-peer apps (like Zelle or Venmo), as no legitimate government agency demands these.
- Do not reply to the message to "opt out" or argue, as this confirms your number is active to the scammer.
- Do not assume a "case number" or "citation number" makes the message legitimate; these are often generated randomly.

## What to do now
1. **Disengage from the message immediately.** Close the messaging app and wait 60 seconds before taking any further action to allow the sense of urgency to fade.
2. **Document the evidence for reporting.** Take a screenshot of the message that clearly shows the sender's phone number or email address and the timestamp.
3. **Locate official contact information independently.** 
   - Search for the official website of the agency mentioned (e.g., "Florida DMV official site" or "E-ZPass New York") and look for a ".gov" or verified official domain.
   - Access the agency's "citation search" or "toll balance" tool directly through their verified portal to check for outstanding balances.
4. **Call the official agency or court clerk.** Use the number found on the verified government website to speak with a representative and confirm if any debt exists.
5. **Secure your financial accounts if you interacted with a link.** 
   - Call your bank or credit card company immediately using the number on the back of your card.
   - Request to cancel any compromised cards and dispute any unauthorized or pending transactions.
6. **Protect your identity if personal data was shared.** 
   - Visit IdentityTheft.gov to create a recovery plan.
   - Place a credit freeze with the three major bureaus (Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion) if you shared a Social Security number or driver's license details.
7. **Report the message to authorities.** 
   - Forward the text message to **7726** (SPAM) to alert your wireless carrier.
   - File a report with the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.
   - If you lost money, file a complaint with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) at ic3.gov.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine how you will pay a potential fine until you have confirmed it is legitimate.
- You do not need to investigate the source of the scam or track down the scammers yourself.
- You can delay changing all of your passwords until your immediate financial accounts are secured and your credit is frozen.

## Important reassurance
These messages are designed to trigger panic through threats of arrest or license suspension. Receiving one does not mean you have a warrant or an unpaid debt. Taking the time to verify through official channels is the standard and safest way to handle government-related fees.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling suspicious enforcement messages only. It does not provide legal defense for legitimate traffic violations or long-term financial recovery services.

## Important note
Legitimate tolling agencies and courts typically communicate through physical mail. Any text message demanding immediate payment via a link should be treated as fraudulent. Always verify through official .gov websites or the Central Violations Bureau for federal tickets.
