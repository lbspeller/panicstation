# What to do if… you are asked to provide a DNA sample to help identify a person who died (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the official identity of the requester through a public agency directory, then provide the sample only in the presence of an authorized official who documents the chain of custody and explains the specific privacy protections for family reference samples.

## Do not do these things
- Don't provide a DNA sample or personal identifiers to anyone who cannot be verified through an official government or agency directory.
- Don't perform a self-collection or use a consumer home-testing kit as a substitute for an official request unless specifically instructed by a verified medical examiner.
- Don't meet a requester in an informal or private location; all collections should occur at an agency office or a documented official site.
- Don't feel pressured to provide a sample immediately if you have not received a written explanation of how the data will be used and stored.
- Don't assume your DNA will be entered into a criminal database; family reference samples are typically searched only against unidentified human remains indices.

## What to do now
1. **Request the caller's full name, agency, badge or employee ID, and a case reference number.** Write these details down immediately and inform them that you will call back once you have verified the request through the agency’s main switchboard.
2. **Verify the requester through an independent, trusted channel.** Look up the official public phone number for the Medical Examiner, Coroner, or Law Enforcement agency mentioned and ask to be connected to the specific person or department handling the case.
3. **Ask for the specific purpose of the sample in writing.** Confirm if the sample is a "Family Reference Sample" (FRS) intended solely for the identification of a deceased individual or missing person.
4. **Confirm the collection method and location.** **Ask for the collection to take place at a secure agency office or an official partner clinic.**
   - Ensure the person collecting the sample is an authorized official or trained medical professional.
   - Verify that they have a standard "Consent for Collection" form and a chain of custody log.
5. **Ask about the scope of the DNA search.** **Inquire if the profile will be uploaded to the National DNA Index System (NDIS) and verify that it will only be searched against the Unidentified Human Index.**
6. **Inquire about the data retention and destruction policy.** **Ask what happens to your physical sample and digital profile once a positive identification is made or if the case is closed.**
   - Request information on how to submit a written request for the removal of your profile in the future.
7. **Read and sign the consent form only after all questions are answered.** **Ensure you receive a full copy of the signed consent form and any associated case paperwork.**
8. **Bring a trusted support person to the collection appointment.** This person can help you track the information provided, take notes, and offer immediate support during the process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to make immediate funeral or burial arrangements until the identification is officially confirmed.
- You do not need to research complex forensic genetic laws or CODIS regulations.
- You do not need to notify the wider family or make estate decisions until you have a confirmed update from the investigating agency.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel hesitant or overwhelmed when asked for a DNA sample. This request is a standard procedural step used when other identification methods, like dental records or fingerprints, are unavailable or inconclusive. Providing a sample is a voluntary act designed to help resolve a case and prevent misidentification. Taking the time to verify the request and understand your rights is a protective measure, not a sign of non-compliance.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate steps of verification, consent, and sample provision. It does not address long-term legal issues regarding estate inheritance or state-specific genetic privacy litigation that may arise later in 2026.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. DNA collection and retention policies are subject to federal and state laws, which are evolving in 2026 regarding genetic privacy. If the agency cannot provide clear answers about data usage or privacy, you may choose to pause and consult with legal counsel or a victim advocate.
