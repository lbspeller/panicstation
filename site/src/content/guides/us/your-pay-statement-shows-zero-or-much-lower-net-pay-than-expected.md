# What to do if… your pay statement shows zero or much lower net pay than expected (USA)

## Short answer
First work out whether the problem is a missing payment, missing gross pay, or deductions reducing your net pay. Then contact payroll in writing and ask for the line-by-line reason, any corrected pay stub, and the date and method of any correction.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the payroll system will self-correct next cycle if you cannot cover essentials.
- Do not ignore a surprise deduction, garnishment, levy, or benefit line; ask what it is and where it came from.
- Do not post your pay stub publicly or forward it widely; it can contain sensitive personal and payroll information.
- Do not quit on the spot, threaten, or sign anything saying you agree before you understand what happened.
- Do not leave the issue only as a phone call or hallway conversation; keep a written record.

## What to do now
1. **Separate the problem into one of three buckets.**
   - **The pay statement shows normal net pay, but your bank received nothing:** this may be a direct deposit, bank, account, or payroll delivery problem.
   - **The pay statement shows low or zero gross pay:** this may be missing hours, wrong pay rate, wrong job code, unpaid approved time, or the wrong pay period.
   - **The pay statement shows normal gross pay but low or zero net pay:** this may be taxes, benefits, retirement contributions, garnishment, levy, overpayment recovery, or another deduction.

2. **Check the pay statement before you contact payroll.**
   - Confirm the pay period dates.
   - Compare hours, salary, units, overtime, tips, commission, bonus, or shift differential against your records.
   - Look for any deduction, withholding, garnishment, benefit, repayment, or adjustment line that is new or much larger than usual.

3. **If the bank deposit is missing, check the payment route.**
   - Look for a pending deposit, return, reversal, or rejected deposit notice in your bank account.
   - Check whether payroll has the correct routing number, account number, account type, and deposit split.
   - If the money may have gone to the wrong place or been transferred without permission, contact payroll and your bank promptly. Ask the bank how to dispute or trace the transfer and whether its electronic transfer error process applies.

4. **Send payroll or HR a short written message today.**
   Include:
   - the payday and pay period
   - what you expected to receive
   - what the pay statement shows
   - what your bank received, if anything
   - a request for the exact line item or payroll entry that caused the zero or low net pay
   - a request for a corrected calculation and corrected pay stub if there is an error
   - a request for the date and method of any corrected payment, including whether an off-cycle payment is possible

5. **Ask directly about the most common hidden causes.**
   - “Was there a garnishment, levy, child support order, tax order, or other agency or court order?”
   - “Was there a benefits, retirement, insurance, or pre-tax deduction change?”
   - “Was an overpayment from a prior check recovered from this check?”
   - “Were any hours, punches, approved time, overtime, tips, commissions, bonuses, or differentials missing?”
   - “Was my bank account, deposit split, or payroll profile changed?”

6. **Save the records before anything changes.**
   - Save the pay statement and any corrected version.
   - Save your timecard, schedule, clock records, approvals, commission records, tip records, offer letter, pay-rate notice, or recent deduction election if you have them.
   - Keep a simple timeline: payday, when you noticed the issue, who you contacted, what they said, and what correction date they gave.

7. **If payroll will not give a clear answer or the correction does not happen on the promised date, escalate.**
   - Use your employer’s internal route: manager, HR, payroll lead, or employee service center.
   - Ask for the issue to be treated as an urgent wage-payment correction, not a general inquiry.
   - If you still are not paid correctly, contact your state labor office about a wage claim and contact the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division to ask whether a federal wage complaint is available for your situation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to hire a lawyer, sue, resign, or make a long-term job decision.
- You do not need to prove intent right now; focus on the pay period, pay lines, records, and correction route.
- You do not need to research every federal and state wage rule at once; start by getting the payroll explanation in writing and saving the records.

## Important reassurance
A zero or much lower net pay amount can be caused by a traceable payroll entry, deduction change, garnishment order, missing hours, or deposit problem. The immediate goal is not to solve the whole employment dispute today; it is to identify the exact cause, protect your records, and push for a clear correction path.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to stabilize the moment, protect your records, and help you choose the right escalation route. Wage-payment rules, deduction rules, complaint routes, and deadlines vary by state and work arrangement, so later decisions may need help from a state labor office, the U.S. Department of Labor, a worker organization, or a qualified employment lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If missing pay means you cannot cover essentials, consider contacting the bill provider, landlord, lender, or service provider promptly to explain that there is an active payroll problem and that you are seeking an urgent correction.
