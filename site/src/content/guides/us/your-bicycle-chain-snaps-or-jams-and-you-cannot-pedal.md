# What to do if… your bicycle chain snaps or jams and you cannot pedal (USA)

## Short answer
Stop pedaling, brake smoothly, and get yourself and the bike out of traffic or off the trail before touching the chain.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep forcing the pedals; this can tighten a jam, damage the derailleur, or lock the wheel.
- Don’t put fingers near the chain, cassette, chainrings, derailleur, spokes, or brake rotor while the wheel can spin or the bike can roll.
- Don’t stop in a travel lane if you can safely coast or walk to a shoulder, driveway, parking lot, sidewalk, or trail edge.
- Don’t try to ride scooter-style in traffic when the bike cannot pedal normally.
- Don’t attempt a roadside repair beside fast traffic, in poor light, or while you are shaken or injured.

## What to do now
1. **Stop pedaling and keep the bike straight.** Use both brakes smoothly if you can. Aim for the nearest safer place, such as a shoulder, driveway, parking lot, wide sidewalk area, or trail edge.
2. **Dismount only when you have a safer pause.** Move yourself and the bike fully out of the lane or main trail line. If you are near an intersection, get beyond the turning area if that is safe.
3. **Make yourself visible.** Turn on bike lights or flashers if you have them. At night or in poor visibility, move toward a lit, visible place and face traffic only from a safe distance.
4. **Check the chain without putting your hands into moving parts.**
   - If the chain has snapped, look for loose chain pieces only if it is safe to retrieve them.
   - If the chain is wedged near the spokes, cassette, chainrings, crank, or derailleur, do not force the pedals or rear wheel.
5. **Only try a simple reset if it is clearly safe.** If the chain has only dropped off and is not wedged, move to a safe place, keep your fingers away from teeth, spokes, and the moving chain, and gently guide it back onto the chainring while turning the pedals slowly by hand. Stop immediately if it catches, tightens, or the wheel will not turn freely.
6. **Skip the repair if the chain is snapped or tightly jammed.** Walk the bike to a safer pickup point, call someone for a ride, use a ride service, or head to a nearby bike shop. If you have a quick link, a chain tool, and enough room away from traffic, you may be able to make a temporary repair; skip it if you are unsure.
7. **Get urgent help if the location is dangerous.** Call 911 if you are injured or stuck where you are in immediate danger, such as a live traffic lane, blind curve, bridge, tunnel, or fast road with no safe shoulder. If it is not an emergency, contact a local non-emergency police line, roadside assistance, a friend, or a local bike shop after you are in a safer place.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose why the chain failed right now.
- You do not need to decide whether the chain, cassette, chainrings, or derailleur need replacing today.
- You do not need to clean or lubricate the drivetrain until you are off the road and calm.
- You do not need to finish the ride if walking, pickup, or a repair shop is safer.

## Important reassurance
A snapped or jammed chain can feel sudden and frightening, but once you and the bike are out of traffic it is usually a mechanical problem, not a decision you have to solve immediately. The main success is getting to a safer pause without forcing the bike.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stop safely, avoid making the jam worse, and get out of danger. A later repair may need a bike mechanic or a careful check of the chain, cassette, chainrings, derailleur, spokes, and rear wheel.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not professional mechanical, medical, legal, or emergency-services advice. Follow local road rules and use your judgement about traffic, injury, visibility, and whether you need emergency help.
