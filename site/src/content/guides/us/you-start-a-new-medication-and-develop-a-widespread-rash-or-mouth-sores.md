# What to do if… you start a new medication and develop a widespread rash or mouth sores (USA)

## Short answer
A widespread rash, mouth sores, or burning eyes after starting a medication is a medical emergency: **seek urgent medical care immediately**, and call **911** or go to the nearest Emergency Room if you experience skin peeling, blisters, or trouble breathing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take another dose of the medication until a doctor tells you it is safe.
- Do not wait for the next scheduled appointment if the rash is spreading or you feel ill.
- Do not use over-the-counter creams, ointments, or allergy medications to treat the rash before being seen.
- Do not pop blisters, peel loose skin, or scrub the affected areas.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you are feeling dizzy, faint, confused, or have facial swelling.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency "red flag" symptoms and call 911 or go to the ER immediately if any are present.**
   - Respiratory issues: trouble breathing, wheezing, or a feeling that the throat is closing.
   - Visible swelling: sudden puffiness of the face, lips, tongue, or eyelids.
   - Skin damage: painful blisters, skin that is peeling/sloughing off, or "raw" weeping areas.
   - Mucous membrane involvement: sores in the mouth, throat, genitals, or eyes that are red, stinging, or crusted shut.
   - Systemic illness: high fever, severe body aches, or feeling extremely weak/faint.
2. **Contact your prescribing doctor or pharmacy immediately if your symptoms are currently mild but widespread.**
   - Clearly state that you have started a new medication and are developing a rash or sores.
   - Provide the medication name, the exact time of the last dose, and a description of the rash.
   - If it is after hours or you cannot reach the prescriber, proceed to urgent care or the ER rather than waiting for a call back.
3. **Stop the medication immediately unless you have been told it is dangerous to stop "cold turkey."**
   - For medications like certain anti-seizure drugs or steroids that require tapering, you must still seek emergency advice immediately to determine the safest way to stop while managing the reaction.
4. **Call Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222 for immediate triage and guidance.**
   - This service is free, confidential, and available 24/7 to help determine if your symptoms require an ER visit or if they can be managed at an urgent care center.
5. **Gather all medications and related information to bring with you to medical care.**
   - Include the prescription bottle, the pharmacy leaflet, any recently started over-the-counter supplements, and a timeline of when the symptoms began.
   - Take a photo of the rash in its current state in case it changes or fades before you are examined.
6. **Ensure the reaction is documented in your official medical records before you are discharged.**
   - Ask the provider to list the specific drug as an "allergy" or "adverse reaction" in your electronic health record.
   - Request written "return precautions" that specify which new symptoms should trigger a return to the hospital.

## What can wait
- Determining the exact clinical name of the reaction (such as SJS, TEN, or DRESS).
- Reporting the incident to the FDA MedWatch program (Form 3500B).
- Researching alternative medications or long-term treatment plans.
- Notifying your insurance company about the emergency visit.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed or frightened when your body reacts unexpectedly to a medication. Seeking immediate medical attention for a widespread rash or sores is the standard safety protocol and is not an overreaction. These symptoms are recognized as significant medical triggers that require professional assessment.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency identification only. It does not replace a clinical diagnosis or a formal allergy evaluation by a specialist.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and does not constitute medical advice. Medication reactions can escalate rapidly or occur weeks after starting a drug; always err on the side of caution by seeking a professional physical examination.
