# What to do if… you start a new medication and develop a widespread rash or mouth sores (USA)

## Short answer
A widespread rash or mouth sores after starting a medication can be urgent. Get medical advice the same day, and call 911 or go to the ER now if you have trouble breathing, swelling of the face or tongue, blistering, peeling, severe skin pain, eye symptoms, fever, confusion, fainting, or you feel very ill.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “push through” more doses while the rash is spreading or you have mouth sores, unless a clinician has just told you to continue.
- Do not ignore blistering, peeling skin, fever, severe skin pain, sore or burning eyes, genital sores, face swelling, tongue swelling, throat tightness, dizziness, or feeling very sick.
- Do not self-treat with leftover antibiotics, steroids, or several new over-the-counter products to “see if it helps.”
- Do not pop blisters or peel skin.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel faint, confused, short of breath, or your face, lips, or tongue are swelling.

## What to do now
1. Check for emergency signs. Call 911 or go to the ER now if any of these are happening:
   - Trouble breathing, wheezing, throat tightness, trouble swallowing, or swelling of your lips, face, or tongue.
   - A rash with blisters, peeling, purple or dark areas, or very painful skin.
   - Sores in your mouth, sore or burning eyes, or sores on your genitals, especially with fever or a flu-like feeling.
   - Dizziness, fainting, confusion, collapse, blue or gray lips, or being hard to wake.
2. If you have a prescribed epinephrine auto-injector and you have serious allergic-reaction symptoms, use it as directed and still call 911.
3. If you are stable but the rash is widespread or you have mouth sores, contact the prescriber or a pharmacist urgently today.
   - Say the medication name, dose, start date, when the rash or sores began, and whether it is spreading or getting more painful.
   - If you cannot reach them promptly, use urgent care or the ER rather than waiting.
4. Do not take another dose until you have urgent medical advice, unless the label or a clinician has clearly warned you not to stop suddenly.
   - If it may be unsafe to stop suddenly, still seek urgent advice now and explain exactly what is happening.
5. Use Poison Control if you are unsure what level of care you need, or if there may have been a dosing error or interaction.
   - Call Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222.
   - Use 911 or the ER instead if you have emergency signs.
6. Bring useful information with you.
   - Bring medication bottles or photos of labels.
   - Include prescriptions, over-the-counter medicines, supplements, creams, patches, and anything new in the last few weeks.
   - Take clear photos of the rash from a distance and close up, and note any fever.
7. Before you leave medical care, ask for two clear safety instructions.
   - Ask whether the medicine should be added to your allergy or adverse-reaction list.
   - Ask what symptoms mean “return to the ER” or “call 911.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to figure out the exact diagnosis right now.
- You do not need to decide on a replacement medication by yourself right now.
- You do not need to report the reaction to FDA MedWatch before you are safe and assessed.
- You do not need detailed documentation beyond a simple timeline, medication list, and photos.

## Important reassurance
Taking this seriously is reasonable. Most rashes are not the worst-case scenario, but a widespread rash with mouth sores after a medication is one of the times when quick medical assessment is the safer choice.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about stopping, restarting, replacing, or labeling a medication as an allergy may need help from a clinician who can review your full history.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, diagnosis, or professional care. If symptoms are worsening, you are unsure, or you cannot get rapid guidance, choose urgent assessment, using the ER or 911 when appropriate.
