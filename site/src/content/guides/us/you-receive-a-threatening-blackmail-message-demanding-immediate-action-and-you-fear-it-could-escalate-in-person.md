# What to do if… you receive a threatening blackmail message demanding immediate action and you fear it could escalate in person (USA)

## Short answer
Do not reply, pay, meet, or click anything. Put real-world safety around you first, preserve the message, and use official reporting routes; call 911 if you feel in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t respond to “buy time,” negotiate, argue, or send a final warning; engagement can give them more chances to pressure you.
- Don’t pay with crypto, gift cards, wire transfer, payment apps, or any other method.
- Don’t send more information, photos, videos, passwords, verification codes, or proof of identity.
- Don’t agree to meet them or go somewhere alone to “settle it.”
- Don’t click links, install apps, open attachments, or use any “reporting site” the sender gives you.
- Don’t delete the conversation, email, account, or payment details in a panic before preserving what you can.
- Don’t post publicly about it in a way that reveals your location, routine, workplace, school, or who is helping you.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safety buffer first.**  
   If you are alone, move near other people you trust, such as family, roommates, a neighbor, workplace security, school staff, or a staffed public place. If you believe someone may come to you soon, is nearby, or may hurt you, call **911**.

2. **Treat specific in-person threats as urgent.**  
   Without contacting the sender, check whether the message includes your home address, workplace, school, current location, a specific time, a weapon, or a direct threat of violence. If it does, call **911** or your local police. If the threat is not immediate, use your local police non-emergency number to make a report.

3. **Preserve the message before changing anything.**  
   Take screenshots that show the sender, username, phone number or email address, timestamps, threat wording, profile page, payment demand, crypto wallet address, links, and any address or location details they mention. Keep the original email or message thread if you can.

4. **Reduce contact without losing evidence.**  
   After saving the evidence, use the platform’s report tool if available, then mute, restrict, or block the sender. Do not keep reading repeated threats if a trusted person can help you save new messages.

5. **Secure the accounts they mention.**  
   If the message includes an old or current password, change that password on the real account and anywhere else you reused it. Turn on two-factor authentication using an authenticator app or security key where possible. Do not use links from the blackmail message to change passwords.

6. **Use official USA reporting routes.**  
   For immediate danger, call **911**. For cyber-enabled extortion or scams, file a report with the **FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center** at **https://www.ic3.gov/**. Type the address yourself and check that it ends in **.gov**, because fake IC3 lookalike sites exist. You can also report scam demands to the **FTC** at **https://reportfraud.ftc.gov/**.

7. **If the message involves intimate images or video, do not send more.**  
   Treat it as extortion, even if you are embarrassed or unsure whether the sender really has anything. Report it through the platform, IC3, and, if the targeted person is under 18, the **NCMEC CyberTipline**. If a child or teen is involved, bring in a trusted adult or local law enforcement; this is not something they need to handle alone.

8. **Add a short-term in-person safety plan for today.**  
   Tell one trusted person what happened and share the screenshots with them. Ask your front desk, school office, workplace security, or household not to let in anyone asking for you. Avoid isolated parking lots, empty transit stops, and opening the door to unexpected visitors while you are still assessing the threat.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to identify the person behind the message right now.
- You don’t need to decide today about moving, changing jobs, changing schools, or deleting accounts.
- You don’t need to prove anything to the sender.
- You don’t need perfect documentation before making a report; start with what you have.
- You don’t need to handle this alone before involving a trusted person, local police, IC3, the FTC, or NCMEC if a minor is involved.

## Important reassurance
These messages are designed to create urgency, shame, and isolation. Pausing contact, getting physically safer, preserving the message, and using official routes are strong first moves even when you feel shaken.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes and hours. If the threats continue, become stalking or repeated harassment, involve intimate images, or include a real-world location, later decisions may need help from law enforcement, victim-support services, a school or workplace safety team, a lawyer, or a qualified security professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, mental-health, security, or other professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call 911. If you are unsure whether the threat is serious enough, you can still contact local police, IC3, the FTC, or NCMEC where relevant and ask what route fits.
