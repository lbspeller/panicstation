# What to do if… you are told a critically ill relative may be transferred to another hospital and you must respond quickly (USA)

## Short answer
Ask for the **attending physician** or **charge nurse** to confirm the specific medical reason for the move and verify that a receiving facility has officially accepted the patient. Immediately identify yourself as the legal decision-maker if you hold Medical Power of Attorney or are the recognized health care proxy.

## Do not do these things
- Do not refuse an urgent medical transfer until you understand if the physician has certified it as medically necessary for stabilization under EMTALA.
- Do not assume that being the "next of kin" automatically grants legal access to medical records without an established proxy or surrogate status.
- Do not permit multiple family members to call the unit for updates; designate one person to handle all communication with the transfer team.
- Do not drive to the new hospital until you have the exact facility name, unit, and room number (or check-in instructions).
- Do not delay the process by searching for paper documents if you can send a digital photo of an Advance Directive or POA to the hospital's secure intake line or portal.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the clinical lead and the coordinator.** Ask to speak with the **attending physician** for the clinical plan and the **Case Manager or Social Worker** for the logistics of the move. 
2. **Record the four essential transfer details immediately.**
   - **Receiving facility:** The exact name and campus address.
   - **Reason for transfer:** Whether it is for a higher level of specialty care, specialized equipment (like ECMO), or facility capacity issues.
   - **Acceptance status:** The name of the accepting physician and the specific unit at the new hospital.
   - **Transport method:** Whether the move is via ground critical care transport or air ambulance.
3. **Verify the legal decision-maker on file.** **Ask the staff who is currently listed as the patient's legal surrogate or personal representative.**
   - If you are the Health Care Proxy or Medical Power of Attorney, state this clearly.
   - Ask for the hospital's secure email or fax number to send a photo of the document right now.
4. **Identify the surrogate if no paperwork exists.** **Ask the hospital social worker what the "state hierarchy for surrogate decision-making" is if the patient cannot speak for themselves.** Most states have a specific order (spouse, adult children, parents) that the hospital must follow.
5. **Ask the safety and stability questions.** **Inquire about the risks versus the benefits of the move.**
   - "What specific treatment is available there that is not available here?"
   - "Is the patient currently stable enough for the duration of the transport?"
   - "What level of critical care staff will be in the vehicle/aircraft during the move?"
6. **Request the written transfer protocol.** **Ask to see a copy of the hospital's standardized transfer policy.** Under 2026 CMS standards (42 CFR § 482.43), hospitals must maintain and follow written inter-hospital transfer protocols.
7. **Obtain the direct "handoff" contact.** **Get the phone number for the specific unit at the receiving hospital.** Ask for a "transfer summary" or "face sheet" with the patient's Medical Record Number (MRN) and the name of the doctor who will be in charge upon arrival.
8. **Clarify the clinical urgency versus the administrative timing.** **If you are being pressured to sign papers for insurance purposes, ask if the transfer is "clinically urgent" or "administratively requested."** Under EMTALA, an urgent transfer for stabilization cannot be delayed for insurance authorization.
9. **Identify items for transport.** **Confirm which personal items and medical devices (like hearing aids or dentures) will travel with the patient.** Ensure a list of these items is included in the transport records.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle the final hospital bill or insurance co-pays before the transfer occurs.
- You do not need to decide on long-term rehabilitation or post-hospital placement during the acute transfer window.
- You do not need to notify the entire extended family until the patient has safely arrived at the receiving facility.
- You do not need to fully understand every medical procedure; focus only on why the move is happening right now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel pressured when a hospital move is announced. The medical team is often moving quickly to secure a specialized bed that may not be available later. Your role is to ensure the communication lines are open and that the right legal authority is recognized so there are no delays in care.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hour of a transfer notice. It does not cover long-term care planning, legal appeals for discharge, or medical second opinions once the patient is stabilized at the new facility.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or medical advice. Patient rights and surrogate hierarchies vary significantly by state. Federal EMTALA (Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act) rules require hospitals to provide "appropriate transfers" for emergency conditions regardless of the patient's ability to pay. HIPAA allows providers to share information with family members involved in care if the provider determines it is in the patient's best interest during an emergency.
