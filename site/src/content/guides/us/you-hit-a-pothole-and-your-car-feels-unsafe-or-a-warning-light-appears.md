# What to do if… you hit a pothole and your car feels unsafe or a warning light appears (USA)

## Short answer
Slow down, keep the vehicle steady, and get to a safer place to stop. If the car pulls, shakes, will not steer normally, loses tire pressure, or shows a serious warning light, do not keep driving; call for roadside help or a tow.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep freeway speed to see whether the problem goes away.
- Do not swerve, brake hard, or test the steering in traffic.
- Do not stand outside the car on a narrow shoulder or in moving traffic to inspect damage.
- Do not ignore a tire sidewall bubble, cut, flat tire, visible rim damage, or pressure that keeps dropping.
- Do not assume a tire-pressure warning is only a sensor issue after a hard pothole hit.
- Do not drive on a spare or repair kit faster or farther than the vehicle manual allows.

## What to do now
1. **Control the car first.** Ease off the accelerator, hold the steering wheel firmly, increase following distance, and avoid sudden steering or braking.
2. **Call 911 if there is immediate danger.** Do this if anyone is injured, there has been a crash, the vehicle is blocking a live lane, there is fire or smoke, or you cannot get out of a dangerous traffic position.
3. **Get out of the highest-risk place if the car is controllable.** Take the nearest exit, pull into a parking lot, or use a wide safe shoulder rather than stopping in a narrow or blind spot.
4. **Make the stopped vehicle visible.** Turn on hazard lights. If being outside the vehicle is unsafe, stay inside with your seatbelt on while you call for help.
5. **Treat serious warning lights conservatively.** Red or flashing warnings, brake warnings, steering warnings, oil-pressure warnings, temperature warnings, and charging-system warnings can mean you should stop driving. Once safely stopped, check the owner’s manual if you can; if you are unsure, arrange roadside assistance or a tow.
6. **Do a quick outside check only from a safe place.** Look for a flat or soft tire, sidewall bubble, cut, visible rim bend or crack, hanging parts, fluid leaking, or a wheel sitting at an odd angle.
7. **If the TPMS light is on, check pressure only when it is safe.** Use a tire gauge if you have one, and use the pressure listed on the Tire and Loading Information label near the driver’s door or in the owner’s manual. Do not drive if the pressure will not hold, drops again quickly, or there is sidewall or rim damage.
8. **Use a conservative drive-or-tow decision.** Do not drive if the car pulls hard, the steering feels loose or off-center, there is strong vibration, a tire is flat or bulging, a rim is cracked or badly bent, fluid is leaking, or a warning light suggests a serious fault.
9. **If it seems safe to move a short distance, keep it short and slow.** Avoid highways and drive only to the nearest safe place, tire shop, or mechanic for inspection.
10. **Ask for a pothole-impact safety check.** Ask the shop to inspect the tires, sidewalls, wheels/rims, steering, suspension, and alignment. Tell them the pulling, shaking, noise, or warning light started immediately after the pothole.
11. **Re-check tire pressure soon.** A slow leak or rim problem may show up after the first stop. If the pressure drops again, stop driving and get the tire and wheel inspected.
12. **Note details later if you can do it safely.** Record the date, time, road name, cross street or mile marker, direction of travel, symptoms, and which warning light appeared. Take photos only from a safe location.
13. **Move over and slow down for stopped vehicles with flashing lights.** State law may require this, and all states have Move Over laws for emergency vehicles with flashing lights.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now about insurance, reimbursement, or a road-damage claim.
- You do not need to diagnose whether the problem is the tire, wheel, suspension, steering, or alignment.
- You do not need to return to the pothole location.
- You do not need to choose a repair plan until the vehicle has been safely inspected.

## Important reassurance
A hard pothole hit can make steering feel wrong, trigger a tire-pressure warning, bend a wheel, damage a tire, or knock alignment out. Stopping safely and getting the vehicle checked is a normal, practical response, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce risk immediately after the impact. Repairs, reports, claims, and longer-term decisions may need help from a mechanic, tire professional, insurer, roadside provider, or local road authority.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, mechanical, financial, insurance, or professional safety advice. If you feel unsafe driving, the car is pulling or shaking, you see tire or wheel damage, pressure will not hold, or a serious warning light is on, prioritize stopping safely and getting professional roadside assistance or a tow.
