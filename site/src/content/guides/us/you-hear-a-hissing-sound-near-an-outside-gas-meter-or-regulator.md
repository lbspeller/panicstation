# What to do if… you hear a hissing sound near an outside gas meter or regulator (USA)

## Short answer
Treat a faint hiss near a gas meter or regulator as a suspected gas leak: leave the area on foot and call 911 from a safe location. Then call the gas utility emergency number if you know it.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t smoke, light matches, use a lighter, or use any open flame near the suspected leak.
- Don’t turn electrical switches on or off, ring doorbells, use power tools, start vehicles, or use phones in the suspected leak area.
- Don’t try to tighten fittings, open the regulator, shut valves, or repair anything yourself.
- Don’t stay close to the meter or regulator to keep listening, filming, or checking.
- Don’t go back into the area until emergency responders or the gas utility say it is safe.

## What to do now
1. Back away from the meter or regulator right away, and move other people and pets away with you.
2. Leave the area on foot. If you can do so without getting closer to the hiss, move upwind and well away from the meter, regulator, building wall, and any nearby gas smell.
3. From a safe location, call 911 and say you suspect a gas leak outside near a gas meter or regulator, with a hissing sound.
4. If you know your gas utility’s emergency number, call it after 911 from the same safe location. If you do not know the utility, stay with 911 instructions.
5. If you can warn a nearby neighbor without entering the suspected leak area, do it by voice or by knocking with your hand. Do not use a doorbell.
6. Wait away from the area for emergency responders and the utility crew, and follow their instructions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether the sound is a normal regulator noise or a leak.
- You do not need to find the source, take photos, record video, or test the air.
- You do not need to call a plumber, HVAC contractor, landlord, or insurer until the immediate safety check is handled.

## Important reassurance
A faint hiss can have more than one cause, but treating it as a possible gas leak is the safer first step. Calling for help is appropriate even if the area is later found to be safe.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repairs, service restoration, property access, or responsibility may need the gas utility, emergency responders, a qualified professional, or other specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information only and is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, emergency, or other professional advice. In a suspected gas leak, follow 911, emergency responder, and gas utility instructions.
