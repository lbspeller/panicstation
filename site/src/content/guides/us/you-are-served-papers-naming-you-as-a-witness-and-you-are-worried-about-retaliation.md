# What to do if… you are served papers naming you as a witness and you are worried about retaliation (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the issuing attorney or Victim-Witness Coordinator listed on your subpoena immediately to report your safety concerns and request protection protocols. For immediate physical threats or active harassment, call 911 or contact local law enforcement to report witness intimidation.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore the subpoena; failure to appear can result in a warrant for your arrest or contempt of court charges.
- Don't contact the defendant, their legal team, or their associates to discuss your fear or negotiate your testimony.
- Don't post about the subpoena, the case details, or your location on social media.
- Don't delete any threatening messages, call logs, or digital evidence of intimidation.
- Don't agree to meet anyone "off the record" to discuss the case.
- Don't assume you are responsible for your own security at the courthouse.

## What to do now
1. **Identify a safe pause and assess immediate risk.**
   **If you are in immediate physical danger or being followed, call 911 now.**
2. **Locate the contact information for the "Issuing Party" on the subpoena.**
   **Find the name and phone number of the attorney (prosecutor or defense) or the court official who sent the papers.**
   - Look for terms like "Assistant U.S. Attorney," "District Attorney," or "Clerk of Court."
3. **Call the issuing office and request to speak with a Victim-Witness Coordinator.**
   **State clearly: "I have been served a subpoena for [Case Number] and I am concerned about my safety and potential retaliation."**
   - Ask for "Witness Assistance" or "Victim Services" if the specific coordinator is unavailable.
4. **Report specific threats of retaliation to law enforcement.**
   **If you have received a direct threat, file a formal police report for witness tampering or intimidation.**
   - Provide the case number and the name of the court where you are subpoenaed.
   - Obtain a copy of the report or the incident number.
5. **Request courthouse safety accommodations from the court clerk or prosecutor.**
   **Ask for specific security measures, such as escorted entry, a secure waiting area away from the public, or separate arrival times.**
   - In federal cases, ask about the "Victim and Witness Assistance Program" protocols.
6. **Secure your digital presence and location sharing.**
   **Disable location services on your mobile devices and set all social media profiles to private.**
   - Instruct close contacts not to tag you or share your current location until the proceedings are concluded.
7. **Maintain a chronological log of all intimidation attempts.**
   **Write down every instance of unwanted contact, including the date, time, location, and a factual description of what was said or done.**
   - Take screenshots of digital messages and save voicemails to a secondary device or cloud storage.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you will attempt to "quash" (cancel) the subpoena.
- You do not need to prepare or practice your testimony today.
- You do not need to hire a private attorney immediately unless you are concerned about self-incrimination.
- Long-term safety planning (such as relocation) can wait until you have discussed the specific risk level with a professional coordinator.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant stress or fear when called to testify, especially in high-stakes cases. You are not "overreacting" by seeking protection. The U.S. legal system has specific statutory protections and dedicated personnel (Victim-Witness Specialists) whose job is to manage witness safety and reduce the risk of intimidation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate safety concerns following the service of a subpoena. It does not cover legal strategies, the rules of evidence, or long-term witness protection programs.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding witness intimidation (such as 18 U.S.C. § 1512) and courthouse security vary by jurisdiction and whether the case is state or federal. Always follow the instructions provided by law enforcement or the court of record. For confidential support and redirection to local resources, you may contact the VictimConnect Resource Center at 855-484-2846.
