# What to do if… you are served papers naming you as a witness and you are worried about retaliation (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer place first. Call 911 if you are in immediate danger; otherwise, use the contact details on the papers or call the court clerk to say that you are a witness with a retaliation concern and need the correct safety contact.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the papers. Missing a required response, date, or appearance may cause serious consequences.
- Do not try to settle threats or pressure privately.
- Do not discuss what you may say as a witness with anyone who is pressuring you.
- Do not agree to a private meeting or call with someone who is trying to influence you.
- Do not post case details, your location, or your court plans online.
- Do not delete threatening messages, voicemails, emails, or social-media contact.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause.**  
   If you are in immediate danger, call **911**. If the danger is not immediate, move to a place where you feel safer before making calls or sending messages.

2. **Read the papers once and note the key details.**  
   Write down the court name, case number, date, time, location, any deadline, and any listed contact details. Take clear photos or scans of every page. Ask promptly about the required response if anything is unclear.

3. **Use the listed contact or the court clerk for routing.**  
   Say: “I am named as a witness and I am worried about retaliation. Who is the correct person to contact about safety?”  
   Keep the first contact focused on safety and routing. If you are unsure who the listed contact represents, or you do not want to contact that person, call the court clerk instead.

4. **Ask what support is available for this specific case.**  
   Ask whether there is a witness-assistance contact, courthouse security contact, or a way to discuss safe arrival, waiting, and leaving court. In a criminal case, ask whether the prosecutor’s office has a witness-assistance route.

5. **Report threats, harassment, or pressure.**  
   Call **911** for immediate danger. Otherwise, use your local law-enforcement non-emergency number and tell the case contact that you are a witness with a retaliation or intimidation concern. Use plain facts: who contacted you, what happened, when it happened, and whether anyone else saw or heard it.

6. **Preserve what you already have.**  
   Save messages, voicemails, emails, screenshots, letters, and envelopes. Keep a simple list of incidents with dates, times, locations, and names.

7. **Make one small privacy change.**  
   Turn off location sharing, tighten social-media privacy settings, or ask people close to you not to post your location or court plans.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to work out the whole legal strategy right now.
- You do **not** need to rehearse testimony or write a long explanation before making the first safety contact.
- You do **not** need to confront anyone or clear the air.
- Long-term decisions about work, housing, or major security changes can wait until the immediate safety steps are underway.

## Important reassurance
Your concern is worth taking seriously. Depending on the circumstances, threats, harassment, or pressure involving a witness may be crimes. Asking for the correct safety contact is a reasonable first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about the papers, court process, safety measures, or legal strategy may need case-specific help from a lawyer or the appropriate local office.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Procedures vary by state, court, and case type. Some resources below are federal; follow the instructions from the court named on your papers. Call 911 if you are in immediate danger.
