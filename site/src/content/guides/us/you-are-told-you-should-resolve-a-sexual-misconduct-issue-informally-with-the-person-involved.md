# What to do if… you are told you should resolve a sexual misconduct issue “informally” with the person involved (USA)

## Short answer
You are not required to resolve sexual harassment or misconduct through direct confrontation or informal mediation — pause, request a formal reporting path in writing, and ask for immediate supportive measures that reduce contact.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to meet the person involved alone or participate in mediation just because a supervisor or administrator says it is the "first step."
- Do not let anyone pressure you into signing "informal agreements" or confidentiality waivers on the spot.
- Do not assume you must file a formal complaint just to get safety measures like no-contact orders or schedule changes.
- Do not delete any emails or messages where you were told to handle the matter informally.
- Do not bypass your right to a support person during any meeting regarding these options.

## What to do now
1. **State clearly (preferably in writing) that you are not comfortable with an informal resolution or direct contact at this time.** **You have the right to decline informal processes even if the institution or employer encourages them.**
   - Send an email stating: "I am not comfortable resolving this directly or meeting one-to-one. Please provide the formal grievance policy and my options for supportive measures in writing."

2. **If this is at a school or college, contact the Title IX Coordinator to request the current written grievance policy.** **Following the 2025 court vacatur of the 2024 Title IX Rule, the 2020 regulations are the active federal standard nationwide.**
   - Under this framework, informal resolution (such as mediation) is strictly voluntary and requires written consent from both parties.
   - You can withdraw from an informal process at any time before a resolution is reached.

3. **Request immediate supportive measures that create distance between you and the person involved.** **These measures are available regardless of whether you choose to file a formal complaint or an informal one.**
   - Examples include no-contact directives, class or housing changes, or campus escorts.
   - These measures must be provided "promptly" and without fee or charge to you.

4. **If this is in a workplace, report the issue through a channel that does not involve the person who told you to handle it "informally."** **While the EEOC rescinded its 2024 guidance in early 2026, Title VII and Supreme Court precedents still prohibit sexual harassment and retaliation.**
   - Contact a different manager, HR representative, or use a company-wide reporting hotline.
   - Specifically mention that you were pressured to resolve the matter informally and that you want to follow the formal anti-harassment policy.

5. **Create a private record of who told you to handle the matter informally and exactly what they said.** **Documenting these "gatekeeping" attempts protects your memory and can be evidence of a failure to follow policy.**
   - Include the date, time, and specific phrasing used (e.g., "we should keep this quiet" or "just talk to them first").
   - Save copies of any emails or texts where these suggestions were made to a personal account you control.

6. **Bring a support person of your choice to any meeting where resolution options are discussed.** **In Title IX cases, you have a right to an advisor of your choice, which can be an attorney, a friend, or a campus advocate.**
   - In a workplace, this may be a union representative or a trusted colleague, depending on your contract and state law.
   - Tell the official: "I will be bringing a support person to our meeting. Please confirm the agenda in advance."

7. **Flag any concerns about retaliation immediately and in writing.** **If you feel pressured to handle this informally because you fear for your job or grades, name that fear to the reporting official.**
   - Ask for a written copy of the organization’s anti-retaliation policy.
   - Keep a log of any changes to your workload, assignments, or evaluations that occur after you raise the issue.

8. **Contact a confidential advocate to discuss your rights without triggering a report.** **Organizations like RAINN or local sexual assault service providers can explain the difference between "supportive measures," "informal resolution," and "formal investigation" without sharing your name with the school or employer.**
   - Call or chat with the National Sexual Assault Hotline (800-656-HOPE).
   - Ask them to help you prepare what to say to HR or Title IX staff.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today if you want to pursue a full formal investigation or legal action.
- You do not need to have a "perfect" or detailed timeline of the original incident ready before asking for a no-contact order.
- You do not need to respond to messages or apologies from the person involved while you are deciding your path.

## Important reassurance
It is common for institutions to suggest informal routes because they are faster or less paperwork, but "informal" should never mean "unsafe." Wanting a clear, recorded, and structured process is a valid safety choice, not a sign of being "difficult."

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for navigating pressure to avoid formal reporting. It does not provide legal strategy for litigation or detailed guidance on criminal proceedings.

## Important note
This is general information and not legal advice. Policies vary by state and individual organization. If you are in immediate physical danger, call 911. Federal regulations (Title IX and Title VII) set a floor for your rights, but your specific school or workplace may have additional protections.
