# What to do if… siblings are arguing or reacting differently after a death and you need to manage the tension (USA)

## Short answer
Pause the argument and check whether anyone is in immediate danger or thinking about suicide or self-harm. Call 911 for immediate danger. For crisis support or emotional distress, call or text 988, or use the online chat.

## Do not do these things
- Do not demand a “correct” grief reaction or compare who is hurting more.
- Do not try to settle long-standing sibling conflict right now.
- Do not make non-urgent decisions about money, belongings, announcements, or major commitments if they can safely wait.
- Do not continue an escalating argument by group text, phone, or social media.
- Do not pressure anyone to talk, make decisions, or react in the same way.
- Do not rely on alcohol or drugs to manage the moment.
- Do not leave someone alone if they are talking about suicide, self-harm, or immediate danger, if it is safe for you to stay nearby or arrange support. Call 911 for immediate danger. Call or text 988, or use the online chat, for crisis support.

## What to do now
1. **Create a calmer pause.** Stop the argument. Move to separate rooms or end the call for a few minutes if that is safe. Keep the pause neutral: “We do not need to settle this right now.”
2. **Check immediate safety.** Calmly ask whether anyone is in immediate danger or thinking about suicide or self-harm. Call 911 for immediate danger. For crisis support or emotional distress, call or text 988, or use the online chat. You can also contact 988 if you are worried about a sibling.
3. **Limit today’s decisions to essentials.** Choose one person to handle necessary calls, such as contacting the funeral home or updating close relatives. Write down other decisions and return to them later.
4. **Reduce the next source of conflict.** Pause the group chat, move sensitive messages to one agreed channel, and ask one person to send practical updates.
5. **Agree on one simple sentence.** Use: “We are reacting differently, and we only need to get through the immediate steps safely.”
6. **Check who wants company.** Ask each sibling whether they want someone nearby, a phone check-in, or some quiet space. Respect the answer unless there is an immediate safety concern.
7. **Use outside support when needed.** Ask 988 what local crisis options may be available. Go to an emergency department or call 911 if there is an immediate medical or safety emergency.
8. **Use disaster-specific support only when relevant.** If the death is connected to a natural or human-caused disaster, call or text the SAMHSA Disaster Distress Helpline at 1-800-985-5990.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide what grief should look like.
- You do not need to divide belongings or settle money questions now.
- You do not need to resolve disagreements about major plans if they can safely wait.
- You do not need to fix the sibling relationship during the immediate crisis.
- You do not need to choose long-term support today unless someone needs urgent help.

## Important reassurance
Siblings may react very differently after a death. Reactions may include numbness, anger, tears, silence, withdrawal, or focusing on practical tasks. The immediate goal is not agreement. It is a safer, quieter pause with fewer decisions.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the current pause and the next immediate decisions only. Later choices may need support from a grief counselor, healthcare professional, local crisis service, funeral professional, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Call 911 for immediate danger. For crisis support or emotional distress, call or text 988, or use the online chat.
