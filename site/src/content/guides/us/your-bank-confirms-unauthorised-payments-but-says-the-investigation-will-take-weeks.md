# What to do if… your bank confirms unauthorised payments but says the investigation will take weeks (USA)

## Short answer
Lock down the account and ask the bank, in writing, what error-resolution timeline applies and whether temporary credit applies while it investigates. The next priority is stopping repeat losses, not proving exactly how the fraud happened today.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using the same debit card, PIN, online banking password, or email password while the investigation continues.
- Don’t trust calls, texts, or emails from anyone claiming they can “reverse” the transfers; use the number on your card, statement, or official app.
- Don’t give a caller a one-time passcode, full card number, password, remote access, or a new payment.
- Don’t assume your email is safe; a compromised email account can let fraud restart after a card is replaced.
- Don’t ignore the written billing-error deadline if any disputed payment is on a credit card statement.
- Don’t close the account in a rush before asking where paychecks, benefits, bills, refunds, and any credit will go.

## What to do now

1. **Lock the payment routes that can be reused.**
   - Freeze or lock the card in the bank app, or call the official number on your card or statement.
   - Ask for a new card number if card details were exposed, not only a new physical card.
   - Change your online banking password and your email password from a device you trust.
   - Turn on multi-factor authentication and sign out of all other sessions if the account lets you.

2. **Get the bank’s record of your report in writing.**
   - Ask: “What date and time did you record as my notice of error or fraud report?”
   - Ask: “Which transactions are included in this report?”
   - Ask: “Is this being handled as a debit, ACH, ATM, bank-account, or other electronic fund transfer issue, or as a credit card billing-error issue?”
   - Ask: “Do you require written confirmation of my oral report? If yes, where exactly should I send it, and by what date?”

3. **If money left a checking, debit, ATM, ACH, or similar bank account, ask about Regulation E timing.**
   - Say: “Please confirm the Regulation E investigation deadline for this report.”
   - Ask whether the bank expects to decide within the initial period, often 10 business days, or 20 business days for some new accounts.
   - If the bank says the investigation may take weeks, ask whether it is using a 45-day or 90-day investigation period, and ask why that longer period applies.
   - If the bank needs longer, ask whether provisional credit applies while it continues the investigation.
   - If they say provisional credit does not apply, ask for the reason in writing, including whether they are waiting for written confirmation from you.

4. **If the charge is on a credit card, send the written billing-error notice.**
   - Send it to the billing-error or billing-inquiries address shown by the card issuer, usually on the statement or in the billing-rights notice.
   - Make sure the issuer receives it no later than 60 days after the issuer sent the first statement where the disputed charge appeared.
   - Include your name, account identifier, the transaction date and amount, and a plain sentence saying you did not authorize the charge.
   - Keep a copy, screenshot, upload receipt, secure-message confirmation, or mailing proof.

5. **Stop repeat drains and linked-payment routes.**
   - Ask the bank or card issuer to block the compromised card number and any recurring charges tied to it.
   - Check scheduled bill pay, saved merchants, wallet apps, P2P payment apps, newly added payees, and external linked accounts.
   - Remove anything you do not recognize, or ask the bank to help disable it if you cannot safely remove it yourself.

6. **Protect rent, food, utilities, and paychecks first.**
   - Tell the bank if the missing funds could cause missed rent, overdrafts, late fees, utilities trouble, or lack of food or medication.
   - Ask whether fraud-related fees can be reversed, whether overdraft effects can be paused, and whether the case can be expedited because essentials are at risk.
   - Move incoming paychecks or benefits only after the bank tells you the safest destination for deposits and credits.

7. **Treat possible identity theft as a separate containment task.**
   - If personal information may have been used, go to IdentityTheft.gov and follow the recovery steps that fit what happened.
   - Consider a credit freeze with each of the three major credit reporting agencies if you think someone could open accounts in your name.
   - Check recent bank, card, and credit-report activity for new accounts, new addresses, or inquiries you do not recognize.

8. **Escalate if the bank will not give clear answers.**
   - Ask for the claim number, department name, and the next written update date.
   - Ask for the bank’s final answer or current position in writing if they keep saying only “wait.”
   - File a CFPB complaint if you cannot get a clear written response about timing, provisional credit, or what rules they are applying.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove exactly how your information was stolen today.
- You do not need to decide today whether to switch banks, sue, or contact every merchant.
- You do not need to write a perfect explanation; a clear list of the transactions you did not authorize is enough for the first steps.
- You do not need to solve every identity-theft concern before locking the affected bank and card access.

## Important reassurance
A weeks-long investigation can feel like being ignored, but you can still ask for the specific rule, deadline, claim number, written confirmation route, and temporary-credit decision that apply to your account. Keeping the next steps narrow helps reduce further losses while the bank’s process runs.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stop further loss and keep essential money routes working while the bank investigates. Later decisions, especially after a denial or partial refund, may need help from a consumer-law attorney, financial counselor, regulator, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, security, or professional advice. Rules and timing can vary by payment type, account type, notice date, written-confirmation requirements, and the facts of the transaction; use official bank contact channels and the regulator resources below.
