# What to do if… you run out of fuel and your vehicle is safely off the road (USA)

## Short answer
Stay visible, stay out of traffic, and call for help. Use roadside assistance for fuel delivery or a tow, and call 911 if your location is unsafe, traffic is close, anyone is hurt, or there may be fire or a fuel leak.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t walk along a highway or shoulder to find a gas station.
- Don’t stand behind your vehicle, beside traffic, or in the roadway to wave cars down.
- Don’t try to push the vehicle unless emergency responders or roadside professionals tell you what to do.
- Don’t siphon fuel or use an unapproved container.
- Don’t smoke, use an open flame, or use flares if you smell fuel, see a spill, or suspect a leak.
- Don’t use your phone battery on non-essentials; keep it available for calls, maps, and location sharing.

## What to do now
1. **Make the vehicle visible.** Turn on hazard lights. If it is dark or visibility is poor, use headlights or interior light briefly if needed, while preserving enough battery for hazards and calls.
2. **Secure the stopped vehicle.** Put the vehicle in park, set the parking brake, and keep everyone calm and away from the traffic side.
3. **Usually stay inside with seat belts on.** This is often safer on fast roads, narrow shoulders, curves, bridges, or at night. Exit only if staying inside is clearly more dangerous, such as smoke, fire, a strong fuel smell, a visible spill, or another immediate threat.
4. **If you must get out, avoid traffic exposure.** Exit from the side away from traffic if you can. Move well away from the roadway and behind a barrier if there is one.
5. **Get your exact location before calling.** Use your phone map for a pinned location. Note the road or highway number, direction of travel, nearest exit, mile marker, cross street, rest area, or visible landmark.
6. **Call the right help.** Call roadside assistance, your insurer’s assistance line, AAA, a tow company, or your vehicle manufacturer’s service for fuel delivery or a tow to a safe fueling place.
7. **Call 911 instead if safety is uncertain.** Use 911 if you are partly in a lane, traffic is very close, visibility is poor, anyone is injured or unwell, you smell fuel, see smoke or fire, or you cannot wait safely.
8. **Use 511 only where it is available.** In some regions, dialing 511 may connect you to highway information or motorist assistance for a non-emergency out-of-gas situation. If 511 is not available or you feel unsafe, use roadside assistance or 911 as appropriate.
9. **Give clear details.** Say that you are out of fuel, safely off the road if true, your exact location, vehicle make, model, color, license plate, number of people with you, and whether anyone is vulnerable.
10. **Add fuel yourself only if the place is genuinely safe.** This usually means a parking lot or fully separated area, not inches from traffic. Use an approved fuel container, turn the engine off, avoid smoking or flames, and stop if there is any spill, smell, or unsafe traffic exposure.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to troubleshoot the fuel system on the roadside.
- You don’t need to decide whether this was avoidable.
- You don’t need to sort reimbursements, memberships, receipts, or complaints until you and the vehicle are somewhere safe.
- You don’t need to make a long-term plan for fuel stops right now.

## Important reassurance
Running out of fuel is stressful, but being safely off the road gives you time to act. The priority now is simple: stay visible, reduce traffic exposure, and get help to come to you.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation only. Later decisions, such as repair checks, insurance questions, reimbursement, or prevention planning, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, insurance, mechanical, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, injured, or cannot wait safely where you are, call 911.
