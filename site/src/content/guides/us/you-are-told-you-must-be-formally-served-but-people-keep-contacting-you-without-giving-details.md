# What to do if… you are told you must be formally served but people keep contacting you without giving details (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the contact as unverified. Do not pay, click a received link, or share personal information; check the claimed case through official court contact details that you find separately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay an unexpected caller to stop service, dismiss a case, clear a warrant, or avoid arrest.
- Do not give an unexpected caller your Social Security number, date of birth, bank details, address, or workplace schedule.
- Do not click links or open attachments in unexpected texts or emails about court papers.
- Do not rely on caller ID or a phone number supplied by the caller.
- Do not ignore papers that arrive or a deadline confirmed through an official court route.

## What to do now
1. Save the voicemail, text, email, screenshot, caller number, claimed court, claimed agency, case number, names used, and any demand or threat.
2. Ask once for the claimed court, case number, and names of the parties. Do not confirm personal details, argue, or treat the caller’s answer as proof.
3. End the call if the person pressures you to pay, share personal information, click a link, or stay on the line.
4. Find the claimed court through an official route that you locate separately. If the caller claims a federal court matter, use the official federal court finder. For a claimed state or local court matter, start from the official USA.gov courts page or the official website for the named court.
5. Use the clerk’s office contact details on the official court website and ask how to check the claimed case and whether any response deadline appears to apply.
6. Do not assume that a call, email, or text proves that valid service happened. Service rules, documents, and permitted methods vary by court and type of case.
7. If papers arrive, or an official court route confirms a case or deadline, keep every page and envelope, note when and how the papers arrived, and deal with the confirmed deadline promptly.
8. If you already paid, contact your bank, card issuer, or payment provider promptly and ask whether the transaction can be reversed. If you shared personal information, use IdentityTheft.gov for recovery steps.
9. Report a suspected scam to the FTC.

## What can wait
- You do not need to pay an unexpected caller or decide anything while the contact is unverified.
- You do not need to decide during the call whether it is a scam or a real case.
- You do not need to keep answering repeated calls.
- Hiring a lawyer, negotiating, or preparing a formal response can wait until you have checked the claimed matter, unless papers have arrived or an official court route confirms a deadline.

## Important reassurance
Pressure, partial personal details, a local-looking caller ID, and official-sounding language do not require an immediate response. Pausing and checking the court through a separately found official route is a reasonable first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If papers arrive or a court confirms a case, later decisions may need help from a lawyer or a local legal-aid service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
