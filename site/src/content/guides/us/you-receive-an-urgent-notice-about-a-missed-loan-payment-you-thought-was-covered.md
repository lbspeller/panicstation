# What to do if… you receive an urgent notice about a missed loan payment you thought was covered (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay through the urgent notice until you verify it is real. Use a trusted contact method, gather proof of the payment or attempted payment, and ask the lender, servicer, or collector to investigate in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay through a link, QR code, or phone number in an unexpected email, text, or letter.
- Do not give anyone one-time codes, passwords, full Social Security number, or remote access to “fix” the payment.
- Do not assume the notice is fake just because you believe the payment was covered.
- Do not ignore the notice; it can lead to fees, collection contact, or credit reporting problems.
- If a debt collector is involved, do not say you owe the debt while you are still checking whether the payment was missed, misapplied, returned, or already paid.

## What to do now
1. **Work out who sent the notice.**  
   Check whether it came from your lender, your loan servicer, or a debt collector. A servicer manages your loan account. A debt collector is trying to collect a debt, often for another company or after a transfer.

2. **Verify the notice using a trusted route.**  
   Use the phone number on your most recent statement, the secure app or portal you already use, or the official website typed into your browser. Say: “I received a missed-payment notice, but I believed this payment was covered. Please check the account before I make any payment.”

3. **Pull together proof before you pay again.**  
   Gather copies of:
   - the bank or card statement showing the payment, attempted payment, reversal, or return
   - any confirmation number, receipt, email, screenshot, or autopay confirmation
   - any recent notice about a changed due date, changed amount, new card, new bank account, or failed autopay
   - the urgent notice itself

4. **Ask the servicer to check posting and allocation.**  
   Ask them to check whether the payment was received but posted to the wrong loan, held as unapplied funds, applied to a different amount, reversed by your bank, returned for insufficient funds, or delayed by a processing issue. Ask for a case number and written confirmation of what they find.

5. **Ask for temporary protection while they investigate.**  
   Ask the lender or servicer to note the account as disputed or under review, waive any late fee if the problem was an error, and hold off on negative credit reporting while they check. They may not agree, but asking creates a record.

6. **Send a short written follow-up.**  
   Use secure message, email, or letter. Keep it simple: “I paid or attempted to pay on [date]. I received a past-due notice. I dispute the missed-payment status and ask you to investigate, confirm the payment status in writing, and explain any returned, reversed, or misapplied payment.” Save a copy.

7. **If a debt collector is involved, use the validation information.**  
   Ask for the validation information if you do not have it. If you believe the debt is wrong, already paid, the wrong amount, or not yours, you generally have 30 days after receiving the validation information to dispute it in writing. If you dispute in writing within that period, the collector generally must stop collection activity on the disputed debt until it sends verification.

8. **Check your credit reports if reporting may have happened.**  
   Use the official free credit report source and check the account on all three reports if possible. If a report wrongly shows the loan as late or past due, dispute the error with each credit reporting company showing the mistake and also with the company that supplied the information. Send copies, not originals.

9. **Escalate if the company will not correct or explain the issue.**  
   If you have already tried the company and still cannot get a clear response, you can submit a complaint to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and ask for a company response.

## What can wait
- You do not need to refinance, consolidate, or open new credit today.
- You do not need to decide whether to switch banks or close accounts right now.
- You do not need to argue about your whole financial history; focus on this payment, this account, and this notice.
- You do not need to panic about your credit score before you confirm whether anything was actually reported.

## Important reassurance
A missed-payment notice can be caused by a fixable issue such as failed autopay, an expired card, a returned payment, a posting delay, or a payment being applied to the wrong place. A calm paper trail gives you the best chance of stopping the problem from growing.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to stabilise the situation and prevent avoidable damage. Later decisions may need specialist financial, legal, housing, or loan-specific help, especially if the payment was truly missed or the account is already in collection.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, credit, or professional advice. Rules and timelines can vary by loan type and state. If you suspect fraud or identity theft, slow down, use only verified contact channels, and do not share sensitive information through the urgent notice.
