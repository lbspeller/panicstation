# What to do if… your phone is missing and the location tracking shows an unexpected place (USA)

## Short answer
Lock the phone remotely, contact your carrier to protect your number, and do not go to the map location to confront anyone.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go to the address shown on the map to retrieve it yourself.
- Don’t meet strangers who claim they found your phone.
- Don’t click “we found your phone” links or enter Apple, Google, banking, or carrier passwords from a message.
- Don’t share your device passcode, Apple or Google password, verification codes, or carrier PIN with anyone who contacts you.
- Don’t remove an iPhone from Find My if it may be stolen; that can remove Activation Lock.
- Don’t delay contacting your carrier if your phone number receives banking, email, or account verification codes.

## What to do now
1. **Use another device from somewhere safe.**
   - Use a laptop, tablet, or trusted person’s phone.
   - If the map shows a home, workplace, parking lot, or place you do not recognize, do not go there yourself.

2. **Lock the phone with the official Apple or Google tool.**
   - iPhone or iPad: use Find My or iCloud Find Devices and choose **Mark as Lost** or **Lost Mode**.
   - Android: use **Find Hub**, formerly shown as Find My Device in some places, and follow the current options to find, secure, lock, or erase the device.
   - If it looks stolen or the location is unfamiliar, do not add your personal contact details to the lost-device message. If it simply looks misplaced, use a contact method you control, such as a trusted alternate email or number.

3. **Call your mobile carrier now.**
   - Ask them to suspend service or block the SIM or eSIM so the number cannot be used for calls, texts, or verification codes.
   - Ask whether they can block or disable the device using the IMEI, MEID, or ESN.
   - Add or confirm a carrier account PIN or passphrase.
   - Ask them to note the account for possible SIM-swap or port-out fraud.

4. **Secure the accounts that could unlock other accounts.**
   - Start with your primary email account.
   - Then secure your Apple Account or Google Account.
   - Then check banking, payment, messaging, and social accounts.
   - Change passwords where needed, review signed-in devices and sessions, and sign out of anything you do not recognize.
   - After each important account is safe, switch away from SMS codes where the account offers an authenticator app, security key, or other stronger method.

5. **Use law enforcement instead of trying to recover it yourself.**
   - If the theft is happening now, someone is threatening you, or you are in immediate danger, call **911**.
   - Otherwise, contact local law enforcement through their non-emergency number or online reporting route.
   - Keep the phone make, model, serial number, IMEI, MEID, or ESN if you have it.
   - Save a screenshot or written note of the map location and timestamp.

6. **Decide about remote erase only after the phone and accounts are locked.**
   - Remote erase can protect data if the phone is not recoverable.
   - Erasing cannot be undone.
   - On Android, erasing generally means the device location will no longer be available in Find Hub after it is erased.
   - On iPhone, do not remove the device from Find My after erasing if it may be stolen.

7. **If you see account misuse or identity theft signs, start a formal recovery record.**
   - Use the federal identity recovery site to make a plan.
   - Also contact the affected bank, card issuer, email provider, or account provider directly through an official app, website, or known phone number.

## What can wait
- Buying a replacement phone.
- Deciding whether to change every password at once.
- Insurance claims, unless your phone plan or AppleCare+ instructions require a specific claim step before removing anything.
- Longer-term upgrades such as password manager cleanup, new recovery contacts, or changing every account to stronger authentication.
- Deciding whether the phone is gone forever if it is already locked and your carrier has protected the number.

## Important reassurance
A phone location can be delayed, estimated, or wrong, but securing the phone, your number, and your key accounts is still the safest first move. These steps help whether the phone is stolen, misplaced, or showing a bad location.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce harm and buy time. Recovery, insurance, police reports, account cleanup, and identity-theft steps may need help from your carrier, device maker, bank, local law enforcement, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe or there is an emergency, prioritize personal safety and call emergency services.
