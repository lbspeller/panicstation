# What to do if… a venue or funeral provider has paused your service due to conflicting instructions from relatives (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the venue or provider to pause non-essential changes, identify anything time-sensitive that cannot be paused, and confirm in writing which person or documents it needs before acting on further instructions. Use one temporary contact channel while the state-specific authority question is checked.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign service, burial, or cremation authorization paperwork if your authority is unclear.
- Do not keep sending competing instructions through different relatives.
- Do not assume that a family relationship, will, or pre-need record settles the issue until the provider confirms what it needs.
- Do not assume that every action can wait; ask the provider what is time-sensitive.
- Do not treat a tentative date, deposit, or service choice as final unless the provider explains what is provisional and reversible.
- Do not pressure staff to choose sides in the family dispute.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for a written pause on non-essential changes.**  
   Tell the venue or provider that relatives are giving conflicting instructions. Ask it to pause non-essential changes and email a checklist of what it needs before accepting further decisions. Ask it to identify anything time-sensitive that cannot safely or lawfully be paused.

2. **Clarify what the venue is being asked to decide.**  
   Ask whether the issue is only the service booking or whether it also affects funeral, burial, or cremation authorization. A service venue may need one contact person, while a funeral, burial, or cremation provider may need state-specific documentation.

3. **Check the documents already available.**  
   Look for any pre-need contract, signed designation, or provider file that may be relevant. Send copies only if the provider asks for them.

4. **Name one temporary contact channel.**  
   Give the provider one person’s name, phone number, and email address for updates while authority is being checked. Ask the provider to confirm in writing who may give instructions it can act on and what documents it will accept. Naming a temporary contact does not establish who may authorize decisions.

5. **Send relatives one neutral message.**  
   Use wording such as: “The provider has been asked to pause non-essential changes while it confirms the required process. Please send preferences to [NAME] rather than contacting the provider separately. [NAME] is the temporary contact only while the provider confirms who may authorize decisions.”

6. **Ask what can remain provisional.**  
   Ask the provider to identify any tentative date, temporary hold, deposit, or service choice that can remain open without an irreversible commitment.

7. **Escalate only the unresolved part.**  
   If the provider cannot explain its required process, or relatives still dispute who can authorize a decision, seek state-specific legal help. If the concern is about the business’s practices, consider contacting your state consumer-protection office.

8. **Keep pricing separate from the authority dispute.**  
   If a funeral provider’s prices, deposit, or itemization are also unclear, ask for the available price information before agreeing to additional costs. FTC Funeral Rule resources explain consumer rights and scope relating to funeral pricing and disclosures.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to finalize music, readings, flowers, reception details, or obituary wording now.
- You do **not** need to resolve the wider family conflict today.
- You do **not** need to decide which relative is most persuasive.
- You do **not** need to approve non-essential upgrades or payments until the provider explains what is provisional and reversible.

## Important reassurance
A written pause on non-essential changes is not a failure. One contact channel can reduce confusion while the provider checks the documents and the state-specific process.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need state-specific legal help or other specialist support if the dispute continues.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Rules, documents, and provider processes vary by state and circumstances. Ask the venue or provider to confirm its requirements in writing.
