# What to do if… your passport expires too soon for your travel plans (USA)

## Short answer
Check the passport-validity rule for every country you will enter or transit, then choose the fastest safe path: urgent U.S. passport service, expedited processing, or changing the trip. Airlines can refuse boarding when the passport does not meet the document rules for the exact itinerary.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume your passport is acceptable just because it has not expired yet.
- Do not ignore layovers, transit points, or separate tickets; a connection can affect document checks.
- Do not rely on an airport agent making an exception at check-in.
- Do not book expensive non-refundable changes until you have checked the requirement for your exact route.
- Do not use a third-party appointment seller for a U.S. passport agency appointment; the State Department says it does not charge a fee to make these appointments.
- Do not renew the passport and forget items tied to the old passport number, such as visas, travel authorizations, airline profiles, or Advance Passenger Information.

## What to do now
1. Write down the fixed facts in one place: passport expiration date, U.S. citizenship, destination, every transit country, departure date, return or onward date, and whether you need a visa.

2. Check the country rule for the destination and every transit point. Use the State Department destination pages and any official immigration page for the country involved. Use the IATA Travel Centre as a second check because airlines commonly use IATA document data, but treat official country guidance and your airline’s answer for the exact itinerary as the deciding sources.

3. Ask your airline this direct question with your exact details: “My U.S. passport expires on [date]. My itinerary is [full route, including connections]. Will I be allowed to board?” Save the reply as a chat transcript, email, or screenshot.

4. Choose the U.S. passport route that matches your travel date:
   - If you are traveling internationally in less than 2 to 3 weeks, check whether you qualify for a passport agency or center appointment. The State Department says these appointments are for urgent travel abroad in the next 14 days, or a foreign visa need in the next 28 days.
   - If you have more time but routine processing is too slow, use expedited processing. Current State Department guidance says expedited processing takes 2 to 3 weeks and routine processing takes 4 to 6 weeks, not including mailing time.
   - Add mailing time when deciding. The State Department says mailing can add up to 2 weeks for the application to arrive and up to 2 weeks for the new passport to reach you.

5. If a new passport will not arrive in time, change the trip in the least risky way. Start with changes that directly solve the passport-validity problem: move the travel dates, remove a transit country with stricter rules, or postpone the trip until the passport clearly meets the entry rules.

6. If you are already abroad and passport validity is blocking urgent onward travel, contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate for instructions. A limited-validity emergency passport may be possible in limited circumstances, but it may be valid for up to 1 year or less and some foreign destinations may not accept it.

7. After any new passport or itinerary change, update anything using the old passport number: visas, eVisas, ETAs, foreign travel authorizations, airline Advance Passenger Information, frequent flyer profiles, saved traveler details, and booking records. Check that your ticket name matches the passport exactly.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to argue with the airline, seek compensation, or prove who should have warned you.
- You do not need to rebuild the whole trip at once; first solve whether this passport works for this exact itinerary.
- You do not need to compare every paid expediting service before using the official State Department passport options.

## Important reassurance
This catches many people because passport expiration rules are not the same everywhere and can depend on transit as well as destination. Once you know the exact rule and your travel date, the next move is usually one of three things: get a faster passport, change the route, or move the trip.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce the chance of being refused boarding or refused entry and to route you toward the correct U.S. passport option. Later decisions about refunds, visas, immigration status, denied boarding, or travel insurance may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Entry requirements, passport processing, appointment availability, and airline document checks can change and can depend on citizenship, transit, visa status, and itinerary.
