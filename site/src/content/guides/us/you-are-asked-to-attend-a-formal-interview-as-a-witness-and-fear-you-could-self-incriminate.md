# What to do if… you are asked to attend a formal interview as a witness and fear you could self-incriminate (USA)

## Short answer
Do not answer questions about the facts until you have spoken with a criminal defense lawyer. First, find out whether the interview is voluntary or legally required, get the request in writing, and clearly say you will not speak about the matter without counsel.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to an interview alone to “clear things up,” even if they say you are only a witness.
- Do not rely on “off the record” promises or casual conversation.
- Do not guess, speculate, or try to fill in missing details.
- Do not lie or change the story to make it sound better.
- Do not delete, alter, hide, or destroy messages, photos, call logs, notes, devices, or documents after being contacted.
- Do not ignore a subpoena, court order, or grand jury subpoena.
- Do not discuss the facts in texts, social media messages, group chats, or recorded calls.

## What to do now
1. **Find out whether this is voluntary or legally required.**
   - Ask: “Is this interview voluntary?”
   - Ask: “Am I required to appear because of a subpoena, court order, or grand jury subpoena?”
   - Ask for the officer’s or agent’s name, agency, badge or ID number if they have one, and the case number.
   - Ask for the request in writing.
   - Before calling back, consider using the agency’s public phone number from its official website rather than only the number given by the caller.

2. **Use one clear boundary sentence.**
   - “I am not answering questions about this without a lawyer.”
   - “Please put the request in writing. I will respond after I speak with counsel.”
   - Repeat the same sentence if they keep asking.

3. **If you are already in an in-person encounter, ask one control question.**
   - Ask: “Am I free to leave?”
   - If they say yes, leave calmly.
   - If they say no, or if you are arrested, say: “I am invoking my right to remain silent. I want a lawyer.”
   - After that, stop talking about the facts.

4. **Get legal help before any interview.**
   - Look for a criminal defense attorney in the state where the interview, investigation, or subpoena is connected.
   - If you are already charged and cannot afford a lawyer, ask the court, assigned counsel, or local public defender intake how to apply for appointed counsel.
   - Tell the lawyer exactly what was requested and whether you received a subpoena.

5. **Do not schedule the interview yourself.**
   - Say: “Any interview needs to be arranged through my lawyer.”
   - Do not agree to a time, place, recorded statement, written statement, or “quick call” about the facts before counsel is involved.

6. **If you received a subpoena or grand jury subpoena, protect the date.**
   - Do not ignore it.
   - Do not assume you can skip it because you may invoke the Fifth Amendment.
   - Send or take the subpoena to a lawyer immediately.
   - Ask the lawyer what the subpoena requires, whether any motion to quash or modify is possible, and how to handle any appearance.
   - A witness may need to assert the Fifth Amendment in response to specific questions, so get legal advice before trying to do that yourself.

7. **Make a simple record for your lawyer.**
   - Write down who contacted you, when, and how.
   - Write down exactly what they asked you to do.
   - Save the written request, subpoena, voicemail, email, letter, card, or text.
   - Note any deadline, appearance date, location, case number, or agency name.

8. **Limit discussion about the facts.**
   - For support, use a narrow sentence such as: “I have been contacted and I am getting a lawyer.”
   - Do not give timelines, explanations, guesses, names, documents, screenshots, or theories to friends, family, coworkers, or online groups.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to “tell your side.”
- You do not need to prepare a full timeline or written statement right now.
- You do not need to work out whether you are really a witness, subject, target, or suspect by guessing from someone’s tone.
- You do not need to negotiate directly about which questions you might answer.
- You do not need to explain your reasons for wanting a lawyer.

## Important reassurance
Being called a witness while fearing self-incrimination is unsettling, and it is reasonable to slow the situation down. Asking for counsel before answering questions is a normal protective step and does not by itself mean you have done anything wrong.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. What to do next can depend on whether the contact is voluntary, whether you are detained, whether a subpoena is valid, what state or federal process is involved, and whether you may have criminal exposure.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Laws and procedures vary by state, court, agency, and facts. If there is any chance your words could be used against you, pause the interview process and involve a lawyer before answering questions.
