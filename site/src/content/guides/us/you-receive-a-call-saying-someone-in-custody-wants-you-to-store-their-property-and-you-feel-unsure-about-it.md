# What to do if… you receive a call saying someone in custody wants you to store their property and you feel unsure about it (USA)

## Short answer
Inform the caller you will contact the facility directly to verify the request, then hang up immediately. Do not agree to accept items, provide personal data, or send money during an unsolicited call.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay any "processing fees" or "transport costs" via gift cards, wire transfers, cryptocurrency, or payment apps.
- Don’t provide your Social Security number, bank details, or photos of your government ID to the caller.
- Don’t agree to meet a "courier" or a third party at your home or a neutral location to exchange items.
- Don’t trust the caller ID, even if it displays a local jail or government agency name, as these numbers are easily spoofed.
- Don’t accept or open sealed packages or bags if you did not witness them being inventoried and released by facility staff.

## What to do now
1. **End the call without providing a commitment.** **Inform the caller that you will contact the facility’s official property unit directly to verify the procedure.**
2. **Document the specific details provided by the caller.** **Write down the name of the facility, the inmate’s name, their booking or ID number, and the specific request made.**
3. **Verify the inmate's actual location and custody status independently.** **Use the Federal Bureau of Prisons (BOP) Inmate Locator for federal prisoners or the VINE (Victim Information and Notification Everyday) system for state and local inmates.**
   - Visit bop.gov for federal facility searches.
   - Visit vinelink.com for state or county facility searches.
4. **Contact the facility using a verified phone number from a .gov website.** **Call the main desk or the "Property Unit" and ask specifically for their standard "Authorization to Release Property" procedure.**
   - Most facilities require the inmate to sign a specific release form naming you as the recipient.
   - You will typically be required to show a valid government-issued photo ID in person at the facility to receive any property.
5. **Report the suspicious contact to the appropriate authorities if money was requested.** **Submit a report to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.**
6. **Alert your financial institution immediately if you shared any sensitive information.** **Contact your bank’s fraud department if you provided card numbers, account details, or one-time passcodes.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether you are willing to store the items long-term right now.
- You do not need to provide any identification or documents until you have physically arrived at a verified government facility.
- You do not need to call the inmate back or attempt to justify why you are not proceeding with the caller’s request.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel pressured or confused when receiving a call involving law enforcement or incarcerated individuals. Legitimate property releases are highly regulated, paperwork-heavy processes that do not involve "couriers" or immediate payments over the phone. Relying on your instinct to verify the call is a necessary safety step.

## Scope note
This guide covers initial stabilization and verification steps for suspicious or unexpected calls regarding inmate property. It does not cover the legal implications of storing specific types of property or the long-term management of an incarcerated person’s estate.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Individual jail and prison policies vary significantly by jurisdiction. If you suspect the property involved is related to a crime, contact a legal professional or local law enforcement via a non-emergency line.
