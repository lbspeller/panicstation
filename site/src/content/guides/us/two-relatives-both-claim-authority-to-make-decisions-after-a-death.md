# What to do if… two relatives both claim authority to make decisions after a death (USA)

## Short answer
Pause non-urgent transfers, access, and commitments until you know what decision is being made and what document or state-law route supports the person’s authority. Court-issued Letters are commonly used to show a personal representative’s authority, but the appropriate proof can depend on the asset, the decision, and state law.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hand over keys, property, passwords, or original documents solely because someone says they are the executor or shows a copy of a will.
- Do not help either person sell, transfer, remove, or distribute estate property while their authority is unclear.
- Do not sign anything on behalf of the estate unless you are satisfied that you are authorized to do so.
- Do not enter property you are not entitled to access or remove items to settle the dispute yourself.
- Do not let the disagreement become a physical confrontation. Step away and call 911 if anyone is in immediate danger.
- Do not post accusations online or in family group chats. Keep notes calm and factual.

## What to do now
1. **Pause your part in non-urgent actions.** Do not assist with property removal, account access, transfers, sales, or major commitments while the competing claims remain unclear.
2. **Ask each relative separately for the same information:**
   - the exact decision or action they want taken
   - the document or state-law route they say gives them authority
   - a copy of any current Letters, court appointment order, or other paperwork they rely on
   - the state, county, court name, and case number for any probate case
   - whether they say the asset passes outside the estate or whether a small-estate or other procedure applies  
   A copy of a will can be relevant, but it may not by itself establish current authority to act.
3. **Check the court information.** Use the official state or local court website for the place where a claimant says a case was filed. If no case number is available, ask the court office handling probate how to identify the correct court, what case information is public, and how certified copies can be requested. Access rules and court terminology vary.
4. **Tell organizations that there are competing claims.** For a bank, insurer, landlord, storage provider, or other organization, say that two people claim authority. Ask what documentation it requires before you assist with access, disclosure, transfer, or release of property. Ask whether it also needs a certified death certificate.
5. **Keep a limited record without taking sides.** If you are already lawfully present, take dated photos of visible property and make a simple list. Keep copies of documents already lawfully in your possession. Do not enter property or remove items without authority or agreement.
6. **Handle funeral questions separately.** If a funeral home or similar provider needs an urgent decision, tell it that family members disagree and ask what documentation or process it follows. Choose the least irreversible option where a pause is possible.
7. **Start a simple log.** Record dates, calls, requests, documents shown, and the organizations contacted. Keep the wording factual.
8. **Get prompt local legal help for urgent risks.** If property appears likely to be removed, sold, or transferred before the dispute can be checked, contact a probate attorney in the relevant state promptly. Call 911 if anyone is in immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today which relative deserves control of the estate.
- You do not need to distribute belongings, close accounts, transfer property, or settle disagreements about the will right now.
- You do not need to argue the full history of the family dispute in each conversation.
- Tax and administration tasks may need attention later, but the first step is to prevent avoidable loss and identify the applicable authority.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to settle the dispute in a pressured conversation. Pausing non-urgent steps, asking both people for the same information, and checking the applicable proof is a neutral and reasonable way to reduce the risk of an irreversible mistake.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The correct authority can depend on state law, the type of property, any court case, and whether the asset passes outside the estate or another procedure applies. Later decisions may need help from a probate attorney in the relevant state.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, or other professional advice. Call 911 if anyone is in immediate danger. Seek prompt advice from a qualified probate attorney in the relevant state if there is an urgent risk of property being taken, sold, or transferred.
