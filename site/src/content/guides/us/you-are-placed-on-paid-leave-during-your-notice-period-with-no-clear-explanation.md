# What to do if… you are placed on paid leave during your notice period with no clear explanation (USA)

## Short answer
Do not provide any explanation to the client. Forward all client inquiries to your manager or the new account owner and wait for written instructions on how to handle the transition.

## Do not do these things
- Do not speculate about the reason for your removal ("I think it's a budget cut" or "I was taken off for a review") even if the client is a personal friend.
- Do not attempt to bypass system lockouts using a coworker’s login or cached credentials.
- Do not vent, complain, or share internal frustration with the client or on public social media platforms.
- Do not "shadow work" or continue providing services to the client through personal email or messaging apps.
- Do not ask coworkers for "insider" information about the decision while the situation is active.

## What to do now
1. **Immediately pause all scheduled or automated communication with the client.** Check your calendar, CRM triggers, or automated email sequences to ensure no messages are sent under your name while your status is in flux.
2. **Review your most recent internal communications for a "no contact" order.** If you have been explicitly told not to contact the client, do not acknowledge any incoming messages from them; forward the emails or voicemails directly to your manager instead.
3. **Draft a neutral, one-sentence holding response if a reply is required by policy.** Use standard language such as: "The account is currently being transitioned to [Name/Team], and I have copied them here to ensure your needs are met without interruption."
4. **Request a specific, written communication protocol from your supervisor.** Send a brief email asking for the designated successor’s contact details and the approved wording to use if the client asks for an explanation.
5. **Document the timeline of access changes in a private, personal log.** Record the exact time you noticed the lockout, which specific systems were affected, and the names of any individuals who notified you of the change.
6. **Secure all company property and confidential client data according to policy.** Ensure that any physical files, hardware, or local digital copies of client data are handled strictly according to your employer's data security guidelines.
7. **Maintain a professional and compliant presence in the office or on shared channels.** Continue with other assigned tasks and avoid discussing the account removal with colleagues unless it is strictly necessary for a handover.
8. **Consult your employee handbook for policies regarding account transitions or internal investigations.** Familiarize yourself with the "at-will" nature of most U.S. employment, which generally allows employers to reassign accounts at their discretion.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to quit or change roles today.
- You do not need to defend your performance or "clear your name" with the client right now.
- You do not need to figure out the "true reason" for the change in the first few hours.

## Important reassurance
Account reassignments and access changes are common operational occurrences in many industries. Feeling a sense of urgency or embarrassment when a client reaches out is a normal reaction to a sudden change in professional status. Following a neutral protocol is the most effective way to protect your professional reputation while internal details are being finalized.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hours following a sudden removal from a client account. It does not cover legal strategies for wrongful termination or long-term career planning.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment laws, including at-will status and non-disclosure obligations, vary significantly by state and by the specific terms of your employment contract or collective bargaining agreement. If you believe the removal is due to illegal discrimination or retaliation, you should consult with a qualified employment attorney.
