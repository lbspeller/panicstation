# What to do if… someone you do not trust pressures you to meet privately (USA)

## Short answer
Do not travel to a private meeting while you feel pressured or unsafe. Create a safer pause, tell someone you trust, and decline the private meeting in writing if it is safe to reply.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree just to stop the pressure or avoid conflict.
- Don’t go to a private location, a car, or either person’s home.
- Don’t accept transport controlled by the person pressuring you.
- Don’t share your address, routine, availability, or real-time location to placate them.
- Don’t stay in a long call or debate your decision.
- Don’t delete messages, voicemails, or call logs if it is safe to keep them.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause.**  
   Do not travel to the meeting. If the person is nearby, you are already on the way, or you are at the proposed location, move to a safer public or trusted place if you can do so safely.

2. **Send one short written message if it is safe to reply.**  
   Example: “I’m not meeting privately. Put any necessary information in writing.”  
   You do not need to explain or continue replying.

3. **Tell someone you trust now.**  
   Share the person’s name or contact details, the proposed time and place, and screenshots or messages if it is safe to do so. Ask your trusted person to stay available for a check-in.

4. **Reduce the information available to the person.**  
   Do not share your location, schedule, address, or travel plans. Check whether you are sharing your live location with them and turn that sharing off if it is safe to do so.

5. **Keep a brief record if it is safe.**  
   Save relevant messages, voicemails, and call logs. Note dates and times for threats, repeated pressure, or unwanted contact.

6. **Use safer formal channels for any necessary contact.**  
   If this involves work or school, keep the communication in writing and ask for a supervisor, staff member, or another appropriate person to be included. You do not need to agree to a one-on-one private meeting.

7. **Use emergency help when needed.**  
   If you are in immediate danger or a crime is in progress, call **911**. For a non-emergency, you may look up your local law-enforcement agency’s non-emergency number. The Office for Victims of Crime hotline list below can help you find support routes. VictimConnect offers confidential support and referrals. Use support websites or helplines only when it is safe to do so.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to block the person, confront them, report the situation, or arrange any later meeting.
- You do not need perfect wording or a detailed explanation.
- You do not need to decide whether the behavior has a legal label.
- If repeated unwanted contact continues, the SPARC safety-planning resource below can help with later steps.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressured or unsafe is enough reason to pause. You do not owe anyone a private meeting or an immediate answer.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent a pressured private meeting and create time to think. Repeated pressure, threats, unwanted monitoring, or work or school complications may need specialist support or local advice later.

## Important note
This is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Use your judgment and seek appropriate professional help for your situation.
