# What to do if… you are considering reporting a sexual assault but you feel overwhelmed and unsure what to do first (USA)

## Short answer
Contact a confidential advocate immediately to discuss your options without the pressure of a formal report. You have a federal right to a medical forensic exam to preserve evidence even if you are not yet ready to involve the police.

## Do not do these things
- Don't feel obligated to make a final decision about the police or legal action while you are in the immediate aftermath.
- Don't delete messages, call logs, or photos until you have spoken with an advocate about how they might be used later.
- Don't assume that showering or the passage of time means you cannot get medical care or support.
- Don't let anyone pressure you into staying silent or into reporting before you feel stabilized.
- Don't post details about the event on social media, as this can impact both your safety and future legal options.

## What to do now
1. **Relocate to a safer environment immediately if you are still in a location where the person who harmed you may return.** If you are in immediate physical danger or have life-threatening injuries, call 911 now.
2. **Contact the National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-HOPE (4673) or use the RAINN online chat for confidential guidance.** These services are available 24/7 and can connect you with local advocates who understand the specific reporting laws in your state.
3. **Go to a hospital or clinic that offers sexual assault medical forensic exams if the assault occurred within the last 120 hours.** Under the federal Violence Against Women Act (VAWA), you have the right to an exam at no cost and, in most cases, without being required to file a police report to receive the exam.
4. **Ask specifically for a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) or a Forensic Nurse Examiner (FNE) when you arrive at a medical facility.** These professionals are specially trained to provide trauma-informed medical care and collect evidence while prioritizing your comfort.
5. **Request that a confidential advocate from a local rape crisis center meet you at the hospital.** Advocates can explain the difference between a "restricted" (anonymous) report and an "unrestricted" report, helping you navigate local mandatory reporting laws for medical providers.
6. **Preserve physical evidence by placing the clothes you were wearing in a paper bag without washing them.** Avoid showering, eating, drinking, or using the restroom if possible before an exam, but if you have already done these things, you should still seek medical attention.
7. **Identify one trusted person to handle logistics like transportation or phone calls so you can focus on your immediate needs.** If you do not have someone you trust available, tell the advocate on the hotline; they can help arrange local support services.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now if you want to participate in a full criminal investigation or go to court.
- You do not need to have a perfectly clear or chronological memory of the event to seek help or care.
- You do not need to notify your employer, school, or family members until you have a support plan in place.
- You can wait to review any physical evidence or digital records until you are in a stabilized environment with an advocate.

## Important reassurance
The feeling of being overwhelmed, frozen, or unsure is a common and natural response to trauma. It does not mean you are doing anything wrong or that you lack the strength to handle the situation. Taking a single step to reach out for confidential support is a significant and sufficient action for right now.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Decisions regarding long-term legal action, forensic evidence testing, or ongoing therapy should be made in consultation with trained professionals and advocates who are familiar with your specific jurisdiction and needs.

## Important note
This information is not legal or medical advice. Mandatory reporting laws for medical providers (especially regarding minors or certain types of injuries) vary by state; always ask an advocate or medical provider about their specific confidentiality obligations before disclosing details.
