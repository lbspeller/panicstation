# What to do if… you are worried a partner may not respect your request to pause or stop during sex (USA)

## Short answer
Prioritize your physical exit immediately; if you do not feel safe verbally saying "no," use a physical excuse to create distance and contact a confidential advocate at 800-656-HOPE.

## Do not do these things
- Don't convince yourself that you "owe" a partner sex because of past intimacy or current status.
- Don't stay in an isolated location if you feel an internal "warning" or "freeze" response.
- Don't use substances to numb your discomfort or to make the situation feel more manageable.
- Don't wait for a "perfect" moment to leave; any moment you are not being physically restrained is a time to move toward a door.
- Don't accept "bargaining" (e.g., "just a few more minutes" or "we're almost there") as a respectful response to a request to stop.

## What to do now
1. **Create an immediate physical "safe pause" if you are currently in a high-pressure situation.** 
   - Say you need to use the bathroom.
   - Say you feel nauseous or have a sudden cramp.
   - Use the time alone to grab your phone and keys.
2. **Move to a location with an exit and multiple people if possible.** If you are at their home, say you forgot an appointment or a family member is calling, and leave immediately without waiting for a reaction.
3. **Use clear, one-word boundaries if you choose to speak.** 
   - "Stop."
   - "No."
   - "Pause."
   - Pair these words with the physical action of sitting up or moving your body away.
4. **Contact the National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN) at 800-656-HOPE for confidential guidance.** 
   - You can also use the online chat at rainn.org or text "HOPE" to 64673. 
   - These services are available 24/7 and do not require you to report a crime.
5. **Seek medical care at a hospital if any non-consensual contact has occurred.** 
   - Ask for a SANE (Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner).
   - Under federal law (VAWA), you are entitled to a forensic exam at no cost, and in many states, you can have this exam "anonymously" without involving the police.
6. **Identify a "safe person" you can call right now to stay on the line while you get to your vehicle or home.** Having an active listener can help ground you and provides an external witness to your safety.
7. **Call 911 if you are being physically blocked from leaving or if you fear an immediate violent escalation.** 

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you are "breaking up" with them right now.
- You do not need to label the experience as a specific crime before asking for help.
- You do not need to explain why you wanted to stop.
- You do not need to worry about "ruining the mood" or hurting their feelings.

## Important reassurance
The "freeze" or "fawn" response—where your body goes still or you act compliant to stay safe—is a normal, biological survival mechanism. It is not a sign of consent. Wanting to stop at any point, regardless of how "far" things have gone, is your absolute right.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only. It focuses on immediate physical safety and accessing confidential support. It does not replace legal advice or long-term therapeutic care.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes in the United States. Laws regarding consent and mandatory reporting vary by state. Contacting a local advocate through RAINN (800-656-HOPE) is the most effective way to get jurisdiction-specific information while remaining confidential.
