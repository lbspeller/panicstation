# What to do if… you discover rumours about your performance are being circulated at work (USA)

## Short answer
Prioritize your immediate physical safety, then **submit a formal written demand for interim protections** (such as a no-contact order or workspace change) while the company's investigation remains pending.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "fix" or "reason with" the person yourself once the behavior has escalated.
- Do not meet with the individual in private or outside of work hours to resolve the issue.
- Do not delete any evidence, including digital messages, photos of desk items, or call logs.
- Do not record conversations in the workplace without verifying both state law and company policy; policy violations can lead to termination even in one-party consent states.
- Do not post details of the situation on social media or internal company-wide channels.
- Do not resign or sign any separation agreements while in an acute state of fear or distress.

## What to do now
1. **Remove yourself from any immediate physical threat.**
   - If you feel physically unsafe, leave the area or the building immediately.
   - Call **911** if there is a threat of violence, stalking, or immediate physical harm.
   - Notify on-site security or a trusted supervisor only after you are in a safe location.

2. **Create a contemporaneous incident log on a personal device.**
   - **List every event by date, time, and specific location.**
   - **Record exact quotes and physical actions without interpreting intent.**
   - **Identify any witnesses who were present or nearby.**
   - **Note the location of any relevant CCTV cameras or digital logs.**

3. **Submit a formal "Notice of Escalation" in writing.**
   - **Email your HR contact and carbon-copy (CC) their immediate supervisor or the company’s legal / compliance department.**
   - **State clearly that the behavior is "repeated," "unwelcome," and "escalating."**
   - **Reference the date of your previous report and note the lack of resolution.**
   - **Identify the specific protected characteristic if applicable (such as race, sex, age over 40, disability, or religion).**

4. **Demand specific interim safety measures immediately.**
   - **Request a formal no-contact directive between you and the other party.**
   - **Ask for a temporary change in seating, shift, or reporting structure.**
   - **Request remote work or a security escort to your vehicle if applicable.**

5. **Follow the employer’s formal handbook procedure exactly.**
   - **Locate the official anti-harassment policy and use the specific reporting channel required (e.g., a portal or ethics hotline).**
   - **Obtain a case or reference number for the submission.**
   - **Ensure you have a copy of the policy to verify if the company is meeting its own investigation timelines.**

6. **Monitor federal and state filing deadlines.**
   - **Note that EEOC charges for private-sector employees must generally be filed within 180 or 300 days of the last incident.**
   - **Contact an agency EEO counselor within 45 days if you are a federal employee.**

7. **Identify a single witness or external support contact.**
   - **Select a union representative, an Employee Assistance Program (EAP) counselor, or a trusted peer to help track documentation.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a lawsuit or quit your job today.
- You do not need to prove that the behavior meets the legal definition of "hostile work environment" before reporting.
- You do not need to provide a complete history; 3–5 recent, specific examples are sufficient for an initial escalation.
- You do not need to respond to requests for "informal mediation" if you feel unsafe.

## Important reassurance
It is a normal physiological response to feel hyper-vigilant, anxious, or unable to focus when workplace behavior escalates. Insisting on a documented process and physical distance is a standard safety protocol, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions for escalating workplace behavior. It does not provide legal strategy, long-term career planning, or medical advice for workplace stress.

## Important note
Laws regarding workplace harassment and recording vary significantly by state. The EEOC rescinded its 2024 Harassment Guidance in January 2026; enforcement now relies on Title VII statutes and established Supreme Court precedents like Bostock. These steps are for informational purposes and do not replace the advice of an employment attorney or the specific requirements of your employer's policies.
