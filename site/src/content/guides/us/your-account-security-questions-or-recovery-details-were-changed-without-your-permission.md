# What to do if… your account security questions or recovery details were changed without your permission (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible account takeover. Secure your email first, then use the provider’s official account-recovery process to regain access and undo any security-setting changes.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click password-reset or security links from unexpected emails or texts; open the provider’s official site or app yourself.
- Don’t enter your password, security answers, or one-time codes on a page opened from an unsolicited message.
- Don’t keep trying passwords repeatedly if they fail; repeated attempts may trigger lockouts.
- Don’t reuse an old password or any password used on another account.
- Don’t share verification codes, backup codes, or security-question answers with anyone, including someone claiming to be support.
- Don’t assume only this one account is affected if the same email, phone number, or password is used elsewhere.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer setup.** Use a trusted device and a private connection. If you must use the same device, update its security software if available and run a scan before recovery.

2. **Secure your email account first if you can still access it.** Email often controls password resets for other accounts.
   - Sign in through the official email site or app.
   - Change the email password to a long, unique password.
   - Turn on multi-factor authentication.
   - Check forwarding, filters, and auto-rules; remove any rule you did not create.
   - Check recovery email addresses and phone numbers.
   - Review recent sign-ins or security activity, and sign out of other sessions if the provider offers that option.

3. **Use the affected provider’s official recovery flow.**
   - Type the provider’s web address yourself, use the official app, or start from the provider’s official help page.
   - In recovery prompts or support forms, state that security questions or recovery options were changed without you.
   - Save screenshots or confirmation numbers from the recovery process if you can do so safely.

4. **After you regain access, lock the affected account down in one focused pass.**
   - Change the password to a long, unique password.
   - Sign out of all other sessions or devices if the provider offers that option.
   - Re-enable multi-factor authentication.
   - Check recovery email, recovery phone, backup codes, connected apps, and trusted devices.
   - If the provider still uses security questions, set answers that are not guessable and not based on real personal facts; store them safely.

5. **Check for phone-number takeover if you use SMS codes.**
   - Warning signs include sudden loss of cellular service, SIM-change notices, port-out notices, or text codes not arriving.
   - Contact your mobile carrier through its official site, app, phone number, or store and ask whether a SIM change or number port occurred.
   - Ask the carrier what extra account protections are available, such as a PIN, passcode, or port-out protection.
   - Where possible, change important accounts from SMS codes to an authenticator app or security key.

6. **Protect the most sensitive linked accounts next.**
   - Prioritize banking, payment apps, email, cloud accounts, Apple ID, Google, Microsoft, social accounts, and any account that can reset others.
   - Change passwords anywhere the same password was reused.
   - Turn on multi-factor authentication for those accounts.
   - Check each account’s recovery email, recovery phone, recent activity, and signed-in devices.

7. **Create an official report trail if there is identity theft, fraud, money loss, or cyber-enabled crime.**
   - Contact your bank, payment provider, or affected service immediately if money is moving or a transaction is pending.
   - Use IdentityTheft.gov if your personal information may be misused, such as new accounts opened in your name or someone using your details to pass security checks.
   - Use IC3.gov if you need to report cyber-enabled crime, online fraud, account takeover tied to fraud, or money stolen through an online scam.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to diagnose the exact cause right now.
- You don’t need to delete accounts immediately.
- You don’t need to factory-reset devices unless a trusted provider or specialist tells you it is needed.
- You don’t need to confront anyone you suspect.
- You can postpone longer-term choices, such as changing your main email address, buying identity monitoring, or doing a full device audit, until the urgent account controls are secure.

## Important reassurance
Attackers often change recovery settings so they can keep control of an account. Working email-first, then restoring passwords, recovery details, sessions, and multi-factor authentication is a practical way to reduce further damage.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about identity theft recovery, device security, financial disputes, or legal options may need help from the account provider, bank, carrier, law enforcement, or a qualified specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, forensic, cybersecurity, or other professional advice. Follow official provider, bank, carrier, and government instructions for your specific situation.
