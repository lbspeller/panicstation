# What to do if… you see an expensive unauthorized charge that is still pending on your card (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the card issuer or bank immediately using the number on the back of the card or the official app. Say that you see an unauthorized pending charge and ask what action the issuer can take now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a pending charge is harmless. It may still post to the account.
- Do not call a phone number or open a link from an unexpected text or email about the charge.
- Do not share screenshots publicly. They may expose account information.
- Do not unlock the card just to test it.
- Do not assume that locking the card reports the unauthorized charge.
- Do not wait for the monthly statement before contacting the issuer.
- Do not delete alerts, messages, or transaction details.

## What to do now
1. **Contact the issuer or bank using an official route.**  
   Use the number on the back of the card or the official app. Say: “I do not recognize this pending charge. I did not authorize it.” Ask what the issuer can do while it is pending.

2. **Lock the affected card in the official app, if that option is available.**  
   Do this while contacting the issuer. Ask whether the issuer recommends replacing the card.

3. **Check whether you can confidently match the charge.**  
   Hotels, rental-car companies, restaurants, and other businesses may place temporary holds or blocks. If you cannot confidently match the amount and merchant to something you did, continue treating the charge as unauthorized.

4. **If it is a debit card, explain any immediate impact.**  
   Tell the bank if the pending amount is reducing your available balance or affecting essential payments. Ask what options are available while the charge is pending.

5. **Save the transaction details.**  
   Write down or privately capture the amount, date, merchant descriptor, and any reference number shown. Note when you contacted the issuer and what the issuer told you.

6. **Keep checking the affected account.**  
   Report any additional unfamiliar transactions promptly. If you see other warning signs, such as unfamiliar accounts or address changes, use IdentityTheft.gov for a recovery plan.

7. **Contact the verified merchant after contacting the issuer, if you can identify it safely.**  
   Navigate to the merchant’s official website or app yourself. Do not use a phone number or link from an unexpected message. Ask whether the merchant can cancel the order or remove any hold.

8. **Follow the issuer’s instructions if the charge posts.**  
   Credit-card and debit-card procedures differ. Report the posted transaction promptly and ask what information the issuer needs from you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly how the card details were obtained before contacting the issuer.
- A full identity-theft recovery process can usually wait unless you see other warning signs, such as unfamiliar accounts, address changes, or several unauthorized transactions.
- Other reports can wait unless the issuer or an identity-theft recovery plan asks for one.
- You do not need to close every account now. Start with the affected card and any clear signs of compromise.

## Important reassurance
An expensive unfamiliar charge can feel alarming, especially while it is still pending. Reporting it promptly is a useful first step. You do not need to solve every part of the problem at once.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce further unauthorized use and start the issuer’s process. Later steps may depend on whether the transaction posts, whether the card is a credit card or debit card, and whether there are signs of broader identity theft.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal or financial advice. Procedures and protections differ for credit cards, debit cards, and other electronic transactions. Report an unauthorized transaction promptly and follow the instructions from your card issuer or bank.
