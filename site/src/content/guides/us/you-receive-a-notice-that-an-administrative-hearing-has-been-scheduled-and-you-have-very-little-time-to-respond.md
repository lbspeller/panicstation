# What to do if… you receive a notice that an administrative hearing has been scheduled and you have very little time to respond (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the agency or hearing office immediately to file a written "Request for Continuance" or "Motion to Postpone," citing "good cause" such as insufficient notice to prepare or lack of access to evidence. You must assume the hearing is still going forward until you receive a written order or formal confirmation that the date has been moved.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice — failure to appear usually results in a "default judgment" or automatic loss of your case.
- Do not rely on a phone call or voicemail alone — administrative agencies generally require a written request to change a record.
- Do not assume a pending request cancels the hearing — if the judge has not signed the order to continue, you must show up.
- Do not send original evidence through the mail — keep your originals and only send copies unless the specific agency rules state otherwise.
- Do not wait for a lawyer to be hired before asking for more time — you can file a request for an extension to find counsel yourself.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific office and case number from the notice.**
   **Locate the "Hearing Office," "Clerk," or "Administrative Law Judge" (ALJ) contact details and your unique case or docket number.**
2. **Download the specific agency form for a "Continuance" or "Postponement" if it exists.**
   **Check the agency's website for a "Forms" section; if no form exists, prepare a short letter titled "Motion to Continue" or "Request for Extension of Time."**
3. **Draft a concise explanation of "Good Cause" for the delay.**
   **State clearly that you received the notice on [Date] and that the short timeframe prevents you from gathering evidence, finding counsel, or arranging an interpreter.**
   - Include the date you received the envelope (check the postmark if possible).
   - List exactly what you are missing (e.g., medical records, case files, or time to consult a representative).
4. **File the request using the agency's fastest verified method.**
   **Use the online portal, fax (with a confirmation sheet), or hand-delivery to the hearing office to ensure it is received immediately.**
   - If using a portal, save a screenshot of the "Submission Successful" page.
   - If using email, request a read receipt and "CC" yourself.
5. **Serve a copy of your request to the "Other Party" if required.**
   **Send the same document to the agency representative or lawyer listed on the notice and file a "Certificate of Service" (a short statement saying when and how you sent it) with the hearing office.**
6. **Request an interpreter or ADA accommodation in the same filing.**
   **State specifically if you need a Spanish / Sign Language / other interpreter or a reasonable modification for a disability to participate effectively.**
7. **Create a "Hearing File" to track all communications.**
   **Keep a log of every person you speak to at the agency, the time of the call, and exactly what they told you about the status of your request.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare your full legal argument or witness list today.
- You do not need to have a lawyer fully retained before you ask for an extension of time.
- You do not need to decide if you want to appeal the entire case right now; focus only on the immediate hearing date.

## Important reassurance
It is a common procedural issue for notices to arrive late or for schedules to conflict. Administrative Law Judges (ALJs) have the authority to move dates when "good cause" is shown, especially when a person’s due process rights (the right to be heard and prepared) are at risk. Filing your request early and in writing is the most effective way to protect your rights.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. Administrative procedures vary significantly between federal agencies (like the SSA or VA) and state-level agencies (like Unemployment or Social Services). 

## Important note
This guide provides general procedural information and does not constitute legal advice. If your hearing involves the potential loss of liberty, professional licensure, or significant financial benefits, prioritize finding a licensed attorney or a legal aid clinic immediately.
