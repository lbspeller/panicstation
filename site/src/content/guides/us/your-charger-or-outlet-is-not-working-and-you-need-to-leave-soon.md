# What to do if… your charger or outlet is not working and you need to leave soon (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using anything hot, sparking, buzzing, smelling burnt, loose, or visibly damaged; then switch to a known-working outlet, cable, or charger and turn on battery saving so you can leave with enough power.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using a charger, cable, outlet, or power strip that is hot, sparking, buzzing, smells like burning plastic, has exposed wire, or looks melted.
- Don’t jam a plug into a loose outlet or hold it at an angle to make it work.
- Don’t run a damaged charging cable through a doorway, under a rug, under a chair leg, or anywhere it can be pinched.
- Don’t open, tape up, or keep using a damaged power adapter or power bank.
- Don’t troubleshoot electrical panels at work; move to another charging option and report the problem.
- Don’t leave a charger or power bank that is acting strangely unattended while you rush out.

## What to do now
1. **Unplug and do a quick safety check.** Look for heat, melting, discoloration, bent plug blades, frayed insulation, buzzing, sparking, a burnt smell, swelling, leaking, or hissing. If you notice any of these, stop using that item or outlet.

2. **Move to a safer charging path.** Try a different wall outlet that you know has power. Avoid using the same power strip, extension cord, or loose outlet.

3. **Swap one charging part at a time.** Try a different compatible cable, then a different compatible charger. Use an undamaged, reputable-brand charger and cable if you have one available.

4. **Check the device end without forcing anything.** Make sure the cable is firmly seated. Look for lint or debris in the charging port; do not scrape or force anything into the port. If you can remove loose lint gently with a soft brush and no resistance, do that and retry once.

5. **If the device shows a moisture or liquid warning, stop wired charging.** Do not override the warning. Use a wireless charger if your device supports it and the device maker allows it, or switch to a backup phone, power bank, or car charging plan.

6. **If a home outlet seems dead, try only simple reset points.** For a bathroom, kitchen, garage, basement, laundry, or outdoor outlet, look for a nearby GFCI outlet and press RESET once. If several outlets are out and there is no heat, burning smell, water, sparking, or visible damage, you may reset the breaker once if you can do so safely. If it trips again, stop using that circuit.

7. **At work, move and report.** Use a known-working outlet in another area. Tell Facilities, IT, security, or your supervisor that the outlet, power strip, or charger may be faulty. If you can do so without touching damaged parts, keep others from using it until it is checked.

8. **Turn on battery saving immediately.** Enable Low Power Mode or Battery Saver, lower the screen brightness, turn off hotspot, close high-drain apps, and use airplane mode only if you do not need calls, texts, maps, rideshare, or emergency contact.

9. **Choose one backup charging plan for the next hour.** Use a trusted power bank with a short undamaged cable, use a car charger while parked or as a passenger, move to a staffed area with a working outlet, or borrow a compatible charger from someone nearby.

10. **If a power bank is acting off, stop using it.** Swelling, hissing, leaking, unusual heat, a strong smell, or scorch marks are reasons to stop. Do not pick it up bare-handed if it is hot, leaking, smoking, or hissing. Do not put a lithium-ion battery or power bank in regular trash.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact failed part before you leave.
- You do not need to buy the best replacement charger right now.
- You do not need to decide warranty, repair, reimbursement, or workplace reporting details beyond a basic safety report.
- You can compare chargers, arrange electrician help, or check recalls after you are safely on your way.

## Important reassurance
A charger, cable, outlet, or power bank can fail suddenly even if it worked earlier. The safe move is to stop using anything suspicious, switch to a known-good charging path, and preserve the battery you still have.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the “I need to leave soon” moment only. Repeated outlet failures, tripped breakers, heat, burning smells, sparks, damaged wiring, or swelling batteries may need qualified electrical, workplace safety, manufacturer, or disposal help later.

## Important note
This is general information, not electrical, legal, workplace safety, repair, or professional advice. Follow your workplace procedures, device manufacturer guidance, and local safety instructions.
