# What to do if… you receive a notice that your home may be entered to take property (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as urgent but not as proof that entry will happen today. Verify the notice with the court or named agency using contact details you find yourself, and do not physically resist if an officer comes to your home.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the notice is real because it looks official; verify it with the court, sheriff, constable, or agency using an official website or phone number you find yourself.
- Don’t block entry, grab paperwork, hide items during a visit, or physically interfere.
- Don’t sign a payment agreement, consent form, or settlement while panicked.
- Don’t explain your whole situation at the door; keep your words short and factual.
- Don’t destroy, transfer, or sell property to try to avoid enforcement.
- Don’t ignore any deadline on the notice, even if you think the notice is wrong.

## What to do now
1. **Work out what kind of paper this is.**  
   Look for the exact words on the notice and write them down:
   - Civil collection words: “writ of execution,” “levy,” “execution,” “judgment,” “sheriff,” “constable,” “civil division,” or “levying officer.”
   - Police warrant words: “search warrant,” “seizure warrant,” “warrant,” “probable cause,” or a named police or federal agency.
   - Housing possession words: “writ of possession,” “writ of restitution,” “eviction,” or “lockout.”

2. **Copy the key details before you call anyone.**  
   Write down the court name, case number, names of the parties, issuing date, deadline, address listed, agency listed, and any officer or deputy name.

3. **Verify the notice using official contact details.**  
   Use the court’s official website, your state court self-help site, or the named sheriff, constable, marshal, or agency website. Ask:
   - Is this case number real and active?
   - Is there a current writ, levy, order, or warrant?
   - What does it authorize: notice only, levy, entry, seizure, removal, or another action?
   - Which office is assigned to carry it out?

4. **If it is civil enforcement, ask about exemptions and how to pause or contest it.**  
   Writs and levies usually apply only to non-exempt property, and exemption rules vary by state. Deadlines may be only days, so ask the court clerk, state court self-help service, or legal aid: “I received a writ or levy notice about property at my home. How do I claim exemptions or ask the court to pause enforcement?”

5. **If it is a police search or seizure warrant, keep the moment safe and documented.**  
   From a safe position, ask to see the warrant and check the address and scope. You can say, “I do not consent to any search beyond the warrant,” but do not argue, block, or interfere at the scene.

6. **If someone comes to your home, slow the interaction down safely.**  
   Ask for ID, badge information, the agency name, and the court document authorizing the action. Step back, keep your hands visible, and write down names, badge numbers, time of arrival, and what was said or taken.

7. **Ask for paperwork showing what happened.**  
   If anything is listed, tagged, seized, or removed, ask for an inventory, receipt, levy record, or instructions for how it will be provided. If they will not give it immediately, write down who you asked and what they said.

8. **Protect people and essential items without interfering.**  
   Move children, pets, or anyone distressed to a calmer room if possible. Keep essential medication, medical devices, ID, phone, charger, keys, and glasses accessible.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your full legal strategy right now.
- You do not need to negotiate a payment plan at the door.
- You do not need to make detailed statements about the debt, case, property ownership, or your finances during a visit.
- You do not need to understand every legal term before you verify the document and ask for urgent state-specific help.

## Important reassurance
A notice can be frightening, but it does not always mean immediate entry or removal will happen. The safest first move is to verify the document, avoid escalation, keep records, and get state-specific help quickly.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: how to verify the notice, handle a visit safely, and avoid panic mistakes. Later decisions about exemptions, hearings, stays, complaints, payment arrangements, or return of property may need help from a lawyer, legal aid, or a state court self-help service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Procedures, deadlines, exemptions, and warrant rules vary by state, county, court, and agency. If you feel in immediate danger, call 911.
