# What to do if… you are pressured to falsify records, hours, or performance figures at work (USA)

## Short answer
Do not alter, submit, or approve a record you believe is false. Pause, ask for the requested change in writing when safe, and keep a factual note of what happened before using a safer internal or external route.

## Do not do these things
- Do not make a change you believe is false just because someone says it can be fixed later. It may create personal risk.
- Do not sign a certification or approval you believe is inaccurate.
- Do not threaten, accuse, or argue with the person in the moment. Keep your response calm and factual.
- Do not delete messages, drafts, calendar entries, or notes about the request.
- Do not copy, move, or retain confidential records, customer information, patient information, or bulk internal data unless you are allowed to keep them.
- Do not post the situation publicly while you are deciding what to do next.

## What to do now
1. **Pause before changing or approving the record.**  
   Say: “I am not comfortable submitting information that may be inaccurate. I need to verify it first.”

2. **Ask for the requested change in writing when it is safe to do so.**  
   Say: “Can you confirm which figures or entries should be changed, the reason for the change, and who authorized it?”  
   If the person will not put the request in writing, note that fact.

3. **Make a factual note for yourself.**  
   Record the date, time, people involved, what you were asked to change, the record or system involved, any stated reason, and any witnesses. Keep the note accurate and brief.

4. **Identify the accurate record without taking restricted material.**  
   Keep copies of your own timecards, schedules, pay stubs, assignments, or approval messages only if you are allowed to keep them. For restricted records, note where they are stored and who can access them instead of exporting or copying them.

5. **Use a safer internal route when that is appropriate.**  
   Consider compliance, internal audit, HR, an ethics hotline, or a speak-up portal. Report the facts: what you were asked to change, why the requested change appears inaccurate, where the record is held, and whether you are concerned about retaliation. If the internal route is controlled by the person pressuring you or does not feel safe, consider an appropriate external route.

6. **For hours, pay, or timekeeping records, consider a labor enforcement route.**  
   The U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division can answer questions about complaints involving hours, pay, and recordkeeping. Laws enforced by that division may prohibit retaliation for raising covered concerns. A state labor agency may also be relevant.

7. **If retaliation starts, act promptly and check the correct route.**  
   Keep noting each threat, write-up, reduction in hours, suspension, demotion, or termination. OSHA handles retaliation complaints under the whistleblower statutes it administers, and deadlines under those statutes vary from 30 to 180 days after the adverse action. Other laws and agencies may have different deadlines, so do not delay seeking the appropriate route.

8. **For regulated records, identify the relevant specialist route.**  
   The correct route depends on the record and industry. Examples include safety logs, environmental reporting, healthcare billing, government contracting, or public-company reporting. For possible securities-law violations, the SEC provides a tip and complaint route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign.
- You do not need to make a public statement or post online.
- You do not need to identify a specific law before making a factual internal report.
- You do not need to decide every later step now.
- You do not need to choose an external route immediately unless a deadline may apply or internal reporting is unsafe.

## Important reassurance
You can slow this down. Refusing to make a record you believe is false, keeping a factual timeline, and choosing a safer route are reasonable first steps that protect your options.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on your state, industry, role, and the type of record, and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Rules and reporting routes vary by state, industry, and the type of record involved. If retaliation is already happening or a deadline may apply, consider prompt confidential advice from an appropriate specialist.
