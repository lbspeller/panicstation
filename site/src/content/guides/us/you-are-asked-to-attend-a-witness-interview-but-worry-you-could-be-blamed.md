# What to do if… you are asked to attend a witness interview but worry you could be blamed (USA)

## Short answer
Do not agree to a substantive interview before speaking with a criminal defense lawyer. If questioning is already happening, ask whether you are free to leave and clearly say that you are invoking your right to remain silent before answering substantive questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go in just to clear things up if you are worried you could be blamed.
- Do not assume an informal, voluntary, or phone conversation is risk-free.
- Do not guess, fill gaps, or speculate to sound helpful.
- Do not lie or give a rushed written statement.
- Do not voluntarily consent to a search, unlock a device, or provide a passcode on the spot because you feel pressured.
- Do not delete, alter, or throw away messages, documents, or devices.
- Do not ignore a subpoena, court order, or other official paperwork.

## What to do now
1. Ask whether the interview is voluntary. If you are already with investigators, ask plainly: “Am I required to stay?” and “Am I free to leave?”
2. If you are told that you are free to leave, leave calmly. If you are not free to leave, or if questioning continues, say: “I am invoking my right to remain silent. I want to speak with a lawyer before answering questions.” Stop answering substantive questions. Do not explain, guess, or chat about the matter. Do not physically resist.
3. Contact a criminal defense lawyer before agreeing to an interview. A state or local bar-association referral service can help you find one. Do not assume a court-appointed lawyer will be available for a voluntary interview.
4. Keep any interview request, business card, email, text message, voicemail, subpoena, or court paperwork. Note the name of the person who contacted you, the agency, the date, and the contact details. Ask a lawyer before creating a detailed written account.
5. If you receive a subpoena, court order, or request for documents or devices, contact a lawyer promptly. Do not assume that a blanket Fifth Amendment claim lets you ignore the paperwork. A lawyer can advise you about the required response.
6. If someone asks to search a device or account, say: “I do not consent to a search.” Do not voluntarily unlock the device or provide a passcode on the spot. Do not physically resist. Tell your lawyer what was requested.
7. For a voluntary interview request, ask your lawyer whether to attend, whether the lawyer can be present, and what conditions to request. Do not agree to an interview structure under pressure.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to cooperate with an interview.
- You do not need to write a full narrative while you are anxious.
- You do not need to decide on the spot whether to provide a device, passcode, account access, or documents.
- You do not need to argue your innocence in the moment.

## Important reassurance
Being described as a witness does not necessarily settle how investigators may view you later. Pausing, asking whether you are free to leave, and getting legal advice are reasonable first steps. They are not admissions of guilt.

## Scope note
These are first steps for an interview request or an encounter already underway. Later decisions may depend on the state, the agency, whether the interview is voluntary, and whether you have received legal paperwork. A qualified lawyer may need to advise you.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional representation. Do not ignore legal paperwork. Get advice from a qualified lawyer about your situation.
