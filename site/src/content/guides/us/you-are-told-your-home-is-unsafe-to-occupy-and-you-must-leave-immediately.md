# What to do if… you are told your home is unsafe to occupy and you must leave immediately (USA)

## Short answer
Get everyone to a safer place away from the hazard and follow any responder instructions. Once you are safe, confirm the access restriction and arrange shelter without delaying urgent needs such as medications.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t stay inside to finish packing if there may be collapse, fire, gas, flooding, damaged wiring, or another immediate hazard.
- Don’t re-enter unless the responder, local official, or other person controlling access says limited entry is allowed. Leave immediately if conditions worsen.
- Don’t use flames, operate switches, or do anything that could create a spark if you smell gas or suspect a leak. Leave the area and contact 911, the fire department, or the gas utility from a safe distance.
- Don’t touch electrical controls from a wet location or if wiring appears damaged.
- Don’t throw away damaged items or begin major cleanup before making a basic record, unless cleanup is needed for immediate safety.
- Don’t sign a document you do not understand or feel pressured to sign. Ask for a copy and review it later.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Move outside and away from smoke, falling debris, floodwater, damaged wiring, downed lines, or other immediate hazards. Follow responder instructions. Call **911** if there is immediate danger or urgent help is needed.
2. **Confirm the restriction from a safe place.** Ask for the name of the person or organization giving the instruction, whether entry is prohibited or restricted, and who controls access. Photograph any posted notice or tag if you can do so safely.
3. **Do not re-enter until limited entry is allowed.** Wait until the responder, local official, or other person controlling access says you may enter. If limited access is permitted, keep the visit brief, use battery-powered lighting, avoid flames, and leave immediately if you notice gas, smoke, unstable surfaces, or worsening conditions.
4. **If you rent, notify your landlord or property manager promptly.** Keep a written record. A short message is enough: “I was told the unit is unsafe to occupy. I have attached photos of the notice or visible damage. Please confirm the next steps for access and any available temporary-housing information.”
5. **Arrange a safe place to stay.** Try calling **211** or use 211.org to find local shelter and housing resources. The USA.gov emergency-housing page below lists additional shelter and temporary-housing routes. If a disaster is involved, check the American Red Cross shelter finder and follow local officials’ instructions.
6. **Retrieve essentials only if access is allowed.** Prioritize identification, keys, phone, charger, medications, medical devices, glasses, hearing aids, essential pet items, and a change of clothes. Do not go back in if entry is not allowed.
7. **Make a basic record from a safe place.** Photograph visible exterior damage and posted notices. Write down when you left, who told you to leave, the reason given, and any access limits. Keep receipts for immediate expenses such as lodging, meals, and essential supplies.
8. **Contact your insurer if applicable.** Use the available contact route for your homeowners or renters insurance. Do not let the claim delay immediate safety, shelter, or medications.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to repair, move permanently, or challenge the restriction.
- You do not need a complete inventory in the first hours. Start with visible photos, a short note, and receipts.
- You do not need to resolve who is responsible right now.
- You do not need to make long-term housing decisions tonight.

## Important reassurance
Leaving quickly can feel disorienting. The immediate priorities are a safer place, essential medications, a place to sleep, and a simple written record. The rest can be handled in stages.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the initial hours only. Later decisions may depend on the cause of the hazard, any official notice, your insurance, and state or local rules. Specialist help may be useful later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are unsure whether the home is safe, stay out until the responder, local official, or other person controlling access says entry is allowed. A qualified professional may also need to assess the property.
