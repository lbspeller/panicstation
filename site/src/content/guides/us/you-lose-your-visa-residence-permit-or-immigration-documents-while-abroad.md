# What to do if… you lose your visa, residence permit, or immigration documents while abroad (USA)

## Short answer
Get your passport and remaining ID secure first, then use the official U.S. route for the document you lost. A missing document or online record does not by itself prove you lost immigration status, but you may need official replacement or carrier documentation before you can travel.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t try to travel on a U.S. passport after you report it lost or stolen; once reported, it is not valid for international travel even if you later find it.
- Don’t pay “visa agents” or unofficial services that promise a quick replacement for a U.S. visa, Green Card, or carrier document.
- Don’t assume a photo of your card or visa will be enough for boarding; airlines and other carriers may require specific official documentation.
- Don’t report an expired U.S. passport as lost or stolen unless official instructions for your situation tell you to.
- Don’t make major immigration decisions while panicked; first work out what is missing, what proof you still have, and which official office handles it.
- Don’t delay basic theft steps if your documents were stolen; a prompt report may reduce identity-theft risk and may help with replacement processes.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safe, steady place and check what is actually missing.**  
   Re-check your bags, hotel safe, pockets, scanned copies, cloud storage, and recent locations. Separate what is still safe: passport, visa page, Green Card, reentry permit, employment card, approval notices, I-94 printout, and travel tickets.

2. **Make a simple proof pack before contacting anyone.**  
   Save or photograph any remaining proof you have: passport bio page, U.S. visa page, Green Card front and back, reentry permit, approval notices, receipt numbers, I-94 record, travel itinerary, police report number if you have one, and a written note of when and where the document went missing.

3. **If your U.S. passport is missing and you are a U.S. citizen, contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate.**  
   You generally need a new passport before returning to the United States. You may need to report the passport lost or stolen and apply in person; if travel is urgent, ask the consular staff about emergency passport options.

4. **If your passport with a U.S. visa was lost or stolen and you are not a U.S. citizen, replace your passport first through your own country’s embassy or consulate.**  
   Then report the lost or stolen U.S. visa to the U.S. embassy or consulate that issued it, or follow that post’s instructions. A lost or stolen U.S. visa generally cannot be replaced inside the United States; you usually need to apply again in person at a U.S. embassy or consulate abroad.

5. **If you are a lawful permanent resident and your Green Card or reentry permit was lost, stolen, destroyed, or damaged abroad, check Form I-131A.**  
   Form I-131A is the USCIS route for carrier documentation in certain situations. It may help an airline or other transportation carrier board you for travel to the United States, but it does not guarantee boarding or admission; CBP still decides admission at the port of entry.

6. **Contact your airline or other carrier before going to the airport.**  
   Tell them exactly what you lost and what official document or receipt you can show. Ask what they will accept for boarding, and write down the answer, the time, and the name or department you spoke to.

7. **If your I-94 is missing or “not found,” use the official CBP I-94 website and CBP help guidance.**  
   Try your name exactly as it appears on your passport and visa, including spacing and order. If the record still does not appear, follow CBP’s help instructions or contact CBP through the route the help page gives rather than guessing that your status is gone.

8. **If theft is likely, get a local police report if you can do so safely.**  
   A police report is not always mandatory, but it may help with a U.S. visa replacement application, passport replacement, carrier discussions, insurance, or identity-theft follow-up.

9. **After you return to the United States, replace the physical Green Card if it was lost or stolen.**  
   Use USCIS Form I-90 for a lost, stolen, or damaged Permanent Resident Card. Keep copies of any I-131A, police report, travel records, and USCIS receipts together until the replacement is resolved.

10. **If an employer, school, sponsor, or immigration lawyer is connected to your status, send a short factual update.**  
   Ask for copies of receipt notices, approval notices, SEVIS or employment records, or prior filings that may help confirm your status or support the correct replacement route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make complaints, insurance claims, refund claims, or formal identity-theft filings unless there is an immediate risk.
- You do not need to reconstruct your whole immigration history before taking the first step; focus on the document that lets you identify yourself and travel.
- You do not need to pay for unofficial advice at the airport counter; use the relevant U.S. government page, embassy or consulate, carrier, or your own qualified adviser.
- You do not need to decide long-term plans about work, study, or future travel until the immediate document problem is stable.

## Important reassurance
Losing immigration or travel documents abroad is alarming because travel depends on physical proof, but it is a known problem with official replacement and carrier-document routes. The next useful move is not to solve everything at once; it is to identify the missing document and contact the official office that handles that document.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilise the situation and help you avoid avoidable travel and status problems. Later decisions, especially about immigration status, long absences from the United States, pending applications, work, study, or re-entry risk, may need help from a qualified immigration lawyer or the relevant official agency.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Requirements can vary by your exact status, document type, time outside the United States, country you are in, carrier policy, and consular practice; use official U.S. government instructions for your situation.
