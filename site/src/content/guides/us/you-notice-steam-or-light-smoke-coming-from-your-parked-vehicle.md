# What to do if… you notice steam or light smoke coming from your parked vehicle (USA)

## Short answer
Stay back, keep others away, and call 911 if you see smoke, flames, sparks, a burning smell, or you are not sure whether it is fire.

## Do not do these things
- Do not move closer to inspect it, take photos, get belongings, or identify the source.
- Do not open the hood or trunk.
- Do not try to drive the vehicle away.
- Do not try to fight an under-hood fire yourself.
- Do not touch charging cables, exposed wires, leaking fluid, or anything dripping from the vehicle.
- Do not assume someone else has called 911.

## What to do now
1. Increase your distance and keep people back. If you can move farther away without crossing traffic or moving toward the vehicle, do that.
2. If the vehicle is in a garage, parking structure, carport, or near a building, move yourself into open air and away from the structure.
3. Watch only from where you are. Thickening smoke, visible flames, sparks, a strong burning smell, popping sounds, or crackling sounds are reasons to treat it as a possible fire.
4. Call 911 if you suspect fire or you are unsure. Say there is a parked vehicle with steam or light smoke, and say whether it seems to be from under the hood, trunk, wheel area, cabin, or charging area.
5. Give the exact location. Include the parking lot name, nearest entrance, cross streets, garage level, row, bay number, and which side of the building.
6. If it is an EV or hybrid, or it was charging, tell 911. Stay away from charging equipment, cables, exposed electrical parts, leaking fluids, smoke, vapors, and gases.
7. If the vehicle is in a managed lot, garage, workplace, apartment complex, or store parking area, notify security or management from a distance after 911 has been called. They may need to keep people back, control traffic, or guide responders.
8. Stay well away until responders arrive. For a possible vehicle fire, use at least 100 feet as a practical minimum distance when you can, and stay well away from traffic.
9. Update 911 if conditions change, especially if smoke increases, flames appear, the vehicle is near other vehicles or a building, or people move too close.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether it is coolant steam, oil smoke, brake smoke, electrical smoke, or a battery issue right now.
- You do not need to retrieve items from the vehicle.
- You do not need to call insurance, roadside assistance, a mechanic, or a tow company until the immediate hazard is clearly over.
- You do not need to explain the cause to bystanders.

## Important reassurance
Light smoke or steam can be hard to judge from a distance. Keeping space, preventing people from getting close, and calling 911 when unsure are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only while you are at a safe distance. Later decisions, such as towing, repair, insurance, or battery-specific handling, may need qualified specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, mechanical, firefighting, or other professional advice. If there may be immediate danger, call 911 and follow dispatcher instructions.
