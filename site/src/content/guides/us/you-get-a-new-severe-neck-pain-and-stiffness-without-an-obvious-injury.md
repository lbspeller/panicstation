# What to do if… you get a new severe neck pain and stiffness without an obvious injury (USA)

## Short answer
New, severe neck pain with stiffness (inability to touch chin to chest) is a medical emergency if accompanied by fever, severe headache, or confusion. **Call 911 immediately** if you feel systemically ill or notice neurological changes.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "crack," manipulate, or forcefully stretch your neck to restore motion.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel confused, dizzy, weak, or have changes in your vision or speech.
- Do not wait to see if the symptoms "wear off" if you have a fever or a severe, escalating headache.
- Do not take fever-reducing medication (like Tylenol or Advil) before speaking to a dispatcher or triage nurse, as this can mask a fever that is critical for diagnosis.
- Do not ignore a rash, even a small one, if it does not disappear when you press a clear glass against it.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 immediately if you have any of the following "red flag" symptoms:**
   - A neck so stiff that you cannot physically touch your chin to your chest.
   - A fever (100.4°F / 38°C or higher) combined with a severe headache.
   - Extreme sensitivity to bright light (photophobia) or persistent vomiting.
   - A rash of small red or purple spots that does not fade when pressed (the glass test).
   - Confusion, seizures, or difficulty staying awake.
   - New weakness, numbness, facial drooping, or slurred speech.
2. **Seek immediate Emergency Room evaluation if symptoms are severe but you are stable:**
   - Go to the nearest ER if you have a stiff neck and feel generally unwell, even without a high fever.
   - Choose the ER over Urgent Care if you are unsure of the severity; bacterial meningitis can progress to a life-threatening state in hours.
3. **Check for a non-blanching rash.**
   - **Press a clear drinking glass firmly against any red or purple spots on the skin.**
   - **If the spots remain visible through the glass and do not fade, tell emergency services immediately.**
4. **Remain in a neutral, supported position while waiting for help.**
   - **Lie down or sit with your head and neck supported by pillows in a position that minimizes pain.**
   - **Minimize movement of the head and avoid bright lights if they cause discomfort.**
5. **Note the exact time symptoms started.**
   - **Write down or remember when the stiffness began and how quickly it reached peak severity.**
   - **List any other symptoms like chills, nausea, or localized numbness.**
6. **Inform medical staff of any medications taken.**
   - **Clearly state if you have taken any pain relievers or fever reducers in the last 6 hours.**
   - **Mention if you have a compromised immune system or have had a recent infection.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause of the pain (e.g., muscle vs. infection) before seeking help.
- You do not need to look up physical therapy exercises or stretches today.
- You do not need to worry about long-term recovery or work absences in this moment.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant alarm when you cannot move your neck. While many cases of neck stiffness are related to muscle spasms, your reaction in seeking immediate clarity is the correct safety protocol. Medical professionals prioritize these symptoms because early intervention is highly effective for serious causes.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency screening only. It is not a diagnostic tool and does not replace a clinical examination by emergency medical personnel.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and is not medical advice. Severe neck stiffness with systemic symptoms is a time-sensitive emergency. If you are in doubt, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room.
