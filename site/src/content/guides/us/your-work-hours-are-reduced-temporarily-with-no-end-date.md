# What to do if… your work hours are reduced temporarily with no end date (USA)

## Short answer
Ask for the reduced schedule and any pay or benefits changes in writing. Check your state unemployment agency’s rules promptly because reduced hours may affect whether you can claim benefits.

## Do not do these things
- Do not quit in a rush. Leaving voluntarily may affect your options and unemployment eligibility.
- Do not rely only on a verbal explanation of the reduced hours.
- Do not assume that “temporary” means your pay, benefits, or paid time off will stay the same.
- Do not ignore a salary change if you are classified as exempt.
- Do not sign a new agreement or waiver while you feel pressured to decide immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the change in writing.** Request your new weekly hours or shift count, the effective date, whether there is an expected end date, and whether your hourly rate, salary, benefits eligibility, or paid time off accrual will change.
2. **Ask what kind of reduction this is.** Ask whether you are being moved to part-time status, placed on a reduced schedule, or included in a short-time compensation or work-sharing plan. Short-time compensation is generally initiated by the employer and requires an approved state plan.
3. **Save the records.** Keep schedules, emails, texts, pay stubs, and time records. Write down who told you about the reduction, when they told you, and what they said about it being temporary.
4. **Check your state unemployment rules promptly.** Your state may provide benefits for some reduced-hours situations. Use your state unemployment agency’s instructions, report hours and earnings accurately, and ask whether ordinary partial unemployment or an approved short-time compensation plan applies.
5. **Ask about salary-basis handling if you are classified as exempt.** Federal salary-basis rules generally require a predetermined salary not to be reduced because of variations in the quantity of work, and generally require the full salary for a week in which an exempt employee performs work, subject to exceptions. Ask HR in writing how your pay and classification will be handled.
6. **Ask for a review date.** Even if there is no end date yet, ask when the reduced schedule will next be reviewed and how updates will be communicated.
7. **Keep a timeline if the reduction followed a workplace complaint or accommodation request.** If the timing concerns you, preserve the sequence and the related messages. The EEOC resource below covers retaliation connected to discrimination, harassment, and disability or religious accommodation rights. Other kinds of complaints may use different official routes.
8. **Use any applicable union or written agreement.** If you are covered by one, contact your union representative or check the agreement for rules about schedule reductions, seniority, and meetings.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to leave the job or take formal action.
- You do not need to settle the employer’s motive before getting the schedule, pay, and benefits details in writing.
- You do not need to plan every financial or career step at once.

## Important reassurance
A temporary hours cut with no end date can feel destabilizing. The useful first move is to turn the verbal change into clear written details, check your state’s benefit rules, and avoid making an irreversible decision while you are under pressure.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from your state unemployment agency, a union representative, or an appropriate employment specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employment and unemployment rules vary by state and depend on the facts, your classification, and any applicable agreement.
