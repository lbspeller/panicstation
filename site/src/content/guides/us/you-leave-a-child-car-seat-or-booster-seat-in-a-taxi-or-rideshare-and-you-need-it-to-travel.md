# What to do if… you leave a child car seat or booster seat in a taxi or rideshare and you need it to travel (USA)

## Short answer
Start the official lost-item process for the exact ride now, and make a backup plan for a safe replacement or non-car travel today. State and local child-restraint rules vary, so the safe default is not to take a car ride with a child who still needs a car seat or booster until you have an appropriate restraint.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t take another car trip with a child who still needs a car seat or booster unless you are dealing with immediate danger and have no safer option.
- Don’t chase the driver or try to track them down yourself.
- Don’t post identifying ride, child, driver, or location details on social media.
- Don’t pay cash outside the app for a rideshare return fee if the app or company tells you not to.
- Don’t accept an unknown used seat unless you can check it is complete, undamaged, not expired, and has no known crash history.

## What to do now
1. **Collect the ride details immediately.**  
   Screenshot the trip or receipt. Note the pickup, dropoff, time, driver name, vehicle details, taxi number, booking number, or receipt number if you have them.

2. **Use the rideshare lost-item process for that exact trip.**  
   For Uber, open the trip and use the lost-item option to contact the driver. If you cannot reach the driver, use Uber’s help route for a lost item. For Lyft, report the lost item through the app or your email ride receipt link; Lyft says passengers can call or message the driver for 24 hours after the ride ends through that process.

3. **If it was a taxi, call the taxi company or dispatch line now.**  
   Use the receipt if you have it. Give the cab number, pickup and dropoff, time, and a plain description of the seat. If you were picked up at an airport, hotel, hospital, station, or venue, also ask their lost-and-found or ground-transportation desk.

4. **Arrange a safe, documented handoff.**  
   Ask for return through a public, staffed place such as a hotel front desk, airport information desk, taxi dispatch office, or rideshare pickup area. Confirm the seat by describing a unique feature such as the cover color or brand, without sharing extra personal details.

5. **Make a backup plan before the return is certain.**  
   Choose the quickest safe option available: buy a replacement seat that fits your child’s age, height, and weight; borrow a known safe seat from someone you trust; ask your hotel, airline, or local family contact about nearby options; or switch to train, subway, bus, or walking until you have the right seat.

6. **Check whether a car-seat rideshare option is actually available in the app.**  
   Uber Car Seat is only offered in limited places and is currently reservation-only where offered. Lyft Car Seat mode says it is currently only available in New York City. If the option does not appear for your location and child, plan as if it is not available.

7. **When the seat comes back, screen it before using it.**  
   Do not use it if it has visible damage, missing parts, cut or frayed straps, missing labels, no instructions when you need them, or any reason to suspect it was in a crash while out of your control. If you cannot be confident it is still safe, replace it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, request a refund, dispute a fee, or claim replacement costs.
- You do not need to research every local child-restraint rule while you are under pressure.
- You do not need to keep negotiating with a driver if the return is uncertain; switch to retrieval through official channels and a safe backup travel plan.

## Important reassurance
Families leave car seats and boosters behind during rushed dropoffs, airport runs, and late arrivals. The useful next move is simple: report the item through the right channel, keep your child out of unsafe car travel, and set up a backup seat or non-car route.

## Scope note
These are first steps to recover the seat and keep travel safe today. Later decisions about complaints, costs, insurance, or installation help may need the rideshare company, taxi operator, a child passenger safety technician, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Child-restraint requirements vary by state, city, vehicle type, and child size; when unsure, delay car travel until you have an appropriate seat and can use it correctly.
