# What to do if… you hear a faint hissing near a gas meter or regulator outside and it worries you (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the sound as a confirmed gas leak: **immediately move at least 330 feet away on foot** and **call 911**, then call your local gas utility's emergency line.

## Do not do these things
- Don't use a cell phone, flashlight, or any electronic device until you are a safe distance away.
- Don't smoke, light matches, or use lighters near the suspected leak.
- Don't start or stop any vehicle engines in the vicinity.
- Don't operate any electrical switches, including doorbells or outdoor lights.
- Don't attempt to tighten valves or investigate the meter with tools.
- Don't ignore the sound even if you cannot smell the "rotten egg" odor.

## What to do now
1. **Move away from the meter or regulator immediately on foot.** 
   - Ensure you move at least 330 feet (roughly one block) away from the source.
   - Stay upwind so any escaping gas is blown away from you.
2. **Warn others in the immediate area to evacuate.** 
   - If you must alert neighbors, knock on their door by hand.
   - Do not use doorbells or intercoms, as these can create a spark.
3. **Call 911 from a safe location once you are clear of the area.** 
   - Inform the dispatcher that you hear a hissing sound at the gas meter.
   - Provide the exact address and your current safe location.
4. **Call your local gas utility’s emergency leak line.** 
   - Most utilities operate these lines 24/7 and prioritize hissing sounds as high-priority emergencies.
   - Do not assume someone else has already called.
5. **Wait for emergency responders or utility technicians at your safe location.** 
   - Follow all instructions from fire department or utility personnel.
   - Do not re-enter the property or approach the meter until they declare it safe.

## What can wait
- You do not need to confirm if the noise is "normal" regulator operation; only a technician can verify this.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of the emergency visit; utilities typically do not charge for leak investigations.
- You do not need to call a private plumber or HVAC contractor until the utility has capped or cleared the leak.
- You do not need to gather personal belongings if they are near the meter or inside the house.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel anxious when hearing unexpected noises from utility equipment. Hissing is a primary physical cue for a high-pressure leak, and acting immediately is the standard safety protocol that saves lives. Utility companies prefer "false alarms" over ignored leaks.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and evacuation only. Technical diagnosis, repair, and restoration of service must be performed by certified utility professionals.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and does not replace the instructions of first responders or utility safety regulations. If you suspect a leak, prioritize immediate distance and professional notification.
