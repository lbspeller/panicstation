# What to do if… you receive an alert that your account security or recovery details have been changed (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the alert as possible account takeover. Go directly to the real service, not the alert link, then secure the account from inside the official app or website.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click the alert link if there is any chance it could be phishing; open the service from a trusted app, bookmark, or typed address instead.
- Don’t share login codes, reset links, screenshots of codes, or screen-sharing access with anyone claiming to be support.
- Don’t keep retrying passwords if the account starts locking or challenging you; use the provider’s official recovery flow.
- Don’t assume everything is fine because nothing looks missing; recovery details are often changed before obvious damage appears.
- Don’t pay, message, or give personal details to third-party or cold-contact “account recovery” offers.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the alert without using the alert link.**  
   Open the official app or type the provider’s address yourself. Check the account’s security area for recent activity, trusted devices, recovery email, recovery phone, and sign-in methods. Menu names vary by provider.

2. **If you can still sign in, secure the account from inside the official service.**  
   - Change the password to a new, unique password you do not use anywhere else.
   - Use the service’s session or device controls to sign out of other sessions if that option is available.
   - Remove devices, apps, passkeys, or sign-in methods you do not recognize.

3. **Restore account recovery and MFA.**  
   - Re-add the correct backup email, recovery email, phone number, or recovery method.
   - Turn on or re-enable MFA or 2FA.
   - If the service offers them, prefer an authenticator app, passkey, or security key over SMS-only verification.

4. **Check for hidden settings that could keep someone in control.**  
   Look for and remove anything you did not set up, especially:
   - Forwarding or redirect addresses
   - Mail rules or filters that auto-forward, auto-delete, archive, or hide messages
   - Connected apps, OAuth access, app passwords, or delegated access you do not recognize

5. **Secure the recovery roots.**  
   If this happened on your main email account, treat it as urgent because that email may reset many other accounts. If your phone number, SMS codes, or carrier account may be involved, contact your mobile carrier using the number on your bill or the official carrier site and ask them to review recent line changes, SIM changes, port-out activity, and call or SMS forwarding.

6. **If you cannot sign in, use official recovery immediately.**  
   Start from the provider’s official account recovery page or official app. If any of your devices are still signed in, look for built-in options such as “Secure your account,” “This wasn’t me,” “Review security events,” or similar provider wording.

7. **Save basic evidence without delaying account recovery.**  
   Save screenshots of the alert and any recent security events, including dates, devices, recovery changes, and locations or IP addresses if shown. Store the copies somewhere not controlled only by the affected account.

8. **If there is fraud, identity misuse, or financial loss, use official reporting portals.**  
   Use the official URLs below or type the address yourself. Report scams, fraud, or bad business practices to ReportFraud.ftc.gov. Use IdentityTheft.gov if your personal information is being used for accounts, benefits, taxes, debt collection, or other identity misuse. IC3.gov may be relevant for cybercrime complaints, including account takeover-related losses.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify exactly how this happened before securing the account.
- You do not need to notify everyone immediately; first stop forwarding, remove unknown access, and end sessions where possible.
- You do not need to delete the account or wipe devices in a panic.
- Broader clean-up, device scans, password-manager review, and linked-account checks can happen after the main account is stable.

## Important reassurance
This kind of alert is unsettling, but it is also a useful early warning. Using the official service, ending unknown access, restoring recovery details, and checking forwarding or rules can reduce further damage and help you regain control.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the account is tied to work systems, banking, healthcare, government benefits, or many linked services, later decisions may need specialist provider support, workplace IT support, financial-institution support, or other professional help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Provider recovery screens vary; follow the official process inside the service and avoid third-party recovery offers.
