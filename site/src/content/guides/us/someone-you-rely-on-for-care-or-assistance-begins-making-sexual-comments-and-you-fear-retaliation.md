# What to do if… someone you rely on for care or assistance begins making sexual comments and you fear retaliation (USA)

## Short answer
Involve a third-party supervisor, agency lead, or protective service immediately to arrange for a different worker so the hazard is removed without you managing the transition alone.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the individual one-on-one if you fear they will withhold essential care or escalate their behavior.
- Do not agree to keep the behavior secret in exchange for help with medication, transportation, or basic needs.
- Do not allow the person to isolate you by taking your phone, locking doors, or discouraging visits from others.
- Do not wait for physical proof before requesting a change in staffing.
- Do not assume you must handle the firing or replacement process yourself.

## What to do now
1. **Ensure you are not in immediate physical danger and call 911 if needed.**  
   If the person is currently present and you feel unsafe, move to a shared area, contact a neighbor or family member, or call 911 if they refuse to leave or are being physically aggressive.
2. **Contact the care agency or facility supervisor to request an immediate staff change.**  
   Call the home health agency, program coordinator, or nursing home administrator and state: "I need a different caregiver assigned immediately due to sexual comments and fear of retaliation."  
   - Request that the current worker be removed from your schedule or unit.
   - Ask for a "no-contact" order regarding your care and access to your home.
3. **Report the situation to Adult Protective Services (APS).**  
   APS is the state-designated agency for protecting adults who are being mistreated by those they depend on for care.  
   - Call the **Eldercare Locator** at 1-800-677-1116 (9:00 AM – 8:00 PM ET) to be connected to your local APS office.
   - If calling after hours, search your state's name and "Adult Protective Services hotline" for 24/7 reporting lines.
4. **Notify the Long-Term Care Ombudsman if you live in a facility.**  
   If the harassment is occurring in a nursing home or assisted living facility, the Ombudsman is a free, independent advocate who helps resolve resident safety issues. Ask facility staff for the Ombudsman’s contact information or call 1-800-677-1116 to find them.
5. **Establish a "no-alone" protocol for any necessary transition visits.**  
   If a new worker cannot arrive immediately and care is essential, ensure a third party is present (a friend, relative, or neighbor) for the duration of the visit. Keep your phone on your person at all times.
6. **Contact a confidential sexual assault advocate for guidance.**  
   Call the **National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN)** at 1-800-656-4673 for confidential support and information on your rights without an immediate requirement to report to law enforcement.
7. **Create a brief, private record of the comments and your specific fears.**  
   Write down the date, time, and specific phrases used, as well as any care tasks they threatened to withhold. Keep this record in a location the caregiver cannot access, such as a secure digital note or with a trusted friend.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to press criminal charges today.
- You do not need to have a "perfect" or detailed memory of every comment to ask for help.
- You do not need to worry about the caregiver's employment status or personal life; your safety is the priority.
- You do not need to provide immediate "proof" to the agency for them to honor a request for a new worker.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of "caregiver hostage" when you rely on someone for basic survival or dignity. Fearing retaliation—such as being left without food, medication, or help getting out of bed—is a rational response to a power imbalance. Taking steps to involve a supervisor or APS is not "causing trouble"; it is a standard safety procedure to ensure your care remains stable and respectful.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying a harassment hazard and initiating a staff replacement. It does not cover long-term legal claims or civil litigation.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and is not legal or clinical advice. If you are in immediate danger or have been sexually assaulted, call 911. Retaliation against residents or clients for reporting abuse is prohibited by law in most jurisdictions; if you face a sudden loss of care services, contact APS or the Eldercare Locator immediately.
