# What to do if… your email account starts sending automatic replies you did not set up (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all attempts to "fix" or repair the drive software. Disconnect the device immediately to prevent further mechanical wear or data overwriting, and treat the situation as a data recovery operation rather than a hardware repair.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click **Format**, **Initialize**, or **Erase** if prompted by the operating system.
- Do not run **Chkdsk /f** (Windows) or **First Aid** (Mac) on an unmountable drive, as these can permanently overwrite corrupted data.
- Do not keep the drive powered on if you hear clicking, grinding, or repetitive beeping sounds.
- Do not attempt to "freeze" the drive or open the sealed enclosure.
- Do not install "cracked" or unknown data recovery software from the internet.
- Do not tap, shake, or hit the drive to get it to spin.

## What to do now
1. **Document the exact error message currently on your screen.**
   - Take a clear photo of any pop-up window that asks you to format or initialize the disk.
   - Note if the drive is making any specific sounds such as a rhythmic "click-click-click," a faint beeping, or a high-pitched whirring.

2. **Power down the drive immediately.**
   - Unplug the USB cable from the computer.
   - If the drive has its own power adapter, unplug it from the wall outlet. 
   - Physical failure often worsens every second the drive receives power.

3. **Check the physical connection once using different hardware.**
   - **Switch to a different USB port directly on the computer, avoiding hubs.**
   - **Use a known-working USB cable of the same type.**
   - **If the drive requires a power adapter, ensure it is the original or a certified replacement.**
   - If the drive still does not mount after one attempt, disconnect it again and stop testing.

4. **Verify the drive's status in a non-destructive management tool.**
   - **Windows:** Right-click the Start button and select **Disk Management**. 
     - Look for a disk labeled "Unknown," "Not Initialized," or "Unallocated." 
     - **Do not** click "OK" if a prompt appears asking to initialize the disk.
   - **Mac:** Open **Disk Utility** (Applications / Utilities).
     - Check if the physical drive name appears in the left sidebar, even if the volume underneath it is greyed out.
     - **Do not** click "Erase" or "Partition."

5. **Determine the value of the data versus the cost of recovery.**
   - If the files are irreplaceable (e.g., family photos, business records), stop all DIY efforts and contact a professional data recovery service.
   - In the USA, reputable labs often offer a "no data, no fee" policy and free evaluations.

6. **Separate warranty claims from data retrieval.**
   - **Contact the manufacturer only after the data is recovered or if you have decided the data is not worth the recovery cost.**
   - Manufacturer warranty service usually involves "recertifying" or replacing the drive, which involves a total wipe of your files.

7. **Consult the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) guidelines for defective products if a manufacturer denies a valid warranty claim.**
   - Review your rights under the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act if the device failed prematurely under normal use.

## What can wait
- You do not need to buy a replacement drive immediately.
- You do not need to decide which recovery lab to use in the next hour; the data is safer with the drive powered off.
- You do not need to research "re-partitioning" or "file system conversion."
- You do not need to worry about your backup strategy until the current crisis is stabilized.

## Important reassurance
The "Format" or "Initialize" prompts you see are not the computer telling you the data is gone; they are the computer's way of saying it no longer recognizes the organizational structure of the drive. As long as you do not write new information to the disk, the raw data often remains intact and recoverable by specialists.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for digital storage failure. It focuses on preventing accidental data erasure and does not provide instructions for advanced software recovery or physical hardware repair.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not guarantee data recovery. Hard drive failures are physical events that can involve permanent media degradation. If files are critical, consult a certified data recovery professional before attempting any further software-based troubleshooting.
