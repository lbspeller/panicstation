# What to do if… a colleague is spreading your personal information at work and it is escalating (USA)

## Short answer
If the sharing creates an immediate safety risk, move to a safer place if you can do so without increasing risk and get urgent help. Otherwise, save what you can lawfully access, make a short timeline, and report the escalation in writing through your workplace’s reporting route or the fastest appropriate internal channel.

## Do not do these things
- Do not respond in a team-wide channel or start a workplace pile-on.
- Do not retaliate by sharing the other person’s personal information.
- Do not delete messages, emails, posts, or notes that may show what happened.
- Avoid secretly recording conversations as a first step.
- Do not quit on the spot while overwhelmed unless staying creates an immediate safety risk.
- Do not try to correct every rumor person-by-person while the sharing is still spreading.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause if needed.** Move to a safer place if you can do so without increasing risk. If there is an emergency requiring immediate police, fire department, or ambulance assistance, call **911**. Tell a supervisor, security contact, or HR contact that you need an immediate safety response.
2. **Preserve what you can lawfully access.** Save screenshots, emails, posts, and messages. Start a short incident log with the date, time, what was shared, where it appeared, who may have seen it, and any change after you objected.
3. **Make a written internal report.** Use the reporting route in your workplace policy or the fastest appropriate channel, such as a supervisor or HR. If your supervisor is involved, use another available route. State what information is spreading, where it is spreading, why it is escalating, and what immediate containment you are requesting.
4. **Ask for practical containment.** Depending on the workplace and the facts, ask whether the employer can remove posts, restrict access to a channel, stop further circulation, reduce direct contact, and preserve relevant logs.
5. **Reduce exposure in workplace systems.** Ask HR or IT whether non-essential personal details can be hidden or restricted in directories, profiles, scheduling tools, or email signatures. Ask them to preserve relevant system records.
6. **Say plainly if discrimination may be involved.** Not every instance of personal information sharing is covered by federal employment discrimination law. If you believe the sharing is connected to race, color, religion, national origin, sex, pregnancy, sexual orientation, transgender status, age of 40 or older, disability, or genetic information, state that in your report. Additional protections may depend on state or local law. If the escalation began after you raised a discrimination concern, document that timing and any adverse changes.
7. **Check deadlines promptly if an external discrimination route may matter.** You do not need to decide every later step now, but do not assume that an internal report changes an external filing deadline. If you are unionized, you can also contact your union representative about immediate workplace steps.

## What can wait
- Writing a perfect or lengthy statement. A short written report, incident log, and saved material are enough for the first step.
- Correcting every rumor individually.
- Making a long-term decision about transfer, leave, or resignation while you are overwhelmed.
- Deciding every external option today, unless a filing deadline may be close or there is an immediate safety risk.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to solve the whole situation at once. The useful first moves are to reach a safer pause if necessary, preserve what is already available, make a clear written report, and ask for containment.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on workplace policy, the type of information shared, the source of the information, and whether employment discrimination or retaliation protections may apply. Specialist help may be useful for later decisions.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If there is an emergency requiring immediate police, fire department, or ambulance assistance, call 911.
