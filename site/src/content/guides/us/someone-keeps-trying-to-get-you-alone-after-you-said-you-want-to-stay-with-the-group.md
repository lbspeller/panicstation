# What to do if… someone keeps trying to get you alone after you said you want to stay with the group (USA)

## Short answer
Maintain physical proximity to your group or venue staff and do not allow yourself to be isolated for any reason.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to a private area to end the conversation or "be polite."
- Do not step outside, go to the restroom, or walk to a vehicle alone if the person is nearby.
- Do not accept a ride, a "shortcut," or a request to help them with something in a secluded spot.
- Do not worry about "making a scene" or being rude by involving others.
- Do not ignore your instincts if you feel the person is waiting for a moment when you are alone.

## What to do now
1. **Physically re-anchor to the center of your group.** Move so that your friends are between you and the person. 
   - Stand near high-traffic areas like the bar, security stations, or the front desk.
   - Use physical objects like tables or chairs to create a barrier.
2. **Explicitly state your need to a trusted person.** Tell a friend or nearby bystander exactly what is happening.
   - Use a clear phrase: "I do not want to be alone with this person; please stay with me."
   - If speaking is difficult, type the message on your phone and show it to a friend or staff member.
3. **Employ the buddy system for all movements.** Ensure a friend accompanies you for every transition, including going to the bathroom, getting air, or checking a coat.
   - If your group is large, designate one "safety buddy" who will not leave your side.
4. **Notify venue staff or security immediately.** Approach a bartender, host, or bouncer and state that you feel unsafe.
   - Say: "This person is ignoring my boundaries and trying to isolate me."
   - Ask for staff to watch you while you wait for a ride or to help you reunite with your group.
5. **Coordinate a secure exit.** Do not leave the building until your transport is confirmed and at the door.
   - Stay inside the venue while waiting for a rideshare or taxi.
   - Confirm the vehicle license plate and driver identity through the app before approaching.
   - Ask a friend or security guard to walk you all the way to the vehicle.
6. **Call 911 if you feel you are in immediate danger.** If it is unsafe to speak, use the "Call if you can, text if you can't" protocol.
   - Texting 911 is available in many US jurisdictions; if it is not supported in your area, you should receive a "bounce-back" message.
7. **Contact the National Sexual Assault Hotline for confidential support.** You can call 800.656.HOPE (4673) or text HOPE to 64673 to speak with a trained staff member from RAINN.
   - This service is available 24/7 and does not require you to report a crime to receive help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a formal report or talk to the police right now.
- You do not need to explain your reasons for staying with the group to the person hovering.
- You do not need to analyze the person's intentions or wonder if you are overreacting.

## Important reassurance
Persistent behavior that ignores a stated boundary is a valid safety concern. Feeling startled, anxious, or "frozen" is a common and normal response to unwanted persistence. It is appropriate and responsible to prioritize your physical safety by seeking support from friends and professionals.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate safety concern in a social setting. It does not cover legal procedures or long-term safety planning.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional legal or security advice. If you are in immediate danger, contact local emergency services.
