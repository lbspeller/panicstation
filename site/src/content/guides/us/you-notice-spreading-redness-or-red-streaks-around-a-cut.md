# What to do if… you notice spreading redness or red streaks around a cut (USA)

## Short answer
Spreading redness, red streaks, fever, chills, or worsening pain around a cut needs same-day medical assessment. Call 911 now if you have emergency warning signs such as trouble breathing, confusion, fainting, clammy or gray-looking skin, extreme pain, or you seem dangerously unwell.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait overnight if redness is spreading, red streaks are appearing, or you feel feverish, chilled, weak, or unusually unwell.
- Don’t squeeze, lance, drain, or “pop” the wound or any swelling.
- Don’t take leftover antibiotics or someone else’s antibiotics.
- Don’t scrub aggressively or use bleach, peroxide, alcohol, or harsh chemicals on the area.
- Don’t wrap the area tightly.
- Don’t keep rings, watches, or tight jewelry on a swelling hand, arm, foot, or leg.

## What to do now
1. **Choose emergency help if there are danger signs.**
   - Call 911 now if there is trouble breathing, confusion, fainting or near-fainting, clammy or gray-looking skin, extreme pain, or the person seems dangerously ill.
   - Go to the ER now if the infection is near an eye, on the face with swelling, spreading very quickly, causing severe pain, or affecting a baby, a very young child, or someone who is immunocompromised.

2. **Get same-day medical care if you are not in immediate danger.**
   - Use urgent care, your clinician’s same-day line, or the ER if no same-day clinic is available.
   - Tell them you have a cut with spreading redness or red streaking. If you have fever, chills, worsening pain, swelling, pus, nausea, vomiting, diabetes, pregnancy, immune suppression, a bite wound, or a dirty wound, say that clearly.

3. **Track the spread without delaying care.**
   - Take your temperature and write down the time.
   - Mark the outer edge of the redness with a pen and write the time next to it.
   - Take one clear photo in good light so the clinician can compare changes.

4. **Cover the wound simply while you are arranging care.**
   - Wash your hands.
   - Rinse the cut gently with clean running water if it is dirty.
   - Pat dry and cover it with a clean, non-stick dressing.

5. **Reduce swelling and avoid extra irritation.**
   - Elevate the affected arm or leg if you can do that comfortably.
   - Keep clothing and bandages loose over the area.
   - Remove rings, watches, and tight jewelry from the affected limb before swelling increases.

6. **Bring the details that change care decisions.**
   - Note when the cut happened and when the redness or streaking started.
   - Bring a list of medicines, allergies, recent antibiotics, and medical conditions such as diabetes, immune suppression, circulation problems, recent surgery, or pregnancy.
   - If an animal or human bite caused the cut, tell the clinician right away.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether this is cellulitis, lymphangitis, an allergic reaction, or something else before being seen.
- You do not need to repeatedly clean the wound or try several creams before getting help.
- You do not need to solve work, transportation beyond getting there safely, insurance details, or follow-up plans before the urgent assessment.
- You do not need to decide whether antibiotics, drainage, tests, or hospital treatment are needed; that can be decided after you are assessed.

## Important reassurance
You are not overreacting by getting spreading redness or red streaking checked the same day. Prompt treatment gives clinicians the best chance to stop a skin infection before it worsens.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours only. Later decisions about antibiotics, wound care, drainage, follow-up, tetanus vaccination, rabies risk after an animal bite, or hospital treatment may need a licensed medical professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, or professional care. If symptoms are spreading or the person seems significantly unwell, seek urgent in-person medical care now.
