# What to do if… you are asked to complete an entry form or declaration but it is only available in a language you cannot read (USA)

## Short answer
Stop and do not sign or submit any document you cannot fully understand. Verbally request an English version or language assistance from the presiding official under the Language Access Plan.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess the meaning of questions on customs, immigration, or health forms, as incorrect answers are legally treated as "willful misrepresentation."
- Do not sign any declaration or statement you cannot read just to expedite the process or avoid a delay.
- Do not use your mobile device for translation in restricted areas without explicit permission from a Customs and Border Protection (CBP) officer.
- Do not allow a bystander or another traveler to handle your passport or sensitive travel documents to "translate" for you.
- Do not agree to an "informal" summary of the form; ensure you understand the specific legal obligations you are certifying.

## What to do now
1. **State your need clearly and immediately to the official.** **Tell the officer, "I cannot read this language and I need an English version or an interpreter before I can complete this form."**
   - Use an "I Speak" card if available or show a digital note that identifies your primary language.
2. **Request language assistance under the official Language Access Plan (LAP).** **If an English form is unavailable, ask for a qualified interpreter to read the form to you line-by-line.**
   - Federal agencies are required to provide meaningful access to those with limited English proficiency.
3. **Request a supervisor if assistance is denied or the officer insists you sign anyway.** **Calmly ask to speak with a "Watch Commander" or a supervisor regarding your right to language assistance.**
   - Do not become confrontational; maintain that you cannot legally certify a document you cannot read.
4. **Clarify specific "Yes" or "No" questions before marking them.** **For each question, ask the officer or interpreter to confirm exactly what is being asked, such as "Is this asking if I have food?" or "Is this asking about my previous travel?"**
   - Confirm the answer they record matches your verbal response before they move to the next section.
5. **Observe the area for digital kiosks with language toggles.** **Check if the entry terminal or Global Entry/APC kiosk has a "Language" or "English" button at the bottom of the screen.**
   - Many modern entry systems allow you to switch the interface language immediately.
6. **Note the officer’s name or badge number if a dispute occurs.** **Write down the identification details and the time of the request for your own records once you are in a permitted area.**
   - This documentation is necessary if you need to file a "DHS TRIP" redress request later.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to file a formal civil rights complaint.
- You do not need to provide an explanation for why you cannot read the specific language provided.
- You do not need to contact your embassy or a lawyer unless you are being detained or directed to sign a "Record of Abandonment" (Form I-407).

## Important reassurance
Encountering a form in a language you do not understand is a common logistical hurdle in international transit. Seeking assistance is a standard procedural right, and officials are trained to handle language barriers daily. A delay for translation is safer than a permanent record of an incorrect declaration.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for document handling at ports of entry. It does not cover legal defense for secondary inspections, criminal proceedings, or specialized visa applications.

## Important note
This information is based on 2026 U.S. federal language access protocols. Specific availability of interpreters may vary by port of entry, and individual officers have discretion over the flow of primary inspection. This is not legal advice.
