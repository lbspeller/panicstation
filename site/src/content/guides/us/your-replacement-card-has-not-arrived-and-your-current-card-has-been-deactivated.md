# What to do if… your replacement card has not arrived and your current card has been deactivated (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately contact your bank via a verified phone number to report the card as "never received," which triggers legal liability protections and allows the issuer to void the missing card and provide an immediate digital or in-person cash workaround.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait for "one more mail delivery" if your current card is already deactivated; this delay can increase your liability for fraud.
- Don’t click links in texts or emails claiming to "track your card" or "update delivery details," as these are common phishing tactics.
- Don’t share your PIN, online banking password, or one-time passcodes with anyone, even if they claim to be from the bank's fraud department.
- Don’t attempt to use the deactivated card repeatedly at different terminals, as this may trigger a secondary security freeze on your entire account profile.

## What to do now
1. **Call the issuer’s official fraud or customer service line immediately.**
   - **Use a trusted number found on your monthly statement, the back of your old card, or the bank’s official mobile app.**
   - **State clearly: "I am reporting a replacement card as not received, and my access to funds is currently blocked."**

2. **Verify the status of the missing replacement card.**
   - **Ask the representative if the new card has already been activated or used for any transactions.**
   - **Request that the missing card be "killed" (invalidated) rather than just "frozen" to prevent future unauthorized use.**
   - **Confirm the exact mailing address on file and ask if a "Change of Address" or "Mail Forwarding" order was recently processed.**

3. **Secure immediate access to your funds using one of three primary methods.**
   - **Request an "Instant Issue" digital card** that can be added to a mobile wallet (Apple Pay, Google Pay, or Samsung Pay) for immediate use.
   - **Ask for "Cardless ATM" instructions**, which allow you to generate a one-time code in the bank’s app to withdraw cash without a physical card.
   - **Locate a local branch** and ask for a temporary debit card or an over-the-counter cash withdrawal using a government-issued photo ID.

4. **Review your account for unauthorized activity (Regulation E/Z triggers).**
   - **Check for small "test" transactions or new recurring subscriptions you did not authorize.**
   - **Report any unauthorized electronic transfers within 2 business days of discovery to cap your liability at $50 under Regulation E.**
   - **Note that waiting longer than 2 business days but fewer than 60 days from your statement date can increase your liability to $500.**

5. **Request expedited shipping for the new replacement.**
   - **Ask the bank to send the next card via overnight or 2-day delivery with a tracking number.**
   - **Inquire if the issuer can ship the card to a local branch for secure pickup instead of your home mailbox.**

6. **Check for secondary indicators of identity theft.**
   - **Contact the USPS or check "Informed Delivery" to see if mail from your bank was scanned but not delivered.**
   - **Check for any unauthorized changes to your phone number or email address within your banking profile.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a police report immediately unless the bank specifically requests one for a fraud claim.
- You do not need to close the underlying bank account unless the bank confirms that your account number (not just the card number) has been compromised.
- You do not need to contact the credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) right this second, though you should do so later if you suspect your full identity was used to intercept the card.

## Important reassurance
Being unable to access your money is high-stress, but banking systems are designed to provide workarounds for exactly this scenario. Reporting the card as "not received" immediately shifts the primary responsibility for unauthorized charges away from you. Most major US banks can restore some form of digital or in-branch access within minutes of your call.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for a missing replacement card and sudden loss of access. It does not replace the formal dispute process required for existing fraudulent charges or long-term identity theft recovery.

## Important note
This information is based on federal regulations (Regulation E and Regulation Z) as of 2026. Specific bank policies regarding digital cards and cardless ATM access vary by institution. Always use the contact information provided directly by your financial institution.
