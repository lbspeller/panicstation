# What to do if… you receive a fraud text or call claiming your account will be closed unless you act now (USA)

## Short answer
Terminate the communication immediately and do not click any links or share any codes. Contact your financial institution directly using the verified phone number on the back of your physical debit or credit card.

## Do not do these things
- Don't click links, download attachments, or install remote-access software (such as AnyDesk or TeamViewer) requested by the caller.
- Don't provide One-Time Passcodes (OTP), multi-factor authentication codes, or PINs to anyone over the phone.
- Don't transfer money to a "safe account" or a "government holding account" to protect it from closure.
- Don't use the "call back" feature or dial numbers provided in the text message.
- Don't stay on the line if the caller asks you to log into your account while they watch or "assist" you.

## What to do now
1. **Break contact and pause.**  
   **Hang up the phone or close the messaging app immediately without replying.** If you feel a sense of extreme urgency or fear, wait 60 seconds before taking further action to allow the physiological pressure of the scam to subside.
2. **Verify your account status through an independent channel.**  
   **Log into your bank's official mobile app or type the bank's web address directly into your browser.** Check for:
   - Unauthorized pending transactions or transfers.
   - New linked accounts or "Payee" profiles you did not create.
   - Recent changes to your contact information (email or phone) in the profile settings.
3. **Call your bank's official fraud department.**  
   **Use the phone number printed on your physical card or found on your most recent paper statement.** 
   - Inform the representative that you received an "account closure" threat.
   - Ask them to verify if any legitimate alerts were sent to you.
4. **Report specific data exposure if you interacted with the scammer.**  
   **Tell the bank exactly what information was shared, such as a password, a security code, or a social security number.** 
   - Request that they "Lock" or "Freeze" the affected accounts immediately.
   - Ask for a "Hold Harmless Letter" or "Letter of Indemnity" if any unauthorized wire transfers occurred.
   - Request new account numbers and replacement cards if account details were compromised.
5. **Secure your digital identity from a clean device.**  
   **Change the passwords for your bank account and your primary email account using a device you know is secure.**
   - If you downloaded software at the scammer's request, do not use that specific device for banking until it has been professionally wiped or scanned for malware.
6. **Report the message to your cellular carrier.**  
   **Forward the scam text message to 7726 (SPAM).**
   - After forwarding the message, wait for the carrier's automated reply and then send a second text containing the scammer's original phone number.
7. **File official federal reports.**  
   **Submit a report to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov and the FBI at IC3.gov.**
   - Include the specific wording used in the "account closure" threat and the phone number or link provided by the scammer.
8. **Initiate identity theft protections if personal data was lost.**  
   **Visit IdentityTheft.gov to create a recovery plan if you shared your Social Security Number (SSN) or Date of Birth.**
   - Contact one of the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion) to place a "Fraud Alert" or a "Credit Freeze" on your file.

## What can wait
- You don't need to determine the identity of the scammer or where they are located.
- You don't need to report the incident to local police unless the bank or a federal agency specifically requires a police report for a claim.
- You don't need to change your phone number or close your email account immediately.

## Important reassurance
The threat of an immediate "account closure" is a standard psychological tactic used to bypass your critical thinking. By hanging up and using a trusted contact method, you have already neutralized the scammer's primary tool of influence.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for responding to impersonation-based financial threats. It does not cover long-term credit repair or legal disputes with financial institutions regarding liability for authorized push payment fraud.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Banking regulations and fraud reporting procedures may vary by institution and state law. Always prioritize the instructions provided by your bank's verified fraud department.
