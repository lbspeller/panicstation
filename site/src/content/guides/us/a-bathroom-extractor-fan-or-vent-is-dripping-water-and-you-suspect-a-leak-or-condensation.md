# What to do if… a bathroom extractor fan or vent is dripping water and you suspect a leak or condensation (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as a water-and-electricity hazard: stop using the fan, keep the area dry, and shut off the bathroom circuit at the breaker if water is dripping from the fan, light, switch, or surrounding ceiling.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep the fan running if water is dripping from the fan, vent, light, or surrounding ceiling.
- Don’t touch the fan, light, switch, grille, or breaker with wet hands or while standing on a wet floor.
- Don’t reset a tripped GFCI or breaker while the fan or light is wet.
- Don’t open the fan housing or pull the unit down while it may be energized or wet.
- Don’t ignore a sagging or bulging ceiling around the fan; water can release suddenly.
- Don’t paint, caulk, or seal over damp staining yet.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safer.** Put a bucket or towels under the drip, dry the floor, and keep children and pets out of the bathroom for now.
2. **Stop adding moisture.** Pause showers and baths. If it is safe, open a window or increase ventilation nearby, but do not run the wet fan.
3. **Shut off power if water may be touching electrical parts.**
   - If water is dripping from the fan, light, switch, or ceiling around the fixture, turn off the bathroom circuit at the breaker panel.
   - If you cannot reach the panel without standing in water or touching wet surfaces, keep people out and get help.
   - GFCI protection can reduce shock risk, but it does not make a wet fan or light safe to use.
4. **Document the pattern quickly.** Take photos or video of the drip, ceiling stain, swelling, and fan cover. Note whether it happens after showers, during cold weather, during rain, during wind, while the fan runs, or while the fan is off.
5. **Do a quick look-only check from the bathroom.**
   - Dripping mostly after showers or in cold weather often points to condensation collecting in cold ductwork.
   - Dripping during rain, spreading stains, soft drywall, or a drip that keeps getting worse may point to a roof, vent flashing, plumbing, or drain leak.
6. **If you can safely access the attic, do a brief visual check only.**
   - Look for a sagging flex duct with water pooled in a low spot, uninsulated duct, disconnected duct, loose exterior termination, or a duct dumping moist air into the attic instead of outdoors.
   - Look for wet insulation, wet framing, roof staining, or dripping pipes above the bathroom.
   - If you see water near wiring, junction boxes, or electrical equipment, back out and keep the breaker off.
7. **Start drying only what you can reach safely.**
   - Once electrical risk is controlled, gently dry the fan cover area and nearby wet drywall surfaces you can reach without disassembly.
   - Keep safe air movement going nearby and aim to dry damp materials within 24–48 hours if you can.
8. **Notify the right person now.**
   - If you rent, contact your landlord or maintenance contact promptly and say: “Bathroom exhaust fan or vent is dripping water; possible duct condensation or leak; breaker turned off for safety.” Share your photos.
   - If you own and the drip is active, worsening, rain-related, or the ceiling is soft or bulging, call a plumber or roofer. If any electrical component got wet, call a licensed electrician before turning the circuit back on.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide today whether to replace the fan, upgrade ducting, add insulation, or open the ceiling.
- You don’t need to identify the exact source before making the bathroom safer and documenting what happened.
- You don’t need to clean for mold while the area is actively wet; stopping the moisture source and drying safely come first.

## Important reassurance
Water dripping from a bathroom fan can be caused by warm, humid bathroom air condensing inside a cold duct and dripping back down. It can also look like a roof or plumbing leak at first, so treating it as an electrical safety issue until confirmed safe is a sensible first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The proper repair depends on whether the source is duct condensation, a disconnected or poorly insulated exhaust duct, a roof or exterior vent leak, or a plumbing leak, and later decisions may need a qualified contractor, roofer, plumber, landlord, or licensed electrician.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional, legal, electrical, building, medical, financial, or insurance advice. If water may have contacted wiring or electrical components, keep the breaker off until a qualified professional confirms it is safe.
