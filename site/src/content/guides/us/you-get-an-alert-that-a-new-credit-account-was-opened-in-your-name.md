# What to do if… you get an alert that a new credit account was opened in your name (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the lender's official fraud department to dispute the account, then immediately place a credit freeze with all three major bureaus and file an official report at IdentityTheft.gov.

## Do not do these things
- Don't use the contact information provided in the alert itself, as it may be a phishing attempt designed to steal your data.
- Don't pay any balance or "verification fee" for an account you did not open.
- Don't ignore physical mail or collection notices regarding the unknown account.
- Don't share your full Social Security number or one-time passcodes with anyone who calls you claiming to be from a "fraud department."
- Don't assume that closing the account with the lender automatically removes the record from your credit report.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the account by contacting the lender through an independently verified phone number.**
   Look up the financial institution’s official website or use the number on the back of a legitimate card from that bank if you already have one. Inform the fraud department that you did not authorize the account, ask them to close it as identity theft, and request a written confirmation of the dispute and a case number.

2. **Place a credit freeze with Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion individually.**
   A credit freeze is the most effective way to prevent new accounts from being opened because it blocks most lenders from viewing your credit file. You must contact each bureau separately to activate this:
   - Equifax: 1-800-685-1111
   - Experian: 1-888-397-3742
   - TransUnion: 1-888-909-8872

3. **Contact Innovis to place a freeze on your fourth credit report.**
   Innovis is a secondary credit bureau often used for pre-approved offers and identity verification, and it must be frozen separately from the "Big Three." Use the official Innovis website or call 1-800-540-2505.

4. **File an official Identity Theft Report at IdentityTheft.gov.**
   This Federal Trade Commission (FTC) site will generate a pre-filled Identity Theft Affidavit and a recovery plan. This document is essential for proving to creditors and bureaus that your information was misused. Save the report ID and a PDF copy immediately.

5. **Pull your free credit reports from AnnualCreditReport.com to check for other damage.**
   Check for any other unfamiliar accounts, "hard" inquiries from lenders you didn't contact, or changes to your personal details like your mailing address. As of 2026, you are entitled to one free report per week from each of the three major bureaus.

6. **Submit formal disputes to the credit bureaus for the fraudulent account and any related inquiries.**
   Use your IdentityTheft.gov report as evidence. Tell the bureaus that the account is the result of identity theft and request that the information be blocked or deleted from your file.

7. **Secure your primary financial communications.**
   Update the passwords and enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on your primary email account and banking apps. Check for unauthorized devices or "trusted" logins in your security settings.

8. **Maintain a chronological log of all communications regarding the theft.**
   Note the date, the name of the representative you spoke to, the department, and the specific outcome or reference number provided during each call.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to pay for a commercial credit monitoring service, as free freezes and federal reports provide the necessary protection right now.
- Filing a local police report, unless a lender or bureau specifically requests one to process your dispute.
- Changing your Social Security number, which is a rare and extreme measure that is not part of immediate stabilization.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a fraud alert is a sign that your monitoring system is working. While the situation requires immediate action, identity theft recovery follows a regulated, step-by-step legal process. By freezing your credit and filing an FTC report, you have already taken the most critical steps to halt further damage.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for a new account alert. It does not cover long-term litigation, complex "coerced debt" situations involving domestic abuse, or the recovery of funds already stolen from existing accounts.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Jurisdictional rules under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) govern your rights; for complex disputes, you may need to consult the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB).
