# What to do if… your main water shutoff valve is stuck and you cannot turn it (USA)

## Short answer
Do not force the stuck main valve. Make the area safer, contain the leak, and shut off water another way: a fixture shutoff, a clearly customer-accessible meter shutoff your utility permits you to use, a well pump switch, or the water utility.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t force a stuck shutoff with extreme leverage. If it will not move with normal hand pressure and a small gentle back-and-forth, stop.
- Don’t try to operate a curb stop or street valve unless your water utility specifically instructs you to.
- Don’t step into water to reach an electrical panel, outlet, cord, appliance, or plugged-in device.
- Don’t use a wet vacuum, pump, or other powered equipment while standing in water or while the cord or outlet is wet.
- Don’t leave a meter box open where someone could trip, step into it, or where traffic could hit you.
- Don’t adjust a private well pressure switch or exposed electrical controls.
- Don’t keep running washing machines, dishwashers, ice makers, water heaters, or other water-using appliances once you suspect an active leak.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safer first.**  
   Keep people, pets, and yourself away from spreading water, wet electrical items, and the water path. If there is sparking, fire, a shock risk, or water reaching an electrical panel and people may be at risk, call 911.

2. **Only shut off electricity if you can reach the breaker safely.**  
   If the breaker panel is dry and you can reach it without stepping in water, shut off power to the affected area or the main. If you would have to stand in water or touch wet electrical equipment, do not try.

3. **Contain and redirect the leak.**  
   Use towels, buckets, pans, plastic sheeting, or a trash can to catch or redirect water. Move phones, laptops, documents, rugs, and other valuables out of the water path.

4. **Try the stuck main valve gently, once.**  
   Use a dry cloth for grip and turn slowly in the usual shutoff direction. If it does not move, try a small gentle back-and-forth. If it still will not move, stop so you do not break the valve or pipe.

5. **Close the nearest fixture shutoff if the leak is from one item.**  
   For a toilet, sink, washing machine, dishwasher, refrigerator line, or water heater supply line, close the small shutoff valve on that item’s supply line. This may stop or greatly reduce the leak even if the main valve is stuck.

6. **Use a customer-accessible meter shutoff only if you can clearly identify it and your utility permits customer use.**  
   Some meters have a customer shutoff valve in or near the meter box. If you are sure which valve it is, access is safe, and it is meant for customer use, turn it slowly to the off position. If it is in the street, in traffic, under deep water, unclear, locked, or hard to move, stop.

7. **Call the water utility if the flow is not controlled quickly.**  
   Say: “Active leak, main shutoff valve stuck, need water shut off at the meter or curb.” Ask whether there is a customer-operable meter valve and whether they can talk you through it or send a crew.

8. **If you rent, contact the landlord, property manager, or emergency maintenance line.**  
   Do this after starting the water shutoff steps, or ask someone else to call while you contain the leak. Tell them the main water shutoff is stuck and water is still flowing.

9. **If the home is on a private well, shut off the well pump if you can do it safely.**  
   Use the clearly labelled pump switch or breaker, often marked “well” or “pump.” Water may still drain from the pressure tank and pipes for a while.

10. **Turn off the water heater only if it is safe and obvious how to do so.**  
   If the home’s water supply is off or very low, and the area around the heater is dry, use the normal control or breaker to turn the water heater off. Do not relight gas, remove covers, or adjust unfamiliar controls.

11. **Check whether the water has stopped or slowed.**  
   Open a cold faucet briefly to confirm the water is off or clearly reduced, then close it again. If water is still flowing strongly, stay focused on containment and utility or plumber help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to replace the main shutoff, change pipe routes, or upgrade plumbing.
- You do not need to start major cleanup, cut drywall, or move heavy furniture until the water source is controlled and the electrical situation is safe.
- You do not need to sort out insurance, landlord responsibility, or repair cost in the first few minutes.
- You do not need to reopen the water yourself before a plumber, utility worker, or responsible maintenance person has checked the stuck valve.

## Important reassurance
A main shutoff can stick because it may not have been used for years. Stopping the water another safe way is a normal first response, not a failure.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. After the immediate flow is controlled, a plumber, water utility, landlord, or well professional may need to inspect, repair, or replace the stuck shutoff before the system is used normally again.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional, utility-specific, legal, electrical, plumbing, insurance, or safety advice. Water meter layouts, customer permissions, rental duties, well systems, and emergency response options vary by location; use your local utility, emergency services, landlord, and qualified professionals where needed.
