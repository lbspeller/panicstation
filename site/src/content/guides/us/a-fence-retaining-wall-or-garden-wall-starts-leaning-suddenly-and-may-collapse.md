# What to do if… a fence, retaining wall, or garden wall starts leaning suddenly and may collapse (USA)

## Short answer
Evacuate the area immediately and establish an exclusion zone at least 1.5 times the height of the wall or fence. Call 911 if the structure threatens a public sidewalk, roadway, or neighboring occupied building.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to prop, brace, or push the structure back into place.
- Do not remove soil from behind a leaning retaining wall, as this can trigger an immediate landslide.
- Do not stand at the top or bottom of the lean to "inspect" the damage.
- Do not allow anyone to use paths, driveways, or doors that fall within the collapse radius.
- Do not touch any wires or cables that have been pulled taut or downed by the movement.
- Do not assume a fence is "just wood" and harmless; the weight of the posts and attached soil can be fatal.

## What to do now
1. **Move all people and pets to a safe distance immediately.** Identify the "fall zone" by measuring the height of the structure and multiplying it by 1.5; ensure no one enters this radius.
2. **Secure the perimeter from a safe distance.** Use caution tape, rope, or heavy furniture to block off access points to the fall zone, but only if you can do so without entering the danger area yourself.
3. **Listen for sensory cues of imminent failure.** Note any sounds of grinding stone, popping wood, or the sight of soil trickling through cracks, as these indicate active movement.
4. **Call 911 if the structure poses an immediate threat to the public.** This includes walls leaning over public sidewalks, streets, or shared alleys, or structures that are actively shedding materials.
5. **Contact your local building department or code enforcement office for non-emergencies.**
   - Report the "unsafe structure" to verify if a city inspector needs to condemn the area or issue an emergency stabilization order.
   - Use the 311 system if available in your municipality to route the report correctly.
6. **Isolate utility risks if the wall or fence is near meters or lines.**
   - If you smell gas or hear hissing, move upwind and call 911 and your gas provider.
   - If electrical wires are under tension or downed, maintain a 30-foot distance and notify the power company.
7. **Notify your neighbors if the structure is on or near a property boundary.** Advise them to clear their side of the fall zone and keep their children or pets indoors until a professional assessment is complete.
8. **Document the lean with photos from a safe, wide-angle perspective.** Capture the relationship of the wall to nearby buildings or paths to help engineers and insurers assess the risk remotely.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine who is legally responsible for the wall or fence right now.
- You do not need to contact 811 for utility marking until the structure is stabilized and you are ready for excavation.
- You do not need to obtain repair quotes or hire a contractor in the first few hours.
- You can wait to review your property survey or insurance policy until the immediate physical hazard is secured.

## Important reassurance
Sudden structural movement often causes a physical "startle" response and a desire to take immediate, hands-on action. It is common to feel an urge to "catch" or support the structure, but the safest and most effective response is to create distance. Professionals have specialized equipment to stabilize these hazards safely.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. Long-term resolution will require a structural engineer or a qualified licensed contractor to determine if the structure can be repaired or must be demolished.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not replace the advice of a structural engineer or emergency services. If you believe there is an immediate risk to life or public safety, contact 911.
