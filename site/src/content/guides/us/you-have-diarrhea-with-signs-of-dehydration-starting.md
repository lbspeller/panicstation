# What to do if… you have diarrhea with signs of dehydration starting (USA)

## Short answer
Start taking 1 teaspoon (5 mL) of an oral rehydration solution every 5 minutes and seek immediate medical evaluation if you cannot keep liquids down, become confused, or faint.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drink large volumes of plain water quickly; it lacks necessary salts and can trigger vomiting.
- Do not use fruit juices (especially apple or pear), sugary sodas, or sports drinks as your primary rehydration source.
- Do not consume alcohol or caffeinated beverages while symptoms persist.
- Do not take anti-diarrheal medication (like loperamide) if you have a high fever or bloody stools.
- Do not wait to seek help if you stop urinating entirely or feel your heart racing.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize oral rehydration solution (ORS) over plain water.** **Begin taking small, frequent doses of a commercial ORS (e.g., Pedialyte, Enfalyte, or store-brand equivalents) immediately.**
   - Start with 1 teaspoon (5 mL) every 5 minutes.
   - If you do not have ORS, use a mix of broth and saltine crackers as a temporary bridge while someone else obtains ORS.
2. **Manage vomiting by slowing the pace rather than stopping fluids.** **If you vomit, wait 10 minutes and then restart ORS intake at a slower rate, such as 1 teaspoon every 10 minutes.**
   - Do not stop drinking entirely; small amounts can still be absorbed even if some is lost.
3. **Monitor for specific emergency red flags that require a 911 call.** **Call 911 or go to the nearest emergency department immediately if you or the affected person experiences any of the following:**
   - Confusion, extreme irritability, or lethargy.
   - Fainting or inability to stand up due to dizziness.
   - Rapid heartbeat or rapid breathing.
   - Sunken eyes or lack of tears when crying (in children).
   - Cold, clammy, or "doughy" skin.
4. **Identify triggers for same-day (non-emergency) medical assessment.** **Contact a healthcare provider today if the situation does not require 911 but includes these signs:**
   - Diarrhea lasting more than 48 hours (adults) or 24 hours (children).
   - Inability to keep any liquids down for more than 4 to 6 hours.
   - Fever over 101°F (adults) or 102°F (children).
   - Stools that are bloody, black, or tarry.
   - Severe abdominal or rectal pain.
5. **Resume eating as soon as the initial fluid deficit is corrected.** **Once you are able to keep fluids down and no longer feel dizzy when standing, begin eating small, bland meals (e.g., rice, toast, bananas, or pasta).**
   - Avoid fatty, spicy, or high-fiber foods until stools return to normal.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific virus or bacteria causing the illness right now.
- You do not need to take vitamins or supplements other than what is in the ORS.
- You do not need to decide on long-term dietary changes while in the acute phase.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant weakness, anxiety, or lightheadedness when your body loses fluids and electrolytes quickly. These sensations are your body's signals that rehydration is required, and most cases of mild-to-moderate dehydration stabilize quickly once frequent, small-volume ORS intake begins.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for acute diarrhea and early dehydration. It does not replace professional medical diagnosis or treatment for chronic conditions or severe emergencies.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not a medical diagnosis. If you are caring for an infant, an elderly adult, or someone with a compromised immune system, have a lower threshold for seeking professional medical advice.
