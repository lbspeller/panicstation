# What to do if… a buy-now-pay-later payment is about to retry and you cannot afford it (USA)

## Short answer
Before the retry happens, either pay the installment if you can do that without risking essentials, or contact the BNPL company now to ask for the retry to be stopped or delayed, a new due date if available, and written confirmation of any fee decision.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore the retry notice; a failed retry may lead to late fees, returned-payment fees, overdraft fees, or more retry notices.
- Don’t use money needed for rent, utilities, food, transportation, or medication just to stop the anxiety.
- Don’t close your bank account or replace your card as your only move; it may not stop every type of payment attempt and can create extra problems.
- Don’t agree to a new date or plan you already know you cannot meet; ask what short pause, due-date change, or hardship option is available.
- Don’t rely only on a phone call; save an in-app message, email, chat transcript, or screenshot.

## What to do now
1. **Find the exact retry details.**
   - Open the BNPL app or email and write down the retry date, expected time if shown, amount, payment method, and any fee they say may be added.
   - Screenshot the payment schedule, failed-payment notice, retry warning, and fee warning.

2. **Check whether paying now is safe and will actually cover the retry.**
   - If you can pay without risking essentials, use the BNPL app or official website before the retry and check that the payment is applied to the due installment.
   - Save the confirmation screen or email.
   - If full payment is not possible, look for options such as change due date, reschedule, hardship, extension, or partial payment. Wording varies by provider.

3. **Message the BNPL company before the retry.**
   - Ask whether they can stop or delay further retries until a new date.
   - Ask whether they can waive or avoid additional fees because you contacted them before the retry.
   - Use a short message: “I cannot make today’s installment without risking essentials. Please tell me whether you can stop or delay retries, move the due date to [date], and confirm in writing what fees will or will not be charged.”

4. **If the retry could overdraw your bank account, contact your bank or credit union too.**
   - If the payment is an ACH or other preauthorized electronic transfer from your bank account, ask your bank or credit union how to place a stop-payment order. For a preauthorized electronic fund transfer, the stop-payment request generally must reach the bank at least 3 business days before the scheduled debit.
   - Also send the BNPL company a written message saying you are revoking authorization for future automatic debits from that bank account.
   - If there are fewer than 3 business days left, still call the bank or credit union now. They may not be able to stop an imminent debit, but they can tell you what is possible and how to prevent later attempts.
   - If the retry is to a debit card or credit card, contact the card issuer and ask what options they have for blocking or disputing future recurring charges from that merchant. Ask for confirmation of what they can and cannot do.
   - Stopping an automatic payment does not erase the amount you owe; it is a way to prevent an automatic withdrawal while you sort out timing, fees, or a dispute.

5. **If a return, cancellation, or order dispute is involved, document it today.**
   - Contact the merchant through its official return, cancellation, or support route.
   - Save proof such as a return receipt, tracking number, cancellation confirmation, merchant email, or chat transcript.
   - Send the same proof to the BNPL company and ask whether they can pause or review installments and fees while the return, cancellation, or dispute is processed.

6. **Make a short record you can use if a fee appears anyway.**
   - Save screenshots of the retry warning, your messages, bank requests, confirmation numbers, and any promise about fees or due dates.
   - If a fee is added after you were told it would not be, use that record to ask the BNPL company to reverse it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to stop using BNPL permanently.
- You do not need to close bank accounts or cards today unless your bank or card issuer tells you that is necessary.
- You do not need to write a long explanation; a short written request with the amount, date, and what you need is enough.
- You do not need to fight every past fee right now; first try to stop the next automatic attempt or reduce further damage.

## Important reassurance
A retry warning can make the situation feel automatic and out of your control. You still have practical moves: check the exact payment route, ask the BNPL company for a written pause or due-date change, and ask your bank or card issuer what can be blocked before the next attempt.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce avoidable fees and regain control of timing. Later decisions about affordability, complaints, credit reporting, collections, or repeated BNPL use may need help from a qualified financial counselor, legal aid office, bank, credit union, or consumer-protection agency.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. BNPL terms, state rules, bank procedures, provider policies, and payment protections vary. Ask the BNPL company and your bank, credit union, or card issuer to confirm what applies to your account before the retry happens.
