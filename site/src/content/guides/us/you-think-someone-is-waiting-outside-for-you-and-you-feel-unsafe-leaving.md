# What to do if… you think someone is waiting outside for you and you feel unsafe leaving (USA)

## Short answer
Do not leave alone. Stay inside a staffed, visible area and ask security, management, or front-desk staff for help leaving safely and for help calling 911 if you feel in danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go outside alone to check whether you are right.
- Do not confront the person or move closer for a photo or video.
- Do not go to a parking lot, garage, alley, stairwell, or isolated pickup point alone.
- Do not post your location, exit, ride, or route publicly.
- Do not drive straight home if you think you are being followed.

## What to do now
1. Move to the safest indoor spot available. Choose a staffed, visible area such as a front desk, lobby desk, bar, ticket counter, security station, or reception area. If the public area feels exposed, ask to wait in an office or staff-only area.

2. Tell staff or security plainly what you need. Say: “I think someone is waiting outside for me. I do not feel safe leaving alone.” Ask them to help you with:
   - a security or staff escort to your ride or car,
   - watching the exit while you leave,
   - using a different exit if one is available,
   - calling 911 if you feel threatened, trapped, or at immediate risk.

3. If using your phone could expose your plan or location, switch devices. Ask staff to call 911 or a trusted person for you from a venue phone. Keep messages brief and practical, such as where you are and what help you need.

4. Call 911 if you feel threatened, trapped, blocked, followed, or at immediate risk. Tell the dispatcher your exact location, that you are inside a venue or building, what the person is doing, and that you are afraid to leave.

5. Make leaving one supported move. Leave only when your ride, escort, or group is ready and you can go directly to the vehicle or safe next location. If using a taxi or rideshare, wait inside until it arrives, confirm the vehicle details before stepping out, and get in with the escort nearby.

6. If you think you are being followed after you leave, do not go home. Go to a well-lit, staffed public place such as a police station, fire station, open hotel lobby, emergency room entrance, or busy store, and call 911 if you feel at risk. If you are driving, keep moving toward a safe public location instead of pulling over somewhere isolated.

7. Record key details only from safety. From inside or from a safe distance, note the person’s appearance, clothing, position, time, direction of travel, and any vehicle make, model, color, or license plate you can safely see. Ask staff to note the time and, if possible, preserve relevant security camera footage.

8. Once you are in a safer place, message one trusted person. Share where you are, where you are going next, and what you want them to do, such as stay on the phone until you are indoors or meet you at a staffed location.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether this is definitely stalking, harassment, a misunderstanding, or something else.
- Deciding whether to make a police report tonight, unless you need police help now.
- Writing a full account; a few accurate notes are enough for the moment.
- Longer-term decisions about protective orders, workplace plans, school plans, housing, transport, or changing routines.

## Important reassurance
You are allowed to act on a safety concern before you have proof. Staying inside, asking staff for help, leaving with support, or calling 911 when you feel in danger are reasonable steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes to hours. If this is part of an ongoing pattern, a victim advocate, local victim service provider, attorney, campus safety office, workplace security team, or other specialist may be able to help you think through later options.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not legal, medical, mental health, security, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
