# What to do if… your windshield wiper blades are torn or detached before driving in bad weather (USA)

## Short answer
Do not start a trip into rain, snow, spray, or poor visibility with a torn, missing, or loose front wiper blade. Restore safe wiping first, delay the drive, or use another way to travel.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t start driving just because it is not raining at this exact moment if bad weather is expected.
- Don’t run the wipers if a blade is missing, loose, or hanging off; the metal arm can scratch the windshield and still will not clear water safely.
- Don’t tape, tie, wedge, or improvise a wiper blade for road use.
- Don’t rely on washer fluid, defrosters, or water-repellent products to make up for a damaged blade.
- Don’t begin a highway or long-distance trip planning to fix the wiper somewhere on the way.
- Don’t keep driving if rain starts and the windshield is not clearing.

## What to do now
1. **Park and check the failed wiper.**  
   With the vehicle safely stopped, look at both front wipers. Check whether the rubber is torn, the whole blade has detached, the blade is unclipped from the arm, or the arm is resting directly on the glass.

2. **Protect the windshield if the blade is missing.**  
   If the metal or plastic arm could touch the glass, lift it gently away if safe to do so, or leave the wipers switched off. Do not test the wipers against bare glass.

3. **Reattach only if it locks securely.**  
   If the blade has only unclipped and you can reattach it so it clicks or locks firmly into place, do that while parked. If it does not lock securely, treat it as not roadworthy for bad weather.

4. **Replace the damaged blade before driving into weather.**  
   Use the correct length and connector type for your vehicle. Replace any front blade that is torn, missing, loose, or unable to clear the driver’s view.

5. **Test visibility before leaving.**  
   After the blade is secure or replaced, use a small amount of washer fluid and run the wipers briefly. The windshield should clear without skipping, smearing across the driver’s view, or scraping.

6. **Switch to a fix-first travel plan if you cannot repair it now.**  
   Ask someone to bring the correct blade, use a nearby auto parts store or repair shop, call roadside assistance, use a ride service, or delay the journey.

7. **Move the car only if the conditions are dry and local.**  
   If you need to reposition the car to reach help, keep it short and local. If rain, snow, spray, or low visibility begins, pull off at a safe place and stop rather than continuing with ineffective wipers.

8. **Use headlights when visibility is reduced.**  
   Many states require or advise headlights when wipers are in use or when weather reduces visibility. Use low-beam headlights in rain, snow, fog, or spray, and check your own state’s rule when you are not in the middle of the immediate problem.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose the perfect brand right now; the urgent need is a secure blade that clears the windshield.
- You do not need to diagnose the wiper motor unless the blades are intact and the wipers still do not move.
- You do not need to solve the whole trip plan immediately; first avoid driving into poor visibility.
- You do not need to check every state rule before acting safely; fix the visibility problem first.

## Important reassurance
A torn or detached wiper is a common, fixable problem. Delaying the drive to restore clear visibility is a reasonable safety decision, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate visibility problem. If blades keep detaching, the wiper arm is bent, the wipers do not move normally, or visibility still does not clear after replacement, later decisions may need help from a mechanic, roadside assistance provider, or other appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information for immediate safety and is not legal, mechanical, financial, or professional advice. Traffic and equipment requirements vary by state, so use official state guidance for legal details when you are no longer dealing with the immediate situation.
