# What to do if… your bank charges an unexpected fee or penalty that puts your account in the red (USA)

## Short answer
Stop the fee cascade first: pause non-essential transactions, add safe funds if you can, and contact the bank right away to ask for a review, a fee reversal, and help preventing more overdraft or NSF fees.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep making small purchases while the balance is negative or unclear; pending items can settle later and create more fees.
- Don’t take a high-cost loan in a panic just to cover today’s gap.
- Don’t close the account in panic while transactions or disputes are pending without asking the bank how pending items, deposits, and possible refunds will be handled.
- Don’t ignore rent, utilities, insurance, loan payments, or other essential bills that may retry and trigger more fees.
- Don’t assume the bank will fix it without you flagging it; ask directly and keep a short record.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the balance from getting worse**
   - Pause non-essential card use, bill payments, transfers, and subscriptions from this account until you can see what is pending.
   - If you have safe funds available, transfer enough to bring the balance above $0 and cover any payments that are about to post.
   - Do not use money needed for immediate essentials unless you have checked what will hit next.

2. **Find the exact fee and what triggered it**
   - In your app or online banking, find the fee name, amount, posting date, and nearby transactions.
   - Look for what happened right before it: a debit card purchase, ATM withdrawal, ACH payment, check, monthly account fee, subscription, or returned item.
   - Take screenshots of the fee, the balance around that time, and any pending items.

3. **Ask the bank for a reversal and a review**
   - Use secure message or chat if available, or call and write down the details.
   - Say: “This fee was unexpected and pushed my account negative. Please review it, explain what triggered it, and consider reversing it. Please also tell me what you can do to prevent additional overdraft or NSF fees while this is being reviewed.”
   - Ask for the fee schedule or account disclosure section the bank is relying on.

4. **If the fee involved an ATM withdrawal or one-time debit card purchase, check your opt-in status**
   - For ATM and one-time debit card overdraft fees, banks generally need your affirmative opt-in before charging the fee.
   - Say: “I do not believe I opted in for ATM or one-time debit card overdraft coverage. Please review this fee and provide the record of any opt-in.”
   - Ask how to revoke that opt-in if you do not want that coverage going forward. Be aware that opting out can mean some transactions are declined, and checks, ACH payments, and recurring debit payments can be treated differently.

5. **If your balance looked positive when the transaction was authorized, ask about timing**
   - Say: “My balance appeared sufficient when the transaction was authorized. Please review whether this was an authorize-positive, settle-negative situation or another unanticipated overdraft.”
   - Ask the bank to explain the posting order, holds, pending items, and why the fee was charged.
   - Ask whether the bank will waive the fee because the overdraft was not reasonably expected.

6. **Protect essential payments from repeat fees**
   - Check what is scheduled to hit in the next 48 hours, especially rent, utilities, insurance, car payments, loan payments, subscriptions, and automatic transfers.
   - If a bill may bounce or retry, contact the biller and ask whether the date can be moved, the payment can be paused, or retries can be stopped.
   - Ask the bank whether any pending fees or returned-item fees are still expected.

7. **Create a short written record**
   - Write down the fee amount, posting date, transaction involved, balance shown before and after, and what you asked the bank to do.
   - If you speak by phone, ask for a case number or confirmation note.
   - Save screenshots, secure messages, emails, and any bank response.

8. **Escalate if the bank will not explain or review it**
   - Ask for a supervisor or the bank’s formal complaint process.
   - If the issue still is not resolved, submit a CFPB complaint with the fee amount, dates, short timeline, screenshots, and the bank’s response.
   - Keep the complaint short and factual; include the most important dates, amounts, and communications.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to switch banks or close the account.
- You do not need to solve your whole budget right now; focus first on stopping new fees and protecting essentials.
- You do not need to write a long explanation; a short timeline, screenshots, and the fee details are enough for the first contact.

## Important reassurance
A sudden overdraft or penalty fee can feel frightening because one charge can trigger several more. The most useful first step is not to solve everything at once; it is to stop new activity, ask the bank to review the charge, and protect the next essential payments.

## Scope note
These are first steps for immediate stabilization, documentation, and escalation. Later decisions, such as changing overdraft settings, moving automatic payments, or choosing a different account, can wait until the account is steadier and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Rules and fee practices vary by institution and transaction type. If you cannot cover essentials or fees are repeating, ask your bank about hardship options and consider contacting a reputable nonprofit financial counselor.
