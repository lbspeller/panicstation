# What to do if… you are told you are the subject of an investigation and you fear you could say the wrong thing (USA)

## Short answer
Verbally invoke your rights by saying: **“I am invoking my Fifth Amendment right to remain silent and I want a lawyer.”** Do not rely on remaining mute; you must explicitly state you are exercising your constitutional protections to ensure your silence cannot be used against you.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume that staying quiet is enough; in the USA, silence during a voluntary or pre-arrest encounter can sometimes be used as evidence of guilt unless you verbally invoke the Fifth Amendment.
- Don’t believe that "off the record" or "unofficial" chats exist; everything communicated to an officer or agent is potentially admissible.
- Don’t wait for Miranda warnings to be read; these are generally only required during custodial interrogation (arrest plus questioning), but your rights exist regardless of whether they have been read to you.
- Don’t attempt to explain your innocence or "clear the air" without an attorney present.
- Don’t consent to a search of your person, vehicle, phone, or residence even if you believe you have nothing to hide.
- Don’t delete any electronic data, as this can lead to separate "obstruction of justice" or "tampering with evidence" charges.

## What to do now
1. **Verbally invoke your right to silence and counsel immediately.** **You must state clearly and aloud: “I am invoking my Fifth Amendment right to remain silent and I want an attorney.”** Once you have said this, do not provide any further information or engage in "small talk," as any subsequent statement may be interpreted as a waiver of the rights you just invoked.
2. **Determine your current legal status by asking a specific question.** **Ask the officer or agent: “Am I free to leave?”** 
   - If the answer is "Yes," leave the area or end the call immediately without further explanation.
   - If the answer is "No," you are being detained or arrested; continue to remain silent.
3. **Provide only the identification information required by law in your specific state.** **If you are lawfully detained, you may be required to provide your name, date of birth, and address in "Stop and Identify" states.** You are not required to answer questions about where you were going, what you were doing, or who you were with.
4. **Identify the agency and personnel involved without discussing the facts of the case.** **Ask for the officer’s name, badge number, and agency, and request a case number if one is available.** Write this information down as soon as you are in a safe pause to provide to your attorney.
5. **Secure legal representation as your primary objective.** **Contact a criminal defense attorney or your local bar association’s referral service immediately.** 
   - If you are arrested and cannot afford an attorney, tell the judge at your first appearance (arraignment) that you are indigent and request a public defender.
6. **Protect your digital and physical evidence from informal disclosure.** **Do not discuss the investigation on social media, via text message, or with anyone other than your legal counsel.** These communications are not privileged and can be subpoenaed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a statement or an "alibi" right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to cooperate or testify at this stage.
- You do not need to search for evidence or contact witnesses until instructed by your lawyer.
- You do not need to apologize or express regret to the investigating officers.

## Important reassurance
Feeling a sense of panic or a sudden urge to "explain everything" is a natural physiological response to a high-stress confrontation. Choosing to remain silent and requesting a lawyer is a standard legal procedure used by people in all circumstances; it is not a confession and does not prevent you from telling your side later with the benefit of professional advice.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate moments following a notice of investigation. It does not cover trial strategy, grand jury proceedings, or specialized federal "target letter" responses, which require specific legal counsel.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Jurisdictional rules on "Stop and Identify" and the scope of the Fifth Amendment can vary by state and federal circuit. If you are questioned, always default to explicitly invoking your rights and seeking an attorney.
