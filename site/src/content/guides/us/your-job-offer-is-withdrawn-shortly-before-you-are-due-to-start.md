# What to do if… your job offer is withdrawn shortly before you are due to start (USA)

## Short answer
Obtain written confirmation of the withdrawal and the specific reason immediately. Then, check for Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) violations if a background check was involved and file for unemployment to preserve your income eligibility.

## Do not do these things
- Do not rely on a phone call as the final word; ask for the decision in writing before ending the conversation.
- Do not delete any hiring-related communication, including texts, emails, or the original offer letter.
- Do not sign any "release of claims" or "severance agreement" documents on the spot.
- Do not ignore the rescission if it happened after you disclosed a disability, pregnancy, or medical need.
- Do not wait to file for unemployment; delays can affect your eligibility or payment timeline.
- Do not vent about the employer on social media while your legal or unemployment options are still being determined.

## What to do now
1. **Request the rescission in writing immediately.** If the news was delivered by phone, send a follow-up email to the recruiter or hiring manager.
   - ask for the specific reason for the withdrawal
   - ask for the effective date of the decision
   - ask if the decision is final or if contingencies are still being reviewed
2. **Determine if a background or credit check triggered the decision.** Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), if an employer uses a "consumer report" to rescind an offer, they must follow a mandatory two-step process.
   - check if you received a "Pre-Adverse Action Notice" before the final withdrawal
   - ensure the notice included a copy of the report and the "Summary of Your Rights"
   - if you did not receive these before the offer was pulled, document the lack of notice
3. **Contact the background check company if the report contains errors.** You have a right to dispute inaccurate information directly with the agency that produced the report.
4. **Notify your previous employer immediately if you have already resigned.** If you are still within your notice period or recently left, ask if your resignation can be retracted or your end date extended.
   - keep the request professional and brief
   - do not provide extensive details about the new employer's failure unless necessary
5. **Secure copies of all "reliance" evidence.** Save documents that prove you took irreversible actions based on the offer.
   - offer letters and signed acceptance forms
   - screenshots of relocation commitments or travel bookings
   - copies of your resignation letter to your previous employer
   - lease agreements or moving company receipts
6. **Identify your specific EEOC filing deadline.** In the private sector, you generally have **180 days** (or **300 days** if a state agency is involved) to file a charge if you suspect discrimination.
   - **Federal applicants** must contact an EEO counselor within **45 days** of the withdrawal
7. **File for unemployment insurance (UI) with your state agency.** Even if you never started the new job, many states allow for "quit-to-accept" exceptions where you remain eligible if you left your old job for a firm offer that was later rescinded through no fault of your own.
8. **Pause all job-related spending.** Immediately cancel any pending relocations, housing deposits, or equipment purchases while you are within refund windows.

## What can wait
- You do not need to hire an attorney today; documenting the timeline is the priority.
- You do not need to explain the situation to your professional network until you have a stable "next step" message.
- You do not need to decide whether to file a lawsuit (promissory estoppel) immediately.
- You do not need to respond to a request for a "feedback interview" about the hiring process.

## Important reassurance
Losing a job offer after you have already committed to the change is a high-stress event that often triggers a panic response. Most people in this situation focus on "why" it happened, but your most effective protection is focused on "what" was documented. Taking fast action on unemployment and background check rights preserves your options while you reset.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and legal preservation only. It does not provide legal strategy for "promissory estoppel" lawsuits or long-term career coaching.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Employment laws and unemployment eligibility criteria vary significantly by state. If the rescission involved a background check error or suspected discrimination, consider consulting a qualified employment attorney or a legal aid organization in your jurisdiction.
