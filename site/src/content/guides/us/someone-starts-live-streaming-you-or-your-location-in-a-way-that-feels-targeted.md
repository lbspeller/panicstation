# What to do if… someone starts live-streaming you or your location in a way that feels targeted (USA)

## Short answer
Move immediately to a staffed, public location and disable all location-sharing features on your mobile device. Report the stream to the hosting platform and contact local law enforcement if you believe your safety is at risk.

## Do not do these things
- Don't physically confront the individual or attempt to seize their device.
- Don't lead the streamer toward your home, vehicle, or a secluded area.
- Don't engage in an argument or "play to the camera," as this often incentivizes the streamer and provides more content.
- Don't post your own real-time location or defensive videos while the situation is active.
- Don't assume the streamer will stop if you ask; prioritize creating distance instead.

## What to do now
1. **Move immediately to a staffed, public area with visible security or surveillance.** Go to a nearby open business, hotel lobby, grocery store, or transit station and remain in the line of sight of staff or other patrons.
2. **Contact 911 if you believe you are being followed or if the streamer is making verbal threats.** If you do not perceive an immediate physical threat but feel targeted, call your local police department’s non-emergency line to report "harassment via live broadcast" and "unauthorized disclosure of location."
3. **Disable all location-sharing settings and background GPS tracking on your smartphone.** Turn off "Find My" features, family tracking apps, and specific location permissions for social media apps to prevent the stream from being used to coordinate further targeting.
4. **Identify the platform and user handle if it is safe to do so.** Look for identifiable markers such as the app interface (TikTok, Instagram, YouTube) and the account name visible on the screen, but do not move closer to the individual to obtain this information.
5. **Report the live broadcast directly within the hosting application.** Use the "Report" function and select categories such as "Harassment," "Doxxing," or "Safety" to trigger the platform's 2026 priority review protocols.
6. **File a complaint with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) if the targeting continues or involves the release of private data.** Use the official ic3.gov portal to document the incident as "cyber-enabled harassment" or "doxxing," and include any local police report numbers in your submission.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the legality of filming in public spaces before taking safety actions.
- You do not need to review the footage or "see what they said" until you are in a secure, private location.
- You do not need to respond to comments or viewers of the stream.
- You do not need to decide on long-term privacy changes or legal action in the next few minutes.

## Important reassurance
Feeling a sense of panic or "freezing" when being broadcast to an unknown audience is a standard physiological response to sudden exposure. Your priority is your physical safety and the isolation of your location; the digital consequences can be addressed once you are secure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate personal safety and digital containment. Ongoing harassment or complex doxxing may require consultation with legal professionals or specialized digital safety advocates.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional safety advice. Laws regarding public recording and digital harassment vary by state; always prioritize immediate physical safety and follow the directions of local law enforcement.
