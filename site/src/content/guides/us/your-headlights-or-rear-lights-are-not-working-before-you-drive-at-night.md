# What to do if… your headlights or rear lights are not working before you drive at night (USA)

## Short answer
Do not start a night drive on public roads unless your headlights and rear lights are clearly working. Park safely, make the stopped vehicle visible if needed, and use another way to get where you need to go.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume daytime running lights mean your rear lights are on; on many vehicles, they do not.
- Do not try to “just get home” on dark roads with failed headlights or rear lights.
- Do not rely on hazard flashers as a substitute for working headlights and rear lights.
- Do not pull panels, probe wiring, or do electrical work in the dark at the roadside.
- Do not ask someone to stand behind the vehicle in traffic to check the lights.
- Do not keep replacing a fuse if the replacement blows again.

## What to do now
1. **Make a safe pause first.**  
   Park somewhere safe and well-lit if you can. Put the vehicle in park, set the parking brake, and turn on hazard flashers while stopped if other drivers may have trouble seeing you.

2. **Turn the lights fully on, not Auto.**  
   Move the light switch from **AUTO** to **ON**. Check low beams, high beams, rear running lights, brake lights, turn signals, hazard flashers, and the rear license plate light.

3. **Confirm the rear lights, because this is easy to miss.**  
   Walk behind the parked vehicle only if it is safe to do so. If you are alone, use your phone camera, a shop window, a wall, or another reflective surface to check whether the rear lights are actually lit.

4. **Treat a wider failure as a stop sign.**  
   One failed headlight or one failed rear light can be a bulb problem. Both headlights out, both rear lights out, or lights that flicker or switch off again may point to a fuse, relay, switch, wiring, or control problem; treat the vehicle as not safe for a night drive unless the lights are repaired and confirmed.

5. **Only try the simplest safe fix.**  
   In a safe, well-lit place, check the owner’s manual for the correct lighting fuse or bulb. If you replace a fuse, use the same rating only. If it blows again, or if you are not sure, stop and arrange help.

6. **Use another way to travel.**  
   Use a taxi, rideshare, public transit, or a lift from someone else. If the car only needs moving out of the way, keep it to private property such as a driveway or parking lot, or wait until daylight if that is safer.

7. **Call for help if you are stuck away from home.**  
   Contact roadside assistance, your insurer’s assistance line, an auto club, or a nearby repair shop. If the place you stopped feels unsafe, move to the safest nearby lit public place if you can do that without driving unlit on public roads.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the relay, wiring, switch, or control module tonight.
- You do not need to choose upgraded bulbs or plan a larger repair right now.
- You do not need to decide how far you can risk driving; if the lights are not clearly working, the immediate job is to avoid a night drive.

## Important reassurance
Catching this before you drive is inconvenient, but it is much safer than discovering it on a dark road. Taking a different ride or delaying the trip is a practical way to stop the problem from becoming more dangerous.

## Scope note
These are first steps to keep the moment safe and prevent a high-risk night drive. Later repair decisions may need a qualified mechanic, roadside assistance provider, or vehicle-specific manual.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, mechanical, insurance, or professional advice. Vehicle lighting rules and enforcement vary by state, but driving at night without clearly working headlights and rear lights is unsafe.
