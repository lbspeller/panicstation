# What to do if… a loved one is missing and you have no contact or confirmation (USA)

## Short answer
Call 911 now if you think there is immediate danger, violence, abduction risk, or a medical emergency. Otherwise, call your local police or sheriff non-emergency number and ask to make a missing-person report; you generally do not need to wait 24 hours.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait because you think there is a required waiting period.
- Do not assume an adult cannot be reported missing; explain why this is unusual or unsafe.
- Do not publicly post that they are dead, harmed, or abducted without confirmation.
- Do not publish sensitive identifiers such as date of birth, medical details, home address, routine locations, or account information.
- Do not pursue risky leads alone or confront someone you suspect.
- Do not delete texts, call logs, emails, voicemails, travel records, or location-sharing history.
- Do not send money to anyone demanding payment or claiming they can locate your loved one.

## What to do now
1. **Contact law enforcement now.**
   Call 911 for immediate danger or urgent risk. If there is no immediate emergency, call the local police or sheriff non-emergency number where the person was last known to be, and ask to file a missing-person report. Ask for the case or incident number and the name or unit handling the report.

2. **Say the risk factors clearly.**
   Tell law enforcement if the missing person is a child, an older adult, has dementia or cognitive impairment, is in severe distress, has made threats of self-harm, is affected by domestic violence or coercion, needs medication or medical equipment, has no phone or transport, is exposed to severe weather, or being unreachable is very out of character.

3. **Give a police-ready information packet.**
   Share their full name, aliases, age, recent photos, height, build, distinguishing marks, clothing last seen in, last known time and place, phone numbers, vehicle details and plate, workplace or school, known companions, likely routes, places they may go, and who last spoke with them.

4. **Do only the fastest safe checks that could confirm a last sighting.**
   Contact places they were expected to be today, such as work, school, an appointment, a care setting, or a travel pickup point. Write down who you spoke to, the time of the call, and the last confirmed sighting or contact.

5. **Ask law enforcement about institutional checks.**
   Hospitals, jails, detention facilities, and shelters may not confirm information to family because of privacy rules or local policy. Tell law enforcement exactly which places you are worried about so they can advise or check through the proper route.

6. **Preserve information without changing anything.**
   Screenshot or save recent messages, missed calls, voicemails, “last seen” information, travel confirmations, ride-share or transit details you already have, and location-sharing history you can already access. Do not try to break into accounts or guess passwords.

7. **If the missing person is a child, ask about rapid child-missing steps and call NCMEC.**
   Ask law enforcement to confirm NCIC Missing Person File entry and explain what local alerts or broadcasts may be appropriate, such as a BOLO. After reporting to law enforcement, call the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children at 1-800-THE-LOST, which is 1-800-843-5678.

8. **Start one simple timeline and one contact log.**
   Write down the last confirmed contact, the first missed contact, every check you make, every person you speak with, and any possible sighting. Keep everything in one note so you can update law enforcement without repeating yourself under stress.

9. **If the case continues, ask about NamUs.**
   Ask the investigating agency whether a NamUs case should be created or updated. NamUs is a national missing and unidentified persons system, and law enforcement may use it as part of a continuing case.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to contact the media, make flyers, or post widely online.
- You do not need to notify everyone at once; start with law enforcement and the quickest safe checks for today.
- You do not need to clear their home, change locks, cancel accounts, or access private accounts unless law enforcement advises it or there is a separate urgent safety need.
- You do not need to decide what has happened without confirmation.

## Important reassurance
Fear can spike when someone is suddenly unreachable, especially when the silence is out of character. Reporting, preserving information, and keeping one clear timeline are useful first steps even before there is any confirmed explanation.

## Scope note
This is first-steps guidance for the first hours only. Investigation steps, public alerts, media contact, legal questions, and longer-term decisions may depend on local procedures and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, investigative, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you believe anyone is in immediate danger, call 911.
