# What to do if… you are pressured to sign a workplace statement you did not write (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before signing. Ask for a copy, ask what your signature is said to mean, and request time to review the full document before deciding. Ask whether you can provide your own short written response after review.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a blank or partly completed document.
- Do not sign before checking whether pages, attachments, or referenced materials are missing.
- Do not assume your signature only acknowledges receipt unless the document or a written message clearly says so.
- Do not argue, speculate, or improvise a long explanation while you feel pressured.
- Do not delete, edit, or clean up messages or files related to the issue.
- Do not send confidential workplace information to a personal email account or personal device just to create a timestamp.
- Do not quit on the spot while you are feeling pressured.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the decision.**  
   Say: “I need time to read the full document before deciding whether to sign. Please give me a copy and tell me the review deadline.”

2. **Check what you were given.**  
   Look for the document title, date, page count, blank spaces, missing pages, and any attachments or policies it mentions. Ask for the complete set. If you are not given a copy, make a dated note that you asked for one.

3. **Ask what the signature is said to mean.**  
   Ask: “Does signing mean I agree with this statement, or only that I received it?” Ask for the answer in writing or ask whether the document can state the meaning clearly.

4. **If you are union-represented, request representation when the meeting may be investigatory.**  
   If management is questioning you about your conduct or performance and you reasonably believe the interview could lead to discipline, say: “I am requesting my union representative before I answer questions.” Make the request yourself. Under current federal Board guidance, an employer may grant the request and delay the interview, end the interview, or offer you the choice of continuing without representation or ending the interview. Federal coverage has exceptions, and public-sector rules may differ.

5. **Ask about the process that applies to you.**  
   If the federal union-representation rule does not apply, ask whether a workplace policy, union agreement, or other applicable rule allows a representative, a support person, or a written response after review.

6. **Prepare your own brief factual response if the process allows it.**  
   Write only what you personally observed, relevant dates or times you can confirm, and anything you do not know firsthand. Ask whether your response will be accepted and attached to the workplace record.

7. **Make a dated note of the meeting.**  
   Record who attended, what document you were shown, what you were told the signature means, what deadline was given, and whether you asked for a copy. Keep the note without moving confidential workplace material outside approved systems.

8. **Read the exact signature wording before deciding.**  
   If the meaning is unclear, ask for clarification and review time. Do not let pressure turn an unclear signature into an immediate decision.

## What can wait
- You do not need to answer every allegation during the same meeting.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to file a complaint, escalate the issue, or contact an attorney.
- You do not need to negotiate discipline, resignation, or any other outcome while you are still reviewing the document.

## Important reassurance
Being handed a statement and asked to sign quickly can feel unsettling. Slowing the process down, asking what the signature means, and keeping a calm dated note are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps for reviewing the document, clarifying the requested signature, and preserving an accurate note of what happened. Later decisions may need help from a union representative, an employment lawyer, or another qualified specialist. Workplace rules vary by employer policy, union agreement, state law, public-sector rules, and federal coverage.
