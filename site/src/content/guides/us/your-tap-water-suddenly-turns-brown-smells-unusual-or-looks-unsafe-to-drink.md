# What to do if… your tap water suddenly turns brown, smells unusual, or looks unsafe to drink (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using the water for drinking, brushing teeth, ice, baby formula, cooking, rinsing food, or handwashing before food preparation or baby care. Switch to bottled water or another known-safe source, then contact your water utility, or your local health department if you use a private well, to ask whether there is an advisory or incident affecting your address.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t “test sip” it or use it for coffee, cooking, ice, baby formula, or brushing teeth.
- Don’t assume boiling makes it safe; boiling can kill germs, but it does not remove many chemicals.
- Don’t try to treat water that smells like fuel, solvents, or sewage unless your health department or utility gives you specific instructions.
- Don’t run hot water through the house to “flush it out” unless you are told to do that by your utility, landlord, building manager, plumber, or health department.
- Don’t run water-using appliances, such as a dishwasher, washing machine, or ice maker, until you have checked what is safe for your situation.
- Don’t ignore a strong fuel, solvent, chemical, or sewage smell; treat it as a stop-and-check situation.

## What to do now
1. **Switch to a safer water source immediately.** Use bottled water or another known-safe source for drinking, cooking, baby formula, brushing teeth, rinsing produce, making ice, and washing hands before food preparation or baby care. If you have an ice maker, turn it off and do not use existing ice until you know the water is safe.
2. **Do a quick cold-water check.** Run only a cold tap briefly and fill a clear glass. Note the color, smell, sediment, black specks, cloudiness, or unusual taste without drinking it.
3. **Check whether hot water is different.** Fill a separate clear glass from a hot tap, again without drinking it. If only the hot water is affected, avoid using hot water and report that detail; it helps the utility, landlord, building manager, or plumber decide whether the issue may be inside your home or building.
4. **Call the right place for your water source.**
   - **Public water system:** Call your water utility’s customer or emergency line. Give your address, when it started, what the water looks or smells like, and whether neighbors are affected. Ask whether there is a boil water advisory, do-not-drink notice, do-not-use notice, water main break, hydrant flushing, or other incident for your area. Ask what the water is safe to use for right now, including bathing, toilets, dishwashing, laundry, and pets. Ask where to find the latest Consumer Confidence Report for your water system.
   - **Private well:** Stop drinking it and contact your local health department or environmental health office. Ask what immediate precautions to take and what testing to order, usually through a state-certified drinking-water laboratory.
   - **Apartment, condo, dorm, or shared building:** Contact the landlord, building manager, maintenance office, or housing office as well as the utility if the problem may affect more than one unit. Ask whether plumbing work, a water heater issue, or building flushing is happening.
5. **If you suspect fuel or toxic chemicals, do not try to make the water safe at home.** Use bottled water or another known-safe source and contact your health department. If you are on a public water system, contact your utility too.
6. **If officials tell you to boil water, follow that instruction exactly.** Use clear water if possible. Bring it to a rolling boil for 1 minute, or 3 minutes at elevations above 6,500 feet. Follow local instructions because some situations call for bottled water or a do-not-drink notice instead of boiling.
7. **Save simple notes for the people who can investigate.** Take a photo or short video of the water in a clear glass. Write down when it started, which taps are affected, whether cold and hot water differ, and any recent nearby construction, hydrant flushing, water main work, flooding, or plumbing repairs.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether the cause is your plumbing, the building, a water main, hydrant flushing, a well problem, or something else.
- You do not need to buy filters, home testing kits, or treatment equipment before you have contacted the utility, health department, or building manager.
- You do not need to drain your water heater, flush every tap, or replace appliances unless a qualified professional or local authority tells you to.
- You do not need to make a long-term repair plan while you are still checking whether the water is safe for basic use.

## Important reassurance
Brown, cloudy, rusty, or smelly water can happen after maintenance, flow changes, plumbing work, or sediment being disturbed. You do not have to guess: pause drinking and food use, switch to a safer source, and get local guidance for your exact address.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate risk and connect you to the right local authority or building contact. Later decisions about testing, plumbing repairs, filters, well treatment, or claims may need help from a water utility, local health department, certified laboratory, plumber, landlord, insurer, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, environmental, plumbing, emergency services, or other professional advice. If someone feels sick after using the water, seek medical advice and mention possible contaminated water exposure. If you suspect serious chemical contamination, stop using the water for drinking or food and follow urgent guidance from your utility or health department.
