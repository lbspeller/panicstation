# What to do if… you find a fake sexual services advert using your photos or contact details (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a safer, more private pause and silence unknown callers or messages. Use the host site’s report or help route when you are ready.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay, send gift cards, or send more photos or personal information if anyone makes demands.
- Do not argue with the poster or try to investigate who created the post.
- Do not reply to every message. You can silence or block unknown contacts.
- Do not post the listing publicly from your main accounts, as that may spread it further.
- Do not use unfamiliar links, payment requests, or account-verification requests sent by strangers.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.**  
   Move somewhere you feel safer. Silence unknown callers and messages. If you need immediate assistance from the police, fire department, or an ambulance, call **911**.

2. **Save basic details only if it feels safe:** save one screenshot, the URL, and any report confirmation.

3. **Use the host site’s report or help route.**  
   Report the post as impersonation, harassment, or sharing personal information, depending on the options the site provides. Ask for removal of the post.

4. **Use the intimate-image removal route only if it applies.**  
   If the post includes a nonconsensual intimate photo or video, including an altered image or deepfake, ask the platform to remove it through its intimate-image reporting route. Covered platforms must provide a request process and remove covered intimate images and known identical copies within 48 hours of a valid request. An ordinary photo or contact details used in a fake sexual-services post may need to be reported through the host site’s relevant report or help route instead.

5. **Report a covered platform violation to the FTC if needed.**  
   If a covered intimate image is not removed within 48 hours of a valid request, or the platform has no working removal process, you can report the platform through the FTC route.

6. **Consider an optional cybercrime report if there are threats or blackmail.**  
   If someone is threatening you, demanding money, or repeatedly targeting you online, you may file a cybercrime report through IC3. You can get support before deciding whether to make any report.

7. **Use the child-exploitation route if a minor may be involved.**  
   If sexually explicit content may involve someone under 18, or the advertisement appears to involve someone under 18, do not forward the content. Use NCMEC’s CyberTipline to report suspected child exploitation. Call **911** if there is immediate danger.

8. **Reduce incoming contact.**  
   Tighten message settings, block unknown contacts, and ask your phone carrier what blocking or number-change options are available if needed.

9. **Ask for support.**  
   A trusted person can sit with you while you report the post or manage incoming messages. RAINN can also offer confidential support without pressure to report.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to contact law enforcement, take legal action, or post publicly.
- You do not need to answer strangers or explain yourself to them.
- You do not need to find every copy of the post right now.
- You do not need to work out who created the post before asking for support or removal.

## Important reassurance
You did not cause this by having photos or contact details online. You can take one small step at a time: reduce contact, ask for removal, and get support.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need support from a specialist service, a lawyer, your phone carrier, or a workplace safety contact, depending on what is happening.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, mental health, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
