# What to do if… a GFCI or RCD outlet keeps tripping and you cannot restore power safely (USA)

## Short answer
Stop trying to force the power back on. If the GFCI will not reset, trips again, or is near water or showing heat, smoke, a burning odor, buzzing, or scorch marks, stop using the circuit and arrange qualified help.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t hold the RESET button in, tape it down, or keep pressing it repeatedly.
- Don’t touch the outlet, cords, plugs, or breaker panel if you are wet or standing on a damp surface.
- Don’t keep using an outlet that is hot, buzzing, crackling, loose, scorched, smoking, or giving off a burning odor.
- Don’t use extension cords, power strips, or another room’s outlets as a workaround for the affected circuit.
- Don’t remove outlet covers, open junction boxes, or remove electrical panel covers.
- Don’t assume repeated tripping is harmless nuisance tripping.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger.** If you see smoke or flames, hear an alarm, or suspect a fire, get outside and call 911. If someone may still be in contact with electricity, do not touch them. If anyone has received an electrical shock or burn, seek medical care; call 911 if they need immediate emergency help.
2. **Stop the reset loop.** If the GFCI trips again or will not reset, stop using that circuit until it has been checked.
3. **Unplug devices only when it is safe and dry.** Unplug appliances and equipment that were using the affected outlet or other outlets that suddenly lost power. A GFCI outlet can also protect outlets farther along the same circuit.
4. **Make one reset attempt only when there are no warning signs.** With dry hands and a dry floor, press RESET once. If it will not hold, stop.
5. **If the reset holds, check for a problem device carefully.** Only continue while the area remains dry and free of heat, odor, buzzing, crackling, or visible damage. Plug devices back in one at a time. If the GFCI trips when one device is connected or switched on, leave that device unplugged and out of service.
6. **Check the TEST and RESET buttons only if the outlet is stable and the area is safe.** Follow the manufacturer’s instructions if available. Otherwise, plug in a small lamp or nightlight, press TEST, and confirm that the light turns off. Press RESET and confirm that the light turns back on. If that does not happen, stop using the outlet and arrange inspection.
7. **Check the breaker only if the panel area is safe and dry.** Skip this step if you are unsure. Look for a breaker in the middle or OFF position. Move it fully OFF, then ON once. If it trips again, stop.
8. **Arrange the right help.**
   - **Owner:** contact a qualified electrician. Ask for a locally licensed electrician where required.
   - **Renting:** report the issue promptly to your landlord or property manager and ask for a qualified electrician.
   - **Essential electricity needs:** if the outage affects medical equipment or medication storage, say so when asking for help and follow the equipment or medication instructions.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to diagnose the wiring yourself.
- You don’t need to decide whether the cause is the outlet, an appliance, moisture, wiring, or the breaker.
- You don’t need to keep trying different devices once the GFCI will not hold.
- You don’t need to replace parts yourself.

## Important reassurance
A GFCI is designed to cut power when it detects a possible shock hazard. Stopping, leaving the circuit unused, and getting it checked is a sensible response.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce risk and stabilize the situation. Finding the cause may require electrical testing by a qualified electrician.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional electrical or medical advice. If there are signs of fire, overheating, arcing, or water involvement, do not use the circuit.
