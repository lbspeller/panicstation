# What to do if… you suspect someone is using your name to approve purchases or invoices at work (USA)

## Short answer
Do not agree to any changes or sign any documents during the initial meeting. Request all role options, compensation terms, and deadlines in writing to allow for a structured review.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign, "self-select out," or send any communication that could be interpreted as a voluntary quit.
- Do not sign a severance agreement, liability waiver, or non-disparagement release while in the meeting.
- Do not accept a new position without seeing the written job description, pay rate, exempt status, and reporting structure.
- Do not remove proprietary company data or files to "prepare" for a layoff, as this can be used as grounds for termination with cause.
- Do not assume a verbal promise of job security in a new role is legally binding in an at-will employment environment.

## What to do now
1. **Use a neutral pause script to end the immediate pressure.**  
   **Inform the manager or HR representative that you need to see the full offer in writing before making a decision.** Say: "I am not in a position to choose immediately. Please provide the role details, compensation terms, and the official response deadline in writing so I can review them."
2. **Request a complete data packet for each role option.**  
   **Ask for the specific documents that define the new terms of employment.** 
   - Written job description and title
   - Salary or hourly pay rate
   - FLSA status (exempt vs non-exempt)
   - Work location and remote work eligibility
   - Reporting lines and supervisory responsibilities
3. **Clarify the consequences of declining the offer.**  
   **Ask directly what happens if you do not select a new role by the deadline.** Request written confirmation on whether declining results in a layoff with severance eligibility, a default assignment to a different role, or a termination of employment.
4. **Identify your specific legal protections based on age.**  
   **If you are 40 or older, verify that any severance offer complies with the Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (OWBPA).** Federal law generally requires that you be given at least 21 days to consider an agreement (45 days in group layoffs) and 7 days to revoke your signature after signing. 
5. **Verify WARN Act applicability for mass layoffs.**  
   **Ask if the restructuring triggers the federal Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) Act.** This act generally requires employers with 100 or more full-time employees to provide 60 days' notice for plant closings or mass layoffs. Check if your state has a "mini-WARN" law with lower employee thresholds.
6. **Inquire about current NLRB standards for severance language.**  
   **Ask if the proposed agreement includes non-disparagement or confidentiality clauses.** As of 2026, the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) rules regarding "gag clauses" in severance for non-managerial employees are subject to specific enforcement criteria that may render broad restrictions unlawful.
7. **Document the interaction for your personal records.**  
   **Create a factual timeline of the meeting, including the names of those present and any specific deadlines mentioned.** Send this summary to a personal email address rather than keeping it on a company device.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to accept a pay cut or a lower title during the first 24 hours.
- You do not need to negotiate the specifics of a severance package until you have the baseline offer in writing.
- You do not need to update your resume or start external job applications until you have clarified your status with your current employer.
- You do not need to justify your hesitation or provide an emotional reaction to the restructuring plan.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when presented with a sudden ultimatum regarding your livelihood. Taking time to move the conversation from verbal pressure to written documentation is a standard professional practice and does not indicate a lack of loyalty or performance.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate procedural stabilization during a restructuring announcement. It does not address long-term career strategy, specific state-level labor codes, or the merits of individual employment contracts.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment laws, including at-will status and WARN Act triggers, vary significantly by state and employer size. Consult with an employment attorney before signing any document that waives your right to sue or alters your compensation.
