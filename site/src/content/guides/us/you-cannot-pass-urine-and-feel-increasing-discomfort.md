# What to do if… you cannot pass urine and feel increasing discomfort (USA)

## Short answer
Go to the **Emergency Department immediately** if you are completely unable to pass urine and have discomfort. If you have not passed urine for 6 to 7 hours despite a full bladder, this is a medical emergency.

## Do not do these things
- Do not continue to strain or push forcefully to urinate.
- Do not drink large amounts of water or tea to "force" the flow.
- Do not take over-the-counter cold, flu, or allergy medications (e.g., Benadryl) which can worsen retention.
- Do not apply ice packs or heating pads to the abdomen unless directed by a doctor.
- Do not insert any objects into the urinary opening or attempt to self-catheterize without medical training.
- Do not wait for the situation to resolve on its own if you are in pain.

## What to do now
1. **Travel to the nearest Emergency Department immediately.** Acute urinary retention requires professional intervention, typically through catheterization, to prevent bladder and kidney damage.
2. **Call 911 if you experience "red flag" neurological symptoms.** These include:
   - Sudden numbness or "pins and needles" in the groin, buttocks, or inner thighs (saddle anesthesia).
   - New or worsening leg weakness or difficulty walking.
   - Loss of bowel control.
   - Severe lower back pain following an injury.
3. **Call 911 if you feel systemically unwell.** Seek immediate help for confusion, fainting, high fever with chills, or severe vomiting.
4. **Limit attempts to urinate to 60 seconds.**
   - You may make one gentle attempt to void.
   - If no urine passes after 60 seconds, stop and focus on getting to medical care.
5. **Gather medical information only if it does not delay your departure.**
   - Note the approximate time of your last successful urination.
   - Have a list or photo of your medications, specifically any new prescriptions or recent doses of antihistamines or decongestants.
   - Note if you have recently had surgery, anesthesia, or a new spinal injury.
6. **Seek emergency care if your existing urinary catheter stops draining.**
   - Do not attempt to flush, pull on, or fix a blocked catheter yourself.
   - Go to the Emergency Department to have the line cleared or replaced.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause (such as prostate issues, infections, or nerve damage) before seeking help.
- You do not need to consult your primary care doctor first if you are in acute pain or have not voided in over 7 hours.
- You do not need to decide on long-term surgical or medical treatments today.

## Important reassurance
Acute urinary retention is a well-recognized medical emergency that hospital staff are equipped to handle quickly. Feeling a "hard" or "basketball-like" sensation in the lower abdomen is a common physical symptom of a full bladder that cannot empty. Medical teams prioritize relieving this pressure immediately upon assessment.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency triggers only. Long-term management of urinary health requires follow-up with a urologist or primary care provider.

## Important note
This information is not a medical diagnosis or a substitute for professional advice. If you are unable to urinate and in pain, or if you experience neurological changes, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room immediately.
