# What to do if… your credit card statement shows a cash advance you did not make (USA)

## Short answer
Call your credit card issuer’s fraud department immediately to report the unauthorized cash advance, request a card replacement, and initiate a formal billing error dispute in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use contact information from an unsolicited text, email, or pop-up warning you about the charge; use the number on the back of your physical card.
- Do not share your PIN, one-time security codes, or account passwords with anyone, even if they claim to be from the bank's fraud department.
- Do not wait for the next billing cycle to report the error, as interest on cash advances typically accrues daily starting from the transaction date.
- Do not mail original receipts or bank statements; keep the originals and send copies for the dispute.
- Do not ignore your regular minimum payment; keep paying the undisputed portion of your bill to avoid late fees or credit damage.

## What to do now
1. **Call your card issuer immediately using the number on the back of your card.**  
   **Ask the representative to take the following actions:**
   - **Invalidate the current card and PIN** and issue a replacement with a new account number.
   - **Open a fraud investigation** for the specific cash advance transaction.
   - **Lower your cash advance limit** to $0 or the lowest possible amount to prevent further withdrawals.
   - Provide a **case number** and the specific mailing address for "billing inquiries" or "disputes."
2. **Submit a formal written dispute within 60 days of the statement date.**  
   **To protect your rights under the Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA), you must send a letter to the issuer's designated dispute address.**
   - Include your name, account number, the transaction date, and the exact dollar amount of the unauthorized advance.
   - State clearly: "I am disputing this transaction as a billing error because I did not authorize this cash advance."
   - Request that all related interest charges and cash advance fees be removed.
   - Send the letter via **Certified Mail with Return Receipt Requested** so you have proof of delivery.
3. **Review your entire account for other suspicious activity.**  
   **Check for small "test" charges or changes to your contact information.**
   - Verify that your email address, phone number, and mailing address in the bank's portal have not been altered.
   - Look for unauthorized "add-on" services or unexpected changes to your credit limit.
4. **Update your digital security credentials immediately.**  
   **Change your online banking password and enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) if it is not already active.**
   - Use a unique password that you do not use for any other website.
   - Ensure the MFA codes are sent to a device you have physical possession of.
5. **Place a fraud alert or credit freeze if you suspect identity theft.**  
   **If your physical card and PIN were both used, your personal data may be compromised.**
   - Contact one of the three credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion) to place a free one-year **fraud alert**.
   - Consider a **credit freeze** at all three bureaus to prevent new accounts from being opened in your name.
6. **File an Identity Theft Report if the issuer requires documentation.**  
   **Visit IdentityTheft.gov to create an official FTC Identity Theft Report.**
   - This report provides you with a recovery plan and serves as official evidence for your bank and the credit bureaus.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact ATM or person who made the withdrawal before reporting it.
- You do not need to file a local police report immediately unless the bank explicitly requests one to process the fraud claim.
- You do not need to pay the disputed amount or the associated interest while the investigation is active, provided you have submitted the dispute in writing.

## Important reassurance
Seeing a cash withdrawal you did not make can feel like a direct violation of your security. However, US federal law provides strong protections for credit card billing errors. Once you report the card as compromised and submit your written dispute, the issuer is legally required to investigate and cannot report the disputed amount as delinquent while the case is open.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for unauthorized cash advances on credit cards under US federal law. It does not cover debit card withdrawals (which fall under different regulations) or long-term credit repair.
