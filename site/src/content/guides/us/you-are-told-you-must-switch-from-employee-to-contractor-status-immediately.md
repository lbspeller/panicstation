# What to do if… you are told you must switch from employee to contractor status immediately (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before signing anything if you can. Ask for the proposed terms and what will happen to your current employment relationship in writing, then save copies of your own records.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a contractor agreement, resignation, or acknowledgment before you have read it carefully.
- Do not assume that a 1099 label or contractor agreement automatically settles your legal status.
- Do not start invoicing, form an LLC, or buy insurance just because you were asked to decide quickly.
- Do not send a rushed or angry reply. Keep your response brief, calm, and written.
- Do not copy confidential customer information, proprietary files, or records you are not allowed to keep.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the proposal in writing and request time to review it.** Ask for the proposed agreement, effective date, pay rate, payment schedule, duties, expenses, equipment arrangements, and termination terms.
2. **Ask what happens to your current employment relationship.** In writing, ask whether you will remain an employee if you decline the proposed change and when any change would take effect.
3. **Ask for the practical changes in writing.** Ask whether the change affects payroll, overtime treatment, benefits, paid time off, expense reimbursement, equipment, tax withholding, and any final pay arrangements. Some details depend on state law and the employer’s policies.
4. **Save your own records carefully.** Keep copies of your offer letter, pay stubs, schedules or time records you can access, job description, benefits information, and messages about the proposed switch. Keep only records about your own employment and the status change.
5. **Write down how the work is actually arranged.** Note who sets your schedule, who directs the work, whose tools or systems you use, whether you can work for other clients, whether you have business expenses, whether the work is ongoing, and whether the work is central to the business. A label alone does not decide worker status, and no single fact settles the question.
6. **Use an official contact route if you need help with the next step.** For federal wage-and-hour questions, you can contact the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division at **1-866-487-9243**. State labor offices may also be relevant because state rules and processes can differ.

## What can wait
- You do not need to solve invoicing, LLC formation, insurance, or estimated-tax questions all at once.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to file a complaint or ask the IRS for a Form SS-8 determination.
- You do not need to make a long-term career decision in the first conversation.
- If the employer gives you a deadline, write it down and seek independent advice promptly if you need it.

## Important reassurance
A sudden request to change your work status can feel overwhelming. Pausing before signing, asking for the details in writing, and saving your own records are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may require advice from an employment lawyer, tax professional, or relevant state agency based on your state and the facts of your work arrangement.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, tax, financial, or professional advice. Worker classification depends on the facts and may involve federal and state rules.
