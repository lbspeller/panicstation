# What to do if… someone is using a secret relationship to pressure you into sexual acts or images (USA)

## Short answer
You do not owe anyone secrecy or sexual contact. Get to a safer pause if you can do so without increasing danger, and contact a support service or a trusted person so you are not handling this alone.

## Do not do these things
- Do not have sexual contact to avoid conflict, guilt, threats, or exposure.
- Do not treat secrecy as something you have to repay with sexual access.
- Do not meet them somewhere private to talk if you feel pressured or afraid.
- Do not send more intimate photos or messages to calm them down or stop threats.
- Do not delete messages in a rush if you may want options later, but do not keep anything where it could put you at risk.
- Do not assume that freezing, going quiet, or not fighting back means consent.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause.** If you can do so without increasing danger, move to a place where you feel safer or contact someone you trust. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
2. **Do not meet privately or send intimate material.** You do not need to reply. If one brief boundary message feels safer than continuing a conversation, you can send it and then stop engaging.
3. **Contact support.** RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline is available 24/7: call **800-656-HOPE (4673)**, text **HOPE** to **64673**, or use online chat. If you are a teen or young adult in a dating relationship, love is respect is available 24/7: text **LOVEIS** to **22522**, call **866-331-9474**, or use online chat.
4. **Consider medical care if sexual contact happened without your freely given consent, or if you are unsure.** A healthcare professional can discuss injury care, emergency contraception when relevant, and STI care. You can ask a RAINN advocate about local options.
5. **Use a safer device if you think your phone or accounts may be monitored.** Before changing passwords or settings, ask an advocate about technology safety if making changes could alert the other person.
6. **Only if it is safe and you may want options later, keep relevant messages or screenshots somewhere the other person cannot access.**
7. **Use emergency help if needed.** If you can safely call, call 911. If you cannot safely call, you can text 911 where text-to-911 is available. If it is not available, you should receive a bounce-back message telling you to contact emergency services another way.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide right now whether to report anything to police or start a formal process.
- You do not have to decide what to call the situation before asking for help.
- You do not have to decide the future of the relationship today.
- You do not have to answer messages immediately.

## Important reassurance
Pressure, guilt, threats, or leverage can undermine freely given consent. Freezing, going quiet, or not fighting back does not mean that you agreed. None of this is your fault.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to stabilize the moment and reduce immediate harm. Later decisions may be easier with specialist support and a safety plan that fits your situation.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Privacy limits can apply when you contact a support service, especially if identifying information about a minor is shared. You can ask what limits apply before sharing identifying details. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If you are under 18 or a minor is involved, you can contact Childhelp for support and guidance: call **800-422-4453**, text **GO** to **800-422-4453**, or use its online chat.
