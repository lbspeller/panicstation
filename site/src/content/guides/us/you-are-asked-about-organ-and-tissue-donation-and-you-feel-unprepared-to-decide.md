# What to do if… you are asked about organ and tissue donation and you feel unprepared to decide (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the Organ Procurement Organization (OPO) coordinator to clarify if "First Person Authorization" already exists and request a brief pause to process the situation. Under 2026 federal guidelines, you have the right to appropriate time to process your loss without inappropriate pressure or coercion.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t feel forced to provide an immediate "yes" or "no" if you lack clear information about what is being authorized.
- Don’t assume you can override a decision if your loved one is already a registered donor; in most states, adult registration is a legally binding "First Person Authorization."
- Don’t worry about the costs of the donation process; federally designated OPOs cover all expenses related to organ and tissue recovery.
- Don’t guess about your loved one’s wishes if you are unsure; you can ask the coordinator to check the state and national registries.
- Don’t allow the conversation to proceed if you feel overwhelmed; you have a right to ask for a different coordinator or a break.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the OPO coordinator and request a slowing of the process.**  
   **Ask the hospital staff to introduce you to the representative from the Organ Procurement Organization (OPO).** Explain that you feel unprepared and need a clear, step-by-step explanation of the current status.

2. **Verify the existence of First Person Authorization.**  
   **Ask if the decedent is already recorded in the state or national donor registry or on their drivers license.** 
   - If they are registered, ask: "Since they authorized this themselves, what is the specific process from here, and what information do you need from us?"
   - If they are not registered, ask: "Who is the legally recognized next-of-kin for this authorization?"

3. **Request a written or verbal checklist of exactly what is being requested.**  
   **Ask the coordinator to separate the request into specific categories.** 
   - Organs (such as heart, lungs, liver, kidneys)
   - Tissues (such as skin, bone, heart valves)
   - Eyes (corneas)
   - Research or education (if applicable)

4. **Inquire about the impact on funeral and viewing arrangements.**  
   **Ask how the recovery process will affect the timing of the body's release to your chosen funeral home.** Most donation processes are completed within 24 to 48 hours and do not prevent an open-casket funeral or viewing.

5. **Contact the OPTN 411 support line if you feel pressured.**  
   **Call 1-866-OPTN-411 (1-866-678-6411) for independent information and support regarding the donation process.** This federal service provides multi-language assistance for families navigating donation decisions.

6. **Designate a single spokesperson to communicate with the OPO.**  
   **Choose one family member to be the point of contact to prevent conflicting messages and reduce stress.** This person should handle the specific medical/social history questionnaire required for all donors.

7. **Consult with the medical examiner or coroner if the death is under investigation.**  
   **Ask if the medical examiner has "released" the case for donation if the death was sudden, accidental, or suspicious.** In some jurisdictions, the medical examiner must provide separate approval for tissue or organ recovery.

8. **Clarify the "no-decision" outcome.**  
   **Ask what happens if the family chooses not to authorize donation within the medical viability window.** Knowing the default outcome can help remove the weight of the "active" choice if you are truly paralyzed by the decision.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a funeral home or plan a memorial service during the donation conversation.
- You do not need to resolve complex family history or long-term grief during this window.
- You do not need to think about future correspondence with transplant recipients right now.
- You do not need to worry about medical bills incurred before the death declaration; those are handled separately from the donation process.

## Important reassurance
Feeling overwhelmed is the standard response to these requests. It is a complex medical and legal process occurring during a period of acute trauma. Taking 15 or 30 minutes to step away and speak privately with your family is a protected right in the 2026 CMS patient safety guidelines.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for families facing an immediate donation request. It focuses on clarifying legal status and reducing pressure. It does not replace the specialized guidance provided by hospital chaplains, social workers, or OPO family advocates.

## Important note
Donation laws (UAGA) are state-specific but generally prioritize the documented wishes of the donor. This guide reflects 2026 federal oversight standards aimed at preventing coercion. Always confirm specific state requirements with the OPO coordinator on-site.
