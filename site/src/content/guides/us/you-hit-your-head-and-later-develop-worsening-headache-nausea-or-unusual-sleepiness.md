# What to do if… you hit your head and later develop worsening headache, nausea, or unusual sleepiness (USA)

## Short answer
Go to the nearest Emergency Department immediately if symptoms worsen after a head injury. Call 911 if the person is difficult to wake, confused, vomiting repeatedly, or has any neurological changes like slurred speech or seizures.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay alone; a responsible adult must monitor the person for at least 24 hours.
- Do not "sleep it off" if the person is unusually drowsy, difficult to wake, or symptoms are progressing.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you are dizzy, confused, sleepy, or have vision changes.
- Do not eat or drink anything until a medical professional has evaluated you.
- Do not take aspirin, ibuprofen, or any other blood-thinning pain relievers.
- Do not use alcohol, sedatives, or sleep medications after the injury.
- Do not engage in physical activity, sports, or heavy screen use until cleared by a clinician.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 immediately if any emergency danger signs are present.** These indicate a potential medical emergency:
   - A headache that gets worse and does not go away.
   - Repeated nausea or vomiting (more than once or twice).
   - Slurred speech, weakness, numbness, or decreased coordination.
   - One pupil (the black part of the eye) appearing larger than the other.
   - Convulsions or seizures (shaking or twitching).
   - Inability to recognize people or places, or extreme confusion/agitation.
   - Loss of consciousness, looking very drowsy, or inability to wake up.
   - Clear fluid or blood draining from the nose or ears.
2. **Seek emergency care immediately for infants or children with specific red flags.** In addition to the adult signs above, seek help if the child:
   - Will not stop crying and cannot be consoled.
   - Refuses to nurse or eat.
   - Has a bulging soft spot (fontanelle) on the front of the head (infants).
   - Shows any visible skull deformity or depressed area.
3. **Go to an Emergency Department if symptoms are worsening but the person is currently stable.** Worsening symptoms after a "safe" period can indicate delayed bleeding (hematoma) and require immediate imaging (CT scan).
4. **Assume higher risk and seek urgent evaluation if specific factors apply.** Treat the situation with increased urgency if:
   - The person takes blood thinners (anticoagulants) or has a bleeding disorder.
   - The injury involved high force (e.g., motor vehicle crash, fall from a significant height).
   - The person is an older adult.
5. **Keep the injured person still while waiting for help or transport.**
   - **Have the person lie down with the head and shoulders slightly elevated.**
   - **Do not move the person's neck if a spinal injury is suspected.**
6. **Gather essential information for the medical team.** Prepare to report:
   - The exact time and cause of the injury.
   - Whether there was any loss of consciousness (even for a few seconds).
   - A list of all medications, particularly blood thinners or antiplatelet drugs.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the "grade" of the concussion or injury yourself.
- You do not need to decide on long-term school or work accommodations today.
- You do not need to perform your own neurological "tests" or memory checks.
- You do not need to wait for your primary care doctor's office to open if symptoms are worsening now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel hesitant or uncertain when symptoms appear hours after an injury, but seeking an evaluation is the standard protective step. Medical protocols for head injuries are designed to rule out serious internal issues even when the external injury looks minor. Feeling startled or overwhelmed is a common reaction to a head injury event.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency identification only. It does not replace clinical diagnosis, imaging, or long-term recovery management.
