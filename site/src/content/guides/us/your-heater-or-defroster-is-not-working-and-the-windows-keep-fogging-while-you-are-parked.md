# What to do if… your heater or defroster is not working and the windows keep fogging while you are parked (USA)

## Short answer
Stay parked and do not attempt to move the vehicle until you have clear visibility through the entire windshield and front side windows. Use the air conditioning and fresh air intake to dehumidify the cabin air, and manually wipe the interior glass if mechanical systems fail to clear it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive "blind" or with a small "peek-hole" cleared in the fog.
- Do not pour hot water on the glass to clear it; the thermal shock can cause the windshield to shatter.
- Do not keep the engine running in a garage or enclosed structure, even if the door is open.
- Do not use the "recirculation" setting, as this traps human breath and moisture inside the cabin.
- Do not ignore symptoms like headache, dizziness, or nausea while idling, which can indicate carbon monoxide buildup.
- Do not open the radiator or coolant reservoir cap if you suspect the engine is overheating.

## What to do now
1. **Secure your parked position immediately.** **Ensure the vehicle is in Park (or Neutral with the emergency brake engaged) and stay off the roadway.** 
   - If you are stopped on a shoulder or in a live traffic lane and cannot see well enough to move, turn on your hazard lights. 
   - If you are in an immediate danger of being struck by other vehicles, call **911**.
2. **Force dry air onto the glass using the available controls.** **Set your climate system to the windshield defrost icon, turn the fan to its highest speed, and ensure the Air Conditioning (A/C) is turned ON.**
   - The A/C system acts as a dehumidifier even if the air coming out is not hot.
   - Ensure the "Fresh Air" or "Outside Air" setting is selected (usually a button without a circular arrow).
3. **Open windows to equalize temperature and humidity.** **Crack at least two windows by one to two inches to allow moist cabin air to escape.**
   - This is especially critical if the heater is not providing warm air to evaporate the condensation.
4. **Identify and remove moisture sources.** **Check for wet items in the cabin such as floor mats, umbrellas, or damp clothing.**
   - If you have passengers, their breathing increases humidity; have them step out of the vehicle if it is safe to do so.
5. **Manually clear the interior glass.** **Use a clean, dry microfiber cloth or paper towels to wipe the inside of the windshield and side windows.**
   - Wipe in a consistent direction to minimize streaks that cause glare at night.
   - Avoid using your bare hands, as skin oils can cause the glass to re-fog more quickly.
6. **Monitor for signs of mechanical or safety hazards.** **Check your dashboard for a rising temperature gauge or "Check Engine" light, and sniff for a sweet, syrupy smell (coolant leak).**
   - If the engine is overheating, turn it off immediately to prevent permanent damage.
   - If you feel dizzy, develop a headache, or feel nauseated, turn off the engine, exit the car immediately for fresh air, and call **911**.
7. **Verify visibility before moving.** **Wait until the entire windshield and both front side windows (providing a clear view of your mirrors) are completely transparent.**
   - If the fog returns within seconds of wiping, do not drive; your ventilation system is not successfully managing the cabin humidity.

## What can wait
- Determining why the heater is blowing cold air (e.g., low coolant, failed thermostat, or airlock).
- Repairing the blower motor or A/C compressor.
- Researching repair shops or scheduling a service appointment.
- Cleaning the exterior of the car.

## Important reassurance
It is a common and physiological response to feel a sense of "tunnel vision" or panic when your visibility is suddenly obscured. Fogging is a physical process of moisture hitting cold glass; it does not mean your car is about to catch fire or explode. Taking a few minutes to wait for the air to clear is a standard safety procedure used by all experienced drivers.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for visibility and immediate safety while parked. It does not cover mechanical diagnosis, engine repair, or long-term maintenance of automotive HVAC systems.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only and does not replace professional mechanical or medical advice. Operating a vehicle with obstructed visibility is illegal in many jurisdictions and constitutes a major safety hazard to yourself and others. If you suspect carbon monoxide exposure or an engine fire, evacuate the vehicle immediately and contact emergency services.
