# What to do if… someone is lingering in your building lobby or entrance and you feel unsafe (USA)

## Short answer
Move behind a locked door or to a staffed area. Contact building staff or security, or a trusted neighbor. Call 911 if you believe there is immediate danger or a crime in progress.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront the person or try to make them leave.
- Don’t let the person in, prop a controlled door open, or allow them to follow you through the entrance.
- Don’t enter an isolated garage, stairwell, laundry room, basement, or package room to check what they are doing.
- Don’t share your unit number, name, schedule, or entry code.
- Don’t take photos or video if doing so would make you more visible or less safe.
- Don’t dismiss the concern solely because the person’s intent is unclear.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause point.** If you are in your unit, lock the door. If you are in a hallway or common area, move into your unit, a staffed area, or a trusted neighbor’s unit rather than waiting nearby.
2. **Contact building support.** Call or text building security, the front desk, the concierge, or the property manager’s emergency line. State which entrance or lobby is involved and what you can directly observe.
3. **Avoid leaving alone through the affected area.** Where possible, wait for building staff or a trusted neighbor to accompany you. Use a staffed or busier route and avoid isolated areas.
4. **Choose the appropriate contact route.**
   - Call 911 if you believe there is immediate danger or a crime in progress. Examples of urgent concerns include someone trying doors, following residents, blocking an entrance or exit, or attempting to force entry.
   - For suspicious behavior without an immediate threat, contact your local police non-emergency number. Look for the number on your local law-enforcement agency’s official website. Do not call 911 solely to ask for the non-emergency number.
   - If you cannot safely speak, text 911 only if text-to-911 is available in your area. A voice call remains the best option when possible. If text-to-911 is unavailable, you should receive a bounce-back message saying the text was not delivered.
5. **Report observable behavior.** Give the exact address, the entrance or lobby involved, what the person is doing, and the direction they move if they leave. Share a description or vehicle details only if you can do so without approaching, following, or making yourself more visible.
6. **Ask management to document the incident.** Ask building staff or management to save relevant camera footage and make an incident note. They may decide whether to contact local law enforcement under building policy and local rules.
7. **Reduce exposure while the concern is active.** Avoid waiting near the entrance, collecting mail or packages alone, or saying your unit number or entry code where others can hear.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the person or determine their intent before asking for help.
- You do not need to decide now whether to move, buy security equipment, or make long-term changes.
- Follow-up about access controls, resident reminders, or a written management report can wait until you are in a safer pause point.

## Important reassurance
You do not need proof of harmful intent to create distance and ask for support. Staying behind a locked door, involving building staff, and using the appropriate emergency or non-emergency route can reduce risk without requiring you to confront anyone.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes to hours. If the behavior repeats, later decisions may need help from building management, local law enforcement, or another appropriate specialist. You can note dates, times, and observable behavior when it is safe to do so, without approaching or tracking the person.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe anyone is in immediate danger, call 911.
