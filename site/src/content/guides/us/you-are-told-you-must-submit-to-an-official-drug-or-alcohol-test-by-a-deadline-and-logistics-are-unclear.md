# What to do if… you are told you must submit to an official drug or alcohol test by a deadline and logistics are unclear (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the deadline as potentially important. Use trusted contact details to ask the office that issued or supervises the requirement for exact instructions, preferably in writing, and keep a simple record of each attempt to comply.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume that missing details automatically cancel the instruction.
- Do not click a link, pay a fee, or send sensitive information in response to an unexpected message until you have verified the request through independently obtained contact details.
- Do not choose a testing site or testing method unless the issuing or supervising office confirms it is acceptable.
- Do not try to alter the sample or use a product marketed as changing a test result.
- Do not send prescription or medical information unless the office or your lawyer tells you what is needed.
- Do not wait for a perfect answer before making your first contact attempt.
- When possible, do not rely on a call alone; send a brief dated follow-up message.

## What to do now
1. Read the notice again and save a copy. Write down the exact deadline, the sender, any case or program number, the contact details shown, and the test type if one is stated.

2. Verify the request using contact details from paperwork you already trust or from an official website for the relevant court, agency, or program. Do not use a new payment link, testing link, or phone number from an unexpected message until the request has been confirmed.

3. Contact the office that issued the notice or supervises the requirement. Ask for:
   - the approved testing location or locations
   - whether you need an appointment or can walk in
   - the opening hours
   - the testing method, if it is not stated
   - what the deadline means for your specific notice or program
   - what identification or paperwork to bring
   - whether any fee applies and how an approved payment would be handled
   - what to do if the site is closed, inaccessible, or unable to test you

4. Ask for a written reply if possible. If you speak by phone, send a short follow-up message stating the time of the call and your understanding of the instructions.

5. If the deadline is close and you have not received an answer, call the main line for the same office and ask for a backup contact, supervisor, or routing help. If you still cannot identify the responsible office:
   - for a state or local court matter, use trusted court paperwork or an official court directory to contact the relevant clerk’s office for routing help
   - for a federal matter, use an official federal court finder or federal court website directory to locate the relevant probation or pretrial services office
   - for a matter that appears to involve state corrections or parole, use your state corrections department’s official website for routing help
   If you have a lawyer, tell them promptly that the logistics remain unclear.

6. Keep a simple record. Save the notice, messages, emails, call times, voicemail timestamps, names of people you spoke with, and any instructions you receive.

7. Follow the confirmed instructions as soon as you reasonably can. Bring only the documents you were told to bring. If a confirmed testing site cannot test you, ask whether staff can provide a receipt or note showing that you attended, and notify the supervising or issuing office promptly.

8. If an illness, disability issue, transport disruption, or emergency may prevent attendance, tell the supervising or issuing office promptly and ask what procedure and documentation they require.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve the reason for the test right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to make a complaint or take a wider legal step in the next hour.
- You do not need to provide a long personal explanation before asking for clear logistics.
- You do not need to send medical or prescription details unless you are told what is required.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to solve the whole situation at once. A prompt request for exact instructions, a careful verification step, and a simple record of your attempts are useful first actions while the logistics are being clarified.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The exact notice, order, supervision conditions, program rules, and confirmed instructions may matter. State, county, local, and federal procedures vary, and later decisions may need help from a lawyer or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice.
