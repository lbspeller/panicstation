# What to do if… police ask you to come to the station to collect property and you are unsure why (USA)

## Short answer
**Verify the request through the department's official Evidence and Property Division and clarify if the visit includes an interview.** If you are asked questions about a crime, **state clearly that you wish to remain silent and want a lawyer present** before proceeding with the property collection.

## Do not do these things
- Don't assume the visit is purely administrative if the property was seized during a criminal investigation.
- Don't answer questions about how you obtained the property, who else used it, or events related to its seizure without a lawyer.
- Don't volunteer your phone passcode or biometric unlock (FaceID / Fingerprint) to "check" if the device works.
- Don't sign any "Consent to Search" or "Waiver of Rights" forms; only sign the "Property Receipt" or "Release Log" acknowledging you received the items.
- Don't wait longer than 30 to 90 days to respond, as many departments dispose of "safekeeping" property after these windows.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the request directly with the Evidence and Property Division.** 
   - **Locate the official non-emergency number for the specific police department or sheriff’s office.** 
   - **Ask to speak with the Property Clerk or Evidence Custodian.** 
   - **Provide the case number or property receipt number to confirm the items are actually ready for release.** 

2. **Clarify the nature of the encounter before arriving.** 
   - **Ask if the release requires a meeting with a detective or if it can be handled at the property window.** 
   - **Ask if there are any active "investigative holds" or if a court order is required for the release.** 
   - **Inquire if the visit is "voluntary" or if you are being "detained" for questioning.** 

3. **Prepare the mandatory documentation for retrieval.** 
   - **Ensure you have a valid government-issued photo ID (Driver’s License, Passport, or State ID).** 
   - **Locate your original property receipt (the "yellow sheet") if one was issued at the time of seizure.** 
   - **If retrieving a firearm, confirm if your state requires a specific "Law Enforcement Release" application or background check prior to the appointment.** 

4. **Arrange for a third-party pickup if you fear self-incrimination.** 
   - **Check the department's policy on "Power of Attorney" for property collection.** 
   - **Prepare a notarized letter of authorization if you are sending a representative to collect the items on your behalf.** 

5. **Invoke your Fifth and Sixth Amendment rights if questioning begins.** 
   - **State clearly: "I am asserting my right to remain silent and I want to speak to an attorney."** 
   - **Stop answering all questions immediately once these rights are invoked, even if the officer continues to speak.** 
   - **Request to complete the administrative property release and leave if you are not under arrest.** 

6. **Inspect the property and document its condition before leaving the window.** 
   - **Verify that serial numbers on electronics or firearms match your records.** 
   - **Note any missing components (e.g., SIM cards, batteries, or accessories) on the department's release form.** 
   - **Ask for a copy of the signed property release document for your own records.** 

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to explain the origin of the property or your relationship to other suspects today.
- You do **not** need to decide immediately whether to file a claim for damage if the property was returned broken.
- You do **not** need to give a statement "for the record" to speed up the process.

## Important reassurance
Police departments are legally required to manage and eventually dispose of vast amounts of property; many calls to "come pick up your things" are routine inventory clearances. It is a standard procedure for evidence technicians to return items once a case is closed or a prosecutor determines the items are no longer evidentiary. Feeling a "fight or flight" response when dealing with law enforcement is a common physiological reaction and does not imply guilt.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for administrative property retrieval in the USA. It does not cover the "Motion for Return of Property" legal process required when police refuse to release items, nor does it cover civil asset forfeiture litigation.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not legal advice. Property release policies vary significantly between federal, state, and local agencies. If you suspect you are a target of a criminal investigation, consult a defense attorney before entering a police facility.
