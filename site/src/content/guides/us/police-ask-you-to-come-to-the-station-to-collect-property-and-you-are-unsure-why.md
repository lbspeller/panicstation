# What to do if… police ask you to come to the station to collect property and you are unsure why (USA)

## Short answer
Before going to the station, verify the request through the department’s publicly listed number and ask whether the visit is only for property release or may include questions. If officers want to ask about an incident, say clearly that you are invoking your Fifth Amendment right to remain silent and want to speak with a lawyer before answering questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the visit is only paperwork if the purpose is unclear.
- Do not explain events, timelines, or other people informally or “off the record.”
- Do not unlock a phone or volunteer access to a device, account, or messages.
- Do not sign a statement or consent form because you feel rushed. Ask what each document is before signing it.
- Do not argue, resist, or physically obstruct officers if they say you are detained, arrested, or that they have legal authority for a search.
- If you choose not to answer incident-related questions, do not rely only on becoming quiet. State clearly that you are invoking your Fifth Amendment right to remain silent.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the request before travelling.**  
   Find the department’s publicly listed phone number independently. Ask to confirm the officer’s name, the case or incident number if there is one, the property involved, and where the pickup would happen.

2. **Ask what the visit is for.**  
   Say: “Is this only a property release, or do you want to ask me questions?”  
   Also ask: “Am I required to come in, or is this voluntary?”  
   Write down the date, time, name of the person you spoke with, and the answers. Treat the answers as useful information, not a guarantee that the visit cannot change.

3. **Pause if the purpose stays unclear.**  
   If the department will not clarify whether questions are planned, say that you want legal advice before arranging the visit. A lawyer in your state can advise you about the specific request.

4. **Ask about the property-release process.**  
   Ask whether the property can be released at a front desk, whether an appointment is needed, and what identification or paperwork you should bring. Ask whether the department allows another person to collect the property if you cannot attend. Policies vary.

5. **Arrange simple support.**  
   Tell a trusted person where you are going and when. Ask whether they can travel with you or wait nearby where permitted. Bring the identification and paperwork the department requests. Keep any phone locked and do not volunteer access.

6. **If questions start, state your position clearly.**  
   Say: “I am invoking my Fifth Amendment right to remain silent. I want to speak with a lawyer before answering questions.”  
   Ask: “Am I free to leave?”  
   If officers say you are free to leave, you can leave calmly if you do not want to continue. You may ask whether the property can still be released without answering questions. If officers say you are detained or arrested, do not resist. Repeat that you are invoking your right to remain silent and want a lawyer.

7. **Check the property release.**  
   Ask for a receipt or release form listing the items returned. Check the list before leaving and note anything missing or obviously damaged. Ask who to contact about any discrepancy.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain your side immediately to appear cooperative.
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a complaint, request records, or take any further legal step.
- You do not need to volunteer consent to a search of a device, account, or property. If officers say they have a warrant or other legal authority, do not obstruct; state that you do not consent and seek legal advice.

## Important reassurance
A request to collect property does not by itself explain why the department wants contact. It is reasonable to verify the request, ask what the visit involves, and pause before attending if the purpose remains unclear.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: verify the request, clarify whether the visit is administrative, avoid accidental consent, and avoid answering incident-related questions before getting advice. Later decisions may need help from a lawyer in your state.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Laws, procedures, and department policies vary by state and agency. Miranda warnings generally concern custodial interrogation. During a voluntary conversation, asking for a lawyer may not by itself end questioning, so state your decision not to answer and then stop answering.
