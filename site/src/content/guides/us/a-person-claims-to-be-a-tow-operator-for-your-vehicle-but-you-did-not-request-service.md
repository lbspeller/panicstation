# What to do if… a person claims to be a tow operator for your vehicle but you did not request service (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a safer place away from traffic and tow equipment, then verify independently before you agree to anything. Call 911 if there is immediate danger, threats, violence, or a suspected theft in progress.

## Do not do these things
- Do not physically block the tow truck, stand between vehicles, or argue at close range.
- Do not remain inside a vehicle that is being hooked up, lifted, or moved.
- Do not hand over keys, ID, registration, insurance details, or lender information until you have independently verified who sent the operator and why.
- Do not sign paperwork or follow a payment link just to end the pressure.
- Do not pay an unverified operator on the spot to stop the tow.
- Do not follow the truck to an unknown location.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.**  
   Step away from traffic, cables, chains, and the space between vehicles. If you are at home, go inside and lock the door. Otherwise, move to a safe nearby place before making calls.

2. **Choose the right police contact route.**  
   Call 911 if there is immediate danger, threats, violence, or a suspected theft in progress. If the situation is calm but suspicious, call your local police non-emergency number and ask what to do next.

3. **Say only what is needed.**  
   Use a short sentence such as: “I did not request a tow. I am verifying independently. Please step back.”

4. **Verify the claimed dispatch independently.**  
   Use a phone number or app you find yourself, not a number supplied by the operator. Contact your roadside provider or insurer if they claim to have been sent by that company. If they claim a property owner sent them, contact the property manager through a number you already have or independently find.

5. **Check a claimed police dispatch.**  
   Law enforcement may sometimes arrange a tow. If an officer is present, ask the officer which company was sent. If no officer is present, verify through the agency’s public number that you independently find, using 911 only when the situation is an emergency.

6. **Treat a repossession claim as a safety and verification issue.**  
   Repossession rules vary by state. Do not physically interfere. From a safe place, contact your lender through the number on your statement, lender app, or official website. If threats, force, or another immediate safety issue develops, call 911.

7. **Record basic details only if it is safe.**  
   Note the time, location, truck plate, visible company name, driver description, paperwork shown, and the destination if it is safely observable. Take photographs only from a safe position.

8. **If the vehicle is taken, prioritize safety.**  
   Do not chase the truck or continue a confrontation. If you suspect theft, there was a safety issue, or you need local police guidance, contact police through the emergency or non-emergency route that fits the situation. Contact your insurer, roadside provider, lender, or property manager as relevant.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether this is a mistake, a legitimate dispatch, a repossession claim, or a scam.
- You do not need to negotiate towing fees, storage fees, or release terms while you feel pressured.
- You do not need to hand over personal or financial details to prove anything in the moment.
- You do not need to resolve an insurance, lender, or property dispute until you are somewhere safe.

## Important reassurance
An unexpected tow operator can be confusing and stressful. You only need to create distance, avoid a confrontation, and verify the claim through a contact route you choose yourself.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Towing and repossession rules can vary by state and locality, so later decisions may need help from your insurer, roadside provider, lender, property manager, local police non-emergency line, or a state consumer protection office.
