# What to do if… your travel companion loses their passport and you need to manage bookings and plans immediately (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safe place with Wi-Fi, do one brief controlled search, and have the passport holder report the valid U.S. passport lost or stolen if it is still missing. Then contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate before making expensive booking changes.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep searching for hours while flights, lodging, or consular opening times slip away.
- Don’t report an expired passport as lost or stolen; the State Department says this is for a valid passport.
- Don’t assume the passport can be used if it turns up later; once reported lost or stolen, it is no longer valid for international travel.
- Don’t pay anyone claiming they can “recover” the passport or get special embassy access.
- Don’t make expensive changes to flights, hotels, tours, or visas before you know the embassy or consulate timing and the airline’s document rules.
- Don’t hand over your own passport, cards, or phone to unofficial helpers to prove anything.
- Don’t argue about blame while you are still trying to secure tonight’s lodging, the next border crossing, or the next flight.

## What to do now
1. **Create a short “admin base”.** Sit somewhere safe with power and Wi-Fi. Put passports, wallets, phones, booking references, and chargers on the table. One person handles calls; one person gathers documents.

2. **Do a strict 5-minute search, then stop.** Check bags, coat pockets, the hotel safe, reception, recent transport, the last restaurant or shop, lockers, and any document pouch. If it is still missing, move to official reporting.

3. **Have the passport holder report the valid U.S. passport lost or stolen.** The fastest official route is the State Department online reporting tool. Online reporting cancels the passport within 1 business day and sends a confirmation email; save that confirmation.

4. **Contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate.** The passport holder will generally need to appear in person to apply for a replacement. Say clearly: “A U.S. passport has been lost or stolen, we have imminent travel, and we need to know the earliest replacement appointment or emergency route.”

5. **Ask exactly what to bring to the appointment.** Start gathering a passport photo, another photo ID such as a driver’s license or expired passport, proof of U.S. citizenship if available, a copy or photo of the missing passport if available, itinerary details, and passport fees. Do not delay contacting the embassy or consulate just because one item is missing.

6. **If theft is suspected, ask whether a local police report is sensible.** A police report is not always mandatory for the passport replacement, but it can help confirm what happened and may support insurance. Keep any report number or document.

7. **Ask whether the replacement may be limited-validity or emergency-only.** If there is not enough time for a regular passport, a consular section can offer an emergency passport that may be valid for up to 1 year. Some destinations may not accept a limited-validity passport, so do not assume the original itinerary still works.

8. **Call the airline before changing the booking.** Use one focused question: “The passenger is replacing a lost U.S. passport through a U.S. embassy or consulate. What document details do you need to keep this booking valid, and when must they be updated?” Save chat transcripts, emails, or screenshots.

9. **Protect the next 24 hours first.** Sort bookings into: must keep, can move, can cancel. Prioritize tonight’s lodging, the next flight, the next border crossing, and any booking where a passport number is already attached.

10. **Contact accommodation if check-in may be blocked.** Ask what identification they can accept while the replacement passport is being arranged. Note the staff member’s name, time, and answer.

11. **Check visas, eVisas, or entry permissions tied to the missing passport.** A new passport number can affect upcoming entry or transit permission. For now, identify the affected border crossing and wait to act until you know what replacement document will be issued.

12. **Contact travel insurance before spending money, if you can.** Ask what documents they require for a lost or stolen passport claim, such as the State Department confirmation, police report if filed, receipts, airline messages, or proof of extra accommodation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to cancel the whole trip right now.
- You do not need to decide who pays for everything while you are still arranging the replacement document.
- You do not need to solve every future visa, tour, or hotel issue before you know the embassy or consulate timing.
- You do not need to rebuild the full itinerary until tonight’s lodging, the next border crossing, and the next flight are under control.

## Important reassurance
This is disruptive, but it has a known official process. Once the passport is reported and the embassy or consulate has told you the replacement route, the next booking decisions can be made one at a time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilize the situation, reduce rushed mistakes, and keep travel options open. Later decisions about insurance claims, visa re-issues, extra costs, or returning home may need specialist help from the airline, insurer, embassy or consulate, or another qualified professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, insurance, or professional travel advice. Requirements can vary by country, airline, accommodation provider, insurer, and individual circumstances, so rely on official U.S. government instructions and written confirmations from the organizations handling your booking or claim.
