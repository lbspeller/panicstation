# What to do if… you see water or condensation inside an outdoor electrical box or near the meter after rain (USA)

## Short answer
Assume the equipment and any surrounding water or metal are energized: **evacuate the immediate area**, keep others at least 10 feet away, and **call your electric utility’s emergency line** to report a hazardous condition at the service entrance.

## Do not do these things
- Do not touch the meter box, conduit, or service cables, even if you are wearing gloves or using a tool.
- Do not open the enclosure, remove the meter, or break any utility seals.
- Do not stand in water, on damp grass, or on a wet ladder while looking at the equipment.
- Do not attempt to dry the equipment with a hair dryer, compressed air, or towels.
- Do not reset any circuit breaker that has tripped; re-energizing a water-damaged circuit can cause an immediate arc flash or fire.
- Do not assume "distilled" or "clean" rainwater is non-conductive; impurities on the equipment make all moisture a path for current.

## What to do now
1. **Move at least 10 feet away from the equipment and any standing water nearby.** Maintain this distance to protect against potential ground gradients or arc flash hazards if the equipment fails.
2. **Look and listen for signs of active electrical failure from your safe distance.** Watch for smoke, flames, or sparking (arcing), and listen for buzzing, crackling, or "sizzling" sounds.
3. **Call 911 immediately if you see fire, smoke, or active sparking.** Inform the dispatcher that there is a water-related electrical hazard at the utility meter.
4. **Call your electric utility's emergency or "wire down" number right away.** Report "water intrusion or heavy condensation inside the meter socket enclosure" so they can prioritize the dispatch of a field technician.
5. **Turn off power only from an internal, dry location if it is safe to do so.** You may switch off the main breaker in an indoor panel **only** if that panel is completely dry, you are standing on a dry surface, and the indoor equipment shows no signs of moisture or heat.
   - If the main disconnect is located outdoors near the wet meter, **do not** attempt to touch it.
6. **Prevent others from approaching the area.** Use cones, tape, or clear verbal warnings to keep family members, neighbors, and pets away from the drip line and the enclosure.
7. **Document the moisture from a distance using a phone camera.** A photo or video showing where the water is entering (e.g., through a top hub or along a cable) can help the technician or a Licensed Electrician diagnose the entry point without needing to get close while it is energized.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact leak source (such as a failed weather head or degraded gasket) before the utility arrives.
- You do not need to determine if the equipment needs replacement or just resealing; this requires a qualified inspection.
- You do not need to clean up any water or debris around the meter box.
- You do not need to worry about your electricity bill or meter reading during the hazard stabilization.

## Important reassurance
Feeling startled or concerned is a normal reaction to potential electrical hazards; water and electricity are a high-risk combination that requires professional intervention. Taking the step to stay back and call the utility is the most effective way to prevent injury and house fires.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Final safety clearance and repairs must be performed by the utility company or a Licensed Electrician as required by local codes and the National Electrical Code (NEC).

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and does not constitute professional electrical advice. Under 2026 safety standards, equipment that has been submerged or heavily contaminated by water must be inspected and often replaced by a qualified person to prevent long-term fire risks.
