# What to do if… you notice a person repeatedly positioning themselves to intercept you at doors or chokepoints (USA)

## Short answer
Do not proceed through the chokepoint; immediately change direction toward a populated, staffed, or well-lit public area. Call 911 if you are being physically blocked or feel in imminent danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t enter confined or isolated spaces such as elevators, stairwells, or narrow corridors if the person is positioned to follow or meet you there.
- Don’t attempt to "test" the person's intent by continuing toward the door to see if they move.
- Don’t go directly to your home, car, or a secluded side entrance.
- Don’t hold the door open for them (tailgating) or engage in "polite" social behaviors that reduce your distance.
- Don’t look down at your phone, keys, or bag while approaching the chokepoint.
- Don’t accept offers of "help" or directions that lead you toward less populated areas.

## What to do now
1. **Interrupt the path and create immediate distance.** **Stop before reaching the chokepoint and turn back toward the nearest open, staffed business or high-traffic public area.**
   - Change your route entirely rather than trying to "sneak" past.
   - Enter a store, lobby with security, or café where other people are present.
2. **Establish a safe observation point.** **Stand in a location where you have a clear exit behind you and can see the person without being cornered.**
   - Avoid dead-end hallways or deep corners of a room.
   - Maintain a "safe pause" to orient yourself and choose your next move.
3. **Notify staff or security immediately.** **Inform the nearest person in authority—such as a security guard, building manager, or store clerk—that you are being followed or intercepted.**
   - Use direct language: "I need assistance; a person is repeatedly positioning themselves to block my path at the doors."
   - Ask for an escort to your vehicle or a safe pickup point.
4. **Initiate an emergency call if you feel threatened.** **Dial 911 if the person makes a move to block your path, follows you into a building, or refuses to leave the chokepoint after you have changed direction.**
   - State your exact location and the specific behavior: "Someone is intercepting me at every exit."
   - If you cannot talk, keep the line open so the operator can hear the environment.
5. **Contact a trusted person to establish a safety line.** **Call a friend or family member and stay on the line with them until you have reached a secure, locked location or are with authorities.**
   - Tell them exactly where you are and provide a description of the person.
   - Set a check-in time for 10 minutes later.
6. **Drive to a police or fire station if you are followed to your vehicle.** **If you believe you are being shadowed while driving, do not go home; instead, drive directly to a well-lit public place or an emergency services station.**
   - Use the horn in short, rapid blasts to attract attention if the person approaches your car.
   - Keep all windows up and doors locked.
7. **Document the behavior for a pattern report.** **Once you are in a secure location, record the times, specific locations, and physical descriptions of the incidents.**
   - Contact the VictimConnect Resource Center at 855-484-2846 for guidance on stalking and safety planning in the USA.
   - Ask building management or store staff to preserve any relevant CCTV footage from that specific timeframe.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if this meets the legal definition of "stalking" before taking safety actions.
- You do not need to confront the person to "set boundaries" or ask why they are there.
- You do not need to worry about being "wrong" or overreacting to a coincidence.

## Important reassurance
Recognizing "interception" behavior early is a critical safety skill. Choosing to avoid a chokepoint and seeking a populated area is a standard, proactive response to suspicious patterns, and you are not required to prove the person's intent before prioritizing your safety.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate encounter. If this person is part of your daily routine or the behavior recurs, professional safety planning and a formal police report may be necessary.

## Important note
This information is for general safety awareness and does not constitute legal advice. Jurisdictional laws regarding stalking and harassment vary by state. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
