# What to do if… your family sharing or purchase sharing settings changed without your permission (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as possible unauthorized account access until you have checked it. Secure your Apple Account or Google Account first, then stop purchase sharing or shared-payment risk, and then review family members, devices, purchase history, and subscriptions.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use links from surprise emails or texts to verify, unlock, or fix the account; open the official app or type the official site yourself.
- Don’t remove people or devices blindly before changing the password, turning on two-factor authentication where available, and checking recovery details.
- Don’t leave a shared payment method active if unauthorized purchases are happening and your bank or card app lets you pause it.
- Don’t reuse the old password or a close variation of it.
- Don’t rush into accusations; secure access and save basic records first.

## What to do now
1. **Stop new spending risk first.**
   - If there are unauthorized charges, contact your card issuer or bank and ask about disputing the charges and blocking further use of the card.
   - If your banking app has a card lock or freeze, use it while you check the account.
   - In Apple or Google family settings, turn off purchase sharing, remove the family payment method, or tighten purchase approvals if those options are available to your role.

2. **Secure the Apple Account or Google Account that controls the family settings.**
   - Change the password to a new, unique password.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication or 2-step verification if it is not already on.
   - Use account security settings to sign out of other devices or sessions where the platform allows it.

3. **Remove ways someone could get back in. Do this from a device you trust.**
   - Check that recovery emails and phone numbers are yours.
   - Remove any recovery route you do not recognize.
   - Check the email account used for password resets for unexpected forwarding rules, filters, recovery emails, or recovery phone numbers.
   - Review devices with account access and sign out unknown devices.

4. **Run the platform security review.**
   - For Google, use Security Checkup and review recent security events, devices, and third-party access.
   - For Apple, review trusted devices, trusted phone numbers, and sign-in prompts in Apple Account settings.

5. **Review the family or purchase-sharing settings.**
   - For Apple, open Settings, tap your name, then Family. Check who is in the family group, who the organizer is, whether Purchase Sharing is on, and what shared payment method is shown.
   - For Google Play, open Google Play, tap the profile icon, then Settings, then Family. Check family members, Family Library settings, and purchase approval settings.
   - If you are the organizer or family manager, remove unknown members and restore purchase approval settings you trust.

6. **Save a small record before changing too much.**
   - Take screenshots of unfamiliar family members, changed purchase-sharing settings, unknown devices, unauthorized charges, and confirmation numbers.
   - Keep emails or notifications about family-setting changes and purchases.
   - Do not spend time building a full timeline right now; capture only what helps you explain the issue to the platform, bank, or a reporting site.

7. **Check purchase history and subscriptions once.**
   - Look for new subscriptions, free-trial conversions, in-app purchases, and shared purchases you do not recognize.
   - Cancel what you can from the official platform subscriptions area after the account is secure.
   - If a charge is already posted or pending, use your bank or card issuer’s dispute route instead of relying only on platform cancellation.

8. **Report it if it involved fraud or persistent unauthorized access.**
   - Use official Apple or Google support if you are locked out, the organizer or family-manager role changed, or access keeps coming back.
   - For cyber-enabled fraud or scams in the USA, you can file a report with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center.
   - Type reporting sites yourself. Avoid sponsored results and look-alike domains.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to permanently stop using family sharing.
- You do not need to audit every device in the household before securing the account and stopping spending.
- You do not need to confront anyone immediately if you suspect someone you know.
- You do not need to decide whether this was a platform mistake, family mistake, or account compromise before taking the safety steps above.

## Important reassurance
A family-sharing or purchase-sharing change can feel invasive because it touches both account access and money. A calm order helps: secure sign-in, remove recovery risks, stop shared-payment exposure, then check family members and purchases.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need official Apple or Google support, your bank or card issuer, a fraud report, or specialist cybersecurity help if access keeps recurring.
