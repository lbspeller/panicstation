# What to do if… officials contact you about a deceased person’s legal case and request urgent information (USA)

## Short answer
If someone is in immediate danger, call 911. Otherwise, do not share details during the unexpected contact: write down the basics, independently verify the office using a contact route you look up yourself, and ask the verified office to confirm what it needs and by when.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t share Social Security numbers, bank details, login codes, or payment information during an unexpected call, email, or message.
- Don’t treat caller ID, an email address, a badge number, or a case number as proof that the contact is genuine.
- Don’t call back using only a number supplied by the caller, and don’t follow links in an unexpected message.
- Don’t press buttons on a robocall.
- Don’t send an original or certified copy of a document until you have independently verified the office, the requested method, and whether a copy is acceptable.
- Don’t guess. Say when you are unsure and check records before replying.
- Don’t feel pressured to make a wider family, financial, or legal decision during the first contact.

## What to do now
1. **Pause before sharing details.** Say: “I need to verify the office before discussing details. I will contact the office using a route published on its official website.”
2. **Write down what was said.** Note the caller’s name, claimed office, callback details, case number, the requested family input, and any stated deadline. Treat caller-supplied contact details as notes only, not as your verification route.
3. **Verify the office independently.** Look up the court, prosecutor’s office, police department, or other claimed agency through its official website. Use a phone number or contact route published there and ask whether the person, case number, and request are genuine.
4. **Ask the verified office to restate the request.** Ask:
   - “What specific information or decision do you need?”
   - “Who needs to respond?”
   - “What is the deadline?”
   - “Can you send a written summary, or explain another verified way to confirm the request?”
5. **Ask what connection or authority is needed.** Family input is not always the same as acting for the deceased person’s estate. Ask whether the office needs a relative, a named family contact, an estate representative, or someone else, and what proof is required.
6. **Ask how to send any documents safely.** Confirm the exact documents needed, whether ordinary copies are acceptable, and the verified submission route. Keep a record of what you send and when.
7. **Share sensitive information only when necessary.** Before providing a sensitive identifier through a verified route, ask why it is needed and whether a less sensitive document or partial identifier is enough.
8. **Ask about support when relevant.** If the verified office says the matter concerns a criminal case and victim or witness support applies, ask whether it has a victim-services or victim-witness contact and what that contact can help with.
9. **Use a short script if you feel pressured.** Say: “I want to respond accurately. I am verifying the office and the request first. Please tell me the deadline and the published route I should use.”

## What can wait
- You don’t need to settle probate, family disagreements, or long-term legal strategy during the first contact.
- You don’t need to choose a single family point of contact immediately, although doing so later may reduce repeated calls and messages.
- You don’t need to send documents or answer detailed questions during the unexpected contact. Verify promptly, then ask the verified office to restate any deadline.

## Important reassurance
An urgent official-sounding request after a death can be disorienting. Pausing, checking the office independently, and asking for a clear verified request are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce pressure, prevent avoidable mistakes, and buy time. Later decisions may need help from a qualified attorney in the relevant state or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Procedures vary by state, office, and case type. If an unexpected caller asks for payment, gift cards, cryptocurrency, account access, or sensitive personal information, end the contact and verify the office independently.
