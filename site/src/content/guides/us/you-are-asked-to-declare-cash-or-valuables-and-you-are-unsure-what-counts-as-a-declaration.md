# What to do if… you are asked to declare cash or valuables and you are unsure what counts as a declaration (USA)

## Short answer
State that you are carrying currency or items and ask the officer to clarify if they require a Currency/Monetary Instrument Report (FinCEN 105) or a standard Customs Declaration (Form 6059B). For cash, you must report an aggregate total exceeding $10,000; for valuables, you must declare all new acquisitions or commercial goods.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t select “No” on any form or kiosk if you are carrying close to $10,000 in combined instruments.
- Don’t divide money between family members or separate bags to keep individual totals under $10,000; this is "structuring" and is a federal crime.
- Don’t assume "cash" only means physical paper bills; it includes signed checks, money orders, and travelers' checks.
- Don’t attempt to hide, discard, or move funds once the declaration process or questioning has begun.
- Don’t include standard bank account balances or credit card limits in your total, as these are not physical instruments.
- Don’t omit gold or silver bullion from your declaration, even though they are treated as merchandise rather than currency.

## What to do now
1. **Ask the officer to specify the type of declaration they are requesting.** **Clarify if they are asking about your "customs declaration" for goods and purchases or a "currency report" for monetary instruments.**
2. **Calculate the aggregate total of all physical currency and instruments you are carrying.** **U.S. law requires a report if the combined value of all cash, coins, and negotiable instruments exceeds $10,000 in U.S. dollars or foreign equivalent.**
   - Include U.S. and foreign coins and paper currency.
   - Include travelers' checks in any form.
   - Include money orders, cashier’s checks, and personal/business checks that are endorsed or in bearer form.
3. **Inform the officer immediately if your total is $10,000 or more.** **Tell the CBP officer "I have over $10,000 in currency and instruments to report" and request FinCEN Form 105.**
   - You can also file this electronically via the CBP FinCEN 105 e-filing system if you have mobile access.
4. **Identify all items acquired abroad that are currently in your possession.** **List all gifts, souvenirs, and personal purchases on your CBP Form 6059B or the electronic kiosk equivalent.**
   - Keep receipts accessible for high-value items like watches, electronics, or jewelry.
   - Distinguish clearly between items you previously owned and new acquisitions.
5. **Report any gold bullion or precious metals as merchandise.** **State that you are carrying bullion and provide the market value, as these are subject to customs declaration even if they do not count toward the $10,000 currency limit.**
6. **Provide short, factual answers without speculation.** **State "I am carrying exactly [Amount]" or "I have these receipts for these new items," rather than guessing or providing narrative explanations.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to calculate the exact exchange rate for foreign currency; CBP will use the official government rate for the date of entry.
- You do not need to determine the specific "duty-free" allowance yourself; the CBP officer will calculate any tax or duty owed after you declare the items.
- You do not need to prove the legal source of the funds immediately unless specifically asked for documentation by an officer.

## Important reassurance
It is common for travelers to feel uncertain about technical definitions like "monetary instruments" when under pressure. Identifying that you have items to discuss and asking for the correct form is a standard part of the border process and does not imply wrongdoing.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and reporting steps for individuals physically crossing the U.S. border. It does not cover the legal defense of seized assets or long-term tax implications of importing currency.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Failure to report currency over $10,000 can result in total seizure of the funds, civil penalties, and criminal prosecution. If in doubt, always disclose the items to a CBP officer.
