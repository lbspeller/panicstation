# What to do if… you receive a notice that your home may be entered to take property under legal authority (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the notice immediately with the issuing court and the executing agency (Sheriff or Constable) using independently sourced phone numbers. If an officer arrives, do not physically resist entry; prioritize documenting the interaction and requesting the specific court order.

## Do not do these things
- Don't pay any individual via wire transfer, cryptocurrency, or payment apps to "stop" the action; official agencies do not collect payments this way.
- Don't physically block a doorway or interfere with an officer's movements once they have declared entry.
- Don't sign any documents acknowledging debt or consenting to additional searches while under duress.
- Don't hide, sell, or destroy property listed in the notice, as this may lead to criminal "contempt of court" or "fraudulent transfer" charges.
- Don't rely on the phone number printed on the notice itself if you suspect it is a scam.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the type of authority claimed in the document.** 
   Determine if the paper is a "Writ of Execution" or "Notice of Levy" (civil debt collection), a "Writ of Possession" (eviction/foreclosure), or a "Search/Seizure Warrant" (criminal investigation). 
   - Note the case number, the name of the court, and the specific agency listed to perform the entry.

2. **Verify the document with the court and the executing agency immediately.** 
   **Contact the Clerk of Court and the Sheriff’s Civil Process Division using numbers from an official government website (.gov).** 
   - Ask if the warrant or writ is active and what specific actions it authorizes (e.g., "notice only" versus "immediate removal of property").

3. **Secure essential health and safety items in a portable bag.** 
   **Gather all prescription medications, medical devices, government-issued IDs, and essential legal documents (like birth certificates).** 
   - Keep these items with you or in a clear, visible location to ensure they are not accidentally moved or blocked during an entry.

4. **File for property exemptions if the notice is for a civil levy.** 
   **Locate your state's "Claim of Exemption" form through the local court's self-help website to protect "exempt" property like basic furniture, tools of trade, or a portion of equity.** 
   - Be aware that deadlines for claiming exemptions are often very short, sometimes as little as 10 days from the date the notice was served.

5. **Maintain a safe distance and observe if officers arrive at the premises.** 
   **Step back from the door, keep your hands visible, and request to see the signed court order through a window or after the door is opened.** 
   - Use a notepad or phone to record the names and badge numbers of all personnel present.

6. **Request a detailed inventory receipt for any property removed.** 
   **Ask the executing officer for a written list of every item taken or "tagged" for seizure before they leave the property.** 
   - Ensure the receipt includes information on where the property will be stored and the process for contesting the seizure.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle the underlying debt or judgment on your doorstep.
- You do not need to provide a statement about your finances or a criminal case in the moment.
- You do not need to hire a private attorney before verifying the notice, though you should seek legal advice as soon as the immediate situation is stabilized.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a notice is a procedural step and does not always result in an immediate forced entry. Many civil processes allow for a "stay" or a "hearing" if you act within the statutory timeframe. Taking these steps to verify and document the situation helps protect your rights regardless of the outcome.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization, verification, and safety procedures. It does not provide legal strategy for bankruptcy, long-term debt defense, or criminal trial representation.

## Important note
Legal procedures for property seizure vary significantly by state and county. This information is for general guidance and does not replace the advice of a qualified attorney or the specific instructions provided by a court. Under recent 2026 rulings (e.g., *Case v. Montana*), law enforcement may enter without a warrant if they have an objectively reasonable basis to believe someone inside requires emergency aid.
