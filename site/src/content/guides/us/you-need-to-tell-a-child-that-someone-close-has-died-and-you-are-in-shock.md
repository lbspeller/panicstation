# What to do if… you need to tell a child that someone close has died and you are in shock (USA)

## Short answer
In the calmest safe moment you can create, tell the child plainly: “[Name] died.” Use literal language, prioritize their immediate safety, and involve another adult to speak or support you if your shock makes it difficult to function.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use euphemisms like “went to sleep,” “lost,” “passed away,” or “went on a journey,” which cause literal-minded children to fear sleep or expect a return.
- Do not provide graphic details or speculative causes while in the initial state of shock.
- Do not make promises you cannot keep, such as “nothing else will change” or “we will never be sad again.”
- Do not allow the child to learn the news from social media, news broadcasts, or overheard adult phone calls.
- Do not force a long, single conversation if the child is only ready for one or two sentences.

## What to do now
1. **Secure a support person immediately.**  
   - Call or text a trusted adult to come over or stay on speakerphone. 
   - If you are shaking, dissociating, or unable to speak, this person can be the one to deliver the news while you provide physical comfort.
2. **Move to a quiet, familiar space and sit down.**  
   - Stay at the child’s eye level. 
   - Use grounding techniques like holding a heavy pillow or placing your feet flat on the floor to stabilize your body.
3. **Deliver the core news in one clear sentence.**  
   - Say: “I have very sad news to tell you. **[Name] died.**”  
   - Stop and wait. Let the child process the words before saying anything else.
4. **Define "died" using concrete biological terms.**  
   - Explain: “Dying means the body has stopped working. They cannot breathe, eat, talk, or move anymore.”
   - Clarify that they are not in pain and cannot feel cold or hunger.
5. **Directly address the two most common childhood fears.**  
   - Say: “**You did not cause this. It is not your fault.**”
   - Say: “**You are safe, and I am here with you.**”
6. **Answer "how" questions simply or admit you do not know.**  
   - If the cause is known: “They died because their heart stopped working” or “They were in a very bad accident.”
   - If unknown: “I do not know exactly what happened yet. When I have more information, I will tell you.”
7. **Transition to immediate "safety mode" if your shock worsens.**  
   - If you feel you may faint, experience chest pain, or cannot think clearly enough to supervise the child, call **911**. 
   - If you need emotional crisis support for yourself to get through the next hour, call or text **988** (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline).
8. **Establish the plan for the next hour.**  
   - Tell the child exactly where they will be (e.g., “We are staying here in the living room”).
   - Provide a comfort object (blanket, toy) or a low-stimulation activity like drawing.
   - Maintain a basic anchor like a scheduled snack or bedtime routine.
9. **Notify the child’s school or childcare provider.**  
   - Ask for a school counselor or social worker to be notified.
   - Request that they monitor the child for distress and contact you immediately if needed.
10. **Set one professional follow-up task.**  
    - Plan to call the pediatrician or a local bereavement center tomorrow for guidance on supporting the child’s specific age group.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on funeral arrangements, viewings, or memorials right now.
- You do not need to explain the full medical history or legal details of the death today.
- You do not need to manage the grief or reactions of other extended family members.
- You do not need to have a "meaningful" talk about feelings if the child just wants to play or be quiet.

## Important reassurance
Shock can make you feel numb, disconnected, or physically clumsy. This is a normal biological response to trauma. Children often "puddle jump" in their grief—they may be intensely upset for a minute and then ask to play or watch a show. This does not mean they didn't understand; it is how their brains process overwhelming information in small doses.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance for the initial disclosure and immediate safety. Long-term grief support should be coordinated through pediatricians, school professionals, or specialized bereavement counselors.

## Important note
This guide provides general procedural information and is not a substitute for professional medical, mental health, or legal advice. If you are in immediate physical danger or experiencing a medical emergency, call 911. If you are unable to care for a child due to the severity of your shock, involve another adult or contact emergency services immediately.
