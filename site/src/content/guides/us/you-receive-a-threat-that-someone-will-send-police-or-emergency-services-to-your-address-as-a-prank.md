# What to do if… you receive a threat that someone will send police or emergency services to your address as a prank (USA)

## Short answer
Call **911** immediately to report the threat of a "swatting" or hoax call, ensuring the dispatch center for your address is aware that a false report is imminent.

## Do not do these things
- Do not engage with the person making the threat, as this may encourage them to escalate the hoax.
- Do not delete any messages, handles, or evidence related to the threat, even if they are distressing.
- Do not exit your home with objects in your hands if you see police arriving.
- Do not run toward responding officers to explain the situation.
- Do not assume the police already know it is a prank.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 immediately to report that you have received a specific threat of a "swatting" or hoax emergency call.** **Explain clearly that you are the resident at [your address] and that you believe a false report involving violence or an emergency is about to be made to your location.**
   - Ask the dispatcher to place a "premise alert" or a note on your address in the Computer-Aided Dispatch (CAD) system.
   - Provide your name and a callback number where you can be reached if a call comes in.
2. **Preserve all digital evidence of the threat without responding to the sender.** **Take screenshots that include the platform name, the sender’s username or handle, the timestamp, and the full text of the threat.**
   - Save any links to profiles or posts involved in the harassment.
   - Do not block the user until after you have secured the evidence, as blocking may sometimes hide the message history.
3. **Notify everyone currently inside the household about the potential for a police response.** **Tell them to remain calm and, if officers arrive, to keep their hands empty and visible at all times.**
   - Ensure pets are secured in a room or crate to prevent them from interfering with a tactical response.
4. **Prepare for a tactical entry even if you have notified dispatch.** **If officers arrive at the door or surround the house, follow all verbal commands immediately and do not reach for your phone or any other object.**
   - Once the situation is stable, say: "I am the resident. I called 911 earlier to report a swatting threat. I believe this is a false report."
5. **Move away from windows and doors to a central, internal part of the home.** **This reduces the risk of accidental injury if officers use force to enter or if they are observing the home from the exterior.**
6. **If you see officers arriving on your property, call 911 again if you are safely able to do so.** **Tell the dispatcher: "Officers are at my address now. This is the resident. I am inside and I am unarmed. Please notify the units on scene that this is likely a swatting incident."**
7. **Contact property management or security if you live in a multi-unit building or gated community.** **Inform them that you have received a threat and have already notified the police, so they are not caught off guard by a large emergency response.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the identity of the person threatening you right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to file a formal police report or pursue charges in this moment.
- You do not need to change your social media privacy settings or delete your accounts immediately.
- You do not need to search for "anti-swatting registries" until the immediate window of threat has passed.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel an immediate spike in heart rate or a sense of panic when threatened with a police response. These threats are designed to cause maximum stress and confusion. By notifying dispatch ahead of time, you have taken the most effective step to ensure that responding officers have the information they need to approach the situation safely.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active threat of a hoax emergency call. It does not cover long-term cyber-investigation, legal remedies, or digital privacy overhaul.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Laws regarding swatting and emergency reporting vary by state. Always prioritize the instructions of on-scene law enforcement officers over any written guide.
