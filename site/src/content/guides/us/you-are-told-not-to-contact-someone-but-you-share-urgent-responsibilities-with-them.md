# What to do if… you are told not to contact someone but you share urgent responsibilities with them (USA)

## Short answer
Do not contact the person while you check the exact written terms, including through someone else unless the terms clearly allow it. Get the written restriction and ask for a permitted way to handle urgent shared responsibilities.

## Do not do these things
- Do not call, text, email, message, or send a social-media message while the restriction is unclear.
- Do not ask a friend, relative, child, co-worker, or other person to pass on a message unless the written terms clearly allow it.
- Do not reply just because the other person contacted you first.
- Do not arrange an in-person meeting or attend a location contrary to the written terms.
- Do not discuss the investigation with the person or try to compare accounts.
- Do not assume that practical topics such as childcare, bills, housing, or property are automatically exempt.

## What to do now
1. **Get the exact written terms.**  
   Find the order, release paperwork, supervision rule, or written investigation directive. Identify who issued it and check whether it mentions direct contact, indirect contact, third-party messages, social media, locations, distance, or shared property.

2. **Ask which route applies before sending any message.**  
   Contact your lawyer or public defender if you have one. If the restriction is part of supervision or a non-court investigation, ask the supervising officer or issuing contact what the written terms allow. A court order may need a formal request to the court before any different arrangement is permitted.

3. **List only the responsibilities that cannot safely wait.**  
   Write down urgent childcare arrangements, medication access, essential property, housing access, utility deadlines, shared-account issues, or work handoffs. Keep the list factual and separate from the investigation.

4. **Ask for a written logistics plan.**  
   Ask the appropriate person how urgent responsibilities can be handled without prohibited contact. The permitted route may involve lawyers, an authorized third person, a written channel, or a planned handoff, but do not use any route until you have checked that it is allowed.

5. **Keep a copy if the person contacts you.**  
   Do not reply unless you have been told that replying is permitted. Save the message, note the date and time, and promptly ask your lawyer, supervising officer, or issuing contact what to do.

6. **Use emergency services for immediate danger.**  
   If someone is in immediate danger, call 911. Do not rely on direct contact with the restricted person as the emergency plan.

## What can wait
- You do not need to solve the relationship, housing arrangement, or long-term co-parenting plan now.
- You do not need to answer non-urgent messages.
- You do not need to settle every shared bill or property issue today.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to challenge the restriction.

## Important reassurance
Shared responsibilities can make a contact restriction feel difficult to manage. Focus only on avoiding unclear contact, getting the written terms, and asking for a permitted route for the few things that cannot wait.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about court requests, family arrangements, housing, employment, or the investigation may need help from a qualified lawyer or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or other professional advice. Restrictions and procedures vary by the exact written terms and the authority that issued them. When the terms are unclear, ask before making contact.
