# What to do if… you suspect your phone or cloud account contains intimate photos that someone else can access (USA)

## Short answer
From a safer pause, secure your primary cloud account immediately by changing your password and signing out all other devices, then use hashing tools like StopNCII or Take It Down to help prevent images from being shared on major platforms.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront the person you suspect right now as this can lead to immediate escalation or the sharing of the images.
- Don’t delete the account or the images in a panic because you may need that evidence later and deleting them from your device does not remove copies already on someone else's server.
- Don’t click password-reset links sent to your phone or email if you didn’t request them; always go directly to the official service website.
- Don’t use a device you believe is being physically monitored to perform security resets.
- Don’t pay or fulfill demands if you are being threatened with "sextortion," as compliance usually results in further demands rather than the deletion of images.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause and use a device the other person cannot access.** **If you are in immediate physical danger, call 911.** If you suspect your current phone or computer is being monitored via "stalkerware" or shared account access, use a trusted device (like a friend's phone or a library computer) to perform security updates.

2. **Secure your primary cloud account (Apple Account or Google Account) immediately.** **Change your password to something long, unique, and never used before.** Once the password is changed, use the "Sign out of all devices" or "Remove devices" feature in your account settings to kick off any unauthorized users.

3. **Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) using an authenticator app rather than SMS.** **MFA adds a critical layer of protection that requires a secondary code to log in.** Phishing-resistant methods like hardware security keys or authenticator apps are safer than text messages, which can sometimes be intercepted if the person has access to your cellular account.

4. **Review and update your account recovery information.** **Check that the recovery email and phone number listed are ones only you can access.** It is common for perpetrators to change these settings so they can regain access after you change your password.

5. **Audit your "quiet" sharing settings and revoke all external access.** **Look specifically for Shared Libraries, Shared Albums, and "Family Sharing" features that may be syncing your photos to another person's device.** Turn off "Partner Sharing" in Google Photos or "Shared Library" in iCloud if they are enabled.

6. **Use StopNCII.org to prevent the sharing of intimate images if you are 18 or older.** **This tool creates a digital "hash" (a unique fingerprint) of your photos on your own device so they can be blocked by participating platforms.** The actual images are never uploaded to StopNCII; only the digital fingerprint is shared with platforms like Facebook, Instagram, and TikTok to stop them from being posted.

7. **Use NCMEC's "Take It Down" service if the person in the images was under 18 at the time they were taken.** **This service is specifically designed to remove or prevent the sharing of child sexual abuse material (CSAM) and imagery of minors.** You can use this service even if you are now over 18, as long as you were a minor when the content was created.

8. **Preserve evidence of threats without keeping the explicit images on your device.** **Take screenshots of messages, emails, or social media posts where someone is threatening you or claiming they have access to your photos.** Keep a record of usernames, timestamps, and URLs, as these are necessary if you choose to involve law enforcement or report to platforms later.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to file a police report.
- You do not need to notify everyone in your contact list about the potential leak today.
- You do not need to identify exactly "how" they got access before you take steps to lock them out.
- You do not need to contact every social media platform manually if you have used hashing tools like StopNCII.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed or ashamed in this situation, but it is important to remember that this is not your fault. Technology-enabled abuse is a serious issue, and there are established legal and technical systems designed to help you regain control. Securing your accounts is the first and most effective step toward reducing further harm.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for securing accounts and utilizing NCII prevention tools. It does not cover long-term legal strategies, forensic device analysis, or psychological counseling.

## Important note
As of May 20, 2026, the TAKE IT DOWN Act requires covered online platforms to remove non-consensual intimate imagery within 48 hours of a valid request. If images are shared, you have a federal right to demand their removal. This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal advice.
