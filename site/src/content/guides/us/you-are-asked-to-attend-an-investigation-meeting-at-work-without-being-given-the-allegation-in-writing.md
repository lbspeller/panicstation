# What to do if… you are asked to attend an investigation meeting at work without being given the allegation in writing (USA)

## Short answer
Do not use personal funds for a business purchase until you have obtained written authorization and a specific timeline for reimbursement.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use a personal credit card if you cannot carry the balance without interest until the reimbursement arrives.
- Do not rely on verbal promises or informal assurances that the company will "make it right" later.
- Do not provide personal payment information to a vendor without first confirming the company will not pay the vendor directly.
- Do not exceed any verbal or written spending limits, as the excess may be considered a personal gift to the company.
- Do not combine personal items and work supplies on a single receipt.

## What to do now
1. **Request written authorization before making any purchase.** Ensure the email or message includes the specific items approved, the maximum allowable total spend (including tax and shipping), and the name of the approving manager.
2. **Confirm the specific reimbursement method and timeline.** Ask whether the payment will be made via the standard payroll cycle, a separate check, or a specialized expense software system.
   - Note that for tax-free status under an IRS accountable plan, you must substantiate expenses with receipts within 60 days.
3. **Propose an alternative company payment method.** Ask for a corporate card, a purchase order, or for the vendor to send an invoice directly to the finance department for payment.
4. **Notify the requester if you are unable to front the cost.** State clearly that you do not have the personal liquid funds available to cover the expense and ask for a cash advance or a company-issued payment tool.
5. **Collect and save an itemized receipt immediately if the purchase is made.** Ensure the receipt shows the date, the vendor name, the items purchased, and the total amount paid.
6. **Submit the reimbursement request within 24 hours of the purchase.** Attaching the written approval to your submission helps avoid delays in processing or questioning by the accounting department.
7. **Verify if your state has mandatory reimbursement laws.** If you work in California, Illinois, Montana, New Hampshire, North Dakota, South Dakota, or Iowa, your employer may have strict statutory obligations to cover "necessary" business expenses.
   - In California, Labor Code 2802 requires full indemnification for all necessary expenditures.
   - In Illinois, the Wage Payment and Collection Act requires reimbursement within 30 days of submission.
8. **Document the impact on your net pay if you are an hourly worker.** If unreimbursed expenses bring your effective hourly rate below the federal minimum wage ($7.25) or your state's minimum wage, this may constitute a wage violation under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA).

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if this practice is a sign of company-wide financial instability today.
- You do not need to file a formal wage claim with the Department of Labor until you have exhausted the internal reimbursement process.
- You do not need to negotiate a permanent update to the company’s expense policy during the immediate purchase request.
- You do not need to decide if you will refuse all future out-of-pocket requests right now.

## Important reassurance
It is standard business practice to require a clear paper trail for expenditures. Asking for a written approval and a reimbursement schedule is a professional administrative step, not a lack of trust or a refusal to help. Most payroll and finance departments prefer these records to ensure their own compliance with tax and audit rules.

## Scope note
This guide focuses on the immediate steps to protect your personal finances when a work-related expense is requested. It does not cover long-term legal strategies for wage theft or independent contractor misclassification.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Laws regarding employee reimbursement vary significantly by state and employment contract. If you are being pressured to spend significant sums or if your employer refuses to reimburse legitimate expenses, consult with a qualified employment attorney or your state’s Department of Labor.
