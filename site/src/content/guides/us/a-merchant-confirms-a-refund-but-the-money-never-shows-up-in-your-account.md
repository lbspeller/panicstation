# What to do if… a merchant confirms a refund but the money never shows up in your account (USA)

## Short answer
Request the Acquirer Reference Number (ARN) from the merchant and submit a formal written "Billing Error" dispute to your card issuer if the credit does not appear within 15 days.

## Do not do these things
- Don't assume a refund is lost before 15 days have passed; banking settlement cycles often require 3 to 10 business days.
- Don't rely solely on phone calls to the bank; federal protections under the Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA) require a written notice to preserve legal rights.
- Don't wait longer than 60 days from the date of the first statement that should have shown the credit.
- Don't close the bank account or credit card while the refund is in transit if possible.
- Don't stop making minimum payments on a credit card bill while waiting for a refund to post.

## What to do now
1. **Gather the merchant's refund confirmation details immediately.**
   - Document the refund amount, date of transaction, and the last four digits of the payment method used.
2. **Request the Acquirer Reference Number (ARN) or Trace ID from the merchant's support team.**
   - This 23-digit number (for Visa/Mastercard) allows your bank to manually locate the funds in the banking network.
3. **Verify the status of the original payment method with your financial institution.**
   - Ask the bank if there are any "pending credits" or "rejected inbound transfers" associated with your account.
4. **Wait a full 15 days from the date the merchant initiated the refund before filing a formal dispute.**
   - Most card networks require this buffer to allow for standard processing delays between the merchant's bank (acquirer) and your bank (issuer).
5. **Send a formal written "Billing Error" notice to your credit card issuer if the 15-day window expires.**
   - Address the letter to the specific "dispute" or "billing inquiry" address listed on your statement, not the payment address.
   - Include the merchant confirmation, the ARN (if provided), and a clear statement that the credit has failed to post.
6. **Report the error to your bank if the missing refund is on a debit card or ACH transfer.**
   - Under Regulation E, you must notify the bank of the error; the bank may require written confirmation within 10 business days of your initial report.
   - Ask the bank if you are eligible for "provisional credit" while they investigate the missing funds.
7. **Submit a complaint to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) if the bank refuses to investigate.**
   - Provide your reference numbers and a timeline of your contacts with the merchant and the bank.

## What can wait
- You do not need to contact an attorney or file a small claims suit in the first 30 days.
- You do not need to "prove" the merchant made a mistake beyond providing their own confirmation receipt.
- You do not need to worry about "pending" authorizations on your app; these often disappear and are replaced by the final credit within one billing cycle.

## Important reassurance
Refund delays are frequently caused by technical "mismatches" in the banking back-end, such as a card being re-issued with a new number or a settlement window being missed. These funds are rarely "lost" permanently once an ARN or Trace ID exists; providing that number to a bank supervisor usually resolves the issue.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for missing credits. It does not cover disputes for "quality of goods" or merchant refusals to refund; it only applies when a refund has been confirmed by the merchant but has not appeared in the user's account.

## Important note
Federal consumer protections (Regulation Z for credit and Regulation E for debit) have strict 60-day deadlines. Failure to submit a written dispute within this window can result in a permanent loss of dispute rights.
