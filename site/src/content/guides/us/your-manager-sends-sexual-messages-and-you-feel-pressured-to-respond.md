# What to do if… your manager sends sexual messages and you feel pressured to respond (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause and stop answering sexual messages if that feels safer. You can choose a trusted support person or workplace contact when you are ready.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel that you have to answer sexual messages to protect your job.
- Do not send sexual content because you feel cornered or rushed.
- Do not meet the manager alone to discuss the messages if you feel unsafe or intimidated.
- Do not delete messages in a panic.
- Do not feel that you have to confront the manager, report to the police, or make a major job decision immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Move to a place where you feel steadier. If the messages are arriving repeatedly, mute notifications for a while if you can do so without missing urgent work information.
2. **Stop engaging with the sexual content.** You do not have to reply. If you want to send a boundary message and it feels safe, keep it brief, such as: “Please keep messages work-related.”
3. **Keep the messages if it feels safe.** You may securely save the original messages or screenshots without sharing them widely.
4. **Choose one person to contact when you are ready.** This could be a trusted support person or a workplace contact listed in your employer’s policy, such as HR, an ethics line, or a different manager. If the usual contact feels unsafe or too close to the manager, choose another listed route.
5. **Ask for a practical boundary.** You may ask for work-only communication, another person to attend meetings, or a different contact for urgent work matters while the situation is being addressed.
6. **If you are a federal employee or applicant, contact your agency’s EEO Counselor promptly.** The EEOC says this generally needs to happen within 45 days of the alleged discrimination.
7. **Use emergency help for immediate danger.** If you are in immediate danger or need immediate police, fire, or ambulance assistance, call **911**. If the situation includes sexual assault or abuse, or you want specialist sexual-violence support, you may contact RAINN.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to leave your job, hire a lawyer, file an external charge, or confront the manager.
- You do not need to write a perfect account of what happened.
- You do not need to choose a label for the messages before asking for support.
- External filing time limits may apply, so check the EEOC information promptly once you have a safe pause.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressured, frozen, or unsure is understandable when the person messaging you has power at work. You are allowed to pause, avoid sexual replies, ask for practical boundaries, and choose your next step without being rushed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on your workplace, your state, whether you are a federal employee or applicant, and what support you want. Specialist help may be useful for those later decisions.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you need immediate police, fire, or ambulance assistance, call **911**.
