# What to do if… your bank freezes your account and you cannot access your money (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately via their official, verified phone number to identify the reason for the freeze, then reroute upcoming income to a secondary account to ensure you can pay for essential needs.

## Do not do these things
- Don't call numbers or click links in text messages or emails claiming your account is frozen — always use the number on the back of your physical debit card.
- Don't pay any third party a "fee" or "service charge" to unfreeze your account; legitimate banks do not charge fees to resolve a security hold.
- Don't attempt to "test" the freeze by repeatedly trying to withdraw money or swipe your card, as this can trigger additional fraud alerts.
- Don't wait to reroute your direct deposit; once a paycheck hits a frozen account, it may become inaccessible for weeks.
- Don't ignore mail from the IRS or a local court, even if it looks like junk; these documents contain the deadlines required to stop a permanent seizure of funds.

## What to do now
1. **Identify your most critical financial obligations for the next 72 hours.**
   - List essential costs like food, life-saving medication, emergency childcare, and immediate housing payments.
   - Use this list to explain "immediate economic hardship" when speaking to the bank or government agencies.
2. **Call your bank's official customer service line and request a specific reason for the freeze.**
   - Ask if the hold is due to **security/fraud verification**, an **internal compliance review**, or a **legal order** (such as a garnishment or IRS levy).
   - If it is a legal order, request the **case number**, the **name of the court or agency**, and the **date the order was received**.
   - Ask for the bank's **fax number**, as you may need to have release documents sent directly to them.
3. **Open a new account at a different financial institution immediately and move your direct deposit.**
   - Do not use a "sister bank" or another account at the same company, as the freeze may extend across all accounts under your Social Security Number.
   - Contact your employer or the Social Security Administration to reroute your next payment to the new account or a physical check.
4. **Determine if the frozen funds include protected federal benefits.**
   - If you receive Social Security, SSI, VA benefits, or certain other federal payments via direct deposit, federal law (31 CFR Part 212) generally requires banks to leave a "protected amount" accessible.
   - This "protected amount" is usually the sum of all federal benefits deposited in the last two months; tell the bank you have "exempt federal benefits" and ask them to perform the required "lookback review" immediately.
5. **Request a hardship release if the freeze is due to an IRS levy.**
   - If the IRS has frozen your account, you typically have a **21-day window** before the bank sends the money to the IRS.
   - Call the IRS at the number on your notice and explain that the levy is causing "immediate economic hardship" (meaning you cannot pay for basic living expenses); have your bank's fax number ready to receive a release order.
6. **File an official complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) if the bank is unresponsive.**
   - If the bank cannot explain the freeze or refuses to process a legitimate identity verification, submit a complaint through the CFPB website.
   - Banks are generally required to respond to CFPB-channeled complaints within 15 days.

## What can wait
- Closing the frozen account permanently.
- Requesting a formal refund of overdraft or late fees caused by the freeze (save this until access is restored).
- Investigating who initiated a private lawsuit/garnishment (focus on the bank's "protected amount" first).

## Important reassurance
A bank freeze is a procedural stop, not a final loss of your money. It is a standard response to suspected fraud or legal notices designed to preserve funds until a specific step (like identity verification or a court response) is completed. Your reaction of urgency is normal because your access to life essentials is blocked; focusing on rerouting income will reduce the immediate pressure while the bank processes the hold.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for account freezes in the United States. It does not cover long-term debt negotiation, legal defense for lawsuits, or tax resolution strategies.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal or financial advice. Banking regulations regarding freezes can vary by state and by the specific nature of the investigation (such as Anti-Money Laundering reviews). Always verify the identity of anyone you speak with regarding your financial accounts.
