# What to do if… your bank freezes your account and you cannot access your money (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank through an official channel to confirm why the account is frozen and what will lift the hold. At the same time, protect the next few days by finding another route for income, food, medication, transport, and essential payments.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t call numbers or click links from unexpected messages about the freeze. Use the number on your card, the bank’s app, or the bank’s official website.
- Don’t pay anyone a “fee” to unfreeze your account or move money through someone else’s account.
- Don’t keep sending paychecks or benefits into the frozen account until you have asked whether new deposits will be accessible.
- Don’t ignore paperwork that mentions a court, a creditor, or the IRS. It may have time-sensitive instructions or response options.
- Don’t rush into high-fee “instant cash” products before you know the cause of the freeze.

## What to do now
1. **Write down what must be covered in the next 48 hours.** Include rent, utilities, food, transportation, medication, childcare, and any time-sensitive bills. This gives you a short list to work from and helps you explain hardship clearly.

2. **Confirm the freeze with your bank using official contact only.** Ask:
   - Is this a fraud or identity verification hold, an internal risk or compliance review, or a legal order such as a garnishment or levy?
   - If there is a legal order, what is the name of the order, who issued it, and what date was it served on the bank?
   - Are new deposits still accepted, and will they be accessible?
   - What exact steps or documents are required to lift the hold?
   - Can the bank make any funds available for urgent essentials while the review is pending?
   Get a case or reference number and ask for a written summary by secure message or mail.

3. **Complete the safest verification step the bank offers.** If identity verification is required, ask whether a branch visit with government ID, a secure upload, or another official process is fastest. Keep copies of what you submit and note the date, time, and name or department you spoke with.

4. **Try to set up a backup money route now.**
   - Try opening an account at a different bank or credit union.
   - Ask your employer, benefits payer, or other income source how quickly direct deposit can be changed.
   - If a new account is not immediately available, ask about a paper check, payroll card, prepaid account, or another official payment method.
   - Update essential autopays only after you have a working payment method, so you do not create more declined payments.

5. **If this is a garnishment or levy, check whether federal benefits are involved.** If you receive direct-deposited federal benefits such as Social Security, SSI, VA benefits, federal civil service retirement, or railroad retirement, banks generally must review the account and protect a calculated amount in many garnishment situations. Ask the bank:
   - whether it performed the required account review,
   - what protected amount, if any, was left accessible,
   - what amount remains frozen above the protected amount,
   - and whether a notice has been sent to you.
   Keep statements showing the benefit deposits and document any transfers you make while this is being sorted out.

6. **If it is an IRS levy, contact the IRS using the number on the levy or IRS correspondence.** Ask about a levy release, including release based on immediate economic hardship. If the IRS denies a release, ask what appeal or return-of-property options are available for your situation.

7. **If the bank is not resolving the issue, file a complaint through an official route.**
   - Start with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau if you are unsure where to complain.
   - If you know the institution’s regulator, you can also use the relevant bank or credit union complaint process: OCC for many national banks, FDIC for many state nonmember banks, the Federal Reserve for some state member banks, and NCUA for federal credit unions.
   - Include the dates, account type, reference number, what the bank told you, what money is blocked, and what urgent harm this is causing.

8. **If identity theft or a scam may have triggered the freeze, contain the risk.**
   - Change your banking and email passwords from a trusted device.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication.
   - Review recent transactions, payees, linked devices, and contact details.
   - Use official identity theft recovery steps and keep a record of any report number.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to close accounts or switch banks long-term.
- Negotiating compensation until you have stable access to money and the bank’s explanation in writing.
- Moving savings or selling assets unless you need money for immediate essentials.
- Arguing every detail with the bank before you know the stated reason for the freeze.

## Important reassurance
This can feel frightening because it blocks normal life. Many freezes involve identity checks, fraud concerns, compliance review, or a legal order, and the next stabilising move is the same: get the reason in writing, complete official verification, and route new money somewhere you can use it.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce harm and buy time. If the freeze involves a levy, garnishment, debt collection, identity theft, or disputed bank conduct, later decisions may need help from a qualified legal, tax, financial, or consumer-protection specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, tax, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Banks may limit what they can explain during certain investigations. Be cautious of anyone claiming they can “unlock” your funds quickly; use official channels only.
