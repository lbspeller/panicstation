# What to do if… you are contacted by a regulator about your work and are unsure what to say (USA)
## Short answer
Pause before giving a detailed answer if you can do so without missing a deadline or obstructing lawful process. Verify the contact independently, find out whether the request is voluntary or compulsory, and get appropriate legal or compliance support.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess, speculate, or fill gaps in your memory.
- Do not treat an unexpected call, email, link, or attachment as genuine until you have verified the sender independently.
- Do not delete, alter, backdate, rename, or clean up work records, messages, files, or logs.
- Do not copy, remove, or send restricted work material to yourself unless an authorised person or your attorney tells you to do so.
- Do not rush into signing a statement, certification, or timeline before reading it carefully and seeking advice where time allows.
- Do not provide passwords, personal-device access, or private-account access solely because of an informal request.
- Do not ignore a subpoena, civil investigative demand, written deadline, warrant, inspection request, or other formal document.
- Do not discuss the situation broadly with coworkers or post about it online.
- Do not obstruct lawful process or interfere with a lawful inspection or search.

## What to do now
1. **Record the basic details.** Write down the person’s name, office, regulator, contact details, reference number, what they asked for, and any stated deadline. Keep the original email, voicemail, letter, or business card.
2. **Verify the contact independently.** Start with the USAGov federal agency index, the USAGov state government directory, or another official government page you found independently. Use a contact route from that source, not one supplied only in the unexpected message.
3. **Ask what kind of contact this is.** Ask whether the request is voluntary or compulsory and whether any deadline applies. Ask for the request and any stated authority in writing where possible. A formal process may include a subpoena, civil investigative demand, inspection request, or another official document.
4. **Use a brief holding response.** Say: “I want to respond accurately. Please send the request and any deadline in writing where possible. I am going to verify the contact and get advice before giving a detailed response.”
5. **Preserve existing records.** Do not delete or change relevant emails, chats, notes, drafts, files, or logs. If automatic deletion may affect records, ask an authorised legal, compliance, records, or IT contact how to handle it. Do not change systems yourself unless you are authorised to do so.
6. **Choose the right support route.** If it is appropriate and safe, notify your employer’s designated legal, compliance, or reporting contact. If the issue may involve your manager, your employer, your professional license, or your own conduct, consider independent legal advice before sharing details broadly.
7. **Ask whom any lawyer represents.** Employer counsel may represent the employer rather than you personally. Ask directly whether the lawyer represents you, the employer, or both, and ask about possible conflicts before relying on that advice.
8. **Treat formal documents as time-sensitive.** Note any deadline and contact an appropriate attorney or authorised internal contact promptly. Share a copy where permitted. If a document authorises immediate action, such as a workplace inspection or search, do not interfere; ask for the designated contact or legal support and follow lawful directions.
9. **If an interview is requested, clarify the rules before agreeing where possible.** Ask whether the interview is voluntary, whether it can be scheduled after you obtain advice, and whether counsel may attend. Do not assume every regulator or type of interview follows the same rules.
10. **If someone arrives at the workplace, stay professional and do not interfere.** Ask for their name, office, purpose, and any official document if appropriate. Contact the designated legal, compliance, or management route promptly and follow lawful directions while support is contacted.

## What can wait
- You do not need to tell your whole side immediately.
- You do not need to decide whether anyone is at fault today.
- You do not need to resign, confront coworkers, or send a broad internal message now.
- You do not need to answer informal follow-up questions in real time.
- You do not need to provide extra material beyond the request before the scope has been clarified.

## Important reassurance
A regulator contacting you does not by itself establish wrongdoing or tell you whether you are a witness, an information source, or the focus of an inquiry. Taking time to verify the contact, preserve records, clarify the request, and obtain advice is a careful response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on the regulator, the type of request, your role, your employer’s procedures, and advice from a qualified attorney or other appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Do not obstruct lawful process. Seek qualified legal advice promptly if you receive a formal demand, face a deadline, or are concerned about personal exposure.
