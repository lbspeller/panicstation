# What to do if… you are abroad and cannot get a police report for a stolen document (USA)
## Short answer
Ask the local police or the locally appropriate reporting office for an incident number and any written confirmation they can provide. If the stolen document is a valid U.S. passport, report it lost or stolen promptly and contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate; a police report can help but is not mandatory for passport replacement.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay an unofficial fixer to create or obtain a police report.
- Do not sign a statement you do not understand.
- Do not send passport or ID scans to unknown email addresses, phone numbers, or messaging accounts.
- Do not hand over an original document unless an identifiable official needs it for the process. Ask why it is needed and when it will be returned.
- Do not wait for a full police report before reporting a valid U.S. passport lost or stolen.
- Do not assume that an incident number, stamped note, or third-party record will meet every requester’s requirements. Ask what they will accept.

## What to do now
1. **Save the request and note the theft details.** Write down what was stolen, where and when it happened, and who is asking for the police report. Save a screenshot or copy of the request.

2. **Ask where local theft reports are made.** Ask your lodging provider, an airport or station information desk, venue security, or another trusted local source. Ask whether you should use a local police station, tourist-police unit, transport-police office, or official online reporting route.

3. **Ask for the most useful record available now.** Request:
   - an incident or reference number;
   - any written confirmation, such as a copy, stamped note, receipt, or official online acknowledgement; and
   - instructions for obtaining a fuller copy later if one is not available immediately.

4. **Use translation help carefully if needed.** Ask a trusted local contact or lodging provider to help with simple translation, or use a translation app for short phrases. Keep your documents with you where possible and make sure you understand anything before signing it.

5. **Record the attempt if a full report is not available.** Note the office name, address, date, time, reporting route, and any reference number. Keep any available record from a hotel, carrier, or venue security team. Send the requester a clear list of what you have and ask what alternatives it will accept.

6. **If the stolen document is a valid U.S. passport, report it promptly.** Use the official State Department reporting process. A reported passport becomes invalid for travel even if you later find it. Contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate because replacing a lost or stolen passport abroad requires an in-person application. Provide a police-report copy if you filed one and it is available.

7. **If another document was stolen, contact its issuer.** Ask the issuing organisation what replacement process applies abroad and which evidence it needs. If an insurer or card-benefits provider is involved, ask exactly which records it will accept for the claim.

8. **If attending a police station feels unsafe, pause and use a safer route.** Ask a trusted lodging provider, transport operator, venue-security team, or the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate what local options are available.

## What can wait
- You do not need to solve every replacement and claim process at once.
- You do not need to obtain a full police-report copy immediately if only a reference number or brief confirmation is available. Ask the requester whether follow-up is needed.
- You do not need to replace documents that do not affect your immediate safety, identity, or travel plans before dealing with the document that matters most now.

## Important reassurance
A local reporting system may provide a reference number, short confirmation, or later collection process rather than an immediate full report. Getting the available record, saving the details of your attempt, and asking the requester what it will accept gives you a practical next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate pressure and unblock travel or paperwork. Local reporting rules and requester requirements vary, and later decisions may need help from the document issuer, insurer, U.S. embassy or consulate, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Prioritise your immediate safety and use official local routes where possible.
