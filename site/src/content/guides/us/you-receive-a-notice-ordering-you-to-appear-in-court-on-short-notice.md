# What to do if… you receive a notice ordering you to appear in court on short notice (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the case through the official court website or clerk's office immediately to rule out a scam, then secure legal representation or file a motion for a continuance if you cannot attend.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore the notice because it arrived late; courts often consider "service" valid even if you just saw the letter.
- Don’t provide payment, gift card numbers, or Social Security details over the phone to anyone claiming to be a court official.
- Don’t call the phone number on the document if you suspect a scam; find the court's number independently on a .gov website.
- Don’t assume a verbal agreement with the other party's lawyer means you don't have to show up.
- Don’t post photos of the summons or subpoena on social media.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific court and case number immediately.** **Find the court name, physical address, case number, and the specific hearing type (e.g., Arraignment, Motion, Status Conference) on the face of the document.**
2. **Verify the hearing date through the official court portal.** **Search for the "Clerk of Court" or "Online Case Search" for the specific county or federal district listed on the notice to confirm the event is on the docket.**
   - For federal cases, use the PACER (Public Access to Court Electronic Records) system.
   - For state/local cases, use the specific county's official .gov website.
3. **Contact the Clerk of Court to confirm your required "mode" of appearance.** **Call the clerk's office and ask if the hearing is in-person, remote (Zoom/WebEx), or if your physical presence is waived if you have an attorney.**
4. **Distinguish between being a "party" and a "witness."** **Determine if you are the Plaintiff/Defendant (party) or if you are being ordered to provide testimony or documents (witness/subpoena).**
   - Parties face "Default Judgments" or "Bench Warrants" for missing court.
   - Witnesses face "Contempt of Court" for ignoring a subpoena.
5. **Secure an attorney or contact the Public Defender right away.** **If the case is criminal, call the local Public Defender’s office; if civil, contact your state’s Bar Association referral service for an emergency consultation.**
6. **File a written "Motion for Continuance" if you cannot physically attend.** **Ask the clerk for the local form or procedure to request a new date, but do not assume it is granted until you see a signed order from the judge.**
   - Note: Federal Rule 45 generally expects a 14-day notice for subpoenas; if you received less, this may be grounds for a "Motion to Quash."
7. **Create an immediate digital paper trail of your efforts.** **Save copies of the notice, take screenshots of the court’s website, and write down the name and ID number of every court staff member you speak to.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare your full legal defense or gather all evidence today.
- You do not need to contact the opposing party to argue about the merits of the case.
- You do not need to worry about long-term penalties until you have verified the notice is legitimate.
- You do not need to decide on a plea or settlement right this second.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a last-minute court notice is a common procedural occurrence and often happens due to clerical delays or process-server issues. While the deadlines are strict, the court system has specific, standard pathways (like continuances) to handle legitimate conflicts if you act within the first few hours of receiving the notice.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for receiving a notice only. It does not cover specific trial strategies, evidentiary rules, or specialized courts (e.g., Military, Tribal, or Immigration).

## Important note
This information is not legal advice. Court rules vary significantly by jurisdiction. If you are at risk of arrest or a significant financial judgment, consult a licensed attorney in your state immediately.
