# What to do if… you think a car is following you while you are driving (USA)

## Short answer
Do not drive home. Stay on main roads, head toward a staffed, well-lit public place, and call 911 hands-free or ask a passenger to call if you feel threatened or in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive home, to work, or to another private address where you could reveal where you live or who you know.
- Do not pull over on a shoulder, empty lot, side street, or isolated driveway to see what happens.
- Do not confront the other driver, get out, block them, or try to make them stop.
- Do not speed, run lights, brake suddenly, or drive aggressively to lose them.
- Do not text, film, search online, or stare at the other vehicle while driving.
- Do not lead them to a friend’s or family member’s home.

## What to do now
1. **Make the car harder to approach without changing how safely you drive.** Lock the doors, keep windows up, stay at a steady legal speed, and keep enough space around your car to move with traffic.
2. **Stay somewhere visible and predictable.** Use main roads, lit streets, and areas with other cars or people. If a normal route change is already safe, continue past your usual turn toward a busier area instead of turning toward home.
3. **Choose a staffed, public destination.** Drive to an open store, gas station, 24-hour business, police station, sheriff’s office, or other busy place with lights and people. A fire station may be an option only if it is clearly staffed or open and you can stop in a public, well-lit area.
4. **Call 911 if you feel threatened, trapped, chased, or unsafe.** Use hands-free calling or ask a passenger to call. Tell the call-taker your location, direction of travel, your vehicle description, and the other vehicle’s description if you can notice it without distraction.
5. **Ask the dispatcher where to go.** Follow their instructions. Do not hang up until they tell you to, unless staying on the call would make driving unsafe.
6. **When you arrive, stay visible and protected.** Park near the entrance, lights, cameras, or people. Keep doors locked at first. If you called 911, tell the dispatcher exactly where you parked and wait for guidance before getting out.
7. **If the other vehicle leaves, do not immediately go home if you still feel unsafe.** Stay in the public place and ask the dispatcher or local law enforcement what they recommend next.
8. **After you are safe, write down what you remember.** Note the time, streets, direction of travel, vehicle colour, make or model if known, plate details if safely seen, and anything distinctive.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove the person was following you before asking for help.
- You do not need to work out their motive right now.
- You do not need to review dashcam footage, post online, or message multiple people while driving.
- You do not need to decide tonight whether this is stalking, road rage, a mistake, or a coincidence.
- You do not need to make long-term security decisions until the immediate risk has passed.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to second-guess yourself in this situation. Choosing not to go home, staying in public, and calling for help are reasonable safety steps even if the situation later turns out to have been a coincidence.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate risk and buy time. If this is part of repeated incidents, later decisions may need help from local law enforcement, a victim-support service, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. Laws, local procedures, dispatch practices, and public-safety options vary by state, county, and city. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 911.
