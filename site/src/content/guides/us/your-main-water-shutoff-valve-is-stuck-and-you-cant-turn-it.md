# What to do if… your main water shutoff valve is stuck and you can’t turn it (USA)

## Short answer
Stop trying to force the stuck valve and immediately isolate the leak using individual fixture valves, shutting off the well pump (if applicable), or calling your local water utility for an emergency street-level shutoff.

## Do not do these things
- Don't use pliers, pipe wrenches, or cheater bars on a stuck valve; excessive force can snap the valve stem and cause a catastrophic, uncontrollable flood inside the home.
- Don't touch electrical panels, outlets, or wet appliances while standing in water or if your hands are wet.
- Don't attempt to operate the utility-side valve (the one closest to the street) unless you have been specifically authorized by your utility provider.
- Don't leave a main water leak unmonitored while searching for tools.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a dry, safe location to assess the situation.**
   - Ensure you are not standing in water near electrical outlets or the breaker panel.
   - If water is approaching electrical systems, stay back and prepare to call the utility or fire department.

2. **Isolate the leak at the nearest fixture valve if possible.**
   - Check under the sink, behind the toilet, or behind the washing machine for a small oval or lever handle.
   - Turn the handle clockwise to stop water flow to that specific fixture only.

3. **Turn off the well pump or water heater if the water supply is interrupted.**
   - **For well systems:** Switch off the "Well" or "Pump" breaker in your electrical panel to stop the pump from running dry.
   - **For all systems:** Switch off the power (electric) or turn the gas control to "Pilot" or "Off" for the water heater to prevent tank damage.

4. **Attempt to move the main valve with hand pressure only.**
   - Use a dry rag for better grip.
   - For a gate valve (round wheel), try a tiny "wiggle" back and forth (clockwise/counter-clockwise).
   - For a ball valve (lever), try to move it 90 degrees so it is perpendicular to the pipe.
   - **Stop immediately** if the valve does not move or if a gate valve handle spins freely without resistance.

5. **Locate and use the customer-side shutoff at the meter box if accessible.**
   - Look for the underground box near the street or property line labeled "Water."
   - Identify the valve on the "house side" (the side of the meter closest to your home).
   - Use a meter key or crescent wrench to turn the valve 90 degrees if permitted by your local utility.

6. **Call your water utility’s emergency line for a street shutoff.**
   - State clearly: "I have an active leak and my main interior shutoff valve is seized."
   - Request an emergency "curb stop" or "meter" shutoff.

7. **Relieve remaining pressure in the lines.**
   - Open the lowest faucet in the house (such as a basement utility sink or an outdoor hose bib) to drain water remaining in the pipes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a permanent valve replacement (e.g., switching from gate to ball valve) right now.
- You do not need to begin water extraction or carpet cleaning until the flow is confirmed as stopped.
- You do not need to calculate the cost of utility fines or plumber fees in this moment.

## Important reassurance
Main shutoff valves often seize because they remain in one position for years without being "exercised." It is a mechanical failure of the hardware, not a personal error. Focus on the alternative shutoff methods (fixtures, well pump, or utility) which are designed for these exact scenarios.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Once the water is off, a licensed plumber must be called to replace the failed valve and repair any damaged piping.

## Important note
Procedures for accessing water meters and curb stops vary significantly by city and state. Tampering with utility-owned equipment may result in fines. If you are a renter, notify your landlord or building management immediately after calling the utility.
