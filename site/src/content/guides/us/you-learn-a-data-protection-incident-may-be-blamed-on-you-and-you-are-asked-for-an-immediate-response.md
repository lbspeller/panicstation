# What to do if… you learn a data protection incident may be blamed on you and you are asked for an immediate response (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all interaction, document the messages exactly as received, and report the conduct through your employer's formal reporting channel or Workplace Violence Prevention Plan.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply to the messages or engage in an argument to defend yourself.
- Do not delete any messages, even if they are distressing or graphic.
- Do not "Reply All" or CC other colleagues into the aggressive thread.
- Do not attempt to meet the individual in person to resolve the issue privately.
- Do not assume that messages sent on personal devices or after hours are outside your employer's jurisdiction.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the hazard by silencing notifications.** Mute the specific contact or thread and enable "Do Not Disturb" mode to prevent further immediate exposure.
2. **Contact emergency services if a physical threat is made.** If any message contains a specific threat of violence or suggests the sender is nearby, **call 911** immediately and move to a secure location.
3. **Preserve digital evidence in its original form.** Capture screenshots that clearly show the sender's name, the timestamp, and the full text.
   - For emails, save the original file (.msg or .eml) to preserve technical header data.
   - For platform messages (Slack / Teams), use the "export" or "copy link to message" function if available before the sender can delete them.
4. **Initiate a contemporaneous incident log.** Record the date, time, platform, a brief summary of the content, and how it is affecting your ability to work or your sense of safety.
5. **Review your company's Workplace Violence Prevention Plan (WVPP).** Check if your state (such as California or New York) or your specific employer requires a formal WVPP, which will outline specific immediate reporting steps for "Type 3" (worker-on-worker) violence or threats.
6. **Submit a formal report to HR or a designated supervisor.** Use the internal reporting path specified in your employee handbook. Request in writing that the report be documented as a formal complaint of harassment or workplace intimidation.
7. **Request "Weingarten Rights" if you are a union member.** If management requests an interview regarding your report that you believe could lead to your own discipline, clearly state: "If this discussion could in any way lead to my being disciplined or terminated, I request that my union representative be present."
8. **Specify if the aggression involves protected characteristics.** Clearly state in your report if the messages reference your race, sex, religion, disability, age (40+), or sexual orientation, as this triggers specific federal legal protections under the EEOC.
9. **Request temporary safety measures if needed.** Ask HR or Security for a temporary change in work location, modified shift times, or a "no-contact" directive while the investigation is pending.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to file a lawsuit or an EEOC charge.
- You do not need to determine if the behavior meets the legal "severe or pervasive" threshold yourself.
- You do not need to respond to follow-up messages while waiting for HR to open the case.
- You do not need to tell other colleagues about the incident until you have spoken with HR.

## Important reassurance
Receiving aggressive or hostile messages outside of work is a violation of professional boundaries and can cause significant distress. Taking the step to document and report this behavior is a standard procedural response designed to transfer the responsibility for the situation to the employer’s established safety and HR systems.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate stabilization and evidence-preservation steps. It does not cover the long-term legal strategy, formal litigation, or the specifics of an employer's internal investigation process.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. While federal law (Title VII) and state laws provide protections against harassment, the EEOC rescinded its 2024 consolidated guidance in January 2026, meaning internal agency procedures may vary. If you believe your rights are being violated or your employer is failing to act, consult an employment attorney or your state's fair employment agency.
