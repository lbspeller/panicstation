# What to do if… a basement or retaining wall is bulging and you fear it may fail (USA)

## Short answer
Treat a bulging basement or retaining wall as a possible collapse hazard. Move people and pets away from the wall and call 911 if collapse seems possible soon, if utilities are affected, or if anyone could be hurt.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go into the basement or stand next to the wall to “check it” if it is bulging, bowing, leaning, or actively cracking.
- Don’t try DIY bracing with jacks, posts, wedges, vehicles, straps, or stacked materials.
- Don’t remove blocks, bricks, soil, or drainage material from the wall area.
- Don’t dig at the base of the wall or behind a retaining wall while it may be unstable.
- Don’t park, walk, sit, or let children play near the base of a retaining wall or near the top edge above it.
- Don’t run heavy equipment, pound stakes, or create vibration near the wall.
- Don’t ignore fast changes, new gaps, louder cracking, fresh soil movement, water suddenly appearing, or doors and windows suddenly sticking.

## What to do now
1. **Move people and pets away from the wall.** For a basement wall, leave the basement and keep people off the floor area directly above the bulging section if you can do that safely. For a retaining wall, stay away from both the top edge and the base.
2. **Create a no-go zone.** Close doors, block access with furniture, cones, tape, or signs if available, and tell others not to enter the area. Make the no-go zone wider than feels necessary.
3. **Call 911 if there is immediate danger.** Use 911 if the wall is rapidly moving, making loud cracking or groaning sounds, shedding blocks or soil, affecting a road or sidewalk, threatening a neighboring property, or putting anyone at risk.
4. **If it is not an immediate 911 emergency, contact your local building department, code enforcement office, or emergency management office.** Say: “A basement or retaining wall is bulging or bowing, and I’m concerned about collapse risk.” Ask whether an urgent safety inspection, evacuation advice, or access restriction is needed.
5. **Arrange an urgent in-person evaluation by a licensed professional engineer with structural experience, a structural engineer where that title is licensed, or another qualified structural professional.** Ask specifically whether temporary shoring, evacuation, drainage control, or keeping loads away is needed before any repair work starts.
6. **Reduce nearby load and vibration from a safe place.** Keep vehicles, stored materials, heavy planters, crowds, and equipment away from the wall and from the ground above or behind it.
7. **Watch for utility hazards without going close.** If you smell gas, hear hissing, see sparks, see water rushing, notice sewage backup, or see strained or downed utility lines, back away and call the utility’s emergency number. Call 911 if the hazard could injure someone soon.
8. **Take photos only from a safe distance.** Photograph the bulge, cracks, leaning, ground movement, water, and nearby loads. Note the time you first noticed it and whether it is changing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the cause is drainage, soil pressure, settlement, frost, workmanship, a landslide, or a foundation defect right now.
- You do not need to choose a repair method today.
- You do not need to dig, drain, anchor, brace, rebuild, or remove the wall before a qualified person has assessed stability.
- You do not need to negotiate with contractors, neighbors, landlords, insurers, or buyers while the wall may still be unsafe.
- You do not need perfect photos or measurements if getting them would put you close to the wall.

## Important reassurance
You are not overreacting by keeping distance. A bulging or leaning wall may turn out to be repairable, but the safest first move is to keep people away and get local emergency or qualified structural help before anyone works near it.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about shoring, engineering design, drainage, permits, contractors, insurance, ownership, or neighbor issues may need specialist help after the immediate safety risk is controlled.

## Important note
This is general information, not engineering, legal, financial, insurance, or other professional advice. For a possible collapse, utility hazard, or threat to people, use emergency services and follow local officials’ instructions.
