# What to do if… you see a new fluid leak under your vehicle and you cannot identify what it is (USA)

## Short answer
Treat an unknown new vehicle leak as potentially unsafe until you have checked for obvious danger signs. Move only as needed to get out of immediate danger, and if there is any fuel smell, brake issue, overheating, warning light, fast dripping, EV/hybrid warning, or uncertainty, do not drive it; call roadside assistance or 911 if there is immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep driving to “see if it goes away” if the leak could be oil, coolant, brake fluid, fuel, or another vehicle fluid.
- Don’t touch the liquid with bare hands or smell it up close.
- Don’t restart the engine repeatedly to watch it leak, especially if you smell gasoline or see smoke.
- Don’t crawl under the vehicle unless it is properly supported with jack stands, not just a jack.
- Don’t ignore warning lights, warning messages, overheating, steering changes, or braking changes.
- Don’t use road flares if you smell gasoline or cannot rule out a fuel leak.
- Don’t wash the spill into a storm drain, gutter, soil, sink, or household drain.
- Don’t inspect around an EV or hybrid battery area if there is smoke, hissing, a burning smell, a high-voltage warning, crash damage, or fluid near the battery pack.

## What to do now
1. **Get to the safest pause you can.**  
   If the vehicle is still controllable, move slowly to a parking lot, driveway, wide shoulder, or another place away from moving traffic. If you are stopped in a live lane, on a blind curve, in fast traffic, near smoke or flames, or you smell gasoline and cannot safely move away, call **911**.

2. **Make yourself visible and reduce traffic risk.**  
   Turn on hazard lights. If it is safer to stay inside because of fast traffic or a narrow shoulder, stay buckled with your seat belt on and call for help. If you must exit, use the side away from traffic and move well away from the roadway. Use reflective triangles only if you can place them without stepping into traffic and without going near a possible fuel leak.

3. **Do a quick no-touch danger check.**  
   From a safe position, look for dashboard warnings, a strong gasoline smell, smoke, steam, active dripping or spraying, or a puddle that is growing quickly. Also notice whether the brake pedal feels soft or spongy, the steering feels wrong, the engine temperature is high, or the vehicle shows an EV/hybrid high-voltage warning. If any of these are present, do not drive; call roadside assistance, a tow provider, or 911 if there is immediate danger.

4. **Record what you can without reaching under the vehicle.**  
   Take photos of the puddle, where it sits under the vehicle, the dashboard warnings, and the area around the vehicle. Note whether it appeared after driving, overnight, after refueling, after using the air conditioning, after a bump, or after recent service. If it is safe, place paper or cardboard near the edge of the drip area to show color and amount, but do not reach underneath the vehicle.

5. **Check only simple, visible fluid levels.**  
   With the engine off, the vehicle in park, and the parking brake set, look only at checks your owner’s manual makes simple. You may be able to check the engine oil dipstick, coolant reservoir markings, or a visible brake fluid reservoir. Do not open a hot coolant cap or any hot pressurized system. If brake fluid looks low, oil is very low, coolant is very low, or you are unsure, do not drive.

6. **Call for the right help.**  
   Call your roadside assistance provider, insurer, manufacturer roadside program, auto club, or a local tow provider and say: “I have an unknown fluid leak and I am not driving until it is assessed.” Ask whether your vehicle needs a tow or flatbed, especially for an EV, hybrid, all-wheel-drive vehicle, or anything your owner’s manual says should not be towed with wheels on the ground.

7. **Keep people and pets away from the puddle.**  
   Unknown automotive fluids can be irritating, toxic, flammable, or harmful to pets and the environment. Keep children and animals away. If the leak is on your driveway and there is no immediate traffic or fire danger, wait for professional advice before cleaning it up, and do not rinse it away.

8. **Report only later if it may be a safety defect.**  
   If the leak is severe, repeats after repair, or seems like a possible vehicle safety defect, you can file a report with NHTSA after the immediate situation is safe. This does not replace towing, repair, insurance, or emergency help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to correctly identify the fluid right now.
- You do not need to decide on repairs, costs, or whether the vehicle is worth keeping right now.
- You do not need to clean the spill immediately if that would mean touching unknown fluid, standing in traffic, or washing it into a drain.
- You do not need to keep testing the vehicle to prove where the leak is coming from.

## Important reassurance
A new puddle under a vehicle can make the situation feel urgent and confusing. Slowing down, not touching the fluid, and choosing a tow when you are uncertain is a normal, practical way to prevent injury, fire risk, brake risk, engine damage, or environmental harm.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilize the situation and avoid unsafe choices. A qualified technician, roadside provider, insurer, or manufacturer support line may need to inspect the vehicle before anyone can identify the fluid or decide what repair is needed.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, mechanical, environmental, insurance, or professional advice. If you suspect a fuel leak, brake problem, overheating, smoke, fire, EV or hybrid battery danger, or you are in an unsafe location, prioritize immediate safety and professional help.
