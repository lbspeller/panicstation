# What to do if… you find an unauthorized crowdfunding page created in a loved one’s name (USA)

## Short answer
Save the page details, then report the concern through the crowdfunding platform’s official website or app. Ask the platform to review the page and whether payouts can be paused while it checks who created the fundraiser and where the money is meant to go.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send ID photos, Social Security numbers, bank details, or other sensitive documents to the organizer or to anyone contacting you unexpectedly.
- Don’t confront the organizer while you are overwhelmed or while you are still checking what happened.
- Don’t post the live fundraiser link widely to warn people; that may spread it further.
- Don’t use links in unexpected emails, texts, or direct messages claiming to be from the platform. Navigate to the platform’s official website or app yourself.
- Don’t assume the page is legitimate or fraudulent before checking who created it and who is meant to receive the money.

## What to do now
1. **Save the page details.** Take screenshots of the fundraiser, its URL, the platform name, the organizer’s name or handle, the claimed relationship, the displayed donation total, the stated use of the money, images, updates, and comments. Note when you found it.
2. **Check whether anyone appropriate authorized it.** If it is safe and practical, ask your loved one directly. If they have died or cannot answer, check with an appropriate family contact or estate representative. Keep this brief; you do not need to confront the organizer.
3. **Report the concern first through the platform’s official route.** If the page may be a crowdfunding scam, use the platform’s official report or support option. State that the page uses your loved one’s name, that you are concerned it may not be authorized, and that you want the platform to review it. Ask whether payouts can be paused during the review.
4. **Describe your relationship accurately.** If the platform asks for information, explain whether you are a family member, an estate representative, or another relevant contact. Provide documents only through the platform’s official process if requested.
5. **Warn the smallest useful circle without sharing the live link.** Tell close family members, friends, and any known donors that the fundraiser is being checked and that they should not donate until its status is confirmed.
6. **Tell known donors to act promptly if they already paid.** They can contact the platform and, where appropriate, their card issuer, bank, or payment provider to ask what recovery steps may be available.
7. **Report a suspected scam if needed.** A suspected scam can be reported to the FTC. For an online scam or other cyber-enabled crime, you can also consider filing a complaint with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center.
8. **Protect your personal information if it may be at risk.** If your own identifying information may have been exposed or misused, use IdentityTheft.gov for recovery steps. A fraud alert or credit freeze may also help protect against new accounts being opened in your name.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to confront the organizer, contact the press, or pursue legal action.
- You do not need to build a complete donor list today.
- You do not need to write a long public explanation.
- You do not need to complete every fraud-reporting or identity-protection step at once. Start with saving the page and asking the platform to review it.

## Important reassurance
Finding a fundraiser like this during a family crisis can feel upsetting and intrusive. You only need to take the first few steps now: save the page, check whether it was authorized, and ask the platform to review it.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about refunds, estate issues, identity misuse, or legal options may need help from the platform, a financial provider, or an appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or other professional advice. If anyone is in immediate danger, call 911. For threats or safety concerns that are not immediate, contact local law enforcement.
