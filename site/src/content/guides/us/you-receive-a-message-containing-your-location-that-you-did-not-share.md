# What to do if… you receive a message containing your location that you did not share (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause point, do not reply, and save the message. If you think the person may be nearby or you feel in immediate danger, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply to “see what they want”; it can confirm you are reachable.
- Do not click links, open attachments, scan QR codes, or install apps they suggest.
- Do not confirm your address, workplace, school, schedule, routines, or current location.
- Do not agree to meet, move to another app because they demand it, or send photos to “verify.”
- Do not pay money, gift cards, or crypto to make it stop; paying may lead to more demands.
- Do not post the message publicly while you are still exposed, because it may spread the location details further.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause point.** If you are alone, move toward other people, such as a store, lobby, front desk, neighbor, or trusted person. If you are at home, lock doors and do not go outside to check whether someone is there.
2. **Choose the urgent route.**
   - If there is immediate danger, they claim to see you, they appear nearby, or you feel unsafe: call 911.
   - If it is threatening or harassing but not an immediate emergency: contact local law enforcement or the local non-emergency line to ask how to make a report.
3. **Preserve the message quietly.** Screenshot or screen-record the thread showing the sender, username, phone number or email, timestamps, the location details, threats, links, and payment demands. Save the original message if you can do so safely.
4. **Stop obvious location-sharing paths.** Check Apple Find My, Google Location Sharing, Maps, family sharing, messaging apps, ride-share apps, fitness apps, and social media location settings. Turn off live location sharing you do not recognise or no longer need.
5. **Reduce fresh location leakage.** Turn off precise location for apps that do not need it right now, remove recent public posts that show where you are, and avoid posting your current surroundings until you are settled somewhere safe.
6. **Secure the account the message came through.** Change its password, sign out of other sessions or devices if the app allows it, turn on multi-factor authentication, and check whether recovery email, recovery phone, linked devices, or forwarding settings have been changed.
7. **Secure your email next.** Email is often the reset route for other accounts. Change the password, turn on multi-factor authentication, check recovery details, and sign out of sessions you do not recognise.
8. **Report without engaging the sender.** Report and block inside the platform where the message arrived. If the message involves cyber-enabled threats, extortion, account compromise, or ongoing online abuse, file a report with IC3 by typing the address yourself. If it appears to be fraud or a scam, report it to the FTC.
9. **Tell one trusted person what is happening.** Send a simple note with where you are, what was sent, and what you are doing next. If you need to move, ask them to stay on the phone while you get to a safer place.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly how they got the location right now.
- You do not need to confront someone you suspect or ask mutual contacts to investigate.
- You do not need to delete every account, change your number, or scrub your whole online presence this minute.
- Deeper device checks, data broker removals, new accounts, and longer-term safety planning can wait until you are safe and have support.

## Important reassurance
A message with location details can feel very alarming, but it does not always mean someone is physically nearby. The detail may have come from public records, old posts, a geotag, a broad IP-based estimate, a shared account, or location sharing that was left on. Treat it seriously, but focus on safety, evidence, location sharing, and account access first.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety and preventing irreversible mistakes. If the messages continue, escalate, or involve someone you know, later decisions may need specialist help from local law enforcement, a victim support service, a cybersecurity professional, or a legal professional.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, cybersecurity, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe, trust that concern and contact emergency services.
