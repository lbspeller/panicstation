# What to do if… you are told to clear out a loved one’s room in a care facility quickly after they die (USA)

## Short answer
Request the facility’s written property policy and a specific deadline, then ask for secure on-site storage and a witnessed inventory before removing any items.

## Do not do these things
- **Don't sign any "all items received" or "property release" forms** until you have physically verified the contents against an inventory.
- **Don't allow the facility to dispose of, donate, or move belongings** to an off-site location without your written consent.
- **Don't assume the facility is allowed to release property to anyone** who asks; they must legally follow the resident representative or estate administrator designation on file.
- **Don't leave the facility without a signed and dated copy** of the inventory list if the facility is taking custody of the items for storage.
- **Don't feel pressured to settle final medical or room bills** in the same conversation as the property removal.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the designated resident representative or "Responsible Party" on the facility file.**  
   - Ask the administrator or social worker to confirm who is legally authorized to receive property.
   - If you are not that person, do not remove items until you have written permission from the designated representative or estate executor.

2. **Request the written "Property After Death" policy and a formal deadline.**  
   - Ask for a copy of the facility's policy regarding the timeframe for room clearance.
   - Request that any deadline be provided in writing (via email or letter) rather than just verbally.

3. **Request a joint inventory of all personal effects and valuables.**  
   - Ask a staff member to walk through the room with you to list all items.
   - Ensure both you and the staff member sign and date the list.
   - Take photographs of the room and specific high-value items (jewelry, electronics, documents) before they are packed.

4. **Ask the facility to move items to a secure, locked storage area if the room must be vacated.**  
   - If the room is needed immediately for another resident, the facility should have a process for securing property on-site.
   - Confirm in writing if there are any daily storage fees and when they begin to accrue.

5. **Perform a "high-priority" sweep of essential legal and financial documents.**  
   - **Locate and secure the following immediately:**
     - Social Security card and ID/Passport.
     - Medicare/Medicaid cards and private insurance info.
     - Checkbooks, wallets, and credit cards.
     - Keys to the resident's home or vehicle.
     - Original legal documents (Wills, Trusts, Power of Attorney).

6. **Request the final accounting and return of the "Resident Trust" or "Personal Funds Account."**  
   - Under federal law (42 CFR 483.10), Medicare/Medicaid-certified nursing homes must convey the resident’s funds and a final accounting within 30 days of death.
   - Ensure the facility has the correct mailing address for the estate representative.

7. **Escalate to the State Long-Term Care Ombudsman if you are being coerced or threatened.**  
   - Find your local advocate through the **National Long-Term Care Ombudsman Resource Center** or the **Administration for Community Living (ACL)**.
   - Mention that you are seeking assistance to ensure the resident's property is handled according to federal "Dignity and Respect" standards.

## What can wait
- **Sorting through clothing and low-value items:** You do not need to decide what to donate or keep today.
- **Settling the final bill:** Property rights are separate from billing disputes; you do not have to pay the bill to get the property.
- **Distributing items to other family members:** Property should remain secured until the legal estate representative makes a final decision.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed by administrative tasks immediately following a loss. Needing more than a few hours to clear a lifetime of belongings is a reasonable request, and seeking the help of an Ombudsman or facility administrator is a standard procedural step, not a sign of failure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for property protection in the United States only. It does not cover specific state probate laws, wrongful death claims, or detailed Medicaid estate recovery procedures.

## Important note
Federal regulations (42 CFR 483.10) provide strong protections for residents in Medicare/Medicaid-certified nursing facilities. Assisted living and private-pay-only facilities are governed by state-specific licensing rules, which vary significantly. Always verify your specific state’s resident bill of rights if the facility is not a certified nursing home.
