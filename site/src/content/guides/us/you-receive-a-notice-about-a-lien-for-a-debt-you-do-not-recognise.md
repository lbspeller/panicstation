# What to do if… you receive a notice about a lien for a debt you do not recognise (USA)

## Short answer
Check the notice against official court or land-record records before paying or giving personal information. If a debt collector is involved, dispute the debt in writing and ask for validation promptly.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay “immediately to stop the lien” or send money by wire, gift card, crypto, payment app, or any unusual method.
- Do not give your Social Security number, bank details, photo ID, or account passwords to someone who contacted you first.
- Do not rely only on the phone number, email address, or payment link on the notice; look up official contact details yourself.
- Do not ignore it just because you believe the debt is not yours; court, lien, and dispute deadlines can be short.
- Do not admit the debt, apologise for it, or agree to a payment plan before you know whether it is valid and yours.
- Do not throw away the envelope, screenshots, emails, texts, or voicemails.

## What to do now
1. **Save the notice and capture the key details.**  
   Keep the notice, envelope, emails, texts, and screenshots. Write down the filer or creditor name, the debt collector name if listed, the person or property named, any case number, recording number, parcel number, account number, dates, and the office or court where it says the lien was or will be filed.

2. **Check whether the lien is real through official records.**  
   Use independent contact details for the relevant county recorder, register of deeds, clerk, land records office, tax office, or court. Ask whether there is a recorded lien, pending filing, lawsuit, or judgment matching the notice. Do not use a contact link from the notice until you have checked it independently.

3. **Identify the type of lien and what it is based on.**  
   Look for whether it claims to be a judgment lien, tax lien, mechanics or contractor lien, HOA lien, or another type. If it refers to a lawsuit or judgment, check the official court docket for that case number. If it refers to property work, compare the claimant, property address, dates, and contract details with anything you recognise.

4. **If a debt collector is involved, dispute the debt in writing.**  
   Ask for debt validation and state that you do not recognise the debt. Under federal debt collection rules, after you receive validation information you generally have 30 days to dispute the debt in writing, and a timely written dispute usually requires the collector to pause collection of the disputed debt until it responds with verification. Keep a copy of what you send and proof of delivery.

5. **If a lawsuit, judgment, or court deadline appears, treat that separately.**  
   A debt-collector dispute does not automatically answer a lawsuit or undo a lien. If the official court record shows an active case, judgment, hearing, or deadline, contact the court clerk for the official process and seek local legal aid or a consumer attorney quickly.

6. **Check your credit reports and dispute any wrong item.**  
   If the debt, judgment, or lien appears on your credit reports, dispute it with each credit bureau that shows the mistake and with the company that reported it. Say what is wrong, why it is wrong, and what you want corrected or removed. Send copies of supporting documents, not originals.

7. **Use identity-theft protections if fraud is possible.**  
   If the notice may involve identity theft, place a free credit freeze with each major credit bureau. You can also place an initial fraud alert by contacting one major credit bureau, which should notify the others. If someone may have used your identity, make an identity theft report through the federal identity theft site and follow the recovery steps it gives you.

8. **Keep communication controlled and documented.**  
   Tell the filer, collector, or company that you want communications in writing. Keep a simple log with dates, names, phone numbers, call-backs, and what was said. Save screenshots of official record searches and copies of all letters.

9. **Escalate if pressure, false reporting, or non-response continues.**  
   If a collector will not validate the debt, keeps pressuring you after a timely written dispute, or inaccurate credit reporting continues, submit a complaint to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau. If there is a recorded lien or court case, use the official local process and get local legal help before deadlines pass.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to sue, negotiate, refinance, sell property, or make a settlement offer.
- You do not need to buy a paid credit-repair service to take the first steps.
- You do not need to provide a long explanation; short, written, factual disputes are enough for now.
- You do not need to speak on the phone if written communication is safer and clearer.
- You do not need to prove everything immediately; first verify the record, preserve documents, dispute what is wrong, and prevent avoidable credit, identity, or enforcement problems.

## Important reassurance
A lien notice for a debt you do not recognise is alarming, but it can happen because of identity theft, mistaken identity, recording errors, old court records, or a scam notice. The safest first moves are to verify through official records, dispute in writing, protect your credit, and avoid rushed payment.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The exact way to challenge, release, or remove a lien depends on your state, county, court record, and lien type, so later decisions may need help from local legal aid, a consumer attorney, a real estate attorney, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, credit, tax, or professional advice. State laws and timelines vary. If you find an active lawsuit, judgment, recorded lien, tax matter, or deadline, respond through the appropriate official process and seek qualified help promptly.
