# What to do if… you are told a person you care about is being transferred to another detention facility with little notice (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately confirm the current custody system (Federal, State, County, or Immigration) and use the corresponding official online locator to verify their arrival at the new facility before attempting contact or travel.

## Do not do these things
- Do not travel to the new facility until you have verified the individual has been fully "booked" and "classified," as intake procedures often prevent visitation for 24 to 72 hours.
- Do not send money, care packages, or legal documents to a new address until you have confirmed the specific mailing format and housing unit for that facility.
- Do not rely on information from unofficial social media groups or third-party "inmate search" sites that charge fees; use only .gov or official sheriff websites.
- Do not post the individual's full ID numbers or personal details on public forums to find them, as this can create security risks for the person in custody.
- Do not call the facility multiple times per hour; updates to digital locators often lag behind physical movement by 12 to 24 hours.

## What to do now
1. **Document every detail provided during the initial notification.**
   - Record the name and badge number of the person who informed you.
   - Note the exact terminology used, such as "in transit," "released to another agency," or "transferred to [specific facility name]."
   - Write down the individual's full legal name, date of birth, and any known ID numbers (BOP Register Number, A-Number, or State SID).

2. **Verify the specific custody agency involved.**
   - Determine if the person was held by the Federal Bureau of Prisons (BOP), U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), a State Department of Corrections (DOC), or a County Sheriff.
   - If the person was in federal custody but is not showing up in the BOP locator, contact the local U.S. Marshals Service (USMS) office, as they manage prisoners during transit.

3. **Use the appropriate official locator system for the jurisdiction.**
   - **Federal:** Search the BOP Inmate Locator using their Register Number or name.
   - **Immigration:** Use the ICE Online Detainee Locator System (ODLS) which requires an A-Number and Country of Birth, or a full name and Date of Birth.
   - **State/County:** Visit the official state DOC website or the specific county sheriff’s "jail roster" page.

4. **Register for automated custody status alerts.**
   - Access **VINELink** (Victim Information and Notification Everyday) to register for phone or email alerts regarding the person's location changes.
   - For immigration cases, use the **DHS-VINE** system specifically designed for ICE detainees.

5. **Contact the receiving facility once the locator shows they have arrived.**
   - **Ask for the "Booking" or "Classification" department.**
   - Verify their "Booking Number" and current "Housing Unit," as these are often required for mail and phone accounts.
   - Confirm when the person will be eligible to use the commissary or phone system, as intake status often restricts these for several days.

6. **Address urgent medical or safety concerns through the Watch Commander.**
   - If the person has a critical medical condition or a safety risk, ask to speak with the **Watch Commander** or the **Health Services Administrator**.
   - State clearly: "I am reporting an urgent welfare concern regarding a person recently transferred."

7. **Notify the attorney of record immediately.**
   - Inform the lawyer of the new location, as transfers can disrupt court dates, legal mail delivery, and attorney-client visitation privileges.

## What can wait
- You do not need to understand the administrative reason for the transfer, which is often related to bed space or security classification rather than individual behavior.
- You do not need to retrieve property left at the previous facility immediately; most agencies hold property for 30 days or transfer it separately.
- You do not need to re-apply for visitation until the person has completed the "initial classification" phase at the new site.

## Important reassurance
Transfers are a standard, albeit disruptive, part of the detention system and often occur with zero advance notice for security reasons. A "not found" result on a locator during the first 24 hours is common and usually indicates the person is physically in transit or awaiting data entry at the new facility. This temporary silence does not necessarily mean an emergency has occurred.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying a person's location and establishing initial contact after a transfer. It does not cover long-term legal appeals, visitation applications, or property recovery.

## Important note
Procedures and terminology vary significantly between federal, state, and local jurisdictions. This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Always prioritize information provided directly by official government agency staff over third-party reports.
