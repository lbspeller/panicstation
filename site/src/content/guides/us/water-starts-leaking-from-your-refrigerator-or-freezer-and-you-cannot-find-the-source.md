# What to do if… water starts leaking from your refrigerator or freezer and you cannot find the source (USA)

## Short answer
Keep away from standing water. If you can reach the plug safely with dry hands and without stepping in water, unplug the appliance; if it has a water supply line, close that line’s shutoff valve.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t touch the plug, outlet, appliance, power strip, or extension cord while standing in water or with wet hands.
- Don’t move the refrigerator quickly or pull hard against a connected water line.
- Don’t use a hair dryer, heater, or other electrical device near the wet area.
- Don’t open electrical or appliance access panels.
- Don’t restore power if water has reached an outlet, wiring, or electrical parts until qualified help says it is safe.
- Don’t keep opening the refrigerator or freezer doors while the appliance is off.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safer.** Keep people and pets away from the wet floor. If the plug is dry and safely reachable without stepping in water, unplug the appliance with dry hands. If the plug or outlet is wet, do not touch it. If you can reach the electrical panel without crossing water and the panel area is dry, turn off the circuit for the appliance. Otherwise, leave the area and get qualified help.
2. **Stop a refrigerator water supply if there is one.** If the appliance has an ice maker or water dispenser, look for the refrigerator water-line shutoff valve. It may be behind the refrigerator, under the kitchen sink, in a nearby cabinet, or in a basement or crawl space. Do not pull the appliance forward if that could strain the line. If a continuing leak appears connected to the supply line and you cannot find the dedicated valve safely, close the home’s main water supply if you know how to do that safely.
3. **Contain the water.** Use towels and a shallow tray or pan to keep water from spreading under cabinets or across the floor. Avoid contact with outlets, plugs, cords, and appliance wiring.
4. **Make only visible, no-tools observations.** Check whether a door is partly open or blocked by food. Look for water inside the appliance, dripping from the dispenser area, or a visibly wet supply line. If a drain pan is visible without removing a panel, check whether it appears to be overflowing or cracked.
5. **Keep food cold while the appliance is off.** Keep the doors closed. Food generally stays safe for up to about 4 hours in an unopened refrigerator, about 48 hours in a full freezer, and about 24 hours in a half-full freezer. If the refrigerator will be off for about 4 hours and you have a cooler and ice or frozen gel packs, move refrigerated perishable foods into the cooler.
6. **Get the right help.** Contact a plumber if the leak appears to come from the supply line, valve, or plumbing connection. Contact an appliance repair technician if the leak appears internal or continues after the refrigerator water supply is off. If water has reached an outlet, wiring, or electrical parts, get a qualified electrician’s advice before restoring power. If you rent or water may be reaching another unit or shared area, notify building management or your landlord promptly.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact internal part now.
- You do not need to dismantle panels or clear an internal drain now.
- You do not need to empty the entire refrigerator or freezer immediately; keep the doors closed unless you are moving refrigerated perishables to a cooler.
- Cleaning behind the appliance and deciding on repairs can wait until the leak is contained and the area is safe.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to solve the cause immediately. Stopping the spread of water, avoiding electrical contact, and protecting food are enough for the first safe pause.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need an appliance repair technician, plumber, electrician, landlord, or building manager.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Use qualified help for electrical work, plumbing work, or appliance repairs that are not clearly safe and simple.
