# What to do if… a key online account says your identity details were changed and you cannot see what was altered (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the alert as a possible unauthorized change. Do not use links in the alert: go directly to the provider through its official app or by typing the site address, then secure the affected account and check its recovery details.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links or call numbers in the alert message unless you independently confirm they belong to the provider.
- Do not keep guessing passwords repeatedly if you cannot sign in; use the provider’s official account-recovery route.
- Do not delay securing access while trying to identify every altered field.
- Do not approve an unfamiliar sign-in, password reset, or verification prompt.
- Do not delete the alert message yet; keep it so you can refer to its time and wording.
- Do not overlook money-related activity if the account stores payment methods or can make purchases.

## What to do now
1. **Open the real service from a device you trust.**  
   Use the provider’s official app or type the site address yourself. If you think your device may be compromised, update its security software and run a scan before entering new passwords, or use another trusted device.

2. **Secure the affected account if you can still sign in.**  
   - Change the password to a new, unique password.
   - Sign out of all devices or end other active sessions if the provider offers this option.
   - Remove unfamiliar devices and revoke unfamiliar third-party app access if those controls are available.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication or another additional authentication method if offered.

3. **Check the recovery details.**  
   Review the recovery email address, phone number, and other sign-in or recovery methods shown in the account. Remove unfamiliar details and replace them with details you control if the provider allows this. Reset backup codes or recovery keys if they are shown and you did not create them.

4. **Check the identity details that may have changed.**  
   Review the account’s profile, personal details, addresses, security settings, payment settings, username, linked accounts, and sign-in connections. Note anything unfamiliar.

5. **Secure the email account used for recovery if it may also be affected.**  
   Change its password if it was reused, turn on two-factor authentication if available, review signed-in devices, and remove forwarding rules or filters you did not create.

6. **Use the provider’s official recovery route if you cannot sign in or cannot correct the details.**  
   Tell support that you received an alert about changed identity details, when the alert arrived, and what unfamiliar details or sign-ins you can see. Ask what account restrictions, reversals, and change information are available.

7. **Check money-related activity if relevant.**  
   Review recent purchases, saved payment methods, and transactions connected with the account. If you see an unauthorized card or bank transaction, contact the card issuer or bank using the number on your card or in your banking app.

8. **Use the federal identity-theft recovery site if personal information may have been misused beyond this account.**  
   IdentityTheft.gov can help you report identity theft and create a recovery plan. You may also consider a credit freeze or an initial fraud alert. A credit freeze generally requires contacting all three nationwide credit bureaus. An initial fraud alert can generally be placed by contacting one bureau.

9. **Consider an Internet Crime Complaint Center report if you believe you were affected by cyber-enabled crime.**  
   You may file a report even if you are unsure whether the complaint qualifies. Keep the alert message, dates, times, suspicious sign-in details, and relevant transaction information.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly how the change happened before securing the account.
- You do not need to reset every password immediately. Prioritize the affected account, the recovery email account if relevant, and any account that reused the same password.
- You do not need to decide whether to delete the account while you are stabilizing access.
- You do not need to make every optional identity-theft decision at once.

## Important reassurance
An unexpected account-change alert can feel disorienting. You can take this one step at a time: go directly to the provider, secure access where possible, and record anything unfamiliar before moving to the next step.

## Scope note
These are immediate first steps only. Later decisions may need help from the provider’s specialist support team, your bank or card issuer, or an appropriate identity-theft resource.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and immediate practical steps. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Provider labels and recovery options vary, so use the provider’s official support and recovery pages for its exact process.
