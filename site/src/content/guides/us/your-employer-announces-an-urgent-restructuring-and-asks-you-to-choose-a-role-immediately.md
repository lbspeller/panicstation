# What to do if… your employer announces an urgent restructuring and asks you to choose a role immediately (USA)

## Short answer
Request the written policy or contract language justifying the clawback immediately and do not sign any new repayment authorizations until you have verified the math and the tax-year implications.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a new "repayment agreement" or promissory note during an initial meeting or phone call.
- Do not give verbal permission for a payroll deduction — keep all authorizations in writing and limited to specific amounts.
- Do not transfer funds from your personal bank account to the employer before reviewing the gross vs net calculation.
- Do not assume the employer has the right to "self-help" by taking the money from your next paycheck without your consent (state laws often prohibit this).
- Do not delete your copies of the original bonus award letter or the offer letter you signed when hired.

## What to do now
1. **Demand a written "Demand for Repayment" and the specific supporting documentation.** **Ask the employer to provide the exact clause in your signed employment contract, offer letter, or bonus plan that allows for a clawback.** 
   - Request the "Plan Document" for the specific bonus (e.g., Annual Incentive Plan or Retention Agreement).
   - Ask for a line-item breakdown of the amount claimed.
2. **Determine if the bonus was "Discretionary" or "Non-discretionary" under 2026 FLSA standards.** **Check if the bonus was promised in advance based on meeting specific metrics (Non-discretionary) or given as a surprise gift (Discretionary).**
   - If non-discretionary, the employer may have already included this in your "regular rate" for overtime; a clawback may require a recalculation of your past overtime pay.
3. **Verify the tax year of the original payment compared to the current date.** **Determine if you are being asked to repay the bonus in the same calendar year it was paid or a subsequent year.**
   - If same-year: You typically only repay the "net" amount (what you actually received), and the employer adjusts their payroll taxes.
   - If cross-year: You usually must repay the "gross" amount (including taxes withheld), and you must then seek a tax credit or deduction on your next personal tax return under IRS Publication 525 (Section 1341 "Claim of Right").
4. **Send a formal "Notice of Dispute" to HR and Payroll if they threaten immediate deduction.** **State in writing that you do not authorize any deductions from your wages until the matter is resolved.**
   - Mention your specific state (e.g., "Under California Labor Code 224, I do not authorize this deduction").
   - For employees in California, reference AB 692 (2026) if the clawback is related to "stay-or-pay" provisions for training or relocation, as these are now strictly limited.
5. **Check your state's minimum wage and "free and clear" thresholds.** **Calculate whether the proposed deduction would drop your hourly pay below the state or federal minimum wage for that pay period.**
   - Deductions that reduce pay below minimum wage (currently $16.90 in CA or $7.25 federal) for the employer's benefit are generally illegal under the FLSA.
6. **Capture your evidence outside of company systems.** **Download and save all paystubs, the original bonus notification, and your signed offer letter to a personal device.**
   - Take screenshots of any HR portal messages or "congratulations" emails regarding the bonus.
7. **Contact your State Department of Labor or the federal Wage and Hour Division (WHD).** **Prepare a summary of the dispute, including the dates of payment and the written demand you received.**
   - Ask for a "wage and hour" investigator to clarify if your state allows "self-help" deductions for overpayments.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to file a lawsuit or a formal DOL complaint in the first 24 hours.
- You do not need to negotiate a monthly payment plan until you are certain the debt is legally owed.
- You do not need to correct your prior year's tax return; cross-year repayments are handled on the return for the year the repayment occurs.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a demand for money you have already spent is highly stressful. It is common for employers to make clerical errors or attempt to enforce "clawback" policies that are not legally binding in your specific state. Slowing the process down to demand documentation is a standard and safe response.

## Scope note
This guide covers initial stabilization of a bonus repayment demand in the USA. It does not cover specific executive "clawbacks" related to SEC filings or Sarbanes-Oxley requirements, which have different legal standards.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal or tax advice. Bonus clawback legality depends heavily on state-specific labor codes and the specific language of your signed agreements. If the amount is significant, you may need to consult an employment attorney or a certified tax professional.
