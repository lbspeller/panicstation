# What to do if… your airline says your travel documents do not meet the destination entry requirements (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the airline to name the exact entry rule they think you fail, verify that rule with official sources, and either fix that specific gap or move quickly to rebooking or refund options so you are not treated as a no-show.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep arguing without getting the exact refusal reason: passport validity, visa, ESTA, ETA, transit rule, name mismatch, or document type.
- Do not check bags while you may need to change flights, change carriers, or leave the airport.
- Do not assume a temporary, emergency, or limited-validity passport will be accepted by every country.
- Do not let the flight depart without asking what happens to onward and return segments.
- Do not rely on social media posts, forum answers, or screenshots from strangers for entry rules at the airport.
- Do not hand over original documents unless you can see them and get them back immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the refusal reason in one sentence.** Say: “Which specific requirement do you believe I do not meet, and is it for the destination or a transit country?”
2. **Ask the airline to show the rule it is using.** Ask the agent or supervisor to re-check the airline’s travel-document system and confirm the nationality, passport type, destination, transit points, and travel dates entered.
3. **Check official sources on your phone.**
   - Check the destination government’s official entry rules for your nationality and passport type.
   - Check the U.S. State Department destination page as a starting point for passport validity, visa, and entry information for U.S. citizens.
   - Use the IATA Travel Centre as a structured cross-check, because many airline document decisions rely on IATA Timatic data.
   - If you are traveling to the United States under the Visa Waiver Program, check that your ESTA is approved, not just submitted or pending.
4. **Check the common document failure points.**
   - Passport validity beyond arrival, stay, or planned departure.
   - Blank passport pages.
   - Transit visa or airport transit rules.
   - Ticket name compared with passport name, including middle names, hyphens, suffixes, and recent name changes.
   - Visa, ETA, ESTA, or other travel authorization status.
   - Whether the destination accepts emergency or limited-validity passports.
   - Whether you need proof of onward travel, return travel, residence status, or permission to enter a later destination.
5. **Escalate before the cutoff.** Say: “Please escalate this for a document re-check. I believe I meet the requirement, and I need to know the exact rule before the flight closes.”
6. **Show proof, but keep control of originals.** Show approval notices, visas, residence cards, onward bookings, passport pages, or official entry-rule pages. Keep your passport and original documents in your possession unless a staff member needs a brief inspection.
7. **Fix only the specific gap if it can be fixed now.**
   - If an authorization is missing or pending, apply or refresh the status only through the official site if you are eligible.
   - If the name is the issue, ask what correction, reissue, or rebooking option is available before departure.
   - If the passport is the issue and you are a U.S. citizen abroad, contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate about urgent passport options, and check whether the destination and any transit country will accept an emergency or limited-validity passport.
8. **If the fix will not happen before departure, switch to damage control.**
   - Ask the airline what it can do now: rebook later, reroute, cancel, or refund.
   - Ask whether change fees or fare differences can be waived because the refusal happened at document check.
   - Ask what happens to your onward and return flights if this segment is missed.
9. **Get the refusal documented.** Ask for a written reason for refusal, a disruption note, a printout, an email, or a note in the booking. Save screenshots of the reservation, check-in status, document message, authorization status, and the official rule you relied on.
10. **Use redress only if the issue looks like a repeated screening or identity mix-up.** If you repeatedly cannot print a boarding pass, are repeatedly sent for extra screening, or are repeatedly delayed for a possible identity match, DHS TRIP may be the right later channel.

## What can wait
- You do not need to calculate compensation at the counter; first secure a workable flight plan and written documentation.
- You do not need to decide today whether to file a DOT complaint; save the facts and deal with the immediate booking first.
- You do not need to prove the whole law to the first agent; a supervisor re-check and a documented rule check may be enough.
- You do not need to solve the entire trip if the next safe step is rebooking one segment, retrieving bags, or leaving the airport to get the right document.

## Important reassurance
This often turns on one narrow issue: passport validity, transit rules, authorization status, document type, or a mismatch in passenger details. Getting the airline to name the exact rule gives you the best chance to fix it or move to a clear rebooking or refund plan.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the airport moment: clarify the reason, verify the rule, fix what can be fixed quickly, and protect your booking and evidence. Later disputes may depend on the airline’s contract of carriage, the route, the destination’s rules, and whether the refusal was really about documents or something else.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, financial, or professional advice. Entry requirements depend on nationality, route, transit points, document type, and personal status, and they can change. Airlines can usually refuse boarding if they believe documentation is inadequate. In the United States, denied boarding compensation rules generally apply to involuntary bumping due to oversales in specific situations, not ordinary refusals based on travel documents.
