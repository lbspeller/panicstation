# What to do if… you notice someone repeatedly positioning themselves to intercept you at doors or narrow passages (USA)

## Short answer
Do not go through the next doorway, elevator, stairwell, narrow hall, or other chokepoint with them. Step back into a safer staffed or public place, and call 911 if you feel in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not enter an elevator, stairwell, narrow hallway, parking-garage door, or doorway if they appear to be setting up to funnel you through it.
- Do not confront them in a confined space or let them get between you and an exit.
- Do not go straight to your home, car, hotel room, or a quiet side entrance while you are unsure what is happening.
- Do not get stuck focusing on keys or your phone at a door while they are close behind you.
- Do not accept “help” that moves you somewhere more secluded, such as “come this way” or “let me show you”.
- Do not test them by letting them get close at the next door.

## What to do now
1. **Interrupt the setup.** Stop before the next door or chokepoint. Turn around, change direction, or step into the nearest open staffed place, such as a store, lobby, reception desk, café, or building office.
2. **Stand where you can leave.** Choose a spot where other people can see you, where you are not boxed in, and where you have a clear route back out.
3. **Bring in staff or security immediately.** Say: “I do not feel safe. Someone keeps positioning themselves to intercept me at doors. Can you stay with me, call security, or help me leave?” If you are in an apartment building, go back to the main entrance, front desk, or management office instead of using a quieter entry.
4. **Call 911 if you feel threatened, blocked, followed into a confined area, or unable to leave safely.** Start with your exact location and what is happening: “They keep moving to doorways to intercept me.” If you cannot safely stay on the line, say that and follow the operator’s instructions as best you can.
5. **If you are driving and think you are being followed, do not drive home.** Keep driving legally to a well-lit public place with people, such as a busy gas station or store, or to a staffed police, sheriff, or fire station if one is nearby. Call 911 when you can do so safely. Do not speed or use your phone in a way that makes driving unsafe.
6. **Collect only the basics if it is safe.** Note the time, exact location, direction of travel, and a plain description. If there may be security camera footage, ask staff to note the incident time and preserve relevant footage if their policy allows.
7. **Report a repeated pattern after you are safe.** Even if nothing happened beyond the intercepting behavior, repeated incidents can matter. For active danger, call 911. For non-urgent reporting, contact your local police department’s non-emergency line or dispatch line and ask how to make a report.
8. **If the same person keeps appearing near your entrances, exits, or routine routes, treat it as a safety issue rather than a debate.** Ask local police, campus security, building security, workplace security, or a victim-support resource about safety planning and local options. Processes vary by state and situation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it “counts as stalking” or whether you can prove intent.
- You do not need perfect evidence, video, or photos. Do not trade safety for documentation.
- You do not need to negotiate, explain yourself, be polite, or continue toward the next doorway to avoid seeming rude.
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to change your whole routine. First get to a safer place and involve support.

## Important reassurance
Noticing a repeated pattern around doors and chokepoints is enough reason to create distance, move toward witnesses, and ask for help. You do not have to wait for the situation to become clearer before taking basic safety steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and buy time. If this is recurring around your home, work, campus, commute, or routine routes, later decisions may need help from local police, a victim advocate, building security, campus security, or another specialist support service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe or threatened, call 911.
