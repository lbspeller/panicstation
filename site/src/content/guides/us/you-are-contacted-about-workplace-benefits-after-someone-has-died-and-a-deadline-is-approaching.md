# What to do if… you are contacted about workplace benefits after someone has died and a deadline is approaching (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the contact through an official employer or plan route. Ask for the exact benefit, deadline, required documents, and submission method in writing, and ask whether any extension or other option is available.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use a phone number, link, or attachment from an unexpected message until you have verified the contact independently.
- Don’t send Social Security numbers, bank details, identity documents, or death certificates until you know who is requesting them and why.
- Don’t assume that the person contacted is automatically the beneficiary or the person authorised to act.
- Don’t assume that a partial submission or an extension request protects a deadline unless the administrator confirms this in writing.
- Don’t pay an unexpected fee to release benefits until you have verified it through an official contact route.
- Don’t make tax, rollover, or distribution decisions while you are trying to meet an immediate paperwork deadline.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the benefit and the deadline.** Ask the verified contact to list each benefit separately, such as group life insurance, a retirement plan, a pension, health coverage, or final wages. Ask what must be received, by whom, and on what date.
2. **Verify the contact independently.** Use the employer’s official website, main switchboard, a benefits booklet you already have, or an official plan statement. Ask to be connected to the benefits office, plan administrator, or insurer.
3. **Ask for written instructions.** Request the claim form, the submission address or portal, the deadline, and the written plan instructions that apply. For an employer retirement or health plan, ask for the Summary Plan Description or other applicable plan documents if you do not have them.
4. **Ask what is required now and what can follow later.** Ask whether they need a certified death certificate or a copy, whether they need proof of your identity or relationship, and whether estate-authority documents are required for this specific request.
5. **If the deadline is close, write before it passes.** Tell the verified contact that you are gathering documents. Ask what must be submitted by the deadline, whether an extension or another option is available, and how any decision will be confirmed. Do not assume extra time has been granted unless you receive written confirmation.
6. **Check health coverage promptly if a spouse or dependents may lose coverage.** Ask when coverage ends, whether federal COBRA or another continuation option may apply, and when an election notice will be sent. For people who qualify for federal COBRA, the election period is generally at least 60 days from the later of the date the election notice is provided or the date coverage would otherwise end.
7. **Save deadline proof.** Keep the written instructions, the name of each contact, copies of what you submit, and any portal receipt, mailing receipt, or confirmation email.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today how to take, roll over, or invest retirement benefits.
- You do not need to resolve a beneficiary dispute before asking for the written plan rules and deadlines.
- You do not need to settle the whole estate before asking what documents are required for this specific benefit.
- Social Security reporting can be checked separately if you are unsure it happened. Funeral homes generally report deaths to Social Security, but the Social Security Administration can explain what to do if that did not happen.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to solve every part of the estate today. Verifying the contact, getting the deadline in writing, and asking exactly what must be submitted first are useful protective steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps for a workplace-benefits deadline after a death. A denied claim, a disputed beneficiary designation, an estate-authority question, or a complicated tax decision may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, tax, financial, insurance, or professional advice. Workplace plans, insurance policies, and state rules vary.
