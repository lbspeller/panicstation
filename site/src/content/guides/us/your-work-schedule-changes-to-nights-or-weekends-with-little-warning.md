# What to do if… your work schedule changes to nights or weekends with little warning (USA)

## Short answer
Get the changed schedule confirmed in writing, save proof, and do not decide in panic whether to quit or refuse the shift. Then check whether your handbook, contract, union agreement, or a local scheduling law applies where you work.

## Do not do these things
- Do not no-call or no-show without notifying someone; it can quickly become a discipline or termination issue.
- Do not quit on the spot before checking whether a workplace policy, union agreement, contract, or local scheduling law may apply.
- Do not sign a new availability form, schedule change agreement, or waiver until you have read it carefully.
- Do not rely only on a verbal argument; keep the key facts in writing.
- Do not post about it on social media or workplace group chats where it could be forwarded.

## What to do now
1. **Save proof before anything disappears.** Screenshot the old schedule, the new schedule, app notifications, texts, emails, and any message saying the change is required. Write down who told you, when they told you, and exactly what changed.
2. **Ask for the change in writing.** Send a short message asking for the new start date, whether the change is temporary or permanent, which nights or weekends are required, and how future schedule changes will be sent.
3. **Check the documents that may control your schedule.** Look at your offer letter, employee handbook, scheduling policy, availability form, and any union contract. If one of them promises advance notice, limits nights or weekends, or sets a process for availability changes, use that wording in your reply.
4. **Check whether a local scheduling rule may cover you.** Federal wage law generally does not require advance notice for schedule changes, except for child labor rules and any separate agreement. Some states and cities have predictive scheduling or fair workweek rules for certain industries, employer sizes, and job types, including:
   - Oregon, for covered large retail, hospitality, and food-service employers.
   - New York City, for covered fast food and retail employers.
   - Seattle, for covered hourly retail and food-service workers.
   - San Francisco, for covered formula retail employers.
   - Chicago, for covered workers under its Fair Workweek rules.
5. **Reply with what you can and cannot do right now.** Keep it calm and practical. Say which nights or weekends you can cover, which ones you cannot cover, and why the conflict exists, such as childcare, transport, school, a second job, or a safety concern.
6. **Offer a short-term alternative.** Suggest a specific option, such as swapping shifts, covering only certain weekends, delaying the change for a set number of days, working a limited trial period, or keeping your previous availability until a meeting happens.
7. **If a disability or medical condition is involved, use the accommodation route.** Tell HR or the person your workplace designates that you are requesting a schedule adjustment because of a medical condition or disability. A request does not have to use special legal words or be in writing, but writing it down helps; HR may ask for reasonable documentation.
8. **If the issue is unpaid wages or overtime, separate that from the scheduling issue.** Save pay records and contact your state labor agency or the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division if you are not paid for hours worked, overtime, or required off-the-clock work.
9. **If you may be covered by a local scheduling law, contact the enforcing office.** Use the official city or state labor standards page for the place where you work and ask how to check coverage before assuming the rule does or does not apply.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to quit, file a complaint, or make a long-term career decision.
- You do not need to write a long statement right now.
- You do not need to prove every legal point before asking for the schedule details in writing.
- You do not need to solve every future week today; focus first on the next changed shifts and a workable short-term plan.

## Important reassurance
A sudden move to nights or weekends can disrupt sleep, childcare, transport, school, and money all at once. A written record and a specific short-term request can slow the situation down and give you more room to decide what comes next.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a union representative, worker center, employment lawyer, accommodation specialist, or the labor standards office that covers the place where you work.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Rules vary by state, city, industry, employer size, job type, contract, union agreement, and workplace policy.
