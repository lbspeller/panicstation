# What to do if… you submitted an online entry declaration with an error and you cannot edit it before you arrive (USA)

## Short answer
Determine if the error is "identity-critical" (Name, Passport Number, Birth Date, Citizenship); if it is, you must submit a new application/declaration immediately. For non-critical errors (Email, Address in US), update the existing record via the official portal or disclose the correction to the officer upon arrival.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to board a flight with a known mismatch in your name, passport number, or date of birth on an ESTA.
- Do not use third-party "processing" websites that charge fees above the official $40 government fee for ESTA.
- Do not submit a second declaration in the Mobile Passport Control (MPC) app until you have deleted the previous incorrect trip profile, or the 4-hour window has expired.
- Do not omit social media handles or contact details to "save time" if the form now requires them.
- Do not ignore the error; airline systems (APIS) perform automated checks against travel authorizations and will likely block your boarding pass.

## What to do now
1. **Verify which specific system contains the error.**
   - **Electronic System for Travel Authorization (ESTA):** Used by Visa Waiver Program travelers.
   - **Mobile Passport Control (MPC):** Used by US citizens and eligible visitors to submit customs declarations via app.
   - **I-94 Arrival/Departure Record:** Generally handled at the point of entry, but pre-payment/application is possible for land borders.
2. **Determine if a new application is mandatory.**
   - **You MUST apply for a new ESTA ($40 fee)** if the error is in the passport number, passport issuing country, country of citizenship, name, or date of birth.
   - **You MAY edit an existing ESTA** for your email address or the physical address where you are staying in the USA.
3. **Rectify errors in the Mobile Passport Control (MPC) app before landing.**
   - **Delete the incorrect "Trip" or "Profile" within the app.**
   - **Create a new submission with the corrected data.**
   - **Note the 4-hour window:** MPC declarations are only valid for 4 hours after submission; if your mistake is older than 4 hours, the record is already inactive.
4. **Prepare physical evidence of the correct information.**
   - **Print the new, corrected confirmation page** if you re-submitted.
   - **Bring the original documents (Passport, Visa)** to demonstrate the mistake was a clerical typo.
5. **Update your airline's Advanced Passenger Information System (APIS) record.**
   - **Access your flight booking online** and ensure the data there matches your passport exactly, even if it now contradicts your first (incorrect) declaration.
6. **Disclose the error at the check-in counter.**
   - **Speak to the gate agent or check-in staff early.** They can often manually verify the new travel authorization if the system has not yet synced.
7. **Be factual with the U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) officer.**
   - **Wait for the officer to review your documents.** If they ask about the discrepancy, state: "I realized there was a typo on my initial submission and I have re-submitted the correct details here."

## What can wait
- You do not need to contact the U.S. Embassy or Consulate for a typo on a travel authorization.
- You do not need to worry about the "lodging address" or "point of contact" errors as much as identity errors; these can often be clarified verbally at the booth.
- You do not need to "cancel" a previous incorrect ESTA; a new approved application automatically supersedes the previous one for the same passport.

## Important reassurance
Clerical errors on travel forms are extremely common and CBP officers are trained to handle "good faith" typos. As long as your physical passport is valid and you have a new, matching travel authorization for identity-critical fields, a secondary typo is rarely grounds for denial of entry.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for errors found after submitting U.S. entry forms but before passing through U.S. immigration. It does not cover visa application (DS-160) errors or legal advice regarding admissibility.

## Important note
This information reflects U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) procedures as of 2026. Travel authorization rules and fees (such as the $40 ESTA fee) are subject to change. If you are denied boarding, contact your airline's supervisor immediately to verify if a new submission has been recognized.
