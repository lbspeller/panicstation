# What to do if… your student loan automatic payment fails because your bank details are incorrect or outdated (USA)

## Short answer
If the payment is due or clearly failed, make one verified one-time payment through your official loan servicer, then update or re-enroll the bank details inside the servicer portal and save proof.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use links in payment-failed texts or emails; log in to StudentAid.gov or your servicer by typing the address yourself.
- Do not pay again until you have checked whether the draft is marked failed, returned, or still processing.
- Do not submit multiple payments in quick succession just in case.
- Do not assume autopay is still active after changing bank accounts, closing an account, or a servicer transfer.
- Do not give anyone your bank login password, full account login access, or one-time codes.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm which loan and servicer are involved.**
   - For federal student loans, log in to StudentAid.gov and confirm your current servicer before using the servicer’s official portal or app.
   - For private student loans, use your lender or servicer’s official portal and your most recent statement to confirm the correct payment site.

2. **Check the exact status of the failed autopay draft.**
   - In the servicer portal, look at the payment history and the most recent draft status.
   - Check whether it says failed, returned, rejected, pending, processing, or scheduled.
   - Save a screenshot, payment-history download, confirmation email, or message from the portal.

3. **If the due date is close, or the payment is clearly failed, make one manual payment.**
   - Pay only through the official servicer portal or another verified servicer payment route.
   - Use an available payment method shown by the servicer.
   - Save the confirmation number, amount, date, and time.

4. **Fix the stored bank details inside the servicer portal.**
   - Retype the routing number and account number carefully.
   - Confirm checking or savings if the portal asks.
   - Remove any old bank account if the portal allows it, so future drafts do not keep going to the wrong account.
   - If autopay was switched off, re-enroll only after the new details are correct, then save the next draft date and amount shown in the portal.

5. **Call your bank if the rejection reason is unclear.**
   - Ask whether the account is closed, frozen, restricted, blocked for ACH debits, or listed with details that do not match the servicer’s debit request.
   - Ask whether any returned-payment or overdraft fee was applied.
   - If a wrong automatic debit is still scheduled, ask about stopping that specific preauthorized transfer. Federal Regulation E generally allows a stop-payment request to your bank at least three business days before the scheduled transfer, but ask the bank what it needs from you and save proof of the request.

6. **Ask the servicer to confirm your account status in writing.**
   - Ask whether the account is current, what the next due date and amount are, and whether autopay is active.
   - Ask whether any returned-payment fee or late charge was applied, and whether it can be reviewed or waived.
   - If the servicer says the failed payment caused a negative report or delinquency marker, ask them to review the account and explain what record they are using.

7. **Escalate only if the account is not corrected or the records do not match.**
   - For federal student loans, submit a case through Federal Student Aid’s feedback route and attach your payment proof, bank information, and servicer messages.
   - For private student loans, federal student loan servicing problems, or bank-account problems, you can submit a CFPB complaint with the same documentation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on consolidation, refinancing, or a new repayment plan today.
- You do not need to dispute credit reporting unless you actually see an error or the servicer says a negative report was made.
- You do not need to optimise extra payments now; focus on getting this payment and the next automatic draft under control.

## Important reassurance
This is a fixable account and payment-routing problem. One verified payment, corrected bank details, and saved written proof usually gives you enough room to stop the same failure repeating.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to reduce repeat payment problems and missed-payment consequences. Later decisions about repayment plans, refinancing, disputes, or complaints may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Rules and options vary by loan type, servicer, and bank. Rely on your official servicer portal, StudentAid.gov for federal loans, and written confirmations for your own account.
