# What to do if… you are asked about organ or tissue donation and feel unprepared to decide (USA)

## Short answer
Ask to speak with the donation professional handling the request. If organs are being discussed, ask for the Organ Procurement Organization (OPO) representative. Say that you feel unprepared, and ask them to explain what donor record was found, what it means in this state and situation, and what—if anything—you are being asked to authorize now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t say yes just to end the conversation if you do not understand what is being discussed.
- Don’t assume you are deciding everything from scratch. A documented donor decision may already exist.
- Don’t treat a driver’s-license or ID marker as the whole answer. Ask the donation professional what records were checked and what they mean.
- Don’t feel you have to absorb every detail at once. Ask for a brief pause and a plain-language recap.
- Don’t assume donation costs will be billed to the family or estate. Donation costs are not charged to the donor’s family or estate, but medical care before death and funeral arrangements are separate.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the donation professional handling the request and slow the pace.**  
   Say: “I feel unprepared. Please tell me which organization you represent, explain the smallest decision that needs attention now, and tell me whether we can take a brief pause.”

2. **Ask what donor record was checked.**  
   Say: “Did you find a documented donor decision in a registry or another donor record? What does that record mean in this state and situation, and what remains for us to decide?”

3. **Ask for a simple list of what is being discussed.**  
   Ask whether the conversation is about:
   - organs
   - tissues
   - eyes or corneas
   - any choices that remain available in this situation

4. **Ask what information the donation professional needs from you.**  
   You may be asked for medical and social history. Say: “Please tell me which questions you need answered now and which questions can wait.”

5. **Ask about practical arrangements.**  
   Say:
   - “Will we still be able to see them afterward or have a viewing or open-casket funeral?”
   - “Could this affect release to the funeral home in this case?”
   - “Please confirm which medical care and funeral costs remain separate from donation costs.”

6. **Ask whether a medical examiner or coroner is involved.**  
   Say: “Is a medical examiner or coroner involved, and does that affect the timing or options in this case?”

7. **Share only what you know.**  
   Tell the donation professional about any wishes, beliefs, or concerns your loved one clearly expressed. Ask what choices remain available. You do not need to fill gaps with guesses.

8. **Ask how much time is available.**  
   Say: “How much time do we have for any decision that is still ours to make? Why does the timing matter, and what happens if we do not authorize anything?”

9. **Make the conversation easier to follow.**  
   Ask the donation professional to write down the immediate question. Choose one person to take notes or help ask questions if that would reduce confusion. Ask who is legally able to make any remaining decision.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide memorial details, obituary wording, estate steps, or wider family communications during this conversation.
- You do not need to resolve every family disagreement before asking the donation professional to clarify the immediate question.
- You do not need to decide anything about recipient contact, letters, or follow-up programs now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling unprepared is understandable. You can ask for repetition, a brief pause, and a simple explanation of what is already documented and what still needs attention.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: reducing pressure, clarifying the donor record, and identifying the smallest immediate question. Later decisions may need support from the donation professional, the hospital team, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. Donation rules and processes can vary by state and by situation. The donation professional and hospital team can explain what applies in your case.
