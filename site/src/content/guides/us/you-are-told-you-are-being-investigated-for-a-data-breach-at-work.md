# What to do if… you are told you are being investigated for a data breach at work (USA)

## Short answer
Do not delete, alter, forward, or try to fix anything. Ask for the allegation and meeting purpose in writing; if you are union-represented and the meeting is an investigatory interview you reasonably believe could lead to discipline, clearly request your union representative before answering investigatory questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete or alter emails, chats, files, tickets, logs, histories, or device settings, even if you think it would help.
- Do not search systems you do not normally use or take extra technical steps to prove your case unless you are specifically authorized to do so.
- Do not move work data to personal email, personal cloud storage, messaging apps, or removable media.
- Do not ask coworkers to align accounts or post publicly about the situation. This does not prevent you from speaking with your union representative or attorney.
- Do not sign a substantive statement, agreement, resignation, or settlement document on the spot without reading it carefully and considering advice. Ask whether you are only being asked to acknowledge receipt.
- Do not resign impulsively to escape the immediate stress.

## What to do now
1. **Preserve the current state.** Avoid non-essential changes to work systems, files, accounts, and devices. Follow authorized incident-response instructions and ask for clarification if an instruction is unclear. Write down the time you were notified and what you were doing at that moment without copying sensitive data.
2. **Ask for the allegation and meeting purpose in writing.** Ask what conduct is being reviewed, which data or systems are involved, the relevant time period, whether the meeting is fact-finding or disciplinary, who will attend, and whether documents will be provided in advance.
3. **If you are union-represented, make a clear representation request before answering investigatory questions.** Current NLRB guidance says union-represented employees may request a representative during an investigatory interview they reasonably believe could lead to discipline. Say: “I request my union representative before answering investigatory questions.” The employer may delay the interview until a representative is available, end the interview, or let you choose whether to continue without a representative or end the interview. Ask your union whether NLRA coverage or another applicable rule applies to you.
4. **If you are not union-represented, ask to slow the process down.** Ask whether the meeting can be rescheduled so you can review the written allegation and consult an employment attorney. Ask whether company policy allows a support person or witness. The employer may decline.
5. **Make a factual timeline.** Note relevant dates, tasks, approvals, instructions, tickets, and meetings, along with what you do not know. Do not add theories or copy sensitive work data into personal notes. Ask your union representative or attorney how to store notes securely.
6. **Keep only materials you are permitted to retain.** This may include meeting notices, your job description, relevant policies, training acknowledgements, and written instructions. Do not export confidential files, logs, customer records, or other sensitive data to build a defense.
7. **Use the designated internal reporting channel for incident facts.** If you know facts that may help the response team, report them through the company’s security, incident-response, compliance, or legal channel. Keep the distribution narrow and stick to facts.
8. **Handle device and credential requests carefully.** Follow authorized instructions for handing over company devices or access. If credentials are requested, ask for the approved method and do not send passwords through an unapproved channel. Do not delete, reset, or alter anything first.
9. **Get case-specific advice promptly if the stakes appear serious.** Consider contacting an employment attorney if termination is threatened, you are asked to sign a substantive document, law enforcement contacts you, or a professional-license issue may be involved.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to resign, threaten legal action, or make a public statement.
- You do not need to produce a complete narrative before you understand the allegation.
- You do not need to contact customers, regulators, or the media personally unless you are specifically authorized to do so.
- You do not need to run your own technical investigation unless that is your assigned role and you have clear instructions.

## Important reassurance
A security alert or workplace investigation is not proof that you did anything wrong. Preserving the current state, using approved channels, and answering carefully can protect both the investigation and your ability to explain what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may require specialist help because workplace processes and representation rules can depend on union status, private-sector or public-sector employment, contract terms, employer policy, and the rules that apply to your situation.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, cybersecurity, employment, or other professional advice. Avoid irreversible actions, keep permitted notes of requests and meeting details, and seek qualified advice for your specific situation.
