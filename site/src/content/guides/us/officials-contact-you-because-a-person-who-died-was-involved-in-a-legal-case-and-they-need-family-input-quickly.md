# What to do if… officials contact you because a person who died was involved in a legal case and they need family input quickly (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the agency and the individual contacting you using a phone number you look up yourself on an official government website (.gov). Obtain the case number, the specific request, and the deadline in writing before providing sensitive information or making a legal decision.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t provide Social Security numbers, bank account details, or payment (gift cards, wire transfers, crypto) during an unsolicited call.
- Don’t trust the name or number shown on your caller ID, as official government numbers are frequently spoofed by scammers.
- Don’t follow links in unexpected emails or text messages claiming to be from a court or law enforcement agency.
- Don’t feel pressured to make an immediate legal decision while grieving; legitimate officials will allow time for verification and for you to consult a representative.
- Don’t send original documents like death certificates or wills; send certified copies only when the recipient's identity is verified.
- Don’t guess or speculate if you are unsure of the answer to an official's question, as inaccurate information can complicate legal proceedings.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the agency and the caller’s specific role.**
   - Ask for their full name, department, office location, and a direct callback number.
   - Note if they mention being a "Victim-Witness Coordinator," "Detective," "Medical Examiner," or "Clerk of Court."
2. **Request the case details for independent verification.**
   - **Ask for the Case Number, the name of the Court or Agency, and the Presiding Judge (if applicable).**
   - **End the call and look up the official main-line number for that specific office on a .gov website.**
   - **Call that main-line number and ask to be transferred to the individual who contacted you to confirm the request is legitimate.**
3. **Move the communication into a written format.**
   - **Ask the official to send the request via email from a .gov address or by physical mail on official letterhead.**
   - **Ensure the written request specifies exactly what input is needed and the legal deadline.**
4. **Determine who has the legal authority to act for the estate.**
   - **Check if a "Personal Representative," "Executor," or "Administrator" has been appointed by a probate court.**
   - **Locate the "Letters Testamentary" (if there was a will) or "Letters of Administration" (if there was no will).**
   - **If no one is appointed yet, inform the official: "We do not have a court-appointed representative yet; please clarify what information can be provided by the next-of-kin in the interim."**
5. **Ask about the Victim Notification System (VNS) for federal cases.**
   - **If the case is federal, ask if you should be registered in the VNS to receive automated updates.**
   - **Request a Victim Identification Number (VIN) and Personal Identification Number (PIN) to access the system securely.**
6. **Set a single point of contact for the family.**
   - **Designate one person to handle all communications with the agency to prevent conflicting information.**
   - **Keep a log of all interactions, including dates, times, names of officials, and summaries of what was discussed.**
7. **Verify the deadline and its flexibility.**
   - **Ask if the deadline is statutory (fixed by law) or if it can be extended while you seek legal counsel or locate documents.**
8. **Contact the Social Security Administration (SSA) if the request involves benefits.**
   - **Call 1-800-772-1213 to verify any claims regarding the deceased’s Social Security status or survivor benefits.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve the entire probate process or settle the deceased's debts today.
- You do not need to agree to a settlement or sign a waiver of rights during the initial contact.
- You do not need to choose a long-term legal strategy for the case immediately.
- You do not need to answer questions about family members who are not directly involved in the specific legal request.

## Important reassurance
Receiving urgent "official" contact shortly after a death is common in cases involving accidents, crimes, or pending litigation. Taking time to verify the caller’s identity is a standard and necessary security step. Professional officials expect and respect this verification process; urgency that ignores verification is a primary indicator of a scam.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling urgent official contact. It does not replace legal advice or the requirements of specific state probate laws. Ongoing legal cases or complex estates will require the assistance of a qualified attorney.

## Important note
Government agencies like the IRS, SSA, and U.S. Courts do not initiate contact via social media or demand immediate payment via gift cards or wire transfers. If you suspect a scam, report it to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) at reportfraud.ftc.gov.
