# What to do if… the name on your flight ticket does not match your passport (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the ticket seller now using an official contact route and ask whether your reservation or passenger details need correction to match the passport you will use. If departure is close or online check-in fails, go to the airline counter early with your passport.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume that updating an airline profile or frequent-flyer account automatically changes an issued ticket.
- Do not assume that a missing middle name or formatting difference is the same as a completely different passenger name.
- Do not buy a replacement ticket before asking the ticket seller what correction, cancellation, credit, refund, or rebooking options are available.
- Do not send passport images or personal details through social media messages or unofficial contact routes.
- Do not rely on the airport security checkpoint to correct the reservation.

## What to do now
1. **Compare the names carefully.** Note whether the issue is a one-letter typo, a missing middle name, a nickname, a surname change, swapped first and last names, or a different passenger name.
2. **Contact the ticket seller through an official route.**
   - If you booked through an online travel agency or travel agent, contact that seller first.
   - If you booked directly with the airline, contact the airline through its official website, app, phone number, or chat.
3. **Ask for a same-passenger name check.** Say: “The name on my booking does not match the passport I will use. Please check whether the passenger details need correction and whether the ticket must be reissued.”
4. **Ask what the change may affect.** Confirm whether there is a fee, a fare difference, a new ticket number, or any action needed for each flight in the itinerary.
5. **Check partner flights.** If another airline operates any part of the trip, ask the seller to confirm that the updated details will be recognised across the itinerary.
6. **Keep supporting documents available if relevant.** If the mismatch follows a legal name change, have your passport and an official name-change document ready in case the seller or airline asks for it.
7. **Go to the airline counter early if needed.** If departure is close, online check-in fails, or the seller tells you to seek airport help, bring your passport and booking confirmation to the airline counter.
8. **Pause before rebooking.** If the seller says the existing ticket cannot be corrected, ask what cancellation, credit, refund, or rebooking options apply before purchasing another ticket.
9. **Check the 24-hour option if it may apply.** If you paid for the ticket directly with an airline less than 24 hours ago and departure is at least seven days away, ask whether you can cancel for a full refund and rebook. The airline rule does not apply to tickets booked through an online travel agency, travel agent, or other third party, although that seller may offer its own policy.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to complain or seek reimbursement while you are trying to secure the trip.
- You do not need to replace or change your passport as the first step unless the passport itself is inaccurate, damaged, expired, or otherwise unusable.
- You do not need to resolve every policy question before asking the ticket seller for the available options.

## Important reassurance
A name mismatch does not automatically mean the trip is lost. The useful next step is to get the ticket seller or airline to check the specific mismatch and tell you what can be corrected before departure.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The available options may depend on the ticket seller, airline policy, fare rules, the type of mismatch, and whether another airline operates part of the itinerary. Later decisions may need help from the ticket seller, airline, or an appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, security, travel-agent, or other professional advice. A clearly different passenger name needs prompt checking. Minor middle-name, spacing, punctuation, or display differences should still be confirmed with the ticket seller or airline rather than assumed to be a problem or assumed to be harmless.
