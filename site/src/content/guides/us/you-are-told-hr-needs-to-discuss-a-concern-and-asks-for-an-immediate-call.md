# What to do if… you are told HR needs to discuss a concern and asks for an immediate call (USA)

## Short answer
Ask for a brief pause so you can move somewhere private, take notes, and get the topic at a high level before joining the call. If you are union-represented and reasonably believe questioning could lead to discipline, clearly request union representation before answering investigatory questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign, offer to resign, or make a major employment decision while you are rattled.
- Do not guess, ramble, or fill silence with details you are not sure about.
- Do not admit wrongdoing just to end an uncomfortable conversation.
- Do not delete, edit, or clean up work messages, files, or calendar entries after receiving the HR message.
- Do not contact coworkers to coordinate accounts or pressure anyone to support your version.
- Do not assume the vague message proves that a decision has already been made.

## What to do now
1. **Reply briefly in writing.** Ask for the topic at a high level, who will attend, and whether the call is expected to involve questions about your conduct or performance. Ask for a short pause so you can move somewhere private and take notes. For example:  
   “I received your message. Please share the topic at a high level and who will be on the call. I can join at [earliest practical time] once I am somewhere private and able to take notes.”
2. **Save the message and get ready to take notes.** Keep the original HR message. Open a dated note with the time, the names of the people involved, and any questions you want to ask at the start.
3. **If you are union-represented, request representation when the conditions may apply.** If the meeting involves questioning and you reasonably believe it could lead to discipline, say:  
   “If this is an investigatory interview that could lead to discipline, I request union representation before answering questions.”  
   You need to make the request yourself. Ask to pause so your representative can attend. If the employer denies the request and keeps asking investigatory questions, say that you do not want to answer further investigatory questions without representation and contact your union promptly.
4. **If you are not union-represented, ask what is allowed.** You may ask for a brief pause, ask whether you may bring a support person, and check whether a workplace policy, contract, or public-sector rule applies. The employer may say no.
5. **Start by clarifying the purpose.** Ask: “What is the concern in plain terms, and what are you asking me to do today?” If the purpose changes during the call, pause and ask for clarification.
6. **Keep your answers accurate and narrow.** Answer what you know. When you are unsure about a date, message, or event, say: “I do not want to guess. I need to check that and follow up.”
7. **Confirm any requested next step.** Before the call ends, ask whether HR wants a written response, another meeting, or documents, and ask for any deadline in writing.
8. **Write a dated note immediately afterward.** Record who attended, the concern described, the questions asked, what you answered, any request for representation or a pause, and any deadline. Send a concise written confirmation only when useful, such as to confirm a deadline or a requested follow-up.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out motives, office politics, or every possible outcome right now.
- You do not need to improvise a complete account before you understand the topic. Ask whether you can check details and provide a written follow-up where appropriate.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to make a complaint or seek legal advice unless an urgent deadline, safety issue, or other immediate risk applies.

## Important reassurance
A vague HR message does not by itself tell you the subject of the call or the likely outcome. A brief pause, a private setting, accurate notes, and careful answers can help you handle the first conversation without making a rushed decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the message and the first call only. Later decisions may need help from a union representative, an appropriate workplace support route, or an employment lawyer, depending on the concern and your situation.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, HR, or other professional advice. Under current information published by the National Labor Relations Board Office of the General Counsel, Weingarten rights generally apply when a union-represented employee requests representation for an investigatory interview the employee reasonably believes could lead to discipline. Not every HR meeting is covered, and some workers, including public-sector employees, are outside National Labor Relations Act coverage; other rules may apply. Workplace processes can also vary by state, sector, contract, collective bargaining agreement, and employer policy.
