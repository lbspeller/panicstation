# What to do if… you realise you left your passport in a hotel safe and have to travel soon (USA)

## Short answer
Your first job is to get the physical passport back today. If that is not realistically possible before you need to travel, contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate now and follow their urgent passport instructions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait until you are at the airport; urgent passport help may still take time, especially outside business hours.
- Do not report the passport lost or stolen unless the physical passport is genuinely lost, stolen, or unrecoverable.
- Do not assume you can fly internationally with only a photo, scan, or passport number.
- Do not send your passport bio-page image widely; share it only through a secure route when it is genuinely needed.
- Do not spend the first hour arguing with the hotel about blame, reimbursement, or liability; focus on getting the passport physically secured.

## What to do now
1. **Call the hotel and ask for the duty manager or security manager.** Say clearly: “My U.S. passport is in the room safe. I travel tomorrow. I need the passport removed and secured today.” Ask them to use their manager or security safe-access procedure.
2. **Ask the hotel to confirm the passport is physically there.** Ask them to open the safe, identify that it is your U.S. passport, place it in a sealed envelope, store it securely, and send you written confirmation that they have it.
3. **If you can reach the hotel today, go in person.** Bring any other ID you have, such as a driver’s license, passport card, or expired passport, plus your booking confirmation and the room number.
4. **If you cannot reach the hotel in person, arrange the fastest safe transfer.** The simplest options are usually collection by you, collection by a trusted person with written authorisation, or a reputable tracked courier if the hotel agrees.
5. **Set a cutoff time today.** If there is no confirmed, realistic way for you to have the passport before you must leave for the airport, stop relying on retrieval as the only plan and start the embassy or consulate route.
6. **Contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate.** Tell them you are a U.S. citizen, your passport is not in your possession, you have urgent travel, and where the passport is believed to be. Follow their local instructions; you may need to appear in person.
7. **Get the likely documents ready.** This may include a passport photo, photo ID, proof of U.S. citizenship such as a birth certificate or photocopy of the missing passport, travel itinerary, passport forms if instructed, fees if applicable, and details of where and when the passport became unavailable.
8. **Check whether an emergency passport will work for your route.** Emergency passports are usually limited-validity, and some countries may not accept them. Check the entry and exit requirements for your destination and any transit country before relying on one.
9. **Only report the passport lost or stolen if it is genuinely unrecoverable or stolen.** Once a U.S. passport is reported lost or stolen, it is no longer valid for international travel, even if you later find it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a formal hotel complaint.
- You do not need to settle reimbursement, travel insurance, or hotel liability before dealing with the passport.
- You usually do not need a police report unless there is suspected theft or the embassy, consulate, police, airline, or insurer asks for one.
- You do not need to replace the passport if you recover it and it has not been reported lost or stolen.

## Important reassurance
This is frightening because the deadline is close, but it is a practical problem with two clear routes: recover the passport quickly, or move early to official urgent passport help. Acting now gives you more options than waiting.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilise the next day of travel. Later decisions about complaints, insurance, full-validity replacement, or changed travel plans may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, travel-agent, or other professional advice. Embassy or consulate procedures, airline boarding rules, and destination entry requirements can vary by country, route, timing, and individual circumstances.
