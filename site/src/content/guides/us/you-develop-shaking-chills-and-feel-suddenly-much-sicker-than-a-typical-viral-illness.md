# What to do if… you develop shaking chills and feel suddenly much sicker than a typical viral illness (USA)

## Short answer
Seek immediate, same-day medical evaluation, as these can be signs of sepsis—a life-threatening medical emergency. If you have any emergency warning signs like confusion or trouble breathing, call 911 or go to an Emergency Department immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if you feel better in the morning if you are worsening rapidly.
- Do not drive yourself if you are shivering uncontrollably, feeling faint, or confused.
- Do not take antibiotics that were not specifically prescribed for this current illness.
- Do not exceed the maximum daily dose of fever-reducing medications like acetaminophen or ibuprofen.
- Do not ignore "double sickening," where you start to feel worse again after a few days of improvement.

## What to do now
1. **Identify emergency warning signs and call 911 or go to the Emergency Department immediately if you have any of the following:**
   - New confusion, disorientation, slurred speech, or difficulty staying awake.
   - Trouble breathing, very rapid breathing at rest, or blue, gray, or pale lips or skin.
   - **Extreme body pain or discomfort** that feels significantly worse than a "normal" illness.
   - Skin that is **clammy, sweaty, mottled (blotchy), or discolored**.
   - A high heart rate or a feeling that your heart is racing.
   - A new rash that does not fade (blanch) when you press a glass against it.
   - A feeling of "impending doom" or feeling "the sickest I have ever been."
2. **If you do not have emergency signs but feel unusually ill, contact a clinician urgently.**
   - Call your primary care provider or their after-hours nurse line for a **same-day** appointment or direction.
   - Visit an **Urgent Care** center if you are stable but need immediate evaluation.
   - Choose the **Emergency Department** if you are worsening quickly or have underlying health conditions.
3. **Use specific advocacy language when speaking to medical staff.**
   - State clearly: "**I am concerned about sepsis.**"
   - Say: "**I have shaking chills (rigors) and I feel suddenly much sicker than a typical viral illness.**"
   - Mention if you are "double sickening"—getting worse after you thought you were recovering.
4. **Prepare a "status snapshot" for the medical team.**
   - Write down the exact time the severe shivering started.
   - Record your temperature if possible (or note if you felt "ice cold" or "burning up").
   - Note if you are urinating much less than usual or if your urine is very dark.
   - List any recent triggers: surgery, dental work, a new wound, a UTI, or a recent cough.
5. **Have a support person stay with you.**
   - Ask someone to monitor you for changes in alertness or breathing while you wait for transport or care.
   - If you are alone, tell someone exactly where you are and that you are seeking emergency care.
6. **Bring a list of essential information.**
   - Include all current medications, allergies, and chronic conditions (especially diabetes or immune suppression).
   - If you have no spleen or are currently undergoing chemotherapy, tell the triage nurse immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific source of the infection yourself.
- You do not need to worry about household tasks or work obligations until you have been medically cleared.
- You do not need to "prove" how sick you are; the medical team will use objective vitals to assess you.

## Important reassurance
Developing shaking chills and a sudden sense of being dangerously ill is a valid and urgent reason to seek medical help. Sepsis moves quickly, and acting early is the most effective way to ensure a safe recovery. Most people who seek care early for these symptoms have much better outcomes.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and advocacy instructions for the first hours of a suspected severe infection or sepsis. It is not a substitute for professional clinical diagnosis or hospital treatment.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute medical advice. If you suspect you have sepsis or are experiencing a medical emergency, call 911 or go to the nearest Emergency Department immediately.
