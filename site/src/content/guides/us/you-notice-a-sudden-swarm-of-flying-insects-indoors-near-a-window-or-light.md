# What to do if… you notice a sudden swarm of flying insects indoors near a window or light (USA)

## Short answer
Move people and pets out of the room, close the door, turn off the light that is attracting the insects, and only open a window or exterior exit if you can do it without getting close to the swarm.

## Do not do these things
- Do not spray pesticides, foggers, or “bug bombs” when you do not know what the insects are.
- Do not swat at a dense cluster that could be bees, wasps, or hornets.
- Do not open interior doors and chase the insects through the house.
- Do not immediately seal a hole, vent, or crack while insects are actively coming through it.
- Do not climb, remove panels, or put your face close to a vent, attic opening, wall gap, or window frame to identify them.
- Do not handle dead insects or shed wings with bare hands if you can avoid it.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Move children and pets out of the room, close the door, and keep other people away from that area. Put on shoes and long sleeves if you need to re-enter briefly.
2. **Remove the light attraction.** Turn off the lamp, ceiling light, or bright screen they are clustering around. If it is dark outside, close curtains or blinds in nearby rooms so the insects are not drawn deeper indoors.
3. **Give them an exterior way out only if it is safe.** If the insects are already concentrated at a window and you can reach the handle without leaning into them, open that window or screen enough for an exit, then leave and close the room door again. If you cannot do this safely, keep the room contained and skip to getting help.
4. **Separate likely stinging insects from likely nuisance insects.** If they look or sound like bees, wasps, hornets, or yellow jackets, keep distance and contact a local pest professional, beekeeper, or county Extension office for help deciding what they are. If you think it may be a honey bee swarm, ask about live swarm capture before anyone sprays.
5. **Treat possible termites as an inspection issue.** If the insects are winged, gather near windows or lights, or leave loose wings on a sill or floor, take clear photos and note the exact room and entry point. If you can do it without getting close to active insects, save a few dead specimens or wings in a small container or on clear tape, then arrange a termite inspection or identification through a pest control company or Extension service.
6. **Check the likely entry points without taking things apart.** From a safe distance, look at window frames, torn screens, attic or soffit vents, fireplace or chimney openings, pipe and cable gaps, and the edge where wall meets floor. A short video showing where insects are emerging can help the person you call.
7. **Respond fast to sting warning signs.** Call 911 for trouble breathing, swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat, fainting, severe dizziness, chest tightness, widespread hives, or severe symptoms after a sting. If the person has a prescribed epinephrine auto-injector, use it as directed and still call 911.
8. **Clean up only after the surge settles.** If you are confident they are non-stinging insects, vacuum dead insects after activity has dropped, then empty the vacuum canister or bag outdoors right away. Keep any termite-like wings or specimens you need for identification before cleaning the rest.

## What can wait
- You do not need a perfect insect identification in the first few minutes.
- You do not need to choose a pesticide, trap, fogger, or treatment tonight.
- You do not need to seal, patch, caulk, or remove building materials while insects are still active.
- You do not need to decide whether there is structural damage before someone qualified has inspected it.
- You do not need to throw away furniture, curtains, or belongings because insects gathered near them.

## Important reassurance
A sudden indoor swarm is alarming, but the first job is simple: contain the room, reduce the attraction, and avoid stirring the insects up. Many flying insects gather at light or glass, and photos plus the exact location are often enough for a beekeeper, Extension office, or pest professional to guide the next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilize the moment, reduce sting and pesticide risks, and preserve useful evidence. Repeat swarms, suspected termites, or insects emerging from walls, vents, attics, or chimneys may need specialist pest, beekeeping, or building help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, environmental, pest-control, building, or other professional advice. For severe sting symptoms or suspected anaphylaxis, call 911 immediately.
