# What to do if… you suspect you were not stamped in on entry and you think it could cause issues later (USA)

## Short answer
**Access your electronic I-94 record immediately via the official CBP website or CBP One mobile app to confirm your legal admission and expiration date.** Physical passport stamps are increasingly rare and the electronic record is the legally governing document for your stay.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume that the lack of a physical ink stamp means you are in the country illegally.
- Do not leave and re-enter the USA solely to "get a stamp" without professional legal advice.
- Do not pay third-party "expediting" services or unofficial agents to "fix" your record.
- Do not alter or forge travel documents or boarding passes to create proof of entry.
- Do not ignore an incorrect "Admit Until" date on your electronic record; this governs your legal stay.

## What to do now
1. **Retrieve your official electronic I-94 record from the CBP website.** **Go to the official U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) I-94 website or use the CBP One mobile application.**
   - Select "Get Most Recent I-94."
   - Enter your name, date of birth, and passport number exactly as they appear on the document used for entry.
   - If the system returns "Not Found," try variations (e.g., combining first and middle names, or entering the passport number found on your visa if different from your current passport).

2. **Verify that the "Class of Admission" and "Admit Until" date are correct.** **Check that the status listed (e.g., B1, B2, H1B) and the expiration date match your visa or travel authorization.**
   - If you are on a visa that allows "Duration of Status," the date may be listed as "D/S."
   - Save a digital PDF copy and print a physical copy of this record immediately.

3. **Review your "Travel History" on the same CBP portal.** **Navigate to the "View Travel History" tab to ensure the most recent arrival is listed with the correct port of entry and date.**
   - This provides a secondary check if the "Most Recent I-94" tab is showing an older trip.

4. **Contact a CBP Deferred Inspection Site if the record is missing or contains errors.** **Locate the nearest Deferred Inspection Site or the one associated with your port of entry to request a record correction.**
   - These sites are specifically authorized to remedy errors made at the time of admission, such as incorrect biographical data or wrong admission periods.
   - Many sites now accept correction requests via email; check the official CBP list for specific regional instructions.

5. **Gather and preserve secondary evidence of your arrival.** **Collect all documents that prove the date and location of your entry into the USA.**
   - Keep your original boarding pass (digital or paper).
   - Save your flight itinerary and luggage tags.
   - Keep receipts from the day of arrival, such as airport transit, taxi, or hotel check-in confirmations.

6. **Submit an inquiry via the CBP Traveler Communication Center (TCC) if local sites cannot assist.** **Use the "Ask a Question" feature on the CBP Information Center website if you cannot resolve the issue through Deferred Inspection.**
   - Select "I-94 / Traveler Compliance" as the topic.
   - Upload scans of your passport bio page, visa, and proof of entry.

## What can wait
- You do not need to contact your embassy or consulate, as they cannot correct U.S. border records.
- You do not need to file a Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) request unless all standard correction paths (Deferred Inspection and TCC) have failed.
- You do not need to apply for a status extension (Form I-539) if the error is purely a clerical mistake by CBP that can be corrected.

## Important reassurance
It is now standard procedure at many U.S. airports and sea ports to forgo physical passport stamps entirely. The electronic I-94 system was designed to replace paper forms and ink stamps. If your electronic record is correct, the absence of a stamp in your passport is expected and will not cause issues with your status or future travel.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying and correcting clerical errors in U.S. entry records. It does not cover situations involving visa denials, deportations, or complex legal status changes.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is based on 2026 CBP procedures. Entry records are managed by U.S. Customs and Border Protection; always refer to their official site (cbp.gov) for the most current protocols. If your stay involves complex immigration filings, consult a qualified immigration attorney.
