# What to do if… someone is loitering in a stairwell or corridor and watching you approach (USA)

## Short answer
Stop your approach immediately and return to a well-lit, public, or staffed area. Do not enter an enclosed space with the individual; contact building security or call 911 if you feel threatened or blocked.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t walk past the person to "get it over with" or avoid appearing rude.
- Don’t enter an elevator or a small, enclosed stairwell with them.
- Don’t go directly to your apartment or room if they are close enough to see which unit you enter.
- Don’t confront the individual or ask them to move.
- Don’t look down at your phone or dig for keys while you are in their line of sight.
- Don’t use a "dead-end" route like a basement or a hallway with only one exit.

## What to do now
1. **Stop and change your direction immediately.** Turn around and walk back toward the most populated area available, such as a staffed lobby, a busy street, or a well-lit public space.
2. **Create a physical barrier between yourself and the individual.** Step back outside a secure door, enter a locked vestibule, or return to your vehicle if it is parked nearby and safe to reach.
3. **Alert someone else to your situation right away.** Call a roommate, friend, or building management on speakerphone so your conversation is audible. 
   - If a front desk or security station is available, go there directly and report the person's location.
4. **Call 911 if you are being followed, blocked, or feel in immediate danger.** A voice call is the fastest way for dispatchers to get your location and hear background noise. 
   - **Tell the dispatcher your exact address, floor number, and describe the person's behavior.**
   - **Use text-to-911 only if speaking aloud would put you at greater risk.** Not all jurisdictions support text; if you receive no "bounce-back" message, assume the text did not go through.
5. **Request a "safe walk" or assistance from building staff.** If the building has onsite security or a concierge, ask them to meet you or monitor the area via CCTV while you move to your unit.
6. **Observe the person's description from a safe distance.** Note their approximate height, clothing colors, and any specific items they are holding without staring or engaging.
7. **Report the encounter to property management once you are safe.** Ask them to check the access logs for that area and preserve any security camera footage from the time of the incident.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the person is a resident or a guest right now.
- You do not need to prove the person has "malicious intent" before choosing to walk away.
- You do not need to file a formal written report until you are behind a locked, secure door.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a "gut instinct" or a physical stress response when being watched in a confined space. Prioritizing your safety by leaving the area is a proactive and appropriate reaction, regardless of the other person's actual intentions.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and harm prevention in the minutes during and after a suspicious encounter. It does not replace a formal police report or long-term building security consultations.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Individual building policies and local laws regarding trespassing and loitering vary. Always prioritize local law enforcement instructions in an emergency.
