# What to do if… someone is loitering in a stairwell or corridor and watching you approach (USA)

## Short answer
Do not continue into an enclosed stairwell or corridor. Create distance, move to a staffed or public area with a clear route away, and call 911 if you are in immediate danger or need emergency help.

## Do not do these things
- Do not walk past the person just to get it over with.
- Do not go straight to your apartment or room door if the person may follow and learn where you live.
- Do not confront the person or try to move them along.
- Do not enter an isolated stairwell, basement level, laundry room, parking garage, or dead-end hallway.
- Do not let searching for keys or sending messages distract you from moving to a safer pause.
- Do not stay nearby to collect evidence if you feel unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Stop your approach and change direction.** Move calmly toward a staffed lobby, front desk, visible public area, open business, or another place with other people and a clear route away.
2. **Do not lead the person to your door.** If reaching your apartment would mean passing the person or revealing your unit, pause somewhere safer instead.
3. **Contact onsite help if available.** Call building security, the front desk, or property management and tell them where the person is. Ask them to check the area using any building procedures available to them.
4. **Call 911 if you are in immediate danger or need emergency help.** Give your location, including the address and your floor or unit number if relevant. Briefly describe what the person is doing. Follow the call-taker’s instructions and do not hang up until told to do so.
5. **Use text-to-911 only when needed.** If speaking could put you at risk, text 911 if that service is available in your area. A voice call is generally the best option when you can safely make one. If a text does not go through, use another safe way to call or get help.
6. **Contact a trusted person only if it helps.** A brief call or message can let someone know where you are, but keep moving toward a safer pause and avoid anything that distracts you.
7. **Once you are safer, note what you already observed.** Record the person’s location, clothing, distinguishing features, direction of travel, and the time. Do not return or stare to gather more details.
8. **Ask how to follow up.** If you contacted 911, local police, or building staff, ask whether a reference number is available. Ask management whether relevant camera footage exists and whether it can be retained.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether the person’s behavior is suspicious enough.
- You do not need to identify the person, confront them, or prove intent.
- You do not need to make decisions about moving, your lease, or security upgrades right now.
- A non-emergency report or longer follow-up can wait until you are in a safer place.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to approach the person to find out what they want. Feeling uncertain is not a reason to ignore the situation or prove that your concern is justified.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes to hours. Later decisions may need help from building management, a local non-emergency police route, or another appropriate specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, prioritize reaching a safer place and calling 911.
