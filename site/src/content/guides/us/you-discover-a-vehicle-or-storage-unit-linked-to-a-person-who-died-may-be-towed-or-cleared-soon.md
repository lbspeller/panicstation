# What to do if… you discover a vehicle or storage unit linked to a person who died may be towed or cleared soon (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the **tow operator or storage facility immediately** to request a **temporary administrative hold** while you gather the death certificate and proof of estate authority. Request the hold and a list of required documents **in writing via email** to stop the auction or disposal clock.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t remove the vehicle or empty the storage unit without legal authority; doing so can lead to personal liability or "intermeddling" claims in probate.
- Don’t assume the death certificate alone grants access; most facilities require a court-issued document or a state-specific small estate affidavit.
- Don’t ignore lien sale or auction notices; storage facilities have statutory "self-help" rights to sell property for unpaid rent, and these timelines are often very short.
- Don’t pay fees via gift cards, wire transfers to individuals, or unverified apps; only use official business payment portals or certified checks.
- Don’t discard the original title, lease agreement, or keys found in the unit or vehicle.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact threat and controlling party.**
   - **Vehicle:** Determine if it is currently in an impound lot, tagged for towing on a public street, or under a private property/HOA removal notice.
   - **Storage:** Locate the specific "Notice of Lien Sale" to find the scheduled auction date and the total amount required to "cure" the debt.

2. **Request an immediate hold and written confirmation.**
   - **Call the facility manager and use this phrasing:** "The account holder has died. I am the [executor/heir] and am initiating the legal process to handle the estate. Please place a temporary hold on the [auction/towing] while I provide the required paperwork."
   - **Ask them to email you:** the current balance, the daily fee rate, the absolute deadline to stop the action, and the specific forms they accept.

3. **Check the physical vehicle title for a "TOD" or "Beneficiary" name.**
   - **If the title lists a "Transfer on Death" beneficiary,** that person owns the vehicle immediately and can usually claim it with just a death certificate and their ID, bypassing probate.

4. **Prepare the necessary "Proof of Authority" packet.**
   - **Death Record:** Obtain a certified copy (though many facilities will accept a scan to start a hold).
   - **Court Documents:** If probate is open, use "Letters Testamentary" or "Letters of Administration."
   - **Small Estate Affidavit:** If probate is not being opened, download your state’s DMV-specific affidavit (e.g., California’s REG 5 or Texas’s VTR-262).
   - **Photo ID:** Your current government-issued identification.

5. **Stop the storage unit lien sale legally.**
   - **Ask if the state requires a "Declaration in Opposition to Lien Sale" form.** In some states (like California), filing this specific form before the deadline requires the facility to seek a court order before auctioning the items.
   - **Pay the minimum amount required** to stop the auction today if you have the funds; this is usually the past-due rent and late fees.

6. **Recover time-sensitive and irreplaceable items only.**
   - **If granted limited access, prioritize:** life insurance policies, original wills, keys, hard drives, photos, and military discharge papers (DD-214).
   - **Photograph the unit or vehicle interior** exactly as it was found before moving any items.
   - **Maintain a log** of every item removed and its current location.

7. **Verify the state-specific DMV waiting period.**
   - **Check if your state (like California) requires a waiting period** (often 40 days) after the date of death before a vehicle title can be finalized via affidavit. Inform the impound lot that you are in this mandatory waiting period to negotiate a fee cap or hold.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide which family member gets the car or the unit contents today.
- You do **not** need to complete a full inventory or clean-out until the immediate threat of disposal is paused.
- You do **not** need to settle the final title transfer today; your focus is solely on preventing the auction or tow.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for administrative deadlines to clash with the timeline of probate. Most reputable facilities prefer to work with a legitimate estate representative rather than manage an auction. Obtaining a named contact and a written confirmation of a hold is the most effective way to stabilize the situation.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization to prevent the immediate loss of property through towing or lien sales. It does not cover the final distribution of assets, long-term estate litigation, or the specifics of state-level probate law.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding storage liens and vehicle impounds vary significantly by state. If the facility refuses to cooperate or a sale is occurring in less than 48 hours, contact a probate attorney or local legal aid immediately.
