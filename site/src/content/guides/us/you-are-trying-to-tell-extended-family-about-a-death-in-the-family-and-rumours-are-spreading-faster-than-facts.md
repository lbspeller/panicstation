# What to do if… you are trying to tell extended family about a death in the family and rumours are spreading faster than facts (USA)

## Short answer
Appoint one primary spokesperson to manage all outgoing information and distribute a brief, verified "holding statement" to all family contacts immediately to stop the spread of unconfirmed details.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use euphemisms like "passed away" or "gone to a better place" as these cause confusion in high-stress situations; use "died."
- Do not attempt to report a death to the Social Security Administration (SSA) via email or online as they do not accept these formats.
- Do not share medical records, police reports, or crime scene details to "prove" the facts to relatives.
- Do not debate or argue with relatives in large group chats.
- Do not post any information on public social media until all immediate next of kin have been reached.

## What to do now
1. **Appoint one spokesperson to be the single source of truth for the family.** Provide this person's contact information to everyone else and instruct them to redirect all inquiries with the phrase: "Please contact [Name] for all verified updates."
2. **Draft and send a brief holding message to all known family groups.** Use a copy-and-paste format to ensure consistency.
   - "I am sorry to share that [Name] died on [date]. We are currently notifying immediate family. We will share confirmed information regarding arrangements as soon as possible. Please do not share or post this information yet."
3. **Prioritize one-to-one phone calls for "must-hear" relatives.** Contact the spouse, children, parents, and siblings first; if they cannot be reached, leave a message asking for an immediate return call without disclosing the death.
4. **Create a "notification tree" to distribute the workload.**
   - Assign one person to contact each branch of the family (e.g., "Aunt Sarah's side," "the cousins," "former colleagues").
   - Require each person to report back to the spokesperson when a contact is "notified" or "not reached."
5. **Use a standard script for all unanswered questions.** If anyone asks for details you are not ready to share, use the following sentence:
   - "We are not sharing further details until we have confirmed information."
6. **Issue a single correction for major misinformation and then cease engagement.** If a specific false detail is causing harm, have the spokesperson send one message:
   - "For clarity, the family has not released a cause of death. We ask that you stop all speculation until further notice."
7. **Ensure the death is reported to the Social Security Administration (SSA).**
   - Provide the deceased person’s Social Security number to the funeral director, as they typically handle the report.
   - If no funeral home is involved, call the SSA at 1-800-772-1213 (TTY 1-800-325-0778) between 8:00 a.m. and 7:00 p.m. Monday through Friday.
8. **Contact the financial institutions of the deceased.** Notify them of the death to stop any incoming direct deposits for Social Security benefits, as payments received for the month of death must be returned to the SSA.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write an obituary or a long public tribute today.
- You do not need to provide a timeline of events or medical history to extended family.
- You do not need to decide on funeral dates, service locations, or burial details in the first 24 hours.
- You do not need to read or respond to social media comments or secondary messages.

## Important reassurance
It is common for news of a death to spread faster than the family can manage. Confusion and conflicting reports are standard reactions to sudden loss and do not mean you have failed. Establishing a single point of contact and using a consistent message provides a structure that people can follow once the initial shock subsides.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization of family communication during a crisis only. It does not cover legal estate management, probate, or long-term grief counseling.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. If the death involves a criminal investigation or sensitive legal circumstances, consult with law enforcement or an attorney before releasing any details.
