# What to do if… you find more than one will and family members disagree about which is valid (USA)

## Short answer
Secure every original document immediately and file all versions with the probate court clerk in the county where the deceased lived. The court—not the family or the executor—is the only authority that can legally determine which document is the valid, final version.

## Do not do these things
- Do not destroy, mark, staple, or unbind any of the documents.
- Do not pick one version to file while hiding or ignoring the others.
- Do not distribute money, property, or personal effects while the documents are in dispute.
- Do not assume the most recent date automatically makes a document valid without court review.
- Do not attempt to "settle" the dispute by voting or making side agreements without legal oversight.
- Do not confront witnesses or legal professionals with accusations of fraud or influence before the court process begins.

## What to do now
1. **Secure every original document in a safe, neutral location.**  
   **Place each will and codicil in separate, protective folders without using clips or staples.** 
   - Note the exact location where each document was found.
   - Record who found each version and the date of discovery.
   - Make high-resolution scans or photocopies for reference, but leave the originals untouched.

2. **Pause all transfers of property and funds immediately.**  
   **Cease any informal distribution of the deceased’s assets, including vehicles, jewelry, or cash.** 
   - If a bank or insurance company asks for the will, explain that multiple versions exist and the court has not yet confirmed the personal representative.

3. **Check state-specific deadlines for filing ("lodging") the original documents.**  
   **Contact the local probate court clerk to determine how many days you have to deliver the original documents.** 
   - Many states, such as California (30 days) and Florida (10 days), mandate that anyone in possession of an original will must deliver it to the court clerk shortly after learning of the death.

4. **File every version of the document with the probate court clerk.**  
   **Submit all discovered wills and codicils to the court rather than attempting to guess which one is "correct."** 
   - If a probate case is already open, notify the attorney of record or the court clerk immediately that a different version has been found.

5. **Request a formal probate hearing if conflict is active.**  
   **Ask the court to initiate "formal" or "supervised" administration to ensure a judge reviews the validity of the competing documents.** 
   - This prevents anyone from acting under "informal" authority until the dispute is resolved.

6. **Protect the physical estate while the legal question is pending.**  
   **Secure the residence of the deceased by changing locks or installing security if there is a risk of family members removing property.** 
   - Maintain utility payments and insurance to prevent the loss of asset value while the court determines the valid will.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide which family member is "right" today.
- You do not need to determine the final distribution of assets.
- You do not need to hire a private investigator or forensic expert immediately.
- You do not need to respond to demands from beneficiaries until the court appoints an official representative.

## Important reassurance
It is common for individuals to update their estate plans several times, often leaving behind outdated or conflicting versions. The probate court system is specifically designed to resolve these overlaps through a standardized evidentiary process. Following the filing rules protects you from personal liability.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and the mandatory duty to file documents with the court. It does not cover the technical litigation of a "will contest" or the specific laws regarding mental capacity, which require specialized legal counsel.

## Important note
Probate laws and deadlines are governed by individual state statutes (e.g., California Probate Code §8200, Florida Statutes §732.901). Failure to file an original will can result in personal liability or court sanctions in some jurisdictions. Consult a licensed probate attorney in the relevant state for guidance on specific filing procedures.
