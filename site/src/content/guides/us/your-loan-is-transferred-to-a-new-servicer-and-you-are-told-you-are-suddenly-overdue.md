# What to do if… your loan is transferred to a new servicer and you are told you are suddenly overdue (USA)

## Short answer
Do not send immediate payment to the new entity; verify the transfer through your original servicer's verified portal and request a written line-item account history before acknowledging any delinquency.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay using links or account numbers provided in an unexpected text, email, or cold call.
- Do not provide a full Social Security number, password, or one-time code to "verify your identity" to an incoming caller.
- Do not agree to a settlement or repayment plan until you have received the official "Hello" and "Goodbye" transfer notices in writing.
- Do not assume your existing autopay transferred automatically; check the status before the next due date.
- Do not provide a recorded statement admitting the debt is overdue until you have compared your bank records against the new servicer's ledger.

## What to do now
1. **Gather your most recent three months of payment evidence.**  
   Locate and save:
   - Your final statement from the old servicer showing a zero or transferred balance.
   - Bank statements or cancelled check images showing the date, amount, and recipient of the last three payments.
   - The "Goodbye" letter from your old servicer and the "Hello" letter from the new servicer (Notice of Transfer of Servicing).

2. **Verify the transfer through a known, trusted channel.**  
   Log in directly to your original servicer’s official website or call the number on your last physical statement. Confirm:
   - The exact "effective date of transfer."
   - The legal name and contact information of the new servicer.
   - Whether any payments sent to the old servicer around the transfer date were forwarded.

3. **Submit a formal Request for Information (RFI) and a Notice of Error (NOE).**  
   **Send a written letter to the new servicer's designated "dispute" or "notice of error" address requesting a complete life-of-loan payment history.**
   - Explicitly label the letter "Notice of Error regarding payment application" if they claim you are overdue.
   - Include the date and amount of the payment you believe is missing.
   - Attach copies (not originals) of your bank proof.

4. **Apply the 60-day mortgage protection if the loan is a home mortgage.**  
   **Note that under Regulation X, for 60 days beginning on the effective transfer date, a payment cannot be treated as late if you sent it to the old servicer on time.**
   - If you are charged a late fee for a payment sent to the old servicer during this window, demand the fee be reversed in your Notice of Error.
   - Inform the new servicer that federal law prohibits negative credit reporting for misdirected payments during this 60-day window.

5. **Verify the status of federal student loans with the Treasury Department.**  
   **Check StudentAid.gov to see if your loan has been transferred to the Department of the Treasury or a Treasury-contracted collection agency.**
   - Be aware that as of 2026, many defaulted or transferred federal portfolios have shifted from the Department of Education to the Treasury.
   - Confirm your new repayment plan (such as RAP) if you were previously on a SAVE or other discontinued plan.

6. **Invoke debt validation rights if a third-party collector is involved.**  
   **If you receive a "Validation Notice" from a debt collector, you have 30 days to dispute the debt in writing.**
   - Once you dispute in writing, the collector must stop collection activities until they provide verification of the debt.
   - Check the notice for an itemized breakdown including interest, fees, and payments since the transfer.

7. **Initiate a credit dispute for "high-risk" transfer errors.**  
   **If a late mark appears on your credit report due to a transfer error, file a dispute with Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion immediately.**
   - Under 2026 FCRA rules, bureaus must perform a 10-day preliminary investigation for errors involving servicing transfers.
   - Provide the transfer notices and your proof of payment as attachments to the dispute.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a loan modification or hardship program until the account balance is corrected.
- You do not need to hire a credit repair service; the legal protections for servicing transfers are specific and can be handled via RFI/NOE letters.
- You do not need to respond to "settlement" offers that involve paying a percentage of the disputed "overdue" amount.

## Important reassurance
Servicing transfers are prone to data migration errors where payments made during the "blackout" period are not immediately visible to the new servicer. These issues are usually administrative rather than a reflection of your payment behavior, and federal law provides specific windows (such as the 60-day mortgage rule) to protect you from penalties.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for financial account transfers only. It does not cover long-term debt management, foreclosure defense, or legal representation for lawsuits.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. If you receive a Summons, a Notice of Default, or a Foreclosure Sale date, contact a qualified attorney or a HUD-approved housing counselor immediately.
