# What to do if… the last ferry of the day is cancelled and you need another way to reach your destination (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately confirm with terminal staff if a replacement shuttle or extra sailing is being provided, then secure either a safe alternative land route or nearby overnight lodging before the terminal closes.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave the terminal building until you have verified whether a replacement bus or shuttle is being arranged.
- Do not assume the ferry operator will pay for a hotel; federal law does not mandate lodging for ferry cancellations.
- Do not attempt to walk long distances along unlit port roads or highways to find alternative transit.
- Do not accept unregulated rides from individuals offering "private transfers" outside of official apps or taxi stands.
- Do not wait until the terminal lights are turned off to begin searching for a hotel or ride.

## What to do now
1. **Identify a staffed "safe point" immediately.**
   - Locate the official ticket office, information desk, or a well-lit, staffed terminal business.
   - If the terminal is outdoors or closing, move to the nearest open hotel lobby or 24-hour convenience store.
2. **Verify emergency replacement services with on-site staff.**
   - **Ask if a "bridge-and-bus" or "shuttle" service is being activated due to the cancellation.**
   - **Ask for the exact time of the first departure tomorrow morning.**
   - **Ask if the terminal allows passengers to wait inside overnight (rare, but worth checking).**
3. **Capture evidence of the cancellation for future claims.**
   - **Take a photograph of the cancellations displayed on the terminal departure board.**
   - **Save a screenshot of the service alert or text notification on your phone.**
   - **Ask staff for a "cancellation slip" or a printed notice if available.**
4. **Assess the viability of a "drive-around" or land route.**
   - **Check a navigation app for the total driving time to your destination.**
   - **Compare the cost of a long-distance rideshare/taxi against the cost of a local hotel room.**
   - **Confirm the "last departure" time for any nearby trains or public buses before committing to a land route.**
5. **Secure overnight accommodation if no transit is available.**
   - **Book a room at the nearest reputable hotel immediately; port-area hotels fill quickly during mass cancellations.**
   - **Ask terminal staff if they have "distressed passenger" rates at local hotels.**
6. **Initiate the ticket refund or rebooking process.**
   - **Request a "change of booking" to the first sailing tomorrow via the operator’s app or website while you have signal.**
   - **Request a full refund for the unused leg if you choose an alternative way home.**
7. **Notify your destination contact of your status.**
   - **Send your current location and "tonight plan" (hotel name or transit route) to a friend or family member.**
   - **Inform your intended accommodation at the destination that you will not be arriving tonight.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a formal complaint or "Contract of Carriage" dispute tonight.
- You do not need to decide how to handle missed appointments or events at your destination until you are safe.
- You do not need to research long-term parking or vehicle retrieval if you are travelling as a foot passenger.
- You do not need to contact your credit card issuer for a chargeback until the operator has officially denied a refund request.

## Important reassurance
Being "stuck" due to a cancelled last sailing is a common occurrence in maritime transit, and port staff are trained to handle the surge of stranded passengers. While the disruption is frustrating, the situation is manageable once you have secured a place to stay or a confirmed ride. Most port areas have established protocols for these events.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for domestic US ferry cancellations. It does not cover international border crossings (e.g., ferries to Canada) or long-term travel insurance claims.

## Important note
Ferry operators in the United States are governed by individual "Contracts of Carriage." Unlike airlines, there is no uniform federal requirement for them to provide meals or lodging for weather or mechanical delays. Safety should be your primary concern; prioritize reaching a secure, well-lit environment over negotiating for compensation in the moment.
