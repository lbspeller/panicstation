# What to do if… you develop sudden back pain with new leg numbness or weakness (USA)

## Short answer
Seek **emergency medical evaluation now** if you have new leg weakness, difficulty walking, or numbness in your groin or buttocks. Call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room if you cannot urinate or have lost control of your bladder or bowels.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for an appointment if you have **new weakness, numbness, or "foot drop"** (inability to lift the front of your foot).
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if your leg feels weak, heavy, or unreliable.
- Do not attempt to "walk it off" or perform heavy stretching to see if the numbness clears.
- Do not stay in bed for long periods; modern guidelines suggest avoiding prolonged bed rest for most back pain unless instructed by a doctor.
- Do not ignore changes in your ability to urinate or feel yourself wiping after using the bathroom.
- Do not seek spinal manipulation or chiropractic care until a doctor has ruled out serious nerve compression.

## What to do now
1. **Identify high-priority "red flag" symptoms immediately.** 
   - **Call 911 or go to the ER** if you have any of the following:
   - Sudden loss of bladder or bowel control (incontinence).
   - Inability to urinate (retention) or a loss of the "urge" to go.
   - Numbness in the "saddle area," including the groin, buttocks, inner thighs, or genitals.
   - Weakness that makes it difficult to stand, walk, or move your foot.
2. **Contact your primary care physician or a spine specialist for same-day guidance.**
   - If red flags are not present but you have new tingling or mild numbness, call your doctor and use the phrase: "**new onset neurological symptoms with back pain**."
   - Ask for an urgent evaluation to check for nerve root compression.
3. **Prevent falls and further injury while arranging transport.**
   - Sit or lie down on a firm surface in a position that minimizes pain.
   - Avoid stairs, carrying heavy objects, or bending and twisting.
   - Have someone assist you with walking if your legs feel unsteady.
4. **Prepare a concise "symptom map" for the medical team.**
   - **Identify exactly where the numbness or weakness is located** (e.g., "outer side of left calf," "sole of right foot," or "both legs").
   - **Note when the symptoms started** and if they were triggered by a specific injury or movement.
   - **Check for sensory changes** in the area you use to wipe after the bathroom.
5. **Gather essential medical information.**
   - List any medications you are taking, especially blood thinners or steroids.
   - Note any history of cancer, recent infections, or previous spine surgeries.

## What can wait
- Finding the specific "cause" (such as a disc herniation or stenosis) before seeing a professional.
- Researching physical therapy exercises or long-term rehabilitation plans.
- Deciding on surgery or other invasive treatments; the immediate goal is triage and stabilization.
- Completing household tasks or work shifts before seeking an evaluation.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant anxiety or panic when your legs feel weak or numb. These sensations are alarming, but seeking a prompt medical exam is the most effective way to manage the situation. Many people experience back pain with nerve symptoms that are successfully managed once the level of urgency is determined by a professional.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions only. It does not replace a clinical examination, imaging, or specialized neurological testing required for a definitive diagnosis.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not a substitute for professional medical advice or diagnosis. If you are experiencing new or worsening neurological symptoms, immediate evaluation in an emergency setting is the safest course of action to prevent potential long-term damage.
