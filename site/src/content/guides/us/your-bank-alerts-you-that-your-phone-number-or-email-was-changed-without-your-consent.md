# What to do if… your bank alerts you that your phone number or email was changed without your consent (USA)

## Short answer
Call your bank immediately using the official number on the back of your card or their verified website to report unauthorized profile changes and freeze your account.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click any links or call any phone numbers provided in the alert text or email.
- Do not reply to the security alert with personal information or one-time passcodes.
- Do not share your online banking login, PIN, or multi-factor authentication (MFA) codes with anyone, even if they claim to be from the bank.
- Do not ignore the alert even if no money appears to be missing yet.
- Do not use the same device to change passwords if you suspect the device itself is compromised.

## What to do now
1. **Call your bank’s fraud department using a trusted number.** Use the number on the back of your physical debit/credit card or the bank’s official, verified website.
   - **Ask to lock online and mobile banking access** to prevent further unauthorized changes.
   - **Request a "security freeze" or "hold" on all outgoing transfers**, including P2P services like Zelle or Venmo.
   - **Verify and revert the contact information** (email, phone, and physical address) to your known-good details.
   - **Ask for a case or reference number** for the report.
2. **Review all recent account activity for unauthorized transactions or modifications.** Check for more than just withdrawals; look for:
   - New payees or linked external bank accounts.
   - New authorized users or additional debit/credit card requests.
   - Changes to statement delivery settings (e.g., switching to "paperless" to hide fraud).
   - Unrecognized "trusted devices" in your security settings.
3. **Secure your primary email account using a different, safe device.** Email accounts are often the "master key" for password resets.
   - **Change your email password** to a unique, complex phrase.
   - **Enable non-SMS multi-factor authentication** (such as an authenticator app or hardware key).
   - **Check email forwarding rules** and "deleted items" to ensure a bad actor isn't silently redirecting your bank's alerts.
4. **Contact your mobile service provider to verify your account security.** Profile changes at the bank are frequently preceded by "SIM swap" or "port-out" fraud.
   - **Ask if any SIM changes or port requests** occurred recently.
   - **Invoke your rights under FCC Order 23-95A**, which requires carriers to use secure authentication before processing SIM or port changes.
   - **Add a "Port-Out PIN" or "Number Lock"** if your carrier offers it.
5. **Freeze your credit reports at all three major bureaus and ChexSystems.** This prevents unauthorized new accounts from being opened in your name.
   - **Equifax:** 1-800-685-1111
   - **Experian:** 1-888-397-3742
   - **TransUnion:** 1-888-909-8872
   - **ChexSystems:** (Specifically for banking identity theft) 1-800-428-9623
6. **Report the incident to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC).** If your profile was changed without consent, your personal data is compromised.
   - **Visit IdentityTheft.gov** to create an official Identity Theft Report and a personalized recovery plan.
7. **Keep a log of all interactions and evidence.** Document the time of the alert, the names of representatives you spoke to, and the specific unauthorized changes you identified.

## What can wait
- You do not need to close all your bank accounts immediately; a freeze or lock is usually sufficient while the bank investigates.
- You do not need to buy a new phone unless your carrier confirms the device hardware itself is compromised.
- You do not need to change every single online password today; prioritize banking, email, and mobile carrier accounts first.

## Important reassurance
Security alerts for contact-info changes are designed to give you a head start before money is moved. Acting within the first few minutes or hours is a highly effective way to prevent financial loss. Confusion or a sense of urgency is a standard response to these notifications.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization only. Long-term recovery may involve formal Regulation E disputes, filing police reports, and ongoing credit monitoring.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Under Regulation E (12 CFR § 1005.11), you generally have 60 days from the date an unauthorized transaction appears on your statement to report it and limit your liability.
