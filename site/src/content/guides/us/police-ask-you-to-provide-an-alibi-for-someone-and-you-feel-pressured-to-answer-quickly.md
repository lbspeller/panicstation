# What to do if… police ask you to provide an alibi for someone and you feel pressured to answer quickly (USA)

## Short answer
Do not guess or agree to a rushed interview. Say that you want to pause, check your records, and speak with a lawyer before giving an account.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess times, places, or sequences to fill gaps in your memory.
- Do not repeat another person’s account as if it is something you personally remember.
- Do not agree to a rushed recorded interview while you are uncertain. Ask to pause and get legal advice.
- Do not sign a statement that you have not read carefully or that contains wording you do not agree with.
- Do not delete, alter, create, or clean up texts, call logs, location history, receipts, photos, calendar entries, or other records that may be relevant.
- Do not coordinate a story or ask anyone else to align their memory with yours.
- Do not ignore a subpoena, court order, or other legal paper. Note what you received and contact a lawyer promptly.

## What to do now
1. **Ask whether the interaction is voluntary.** In person, ask: “Am I being detained, or am I free to leave?” On the phone, ask: “Is this a voluntary request, and am I free to end the call?” If the officer confirms that you are free to leave or end the call, say that you want to pause and get advice before answering questions about the matter.

2. **Use one clear pause phrase.** Say: “I do not want to guess or give inaccurate information. I want to speak with a lawyer before answering questions about the matter.”

3. **If officers say that you cannot leave, be clear.** Say: “I want a lawyer. I am invoking my right to remain silent.” Do not answer questions about the matter.

4. **Make a simple list of records that already exist.** Note any messages, call logs, calendar entries, card transactions, receipts, work schedules, rideshare records, parking records, transit records, or photos that may help you remember. Leave the originals unchanged. Ask a lawyer before editing, reorganizing, or sharing records.

5. **Write down the contact details.** Note the agency, the officer’s name and contact information, when the request was made, what they asked for, and whether they gave you a deadline or any legal paper.

6. **Contact a lawyer before deciding how to respond.** Tell the lawyer whether the contact was by phone or in person, whether officers said that the interaction was voluntary, whether you were told that you could leave, and whether you received any subpoena, court order, or other legal paper.

7. **Be especially careful if federal agents are involved.** Knowingly and willfully making a materially false statement in a matter within federal jurisdiction can be a federal offense under 18 U.S.C. § 1001. Do not guess or fill gaps.

8. **If you later choose to give an account after getting advice, keep it narrow.** Use phrases such as: “I personally remember…”, “I do not remember”, or “I cannot confirm that.” Clearly separate what you remember from anything another person told you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether you are on someone’s side.
- You do not need to reconstruct an entire timeline while you feel rushed.
- You do not need to agree to a voluntary interview on the spot.
- You do not need to decide the best response format before speaking with a lawyer.

## Important reassurance
Pressure can make memory feel less clear. Accuracy matters more than speed. It is acceptable to say that you do not remember or cannot confirm something when that is the truth.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce the risk of guessing, story alignment, record changes, or signing inaccurate wording. Later decisions about whether and how to respond may need advice from a lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Laws and procedures vary by state and by the circumstances. Get advice from a lawyer if you may be at legal risk or receive a subpoena, court order, or other legal paper.
