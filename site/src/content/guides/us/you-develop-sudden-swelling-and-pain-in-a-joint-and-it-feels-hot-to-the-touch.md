# What to do if… you develop sudden swelling and pain in a joint and it feels hot to the touch (USA)

## Short answer
A sudden **hot, swollen, and painful joint** requires an **immediate medical evaluation** today at an Emergency Room (ER) or Emergency Department to rule out a joint infection (septic arthritis). Do not wait for a primary care appointment if you have a fever, chills, or cannot move the joint.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delay seeking professional care to "see if it gets better" overnight.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics or someone else's prescription medications.
- Do not apply direct heat, heating pads, or hot compresses to the area.
- Do not attempt to drain, needle, or puncture the swelling yourself.
- Do not force the joint to move or "walk off" the pain if it is severe.
- Do not apply ice or cold packs directly to the skin without a cloth barrier.

## What to do now
1. **Go to an Emergency Room (ER) or Emergency Department immediately if you meet high-risk criteria.** **You must seek emergency care right away if you have a fever, chills, or feel generally unwell, as these can indicate a systemic infection.**
   - You should also go to the ER if you have an artificial (replacement) joint in that location.
   - Seek immediate care if you see red streaks spreading away from the joint or if you are immunocompromised due to medication, chemotherapy, or underlying conditions like diabetes.
   - Emergency evaluation is required if the pain is so severe that you cannot bear weight or move the joint at all.

2. **Contact an Urgent Care center only if they can perform an immediate evaluation and joint aspiration.** **Verify that the facility has the capacity to escalate your care to a hospital if a joint infection is suspected.** 
   - If they cannot see you immediately or do not have an orthopedic specialist or rheumatologist available for consultation, proceed to the nearest ER.
   - Call **911** if you feel faint, confused, or are unable to travel safely to a medical facility.

3. **Immobilize and protect the joint while you are in transit.** **Stop all physical activity and keep the affected joint as still as possible to prevent further irritation.**
   - If the swelling is in a leg or foot, do not put any weight on it; use crutches or a wheelchair if available.
   - If the swelling is in an arm or hand, use a sling or a pillow to support the limb.
   - Remove rings, watches, or restrictive clothing near the swelling before the area becomes too tight to do so safely.

4. **Prepare a concise summary of your symptoms for the medical team.** **Write down or record the exact time the pain started and whether the onset was sudden or gradual.**
   - Include a list of your current medications, especially blood thinners, steroids, or biologics.
   - Note any recent injuries, skin breaks, animal bites, or medical procedures (like a joint injection or surgery) involving that joint.
   - Mention if you have a history of gout, rheumatoid arthritis, or kidney disease.

5. **Manage pain only using medications you know are safe for you.** **If you typically tolerate them and have no contraindications, over-the-counter NSAIDs (like ibuprofen or naproxen) or acetaminophen may be used as directed on the packaging.**
   - Avoid NSAIDs if you have a history of stomach ulcers, kidney disease, or are on certain blood thinners, unless specifically told otherwise by a doctor.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause (e.g., gout vs. infection) yourself; a clinician must do this through testing.
- You do not need to apply topical creams or ointments to the area right now.
- You do not need to decide on long-term treatment plans or surgical options today.

## Important reassurance
Sudden joint inflammation is physically distressing and often causes significant alarm. Seeking an urgent evaluation is the standard medical protocol for these symptoms and is the most effective way to protect the joint from long-term damage.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage guidance only. A definitive diagnosis requires a physical exam and potentially a joint fluid analysis (aspiration) by a healthcare professional.
