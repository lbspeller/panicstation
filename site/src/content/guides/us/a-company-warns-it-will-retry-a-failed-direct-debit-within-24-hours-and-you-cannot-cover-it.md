# What to do if… a company warns it will retry a failed direct debit within 24 hours and you cannot cover it (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank or credit union immediately to request a "best-effort" stop payment or an ACH block on the merchant, and formally revoke the company's authorization to debit your account in writing to prevent a repeat-debit loop.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the retry will fail again just because the first one did; if it clears while your balance is low, it may trigger expensive overdraft or non-sufficient funds (NSF) fees.
- Don’t empty the account entirely; this can cause other essential payments (like rent or insurance) to fail and lead to cascading penalties.
- Don’t rely only on a phone call; verbal requests to merchants or banks are often temporary and harder to prove if a dispute is needed later.
- Don’t open a new bank account as a primary solution; this does not stop the legal obligation to the merchant and may lead to "account closure" fees or negative reports to ChexSystems.
- Don’t take out a high-interest "payday" or "emergency" loan to cover the retry without first exhausting bank-level blocking options.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact merchant name and the "Company ID" if possible.**  
   Check your transaction history or the "failed payment" notification for the specific name appearing on your bank statement.
   - Note the exact dollar amount of the failed debit.
   - Look for an "ACH ID" or "Originator ID" (a 10-digit number) often found in the transaction details.

2. **Call your bank or credit union and ask for an immediate ACH Block or Stop Payment.**  
   Inform the representative that you have a merchant attempting an unauthorized retry within a 24-hour window.
   - **Ask for an ACH Block:** This is often more effective than a "Stop Payment" because it blocks the merchant's ID entirely, preventing them from resubmitting the request under a different transaction number.
   - **Request a "Best-Effort" Stop:** Under Regulation E, banks must honor stop-payment orders made at least 3 business days in advance. Since you are within 24 hours, the bank is not legally required to stop it, but most will attempt a "best-effort" block if you call immediately.
   - **Verify Fees:** Ask what the fee is for an ACH block or stop payment (often $20–$35).

3. **Send a formal "Revocation of Authorization" to the company.**  
   Use the merchant's customer service portal or email to send a clear statement.
   - Use the phrase: "I am revoking my authorization for [Company Name] to debit my account ending in [last four digits] via ACH or electronic transfer, effective immediately."
   - Keep a screenshot or copy of the sent message and any confirmation number provided.

4. **Notify your bank that authorization has been revoked.**  
   After messaging the company, tell your bank: "I have formally revoked authorization with this merchant. Any further attempts to debit the account should be treated as unauthorized."
   - This creates a record that helps you dispute the charge under Regulation E if the merchant ignores your revocation.

5. **Disable "Overdraft Protection" temporarily if available.**  
   If your bank allows you to toggle "overdraft coverage" off in their mobile app, doing so may cause the retry to be declined (NSF) rather than allowing the account to go negative.
   - Be aware that your bank may still charge an "NSF Fee" even if the payment is declined, but you avoid the merchant receiving the funds and your balance dropping further.

6. **Prioritize essential funds.**  
   If the retry appears certain to go through and you cannot stop it, ensure that money for medicine, food, or housing is held in a separate location (such as a different account or cash) so it is not consumed by the merchant and the resulting bank fees.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle the underlying debt today; the immediate goal is to prevent a "fee trap" where multiple retries drain your account.
- You do not need to file a formal CFPB complaint yet; this is an escalation step if the bank fails to honor a valid, timely stop-payment or dispute.
- You do not need to decide on a new bank today; focus on stabilizing your current balance first.

## Important reassurance
A 24-hour retry notice is often an automated system attempt. While the timeframe is short, banks have internal "exception lists" and fraud-monitoring tools (enhanced by NACHA 2026 rules) that can often intercept these transactions if you alert them before the morning processing window. Taking these steps creates a "paper trail" that protects your right to a refund later if the merchant ignores your revocation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a 24-hour financial window. It does not cover long-term debt management, legal disputes over contracts, or credit score repair.

## Important note
Policies regarding ACH blocks and stop-payment fees vary significantly between financial institutions. A verbal stop-payment order at a bank may expire after 14 days unless confirmed in writing. Always check your specific bank's "Account Agreement" or "Electronic Fund Transfer" disclosure for their exact requirements.
