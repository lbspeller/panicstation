# What to do if… a hospital asks you to come in urgently to discuss end-of-life decisions for a relative (USA)

## Short answer
Join the meeting immediately (in person or via video/phone) and ask the medical team to identify the legally recognized surrogate decision-maker before any medical choices are made.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel obligated to make irreversible decisions in the first five minutes of the meeting.
- Do not assume that being the "next of kin" automatically grants legal authority in all states (especially MA, MN, MO, or RI).
- Do not interpret a "Do Not Resuscitate" (DNR) order as an instruction to stop all medical care; it applies only if the heart or breathing stops.
- Do not sign any POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) or similar medical orders without a plain-language explanation of each section.
- Do not attempt to resolve long-standing family conflicts during the clinical update.

## What to do now
1. **Request a brief medical status update before the formal meeting begins.** **Ask the nurse or attending physician for a "one-sentence summary" of your relative's current physiological stability.**
   - Determine if the patient is currently conscious or able to speak.
   - Confirm if any life-sustaining interventions (like a ventilator) are currently active.
2. **Verify the legally recognized decision-maker according to the hospital's records.** **Ask the staff: "Who is documented as the primary health care agent or surrogate in the patient's chart?"**
   - Provide copies of a Durable Power of Attorney for Health Care or Living Will if you have them.
   - Ask for the state’s default surrogate hierarchy if no documents exist.
   - If in Massachusetts, Minnesota, Missouri, or Rhode Island, ask if a court-appointed guardian is required in the absence of a named proxy.
3. **Clarify exactly which specific medical decision is being requested at this time.** **Ask the physician: "What specific intervention or treatment change requires a decision in the next few hours?"**
   - Identify if the focus is on "Code Status" (DNR/DNI), intensive care (ICU), or "Comfort Care" (Hospice).
4. **Request a Palliative Care consultation to assist with the decision-making process.** **Ask the attending physician to "place a consult for the Palliative Care team" to help discuss goals of care.**
   - Note that Palliative Care is not the same as Hospice; it provides symptom management and decision support at any stage of illness.
5. **Ask for the "Best Case, Worst Case, Most Likely" outcomes for the proposed treatments.** **Use this specific framework to understand the clinical trajectory and avoid vague descriptions like "doing everything."**
   - Ask: "What does recovery look like for this specific patient in their current condition?"
6. **Identify the hospital's Ethics Consultation Service if there is disagreement or confusion.** **Tell the team: "I would like to request an ethics consult to help us navigate this decision-making process."**
   - Most US hospitals provide this as a free, advisory service for families and clinicians.
7. **Ensure your right to information under 2026 HIPAA guidelines is acknowledged.** **Ask the hospital’s patient advocate or privacy officer to confirm your status as a "Personal Representative" if you are being denied access to medical records.**
   - Note that as of February 2026, privacy notices must explicitly detail how specific records (such as substance use disorder data) are shared.
8. **Request a printed or written summary of the care plan discussed during the meeting.** **Ask the team to "document the agreed-upon goals in the electronic health record" and provide you with a copy.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a funeral home or burial arrangements right now.
- You do not need to notify every extended family member before the initial clinical meeting is over.
- You do not need to settle financial or estate matters while the patient is in acute crisis.
- You do not need to decide on long-term nursing facility placement today.

## Important reassurance
Hospital teams call these meetings because they are legally and ethically required to involve family in major transitions of care. The "urgency" is often a reflection of the medical timeline, not a reflection of your speed as a decision-maker. It is normal to feel overwhelmed or to ask the same question multiple times.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for urgent hospital family meetings in the USA. It does not replace legal advice regarding guardianship or state-specific probate laws.

## Important note
Surrogate hierarchy and the legal standing of POLST/MOLST forms vary by state. If you are unsure of your legal standing, ask to speak with the hospital’s Risk Management department or a Social Worker. This information is based on standard 2026 US hospital protocols and HIPAA regulations.
