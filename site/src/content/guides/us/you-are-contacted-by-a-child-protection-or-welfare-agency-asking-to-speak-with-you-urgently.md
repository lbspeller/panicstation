# What to do if… you are contacted by a child protection or welfare agency asking to speak with you urgently (USA)

## Short answer
If anyone may be in immediate danger, call 911. Otherwise, verify the caller through an independently found official agency number, then return the call promptly and ask what immediate child-safety concern needs attention now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t rely only on caller ID, a voicemail number, or a link sent by the caller when checking whether the contact is genuine.
- Don’t ignore verified contact or delay a return call unnecessarily.
- Don’t discuss allegations in front of the child or coach the child about what to say.
- Don’t confront anyone you suspect made a report.
- Don’t sign a form that you have not read or do not understand. Ask for a copy and time to get advice where circumstances allow.
- Don’t physically resist or block anyone. Ask calmly about the stated authority and seek local legal advice promptly.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe, private place if you can.** If anyone may be in immediate danger, call 911. Otherwise, take a moment to write down the caller’s details before continuing.
2. **Collect the basic details.** Ask for the worker’s full name, title, agency name, office location, supervisor name, reference number if available, and return-call number. Say that you will verify the office and call back promptly.
3. **Verify independently.** Find an official state, county, territorial, or tribal agency website, as applicable. Use the public number listed there and ask to be connected to the worker or ask how to return the call safely. Do not rely only on the number in the message.
4. **Ask what needs attention now.** Once the contact is verified, ask what immediate safety concern they need to address, what they are asking you to do today, and whether the matter is an investigation or a family-assessment or differential-response pathway if your local agency uses one.
5. **Keep a simple written log.** Note the date, time, worker’s name, agency, phone numbers used, what was requested, and any next agreed contact. Keep voicemails and messages.
6. **Ask for local legal help promptly if you need it.** A local attorney or legal-aid organization may be able to explain the rules in your area, especially before you sign documents or make decisions you do not understand. Do not delay an urgent child-safety response solely while seeking advice.
7. **If someone arrives without an appointment, ask for identification.** Ask for the agency office number and supervisor contact. Ask whether a short pause is possible so you can verify the visit and have a support person present. Do not assume a delay is available. If law enforcement is present or paperwork or an order is shown, remain calm, ask what authority they are acting under, ask for a copy where possible, do not physically resist or block anyone, and seek local legal advice promptly.
8. **Keep the child’s surroundings calm.** Avoid adult discussions within the child’s hearing and do not interview or coach the child yourself.

## What can wait
- You do not need to guess who made a report.
- You do not need to write a full account before making a verified return call.
- You do not need to decide every longer-term issue before the immediate safety concern is clear.
- You can ask to read and understand proposed plans, releases, or other documents before signing them, while still addressing any immediate child-safety issue.

## Important reassurance
Urgent contact can feel frightening, but the contact alone does not establish what outcome will follow. Verify the caller, focus on any immediate safety concern, and take one clear step at a time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Child-welfare procedures, terminology, and legal options vary by state, county, territory, tribe, and case type. Later decisions may need help from a local attorney, legal-aid organization, or other appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If anyone may be in immediate danger, call 911. For advice about your situation, use an official local agency contact route and seek local professional help.
