# What to do if… you accidentally triggered a panic alarm or emergency SOS alert (USA)
## Short answer
Move to a safe place and check whether there is any real emergency. If 911 has connected, do not hang up; tell the call-taker that the activation was accidental and answer their questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to cancel the alert if there is a real emergency or you are unsure whether anyone is in danger.
- Do not hang up on a connected 911 call, even if the call was accidental.
- Do not keep pressing the panic or SOS button to try to cancel the alert.
- Do not assume that silencing or disarming a monitored alarm cancelled a dispatch request.
- Do not share an alarm passcode with a caller unless you are confident that the caller is your monitoring provider.
- Do not remain in an unsafe place merely to wait for a callback; take your phone with you and move to a safer place.
- Do not reset the device or start troubleshooting until the immediate follow-up is resolved.

## What to do now
1. **Check which alert was triggered.**
   - If there is any real danger, fire, smoke, possible carbon-monoxide exposure, injury, medical need, or uncertainty, call 911 or remain on the connected 911 call.
   - If you are safe, identify whether this was a 911 call, a monitored home or workplace alarm, a phone SOS alert to contacts, or more than one of these.

2. **If a 911 call connected, stay on the line.**
   - Say: *“This was an accidental activation. I am safe. There is no emergency.”*
   - Answer the call-taker’s questions and do not hang up until you are told to do so.
   - If the call disconnected, keep your phone reachable and answer any callback.

3. **If a monitored alarm or panic button was triggered, contact the verified monitoring provider.**
   - If it is safe, use your normal code, fob, app, or control panel to silence or disarm the alarm.
   - Answer an expected monitoring call and explain that the activation was accidental.
   - Follow the provider’s instructions to cancel or close the alert if cancellation is available.
   - Do not assume that silencing the alarm, disarming the system, or clearing an alert message cancelled a response.
   - Provide a verbal passcode only when you are confident that you are speaking with the correct provider. When verification is needed and it is safe to do so, use a known number from your alarm app, account, or paperwork.
   - Some panic, duress, fire, smoke, or carbon-monoxide alerts may follow different procedures.

4. **If your phone sent an SOS alert to contacts, check the phone screen.**
   - Use an on-screen cancel or stop-sharing option if one is displayed.
   - Send recipients one clear message: *“Accidental SOS alert. I am safe. No help is needed.”*
   - If the phone also called 911, follow step 2.

5. **If responders may already be on the way, remain reachable from a safe location.**
   - Keep your phone with you.
   - Follow instructions from the 911 call-taker, the verified monitoring provider, or responders.
   - Calmly explain that the alarm was triggered accidentally and state whether anyone needs help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to troubleshoot the device or change its settings right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to replace the device or change your alarm service right now.
- You do not need to contact every person you know. Focus only on the call-taker, the verified monitoring provider, and any recipients who received the alert.

## Important reassurance
An accidental activation can be handled. Focus on safety, one clear explanation, and staying reachable until the immediate follow-up is resolved.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about device settings, monitoring procedures, or local requirements may need help from the alarm provider or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Procedures vary by device, alarm provider, and local emergency communications center. Follow instructions from the 911 call-taker, responders, or your verified monitoring provider.
