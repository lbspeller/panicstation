# What to do if… you are told a loved one is likely to die within days and need to alert family (USA)

## Short answer
Choose one person to coordinate updates, tell the smallest close-family circle first, and ask the care team what information can be shared and how updates should be handled. You do not need to call everyone yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not promise an exact timeline. Use cautious wording such as `may be in their last days`.
- Do not make dozens of calls alone if another person can help.
- Do not put private medical details, travel details, or information your loved one would not want widely shared into a large group chat.
- Do not assume staff can discuss medical details with every person who calls.
- Do not get pulled into family arguments while you are sending the first essential update.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the essentials.** Note your loved one’s full name, the facility name, the main phone number, and the name or role of the care-team member who spoke with you.
2. **Ask the care team how updates should work.** Ask:
   - `Who should staff contact with updates?`
   - `What information can be shared with close family?`
   - `What process should family use if they call?`
   If your loved one can express their wishes, ask staff how those preferences can be recorded.
3. **Choose one update coordinator.** This can be you or another trusted person. Use one small group text or email thread so close family receive the same information and the bedside or care unit is not flooded with repeated calls.
4. **Decide who needs the first alert.** Start with the smallest circle of close family who would reasonably want to know now. Extended-family messages can wait.
5. **Send one short first message.** For example: `I’m sorry to send this news. The care team has told us that [Name] may be in their last days. They are at [facility and city]. Please reply when you can if you would like help arranging a call or visit. I will share updates as I receive them.`
6. **Ask about immediate access.** Confirm current visiting hours, visitor limits, and whether staff can help arrange a phone or video call. Share only the details relatives need to decide what they want to do.
7. **Ask whether additional support is relevant now.** If hospice or palliative care has been mentioned, ask: `Who can explain the immediate options and support available today?`
8. **Time-stamp later updates.** Start each update with the date and time so relatives can tell which message is current.

## What can wait
- You do not need to call every extended relative immediately.
- You do not need to write a perfect announcement or post publicly.
- You do not need to give everyone full medical details.
- You do not need to decide funeral arrangements, legal paperwork, or financial matters right now.
- Unless something is urgent, work paperwork and longer-term arrangements can wait until the closest family have been alerted.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements before people can decide whether they want to call or visit.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable if this feels hard to process. A brief, factual message is enough for now. You are not failing if you cannot answer every question or contact every person yourself.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours. Later decisions may need help from the care team, a hospice or palliative-care provider, an employer, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, employment, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. Privacy practices, visiting arrangements, and available support vary by facility and individual circumstances.
