# What to do if… you find a bat in your room while you were asleep and are unsure about contact (USA)

## Short answer

Treat this as urgent to assess today: if you woke up with a bat in the room and cannot confidently rule out a bite, scratch, or other contact, contact your local health department, animal control, or a clinician right away, and do not release the bat if it can be safely kept available for guidance or testing.

## Do not do these things

- Do not touch the bat with bare hands.
- Do not release the bat, throw it away, or damage it before you have spoken with public health or animal control, if it can be safely kept available.
- Do not delay just because you do not see a bite; bat bites and scratches can be tiny and hard to notice.
- Do not swing at, crush, or grab the bat.
- Do not attempt capture if the bat is flying around you, you feel panicked or unsteady, or you cannot do it without contact.

## What to do now

1. **Create a safer pause.** Turn on the lights, put on shoes, and move children and pets away from the room.

2. **If the bat is still present, contain the room without touching the bat.**
   - Close the door.
   - Put a towel at the bottom gap if there is one.
   - Keep everyone, including pets, out of the room.

3. **Call for local rabies guidance now.**
   - Contact your local health department or animal control.
   - Say: “I woke up with a bat in the room and cannot rule out contact. I need guidance on rabies exposure and what to do with the bat.”
   - Follow their instructions about whether the bat should be captured or tested.

4. **Get same-day medical guidance about rabies post-exposure prophylaxis.**
   - Ask: “Because I was asleep, do you recommend rabies PEP, and where should I go today?”
   - Use urgent care, an emergency department, or another route if public health or a clinician directs you there.

5. **If the bat lands and you can do this without contact, keep it available for testing.**
   - Use thick gloves, a container, and cardboard only if you can stay calm and avoid touching the bat.
   - If you cannot safely do that, keep the room closed and wait for animal control or public-health instructions.

6. **Check the basic facts without a long inspection.**
   - Note the time you found the bat, the room, and who was asleep there.
   - Include children, anyone who may not reliably notice or remember contact, and any pets that were in or near the room.
   - Look briefly for visible scratches or marks; take a clear photo if you can do so easily.

7. **If there is an obvious bite, scratch, or saliva contact with broken skin, eyes, nose, or mouth, wash the area with soap and running water and seek urgent medical care.**

8. **If the bat has already escaped or cannot be tested, still contact public health or medical care today and explain that the bat is not available.**

## What can wait

- You do not need to decide right now whether you were definitely bitten.
- You do not need to research rabies statistics or read forums before calling public health or medical care.
- You do not need to solve how the bat got into the home tonight.
- You do not need to plan long-term bat-proofing until the exposure question has been handled.

## Important reassurance

This is unsettling, and uncertainty after waking up is common. The safest first step is not to prove what happened on your own; it is to keep people away from the bat, keep the bat available if safe, and get prompt local guidance.

## Scope note

These are first steps only for the first hours: immediate safety, keeping testing options open, and getting prompt assessment for possible rabies exposure. Later decisions about medical care, pets, home repairs, and bat-proofing may need help from public health, a clinician, a veterinarian, animal control, or a qualified wildlife professional.

## Important note

This is general information, not medical advice or professional public-health advice. Rabies can usually be prevented when appropriate post-exposure care is given before symptoms start, so contact your local health department, animal control, or medical care today if you woke up with a bat in your room and cannot confidently rule out contact.
