# What to do if… you discover someone has died with no close family and you are being asked to take responsibility as a friend (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign any document that labels you as the "responsible party" or "guarantor" unless you intend to pay all costs personally. Direct the funeral home or hospital to the county medical examiner or public administrator to initiate the "unclaimed remains" or "indigent burial" process.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign funeral home contracts as the "authorizing agent" or "responsible party" if you cannot afford the bill.
- Do not pay deposits or transport fees from your own bank account with the expectation of easy reimbursement.
- Do not remove any property, cash, or jewelry from the decedent's home without explicit written authorization from a coroner or police officer.
- Do not claim to be "next of kin" or a "legal representative" to speed up the process, as this carries legal liability.
- Do not feel obligated to take on the role of executor or administrator just because you were the person who found them.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 immediately if the death has just occurred or was unexpected.**  
   **The responding officers or emergency medical services will determine if the case falls under the jurisdiction of the Coroner or Medical Examiner (ME).** If the death happened in a hospital, contact the "Decedent Affairs" or "Patient Relations" office.

2. **Establish a clear boundary of "friend only" with all agencies.**  
   **State clearly to the police, hospital, and funeral home that you are a friend, not next of kin, and that you are not assuming financial responsibility.** Request that this "friend / contact only" status is noted in the official file or case report.

3. **Request the case number and the name of the investigator.**  
   **This number is essential for communicating with landlords, banks, and the county.** Ask the ME or hospital for the specific procedure used for "unclaimed" or "indigent" remains in that county.

4. **Provide known biographical data to the investigator.**  
   **Supply the decedent’s full name, date of birth, social security number (if known), and any information regarding their Veteran status.**
   - If the person was a Veteran, they may be eligible for a federal burial at a National Cemetery at no cost to you.

5. **Inquire about the "Public Administrator" or "County Conservator."**  
   **If there is no will and no family, the county Public Administrator is typically the official tasked with securing assets and managing the estate.** Direct creditors, landlords, and utility companies to this office once a case is opened.

6. **Secure the residence without taking possession.**  
   **If you have keys, ensure the windows and doors are locked, then turn the keys over to the landlord or the police.** Do not sort through belongings; the property is technically part of an estate that you do not yet have legal authority to manage.

7. **Notify the landlord or property manager in writing.**  
   **Provide them with the Coroner/ME case number and the contact information for the Public Administrator.** This helps prevent them from pressuring you to clear the apartment.

8. **Read the fine print on "Body Release" forms.**  
   **Before signing anything to move a body from a hospital to a funeral home, ask if the signature creates a "contractual obligation to pay."** If the form implies financial liability, refuse to sign and ask the hospital to follow their internal protocol for unclaimed bodies.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a funeral service or cremation type today.
- You do not need to notify distant acquaintances or social media contacts immediately.
- You do not need to worry about the person’s debt, credit cards, or medical bills; these are the responsibility of the estate, not you.
- You do not need to decide if you want to apply to be the administrator of the estate; this is a complex legal process that can be addressed later.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel intense pressure from institutions (hospitals, funeral homes, landlords) to "step up" and sign papers. However, "stepping up" legally often means accepting thousands of dollars in debt. You are being a good friend by providing the authorities with the information they need to provide a dignified disposition through county or federal programs.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for friends navigating the immediate aftermath of a death without family. It does not cover long-term probate litigation or the complexities of state-specific inheritance laws.

## Important note
Rules for the "right of disposition" and "indigent burial" vary significantly by state and county. This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Always confirm with the local Coroner, Medical Examiner, or a probate attorney before signing documents that involve financial or legal warranties.
