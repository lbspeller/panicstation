# What to do if… police ask to search your car and you are unsure what your options are (USA)

## Short answer
If an officer asks for permission to search your vehicle, you have the legal right to clearly and calmly say: **"I do not consent to a search."** If the officer proceed with a search anyway, do not physically resist or interfere; instead, state your lack of consent again and observe the process from a safe distance.

## Do not do these things
- Do not physically block the officer or interfere with their movements if they begin a search.
- Do not lock the doors or roll up windows as a way to "stop" an officer who has already ordered a search.
- Do not make sudden movements or reach into the glove box, center console, or under the seat.
- Do not argue about constitutional law or threaten the officer with a lawsuit at the scene.
- Do not lie about the contents of the car or provide false identification documents.
- Do not say "okay" or "I guess so" if you do not want the search to happen; these can be interpreted as consent.

## What to do now
1. **Stabilize the environment immediately.** Pull your vehicle over to a safe area, turn off the engine, and turn on the interior light if it is dark outside.
   - Roll down the driver-side window approximately halfway.
   - Place both hands visibly on the top of the steering wheel.
2. **Announce your movements before acting.** If the officer asks for your license, registration, or insurance, state exactly where the items are before you reach for them.
   - Say: "My registration is in the glove box. I am reaching for it now."
3. **Establish your detention status.** Ask the officer: **"Am I free to go?"**
   - If the officer says yes, leave the area calmly and safely.
   - If the officer says no, you are being detained, but this does not automatically give them the right to search the car.
4. **State your refusal clearly if asked for consent.** If the officer asks "Mind if I have a look?" or "Do you have anything in there I should know about?", use a single, direct sentence:
   - **"I do not consent to a search."**
   - Do not follow this with explanations or justifications.
5. **Observe for "probable cause" cues.** Note if the officer mentions specific sensory information that allows them to search without your consent.
   - Common cues include the smell of marijuana or alcohol, visible contraband in "plain view," or the arrival of a K9 (drug-sniffing dog) unit.
6. **Remain silent during the search.** If the officer searches anyway, do not answer questions about what they find or why you refused consent.
   - Say: **"I am going to remain silent."**
7. **Document the interaction safely.** If you choose to record the interaction on a phone, do so openly and ensure the device is not held in a way that could be mistaken for a weapon.
   - Position the camera so it does not interfere with the officer's physical workspace.
   - Note the officer’s name, badge number, and the agency listed on their vehicle.
8. **Request a receipt for any seized items.** If the officer removes property from your vehicle, ask (without being confrontational) for a property receipt or an inventory list before you leave.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now if the officer's "probable cause" was legally valid; that is determined later in court.
- You do not need to file a formal complaint at the roadside.
- You do not need to explain your travel history or destination if you have already provided required identification.
- You do not need to "prove" you have nothing to hide by agreeing to a search.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant stress, a racing heart, or shaking hands during a police stop. Choosing to withhold consent is a standard exercise of your legal rights and does not imply guilt. Staying physically calm and keeping your hands visible is the most effective way to manage your immediate physical safety.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a roadside encounter. It does not cover long-term legal defense or the specific variations in "stop and identify" laws that exist across different U.S. states.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. While the Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches, there are numerous exceptions (such as "probable cause" or "search incident to arrest") that allow officers to search without your consent. Do not physically resist an officer under any circumstances.
