# What to do if… you are asked to complete an official form or declaration in a language you cannot read (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before answering. Tell the official who gave you the form that you cannot read the language and ask for a version you can understand or official help with the questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess at questions or tick boxes you do not understand.
- Do not sign or confirm an answer until you understand what is being recorded.
- Do not hand your passport, phone, or travel documents to an unknown bystander offering help.
- Do not rely on a phone translation alone for an important declaration if the wording is unclear.
- Do not argue about the delay; repeat that you want to complete the form accurately.

## What to do now
1. Say: “I cannot read this language. Please give me a version I can understand or help me understand each question.”
2. Ask the official who handed you the form whether an English version, another readable version, or official language help is available.
3. Point to any question you do not understand. Ask what it means before you write, select, sign, or confirm an answer.
4. If you are being rushed, ask where you can stand or sit while the issue is resolved.
5. If staff allow phone use, use a translation tool only as a backup. Confirm any unclear declaration with the official before submitting it.
6. If the form is from U.S. Customs and Border Protection and the issue cannot be resolved at the time, use the official CBP contact route later for follow-up.
7. If a foreign authority is handling the form while you are abroad, follow the local process. If you are a U.S. citizen abroad and the situation becomes serious or urgent, such as detention or a threat to your safety, contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a complaint.
- You do not need to research every rule that may apply to your trip while you are standing at the desk.
- You can review local laws and customs guidance later if the form was issued abroad.
- You can consider enrolling in STEP later if you are traveling abroad and want State Department alerts and updates.

## Important reassurance
Asking for help before completing an unreadable form is a reasonable step. A short pause to understand the questions is better than guessing and needing to correct an answer later.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Available language help and form procedures vary by authority and location. A U.S. embassy or consulate may assist U.S. citizens with urgent problems abroad, but it does not replace the local authority or guarantee a particular outcome. If the issue involves detention, criminal allegations, or significant penalties, specialist legal help may be needed.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, travel, consular, or professional advice.
