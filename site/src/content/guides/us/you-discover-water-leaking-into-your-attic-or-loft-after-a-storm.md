# What to do if… you discover water leaking into your attic or loft after a storm (USA)

## Short answer
Treat wet insulation or dripping in the attic as a possible electrical and ceiling-collapse hazard first. Keep away from wet wiring, fixtures, and sagging ceilings; shut off power only if you can reach the breaker panel from a dry, safe place.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go into the attic if the ceiling below is sagging, cracking, bulging, or soft.
- Don’t touch wet wires, junction boxes, recessed lights, ceiling fans, switches, outlets, or any wet electrical device.
- Don’t flip breakers on or off while standing in water or touching wet surfaces.
- Don’t climb on the roof during a storm, in high wind, on wet surfaces, or in the dark.
- Don’t wade into wet insulation; it can hide wiring, sharp objects, weak framing, and irritating dust or fibers.
- Don’t cover, repaint, or repair stained areas before you have taken photos.
- Don’t delay simple temporary damage-control steps, such as catching drips, just because you have not spoken to insurance yet.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area below the leak safe.** Keep people and pets out from under bulging ceilings, wet light fixtures, sagging drywall, or active dripping near electrical items.

2. **Check for urgent danger from a dry, stable place.** Look and listen for sparks, buzzing, crackling, smoke, a burning smell, water through a ceiling light, or a ceiling that looks ready to give way. If there is fire, sparking, or possible collapse, leave that area and call 911.

3. **Make power safer only if you can do it safely.** If you can reach the breaker panel without standing in water or touching wet surfaces, shut off power to the affected area. If you are unsure which breaker controls it, shut off the main breaker only if you can do that from a dry, safe place. If you cannot reach the panel safely, stay away from the wet area and call a licensed electrician or your electric utility.

4. **Do not turn wet electrical items back on.** Leave wet fixtures, outlets, appliances, fans, and equipment off until a qualified electrician or repair professional says they are safe.

5. **Catch and contain water from inside.** Put buckets, pans, or towels under drips. Move furniture, boxes, electronics, and stored items away from the leak path. If you can reach the attic hatch safely, you may place plastic sheeting near the hatch area to guide water into a container, but do not walk across wet insulation or wet ceiling areas.

6. **Document before disturbing the scene.** Take photos and video of the drip, wet insulation, rafters, ceiling stains, bulging areas, nearby light fixtures, damaged belongings, and any visible roof problem from the ground. Write down when the storm happened, when you noticed the leak, and which rooms are affected.

7. **Contact the right people for make-safe help.** Contact your homeowners or renters insurer and ask how they want temporary repairs documented. Contact a licensed roofer for emergency patching or tarping and to find the entry point, such as missing shingles, flashing, vents, chimney, or skylight. If you rent, notify the landlord or property manager immediately and keep your own dated record.

8. **Use only safe temporary repairs.** Reasonable temporary steps can include interior water-catching, moving belongings, and professional tarping. Save receipts. Avoid risky DIY roof work, especially on wet, steep, damaged, or storm-exposed surfaces.

9. **Start drying only after electrical safety is clear.** If power has been confirmed safe, increase ventilation if weather allows and use fans or dehumidifiers in safe areas. If you can see mold, avoid blowing air across it because that may spread spores; ask a qualified cleanup professional about the next safe step.

10. **Treat contamination or health sensitivity as higher risk.** If the water may include sewage, backed-up drain water, or floodwater, or if someone has asthma, a weakened immune system, or strong sensitivity to mold or dust, avoid disturbing wet insulation and consider professional cleanup.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether all attic insulation must be replaced.
- You do not have to identify the exact roof entry point yourself.
- You do not need to repaint, patch, or cover stains now.
- You do not need to throw everything away immediately; move items dry, photograph them, and decide later.
- You do not need to finalize permanent repair bids before the leak source and damage extent are confirmed.

## Important reassurance
A storm leak in an attic can look chaotic because water often travels along rafters, wiring paths, insulation, and drywall before it appears below. The right first response is to keep away from electrical and collapse risks, limit more water damage, document clearly, and bring in the right help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, temporary damage control, documentation, and getting qualified help involved. Permanent roof repairs, insulation removal, mold cleanup, and insurance decisions may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, insurance, electrical, construction, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you cannot make the area safe, suspect electrical involvement, or see signs of ceiling failure, leave the affected area and get urgent qualified help.
