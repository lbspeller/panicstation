# What to do if… your cloud storage is deleting files and you did not start it (USA)

## Short answer
Go to the cloud provider directly, not through the message link. Try to stop more deletions from syncing by pausing sync on your devices, then secure the cloud account and start recovery from the provider’s deleted-items and version-history tools.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep devices syncing while you investigate, if you can pause sync safely.
- Don’t click “stop deletion,” “verify your account,” or password-reset links inside the notice until you have checked the account by going to the provider directly.
- Don’t reuse an old password or a small variation of one.
- Don’t ignore the email account linked to the cloud account; it may be used to reset cloud passwords.
- Don’t permanently empty Trash, Recycle Bin, Recently Deleted, or deleted-items folders.
- Don’t factory-reset devices yet unless a trusted support or IT person tells you to; it may remove useful clues while the account remains exposed.

## What to do now
1. **Sign in safely without using the notice link.**
   - Open the provider’s official app, or type the provider’s website address yourself.
   - If you are on a public or shared device, use a private browser window if available, and sign out when finished.

2. **Pause or disconnect syncing on every device you can reach.**
   - On computers, pause syncing in the desktop sync client, quit the sync app, or sign out of the sync client if pausing is not available.
   - On phones and tablets, pause cloud photo or file backup if the app offers it.
   - Start with the device that received the alert, the device you last used, and any device that is currently online.

3. **Check the cloud account’s security and activity pages.**
   - Look for unfamiliar sign-ins, unknown devices, bulk deletions, newly added shared users, new sharing links, or connected apps you do not recognize.
   - If this is a work or school account, contact your IT or help desk now and say there may be active cloud-file deletion.

4. **Secure the cloud account.**
   - Change the password to a new, unique password.
   - Use “sign out of all devices,” “revoke sessions,” or similar wording if the provider offers it.
   - Turn on multi-factor authentication.
   - Remove unfamiliar third-party app access, connected apps, shared collaborators, and sharing links.

5. **Secure the email account linked to the cloud account.**
   - Change the email password and turn on multi-factor authentication there too.
   - Check for forwarding rules, filters, or mailbox rules you did not create.
   - Check recovery email addresses and phone numbers, and remove anything unfamiliar.

6. **Start recovery inside the cloud service.**
   - Check Trash, Recycle Bin, Deleted items, or Recently Deleted first.
   - Use version history for files that were changed, overwritten, encrypted, or replaced.
   - If the provider offers a whole-account or point-in-time restore, use it only after you have secured the account, because restoring before securing access may allow the same problem to continue.

7. **Save quick evidence before it disappears.**
   - Screenshot the deletion notice, recent activity page, device list, connected-app list, shared-user list, and any password-reset emails.
   - Write down when you first noticed the issue, which device received the notice, and which folders or photos appear affected.

8. **Use the provider’s official support or recovery route if files are still missing.**
   - Ask about bulk restore, deleted-file recovery, version history, account activity logs, and whether support can help stop ongoing deletion.
   - For a managed work or school account, ask IT whether retention rules, admin recovery tools, or security logs are available.

9. **Report fraud or cybercrime when relevant.**
   - If money was lost, a scam was involved, or someone used the account to defraud you, report it to the FTC.
   - If you believe this was cyber-enabled crime, file a complaint with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center.
   - If anyone is in immediate danger, call 911 or local police now.

## What can wait
- You do not need to know yet whether this was phishing, malware, a reused password, an app connection, a shared-folder mistake, or an admin change.
- You do not need to decide today whether to wipe devices, change cloud providers, replace computers, or rebuild your whole backup system.
- You can postpone password-manager setup, long-term backup planning, and device cleanup until the account is secured and recovery is underway.

## Important reassurance
A deletion alert can feel urgent and frightening, but many cloud services have recovery tools such as deleted-items folders, recently deleted folders, version history, and support routes. Your best first move is to slow the spread, secure access, and begin recovery through the provider.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce ongoing file loss, regain account control, and start recovery. Later decisions about malware cleanup, forensic review, workplace incident response, legal reporting, or long-term backup may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, technical, cybersecurity, financial, or professional advice. If you suspect malware, a work account incident, extortion, or a broader compromise, consider getting help from a trusted IT or security professional after you have paused syncing where possible and secured the account.
