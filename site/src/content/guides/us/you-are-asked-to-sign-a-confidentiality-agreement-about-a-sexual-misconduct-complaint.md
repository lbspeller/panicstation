# What to do if… you are asked to sign a confidentiality agreement about a sexual misconduct complaint (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign on the spot. Ask for a copy or a secure way to review the exact wording, and get independent legal advice before agreeing to confidentiality, nondisclosure, or non-disparagement terms.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign only because you feel rushed, overwhelmed, ashamed, or scared.
- Do not rely on a verbal summary instead of reading the written terms.
- Do not assume the agreement is automatically enforceable or automatically harmless. The wording, timing, facts, and state law can matter.
- Do not let pressure to sign cut you off from support.
- Do not keep your only copy or notes on an organization-controlled device or account if that feels unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for a pause and the exact wording.** If it feels safe, say: “I need time to review this before I decide whether to sign. Please give me a copy or a secure way to review the exact wording.”
2. **Ask what kind of document it is.** Ask whether it is a settlement agreement, severance agreement, confidentiality agreement, nondisclosure agreement, non-disparagement agreement, or part of an internal process.
3. **Flag terms that need legal review.** Look for confidentiality, nondisclosure, non-disparagement, no-cooperation, no-contact, liquidated damages, penalties, return of documents, arbitration, and joint-action-waiver wording.
4. **Ask an attorney to review the wording and timing.** Federal and state rules may affect some predispute nondisclosure, non-disparagement, arbitration, and joint-action-waiver terms in sexual assault or sexual harassment disputes. Ask an attorney whether any of those rules may apply to this document.
5. **Ask which conversations and routes remain open.** Ask an attorney about speaking with a counselor, a medical professional, a support service, law enforcement, or an agency if you choose.
6. **Use specialist support without pressure.** RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline offers confidential support and can help you think through immediate next steps.
7. **Keep a private note in a safe place.** Record the document name, who presented it, the requested deadline, whether you asked for time or a copy, and the response.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to file a charge, bring a lawsuit, tell other people, or leave a job, school, or other setting.
- You do not need to negotiate wording while you are under pressure.
- You do not need to make every later decision before getting advice.

## Important reassurance
Feeling rushed does not require an immediate signature. Pausing, asking for the written terms, getting independent advice, and prioritizing support are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist legal advice and can depend on the document, the setting, your state, and your safety.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
