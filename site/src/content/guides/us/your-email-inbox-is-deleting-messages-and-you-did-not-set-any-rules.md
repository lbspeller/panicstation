# What to do if… your email inbox is deleting messages and you did not set any rules (USA)

## Short answer
Use the email provider’s website, not a mail app, and check rules, filters, forwarding, Trash, Spam, Archive, and All Mail. If anything looks unfamiliar, secure the account by changing the password, signing out unknown sessions, and turning on multi-factor authentication.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep opening or refreshing the mailbox on lots of devices; sync may spread deletions across the account.
- Don’t empty Trash, Deleted Items, Spam, or Junk while you are still trying to recover messages.
- Don’t click “security” links in emails to fix it; go to the provider’s website directly.
- Don’t assume it is harmless while messages are still disappearing; treat it as possible account compromise until you check.
- Don’t delete suspicious rules, forwarding addresses, or sign-in notices before you capture what they show.

## What to do now
1. **Reduce syncing.**
   - Close mail apps on your phone, tablet, and computer.
   - Use one trusted device, preferably a computer, for the next steps.
   - Sign in through the email provider’s website.

2. **Find where the messages are going.**
   - Check Trash, Deleted Items, Spam, Junk, Archive, and All Mail.
   - Search for a missing message by sender, subject, or date.
   - If you find important messages in Trash or Deleted Items, move them to Inbox or a new folder called “Recovered”.

3. **Check rules, filters, and forwarding.**
   - Open webmail settings and look for inbox rules, filters, forwarding, POP, and IMAP settings.
   - Disable or delete any rule or filter you did not create, especially anything that deletes, archives, moves, marks as read, or forwards messages.
   - Remove any forwarding address you do not recognize.

4. **Check account access.**
   - Review recent sign-in activity, active sessions, connected devices, and connected apps if your provider offers those pages.
   - Sign out sessions or devices you do not recognize.
   - Confirm the recovery email address and phone number are yours.

5. **Secure the account.**
   - Change the password from the provider’s official account security page.
   - Turn on multi-factor authentication if it is available.
   - If the provider offers backup codes, save them somewhere safe after the account is secure.

6. **Recover messages while recovery options may still be available.**
   - Use the provider’s restore or recover deleted items option if messages are not still in Trash or Deleted Items.
   - If using Gmail, check filters, forwarding, POP, IMAP, Spam, Trash, and the Gmail missing-messages recovery option.
   - If using Outlook or Microsoft mail, check Deleted Items and any recover deleted items option.
   - If using iCloud Mail, check iCloud Mail rules and any cleanup or unsubscribe settings that may be moving mail.

7. **Keep quick proof before changing suspicious settings.**
   - Take screenshots of unknown rules, filters, forwarding addresses, connected apps, unfamiliar sign-ins, and account-change notices.
   - Keep any suspicious emails, texts, or alerts related to the account.

8. **Use official recovery or reporting routes if needed.**
   - If you cannot stop the deletions or cannot regain control, use the provider’s official “account compromised” or “secure account” process.
   - If this is a workplace or school account, contact IT or security.
   - If you think personal information was stolen, use IdentityTheft.gov.
   - If this connects to fraud, scams, or cyber-enabled crime, you can report it through ReportFraud.ftc.gov or IC3.gov.

## What can wait
- You do not need to switch email providers right now.
- You do not need to reorganize folders or clean up old mail right now.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to notify everyone; first stop the deletion and secure access.
- You do not need to do a full device rebuild as the first step unless your provider, workplace, school, or security software tells you to.

## Important reassurance
Missing or deleting messages can come from a rule, filter, forwarding setting, mail app sync setting, or account compromise. The first steps are the same: pause extra syncing, use webmail, find where messages are going, remove anything unfamiliar, and secure the account.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce further loss and regain control. Later decisions, such as checking other accounts that use this email for password resets, reviewing devices for malware, or getting specialist help, may be needed after the account is stable.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, technical, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. For account recovery, fraud, identity theft, workplace systems, or school systems, use official provider, government, workplace, school, or qualified professional support.
