# What to do if… you lose your company ID badge and are worried it could be misused (USA)

## Short answer
Report the missing badge now to your company’s security, facilities, reception, manager, or IT service desk so the credential can be disabled and any misuse can be checked.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait to see if it turns up before reporting it.
- Don’t post publicly about the loss or share photos, numbers, barcodes, QR codes, or visible details from the badge.
- Don’t borrow, lend, or share a badge while yours is missing.
- Don’t follow someone through a controlled door without using the temporary process your workplace gives you.
- Don’t try to confront someone if you think they have the badge.
- Don’t assume a PIN or photo removes all risk; it may still create impersonation or tailgating risk.

## What to do now
1. **Do one quick physical check, then report it.** Check the last place you used it, such as a parking gate, turnstile, reception desk, printer, locker, cafeteria, bag pocket, jacket pocket, or car console. Stop searching once the obvious places are checked.

2. **Use the fastest company channel that is open.** Contact building security, reception, facilities, your manager, or the IT service desk. Say: “My company ID badge is missing and could be misused. Please disable the access credential.”

3. **Ask for the badge to be disabled now.** Ask them to mark it lost or stolen in the access-control system, refuse it if it is presented, and tell you what temporary access process to use.

4. **Ask how to get a temporary or replacement credential.** Ask where to go, what government or company ID to bring, and whether you need your manager, security, or HR to approve the replacement.

5. **Tell IT if the badge also works as a smart card, login token, printer card, time-clock card, locker card, parking pass, or payment card.** Ask whether they need to revoke certificates, disable linked services, reissue credentials, or reset a PIN or password if it may have been exposed.

6. **Tell security what was attached or visible.** Mention keys, a parking pass, room numbers, access stickers, a lanyard, a note with codes, your name, photo, job title, company name, site address, or anything else that could help someone use it.

7. **Ask security to check for attempted use.** Give the last time and place you remember having the badge. Ask them to check access logs and, if their process allows, entry-point video for unusual activity between that time and disablement.

8. **If the badge was stolen, targeted, or already used by someone else, consider a local police report.** Non-emergency reporting varies by city and county, and is usually through the local law enforcement agency where the theft or misuse happened. Call 911 only for an emergency requiring immediate police, fire, or medical help.

9. **If you are a federal employee or contractor with a PIV or other agency credential, contact your agency ID, badging, PIV, or security office.** Tell them it is missing and ask for revocation and reissuance under your agency’s process.

## What can wait
- Replacement fees, HR forms, or longer explanations can wait until the badge has been reported and disabled.
- Fully retracing your day can wait until security knows the badge is missing.
- Deciding who is at fault can wait.
- Writing a detailed statement can wait unless security or your manager asks for one; a short factual timeline is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
Losing a work badge can feel alarming because it may involve building access and identity details. Fast reporting is the main harm-reduction step: once the credential is disabled and entry points know, misuse becomes much harder.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Your workplace may have extra rules depending on the site, industry, contract, security level, and whether the badge is tied to IT systems or federal credentials.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, HR, security, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Follow your organization’s instructions. If you feel unsafe or believe someone is trying to access your workplace using your identity, prioritize your safety and contact workplace security or law enforcement.
