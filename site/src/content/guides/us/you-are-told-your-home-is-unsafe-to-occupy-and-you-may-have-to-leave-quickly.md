# What to do if… you are told your home is unsafe to occupy and you may have to leave quickly (USA)

## Short answer
Prioritize immediate physical safety by exiting the structure and moving to a safe distance, then obtain official documentation of the vacate order (who issued it and why) before seeking emergency shelter through 211.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay inside to pack or clean if there is an active risk of collapse, fire, gas leak, or electrical arcing.
- Do not ignore an official "red tag" or "vacate order" even if the house looks safe from the outside.
- Do not touch light switches, breakers, or use landline phones if you smell gas or if there is standing water.
- Do not use candles, matches, or lighters for illumination during a quick exit or permitted re-entry.
- Do not sign any "surrender of possession" or liability waivers provided by a landlord or insurer without a secondary review.
- Do not re-enter the building once you have exited until an official (fire marshal, building inspector) gives explicit permission.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause immediately.** Exit the building and move away from immediate hazards like downed power lines, leaning walls, or the smell of gas.
2. **Obtain the official restriction details.** Identify the agency or person issuing the order (e.g., Fire Department, Department of Building Inspection, Code Enforcement).
   - Photograph any posted notice, "red tag," or "unsafe" placard.
   - Note the specific reason given for the closure (structural, utility, hazardous material).
3. **Notify your landlord or property manager in writing if you rent.** State clearly that you have been ordered to vacate by an official authority.
   - Request immediate information on temporary housing options or rent abatement.
   - Attach a photo of the vacate notice.
4. **Locate emergency shelter or housing assistance.** 
   - **Call 211** to reach the local clearinghouse for shelter intake, hotel vouchers, and disaster services.
   - Follow the operator's instructions for "coordinated entry" or "emergency housing specialists."
5. **Retrieve critical essentials only if the area is declared safe for a "quick grab."** If an official allows a 2-5 minute entry, prioritize:
   - Wallet, government IDs, and house/car keys.
   - Prescriptions, medical devices (CPAP, monitors), and glasses.
   - Phone, chargers, and a portable power bank.
   - Pet leashes, carriers, and immediate pet medications.
6. **Secure the exterior if possible and safe.** If you can do so without entering or risking injury, ensure doors are closed to prevent looting or weather damage. 
7. **Document the situation for insurance claims.** Take photos of the damage and the surrounding area from a safe distance.
   - Keep a log of the time you left and the names of any officials you spoke with.
   - Save all receipts for lodging, transportation, and emergency meals starting immediately.
8. **Verify your legal protections regarding habitability.** In many states (e.g., California under SB 610), you may have a "right of return" once repairs are made and may be entitled to a refund of prepaid rent for days the home is unoccupiable.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the long-term future of your lease or mortgage today.
- You do not need to perform a full inventory of your belongings right now; a general list and photos are sufficient for the first 24 hours.
- You do not need to hire a contractor or start repairs until the building is officially released by the city.
- You do not need to argue with officials about the vacate order; this can be appealed later through formal channels.

## Important reassurance
Being forced to leave your home is a high-stress event that often triggers confusion or a "freeze" response. Your primary responsibility is the physical safety of your household. Once you are in a safe location with a documented notice, the logistical and legal steps can be addressed one at a time.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the first few hours of a sudden displacement. Long-term housing, legal disputes, and major repairs require specialized professional assistance.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or structural engineering advice. Safety standards and tenant rights vary by state and municipality; always follow the directions of local emergency responders and building officials.
