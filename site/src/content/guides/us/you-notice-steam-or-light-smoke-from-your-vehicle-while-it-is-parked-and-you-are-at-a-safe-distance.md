# What to do if… you notice steam or light smoke from your vehicle while it is parked and you are at a safe distance (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately move at least 100 feet away from the vehicle, stay upwind and uphill, and call 911 if you see any signs of fire or are unsure of the source.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t approach the vehicle to investigate the source of the smoke or steam.
- Don’t open the hood or trunk, as this introduces oxygen that can cause a fire to flash or grow rapidly.
- Don’t attempt to drive the vehicle or move it to a different location.
- Don’t try to use a portable fire extinguisher on an engine compartment fire yourself.
- Don’t inhale any vapors, as vehicle smoke and battery off-gassing are highly toxic.

## What to do now
1. **Ensure you are at least 100 feet (30 meters) away from the vehicle.** Maintain this distance even if the smoke appears light or intermittent.
2. **Position yourself upwind and uphill from the vehicle.** This prevents the inhalation of toxic fumes, smoke, or leaking fluids that may flow toward you.
3. **If the vehicle is in a garage or structure, move to an open outdoor area.** Do not remain inside the building to observe the vehicle, as smoke and heat can accumulate quickly.
4. **Call 911 immediately if you see flames, sparks, or hear popping sounds.** Provide the dispatcher with the exact location, including level and bay numbers if in a parking garage.
5. **Identify the vehicle type to the dispatcher.** Specifically state if the vehicle is an Electric Vehicle (EV), a Hybrid (HEV), or was actively charging when the issue began.
6. **Watch for specific sensory cues from a distance.** Note if you see thick black smoke (oil/fuel), white vapor (coolant steam), or smell burning plastic (electrical).
   - Do not move closer to confirm these scents.
7. **Notify site security or building management once you are safe.** They may need to evacuate nearby vehicles or restrict access to the area until emergency responders arrive.
8. **Stay clear of the roadway and oncoming traffic.** Ensure you are in a location where responders can see you but where you are not obstructing their access to the scene.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to determine the mechanical cause (such as a radiator leak or oil spill) right now.
- You don’t need to retrieve personal belongings, insurance documents, or tools from the vehicle.
- You don’t need to call a tow truck or insurance agent until the fire department has cleared the scene.
- You don’t need to worry about the cost of professional inspection while the hazard is active.

## Important reassurance
Feeling a sense of urgency or alarm when seeing smoke is a standard biological response. Taking the "100-foot" precaution is the professionally recommended safety standard and does not represent an overreaction. Most vehicle fires start small and can escalate in seconds; distance is your most effective protection.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a parked vehicle situation where the user is already at a distance. It does not cover vehicle fire suppression or high-speed roadside emergencies.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and does not replace the instructions of 911 dispatchers or on-scene emergency personnel. Vehicle fires involving lithium-ion batteries require specialized firefighting techniques and may present delayed ignition risks. Always defer to professional responders.
