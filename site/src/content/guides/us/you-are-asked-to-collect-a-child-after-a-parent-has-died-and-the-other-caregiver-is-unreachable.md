# What to do if… you are asked to collect a child after a parent has died and the other caregiver is unreachable (USA)

## Short answer
Keep the child with the school or childcare setting until staff can arrange a safe handoff under their procedure. If there is immediate danger or an immediate need for police, fire, or ambulance assistance, call 911; otherwise ask the setting to use its local child-safety process.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pressure staff to release the child because the situation is urgent.
- Do not take the child unless the staff or agency arranging the handoff has confirmed that the child can leave with you.
- Do not claim to be the child’s parent or legal guardian, or sign forms as though you are.
- Do not post about the death or the child’s situation online or in group chats.
- Do not promise the child where they will live or what will happen next.
- Do not tell the child details about the death unless you are sure what they have already been told and the setting or family has asked you to do so.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the request through the school or childcare setting.** Call the setting back using its usual public contact number if you did not expect the request. Ask where the child is and whether the child is supervised and safe.
2. **Ask staff what their safe-handoff procedure requires.** They may ask for identification or check their authorized-contact records. Bring photo ID if you have it, but do not assume the child will be released to you.
3. **Try the unreachable caregiver and the available emergency contacts.** Call and text. Leave a brief factual message with your name, your number, the child’s location, and a request to call back.
4. **If no safe handoff can be arranged, ask the setting to use its local child-safety procedure.** Follow any instructions from the staff or agency arranging the handoff. If the child is in immediate danger or immediate police, fire, or ambulance assistance is needed, call 911.
5. **If staff or an agency arrange for the child to leave with you, confirm the immediate plan.** Ask where the child should go, whether anyone else is expected to meet you, and whether staff can record the handoff and your contact details.
6. **Do not guess your authority if the child needs medical care.** For an emergency, call 911. Otherwise, tell the healthcare provider that you are not the parent or legal guardian and ask what they need before care can proceed.
7. **Keep the next few hours simple.** Go only to the agreed safe place, bring essential items if staff provide them, and avoid discussing adult details or long-term plans in front of the child.
8. **Keep a short note for yourself.** Write down who contacted you, who arranged the handoff, the time, the destination, and any callback numbers or instructions you were given.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide permanent custody, guardianship, or long-term placement now.
- You do not need to decide or complete longer-term court paperwork now.
- You do not need to solve every practical issue tonight. Focus on a safe handoff, a calm place for the child, and a clear next contact.

## Important reassurance
Pausing while staff follow their safety procedure is not making the situation worse. A careful handoff protects the child and gives everyone time to work out the next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation only. If the child may remain with you beyond the first safe pause, ask a local child-welfare agency, kinship-care program, legal-aid service, or court self-help service about the state-specific next steps.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Procedures vary by state, county, school, and childcare setting.
