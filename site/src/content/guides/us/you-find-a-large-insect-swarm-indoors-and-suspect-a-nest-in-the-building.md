# What to do if… you find a large insect swarm indoors and suspect a nest in the building (USA)

## Short answer
Move people and pets away from the swarm, close doors to contain it, and avoid provoking the insects. If anyone has trouble breathing or signs of a severe allergic reaction after a sting, call 911 now and use prescribed epinephrine according to the person’s plan or the label.

## Do not do these things
- Do not swat at, vacuum, or stand close and spray a large swarm; this can provoke stinging insects and scatter them through the building.
- Do not seal or plug the suspected entry or exit hole while insects may still be active, unless a qualified professional tells you to.
- Do not use outdoor pesticides indoors.
- Do not use more pesticide than the label says.
- Do not set off bug bombs or foggers as a first move when you suspect a nest in walls, ceilings, or an attic.
- If you already used a fogger, do not re-enter early; follow the label’s time-out and ventilation instructions.

## What to do now
1. **Make space and contain the swarm.** Move people and pets to a separate area, close doors between you and the swarm, and keep that area off-limits. If you can do it without entering the swarm area, place a towel at the bottom of the door to reduce insects moving under it.
2. **Check for a medical emergency.** If anyone is wheezing, struggling to breathe, dizzy, faint, vomiting repeatedly, confused, or has swelling of the lips, mouth, throat, or tongue after a sting, call 911 immediately. If they have a prescribed epinephrine auto-injector, help them use it according to their plan or the label.
3. **Reduce spread only from a safe place.** Turn off fans that could move insects around. If the HVAC control is accessible without entering the swarm area or opening equipment panels, turn it off temporarily.
4. **Get a distant photo or video.** Use phone zoom from outside the swarm area. Note whether insects are clustering at a window, vent, light fixture, baseboard, ceiling gap, chimney, or wall crack.
5. **Look for one repeated entry point from a distance.** Insects streaming in and out of one gap, vent, crack, or fixture may mean a nest is in a wall, ceiling, attic, chimney, or other building void. Do not open the wall, ceiling, fixture, vent, or attic to check.
6. **Choose the right help.** If it looks like a temporary honey bee swarm outside the living space, such as a dense ball of bees on an outdoor branch or exterior surface, contact a local beekeeper association or Cooperative Extension for a swarm-catcher referral when feasible. If insects are indoors or you suspect a nest in the structure, contact a licensed pest control company or specialist bee-removal service experienced with structural nests.
7. **If it looks like wasps, yellowjackets, hornets, or you are unsure, contact licensed pest control now.** Say it is an indoor swarm, say you suspect a structural nest, and share the photo, video, and likely entry point.
8. **If you live in an apartment or condo, notify the property manager or HOA immediately.** A nest in a wall, roof void, attic, chimney, or shared structure may require coordinated access and treatment.
9. **If pesticide was sprayed or a fogger was used and anyone feels ill, call Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222.** If symptoms are severe, call 911 first.
10. **If the swarm is mostly in one room, keep doors closed.** Opening an exterior window can help only if you can do it without getting near the cluster; do not try this if insects are concentrated on or around that window.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact species right now.
- You do not need to open walls, enter attics, remove vents, or confirm the nest yourself.
- You do not need to buy pesticides immediately.
- You do not need to decide on repairs, sealing, cleanup, or prevention until after removal advice.
- Sealing cracks, vents, or holes can wait until a qualified professional has assessed the situation.

## Important reassurance
A sudden indoor swarm can feel out of control, but the safest early moves are simple: separate people from insects, avoid provoking the swarm, keep the area contained, and bring in the right help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Nest removal, pesticide decisions, building repairs, and allergy follow-up may need a licensed pest control professional, specialist bee remover, property manager, medical professional, or local Cooperative Extension office.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional pest control, medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you suspect anaphylaxis or any breathing difficulty after a sting, call 911 immediately and use prescribed epinephrine if available.
