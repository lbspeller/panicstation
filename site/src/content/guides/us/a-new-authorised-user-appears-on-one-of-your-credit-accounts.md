# What to do if… a new authorised user appears on one of your credit accounts (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the card issuer’s fraud department immediately to remove the unauthorized user, deactivate any secondary cards, and secure your account credentials.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait to see if the user makes a purchase before taking action.
- Don’t use the same password for your bank and your email account when resetting credentials.
- Don’t assume the issue is resolved just because the user was removed; the breach of your login info must be addressed.
- Don’t close the entire credit line until you have confirmed with the fraud department that a new card number (re-issue) is insufficient.
- Don’t confront individuals you suspect of adding themselves if you are in a domestic or unsafe situation.

## What to do now
1. **Secure your digital access points immediately to prevent further changes.**
   - **Change your password** for the credit card account and the **email account** associated with it.
   - Use a unique, complex password for each.
   - Enable **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** or Two-Step Verification on both the card account and your email.
   - Check your email settings for any **forwarding rules** you did not set, as these are often used by attackers to intercept alerts.

2. **Call the card issuer’s fraud department using the number on the back of your card.**
   - Request the **immediate removal** of the authorized user and the **deactivation** of any cards issued in that name.
   - Ask for a **new account number** and a replacement card (this is more secure than just replacing the physical card for the same number).
   - Ask the representative to check for other recent changes, such as an updated **mailing address**, **phone number**, or **bank account** linked for payments.
   - Request a **written confirmation** or a case number for the fraud report.

3. **Place a fraud alert on your credit file with one of the three bureaus.**
   - Contact **Equifax**, **Experian**, or **TransUnion** to place a free **initial fraud alert**.
   - You only need to contact **one** bureau; they are required by law to notify the other two.
   - This alert stays on your file for **one year** and requires lenders to verify your identity before opening new accounts.

4. **Review your full credit reports for other unauthorized activity.**
   - Go to **AnnualCreditReport.com** (the only federally authorized site) to request your free reports from all three bureaus.
   - Look specifically for **new accounts**, **hard inquiries**, or **addresses** you do not recognize.
   - If you find additional fraud, you must dispute those specific items with each bureau where they appear.

5. **Freeze your credit files at all three nationwide bureaus individually.**
   - Unlike an alert, you must contact **Equifax**, **Experian**, and **TransUnion** separately to place a **credit freeze**.
   - A freeze is free and prevents lenders from accessing your credit report to open new accounts until you lift it.

6. **File an official Identity Theft Report with the Federal Trade Commission (FTC).**
   - Visit **IdentityTheft.gov** to create a formal report.
   - This document provides you with specific legal rights, including the ability to permanently block fraudulent info from your credit report and stop debt collectors from contacting you about fraudulent debts.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to close the account permanently, as a new account number usually stops the breach.
- You can wait to perform a "full security audit" of all your other non-financial online accounts until your money and credit are locked down.
- You do not need to hire a private investigator or lawyer in the first 24 hours.

## Important reassurance
Unauthorized user fraud is a recognized form of identity theft that banks have established protocols to handle. Once you have secured your login and notified the fraud department, the issuer typically assumes liability for unauthorized charges. Taking these steps creates a documented trail that protects your credit score and legal rights.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for authorized user fraud on existing accounts. It does not cover the long-term resolution of complex mixed-file cases or criminal prosecution of the offender.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. In cases of identity theft involving known persons or domestic disputes, specific legal protections may apply; consult a qualified professional if needed.
