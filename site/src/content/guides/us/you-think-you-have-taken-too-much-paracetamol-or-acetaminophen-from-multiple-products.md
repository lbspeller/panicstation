# What to do if… you think you have taken too much paracetamol or acetaminophen from multiple products (USA)

## Short answer
Stop taking anything containing acetaminophen, often labeled APAP, and contact Poison Help now at 1-800-222-1222 for case-specific guidance. If the person has collapsed, had a seizure, has trouble breathing, or cannot be awakened, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take another dose “to get through the night” while you figure it out.
- Do not take a second pain, cold, flu, sleep, or prescription product without checking for acetaminophen or APAP.
- Do not drink alcohol until Poison Help or a clinician tells you what is safest in your situation.
- Do not try to make yourself vomit or “detox” at home.
- Do not wait for symptoms to appear before reaching out; symptoms can be delayed.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel faint, confused, very sleepy, very unwell, or you may need urgent care.

## What to do now
1. Stop all acetaminophen and APAP immediately. Put the bottles or packages aside so you do not take more by habit.

2. Collect every medication you took in the last 24 hours, or since the first overlapping dose you are worried about. Include pain relievers, cold and flu medicines, “PM” products, prescription pain medicines, and any combination products.

3. Check each label for “acetaminophen” or “APAP.” Look at the active ingredients section on over-the-counter products and the drug name or abbreviation on prescription labels.

4. Make a quick timeline. Write down the product names, strength in mg per tablet, capsule, or liquid dose, how much you took, and roughly what time you took it. Rough notes are better than delaying the call.

5. Contact Poison Help now. Call 1-800-222-1222 and say: “I may have taken too much acetaminophen from multiple products.” Give your timeline, the product names, and what symptoms, if any, are happening.

6. Use online Poison Control only if the person is awake, breathing normally, and not having emergency symptoms. If you cannot call, use the webPOISONCONTROL online tool and follow its instructions.

7. Call 911 instead of using the online tool if the person has collapsed, had a seizure, has trouble breathing, or cannot be awakened.

8. If Poison Help or emergency services tells you to go to the ER, bring all medication bottles, blister packs, packets, and any remaining pills. This helps clinicians work out what was taken.

9. Until you have been advised, do not “switch” to another product that contains acetaminophen. If you need symptom relief, ask Poison Help or a clinician what is safe given what you already took.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you “definitely overdosed.”
- You do not need perfect math before calling; an estimate and the product containers are enough to start.
- You do not need to search for every possible outcome online.
- You do not need to decide future pain, fever, cold, or flu treatment right now.

## Important reassurance
Accidental acetaminophen overdoses can happen because acetaminophen is in many pain, cold, flu, sleep, and prescription combination medicines. Reaching out quickly is a sensible protective step, even if you are not sure how much you took.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and connect you to urgent expert guidance. Later decisions, including whether testing or treatment is needed, should be made with Poison Help, emergency services, or a clinician.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical or other professional advice. If you suspect you have taken too much acetaminophen or APAP, contact Poison Help at 1-800-222-1222 promptly, and call 911 for emergency symptoms.
