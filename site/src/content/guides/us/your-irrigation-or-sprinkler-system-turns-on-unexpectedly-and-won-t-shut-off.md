# What to do if… your irrigation or sprinkler system turns on unexpectedly and won’t shut off (USA)

## Short answer
Stop the water first: close the irrigation system shutoff if you can find it quickly, or close the home’s main water shutoff if water is spreading or getting near the house or electrical equipment.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep toggling the controller repeatedly; a stuck valve can keep running even when the controller is off.
- Don’t open a valve bonnet or remove valve parts while the line is pressurized.
- Don’t over-tighten small plastic parts such as bleed screws or solenoids; snug by hand is enough.
- Don’t work around pooled water near outdoor outlets, pumps, landscape lighting transformers, or extension cords.
- Don’t dig in the yard in a panic to find the pipe.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safer first.** Watch for slippery surfaces and spraying water near outlets, landscape lighting, pump wiring, or extension cords. Keep children and pets away from the spray and puddles.
2. **Turn off the controller.** Switch the controller to off. If it is safe and easy, unplug it or turn off the breaker that feeds only the controller. This can stop a controller or programming problem, but a valve can still stay open mechanically.
3. **Shut off water to the irrigation system if you can find the valve quickly.** Look near the backflow preventer if you have one, where the irrigation pipe branches from the house supply, in a garage, basement, crawl space, or in a valve box near the point of connection. A ball valve is usually off when the handle is perpendicular to the pipe.
4. **If you cannot find the irrigation shutoff fast, shut off the home’s main water.** A gate valve usually closes by turning clockwise until it stops. A ball valve usually closes with a quarter-turn so the handle is perpendicular to the pipe. If it is safe, open a nearby hose bib, spigot, or cold tap to check that the flow has stopped.
5. **If only one zone is running and the valve box is safe to reach, try the quick manual-off checks.** Locate the valve for that zone. The solenoid is usually a cylinder with two wires. Turn the solenoid clockwise by hand until snug. If the valve has an external bleed screw or bleed knob, turn it clockwise by hand until snug.
6. **If it still will not stop, keep the water isolated and call for help.** Call a plumber urgently if water is reaching the house, garage, basement, crawl space, foundation, or electrical equipment. For a yard-only stuck zone, contact an irrigation contractor who services sprinkler valves. If you cannot safely operate the main shutoff, call a plumber or your water utility’s emergency contact route.
7. **After the water is off, take quick photos.** Photograph the controller screen, the running zone, the valve box, and any water near the structure before anything else is changed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to disassemble valves, flush debris, replace solenoids, or replace diaphragms right now.
- You do not need to troubleshoot wiring, sensors, pump controls, or schedules in the moment.
- Cleanup, repair decisions, and deeper diagnosis can wait until the water is reliably off and the area is safe.

## Important reassurance
A sprinkler zone can keep running even when the controller is off if a valve is stuck open, has debris inside, or was left in manual operation. Once the water is isolated, you have handled the most important immediate step.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stop water and reduce damage. Later repair may need an irrigation professional, plumber, electrician, or your water utility, depending on where the water is going and which shutoff works.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not professional plumbing, electrical, insurance, or other professional advice. If water is spreading, reaching electrical equipment, or you cannot safely isolate it, get professional help.
