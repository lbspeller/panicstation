# What to do if… you notice your roofline sagging after severe weather (USA)

## Short answer
Treat a newly sagging roofline after severe weather as a possible structural safety problem: keep people out from under it, do not enter the attic or climb onto the roof, and get local safety guidance or a licensed structural assessment before using that area again.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go into the attic or climb onto the roof to investigate.
- Do not try to “shore it up” with posts, jacks, or improvised bracing.
- Do not cut into ceilings, remove framing, or pull down drywall to “see what’s happening”.
- Do not stay inside the affected area if you hear unusual shifting, creaking, popping, or see cracks spreading.
- Do not stand under the sagging section to take photos.
- Do not agree to immediate, high-pressure repair deals from unsolicited crews.

## What to do now
1. **Move everyone away from the affected area.** Stay out of rooms directly under the sag and keep everyone out of the attic.
2. **Leave and call 911 if there are signs of immediate danger.** Leave immediately if you hear shifting or unusual noises, see falling debris, smell gas, see sparking, or the sag appears to worsen.
3. **Check only from safe places.** From ground level outside, look for a newly dipping ridge line, separated joints, leaning sections, loose roof materials, or parts sitting lower than before.
4. **Look from safe interior doorways only if the area feels stable.** Do not go under the sag. Watch for new ceiling cracks, doors or windows suddenly sticking, gaps where walls meet ceilings, bowed ceilings, or water coming through.
5. **Contact local officials for safety guidance.** Your local building department, code enforcement office, emergency management office, or fire department non-emergency line can tell you what local inspection or unsafe-building process applies. After a major storm, stay away from damaged buildings until local authorities or building inspectors say it is safe.
6. **Arrange a structural assessment quickly.** Ask for a licensed structural engineer, or a qualified building inspector recommended by local officials or your insurer, to decide whether the structure is safe and what temporary make-safe steps are appropriate.
7. **Notify your homeowners insurance and document safely.** Take photos or video only from ground level outside or from safe interior doorways. Note when you first noticed the sag, what severe weather happened, and any new cracks, sticking doors, leaks, or noises.
8. **Keep receipts for urgent safety measures you are told to take.** This can include temporary lodging, tarping by a professional, boarding, or other emergency steps, but do not start permanent repairs until the unsafe area has been assessed unless your insurer or local officials instruct you otherwise.
9. **If recent construction or roof work may be involved, report it without deciding the cause yourself.** Pull builder warranty papers, roof contractor invoices, permits, inspection records, and photos from before the storm if you have them. Tell both your insurer and the builder or roof contractor that the roofline appears newly sagged after severe weather.
10. **Screen repair offers carefully.** Before signing anything, check licensing and insurance where required, ask for a written scope and contract, avoid cash-up-front pressure, and do not sign over an insurance check to a contractor.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether this is a storm claim, warranty issue, workmanship defect, or contractor liability.
- You do not need to choose a permanent repair contractor before the structure has been assessed.
- You do not need to enter the attic to look for leaks right now.
- You do not need to argue about cause before safety, photos, and professional assessment are in place.

## Important reassurance
A roofline that suddenly looks different after severe weather is unsettling, and it makes sense to pause. Moving people away, avoiding the attic and roof, and getting the right safety check are cautious first steps, not overreacting.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repairs, permits, insurance, warranties, engineering reports, or contractor disputes may need help from licensed professionals, local officials, your insurer, or a qualified adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not engineering, legal, medical, financial, insurance, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you suspect the building may be unsafe, stay out of damaged areas and follow local authority instructions.
