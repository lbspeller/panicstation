# What to do if… your circuit breaker will not reset and you still have no power on that circuit (USA)

## Short answer
Stop trying to force the breaker handle, unplug all devices on that circuit to remove the load, and if it still fails to reset after one proper attempt, leave it OFF and call a licensed electrician.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t repeatedly flip the breaker back and forth if it doesn't stay in the ON position.
- Don’t use tape, tools, or force to hold a breaker handle in the ON position.
- Don’t replace a breaker with one of a higher amperage rating (e.g., replacing a 15A with a 20A).
- Don’t remove the "dead front" (the metal cover) of the electrical panel to look at wires.
- Don’t stand in water or have wet hands while touching the breaker box or any outlets.
- Don’t use high-draw appliances (like space heaters or portable ACs) on extension cords to bypass the dead circuit.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate signs of fire or severe electrical faults.** **If you see sparks, smoke, or flames, or if the breaker box is hot to the touch, evacuate the area and call 911 immediately.** 
   - If it is safe to do so, turn off the large "Main" breaker at the top of the panel to shut down power to the entire building.
2. **Identify and unplug every device on the affected circuit.** **Walk through the area without power and physically unplug appliances, lamps, chargers, and electronics from the outlets.** 
   - Ensure all wall switches (lights or disposals) on that circuit are in the OFF position.
3. **Position yourself safely at the electrical panel.** **Stand to the side of the breaker box and use the metal door as a shield between your face and the breakers.**
   - Use only one hand (preferably your non-dominant hand) to operate the switches.
   - Look away from the panel while flipping the breaker to protect your eyes from potential arc flashes.
4. **Perform a single, correct reset sequence.** **Push the tripped breaker handle firmly to the full OFF position until you hear or feel a distinct click, then flip it back to the ON position.**
   - A tripped breaker often sits in a middle "neutral" position; it must go to OFF before it can go to ON.
5. **Leave the breaker OFF if it trips again immediately.** **If the handle snaps back to the middle or OFF position, this indicates a "hard fault" (a short circuit or ground fault) that is too dangerous to override.**
   - Do not attempt a third reset.
6. **Check for a tripped GFCI outlet if the breaker stays ON but power is still missing.** **Locate any Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter (GFCI) outlets—the ones with "Test" and "Reset" buttons—in kitchens, bathrooms, or outdoors that may be "upstream" from your dead outlets.**
   - If the outlet is dry and shows no damage, press the RESET button once.
   - If the GFCI pops out immediately, there is a ground fault; stop and call a professional.
7. **Notify your landlord or call a licensed electrician.** **If you are a tenant, inform your property manager that a circuit has a persistent fault and cannot be reset, as this may affect the "implied warranty of habitability" depending on your state laws.**
   - If you own the home, contact a licensed electrical contractor and specify if the dead circuit powers critical items like a sump pump, furnace, or medical equipment.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to determine if the fault is in the walls or in a specific outlet right now.
- You don’t need to buy a replacement breaker; an electrician must verify if the breaker or the wiring is the failure point.
- You don’t need to move heavy furniture to inspect every hidden outlet today.

## Important reassurance
A breaker that refuses to reset is functioning exactly as intended. It is a safety valve designed to prevent a house fire or electrocution by stopping the flow of electricity when it detects a dangerous path. Leaving it off is the correct, safe response to an electrical fault.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. Electrical faults require diagnostic tools (multimeters, circuit analyzers) and professional training to resolve safely.

## Important note
This guide provides general safety information and does not replace the expertise of a licensed electrician. Electrical work carries a risk of death or permanent injury. In most US jurisdictions, significant electrical repairs must be performed by a licensed professional to comply with the National Electrical Code (NEC) and local building ordinances.
