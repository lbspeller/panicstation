# What to do if… you feel throat tightness or hoarseness after exposure to a potential allergen (USA)

## Short answer
Treat new throat tightness or hoarseness after allergen exposure as possible anaphylaxis: use your prescribed epinephrine now if you have it, and call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see whether throat or voice symptoms “settle down” after a likely allergen exposure.
- Do not rely on antihistamines, steroids, or an inhaler as the main treatment for throat, voice, or airway symptoms.
- Do not drive yourself to the emergency department.
- Do not stand up, walk around, or take a hot shower to calm down.
- Do not eat or drink to test whether swallowing is okay.
- Do not go off alone or lock yourself in a room.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the exposure.**
   - Stop eating or drinking the suspected trigger; spit out anything still in your mouth.
   - If it was food, you can rinse and spit once, but do not swallow.
   - If it was a medication, do not take any more of it.
   - If it was an insect sting and the stinger is visible, remove it quickly by scraping it away rather than squeezing it.

2. **Use epinephrine right away if you have it.**
   - Use your prescribed epinephrine auto-injector or prescribed intranasal epinephrine now.
   - Follow the instructions for your own device.
   - Note the time you used it.

3. **Call 911 and say this may be anaphylaxis.**
   - Say you had an allergen exposure and now have throat tightness or hoarseness.
   - Say whether epinephrine was used, and when.
   - Keep the phone on speaker if you can.

4. **Position yourself safely while waiting for help.**
   - Lie down and raise your legs if you can.
   - If breathing feels worse lying flat, sit upright with support.
   - Avoid standing or walking around.

5. **Use a second epinephrine dose if symptoms are not clearly improving, symptoms return, or your plan says to repeat.**
   - Use a second dose if you have one and it has been about 5 minutes, or as your device, emergency plan, or dispatcher says.
   - Do not delay 911 or emergency care while trying to decide whether the first dose “worked enough.”

6. **Make it easy for EMS to reach and treat you.**
   - Unlock the door if you can do this safely.
   - Turn on a light.
   - Ask someone nearby to stay with you and bring your epinephrine devices, allergy plan, medication list, and medical ID if you have them.

7. **If you do not have epinephrine, still treat this as an emergency.**
   - Call 911 now.
   - Stay with someone if possible.
   - Keep still and avoid walking around while waiting for help.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether this was “really” anaphylaxis.
- Researching the trigger online.
- Testing the suspected trigger by eating, drinking, touching, or taking it again.
- Calling for refills, follow-up appointments, insurance questions, work messages, or school messages.
- Working out who is at fault or how the exposure happened.

## Important reassurance
Throat tightness and hoarseness after allergen exposure are frightening symptoms, and acting early is appropriate. Feeling shaky, panicked, embarrassed, or unsure does not mean you are overreacting.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first minutes only. Later decisions about allergy testing, prevention, replacement epinephrine, school or work plans, and follow-up care may need help from a qualified clinician or allergy specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, diagnosis, treatment, or professional advice. Throat tightness or hoarseness after allergen exposure can be a sign of anaphylaxis and needs emergency assessment, even if symptoms improve after epinephrine.
