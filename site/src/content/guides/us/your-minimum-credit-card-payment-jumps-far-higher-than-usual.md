# What to do if… your minimum credit card payment jumps far higher than usual (USA)

## Short answer
Check the latest statement for the item that changed, then contact the card issuer before the due date if the new minimum is wrong or you cannot safely pay it.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore the due date; late fees, penalty APRs, or credit-history harm may follow.
- Don’t pay the new minimum if it means missing essentials like rent, utilities, food, or medicine without first asking the issuer about options.
- Don’t use payday loans, car title loans, or another high-cost emergency loan just to cover one card minimum.
- Don’t schedule a payment unless you are reasonably sure the money will clear.
- Don’t assume the statement is right if there is an unfamiliar charge, fee, or interest change.

## What to do now
1. **Open the latest statement and write down three numbers.**  
   Write down the minimum due, the due date, and the statement balance. Then compare this statement with the last one.

2. **Find what caused the jump.**  
   Look specifically for:
   - a promotional APR ending
   - new interest charges
   - a late fee or returned-payment fee
   - a cash advance fee
   - a penalty APR or APR change
   - new spending, reversed credits, or balance transfers
   - a charge or fee you do not recognize

3. **Check your account messages and card agreement.**  
   Look for a notice about a rate, fee, or minimum-payment change. If you cannot find the reason, ask the issuer to identify exactly which line items changed the minimum.

4. **If you can safely pay the new minimum, schedule it and save proof.**  
   Use a payment amount and bank account that will not cause an overdraft. Save the confirmation number or screenshot, and stop using the card until you understand the new cost.

5. **If you cannot afford the new minimum, contact the issuer before the due date.**  
   Ask what changed and what temporary help is available. You can ask for:
   - a reduced temporary payment you can actually make
   - a fee waiver, especially for a one-off problem
   - hardship options or interest relief, if available
   - confirmation of whether the arrangement changes your account status, interest rate, or credit reporting

   Script: “My minimum payment jumped to $___ and I cannot afford that this month. What changed, and what temporary payment or hardship options are available before the due date?”

6. **If there may be a billing error, dispute it quickly and in writing.**  
   Call the issuer now, but also send a written billing-error notice to the address or channel the issuer gives for billing disputes. To protect billing-error rights, make sure the issuer receives it no later than 60 calendar days after the first statement showing the error was sent. Keep copies, dates, and the disputed amount.

7. **If you still cannot make the minimum, pay only what you safely can and keep records.**  
   A partial payment may reduce the balance and interest that accrues, but it usually will not count as making the minimum. Keep a log of the date, time, person or department contacted, and what was said or agreed.

8. **If this is not a one-month problem, contact a reputable nonprofit credit counselor.**  
   Ask about fees before signing anything, and be cautious of any company that guarantees debt will disappear, asks for upfront settlement fees, or tells you to stop talking to your card issuer.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to close the card.
- You do not need to choose a balance transfer or consolidation loan while panicked.
- You do not need a full long-term payoff plan before stabilizing this due date.
- You do not need to dispute every small item; focus first on the item that caused the jump or any clear error.

## Important reassurance
A sudden minimum-payment jump usually has a traceable cause on the statement. Finding the trigger and contacting the issuer early can help you avoid making the situation worse.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: identify the cause, protect the due date where possible, and use the right channel for help or a billing dispute. Later decisions about debt payoff, credit counseling, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, credit, or professional advice. Issuer policies, account terms, and state rules can vary.
