# What to do if… your minimum credit card payment jumps far higher than usual (USA)

## Short answer
Identify the specific cause (fees, interest, or error) on your statement immediately, then either pay the minimum to protect your credit or submit a formal written dispute within 60 days if the amount is incorrect.

## Do not do these things
- Don't assume a phone call protects your legal rights — only a written notice to the correct address triggers Fair Credit Billing Act protections.
- Don't ignore the due date — even if the amount is wrong, missing the deadline can trigger late fees and damage your credit score.
- Don't take out a high-interest payday or title loan to cover the gap.
- Don't pay the new minimum if it will cause you to miss rent or essential utilities without first contacting the issuer for hardship assistance.
- Don't close the account in a panic — this could negatively impact your credit utilization and score immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Compare your current statement line-by-line with the previous month's statement.** **Look specifically for three things: new fees (late, returned payment, or annual fees), a change in your Interest Charge section, and any transactions you did not authorize.** 
   - Note if a promotional 0% APR period ended, which causes interest to start accruing.
   - Check if you lost your "grace period" because of a late payment last month; this causes interest to be charged on every new purchase starting the day you buy it.

2. **Verify the calculation of the minimum payment if the balance itself hasn't changed.** **Most issuers calculate the minimum as 1% to 3% of the total balance plus any new interest and fees.** 
   - If you have a large balance, even a small interest rate (APR) increase can cause a significant dollar-amount jump in the minimum due.

3. **Call the credit card issuer's customer service number if you cannot afford the jump.** **State clearly that you have had a sudden increase in your minimum payment and need to discuss "hardship options" or a "payment assistance program."** 
   - Ask for a "temporary interest rate reduction" or a "fee waiver" if the spike was caused by a one-time late fee.
   - Record the name of the representative, the date, and the specific terms of any agreement made.

4. **Submit a formal written "Billing Error Notice" if you suspect the jump is due to a mistake.** **To preserve your legal rights under the Fair Credit Billing Act, you must mail this letter so it is received within 60 days of the statement date.** 
   - Use the "Billing Inquiries" address found on the back of your statement (this is usually different from the payment address).
   - Include your name, account number, the dollar amount in error, and the reason you believe it is a mistake.

5. **Stop all new spending on the card immediately to prevent the minimum from rising further.** **Every new purchase adds to the principal balance and potentially the interest charge for the next cycle.** 

6. **Prioritize essential living expenses over the credit card if you are forced to choose.** **Ensure your housing, food, and necessary medicine are covered before sending money to a credit card issuer.** 

## What can wait
- You don't need to decide on a long-term debt consolidation plan or bankruptcy today.
- You don't need to determine how you will pay off the entire balance right now.
- You don't need to shop for a new credit card or balance transfer until you have stabilized this month's payment.

## Important reassurance
Minimum payment spikes are almost always triggered by a specific, identifiable change such as a promotional rate ending or a fee being applied. Once you identify the cause, you can often negotiate with the bank or use federal law to correct errors. Taking the first step to read the statement is the most effective way to regain control.

## Scope note
This guide is for the immediate stabilization of a sudden credit card payment increase. It focuses on identifying the cause, protecting legal rights, and immediate communication with lenders. It does not cover long-term debt management or credit repair strategies.
