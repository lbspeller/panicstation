# What to do if… you discover someone has saved intimate photos you sent them (USA)

## Short answer
Stop sending intimate content and do not pay, bargain, or send more images if you feel pressured. Save the key proof without re-sharing the images, then use the relevant platform, takedown, or specialist support route.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send more images, “verification,” money, gift cards, passwords, or anything else to “make it go away.”
- Don’t bargain with threats, especially if someone is demanding money, more images, or silence.
- Don’t publicly post about it while you are still trying to contain it.
- Don’t keep rereading upsetting messages; save the key proof, then mute, archive, or hide the chat if you can.
- Don’t meet the person alone to demand deletion if there is any intimidation, coercion, or safety risk.
- Don’t forward the intimate image to friends or helpers unless a trusted support service specifically asks and it is safe and lawful to do so.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause first.** If you are in immediate physical danger, leave if you can and call **911**.

2. **Stop the flow of new material.** Do not send any more intimate content while this is active. Turn off chat settings that automatically save images to your camera roll, cloud backup, or shared devices if you can do that safely.

3. **Preserve the key proof without spreading the image.**
   - Screenshot messages, threats, usernames, handles, profile URLs, dates, times, payment demands, and any visible links.
   - If the image is posted, copy the exact URL and note the platform and account.
   - Store the proof somewhere the other person cannot access, such as a private email account, secure folder, or trusted device.

4. **If the image is already posted, report it on the platform first.**
   - Use the in-app report option for nonconsensual intimate imagery, intimate image abuse, harassment, or the closest available category.
   - If the platform has a TAKE IT DOWN Act notice process, submit that request too.
   - Covered platforms generally must remove reported nonconsensual intimate images and known identical copies within 48 hours of your request. If the platform has no process, the process is broken, or it does not act after your request, you can report the platform to the FTC.

5. **If you are 18 or older and still have the image, consider StopNCII.org.** It can create a hash on your device so participating platforms can help detect and block matching intimate images. This is most useful when you are worried the image may be shared or reshared online.

6. **If there are threats, blackmail, or demands, treat it as sextortion.**
   - Save the threats.
   - Stop replying if you can do so safely.
   - Do not pay, send gift cards, or send more images.
   - Report cyber-enabled threats or scams to IC3, and use the FBI sextortion resources for FBI reporting options. If you are in immediate danger, call **911**.

7. **If anyone in the image was under 18 when it was taken, use child-safety routes now.**
   - Report suspected child sexual exploitation to NCMEC CyberTipline.
   - Use NCMEC Take It Down to help participating platforms detect and remove or stop the spread of youth sexual images or videos.
   - Do not send, share, or download an image in order to use Take It Down. Use only images or videos already on your device, and follow the official tool’s instructions.

8. **Get specialist support before deciding what to do next.** The Cyber Civil Rights Initiative Safety Center can help with image abuse support, referrals, and practical takedown guidance.

9. **Check account access if the person may have had your device, passwords, or cloud login.**
   - Change your email password first.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication.
   - Sign out of sessions or devices you do not recognize.
   - Check cloud photo backups and shared albums for access you did not intend.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to confront them, report to police, contact a lawyer, or tell your workplace, school, or family.
- If there are no threats and nothing has been shared, you do not have to do a big confrontation today. A short written boundary later, such as “Delete all copies and do not share them,” may be enough, and you can stop engaging after that.
- You do not need to contact every platform at once. Start with the platform where it is posted, threatened, or most likely to spread.
- You do not need to know the exact legal label today. “Revenge porn,” “sextortion,” “image abuse,” and “nonconsensual intimate imagery” can overlap, and a specialist service can help you choose the right route.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shocked, scared, ashamed, angry, or frozen is a normal stress response to a privacy violation. You still have practical options, and you do not have to handle the next steps alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the moment, reduce the risk of spread, and connect you to specialist help. Later decisions about state-specific legal options, school or workplace support, protection orders, or civil action may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. Laws and platform processes can vary by state and by service. If you are in immediate danger, call **911**.
