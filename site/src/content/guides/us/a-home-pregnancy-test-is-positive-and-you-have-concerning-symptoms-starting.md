# What to do if… a home pregnancy test is positive and you have concerning symptoms starting (USA)

## Short answer
Go to the nearest emergency room or call 911 immediately if you have severe abdominal pain, heavy bleeding (soaking two pads per hour), or shoulder tip pain. For milder symptoms like spotting or light cramping, contact an OB-GYN or clinic for an urgent assessment today.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for a scheduled appointment if pain is severe, worsening, or localized to one side.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel faint, dizzy, or are bleeding heavily.
- Do not take ibuprofen, naproxen, or other NSAIDs unless specifically directed by a clinician.
- Do not use tampons or menstrual cups; use pads to track the amount of bleeding.
- Do not ignore "shoulder tip pain" (pain where your shoulder meets your arm) even if your stomach pain feels manageable.
- Do not stop any regularly prescribed medications without consulting a healthcare provider.

## What to do now
1. **Determine if you require emergency stabilization at a hospital.**
   - Seek immediate emergency care if you experience:
   - **Heavy vaginal bleeding** (soaking through two large maxi pads per hour for two hours straight).
   - **Severe or stabbing abdominal or pelvic pain**, especially if it is only on one side.
   - **Shoulder tip pain**, which can indicate internal bleeding irritating the diaphragm.
   - **Fainting, extreme dizziness, or loss of consciousness.**
   - **A severe headache, vision changes (blurriness or spots), or high fever (100.4°F or higher).**
2. **Contact your healthcare provider or a local clinic immediately for non-emergency symptoms.**
   - Call an OB-GYN, midwife, or urgent care center if you have light spotting, mild cramping, or feel generally unwell.
   - State clearly: "I have a positive pregnancy test and I am experiencing [list symptoms]."
   - Ask for a same-day evaluation to rule out complications such as an ectopic pregnancy or early pregnancy loss.
3. **Document your symptoms for the medical triage team.**
   - Note the exact time symptoms started and the color/consistency of any bleeding.
   - Record the date of your last normal menstrual period.
   - Note if the pain is sharp, dull, constant, or intermittent.
4. **Prepare your medical and identification essentials for transport.**
   - Gather your ID, insurance card (if available), and a list of all current medications and supplements.
   - Bring the positive pregnancy test or a clear photo of the result.
   - Ensure your phone is charged and bring a charger.
5. **Manage pain safely while awaiting professional evaluation.**
   - Acetaminophen (Tylenol) is generally the first-line recommendation for pain or fever in pregnancy when taken at the lowest effective dose.
   - Do not rely on over-the-counter medication if symptoms are worsening; prioritize reaching a clinical setting.
6. **Notify a trusted contact of your location and status.**
   - Inform someone that you are seeking medical evaluation and ask them to remain available by phone.
   - If you are alone, keep your doors unlocked and your phone within reach until help arrives.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a long-term prenatal care plan or birth site today.
- You do not need to determine the cause of the symptoms yourself.
- You do not need to notify your employer or family about the pregnancy itself; you only need to address the immediate medical need.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of an initial emergency screening; federal law (EMTALA) requires hospitals to screen and stabilize you in an emergency regardless of your ability to pay.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when a positive test is followed by physical symptoms. Many individuals experience spotting or mild cramping in early pregnancy that does not lead to a complication. However, reacting quickly to warning signs is the standard and safest procedure to ensure your health is protected.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage guidance only. It is not a diagnostic tool and does not replace a physical examination by a qualified medical professional.

## Important note
Under the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (EMTALA), Medicare-participating hospitals in the USA are required to provide a medical screening examination and stabilizing treatment for emergency medical conditions, including pregnancy-related emergencies. If you are in pain or bleeding heavily, you have a right to be evaluated in an emergency department.
