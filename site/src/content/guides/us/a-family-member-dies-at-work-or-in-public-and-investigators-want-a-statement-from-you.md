# What to do if… a family member dies at work or in public and investigators want a statement from you (USA)

## Short answer
Ask if you are free to leave and request a case number. You have the right to decline a statement until you have consulted with a lawyer or reached a safe environment where you can provide an accurate account.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide technical guesses regarding times, speeds, distances, or medical causes.
- Do not sign any document that you have not read or that contains phrasing you did not use.
- Do not offer theories, motives, or "theories of the case" to investigators.
- Do not consent to a search of your phone or belongings without a warrant.
- Do not post details of the active investigation or the incident on social media.

## What to do now
1. **Determine your current legal status immediately.** Ask the officer or investigator: "Am I free to leave?"
   - If the answer is yes, you may depart.
   - If the answer is no, ask: "Am I being detained?" and "Am I a suspect or a witness?"
2. **State your need for a pause if you are in shock.** If you are experiencing nausea, dissociation, or disorientation, say: "I am not in a condition to provide an accurate statement right now. I will follow up later."
3. **Request the investigator's identifying information.** Ask for the lead investigator’s name, badge number, department, and the official **case number**.
4. **Invoke your right to remain silent if you feel pressured.** Use the specific phrase: "I am exercising my right to remain silent and I want a lawyer." 
   - Once you say this, stop answering questions.
5. **Identify the agency leading the investigation.** 
   - In public deaths, this is usually local police or the Sheriff’s Department.
   - In workplace deaths, this involves OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration) or a state-level equivalent.
6. **Request contact information for Victim-Witness services.** Ask: "Is there a Victim-Witness Coordinator or a Victim Services office I should contact?"
   - Families of the deceased often have specific rights under the Crime Victims' Rights Act (18 U.S.C. § 3771).
7. **Inquire about the OSHA National Family Liaison if it is a workplace death.** Ask the OSHA inspector for their **Area Office** contact and the name of the liaison assigned to communicate with the next of kin.
8. **Control the format of any statement you choose to give.** You may request to provide a written statement later or have the interview conducted at a scheduled time in a private location.
9. **Separate personal observations from hearsay.** When speaking, use clear qualifiers: "I saw," "I heard," or "I was told by [Name]."
10. **Designate a single point of contact for the family.** Select one person to handle incoming calls from investigators and the media to ensure consistent information and prevent repeated questioning of grieving relatives.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a complete narrative of the event immediately.
- You do not need to decide today whether to release the deceased's personal records or electronic devices.
- You do not need to speak to the employer's insurance company or their legal team.
- You do not need to review crime scene photos or autopsy reports today.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for your memory to feel fragmented or for you to feel overwhelmed during initial questioning. Taking a pause before giving a statement is a standard procedure used to ensure that the record is accurate and that your rights are protected.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and the immediate interaction with law enforcement or regulatory investigators. It does not cover the long-term probate, civil litigation, or workers' compensation processes.

## Important note
This information does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding "stop and identify" and witness requirements vary by state. If you believe you may be held responsible for any aspect of the incident, seek independent legal counsel before providing any information to authorities.
