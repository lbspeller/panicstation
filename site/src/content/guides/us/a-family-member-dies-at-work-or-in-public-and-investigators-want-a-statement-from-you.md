# What to do if… a family member dies at work or in public and investigators want a statement from you (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before giving a detailed statement. Ask whether you are free to leave; if not, ask whether you are being detained. If you may be blamed, or you are unsure about your legal risk, ask to speak with a lawyer before answering further questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess times, distances, speeds, causes, or technical details.
- Do not try to solve the case or explain another person’s motives.
- Do not sign a statement you have not read carefully or that contains wording you do not agree with.
- Do not consent to a search of your phone or belongings on the spot unless you choose to. Ask whether the investigator is claiming legal authority, and do not physically interfere.
- Do not delete or alter messages, photos, notes, or other records that may relate to the death.
- Do not post details online or argue about blame while you are overwhelmed.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause.** If the area is unsafe or emergency directions are being given, move to a safer place and follow emergency instructions. If allowed, ask to step somewhere quieter before discussing details.
2. **Ask who is contacting you.** Write down the investigator’s agency, name, callback route, and case or incident number. Ask: “Am I free to leave?” If the answer is yes, ask to arrange any voluntary interview for later. If the answer is no, ask: “Am I being detained?”
3. **Pause the questions when needed.** If you are detained, arrested, unsure whether your answers could put you at risk, or do not want a voluntary interview now, say: “I want to remain silent. I want to speak with a lawyer before answering questions.” Do not keep explaining. Do not ignore a subpoena, court order, or other formal legal demand; get legal advice promptly if you receive one.
4. **If you choose to answer now, keep it factual.** Say only what you personally saw, heard, or received. Separate firsthand information from what someone else told you. It is okay to say that you do not know or do not remember. Ask for a break if you need one. Read any written statement carefully and ask for corrections before signing.
5. **If you want more time, ask for it.** Ask whether a voluntary interview can be scheduled later and whether a written statement would be acceptable. Do not assume that a particular format will be accepted.
6. **If the death happened at work, ask which workplace-safety office is involved.** Ask whether federal OSHA or a state workplace-safety office is handling the incident. OSHA says family members may be contacted early in an investigation and may share relevant information with the local Area Office overseeing the inspection.
7. **If a crime may be involved, ask about support.** Ask whether the investigating agency has a victim or witness assistance contact.
8. **Make a short record of each contact.** Note the agency, names, callback details, case number, date, time, questions asked, and anything you provided.
9. **Use one trusted person for logistics.** Ask them to log calls, relay practical messages, and help you avoid repeating partial explanations to several people. They do not need to answer substantive questions for you.

## What can wait
- A detailed voluntary narrative usually does not need to be given on the spot.
- You do not need to decide today whether to voluntarily share personal records, devices, or family documents.
- You do not need to speak to the media, an employer’s lawyers, or other outside parties right now.
- You do not need to make long-term legal or financial decisions while you are overwhelmed.
- If you receive a warrant, subpoena, court order, or other formal legal demand, do not obstruct or ignore it. Get legal advice promptly.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to slow down. Saying only what you know, asking for a break, and arranging a voluntary conversation for later can reduce avoidable mistakes.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: how to avoid being rushed into an inaccurate or risky statement while you are grieving. Later decisions about an investigation, medical examiner or coroner procedures, workplace-safety processes, records, and support may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Laws and practices vary by state and situation. If you are not clearly taking part in a voluntary interview, or there is any chance you could be blamed, seek independent legal advice before answering detailed questions.
