# What to do if… you receive notice that someone in custody is being released soon and they have nowhere to go (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the facility’s **Unit Team or Reentry Coordinator** immediately to confirm the person's designated county of release and supervision requirements, then initiate a **Coordinated Entry** or emergency shelter intake process.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t offer your address as a "placeholder" if you cannot actually house the person; this can lead to technical violations or legal trouble for you.
- Don’t assume the facility will automatically provide housing; many systems only provide a list of local shelters or a bus ticket to the county of conviction.
- Don’t delay calling the supervising agency (Probation/Parole) if the person is being released on supervision, as they must approve any address.
- Don’t pay for a motel or temporary room without confirming if that address is acceptable to the supervising officer.
- Don’t promise the person a specific outcome until you have a confirmed intake appointment or a bed reservation.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the person's specific legal "County of Release" or "District of Supervision."** Most jurisdictions require individuals to be released to the county where they lived or were convicted; confirm if the person is legally allowed to seek housing in your specific area.
2. **Contact the facility’s Unit Team, Case Manager, or Reentry Coordinator immediately.** Request the "Release Plan" or "Discharge Plan" and ask specifically if the person has been cleared for "Transient Status" (homeless release) or if a physical address is mandatory for their release to proceed.
   - If federal (BOP), ask for the **Reentry Affairs Coordinator** and the status of any **Residential Reentry Center (RRC)** or halfway house referrals.
3. **Verify if the person is being released to mandatory supervision (Parole, Probation, or Supervised Release).** If they are, obtain the name and contact information for the assigned field officer and ask what types of temporary housing (shelter, sober living, or transitional programs) they will approve.
4. **Initiate the local "Coordinated Entry" process for the intended release area.** This is the standardized HUD-mandated system for accessing homeless services in the US; search for "[County Name] Coordinated Entry" or call a local housing referral line to start the intake.
5. **Secure a "first night" emergency shelter placement if no transitional housing is available.** Call local shelters directly to ask about their intake hours and whether they accept individuals directly from correctional facilities.
   - Mention specifically if the person has a "release date" as some programs prioritize those transitioning from custody.
6. **Ensure the person will have their "Release Packet" and vital documents upon exit.** Confirm with the facility that the person will be released with state-issued ID (or a temporary ID), their social security card, medication supplies (typically 30 days), and their discharge/supervision paperwork.
7. **Establish a clear meeting point and communication method for the hour of release.** Confirm the physical exit point of the facility and ensure the person has a way to contact you or the shelter intake office immediately upon crossing the gate.
8. **Contact 988 if the person experiences a mental health or substance use crisis during the transition.** If the stress of the release leads to a non-violent psychiatric emergency, use the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline for immediate stabilization and referral.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to find a permanent apartment or long-term lease right now.
- You do **not** need to provide furniture or household goods until the person is stabilized in a placement.
- You do **not** need to resolve long-term employment issues in the first 24 hours.

## Important reassurance
The transition from custody is often chaotic, and it is normal to feel overwhelmed by the lack of immediate housing options. Focus on the next 24 hours; as long as there is a confirmed location for the first night and a clear check-in plan with supervision, the immediate crisis is managed.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for immediate housing crises during the release process. Rules for sex offenders or those with specific violent convictions may differ significantly, and local housing availability varies by jurisdiction.

## Important note
This information is for stabilization purposes and is not legal advice. Release requirements are determined by the sentencing court and the supervising agency. Always prioritize instructions given by a Parole or Probation officer over general guidance.
