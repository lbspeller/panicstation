# What to do if… you receive bills addressed to a person who died immediately after the death (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay any bills from your personal funds or acknowledge responsibility for the debt. Forward all billing correspondence to the estate's personal representative and notify creditors of the death in writing to initiate formal estate settlement.

## Do not do these things
- Don't make "good faith" payments from your own bank account to stop collection calls.
- Don't sign any "responsible party" or "guarantor" forms provided by nursing homes, hospitals, or funeral directors without legal review.
- Don't provide your Social Security number or personal financial details to anyone claiming to collect on the deceased's accounts.
- Don't assume you are immune to liability if you live in a community property state (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI) or if the bill is for "necessaries" like medical care.
- Don't ignore bills for secured assets (like a car or home) if you intend to keep those assets, as non-payment can lead to repossession even during probate.

## What to do now
1. **Create a physical and digital log of every bill received.** **Record the sender’s name, account number, amount requested, date received, and the nature of the service provided.**
   - Include utility notices, medical invoices, credit card statements, and subscription renewals.
2. **Identify the legally authorized personal representative for the estate.** **Check the deceased’s will for a named executor or determine if a court has appointed an administrator or universal successor.**
   - If you are not the representative, stop all interaction with creditors and physically hand the bills to the authorized person.
3. **Notify the creditor of the death via certified mail with a return receipt.** **Include a copy of the death certificate and the contact information for the estate’s personal representative.**
   - Explicitly request that all future correspondence be directed to the estate.
4. **Demand a written "Validation Notice" from any debt collector who contacts you.** **Verify the debt's legitimacy by reviewing the itemized breakdown of charges, the original creditor's name, and the date of the last activity.**
   - Under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA), you have 30 days to dispute the debt in writing once this notice is received.
5. **Issue a written "Cease Communication" letter if you are being harassed.** **State clearly that you do not wish to be contacted further regarding the debt, which legally forces the collector to stop most types of contact.**
   - Keep a copy of this letter and the post office tracking number.
6. **Verify high-pressure demands through official company channels.** **Ignore any caller who refuses to provide a physical mailing address, threatens arrest, or demands payment via wire transfer or gift cards.**
   - Cues for illegitimate bills include misspelled names, vague descriptions of services ("administrative fees"), or dates of service that occurred after the death.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the priority of which debts get paid first in the first few weeks.
- You do not need to settle the final balance of medical or credit card bills until the probate process is officially opened.
- You do not need to close utility accounts immediately if someone is still residing in the home or if the property is being prepared for sale.

## Important reassurance
Receiving bills immediately after a death is a common result of automated billing cycles. It is normal to feel overwhelmed by these demands, but the law provides a structured process (probate) specifically designed to handle these claims. You are allowed to take the time needed to verify every charge before any money changes hands.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and harm reduction regarding incoming bills and debt collection. It does not cover the long-term legal process of probate, tax filings, or the final distribution of estate assets.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Debt liability varies significantly by state law, particularly in community property jurisdictions or states with "necessaries" statutes. Consult a probate attorney if you are unsure of your personal liability for a spouse's or relative's medical or marital debts.
