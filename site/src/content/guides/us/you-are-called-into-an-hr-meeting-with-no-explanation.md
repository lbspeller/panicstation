# What to do if… you are called into an HR meeting with no explanation (USA)
## Short answer
Ask what the meeting is about and whether it could lead to discipline. If you are union-represented, clearly ask for your union representative before investigatory questions continue; the rules that apply can depend on your workplace and coverage.

## Do not do these things
- Do not quit, agree to resign, or sign a separation document while you feel pressured or unclear about what is happening.
- Do not sign a statement, write-up, or agreement you have not read and understood.
- Do not guess, speculate, or blame other people to fill gaps in your memory.
- Do not agree immediately to access to a personal device or personal account while you feel cornered. Ask what access is being requested and whether you can take time to review the request.
- Before recording, check applicable law and workplace rules. Do not assume recording is permitted.
- Do not assume the meeting is either routine or a final decision. Ask what process is taking place.

## What to do now
1. **Take a brief pause before the meeting starts.** Note who called the meeting, when it was requested, and who is attending.
2. **Ask for the purpose of the meeting.** Say:
   - “What is the purpose of this meeting?”
   - “Is this an investigatory or fact-finding meeting?”
   - “Could this lead to discipline?”
3. **If you are union-represented, request representation clearly.** If you reasonably believe investigatory questions could lead to discipline, say: “I am requesting my union representative before we continue.” For employees covered by the NLRA, current NLRB guidance says the employer does not have to remind you to ask. After you ask, state that you do not want to continue investigatory questioning without your representative. Under that guidance, the employer may delay the interview until a representative is available, end the interview, or offer you the choice of continuing without a representative or ending the interview. Coverage and any additional representation rules can vary.
4. **If you are not union-represented, ask to slow the process down.** Say: “I want to cooperate, but I need to understand the concern. Can you explain the issue, put it in writing, or give me time to review anything you want me to respond to?” HR may decline, but asking helps clarify the process.
5. **Ask for the specific concern.** If HR refers to emails, messages, logs, complaints, or policies, ask which item or policy section they mean. Ask whether they can share it or give you time to review it. They may say no.
6. **Keep your answers narrow and factual.** Useful phrases include:
   - “I need the specific concern before I can answer accurately.”
   - “I do not recall. I would need to review my records.”
   - “I do not want to guess.”
7. **Pause before signing anything.** Ask what the document is, what your signature would mean, whether you can take time to read it, and whether you can receive a copy. Sign only if you understand what you are signing.
8. **Before leaving, ask what happens next.** Ask whether there is anything you need to provide, whether there is a deadline, and who to contact if you remember something relevant.
9. **After the meeting, make a factual note.** Record who attended, what was asked, what you answered, any documents shown, and any deadlines mentioned. Keep the note somewhere permitted and secure. Do not take or copy material you are not allowed to retain.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to file a complaint, seek legal advice, or raise the issue outside the workplace, unless you are told about a deadline or need urgent help.
- Writing a long response before you know the specific concern.
- Deciding what the meeting “must” mean.
- Making a final decision about your job while you are still processing the meeting.

## Important reassurance
An unexplained HR meeting can feel unsettling because the purpose is unclear. For now, focus on asking what process is taking place, answering carefully, pausing before signing anything, and keeping a factual record.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment you are called into a meeting. Later decisions may depend on your state, your employer’s policies, whether you are union-represented, whether the NLRA or other rules apply, and what HR says the meeting is about. Specialist help may be useful if the situation becomes more serious.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Laws and workplace rules can vary. If you think discrimination or retaliation may be involved, keep permitted records and consider qualified local advice.
