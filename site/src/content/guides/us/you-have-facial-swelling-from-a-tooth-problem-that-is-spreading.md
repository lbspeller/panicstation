# What to do if… you have facial swelling from a tooth problem that is spreading (USA)

## Short answer
Spreading facial swelling from a tooth problem needs urgent in-person assessment today. Call 911 now if breathing is difficult, you cannot swallow saliva, you are drooling because you cannot swallow, you feel confused, or you cannot get to emergency care safely. Go to the emergency department now if swelling is rapidly worsening or spreading under your jaw, into your neck, or into the floor of your mouth.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for spreading swelling to settle on its own.
- Do not try to puncture or drain swelling in your gum, mouth, or face yourself.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics or antibiotics prescribed for someone else.
- Do not place aspirin directly on your tooth or gums.
- Do not exceed label directions for pain medicines or take two products containing the same ingredient.
- Do not assume the problem has resolved if pain eases after fluid drains from the area.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 now** if breathing is difficult, you cannot swallow saliva, you are drooling because you cannot swallow, you feel confused, or you cannot get to emergency care safely.
2. **Go to the emergency department now** if swallowing is becoming difficult, swelling is rapidly worsening, or swelling is spreading under your jaw, into your neck, into the floor of your mouth, or toward your tongue. If you have fever with facial swelling and cannot reach a dentist, go to the emergency department.
3. **If none of those signs apply, contact a dentist for urgent assessment today.** Say: “I have facial swelling from a tooth problem and it seems to be spreading.” If you cannot get prompt dental assessment or you become more unwell, go to the emergency department.
4. **While arranging care, keep checking for change.** Notice whether swelling is spreading, swallowing is becoming harder, your voice sounds different, your tongue feels swollen, or you develop fever or confusion. If any of these develop or worsen, go to the emergency department. Call 911 for breathing difficulty, inability to swallow saliva, confusion, or unsafe travel.
5. **Use low-risk comfort measures only if they do not delay care.** Follow label directions for over-the-counter pain relief and avoid products you have been told not to take. A warm saltwater rinse may ease pain if you can swallow normally.
6. **Bring useful information.** Tell the clinician when the swelling started, how quickly it has changed, whether you have fever, drainage or a bad taste, any recent dental work or injury, your medicines and allergies, and whether you have diabetes, immune suppression, or pregnancy.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether you may need antibiotics, drainage, a root canal, or an extraction.
- You do not need to identify the exact tooth before seeking care.
- You do not need to finish work, travel, or other plans before arranging urgent assessment.

## Important reassurance
Facial swelling can feel alarming. Seeking urgent assessment is a sensible step, and a clinician can identify the safest next action.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later treatment decisions may need a dentist, emergency clinician, or another appropriate healthcare professional.
