# What to do if… you are asked to interview for a role that looks like your own job (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the request as a formal selection event for a Reduction in Force (RIF) or restructuring; request the full job description and evaluation criteria in writing before attending.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign immediately or in a state of frustration; doing so may forfeit your right to unemployment benefits or severance packages.
- Do not sign any "voluntary resignation" or "waiver of rights" documents during the initial meeting or interview.
- Do not assume the process is illegal "at-will" employment generally allows for restructuring, provided it does not violate anti-discrimination laws.
- Do not skip or boycott the interview; refusal to participate can be documented as a performance failure or job abandonment.
- Do not use company devices or email to discuss your frustration or organize with coworkers if you have an expectation of privacy.

## What to do now
1. **Request the formal purpose of the interview in writing.** Send a neutral email to HR or the hiring manager asking if this is a "competitive selection process for existing staff" or "hiring for a newly created position."
2. **Demand the specific job description and scoring rubric.** **Ask for the criteria by which candidates will be ranked.** 
   - Request the salary range or hourly wage, especially if you are in a state with active pay transparency laws (e.g., CA, NY, WA, NJ).
   - Ask for a list of the interviewers and their roles in the decision-making process.
3. **Secure copies of your current performance records and job description.** **Download or print your most recent performance reviews and your current official duties list to a personal device.** 
   - Having these records allows you to demonstrate that the "new" role's requirements match the work you are already successfully performing.
4. **Clarify if this is a "qualifying" event for a layoff.** **Ask if employees who are not selected for the "new" role will be subject to a Reduction in Force (RIF).** 
   - If the employer has 100 or more employees and a mass layoff is triggered, ask if a federal or state WARN Act notice has been or will be issued.
5. **Request reasonable accommodations if a medical condition affects your performance.** **Notify HR immediately if you require changes to the interview format or timing due to a documented disability.** 
   - You do not need to disclose your full diagnosis, only the functional limitation and the needed accommodation.
6. **Maintain a private log of all communications.** **Record dates, times, participants, and summaries of every conversation regarding the restructure.** 
   - Store this log on a personal, non-company device. 
   - Include any "sensory" observations, such as being the only person in your age group or department asked to re-interview.
7. **Discuss working conditions only with coworkers you trust.** **Exercise your Section 7 rights under the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) to discuss wages and conditions with peers.** 
   - Be aware that as of 2026, NLRB protections for supervisors and managers remain more limited than for non-supervisory staff.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to accept a "demotion" or lower-paying role during the interview.
- You do not need to hire an attorney before the first interview unless you are being asked to sign a complex legal waiver.
- You do not need to "prove" the process is unfair or biased in the moment; focus on performing for the record.
- You do not need to update your external resume immediately, though gathering your data is a priority.

## Important reassurance
Sudden changes to job security and organizational structure often cause intense stress or a sense of confusion. This reaction is a normal response to an unexpected threat to your livelihood. By slowing down and requesting the process in writing, you are taking the most protective step possible to preserve your options.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an internal employment crisis. It does not cover long-term litigation strategy, unemployment insurance applications, or detailed severance negotiations.

## Important note
Employment law is highly dependent on state-specific statutes and individual contract terms. As of 2026, federal enforcement focuses heavily on intentional "disparate treatment" rather than "disparate impact." If you suspect you are being targeted due to age (over 40), race, sex, disability, or another protected characteristic, consult a qualified employment attorney in your jurisdiction.
