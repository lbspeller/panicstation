# What to do if… you are asked to interview for a role that looks like your own job (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before making any major decision. Ask in writing what process is underway and request the job description, interview purpose, and any stated deadline.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign in anger or assume that you already know the outcome.
- Do not sign a resignation, severance agreement, release, or document changing your employment terms because you feel rushed. Ask for a copy and time to review it.
- Do not assume the process is lawful or unlawful based only on how it feels. Record the facts first.
- Do not post about the situation online while you are still trying to understand the process.
- Do not ignore the invitation. Ask what response is required and note any stated deadline.

## What to do now
1. **Ask what the interview is for.** Reply in writing: “Can you confirm whether this is for a newly created role, part of a reorganization or reduction-in-force process, or a competitive process for existing positions? Please also share the job description, evaluation criteria, and any response deadline.”
2. **Request the practical details.** Ask for the posting, required qualifications, reporting line, pay range or band if available, interview format, interviewers, and how the decision will be made.
3. **Keep a factual timeline.** Record dates, invitations, messages, and what you were told. Keep only records you are allowed to retain, and do not remove confidential company material.
4. **Prepare a one-page summary of the work you already do.** List your responsibilities, current projects, systems you maintain, and recent results. Use specific examples that connect your existing work to the posted role.
5. **Ask early about an interview-process accommodation if you need one.** An accommodation may be available if a disability or medical condition affects the interview process. Contact HR or the person handling the interview, state the adjustment you need, and explain that it relates to a medical condition or disability. The employer may ask follow-up questions or request reasonable documentation if the disability and need are not obvious.
6. **Keep coworker conversations factual.** Covered employees may have protections when discussing wages or acting together about workplace conditions. Coverage varies, and some workers, including supervisors and some other categories, may not be covered. Check before sharing internal material that you may not be allowed to disclose.
7. **Ask whether a formal layoff notice process applies.** If the employer says this is part of a larger layoff or closure, ask: “Does a federal WARN notice process or any applicable state notice process apply, and what timeline should employees plan around?” Federal WARN applies only to qualifying situations, and state rules may differ.
8. **Write down specific concerns about possible discrimination.** Record dates, statements, and concrete examples. You do not need to argue about them during the interview invitation stage. If you may need to act, check the applicable deadline promptly.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your whole career plan today.
- You do not need to decide whether the role is acceptable before you receive the job description and process details.
- You do not need to make a formal complaint before you understand the facts.
- You do not need to solve every later legal or negotiation question now. Note any written deadline and seek advice promptly if a document, deadline, or urgent concern arrives.

## Important reassurance
An invitation to interview for a role that resembles your current job can feel unsettling. Asking for the process in writing, keeping a factual record, and preparing examples of your current work gives you a steady first step without forcing an immediate decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on the employer’s explanation, the documents you receive, your state, and whether the process involves a reorganization, a reduction in force, a change in terms, or a possible discrimination concern. Specialist help may be useful later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employment rules and deadlines can vary by state, employer, worker status, and the facts of the situation.
