# What to do if… you get an alert that a new passkey was added to an account you use (USA)

## Short answer
Assume your current password and recovery methods are compromised; go directly to your account security settings (not via the alert link) and remove the unauthorized passkey and all other active sessions immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click "secure your account" or "review activity" links inside the alert email or text—attackers often send fake alerts to steal your remaining credentials.
- Don’t ignore the alert because you think passkeys are "unhackable"—the alert means the authentication system has already been bypassed or altered.
- Don’t keep using the account for sensitive communications or payments until you have confirmed you are the only one with access.
- Don’t attempt to "test" the new passkey to see if it works.
- Don’t delete the notification—you may need the timestamp and device info for a formal report later.

## What to do now
1. **Access the account through a trusted, independent path.**  
   **Open a fresh browser tab and manually type the website address or use the official mobile app to log in.**
   - Navigate to the **Security**, **Sign-in**, or **Privacy** tab.
   - Look for **Passkeys**, **Security Keys**, or **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** settings.
2. **Remove the unauthorized passkey immediately.**  
   **Find the list of registered passkeys and delete any entry you do not personally recognize or that was added at the time of the alert.**
   - If the provider has a "Security Delay" (common in 2026 for sensitive changes), cancel any pending passkey additions that are in a waiting period.
3. **Terminate all other active sessions and "trusted" devices.**  
   **Use the "Sign out of all other sessions," "Log out everywhere," or "Remove all trusted devices" command.**
   - This forces the attacker's device to re-authenticate, which they cannot do if you have removed their passkey.
4. **Audit and reset your recovery information.**  
   **Check every recovery phone number, backup email address, and physical recovery code for changes you did not make.**
   - Remove any unknown email addresses or phone numbers.
   - Generate new "Backup Codes" or "Recovery Keys" if the service provides them; this invalidates the old ones.
5. **Change your account password (if applicable).**  
   **If the account still uses a password as a fallback, create a new, complex password that you have never used elsewhere.**
6. **Check for "Persistence" settings.**  
   **Look for settings that allow an attacker to maintain access even after a password change.**
   - Check for **Email Forwarding** rules (to hide future security alerts).
   - Check for **Third-Party App Access** or **Linked Accounts** (like Facebook or Google) that you did not authorize.
7. **Secure your primary email account.**  
   **If the compromised account was linked to your email for password resets, you must repeat these steps on your email account immediately.**
8. **Report the incident to the provider.**  
   **Use the official "Report Unauthorized Access" or "Account Recovery" portal if you are unable to remove the passkey or if you find you are locked out.**

## What can wait
- You don’t need to determine how the compromise happened (e.g., malware vs. phishing) in the first few minutes.
- You don’t need to notify all your contacts unless you see evidence that messages are being sent in your name.
- You don’t need to replace your hardware unless you see clear evidence of a local system infection.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a security alert is a sign that the system’s primary defense worked. By acting within minutes of the notification, you are likely cutting off the attacker before they can finalize a full account takeover or lock you out of your own recovery options.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization when you still have some level of account access. If you are entirely locked out and your recovery methods have been changed, you must move directly to the service provider's official "Account Recovery" or "Identity Verification" process.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and does not constitute legal or technical advice. If the account involves financial services, government benefits, or sensitive personal data, you should also monitor your credit and report the incident. In the USA, you can report identity theft at IdentityTheft.gov and significant cyber incidents or financial fraud to the FBI at IC3.gov.
