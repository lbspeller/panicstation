# What to do if… you learn a friend is being pressured for sexual photos and they are afraid to tell anyone (USA)

## Short answer

Treat this as sexual coercion and help your friend reach one safe adult or specialist support service now. The immediate goal is not to solve everything yourself, but to stop them being alone with it.

## Do not do these things

- Do not blame them for chatting, flirting, trusting someone, or sending anything before.
- Do not pressure them to report to police right away unless there is immediate danger.
- Do not message, threaten, bargain with, or expose the person pressuring them.
- Do not ask to see, receive, forward, save, or describe any sexual images.
- Do not tell them to send another image, money, apology, or explanation to make the pressure stop.
- Do not promise total secrecy if they are a minor, in immediate danger, or another child may be at risk.
- Do not tell them to delete everything immediately.
- Do not post about it or contact other people involved.

## What to do now

1. **Check immediate safety first.**  
   Ask: “Are you safe right now?” and “Are you alone with this person or afraid they will come to you?” If there is immediate danger, call **911**.

2. **Help them stop engaging for the moment.**  
   They do not need to answer threats, explain themselves, send more photos, send money, or prove anything. Sit with them while they put the chat down, silence notifications, or move away from the device for a few minutes.

3. **Help them tell one trusted person today.**  
   Keep the choice small: a parent, caregiver, trusted relative, school counselor, trusted teacher, school administrator, therapist, campus counselor, residence-life staff member, or another adult they already trust. If speaking feels impossible, help them send: “Something sexual online is happening and I’m scared. I need help, but I can’t explain it well yet.”

4. **Use specialist support instead of handling it person-to-person.**  
   If they want confidential support around sexual abuse or coercion, help them contact **RAINN**. RAINN offers free, confidential, 24/7 support by phone, online chat, and text. You can stay with them while they make first contact.

5. **If your friend is under 18, involve a trusted adult the same day.**  
   A trusted adult can help decide whether to contact the **CyberTipline**, which is the U.S. reporting route for suspected online child sexual exploitation. **Take It Down** can help with nude, partially nude, or sexually explicit images or videos taken before someone turned 18. Do not download, send, or handle your friend’s images to try to help.

6. **Reduce immediate exposure only if it feels safe.**  
   Help them turn off message previews, mute notifications, make the account private, or block and report the account if they feel safe doing that. If they are terrified that a platform action will make the person angrier, focus first on getting support from a trusted adult or specialist service.

7. **Keep only simple information without investigating.**  
   If messages already exist, do not delete, forward, or reply to them for now. A simple note of usernames, platform names, dates, and what was threatened is enough until a trusted adult or specialist service can help.

8. **If an intimate image has already been shared without consent, use official removal routes.**  
   For adults, platform reporting is often the first practical step. The **FTC** has current guidance on image-based abuse and what to do if a platform does not respond to a removal request. **StopNCII.org** may help adults create a private case to reduce sharing of intimate images on participating platforms.

9. **Stay with them through the first handoff.**  
   Do not leave them with a list and walk away. The useful action here is being physically or virtually present while they tell one person or contact one service.

## What can wait

They do not need to decide right now whether to make a police report, explain every detail, save every possible piece of information, confront the person, change schools, change accounts, or tell a wider group of people. Bigger relationship, school, family, and legal decisions can wait until they have support.

## Important reassurance

People who are being threatened or sexually pressured often feel trapped, embarrassed, or convinced they will make things worse by telling someone. That reaction is common. Calm support, belief, and one safe handoff can make the situation safer without forcing them into decisions they are not ready to make.

## Scope note

These are first steps only. Later decisions about reporting, school action, image removal, safety planning, or ongoing support may need specialist help.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. In highly sensitive situations like this, it is usually safer to involve a trusted adult or specialist service early rather than trying to manage the pressure privately.
