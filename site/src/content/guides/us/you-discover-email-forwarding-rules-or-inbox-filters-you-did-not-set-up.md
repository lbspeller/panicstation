# What to do if… you discover email forwarding rules or inbox filters you did not set up (USA)

## Short answer
Treat unknown forwarding rules as a possible email account takeover: remove the rules, secure the account from your provider’s official site or app, then check the accounts that depend on that inbox.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume it is only a harmless setting; forwarding rules can expose password resets, banking alerts, invoices, and personal mail.
- Don’t click links in emails or texts that claim to fix the account; go to the provider directly.
- Don’t reuse the old password, or any password you have used on another account.
- Don’t stop at deleting the rule if it comes back; that may mean someone still has access.
- Don’t ignore recovery details, connected apps, app passwords, or signed-in devices.

## What to do now
1. **Use a clean route to the account.** Open the email provider’s official website or app directly. Use a device you trust if you can, especially if the current device is showing pop-ups, strange extensions, or other signs of compromise.
2. **Remove the suspicious forwarding rule.** Delete any unknown forwarding address, auto-forwarding setting, inbox rule, or filter that sends mail away from your account.
3. **Look for hiding rules too.** Remove unknown rules that auto-delete, archive, mark as read, or move messages, especially rules involving words like “security,” “password,” “bank,” “invoice,” “wire,” “payroll,” or “gift card.”
4. **Sign out other sessions and remove unknown access.** Use the provider’s security settings to sign out of other devices where possible. Remove unfamiliar devices, connected apps, third-party access, and app-specific passwords.
5. **Change the password and turn on MFA.** Use a new, unique password. Turn on multi-factor authentication if it is available, then save backup codes somewhere you can access later.
6. **Check recovery settings.** Confirm the recovery email address and phone number are yours. Remove anything unfamiliar before relying on the account again.
7. **Check for misuse.** Review sent mail, deleted mail, archived mail, and security notifications. If scam messages were sent from your account, warn affected contacts briefly not to click or respond.
8. **Protect accounts tied to this inbox.** Start with banking, payment apps, payroll, shopping accounts, cloud storage, and any account where this email is the login or recovery address. Change passwords anywhere you reused the old email password.
9. **Act quickly if money, work, or identity may be involved.** Contact your bank or payment provider if any payment, wire, payroll, card, or account change may be affected. If this is a work or school account, contact IT or security support. If identity theft or cyber-enabled fraud may be involved, use the official federal resources below.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove exactly how the attacker got in before you stop the forwarding and secure access.
- You do not need to make a complete list of every affected account before securing the most sensitive ones first.
- You do not need to decide now whether this was identity theft, business email compromise, or another scam label.
- A deeper device check, provider support follow-up, and a full password review can wait until the account is stable.

## Important reassurance
Finding an unknown forwarding rule is alarming, but the first steps are concrete: stop the forwarding, remove outside access, secure recovery settings, and protect the accounts that use this inbox. The aim right now is to stop further access and reduce follow-on damage.

## Scope note
This guide covers first steps only. If the rule keeps returning, you cannot regain control, money has moved, or identity theft may be happening, you may need help from the email provider, your bank, your workplace or school, and official reporting channels.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, professional cybersecurity, or other professional advice. Use official provider support and official federal resources, and seek qualified help promptly if there is financial loss, identity theft, or ongoing unauthorized access.
