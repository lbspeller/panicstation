# What to do if… the only lift or elevator you need is out of service and you cannot reach the platform or exit (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a safe, visible area and immediately contact transit staff via a Help Point, intercom, or phone to request an ADA-mandated accessible alternative to your destination or the exit.

## Do not do these things
- Do not enter onto the tracks or into restricted "Employees Only" areas to seek an alternate path.
- Do not attempt to use stairs or escalators if doing so poses a risk to your physical safety, balance, or equipment.
- Do not attempt to manually force elevator doors or interact with mechanical equipment.
- Do not leave the station to "find another entrance" until staff confirm that the second entrance is fully accessible and that you can re-enter.
- Do not attempt to carry heavy mobility devices or strollers up stairs if you are alone or feel unstable.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a visible, well-lit area away from platform edges, moving escalators, or heavy foot traffic.** This ensures you are safe from immediate hazards while you wait for assistance or a response from staff.
2. **Contact the transit agency immediately using the most direct communication tool available.**
   - Press the "Help" or "Information" button on the station intercom/Help Point.
   - Speak to the station agent or booth attendant if the station is staffed.
   - Call the transit agency’s customer service or emergency operations line found on their mobile app or station signage.
3. **State clearly that you are requesting an ADA accommodation because the elevator is out of service.** Use specific phrasing such as: "The only elevator to the [platform / exit] is out of service and I cannot safely use stairs. I am requesting an accessible way to complete my trip or exit the station."
4. **Identify which specific accommodation you require based on your location.**
   - Ask for an alternative accessible route within the station (e.g., a service elevator or staff-guided path).
   - If you are trapped behind fare gates, request that staff open an accessible gate and escort you to a street-level exit.
   - If the headway to the next accessible train or bus is more than 30 minutes, request a paratransit vehicle or accessible shuttle bus (bus bridge) to the nearest accessible station.
5. **Inform the train conductor or operator immediately if you are on a train and cannot disembark at your stop.** 
   - Use the on-car emergency intercom to tell the operator you require an elevator that is currently out of service.
   - Ask the operator to coordinate with dispatch for a backtrack plan or an accessible transfer at the next available station.
6. **Ask for the estimated time of arrival (ETA) for any requested shuttle or alternative transportation.** Under Federal Transit Administration (FTA) guidelines, agencies should provide alternative accessible service within a short response time (ideally 30 minutes or less) if the primary route is blocked.
7. **Contact 911 only if you are in immediate physical distress or the environment becomes unsafe.** This includes experiencing medical symptoms like dizziness or heat exhaustion, or if you are stranded in an unstaffed area with no way to communicate with the agency.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine how to file a formal ADA complaint or request a fare refund while you are still stranded.
- You do not need to figure out your own detour through local streets; the agency is responsible for providing the accessible connection.
- You do not need to worry about being "in the way" for requesting help—accessibility features are a legal requirement for transit operations.

## Important reassurance
Encountering a broken elevator when you rely on step-free access is a failure of the transit infrastructure, not a failure of your planning. It is normal to feel frustrated or anxious when your mobility is restricted; transit agencies have specific protocols to handle these outages and provide alternative transportation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for individuals stranded by elevator outages in US transit stations. It covers immediate communication and safety but does not cover long-term legal remedies or non-emergency maintenance reporting.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice regarding the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) or medical advice for physical distress. Availability of shuttles and staffing levels vary significantly between transit agencies (e.g., MTA, CTA, WMATA, BART). If you feel medically unsafe or are in a life-threatening situation, contact emergency services immediately.
