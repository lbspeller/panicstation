# What to do if… you have a painful, rapidly worsening sore throat and trouble swallowing (USA)

## Short answer
If you have trouble breathing, drooling, a muffled or changed voice, noisy breathing, or you cannot swallow your saliva, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency department now. If it is rapidly worsening but you can still breathe and swallow saliva, get same-day urgent medical evaluation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sleep it off if the pain is severe, worsening quickly, or making swallowing hard.
- Do not drive yourself if you have trouble breathing, drooling, weakness, confusion, or feel like you may pass out.
- Do not lie flat if it makes breathing or swallowing harder.
- Do not force food, pills, or large amounts of liquid down if you are gagging, choking, or cannot swallow safely.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics or someone else’s prescription medicine.
- Do not smoke or vape while your throat feels swollen, tight, or painful.
- Do not keep poking, scraping, or trying to look deep into your throat if it triggers gagging or distress.

## What to do now
1. Do an emergency check now. Call 911 or go to the emergency department if you have any of these:
   - Trouble breathing, noisy breathing, high-pitched breathing, or worsening shortness of breath.
   - Drooling or spitting out saliva because swallowing is too hard.
   - Unable to swallow saliva.
   - A suddenly muffled, hoarse, or odd voice.
   - Blue, gray, or very pale lips, face, or skin; severe confusion; or feeling like you may pass out.
   - Fast worsening throat swelling, neck swelling, or severe trouble opening your mouth.

2. If there are no 911 warning signs but the sore throat is rapidly worsening with trouble swallowing, get seen today.
   - Choose an emergency department if symptoms are progressing, you feel unsafe waiting, or you are unsure where to go.
   - Use urgent care only if you are clearly breathing normally, can swallow saliva, and they can assess you promptly.
   - If you worsen at any point, switch to 911 or the emergency department.

3. Keep your airway and swallowing as easy as possible while arranging care.
   - Sit upright.
   - Lean slightly forward if that makes breathing or swallowing easier.
   - Stay calm and avoid unnecessary talking if talking makes symptoms worse.
   - Avoid solid food for now.
   - If you can swallow safely, take small sips of water.

4. Use basic pain or fever relief only if you can swallow safely and it is normally safe for you.
   - Acetaminophen or ibuprofen may help; follow the label and avoid medicines you have been told not to take.
   - Use a liquid form only if you already have it and can swallow it safely.

5. Prepare for the visit without delaying care.
   - Write down when symptoms started and how quickly they are worsening.
   - Note fever, voice change, drooling, neck swelling, rash, one-sided throat pain, and trouble opening your mouth.
   - Bring your medication list, allergies, major medical conditions, and insurance information if you have it.
   - Ask someone to stay with you or check in by phone until you are being assessed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to decide whether you need antibiotics before you are examined.
- You do not need to keep checking your throat or comparing it with pictures online.
- Work, errands, messages, and non-urgent decisions can wait until you have been assessed.

## Important reassurance
Many sore throats are not dangerous, but rapid worsening with trouble swallowing is a valid reason to act quickly. Getting checked is a sensible safety step, especially when breathing, saliva swallowing, voice, or neck swelling is involved.

## Scope note
This guide is first steps only to reduce immediate risk and get you to appropriate care. Later decisions may depend on an exam, testing, treatment options, and specialist medical advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, legal advice, financial advice, therapeutic advice, or other professional advice. If you develop breathing difficulty, drooling, inability to swallow saliva, noisy or high-pitched breathing, a muffled voice, severe weakness, confusion, or rapid deterioration, treat it as an emergency and call 911.
