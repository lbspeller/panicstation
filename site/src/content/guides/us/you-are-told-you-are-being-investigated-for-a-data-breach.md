# What to do if… you are told you are being investigated for a data breach (USA)

## Short answer
Request a written notice of the change and immediately contact your state unemployment agency to check eligibility for partial unemployment benefits or a Work Sharing (Short-Time Compensation) program.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign or quit immediately, as this usually disqualifies you from receiving unemployment benefits for the lost hours.
- Do not sign any document stating the reduction was "voluntary" or "at your request" unless that is factually true.
- Do not agree to an "under the table" or verbal-only pay arrangement that is not reflected in your official payroll records.
- Do not assume your health insurance, 401(k) eligibility, or PTO accrual will remain unchanged if your hours drop below "full-time" thresholds.
- Do not ignore your pay stubs; verify that you are being paid for every minute worked, including any "transition time" or meetings.

## What to do now
1. **Request a formal written notice of the reduction in hours from your manager or HR department.** **This notice should include the effective date, the specific number of hours or shifts being cut, and whether the change affects your hourly rate or salary.**
   - Ask if there is a specific date set for a status review.
   - Ask if your benefit eligibility (health, dental, life insurance) is impacted by the new hour count.
2. **Apply for partial unemployment benefits through your state’s department of labor or unemployment agency immediately.** **Many states provide "partial UI" to workers whose hours are reduced through no fault of their own, provided they meet minimum earnings requirements.**
   - Be prepared to provide your "base period" earnings (typically the last 12-18 months of pay).
   - Report your gross earnings (before taxes) for each week you work reduced hours.
3. **Ask HR if the company is participating in a state-approved Work Sharing or Short-Time Compensation (STC) program.** **These programs allow employers to reduce workweeks by 10% to 60% while employees receive a pro-rated percentage of their unemployment benefits to offset the loss.**
   - Note that STC programs are employer-initiated; you cannot apply for STC individually if your employer has not set up a plan.
4. **Clarify your status if you are a salaried / exempt employee.** **Under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), exempt employees must generally receive their full weekly salary for any week in which they perform work, regardless of the number of hours.**
   - Note that an employer can prospectively reduce your total salary for future workweeks due to a business slowdown, but they cannot make deductions for partial-day absences or "quality of work" issues.
   - Verify if your new salary still meets the federal threshold ($684/week) or your state's 2026 threshold (e.g., California, New York, or Washington).
5. **Begin a detailed log of all communications regarding your schedule and pay.** **Save copies of schedules, emails, text messages, and pay stubs that show the transition from full-time to reduced hours.**
   - Record the names of supervisors who informed you of the changes and the reasons provided (e.g., "lack of work" or "seasonal slowdown").
6. **Review your employee handbook or union contract for "reduction in force" or "seniority" clauses.** **If you are a union member, contact your shop steward immediately to ensure the reduction follows the collective bargaining agreement.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now if you are going to look for a different job.
- You do not need to file a legal claim or complaint today; focusing on securing partial income is the priority.
- You do not need to address "what if" scenarios for six months from now; focus on the next two pay periods.

## Important reassurance
Receiving notice of reduced hours is a significant disruption, but it is a common procedural step businesses take to avoid total layoffs. Most states have established partial unemployment systems specifically designed to bridge the gap in your income while you remain employed. Taking immediate steps to document the change and file for benefits puts you in control of your financial stabilization.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a reduction in work hours. It does not cover total layoffs, wrongful termination, or legal strategy for wage theft. Later steps may require consultation with a state labor agency or an employment attorney.

## Important note
Employment laws in the United States vary significantly by state. Eligibility for unemployment benefits depends on your specific earnings history and the reason for the reduction. This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. If you suspect your hours were reduced due to discrimination (race, age, sex, disability, etc.) or in retaliation for whistleblowing, contact the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) or your state’s fair employment agency.
