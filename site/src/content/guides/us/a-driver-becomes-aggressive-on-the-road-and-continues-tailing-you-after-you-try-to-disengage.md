# What to do if… a driver becomes aggressive on the road and continues tailing you after you try to disengage (USA)

## Short answer
Do not drive home; instead, call 911 immediately and drive to the nearest police station, fire station, or busy public hospital while remaining inside your locked vehicle.

## Do not do these things
- Don't drive to your home, workplace, or a friend's house.
- Don't pull over in a dark, quiet, or isolated area.
- Don't exit your vehicle to confront or talk to the other driver.
- Don't use your brakes to "check" the tailgater or engage in high-speed racing.
- Don't make eye contact, return gestures, or yell back at the aggressor.
- Don't stop your car in a way that pins you in or prevents you from driving away.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm you are being followed without escalating.** If you are unsure if the pursuit is intentional, make four consecutive right turns (driving in a large circle) or take three turns that would not be on a normal route.
2. **Call 911 or #77 (Highway Patrol) immediately.** Tell the dispatcher you are being followed by an aggressive driver and provide your current street, direction of travel, and the nearest cross street or landmark.
   - Describe the other vehicle (color, make, model).
   - Provide the license plate number only if you can read it safely without looking away from the road.
3. **Head directly to a secure public location.** Use your GPS to find the nearest police station, fire station, or hospital emergency room entrance.
   - If these are unavailable, go to a 24-hour gas station or a crowded shopping center with visible security cameras.
4. **Keep your vehicle in motion until you reach help.** Avoid stopping completely if the other driver is right behind you, unless at a police station. 
   - If you are forced to stop at a red light and the aggressor exits their vehicle to approach you, check for cross-traffic and safely drive through the light or around obstacles to create distance.
5. **Park in a way that allows for an immediate exit.** When you arrive at a safe location, do not pull into a space that traps you. Leave enough room in front of your bumper to pull out and drive away if the aggressor approaches.
6. **Remain inside your locked vehicle.** Even if the other driver stops and approaches your window, do not roll it down or unlock the doors. Keep your engine running.
7. **Attract attention if the threat becomes imminent.** If the driver attempts to enter your vehicle or damage it while you are stopped in a public place, use your horn in short, repeated blasts to alert bystanders and security.

## What can wait
- You don't need to determine why the driver is angry or explain your own driving actions.
- You don't need to capture video or photos if it interferes with your ability to drive safely.
- You don't need to file a formal police report until you are in a secure, controlled environment.
- You don't need to assess damage to your vehicle until the aggressor is no longer present.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a surge of adrenaline, shaking, or a rapid heartbeat when being followed. This is a physiological response to a threat. Focusing on the physical act of driving to a police station is a concrete way to manage this reaction. You are doing the right thing by refusing to engage and seeking professional assistance.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active pursuit on the road. It does not cover long-term legal actions, insurance claims, or self-defense laws.

## Important note
If you are in immediate danger and cannot reach a police station, stay on the line with 911 dispatchers and follow their specific instructions for your current location. This guide is for informational purposes and does not replace the direction of emergency services.
