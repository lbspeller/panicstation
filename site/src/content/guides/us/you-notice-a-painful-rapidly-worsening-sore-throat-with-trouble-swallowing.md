# What to do if… you notice a painful, rapidly worsening sore throat with trouble swallowing (USA)

## Short answer
If you have **any trouble breathing, involuntary drooling, a muffled voice, or an inability to swallow saliva**, call **911** or go to the nearest Emergency Room immediately. These symptoms can indicate a rapidly closing airway which requires professional intervention.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "sleep it off" if you have any difficulty catching your breath or swallowing your own spit.
- Do not lie flat if you feel any respiratory distress; stay upright to help keep the airway open.
- Do not use a tongue depressor, spoon, or your fingers to "check" the back of your throat, as this can trigger a fatal airway spasm.
- Do not try to force down food or liquids if you are struggling to swallow; this increases the risk of choking or aspiration.
- Do not take oral medications (pills or liquids) if you are drooling or unable to swallow easily.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel lightheaded, are struggling to breathe, or feel your condition is deteriorating by the minute.

## What to do now
1. **Assess yourself immediately for "Red Flag" symptoms that require 911 or an emergency room visit.**
   - **Difficulty breathing**, including short breaths, gasping, or a high-pitched whistling sound when inhaling (stridor).
   - **Involuntary drooling** or needing to spit out saliva because it is too painful or physically impossible to swallow.
   - **A muffled "hot potato" voice**, where speech sounds thick or as if you are holding something in your mouth.
   - **Trismus**, which is a significant difficulty or inability to open your mouth fully.
   - **Visible swelling** on one side of the throat or neck that is pushing your tonsil or uvula toward the center.
2. **Position yourself to maximize your airway clearance while waiting for help or transport.**
   - **Sit bolt upright** and lean slightly forward (the "tripod position") if that makes breathing easier.
   - Avoid leaning back or lying down, which can allow gravity to worsen airway obstruction from swollen tissues.
3. **Notify emergency dispatch or hospital staff specifically about your "swallowing and breathing" status.**
   - If calling 911, clearly state: "I have a rapidly worsening sore throat and I am having trouble breathing and swallowing my saliva."
   - This ensures you are triaged for an immediate airway assessment rather than a standard sore throat wait.
4. **Remain "NPO" (nothing by mouth) until you have been evaluated by a clinician.**
   - **Do not drink water**, eat, or take throat lozenges if you have emergency signs.
   - If you are stable and only have pain without swallowing/breathing difficulty, you may take small sips of water, but stop immediately if the pain prevents a clear swallow.
5. **Gather minimal essential information for the medical team if it does not delay your departure.**
   - Note the exact time your symptoms began to worsen rapidly.
   - Identify any recent exposures to strep throat or if you have a history of peritonsillar abscesses.
   - Have a list of your allergies, especially to antibiotics like penicillin.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if this is "just a cold" or "strep" before seeking help.
- You do not need to wait for a primary care appointment if you have any of the 4 Ds (Distress, Drooling, Dysphagia, Dysphonia).
- You do not need to worry about the cost of an ER visit if your airway is at risk; stabilization is the legal priority.
- You do not need to pack a bag or finish household tasks; move toward care immediately.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant anxiety when it is hard to swallow or breathe. This physical sensation often triggers a "fight or flight" response. Focus on sitting upright and getting to a medical professional who can safely examine your throat with the correct equipment.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and emergency recognition only. It does not replace clinical diagnosis or treatment for conditions like epiglottitis or peritonsillar abscess.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not medical advice. If you are experiencing a medical emergency, including signs of airway obstruction or severe respiratory distress, call 911 immediately.
