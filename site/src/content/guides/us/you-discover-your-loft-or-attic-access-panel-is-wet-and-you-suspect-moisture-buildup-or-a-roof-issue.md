# What to do if… you discover your loft or attic access panel is wet and you suspect moisture buildup or a roof issue (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the wet panel as a combined electrical and structural hazard: stay clear of wet surfaces, isolate power to the area only if it is safe to do so, and arrange a professional inspection.

## Do not do these things
- Do not touch wet light fixtures, switches, or outlets near the damp area.
- Do not enter the attic if the floor is wet, the lighting is poor, or you must step off stable walkways.
- Do not attempt to move or handle water-logged insulation, as it is extremely heavy and can cause ceiling collapse.
- Do not operate electrical breakers if you are standing on a damp floor or if the panel itself shows signs of moisture.
- Do not climb onto the roof to investigate during or immediately after rain.
- Do not use a standard vacuum to "suck up" water near the hatch unless it is specifically a wet-dry vacuum and you are plugged into a dry, distant outlet.

## What to do now
1. **Clear the area below the wet hatch immediately.**  
   - Move pets, children, and furniture away from the ceiling area where water is present.  
   - If the ceiling appears to be sagging or bulging, evacuate that room entirely and close the door.
2. **Isolate power to the affected ceiling area if safe.**  
   - **Locate your electrical panel only if you can reach it via a dry path.**  
   - **Switch off the specific breaker labeled for the room or ceiling lights where the moisture is located.**  
   - **Avoid touching the main breaker if you are unsure of the circuit, unless there is active sparking or a smell of burning.**
3. **Set up passive water containment.**  
   - Place a bucket or plastic bin directly under any drips to prevent floor damage.  
   - Use towels to catch splashes, ensuring they do not come into contact with any electrical cords or baseboard heaters.
4. **Perform a visual-only check from the floor.**  
   - Use a high-powered flashlight to look at the hatch seams and surrounding drywall.  
   - Look for secondary signs like water tracking along a ceiling joist or moisture around an HVAC vent.  
   - Do not open the hatch if water is actively pooling on top of it, as the weight may cause the panel to fall.
5. **Document the current conditions for insurance or repairs.**  
   - Take clear photos of the wet access panel, the extent of the water staining, and any active drips.  
   - Note if the moisture appeared after a specific event, such as heavy snow, wind-driven rain, or a sudden change in outdoor temperature.
6. **Contact a licensed professional for an inspection.**  
   - **Homeowners:** Call a licensed roofing contractor or an HVAC technician if you have a furnace or AC unit in the attic.  
   - **Renters:** Notify your landlord or property manager immediately in writing, using the phrase "active water intrusion detected at attic access."  
   - **Electrical safety:** If water has entered a light fixture, contact a licensed electrician before restoring power.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact source (e.g., "roof leak vs. condensation") before professional help arrives.
- Permanent repairs to drywall, insulation replacement, and mold remediation should wait until the primary leak or moisture source is confirmed and stopped.
- Filing a formal insurance claim can usually wait until you have a contractor's preliminary assessment, unless your policy requires immediate "first notice of loss."

## Important reassurance
Finding moisture at an attic hatch is a common occurrence because it is often the coldest point where warm house air meets cold attic air. Taking these immediate safety steps prevents a manageable maintenance issue from escalating into a significant electrical or structural problem.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Resolving attic moisture typically requires specialized tools (moisture meters, thermal cameras) and professional knowledge of building envelopes or mechanical systems.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and is not a substitute for professional electrical, structural, or roofing advice. If you see smoke, smell burning plastic, or observe electrical arcing, evacuate the building and call **911** immediately.
