# What to do if… you are asked to send passport or ID scans over an insecure channel urgently (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before sending anything. Independently verify who is asking and move the request to the employer’s approved secure process.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send passport or ID images to a personal email address, unknown phone number, or unverified link.
- Do not use contact details from the urgent message as your only way to verify the request.
- Do not send extra documents, such as a Social Security card, just in case.
- Do not assume a message is genuine because it uses a company name, logo, or familiar wording.
- Do not alter an ID image for a document-verification process unless verified instructions tell you to do so.
- Do not let a same-day deadline push you into using an unapproved workaround.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the requester independently.** Use the employer’s official website, official staff directory, or main company number. Ask to be connected to HR, onboarding, or security. If a recruiter or outside vendor contacted you, confirm the request with the employer directly.
2. **Ask what the documents are for.** Ask whether the request relates to Form I-9, a background check, building access, or another named process.
3. **If it is for Form I-9, keep your document choice open.** For Form I-9, an employer cannot specify which acceptable document or combination of documents you must present. Ask for the official instructions and choose from the acceptable options.
4. **Ask for the approved secure method.** Request the employer’s secure HR portal, an approved document-verification process, or physical document examination. Ask for a short extension if the secure option is not immediately available.
5. **If remote Form I-9 examination is offered, check the process.** The optional DHS-authorized alternative procedure is for qualified E-Verify employers in good standing. It includes sending copies of the documents, including the front and back when a document is two-sided, presenting the same documents during a live video interaction, and the employer retaining clear, legible copies. If you are unable or unwilling to send copies this way, ask for physical document examination.
6. **Keep a brief record.** Save the request and note who you contacted, what process was confirmed, and whether you sent anything.
7. **If you already sent documents to an unverified or wrong recipient, contain the exposure.** Tell the employer’s verified HR or security contact and ask the organization to investigate, limit access where possible, and explain its next steps. If the documents may have reached a scammer or unauthorized recipient, use IdentityTheft.gov and consider whether a credit freeze or fraud alert is appropriate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether the message was definitely a scam before pausing the transfer.
- You do not need to send extra documents while the correct process is being checked.
- You do not need to confront the sender.
- You do not need to make every identity-theft decision at once.

## Important reassurance
Pausing is reasonable. A verified employer can explain the purpose of the request and provide an approved way to handle sensitive documents.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce exposure and move the request onto a verified process. Later decisions may need help from HR, security staff, or an appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Use verified employer contacts and official government resources for your situation.
