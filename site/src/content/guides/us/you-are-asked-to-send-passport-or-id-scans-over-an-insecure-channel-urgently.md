# What to do if… you are asked to send passport or ID scans over an insecure channel urgently (USA)

## Short answer
Do not provide substantive answers or sign documents immediately. Request the inquiry in writing, verify the official's identity, and notify your employer’s legal department to determine if you require independent counsel.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attempt to answer "off the record" or "just to be helpful" without representation.
- Don’t guess at facts, speculate on intent, or offer information not explicitly requested.
- Don’t delete, encrypt, or "clean up" any digital or physical records related to the inquiry.
- Don’t assume the company's lawyer represents your personal interests.
- Don’t sign any "voluntary" statements, declarations, or interview summaries on the spot.
- Don’t discuss the contact with coworkers, on social media, or via company messaging systems.
- Don’t provide personal passwords or hand over personal devices without legal advice.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the identity and authority of the contact person.** Get their full name, title, agency (e.g., SEC, OSHA, DOJ), and a case or reference number.
   - Use an official .gov website to find the agency's general number and call back to confirm the individual is a legitimate employee.
2. **Request all questions and demands in writing.** Use a standard procedural response: "I intend to be accurate and compliant. Please send your request and the legal authority for it in writing so I can review it with counsel."
3. **Notify your employer’s legal or compliance department immediately.** Forward the written request or provide the agent's contact details to your manager and the legal team.
   - Ask if a "legal hold" or document preservation notice has been issued for your files.
4. **Identify and safeguard all relevant records.** Stop any automated deletion settings on your email or cloud storage if you have the authority to do so; otherwise, alert IT.
   - Do not review or "reorganize" the files; simply ensure they are not altered or destroyed.
5. **Listen for and identify an "Upjohn Warning" if speaking with company counsel.** If the company's lawyer tells you they represent the "entity" and not you personally, acknowledge that you understand.
   - If this warning is given, assume your conversation is not protected by personal attorney-client privilege.
6. **Determine if you need independent legal counsel.** If the regulator or company counsel suggests you are a "target" or "subject," or if your interests conflict with the company's, stop the interview and seek your own lawyer.
   - Under 2026 DOJ policies, companies may waive privilege to earn "cooperation credit," meaning your statements could be handed directly to the government.
7. **Read any provided rights disclosures carefully.** If you receive SEC Form 1662 or a similar agency document, read the sections on "Voluntary" vs. "Compulsory" information.
   - Note that for voluntary inquiries, you generally have the right to decline answering or leave the interview at any time.
8. **Clarify the status of the request.** Ask if the regulator is seeking voluntary cooperation, an informal interview, or responding to a formal subpoena / Civil Investigative Demand (CID).
9. **Direct on-site investigators to management or legal.** If agents arrive in person, ask them to wait in a lobby while you contact the legal department.
   - Do not engage in "small talk" while waiting; remain professional but silent regarding work matters.
10. **Calendar all deadlines for written responses.** If you have been served with a formal subpoena, ensure the return date is clearly marked and shared with counsel to avoid contempt or sanctions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain "your side" or provide context immediately.
- You do not need to decide whether to remain employed or resign today.
- You do not need to determine who is "at fault" for the matter under investigation.
- You do not need to provide materials that are outside the specific scope of a written demand.

## Important reassurance
Regulatory inquiries are a standard component of many industries and do not automatically imply you have committed a crime or a policy violation. Taking time to verify identity and consult with counsel is a standard professional procedure that regulators expect from sophisticated parties.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. It does not cover specific defense strategies for different agencies or the complexities of grand jury proceedings.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice or an attorney-client relationship. Regulatory inquiries can carry significant civil or criminal penalties; always consult with a qualified attorney if you believe you have personal legal exposure.
