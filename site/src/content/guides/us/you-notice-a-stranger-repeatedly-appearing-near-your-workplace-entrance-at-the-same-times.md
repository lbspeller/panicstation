# What to do if… you notice a stranger repeatedly appearing near your workplace entrance at the same times (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a secure, populated indoor area and immediately report the pattern to your supervisor or security following your company’s Workplace Violence Prevention Plan (WVPP). Secure your commute by using buddy systems and request a security escort for all entries and exits.

## Do not do these things
- Do not approach or confront the individual to ask why they are there.
- Do not leave the building alone if the individual is visible or if you have observed the pattern today.
- Do not use the same entrance or exit at the same time if an alternative route is available.
- Do not post photos of the individual or details of the situation on public social media.
- Do not wait for a physical threat or approach before notifying management.

## What to do now
1. **Relocate to a populated indoor "safer pause."** Ensure you are in a location with controlled access (keycard entry), cameras, and other people (reception, main lobby, or office interior).
2. **Report the pattern according to your company’s Workplace Violence Prevention Plan (WVPP).** Tell a manager, HR representative, or security lead: "I have observed a specific person loitering at the entrance at [times] on [dates]." Ask for a copy of the formal incident report for your records.
3. **Request an immediate security or peer escort for all arrivals and departures.**
   - Ask for someone to walk you to your vehicle, bus stop, or rideshare pickup.
   - Request that a coworker or security guard meet you at the entrance when you arrive.
4. **Demand that management preserve surveillance footage.** Specifically ask that building security or management tag and save CCTV footage from the specific dates and times you observed the individual, as many systems overwrite data within 24 to 72 hours.
5. **Document the pattern in a factual incident log.** Keep this log on a personal device or in a physical notebook, not solely on a work computer.
   - Record dates, exact times, and specific behaviors (e.g., "stood by the bike rack for 20 minutes looking at the door").
   - Note physical descriptions: height, build, clothing, and any distinguishing marks.
   - Include vehicle information (make, model, color, license plate) only if you can observe it safely from a distance.
6. **Tighten your personal and professional digital privacy.**
   - Set social media profiles to private and remove "check-ins" at your workplace.
   - Request that your employer remove your photo or specific contact details from public-facing "About Us" or staff directory pages.
7. **Contact the VictimConnect Resource Center for guidance.** Call or text **855-484-2846** to speak with a specialist about stalking and harassment resources and safety planning specific to your jurisdiction.
8. **Notify law enforcement if the behavior escalates.**
   - **Call 911** if the person approaches you, blocks your path, follows you as you leave, or attempts to enter a restricted area.
   - **Call the local police non-emergency line** if the individual remains on public property (like a sidewalk) to report the pattern and request increased patrols during shift changes.
9. **Share your schedule with a trusted contact outside of work.** Provide your "arrived safely" and "leaving now" check-in times to a friend or family member who can escalate if they do not hear from you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the individual’s identity or motive before reporting.
- You do not need to file for a Restraining Order or Order of Protection in the first hour.
- You do not need to decide on long-term career or routine changes immediately.

## Important reassurance
Recognizing a pattern of behavior is a valid reason to seek support. Most workplaces have established protocols for loitering and unauthorized presence, and utilizing these systems is a standard safety procedure, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying and responding to a potential stalking or casing pattern at a workplace. Ongoing safety planning should be coordinated with specialized victim services and legal professionals.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal advice or a guarantee of safety. If you believe a crime is in progress or you are in immediate danger, contact 911.
