# What to do if… you are told to surrender your passport or travel documents due to a legal order (USA)
## Short answer
Ask for the written order or release conditions and confirm exactly where and when each document must be surrendered. Use the receiving office named in the instruction and ask for a receipt or written confirmation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hide, alter, destroy, mail, or give the document to someone else unless the receiving office confirms that route through official contact details.
- Do not try to travel until you have confirmed that travel is allowed.
- Do not apply for a replacement document or report a document lost or stolen to get around the instruction.
- Do not share passport details with an unverified caller, email sender, or website.
- Do not let an unclear deadline pass without contacting the office named in the paperwork or your legal representative.
- Do not assume that a passport book, passport card, foreign passport, or other travel document is treated the same way. Ask what the order covers.

## What to do now
1. **Get the instruction in writing.**  
   Ask for a copy of the order, release conditions, supervision condition, or other written directive. Note the case number, any surrender deadline, and the office named in the paperwork.

2. **Ask what documents are covered.**  
   Ask whether the instruction includes your passport book, passport card, foreign passports, emergency travel documents, or any other document you hold. Do not guess when the wording is unclear.

3. **Confirm the receiving office through an official route.**  
   Start with the contact details on the paperwork. For a federal matter, the United States Courts finder can help you locate official court, probation, or pretrial-services contact details. For another type of court or agency, use its official website. Ask where to go, when the office is open, and whether you need to bring the written order or any form.

4. **Record basic details when allowed.**  
   If it does not delay the surrender and you are allowed to do so, make a copy or note the document type, issuing country, document number, and expiry date.

5. **Ask for a receipt or written confirmation.**  
   When you hand over the document, ask for a receipt, property form, or written confirmation identifying the surrendered documents, the date, and the receiving office. Keep it with your case paperwork.

6. **Act promptly if a document is missing or the deadline is difficult.**  
   Contact the named receiving office and your lawyer, public defender, or other appointed counsel if you have one. Ask how to document that you do not have the document or how to request instructions before the deadline.

7. **Ask who will hold the document and how return requests work.**  
   Ask whether the receiving office will keep it, forward it elsewhere, or require a later request or further order before release. Procedures vary, so do not assume return is automatic.

8. **Use the Department of State route only when it applies.**  
   If the receiving office confirms that a valid U.S. passport was sent to the U.S. Department of State and you are on or have completed probation or parole, follow the Department of State instructions for requesting its return.

9. **Ask about review before making travel plans.**  
   If you believe the instruction is mistaken or urgent travel is involved, ask your lawyer or other legal representative how to request review or permission. Avoid new non-refundable bookings until the position is clear.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately how to challenge the underlying case or order.
- You do not need to contact every office that might be involved. Start with the office named in the written instruction.
- You do not need to solve future travel plans now.
- You do not need to work out the return process before completing the immediate surrender step, but keep your receipt and ask where to get later instructions.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to ask for the written instruction, the correct receiving office, and a receipt. Taking these steps can reduce mix-ups without requiring you to make wider legal decisions in the moment.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation. Later decisions about review, modification, travel permission, or return of the documents may need qualified legal help and will depend on the exact order and the office handling the documents.
