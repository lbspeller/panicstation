# What to do if… you are asked to identify someone in a photo lineup or video and you are not confident (USA)

## Short answer
State clearly that you cannot make a positive identification and request that your exact level of uncertainty be recorded in writing immediately. Do not select a "best match" if you are not certain.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess or select the person who looks "most like" the perpetrator.
- Do not feel obligated to make a choice even if you want to be helpful to the investigation.
- Do not ask the administrator for feedback or "hints" about whether you picked the right person.
- Do not discuss your thoughts or the photos with other witnesses who may be present.
- Do not look up the incident or suspects online after the procedure, as this can contaminate your memory for future proceedings.

## What to do now
1. **State your inability to identify anyone using a single, clear sentence.**
   - "I do not recognize anyone in these photos."
   - "I am not confident enough to make an identification."
   - "None of these individuals match my memory of the person."
2. **Ask the administrator to record a "confidence statement" in your own words.** **Ensure they write down exactly what you say regarding your uncertainty before any other conversation occurs.**
3. **Confirm the standard instructions provided by the officer.** **Verify that you have been told the suspect may or may not be present in the array and that the investigation will continue regardless of your choice.**
4. **Ask if the procedure is "double-blind."** **Inquire if the person showing you the photos knows which one is the suspect; if they do, note this for your own records as it is not best practice.**
5. **Request that the entire process be audio or video recorded.** **If the agency is not recording the session, ask that your specific statements of doubt be noted in the official police report.**
6. **Stop the process if you feel pressured to "just pick one."** **State clearly: "I feel pressured to make a choice I am not sure of, so I am stopping the procedure now."**
7. **If you are being treated as a suspect rather than a witness, invoke your right to counsel.** **Say: "I want an attorney and I will not participate in any identification procedures or questioning without one present."**

## What can wait
- You do not need to justify why your memory is unclear or provide reasons for your uncertainty right now.
- You do not need to decide if the police are conducting the investigation correctly.
- You do not need to worry about "ruining the case" by failing to identify a suspect.

## Important reassurance
Uncertainty is a frequent and scientifically expected response to high-stress situations, poor lighting, or brief sightings. Reporting that you are "not sure" is a valid and helpful contribution to the legal process, as it prevents the introduction of unreliable evidence.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for witnesses or suspects during an identification procedure. It does not cover trial testimony or long-term legal strategies, which require consultation with a qualified attorney.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Eyewitness identification laws and "best practice" mandates (such as double-blind or sequential requirements) vary significantly by state and local jurisdiction. If you believe your rights have been violated or you have been coerced, seek legal counsel immediately.
