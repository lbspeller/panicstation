# What to do if… you feel pressured into sexual contact during a healthcare or support appointment and you feel unsafe (USA)

## Short answer
Stop the interaction immediately by leaving the room or the building, or by calling for another staff member to enter the space. You are not required to provide an explanation or complete the appointment to get to safety.

## Do not do these things
- Don't stay in the room to avoid social discomfort or a perceived "scene."
- Don't accept any request to move the conversation to a private, non-clinical location or personal communication channel.
- Don't feel obligated to decide whether to file a formal report while you are still in the facility.
- Don't worry about being "polite" if you need to interrupt the provider to leave.

## What to do now
1. **End the physical interaction and create distance immediately.** Say "Stop" or "I am done with this appointment," and move toward the door or a public area like the waiting room.
2. **Request that another staff member or a chaperone enter the room if you cannot leave immediately.** State clearly: "I want a chaperone in here right now" or "Please get the clinic manager."
   - Under American Medical Association (AMA) ethical guidelines, patients have the right to a chaperone for any part of an exam.
3. **Leave the facility if you feel your safety is still at risk.** You do not need to check out at the front desk or pay your co-pay in the moment if you feel unsafe; you can handle administrative tasks by phone later.
4. **Ask for an escort to your vehicle or public transport if you are in a hospital or large complex.** Contact hospital security or go to the main reception desk and ask for a security escort.
5. **Contact a confidential support service to process the event and discuss options.** Call the RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-HOPE (4673) or use their online chat service for 24/7 assistance that does not require a police report.
6. **Make a brief, timestamped note of the facts for your own records.** Write down the provider’s name, the facility location, the specific actions or words used, and the names of any staff you spoke to while leaving.
7. **Identify the organization's internal safety contact if you wish to report the incident locally.**
   - Ask for the **Patient Advocate**, **Patient Relations** department, or the **Ombudsman**.
   - In a private practice, ask for the **Practice Manager**.
8. **Contact your State Medical Board or professional licensing board if you decide to report the provider’s license.** These boards handle complaints regarding professional misconduct and sexual boundaries for licensed clinicians.
9. **Call 911 if you are being prevented from leaving or are in immediate physical danger.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to define exactly what happened or use specific legal terminology right now.
- You do not need to decide if you want to pursue a criminal or civil case today.
- You do not need to return to that facility for any reason, including picking up records or prescriptions, which can be transferred elsewhere.

## Important reassurance
Feeling hesitant, confused, or physically unable to react ("freezing") is a documented biological response to a perceived threat or a violation of trust. This is especially common in environments with a significant power imbalance, such as healthcare settings. Your primary goal is reaching a safe space, and you have the right to stop any medical interaction at any time.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety actions for the immediate aftermath of feeling unsafe or pressured in a healthcare setting. It does not cover long-term legal strategies or clinical therapy for trauma.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and does not constitute legal or medical advice. Procedures for reporting vary by state and institution. If you are in an emergency situation, prioritize local emergency services.
