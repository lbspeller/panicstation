# What to do if… you wake with puffy eyelids and notice new frothy urine (USA)

## Short answer
Seek **same-day medical evaluation** through your primary care physician or an urgent care center. These symptoms can indicate protein loss from the kidneys (proteinuria) or an acute change in kidney function that requires prompt laboratory testing.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not ignore the symptoms** even if they seem to improve as the day progresses.
- **Do not take NSAIDs** (ibuprofen, naproxen) as these can further stress kidney function.
- **Do not take over-the-counter diuretics** or "water pills" without a direct prescription for this specific episode.
- **Do not restrict or force fluid intake** until a clinician provides specific guidelines based on your lab results.
- **Do not wait for a routine appointment** if you are pregnant or recently gave birth; this combination requires immediate assessment.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate emergency "Red Flags."** Go to an Emergency Room or call 911 immediately if you experience:
   - Sudden shortness of breath or chest pain.
   - Severe headache, blurred vision, or seeing flashing lights.
   - Sudden confusion, fainting, or seizures.
   - Swelling of the tongue, throat, or lips.
   - Complete or near-complete stop in urination.
2. **Contact your primary care clinic or go to Urgent Care.** 
   - **Call your doctor’s office** and report "new facial edema and persistent foamy urine."
   - **Request a same-day urinalysis** and blood pressure check. 
   - **If your doctor is unavailable**, go to a walk-in urgent care facility today.
3. **Record vital baseline data.** 
   - **Check your blood pressure** at home or a pharmacy and write it down.
   - **Note the location of any other swelling**, specifically checking your ankles, shins (press for 5 seconds to see if a dent remains), and hands.
   - **Estimate your weight change** if you have weighed yourself recently; sudden gain (2+ lbs overnight) is significant.
4. **Prepare for the medical assessment.**
   - **Note your pregnancy status** (if applicable), specifically if you are over 20 weeks pregnant or up to 6 weeks postpartum, as this suggests a preeclampsia risk.
   - **List all medications**, including supplements and recent antibiotics (for infections like strep throat).
   - **Be ready to provide a "clean catch" urine sample** at the facility.
5. **Request specific diagnostic tests.** Ensure the clinician evaluates:
   - Urinalysis (dipstick and microscopic).
   - Urine protein-to-creatinine ratio (UPCR) or Albumin-to-creatinine ratio (uACR).
   - Serum creatinine and Electrolyte panel (kidney function blood test).
   - Blood pressure.
6. **Maintain a stabilizing holding pattern.** 
   - **Avoid high-sodium foods** for the next 24 hours to prevent worsening fluid retention.
   - **Rest with your head elevated** on pillows to help reduce eyelid puffiness.
   - **Monitor for new symptoms** like abdominal pain or fever until you reach the clinic.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the underlying cause (e.g., Nephrotic Syndrome vs. infection) before your appointment.
- You do not need to purchase specialized kidney supplements or "cleanses."
- You do not need to decide on long-term dietary changes until your protein levels are confirmed.

## Important reassurance
Waking up with sudden facial swelling can be alarming. These symptoms—puffy eyelids and foamy urine—are well-recognized clinical signs that allow doctors to quickly narrow down the necessary tests. Getting seen today is the most effective way to address the cause and prevent complications.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and triage only. It does not provide medical diagnosis or long-term management for kidney disease or preeclampsia.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not a substitute for professional medical advice. If you are pregnant or postpartum and have these symptoms, you must be evaluated immediately to rule out preeclampsia, which can escalate rapidly.
