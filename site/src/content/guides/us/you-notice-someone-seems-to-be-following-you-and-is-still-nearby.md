# What to do if… you notice someone seems to be following you and is still nearby (USA)

## Short answer
Go into a busy, staffed building immediately and call **911** if you feel at risk. Do not go home or to a quiet area to "check" if the person is truly following you.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t head straight home or to your workplace; this reveals where you can be found later.
- Don’t confront, threaten, or try to intimidate the person.
- Don’t use "shortcuts" through isolated areas like alleys, stairwells, or quiet trails.
- Don’t accept a ride or "a place to wait" from a random stranger offering help.
- Don’t pull over to the side of the road or park in an empty lot if you are driving.
- Don’t wait for "certainty" before seeking help; your feeling of being unsafe is sufficient reason to act.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a staffed “anchor” location immediately.** Step into the nearest open business with employees and other people, such as a grocery store, pharmacy, coffee shop, hotel lobby, or a Hospital Emergency Room entrance. Stay inside.
2. **Tell staff directly about the situation.** Say clearly: “I believe someone is following me and they are still outside. I need to stay here while I call 911.” Ask for a manager or security guard to stand with you away from the windows or doors.
3. **Call 911 if you feel at risk right now.** Do this if the person is approaching you, waiting directly outside the door, blocking your exit, or if you are too frightened to leave. Explain your exact location and that the person following you is still nearby.
4. **Stay inside the building; do not try to “lose” them on foot.** Movement in public makes you more vulnerable. Let the building be your physical buffer. If the person appears to leave, remain inside until a safe ride or a police escort arrives.
5. **Share your situation with a trusted contact without stepping outside.** Call a friend or family member and keep them on the line. Share your live GPS location and the specific address of the building you are in. 
6. **Note identifying details only if it is safe and subtle.** Briefly observe clothing, approximate height, hair color, or vehicle details (color, make, license plate) if visible from a safe distance. Do not stare or point, as this may escalate the behavior.
7. **If you are in a car, keep all doors locked and windows up.** Do not drive to your home. Drive toward a busy, well-lit public place or directly to a Police, Sheriff, or Fire station. If you believe you are being followed, call 911 while driving (using hands-free features) and follow the dispatcher's instructions.
8. **Ask a rideshare or taxi driver to drop you at a busy, staffed location.** If you are already in a vehicle and notice a tail, change your destination to a public "anchor" point rather than your home. Call 911 from the back seat if the driver’s behavior is the cause of your concern.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide if this meets the legal definition of "stalking" right now.
- You don’t need to gather perfect evidence or take photos of the person.
- You don’t need to change your permanent routine or buy safety devices today.
- You don’t need to justify your reaction to anyone.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a "freeze" response or to doubt your instincts when you think you are being followed. Choosing to enter a staffed building and involving help is a proactive and sensible safety measure. Taking these steps does not mean you are overreacting; it means you are prioritizing your physical security.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate, active concern. If this behavior is part of a pattern, involves someone you know, or persists near your home, you will likely need to seek specialist support or formal legal reporting later.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and does not constitute legal advice. In any situation where you feel your life or safety is in immediate danger, call 911.
