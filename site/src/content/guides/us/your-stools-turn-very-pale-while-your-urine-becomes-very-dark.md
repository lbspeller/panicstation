# What to do if… your stools turn very pale while your urine becomes very dark (USA)

## Short answer
Arrange same-day medical advice or assessment. Very pale or clay-colored stools together with very dark urine can happen with jaundice, hepatitis, or a bile-flow problem, and it is not something to wait out if it is new, repeated, or paired with other symptoms.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume dehydration alone explains the combination of very pale or clay-colored stool and very dark urine.
- Do not wait several days to see if it fixes itself if this is new, repeated, or happening with yellow eyes or skin, fever, belly pain, vomiting, confusion, fainting, or worsening illness.
- Do not drink alcohol to calm yourself or to test whether symptoms change.
- Do not start, stop, or increase medicines or supplements to treat your liver on your own.
- Do not abruptly stop prescribed medicine unless a clinician tells you to, or unless the label or your prescriber has already told you what to do in this situation.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel faint, confused, severely weak, or unsafe to travel.

## What to do now
1. Do a quick check in good light:
   - Look at the whites of your eyes and your skin for yellowing.
   - Notice whether you also have itching, upper or right-sided belly pain, fever or chills, nausea, vomiting, unusual fatigue, confusion, fainting, or trouble keeping fluids down.
2. Call your primary care clinician, nurse advice line, or health plan advice line now and say: "My stools are very pale and my urine is very dark." Ask where you should be seen today.
3. If you are pregnant or recently postpartum, call your pregnancy care team or the hospital labor and delivery triage line if you have one, and ask for same-day advice.
4. Go to an emergency department now, or call 911, if you have severe or worsening belly pain, fever, repeated vomiting, confusion, fainting, collapse, clearly yellow skin or eyes, or you are too weak or unsafe to travel.
5. If you cannot get same-day medical advice or a same-day appointment, go to urgent care or an emergency department. Choose the emergency department if symptoms are significant, worsening, or you are unsure whether it is safe to wait.
6. Gather only the details clinicians are likely to need:
   - When the color change started.
   - Whether it happened more than once.
   - Any yellowing, itching, pain, fever, nausea, vomiting, fatigue, confusion, or fainting.
   - All prescription medicines, over-the-counter medicines, vitamins, supplements, and recent changes, including antibiotics or acetaminophen.
7. While waiting to be seen, avoid alcohol, sip water if you can keep it down, and eat lightly if you feel able. If you suddenly feel worse, switch to emergency care rather than continuing to wait.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause on your own.
- You do not need to compare stool or urine pictures online.
- You do not need to decide on diet changes, supplements, travel plans, or work plans before you have medical advice.
- You do not need to prepare a long medical history; the basic timing, symptoms, and medicine list are enough for now.

## Important reassurance
This combination is understandably alarming, but the next step is simple: get medical advice today and let a clinician decide what needs checking. Many causes can only be sorted out with an exam and tests, so you do not have to solve it by yourself.

## Scope note
This guide is for immediate first steps only. A clinician may check bloodwork such as liver tests or bilirubin, and may use imaging depending on your symptoms and exam; later decisions may need specialist medical help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical diagnosis, treatment, or professional medical advice. If you feel very unwell, are getting worse quickly, or are not safe to travel, use emergency services.
