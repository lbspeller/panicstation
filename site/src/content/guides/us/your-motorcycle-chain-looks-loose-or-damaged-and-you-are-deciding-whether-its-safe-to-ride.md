# What to do if… your motorcycle chain looks loose or damaged and you are deciding whether it’s safe to ride (USA)

## Short answer
Do not ride the motorcycle if the chain or sprockets show visible damage or if the slack exceeds the manufacturer's specification. A failed chain can lock the rear wheel or cause a total loss of power, leading to a crash.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ride at low speeds to "test" if the chain stays on the sprocket.
- Do not attempt to adjust the chain tension if you do not have a torque wrench or the specific torque values for your rear axle.
- Do not touch the chain, sprockets, or rear wheel while the engine is running or the bike is in gear.
- Do not use a chain that has missing O-rings, cracked side plates, or a loose master link clip.
- Do not ignore rhythmic clicking, grinding, or slapping sounds coming from the drivetrain.

## What to do now
1. **Park the motorcycle on a flat, stable surface and turn off the engine immediately.** Ensure the bike is secure on its side stand or center stand before beginning an inspection.
2. **Perform a visual check for "stop-ride" damage markers.** Look for any of the following indicators that the motorcycle is unsafe to operate:
   - Cracked, bent, or missing link plates.
   - A master link clip that is backwards, loose, or missing.
   - Sprocket teeth that appear "hooked," pointed like shark fins, or chipped.
   - Excessive rust that causes links to stay in a "kinked" or bent position.
3. **Measure the chain slack at the tightest point of the rotation.** **Rotate the rear wheel by hand to find the spot where the chain has the least movement, then check the vertical slack against the specification listed on your swingarm sticker or owner's manual.**
   - If the slack is greater than the maximum limit (typically 1 to 1.5 inches for most street bikes), do not ride.
   - If the chain can be pulled away from the rear of the sprocket to reveal more than half a tooth, the chain is severely stretched and must be replaced.
4. **Listen for specific sensory cues of drivetrain failure.** **Note if you have experienced any of the following during your recent ride:**
   - Metallic slapping or banging against the swingarm or chain guard.
   - A rhythmic "thumping" or vibration through the footpegs.
   - Jerky power delivery or a sensation of the chain "skipping" under acceleration.
5. **Request a professional tow or roadside assistance if any damage or out-of-spec tension is found.** Do not attempt to ride the bike to a shop.
   - Contact a motorcycle-specific towing service to prevent damage to the fairings or frame.
   - Notify the technician if you suspect the chain has already skipped or damaged the engine case.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the front sprocket is worn; an inspection of the rear sprocket and chain is sufficient for an immediate ride/no-ride decision.
- You do not need to clean or lubricate the chain if you have already identified structural damage or excessive stretch.
- You do not need to purchase a replacement kit until a professional confirms the chain and sprocket wear levels.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for motorcycle chains to require periodic adjustment and eventually wear out. Noticing a loose chain before a ride is a sign of an effective pre-ride routine. Choosing to secure alternative transport is a standard safety protocol used by experienced riders to avoid catastrophic mechanical failure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety assessment only. It does not replace the specific maintenance procedures or tension specifications found in your motorcycle's factory service manual.

## Important note
Chain failure can lead to engine case damage or rear-wheel lockup. This information is for general safety guidance and does not constitute mechanical advice. Always refer to the Motorcycle Safety Foundation (MSF) T-CLOCS standards and your specific manufacturer's manual for drivetrain maintenance.
