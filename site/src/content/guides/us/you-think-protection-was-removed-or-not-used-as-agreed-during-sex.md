# What to do if… you think protection was removed or not used as agreed during sex (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safe place, then contact confidential sexual assault support and seek medical care as soon as you can. Some pregnancy prevention, HIV prevention, and STI care options are time-sensitive, and you do not have to decide about reporting to law enforcement right now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the other person in person while you feel unsafe, trapped, or overwhelmed.
- Do not delay medical care while trying to decide exactly what to call what happened.
- Do not assume it is too late if time has passed; ask a clinician or hotline advocate what options remain.
- Do not post details online while distressed.
- Do not pressure yourself to make a police report, relationship decision, or final explanation right now.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If you are still with the person and feel uneasy, create distance by going to a bathroom, moving near other people, calling someone you trust, or leaving if you can do that safely. If you are in immediate danger, call **911**.
2. **Contact confidential sexual assault support.** RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline is available 24/7. You can call **800-656-HOPE (4673)**, text **HOPE** to **64673**, or use online chat at RAINN. You can say, “I think protection was removed or not used as agreed during sex, and I need help with medical options.”
3. **Seek medical care soon, even if you are unsure about police.** An emergency room, urgent care clinic, sexual health clinic, or sexual assault service can assess injuries, pregnancy risk, HIV risk, and STI concerns. Ask whether a **SANE** or **SAFE** clinician, or a sexual assault medical forensic exam service, is available nearby.
4. **Ask about emergency contraception as soon as possible.** Emergency contraceptive pills and the **copper IUD** are time-sensitive, often up to **5 days** depending on the method and timing. A clinician or pharmacist can help you choose what is still available.
5. **Ask urgently about HIV PEP if there may have been HIV exposure.** PEP is time-sensitive and must be started within **72 hours** after a possible HIV exposure. Say clearly when the sex happened and that the condom or agreed protection may not have been used.
6. **Ask about STI testing and treatment.** Tell the clinician what protection you thought was being used, what you believe changed, and whether there was oral, vaginal, or anal contact. Ask what testing, vaccines, or preventive treatment may be appropriate now and what follow-up testing may be needed.
7. **If you may want a forensic exam later, preserve what you can without panicking.** If it feels doable, avoid washing, bathing, changing clothes, or cleaning items that might hold evidence until you have spoken to an ER, SANE/SAFE clinician, or advocate. If you already washed, changed, or cleaned up, you can still get medical care and support.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to report to police.
- You do not need to label what happened with a legal term before getting care.
- You do not need to confront the other person now.
- You do not need to make a final relationship decision now.
- You do not need to tell many people; one safe support person or advocate is enough for the next step.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shocked, numb, frozen, confused, or unsure is common after a sexual boundary may have been crossed. If protection was removed or not used in a way you did not agree to, it makes sense to seek care and support.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: safety, urgent medical options, and confidential support. Later decisions about reporting, evidence, relationships, school, work, or legal options may need specialist help if you want it.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, forensic, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call **911**.
