# What to do if… you think protection was removed or not used as agreed during sex (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a physically safe location immediately, then contact a confidential support service like RAINN and seek medical care to access time-sensitive pregnancy prevention and HIV/STD prophylaxis. You do not need to decide whether to report to the police to receive medical treatment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the other person if you are alone or feel unsafe.
- Do not delay seeking medical care while waiting to "feel sure" about what happened.
- Do not assume it is too late for help if hours or days have passed.
- Do not shower, bathe, or change clothes if you are considering a forensic exam.
- Do not post details of the incident on social media or public forums.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause by creating physical distance from the other person.** If you are still in the same location, go to a different room, leave the building, or call **911** if you are in immediate danger.
2. **Contact the National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN) for confidential guidance.** **Call 800-656-HOPE (4673)**, text **HOPE to 64673**, or use the online chat at rainn.org to speak with a trained specialist who can help you locate local medical and advocacy resources.
3. **Seek medical care at an Emergency Room or specialized clinic as soon as possible.** **Ask for a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) or Sexual Assault Forensic Examiner (SAFE) to discuss your options for a forensic exam and medical treatment.**
   - Forensic exams (rape kits) can generally be performed up to 120 hours (5 days) after an incident, though earlier is better for evidence collection.
4. **Access emergency contraception (EC) to prevent pregnancy within the required time windows.** **Discuss your options with a clinician, as effectiveness varies by method and body weight.**
   - **IUD (Copper or certain hormonal IUDs):** The most effective EC (99%+); must be inserted by a provider within 5 days.
   - **Ella (ulipristal acetate):** A prescription pill effective up to 5 days; works better than Plan B for those weighing 165–195 lbs.
   - **Plan B (levonorgestrel):** Available over-the-counter; most effective if taken within 72 hours and may be less effective for those over 165 lbs.
5. **Request Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) if you are concerned about HIV exposure.** **PEP is a 28-day course of medication that must be started within 72 hours (3 days) of exposure to be effective.** Every hour counts; the sooner it is started, the more effective it is.
6. **Request prophylactic treatment for other STIs/STDs.** **Clinicians can provide immediate "presumptive treatment" (antibiotics) for common infections like gonorrhea, chlamydia, and trichomoniasis.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to press charges or involve law enforcement.
- You do not need to define or label the experience right now to be eligible for support.
- You do not need to decide the future of your relationship with the other person immediately.

## Important reassurance
Physical and emotional reactions such as numbness, confusion, or "freezing" are common and valid responses to a violation of boundaries. Seeking medical care and support is a step toward physical safety and health, regardless of how you feel about the situation right now.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization focusing on immediate safety, time-sensitive medical options (EC, PEP, and STI care), and confidential support. It does not provide legal advice or long-term therapeutic strategies.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not replace professional medical or legal advice. If you are in immediate danger, call **911**. Emergency contraception and PEP are time-sensitive medical interventions that require professional assessment.
