# What to do if… a hospice provider cannot start support because of missing or incomplete paperwork (USA)

## Short answer
Ask hospice admissions to identify the exact item or items blocking admission and the quickest accepted way to complete them today. If the person has severe or life-threatening symptoms, or there is an immediate safety risk, call 911 rather than waiting for paperwork.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign blank forms or forms you have not read or do not understand.
- Do not assume that a family relationship or any power-of-attorney document automatically allows someone to sign for the patient.
- Do not wait for hospice paperwork before calling 911 for severe or life-threatening symptoms or an immediate safety risk.
- Do not send original legal documents unless the hospice specifically requires them; ask whether a copy, scan, or photo is accepted.
- Do not spend the first minutes on non-urgent coverage questions unless the hospice says they are blocking admission.

## What to do now
1. **Ask hospice admissions to name every blocker.**  
   Say: “Please tell me the exact document or information that is missing, who must complete or sign it, and the quickest accepted way to send it today.”

2. **Ask whether the missing item is part of the Medicare hospice process.**  
   If Medicare applies, the hospice benefit generally requires an election statement signed by the patient or representative and initial certification involving the hospice doctor and the patient’s regular doctor if the patient has one. Ask whether either item is incomplete. The election start date cannot be earlier than the date of the election statement.

3. **If the patient cannot sign, ask what representative documentation is required.**  
   Ask: “What document do you need to recognize the person who can sign for the patient?” Do not assume that being next of kin or holding a financial power of attorney is enough. Ask whether the hospice accepts an electronic signature, scan, or photo.

4. **Ask for the fastest available admissions route.**  
   Ask for the admissions supervisor or on-call contact. Say: “Support is needed today. Can the paperwork review be accelerated, and what clinical support is available while it is being completed?” If Medicare applies, ask whether a one-time consultation with a hospice doctor about care options and symptom management is available before hospice starts. Ask for the name or role of the point of contact and the next number to call if the callback does not happen.

5. **Contact the current or referring clinician.**  
   Tell the clinician’s office exactly what the hospice says is missing. Ask the clinician and hospice to coordinate the required certification or other documentation. If written certification is the blocker, ask the hospice whether any permitted interim certification process is available while written documentation follows.

6. **Use an urgent medical route when symptoms cannot wait.**  
   Call 911 for severe or life-threatening symptoms, or if there is an immediate safety risk. For urgent but non-life-threatening symptoms, call the current clinician’s urgent line and tell hospice admissions that symptoms are worsening while admission is pending.

7. **Write down the today plan.**  
   Record the missing item, who is handling it, the expected callback time, and the next number to call if the plan stalls.

## What can wait
- Non-urgent service preferences and scheduling details beyond today.
- Broader insurance questions that are not blocking admission.
- Optional documents that the hospice confirms are not needed to start support.
- Wider family decisions while the immediate plan is being stabilized.

## Important reassurance
A paperwork delay does not mean you have done anything wrong. It is reasonable to ask for a clear same-day plan, a named point of contact, and an urgent medical route if symptoms cannot wait.

## Scope note
These are first steps to clarify the admission blocker, complete the required paperwork, and get urgent backup when needed. Later decisions about coverage questions, provider changes, or complaints may need specialist help after the immediate situation is stabilized.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. For severe or life-threatening symptoms, or an immediate safety risk, call 911.
