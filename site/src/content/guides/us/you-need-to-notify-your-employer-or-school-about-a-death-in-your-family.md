# What to do if… you need to notify your employer or school about a death in your family (USA)

## Short answer
Send one short message to the right contact saying there has been a death in your family, you need time off or flexibility, and you need to know the correct policy or process.

## Do not do these things
- Do not share more detail than you want to. “There has been a death in my family” is enough to start.
- Do not commit to a return date until you know the next funeral, travel, caregiving, or family arrangements.
- Do not assume paid bereavement leave is automatic. Many workplaces handle this through employer policy, PTO, unpaid leave, a union contract, or state or local rules.
- Do not stay silent if you will miss a shift, class, exam, clinical, lab, or required attendance.
- Do not send several different explanations. Keep one brief, consistent message.

## What to do now
1. **Send a simple notification to the correct contact.**  
   Work: contact your supervisor or manager, and HR if your workplace requires it.  
   K–12 school: contact the attendance office or main office, and the teacher if a specific class or assignment is affected.  
   College or university: contact the instructor or professor and the Dean of Students, student affairs office, or absence-notification office your school uses.  
   Use this script: “Hi [Name], there has been a death in my family. I cannot [work/attend] on [date(s)] and may need some flexibility this week. Please tell me the next step and who I should keep updated. Thank you.”

2. **Ask which absence or leave category they want you to use.**  
   For work, ask: “Should I request bereavement leave, PTO, sick leave, unpaid leave, or another leave type?”

3. **Ask what documentation is needed, but do not scramble for it immediately.**  
   Say: “If documentation is needed later, please tell me what you accept and when you need it.”

4. **Ask HR about your actual job-protection and pay options.**  
   For many private-sector jobs, federal wage law does not require paid time off for attending a funeral, so the answer may depend on workplace policy, a union contract, or state or local rules. If grief or another health issue is making you unable to work, ask HR whether the FMLA or another medical-leave process may apply; FMLA is for qualifying family and medical reasons, not bereavement alone.

5. **If you are a federal employee, ask your agency HR office about the specific federal options.**  
   Ask whether you can use sick leave for family care or bereavement, annual leave, advanced leave, leave without pay, or another workplace flexibility. Ask separately whether any narrow funeral-leave rule applies to your situation.

6. **For school or university, request the immediate academic flexibility you need.**  
   Examples: “I am requesting an excused absence due to a family bereavement and guidance on extensions or make-up work.”  
   “I may miss [exam/lab/clinical]. Please tell me the formal process and any immediate deadline.”

7. **Give one check-in time so you are not chased all day.**  
   Example: “I can send an update by 4 p.m. tomorrow,” or “I will update you once travel or funeral plans are confirmed.”

8. **Save your messages and approvals.**  
   Keep the email, text, portal message, or screenshot showing what you sent, who replied, what was approved, and which dates were covered.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today how to handle long-term leave, your whole workload, or your full school schedule.
- You do not need to explain the death or family circumstances in detail.
- You do not need to negotiate every deadline at once. Start with the next shift, class, exam, lab, clinical, or required meeting.
- You do not need to answer every follow-up immediately. One planned update is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
In the first days after a death, it is common to feel numb, forgetful, or overwhelmed by simple administrative tasks. A short message that reaches the right office is a good enough first step and can reduce avoidable penalties or misunderstandings.

## Scope note
These are first steps to notify work or school and prevent avoidable administrative harm. Later decisions about leave, pay, academic standing, or legal rights may need help from HR, a union representative, a school official, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Leave rights and school absence rules can depend on your employer, union contract, state or local law, school policy, and your specific circumstances.
