# What to do if… you return home and find a window screen removed or a window left slightly open when you know you closed it (USA)

## Short answer
Assume an unauthorized entry may have occurred: **do not enter the home**—immediately retreat to a safe location and call **911** to request a welfare check or a search of the premises.

## Do not do these things
- Don't enter the property to investigate, shout for occupants, or "check things out" yourself.
- Don't touch the window, screen, frame, or nearby surfaces to avoid contaminating potential forensic evidence.
- Don't attempt to close or secure the window before police arrive.
- Don't wait in your driveway or on your porch where you are visible from the home's interior.
- Don't assume it was "just the wind" or a mistake if you have a clear memory of securing the window.
- Don't let your pets run inside if they are currently outside or in your vehicle.

## What to do now
1. **Retreat immediately to a safe distance.** Move away from the immediate vicinity of the house. Go to a neighbor's home, a nearby public business, or sit in your car with the doors locked and the engine running.
2. **Call 911 and state your observations clearly.** Tell the dispatcher you have arrived home to find signs of a possible forced entry.
   - Specify that a window screen has been removed or a window is ajar.
   - Inform them if you see lights on that should be off, or vice versa.
   - Mention if any vehicles are in the driveway or nearby that do not belong there.
3. **Report the presence of other residents or pets.** Tell the dispatcher if anyone else (roommates, family, or service workers) is expected to be home or is unaccounted for.
4. **Monitor the exits from a safe, obscured vantage point.** If you can do so safely from a distance (e.g., from across the street or inside a car), watch the doors and windows for movement. 
   - Do not attempt to block any exits or confront anyone who leaves.
   - If someone exits, note their physical description and direction of travel for the dispatcher.
5. **Check remote security feeds if available.** If you have a doorbell camera or internal security app, check the footage from your safe location. 
   - Do not return to the property to "fix" a camera or get a better signal.
6. **Provide descriptions of missing or tampered items only after police clear the scene.** Once officers have searched the home and declared it safe, they will escort you inside. 
   - Point out the specific window you noticed.
   - List any obvious missing items (laptops, jewelry, safes) to the responding officer.
7. **Obtain a case or incident report number.** Write this number down immediately, along with the names of the responding officers. You will need this for insurance claims or follow-up reports.

## What can wait
- You don't need to determine how the person got in or what their motive was right now.
- You don't need to call your insurance company until after the police have finished their initial walkthrough.
- You don't need to start cleaning the area or repairing the window screen until the police explicitly tell you they are finished with the scene.
- You don't need to notify your landlord or property manager until you are in a stable, long-term safe environment.

## Important reassurance
Finding a sign of intrusion is a high-stress event that triggers a "fight or flight" response. It is normal to feel a sudden surge of adrenaline, confusion, or the urge to dismiss the danger to regain a sense of normalcy. Trusting your observation that something has changed is a vital safety skill.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate discovery of a potential break-in. It does not cover long-term home security upgrades, victim advocacy services, or legal proceedings against suspects.

## Important note
Emergency response protocols and non-emergency reporting methods vary by municipality. Always prioritize the instructions given by your local 911 dispatcher or responding law enforcement officers over general guidance.
