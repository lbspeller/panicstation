# What to do if… you receive a sudden warning about work attendance that you think is inaccurate (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately notify IT Security and Accounts Payable to freeze your approval permissions and pause pending payments, then report the activity in writing to your manager or a neutral compliance hotline.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront any person you suspect of misusing your identity.
- Do not attempt to delete, edit, or "fix" the fraudulent records or approval logs.
- Do not use personal email or external messaging to forward company invoices or vendor data.
- Do not wait for a full internal investigation before requesting a pause on payments.
- Do not ignore the situation even if the amounts seem small or "clerical."

## What to do now
1. **Contact IT Security or the Helpdesk to isolate your account.** **Request an immediate password reset and a review of active login sessions to terminate unauthorized access.**
   - Ask for a check of mailbox "forwarding rules" or "delete rules" that might be hiding approval notifications.
   - Request a review of any delegated permissions or shared access on your account.
2. **Alert Accounts Payable and Procurement to pause payments.** **Ask them to place a temporary "hold" on all approvals associated with your name until the transactions are verified.**
   - Request that they check for recent changes to vendor bank account details.
   - Ask if a "proxy" or "delegate" role was used to sign in your name.
3. **Report the incident through multiple internal channels simultaneously.** **Send a factual, written summary to your manager, the Finance Director, and the Internal Audit or Compliance department.**
   - Use a confidential ethics hotline if you suspect management involvement or fear retaliation.
   - Include specific invoice numbers, dates, and the specific platform where the misuse occurred.
4. **Preserve evidence exactly as it appears.** **Take screenshots of the unauthorized approvals and save relevant notification emails without moving or deleting them from the system.**
   - Create a private timeline of when you first noticed the discrepancy and any unusual system behavior you observed.
5. **Report to federal authorities if corporate funds were stolen.** **File a report at IC3.gov (FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center) for Business Email Compromise (BEC) if company money was diverted to external accounts.**
   - Provide the specific vendor names and bank details used in the fraudulent transactions.
6. **Determine if your personal information was compromised.** **Ask IT if your Social Security Number (SSN), home address, or personal banking details were accessible in the system where the misuse occurred.**
   - If personal data was exposed, visit IdentityTheft.gov to create a recovery plan.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove who committed the act before you report the red flags.
- You do not need to hire a private attorney immediately unless you are formally accused of a crime.
- You can wait to freeze your personal credit reports unless there is evidence that your personal PII (like your SSN) was accessed.
- You do not need to decide whether to resign while the initial containment is underway.

## Important reassurance
Discovering that your professional identity is being misused is a high-stress event that often triggers fear of job loss or legal trouble. Taking immediate, documented action to alert the correct departments is the most effective way to demonstrate that you are not complicit and are acting in the company's best interest.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for workplace identity misuse. It does not replace your employer's specific internal reporting policies, whistleblower procedures, or the need for professional legal advice if the situation escalates.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal or professional financial advice. Employment laws and corporate fraud protocols vary by state and individual company policy. If you are being pressured by a superior to ignore these red flags, consider contacting an employment attorney or a protected whistleblower hotline.
