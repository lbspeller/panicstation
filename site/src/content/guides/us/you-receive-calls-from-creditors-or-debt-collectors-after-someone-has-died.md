# What to do if… you receive calls from creditors or debt collectors after someone has died (USA)

## Short answer
Do not agree to pay anything on the phone. Tell the caller the person has died, ask for details in writing, and route the contact to the estate’s personal representative if one has been appointed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay from your own money just to make the call stop unless you have confirmed you are legally responsible.
- Do not let a caller rush you into saying the debt is yours, promising payment, or setting up a payment plan.
- Do not share a Social Security number, bank details, card number, or date of birth with an incoming caller.
- Do not assume “next of kin” means you personally owe the debt.
- Do not argue about the debt on the phone when you are grieving or overwhelmed.
- Do not sign anything from a creditor or collector until you understand whether it affects you or the estate.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the call and set one clear boundary.** Say: “The person has died. Please send any information in writing.” Then end the call if you need to.

2. **Write down the call details before they blur.** Note the date, time, phone number, company name, caller name, the debt they mentioned, and whether they asked you to pay personally.

3. **Ask for written debt information.** If the caller is a debt collector, ask them to send validation information in writing, including:
   - the debt collector’s name and mailing address,
   - the creditor name,
   - the amount claimed,
   - what to do if the debt is disputed,
   - any account or reference number.

4. **Do not confirm responsibility.** Use wording such as: “I am not agreeing that I owe this. Send it in writing.” This helps you avoid an accidental promise or payment while you check the situation.

5. **Route the contact to the estate process.**
   - If a court has appointed an executor, administrator, or personal representative, give the caller that person’s mailing address or ask the caller to send correspondence there.
   - If no one has been appointed, say: “No personal representative has been appointed yet. Please send written correspondence only.”
   - If you are not the spouse, personal representative, executor, administrator, lawyer, legal guardian, parent of a deceased minor, or another person legally allowed to discuss the debt, do not discuss the debt itself.

6. **Move repeated calls into writing.** If calls keep coming, send a short letter saying the person has died and that you want communication by mail only. Keep a copy.

7. **Use a stop-contact letter carefully if needed.** For a debt collector, you can generally mail a letter telling the collector to stop contacting you. After receiving it, the collector may still contact you to confirm they will stop or to tell you about a specific action, such as a lawsuit. This does not make the debt disappear, so “mail only” may be a calmer first boundary.

8. **Watch for red flags.** Treat it as a warning sign if the caller says you must pay only because you are family, refuses to send anything in writing, demands immediate payment, asks for sensitive personal information, or threatens you.

9. **Report harassment or deception.** Keep your call log and copies of letters. You can report problems to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, the Federal Trade Commission, and your state attorney general’s office.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether the estate is insolvent.
- You do not need to work out which debts get paid first.
- You do not need to contact every creditor immediately because one caller reached you.
- You do not need to choose a probate lawyer during the call.
- You do not need to debate whether the debt is valid until you have written information.

## Important reassurance
Debt calls after a death can feel harsh and urgent, but you are allowed to slow the conversation down. In many situations, debts are handled through the estate, and family members usually do not pay from their own money unless a legal exception applies.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Probate, estate administration, creditor claims, spouse responsibility, community property, and debt-collection rights can vary by state and by the type of debt, so later decisions may need help from legal aid, a probate attorney, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Do not pay, promise to pay, or sign anything if you are unsure whether you are personally responsible.
