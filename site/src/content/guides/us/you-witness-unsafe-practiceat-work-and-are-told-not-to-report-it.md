# What to do if… you witness unsafe practiceat work and are told not to report it (USA)

## Short answer
Secure your pay and benefits records immediately and obtain the bankruptcy case number and chapter. Do not resign until you have verified how it affects your unemployment eligibility and your status as a priority creditor for unpaid wages.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign impulsively without checking state unemployment rules, as a voluntary quit may disqualify you from benefits.
- Do not continue working without a written guarantee of payment if the company has already missed a payroll cycle.
- Do not sign new releases, waivers, or severance "adjustments" without legal review during the initial panic.
- Do not leave personal items or original documents at the workplace if a site closure is imminent.
- Do not assume COBRA will be available if the company terminates its group health plan entirely.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific type of bankruptcy and the case number immediately.**
   - Ask HR or the legal department if the filing is Chapter 11 (reorganization) or Chapter 7 (liquidation).
   - Record the **bankruptcy case number**, the **court district**, and the name of the **Trustee** (for Chapter 7) or **Debtor-in-Possession counsel** (for Chapter 11).
2. **Download and save all personal compensation and benefit records before system access is revoked.**
   - Prioritize your most recent pay stubs, W-2s, offer letters, commission agreements, and PTO balance statements.
   - Save these to a personal device or cloud account that is not controlled by the employer.
3. **Audit your retirement accounts to ensure all recent payroll deductions were actually deposited.**
   - Log into your 401(k) or 403(b) portal and check the "last deposit" date against your last pay stub.
   - Note any "unremitted" contributions (money taken from your check but not showing in the account), as these require specific reporting to the Department of Labor.
4. **Determine your "Priority Claim" status for any unpaid wages or benefits.**
   - Under the 2025-2028 adjustments, employees have a "priority" status for up to **$17,150** in wages and benefits earned within 180 days before the filing.
   - Prepare a list of exactly what you are owed, including hours worked, earned commissions, and reimbursable expenses.
5. **Contact your state's unemployment insurance agency to discuss your specific situation.**
   - If your hours are reduced or you are furloughed, you may be eligible for partial benefits even if the business is still technically open.
6. **Clarify the exact end date of your health insurance coverage.**
   - Ask if the "Plan Sponsor" is continuing the group health plan; if the plan is terminated entirely, COBRA rights usually cease to exist.
   - Use the 60-day Special Enrollment Period to look for coverage on the Health Insurance Marketplace (Healthcare.gov) if your workplace plan ends.
7. **Monitor the mail for an official "Notice of Commencement" and "Proof of Claim" form.**
   - Follow the instructions to file a **Proof of Claim** by the "Bar Date" (deadline) listed in the notice to ensure you are included in any court-ordered payouts.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a long-term legal strategy or lawsuit today.
- You do not need to understand the full complexities of the bankruptcy code to protect your records.
- You do not need to negotiate a new contract or severance package in the first few hours of the announcement.

## Important reassurance
Bankruptcy announcements often cause immediate confusion and administrative delays. It is normal to feel uncertain about your next paycheck or the status of your benefits. These proceedings are highly regulated, and employees are granted specific legal priorities to help them recover unpaid earnings before other general creditors.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for employees in the initial days of a bankruptcy announcement. It does not cover long-term litigation, collective bargaining negotiations, or specific state-level "mini-WARN" laws.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Bankruptcy laws are subject to specific court orders and jurisdictional variations. If you are a member of a union, contact your representative immediately. For significant unpaid sums, consider consulting an employment or bankruptcy attorney.
