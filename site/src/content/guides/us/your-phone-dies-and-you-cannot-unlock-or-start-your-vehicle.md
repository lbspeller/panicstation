# What to do if… your phone dies and you cannot unlock or start your vehicle (USA)

## Short answer
Get away from traffic or other immediate danger first. Then use the quickest non-phone backup, such as a physical key, fob, key card, or spare; if you do not have one, get a small amount of power to the phone so you can unlock, start, or call for help.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t stand in traffic, between vehicles, or on the edge of a high-speed road while you keep trying the app.
- Don’t let a stranger take your phone, ask for your passcode, or have you log into your vehicle, Apple, Google, or manufacturer account on their device unless there is no safer option.
- Don’t factory reset the phone, change your main password, remove the digital key, or delete the vehicle app while you are locked out; that can make access harder.
- Don’t keep walking around a parking lot or roadside with your attention on the app if the area feels unsafe.
- Don’t break a window unless there is immediate danger to life, such as a person trapped inside and in medical danger.

## What to do now
1. **Make the scene safer.**
   - Turn on hazard lights if you can reach the controls.
   - If you are inside and the vehicle still moves, pull into the safest nearby place you can, such as a shoulder, parking lot, gas station, or well-lit business.
   - If you are already outside, step away from traffic and wait in a safer nearby place.
   - If there is immediate danger, such as traffic risk, fire, medical danger, or someone trapped in the vehicle, call 911.

2. **Try your vehicle’s non-phone backup methods.**
   - Use the physical key or emergency blade key, often stored inside a fob, and look for the manual door lock, which may be behind a small cover.
   - Use the key fob if you have it.
   - Use a key card, valet card, or wallet card if your vehicle supports one.
   - Check your wallet, bag, coat pocket, phone-case sleeve, or glove-safe pouch for a backup card or key.
   - Ask someone you trust to bring a spare key if one exists.

3. **Check whether the “dead” phone can still work as a key briefly.**
   - For iPhone Wallet car key with Express Mode, some keys may work in power reserve for up to five hours after the phone needs charging.
   - For Android digital car key, you may still be able to unlock and start for a few hours after the battery dies, but generally only if the key does not require the phone to be unlocked.
   - If your vehicle supports a tap method, hold the back of the phone near the door handle or reader, then place the phone on the vehicle’s key reader area before pressing start.
   - Try this only a few times from a safe position; do not stay exposed beside traffic.

4. **Get a small amount of phone power.**
   - Ask a nearby store, gas station, hotel, restaurant, or security desk to let you plug in briefly.
   - Borrow a power bank or compatible cable, such as USB-C or Lightning, if someone nearby can help.
   - Keep the phone with you while it charges, and do not share your passcode.
   - If you can safely use someone else’s phone, use it to call roadside assistance, a trusted person, your insurer, or your vehicle maker’s roadside line rather than logging into your vehicle account on that device.

5. **Call the right help and state the key problem first.**
   - Contact your insurer’s roadside service, your vehicle maker’s roadside line, AAA if you are a member, or a local tow or locksmith service.
   - Say: “I’m locked out. My phone is dead and my vehicle access is digital or app-based. I may not be able to open or start the car.”
   - Give your exact location, nearby landmarks, whether you are in a safe place, and whether anyone is trapped inside.

6. **After you regain access, stabilize the next hour only.**
   - Start charging the phone immediately using the vehicle USB port, 12V outlet, or a power bank.
   - Before driving away, confirm that you have a carry-without-phone backup, such as a fob, key card, physical key, or spare key plan.
   - Do not remove or re-pair digital keys until you are safely home or at a repair location and can follow the manufacturer’s instructions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to troubleshoot the app, update software, re-pair the phone, or change security settings while you are stranded.
- You do not need to decide whether to stop using digital keys long-term right now.
- You do not need to file a vehicle safety complaint right now; consider it later only if you suspect a repeated safety defect or unsafe failure pattern.
- You do not need to solve the whole backup-key plan before you are safe, reachable, and able to charge the phone.

## Important reassurance
This is a known weak point of digital and app-based keys: the phone, battery, signal, app, and vehicle all have to cooperate. Many situations become manageable once you get a little charge, use a physical backup, or bring in roadside help.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and restore safe access or communication. Your exact backup entry and start method depends on the vehicle make, model, phone, digital key setup, and manufacturer instructions, so later decisions may need help from roadside assistance, the vehicle maker, a locksmith, insurer, or repair professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, security, mechanical, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe or there is immediate danger, prioritize 911 and moving to a safer place when feasible.
