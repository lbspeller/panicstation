# What to do if… you cannot receive verification codes abroad and you are locked out of travel accounts you need (USA)

## Short answer
Stop repeated login attempts to avoid permanent lockouts, restore your US cellular connection using manual roaming settings, and prioritize recovering your primary email account first.

## Do not do these things
- Do not request a new code more than three times in a row (this can trigger a 24-hour security lockout).
- Do not share any code you eventually receive with anyone claiming to be "support" or "security."
- Do not move your physical SIM card to a local phone if your US number is tied to device-specific security (like iMessage or Google Prompt).
- Do not pay third-party "account recovery" services found on social media; these are frequently scams.
- Do not use public computers or shared kiosks to enter sensitive passwords or recovery codes.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific accounts required for the next 24 hours of travel.**  
   - Prioritize access to your primary email, as this serves as the recovery hub for other services.
   - List urgent travel bookings, such as flight itineraries, hotel confirmations, or rail tickets.

2. **Execute a manual network refresh on your mobile device.**  
   - Toggle **Airplane Mode** on for 30 seconds, then off to force a reconnection to local towers.
   - Go to your cellular settings and ensure **Data Roaming** is enabled for your US line.
   - For T-Mobile users, dial **#RON#** (#766#) to ensure roaming is active.
   - Check the **Unknown Senders** or **Junk** folder in your messaging app, as short-codes are often filtered there automatically while abroad.

3. **Verify your carrier account status via a Wi-Fi connection.**  
   - Use the carrier’s official app or web portal to confirm that international roaming and "short-code" messaging are not restricted.
   - Contact carrier support via web chat to ask if there is an active "roaming block" on your line.

4. **Attempt email recovery using non-SMS methods.**  
   - Select **Try another way** or **I don't have my phone** on the sign-in screen.
   - Look for options to use a recovery email address, a previously trusted device (like a laptop already in your possession), or offline backup codes you may have saved.

5. **Contact travel providers directly with secondary identification.**  
   - Call the airline or hotel customer service line and provide your **legal name, booking reference (PNR), and the last four digits of the payment card** used.
   - Request that they send a copy of your itinerary to a secondary email address or provide the information over the phone.

6. **Initiate the Login.gov deletion process if you are locked out of US government services.**  
   - Go to the Login.gov sign-in page, enter your email and password, and select **Choose another security option**.
   - Select **deleting your account** at the bottom of the list.
   - **Wait 24 hours** for the mandatory security delay before clicking the final deletion link sent to your email.
   - Create a new account using the same email address once the deletion is finalized.

7. **Report a lost or stolen physical passport to the nearest US embassy or consulate.**  
   - Use the State Department's online **Form DS-64** to report the loss immediately and invalidate the document.
   - Schedule an emergency appointment at the nearest US mission to apply for a limited-validity emergency passport.
   - Obtain a local police report if you intend to file an insurance claim or if it is required for your specific host country's exit visa.

## What can wait
- You do not need to update your long-term security settings or add new 2FA methods until you have stable internet and cellular service.
- You do not need to replace your phone or change your permanent US phone number while currently abroad.
- You do not need to file formal complaints with service providers regarding the lockout until you have returned to the US.

## Important reassurance
Lockouts due to roaming issues are a standard security response by automated systems and do not necessarily mean your account has been compromised. Most travel providers have manual verification protocols designed for users who cannot access their primary phone.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for restoring urgent account access while abroad. It does not cover long-term identity theft recovery or complex legal disputes with service providers.

## Important note
This information is for situational stabilization only. Individual service provider policies regarding account recovery and multi-factor authentication vary and are subject to change. For passport emergencies, always defer to the official instructions provided by the U.S. Department of State.
