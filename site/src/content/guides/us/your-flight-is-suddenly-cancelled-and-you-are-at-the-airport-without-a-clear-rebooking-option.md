# What to do if… your flight is suddenly cancelled and you are at the airport without a clear rebooking option (USA)

## Short answer
Get the airline to give you a clear choice in writing: rebook you, or refund you if you choose not to travel or accept an alternative. Do not accept a voucher, credit, or replacement itinerary unless you are sure that is the option you want.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click “accept voucher” or “accept credit” if what you want is a refund to your original payment method.
- Don’t leave the airport help route without saving proof of the cancellation and what the airline offered.
- Don’t assume the airline must pay for hotels, meals, or transport in the U.S.; those usually depend on the airline’s policy, its public commitments, and whether the disruption was controllable.
- Don’t buy a replacement flight in a panic before checking whether the airline can rebook you at no extra cost.
- Don’t rely on memory. Keep screenshots, receipts, times, names, and confirmation numbers.

## What to do now
1. Get physically settled for a few minutes. Sit somewhere safe, charge your phone, connect to airport Wi-Fi, and open your confirmation email, ticket number, and airline app.
2. Save proof before screens change. Screenshot the cancelled status in the app, photograph the departure board if visible, and save any text or email saying the flight is cancelled.
3. Use more than one airline contact route at the same time. Join the airport customer service line, open airline chat or callback, and try a self-service kiosk if one is available.
4. Ask for a specific rebooking plan. Say: “Please rebook me to my final destination today if possible, including a connection. If that is not available, what is the earliest confirmed option?”
5. Ask about partner or other-airline rebooking carefully. Say: “Does your policy or DOT dashboard commitment allow rebooking me on a partner airline or another airline you have an agreement with at no extra cost?”
6. Ask about overnight help if the next option is much later. Say: “Is this considered controllable by the airline, and what hotel, transport, or meal support will you provide under your policy? Can you put that in writing?”
7. Decide between travel and refund. If the airline cancelled the flight and you do not travel or accept alternative transportation, a voucher, credit, or other compensation instead, you are generally entitled to a refund. Make your choice clear and save proof.
8. If you want the refund, keep the request clean. Select or ask for “refund to original form of payment.” If you booked through a travel agent or online travel agency, check who appears as the merchant on your card or bank statement and use that route too.
9. If you must self-book another flight, do it deliberately. Check nearby airports you can realistically reach, save screenshots showing the airline had no workable option, and keep every receipt. Extra costs are not automatically reimbursed, but the record may help with the airline, insurance, or your card provider later.
10. Write one simple timeline note. Include flight number, date, cancellation time, what was offered, who you spoke to, screenshots saved, and costs for food, hotel, transport, or bags.
11. Use DOT resources after the immediate airport problem is stable. Check the cancellation and delay dashboard for your airline’s public commitments, and consider a DOT complaint if the airline or ticket agent does not resolve a refund or service issue after you have tried their complaint route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove fault at the airport.
- You do not need to write a long complaint right now.
- You do not need to decide now about insurance, a card dispute, or further claims.
- You do not need to settle every cost question before you have a safe place to wait and a written rebooking or refund position.

## Important reassurance
A sudden cancellation with no clear rebooking option can make the system feel unusable. The next useful move is narrow: get the airline’s offer in writing, choose rebooking or refund, and preserve enough proof that you are not relying on memory later.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the airport moment only. Later decisions about insurance, card disputes, complex itineraries, international flights, baggage claims, or formal complaints may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, travel, or professional advice. Airline policies and disruption handling vary, and official resources or the airline’s written position should be checked for your specific ticket and situation.
