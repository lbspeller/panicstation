# What to do if… you have a fever and neck stiffness or sensitivity to light begins (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a medical emergency: **go to the nearest Emergency Room immediately** or **call 911** if you are alone, confused, or symptoms are progressing rapidly.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for a rash to appear before seeking help.
- Do not take fever-reducing medications (acetaminophen or ibuprofen) as they can mask clinical signs needed for diagnosis.
- Do not eat or drink anything (remain NPO) in case an emergency procedure or surgery is required.
- Do not sleep or allow the person to sleep until they have been evaluated by a clinician.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel confused, dizzy, or have a severe headache.

## What to do now
1. **Get to an Emergency Room immediately.**
   - **Call 911** if there is altered mental status (confusion), extreme lethargy, trouble breathing, or if a safe ride is unavailable.
   - **Tell the dispatcher or triage nurse** exactly this: "I have a fever with neck stiffness and sensitivity to light, and I am concerned about meningitis."

2. **Check for a non-blanching rash.**
   - **Look for small red/purple spots** or larger bruise-like marks on the skin.
   - **Perform the "glass test"** by pressing a clear drinking glass firmly against the rash.
   - **Alert medical staff immediately** if the spots do not fade (stay visible) under the pressure of the glass.

3. **Identify signs of sepsis (bloodstream infection).**
   - **Check for cold hands and feet** despite having a high fever.
   - **Monitor for rapid breathing** or severe, unexplained muscle and joint pain.
   - **Note any shivering** or mottled/pale skin.

4. **Observe for atypical signs in vulnerable groups.**
   - **Watch for a bulging fontanelle** (soft spot on the head), poor feeding, or a high-pitched cry in infants.
   - **Identify confusion or lethargy** as the primary sign in elderly patients, who may not always present with a high fever.

5. **Prepare critical information for the medical team.**
   - **List the exact time** symptoms began and how quickly they changed.
   - **Document recent infections**, recent travel, or close contact with someone who was seriously ill.
   - **Keep a list of current medications** and known allergies ready to provide upon arrival.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause (viral vs. bacterial) yourself; this requires a hospital lumbar puncture.
- You do not need to notify employer or school contacts until after you are under medical supervision.
- You do not need to pack a bag; immediate arrival at the ER is the priority.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel startled or overwhelmed when these symptoms appear suddenly. Physiological reactions like a racing heart, shaking, or difficulty focusing are common responses to acute physical stress. Seeking immediate professional evaluation is the standard, safest protocol for this specific combination of symptoms.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency navigation only. It is not a diagnostic tool.

## Important note
This information is for emergency orientation and does not constitute medical advice or a clinical diagnosis. Bacterial meningitis can be fatal within hours; if you suspect it, seek emergency care immediately.
