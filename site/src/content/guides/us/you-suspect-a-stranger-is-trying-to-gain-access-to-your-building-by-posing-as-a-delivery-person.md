# What to do if… you suspect a stranger is trying to gain access to your building by posing as a delivery person (USA)

## Short answer
Do not buzz them in, open the door, or share an entry code. Keep the barrier in place, verify only through routes you already trust, and call 911 if someone is forcing entry, threatening anyone, or you need immediate police, fire, or medical help.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t let someone in because they say they are delivering to “another unit,” “the office,” or “someone upstairs.”
- Don’t share gate codes, entry codes, lockbox codes, or use your fob or key for a stranger.
- Don’t step into the hallway, lobby, stairwell, or parking area to meet them if you feel unsure.
- Don’t argue through the door or try to prove they are lying.
- Don’t trust a link, QR code, phone number, or tracking page they provide as proof.
- Don’t rely on a uniform, vest, badge, box, or clipboard by itself.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Lock your door, step back, and keep the door, lobby door, gate, or intercom barrier closed. If you are at a callbox, do not buzz them in while you are unsure.

2. **Use a short script from behind the barrier.** Say: “I can’t let you in. Please contact the recipient, ring the correct unit, or use the building’s delivery process.” You do not need to explain or negotiate.

3. **Ask only simple identifying questions if it feels safe.** Through the closed door, peephole, camera, or intercom, ask who the delivery is for, which unit, and which company they say they represent. Do not open the door to check ID.

4. **Send them to the proper building route.** If your building has a front desk, concierge, security desk, leasing office, package room, or package locker, tell them to use that route. If they insist that you personally have to let them in, end the conversation.

5. **Verify independently only if you can do it quickly.** Use the retailer, delivery company app, or website you already use. Do not use a link, QR code, or phone number the person gives you at the door or by text.

6. **Tell someone who can act for the building.** Contact the front desk, building security, property manager, landlord, or a trusted neighbor and say there is a suspicious person trying to gain entry as a delivery worker. Give the time, entrance, description, and what they said.

7. **Escalate if the risk changes.** Call 911 if they try to force the door, threaten you, follow someone inside, block an exit, damage a lock, or you believe an emergency is happening. If it is suspicious but not an emergency, use your local police department’s non-emergency route and notify building management.

8. **If they claim to be USPS or you suspect mail-related impersonation, keep the same boundary.** You can ask them to show ID without opening the door, but you can still decline entry and ask them to leave a notice or follow the building’s delivery process. Report suspected mail theft, postal impersonation, or mail-related fraud to the U.S. Postal Inspection Service.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide right now whether it was definitely a scam.
- You don’t need to confront them, follow them, film them up close, or try to catch them.
- You don’t need to solve building-wide security while they are still there.
- Door repairs, camera review, resident notices, and policy changes can wait until you are safe and the immediate situation has ended.
- If you clicked a delivery link or entered payment details, handle that after you are safe by using verified bank, card, retailer, or account routes.

## Important reassurance
Feeling awkward does not mean you are being rude. In a secured building, refusing unknown entry and using the official delivery process is a reasonable safety-first default.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to prevent unsafe entry and stabilize the moment. Later decisions about reports, building access rules, locks, cameras, or insurance may need help from building management, local authorities, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, prioritize getting to safety and contacting emergency services.
