# What to do if… an infant under three months has a fever or feels unusually sleepy (USA)

## Short answer
Seek immediate medical evaluation if an infant under three months has a rectal temperature of **100.4°F (38°C) or higher** or appears **unusually sleepy**. Call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room if the baby is struggling to breathe, is floppy, or cannot be awakened.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if the fever or sleepiness improves on its own.
- Do not give any fever-reducing medication (acetaminophen / Tylenol or ibuprofen / Motrin) until a clinician has evaluated the baby.
- Do not use ice baths, cold water, or rubbing alcohol to cool the baby.
- Do not rely on "feeling" the forehead or using temporal / forehead strips for accuracy.
- Do not give aspirin to an infant.
- Do not overdress or bundle the baby in heavy blankets.
- Do not force the baby to feed if they are struggling to breathe or are semi-conscious.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate emergency triggers requiring 911 or an emergency room.** Go to the ER immediately if you observe:
   - **Difficulty breathing** (grunting sounds with breaths, nostrils flaring, or skin pulling in around the ribs).
   - **Skin color changes** (lips or skin looking blue, pale, gray, or mottled / blotchy).
   - **Extreme lethargy** (the baby will not wake up, or if they do wake, they cannot stay awake or appear very limp / floppy).
   - **Seizures** or rhythmic jerking movements.
2. **Confirm the temperature using a rectal thermometer if available.** **A rectal temperature is the standard for accuracy in infants under three months.**
   - If the thermometer reads 100.4°F (38°C) or higher, it is a fever.
   - If you cannot take a rectal temperature, use whatever method you have and tell the clinician exactly how it was measured.
3. **Contact your pediatrician or an after-hours nurse line immediately.** **Explain that the infant is under three months old and provide the exact temperature reading.**
   - If you cannot reach your pediatrician within minutes, proceed directly to the nearest pediatric emergency room.
4. **Prepare the baby for transport.** **Dress the baby in a single layer of light clothing to prevent overheating.**
   - Gather your diaper bag, any current medications, and insurance information, but do not delay departure for more than a few minutes.
5. **Monitor for other symptoms while waiting for care.** **Note the time and nature of any specific changes.**
   - Observe if the baby has a new rash, is vomiting, or has had fewer wet diapers than usual.
   - Record the time of the last feeding and how much the baby consumed.
6. **Be ready to provide a 60-second summary to the medical team.** **Write down or memorize these facts:**
   - The baby's exact age in weeks or days.
   - The highest temperature recorded and how it was taken.
   - Any recent vaccinations or known medical conditions.
   - Specific behavior changes (e.g., "too sleepy to feed," "weak cry").

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause of the illness yourself.
- You do not need to pack a full bag; priority is rapid medical assessment.
- You do not need to decide on specific tests or treatments before arriving at the clinic or hospital.
- You do not need to worry about "breaking" the fever at home.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant concern when a young infant is unwell. Medical professionals prioritize infants in this age group because they require specialized assessment. Seeking help immediately is the most effective action you can take to ensure the baby's safety.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions only. It does not replace the diagnostic evaluation of a licensed medical professional.

## Important note
In infants under three months, a fever can be the only sign of a serious infection. Clinical protocols in the USA often require blood, urine, or other tests for infants in this age group with a fever of 100.4°F or higher. Always follow the specific instructions provided by your healthcare provider or emergency personnel.
