# What to do if… someone has reposted your photos with your location without your consent (USA)

## Short answer
Prioritize physical safety: stop any real-time location sharing and get to a safer pause if the post reveals where you are or where you regularly go. Save evidence, report the posts, and call 911 if you believe there is immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the poster publicly or negotiate in comments; it can escalate and spread your location further.
- Do not repost the content to call it out; it can amplify the location.
- Do not delete everything immediately if you may want help from a platform, school, workplace, victim advocate, or law enforcement; save evidence first.
- Do not click unknown links, open files, or enter login details from messages connected to the poster.
- Do not go alone to a predictable place if your home, workplace, school, route, or current location has been exposed.
- Do not assume you have to handle this by yourself.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If the repost reveals where you are right now, or you fear someone could show up, move to a public place or be with trusted people. Tell one trusted person what is happening and where you are.
2. **Stop live location exposure.**
   - Turn off live location sharing in messaging, map, family-tracking, and device-finding apps.
   - Turn off map-style sharing in social apps.
   - Pause new stories, check-ins, and location-tagged posts until you have checked settings.
3. **Preserve evidence before reporting or removing anything.**
   - Screenshot the reposts showing the account name, date or time, location tag, caption, and any threats or identifying details.
   - Copy the post links and profile links into a note.
   - If posts may disappear, record your screen while you show the post, the account, and the location detail.
4. **Make a short incident log.** Write down the date and time you noticed it, the platform, the account name, what location was revealed, whether it was home, work, school, a venue, or your current location, and any messages or threats.
5. **Report the post and account to the platform.**
   - Report the specific post as doxing, harassment, sharing personal information, stalking, or non-consensual sharing, depending on the platform’s choices.
   - In the free-text box, say the post shares your location without consent and you are concerned for safety.
6. **Limit who can keep spreading it.**
   - Change the audience on recent posts that show your home, workplace, school, routine, street signs, license plates, badges, or distinctive landmarks.
   - Turn off photo location tagging for new pictures.
   - Review tagging, mention, share, duet, stitch, remix, and message settings on the affected platforms.
7. **Lock down accounts that could be involved.**
   - Change the password for your email first, then your social and photo accounts.
   - Turn on multi-factor authentication.
   - Sign out of other sessions or devices you do not recognize.
8. **Reduce real-world risk for the next day.**
   - If your home, work, or school is identified, consider staying with someone else, changing your route or timing, and having someone accompany you.
   - Ask your workplace, school, building desk, or venue staff not to confirm your schedule, location, or contact details to anyone.
9. **Contact law enforcement if you feel unsafe, threatened, or targeted.**
   - Call 911 if someone is nearby, you believe there is an immediate threat, or you are afraid to go home.
   - If it is not an emergency, contact your local police department’s non-emergency line and ask how to make a report. Share your screenshots, links, and short log.
10. **Consider an IC3 report if this is cyber-enabled harassment or account compromise.** IC3 accepts reports about cyber-enabled crime, and you can file even if you are unsure whether it qualifies.
11. **Use confidential victim support if you need help deciding next steps.** VictimConnect can help with support and referrals by phone or text at 855-4-VICTIM (855-484-2846) or by online chat.
12. **If you are under 18, involve a safe adult now.** Tell a parent, guardian, school staff member, or another trusted adult so you are not handling the exposure alone.

## What can wait
- A full audit of every account and every old post can wait; first stop the location exposure and save evidence.
- Deciding whether to pursue legal or court options can wait unless you are in immediate danger or a qualified professional tells you something is urgent.
- Trying to prove the poster’s motive can wait.
- Data-broker removals, privacy cleanups, and long-term account rebuilding can wait until you are safer and less overwhelmed.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaken makes sense. Having your location shared without consent can make ordinary places feel unsafe, and taking simple containment steps is a reasonable response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for immediate safety, containment, documentation, and support. Later decisions may need help from a victim advocate, platform safety team, school or workplace safety staff, law enforcement, or a lawyer.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel in danger, prioritize safety and use emergency services.
