# What to do if… you notice bricks, stone, or render falling from a building and fear more could come down (USA)

## Short answer
Move well away from the building and keep others out from under the affected area. Call 911 now if debris is still falling, someone may be hurt, people are passing underneath, or the building looks unstable.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stand under the façade to check where the debris came from.
- Do not walk into the drop zone to move bricks, stones, stucco, plaster, or render.
- Do not pull at loose material or try a quick patch.
- Do not send people through the affected entrance, sidewalk, balcony area, or overhang if there is a safer route.
- Do not assume it is safe because the falling has paused.
- Do not rely on posting online instead of making an official report.

## What to do now
1. **Get out of the drop zone.** Move across the street if you can, or at least well beyond where more pieces could land. Stay out from directly under the wall, parapet, cornice, balcony, overhang, sign, or entrance canopy.

2. **Keep other people back without putting yourself underneath it.** From a safe place, tell nearby people not to walk under the affected area. If a shop, lobby, or building entrance is involved, ask staff or building occupants to use a different entrance if one is safely available.

3. **Call 911 if there is any immediate danger.** Call if debris is actively falling, someone is injured, people are still walking underneath, the sidewalk or road is affected, or you hear cracking, shifting, or other signs that more material may come down.

4. **If it is not an immediate 911 situation, report it to the local authority.** Contact your local building department, code enforcement office, inspections department, or city or county non-emergency route. If your area uses 311, 311 may be the right route for a hazardous building or falling-debris report.

5. **Give clear location details.** Say the exact address or nearest cross streets, which side of the building is affected, what is falling, where it landed, whether it is still falling, and whether the sidewalk, entrance, parking area, or road is being used by people.

6. **If you live or work there, reduce exposure from a safe place.** Keep away from windows, doors, balconies, and rooms directly under the affected exterior area if you can do that without going near the façade. Use another exit only if it is clearly away from the falling material.

7. **Notify the responsible person after emergency safety is handled.** Contact the landlord, property manager, building management, condo or HOA contact, or business manager. Keep the message simple: falling exterior material, exact location, whether emergency services or local authorities were called.

8. **Document only from a safe distance.** If you can do it without approaching the building, take a photo or video from well back and note the time. Do not step into the debris area for a better angle.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether the building is safe to occupy long term.
- You do not need to clean up debris in the drop zone.
- You do not need to argue about who caused it or who pays for repairs.
- You do not need to choose a contractor, engineer, lawyer, insurer, or housing plan in the first minutes.
- You do not need to touch or save pieces of debris if doing so puts you under the affected area.

## Important reassurance
You are not overreacting by moving back and reporting it. Small pieces falling from height can still hurt someone, and the safest first step is to keep people away until the right local responders or inspectors can assess it.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Building safety, access restrictions, repairs, insurance, enforcement, and housing decisions may need help from local officials, building management, a qualified building professional, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not engineering, legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If there is any chance someone could be struck or the structure could be unstable, call 911 and keep people back.
