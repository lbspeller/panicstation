# What to do if… a buy-now-pay-later instalment is about to retry and additional fees are threatened (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the Buy-Now-Pay-Later (BNPL) provider immediately through their app or website to request a payment reschedule or fee waiver before the automated retry occurs. If the payment is being pulled via ACH (bank transfer), you can also notify your bank to stop the payment at least three business days in advance.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait for the retry to fail; repeated automated attempts can trigger both BNPL late fees and bank-side non-sufficient funds (NSF) fees.
- Don’t prioritize a BNPL payment over essential survival costs like rent, utilities, food, or necessary medication.
- Don’t assume that deleting the app or removing a card from the app will successfully stop an already-initiated retry process.
- Don’t agree to a "repayment plan" that you know is mathematically impossible for you to meet.
- Don’t rely on a verbal promise from a customer service agent without a confirming email, chat transcript, or in-app notification.

## What to do now
1. **Document the exact details of the upcoming retry and the threatened fee.** 
   - Identify the **date and time** of the next attempt and the **specific dollar amount** of the fee they intend to charge. 
   - Take a screenshot of the payment schedule and any "payment failed" or "retry scheduled" notifications you have received.

2. **Check the app for a "Reschedule" or "Change Due Date" button immediately.** 
   - Most major providers (such as Klarna, Afterpay, or Zip) allow one free payment move per order if requested before the due date. 
   - If the button is greyed out or missing, move immediately to contacting their support team.

3. **Contact the BNPL company to request a manual hold on retries and a fee waiver.** 
   - Use the in-app chat or phone support and state clearly that you are experiencing financial hardship. 
   - Use this script: "I cannot make the upcoming instalment. I am requesting that you stop all retries until [Date] and waive any late fees associated with this delay." 
   - Save the transcript or get a confirmation number for the request.

4. **Notify your bank or credit union to stop the payment if the retry is via ACH.** 
   - **Federal Regulation E** gives you the right to stop a preauthorized transfer by notifying your bank at least **three business days** before the scheduled transfer. 
   - If your bank takes an oral stop-payment order, you must usually provide a **written confirmation** within 14 days if they request it to keep the stop-payment active. 
   - Note that stopping the payment at the bank level does not cancel the debt; you will still owe the BNPL provider, but it prevents an immediate overdraft.

5. **Revoke authorization for future debits in writing to the BNPL provider.** 
   - Send a message through the provider’s official support channel stating: "I am revoking my authorization for [Company Name] to debit my account [Last 4 digits of account] for all future payments." 
   - This provides a legal basis to dispute any "retry" that happens after your revocation.

6. **Pause the payment flow if the issue is a return or a merchant dispute.** 
   - If you have already returned the item, select the "Report a Return" option in the BNPL app. 
   - Provide the return tracking number or receipt immediately to trigger an automatic "payment pause" while the merchant processes the refund.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to close your bank account or stop using BNPL services entirely.
- You do not need to provide a detailed personal history or "proof" of hardship in the first 24 hours unless the provider specifically asks for it to approve a long-term plan.
- You do not need to worry about your credit score in the next few minutes; most "Pay in 4" providers in the USA do not report minor late payments to credit bureaus immediately, though some (like Affirm or monthly financing plans) may do so after 30 days.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a "retry" notification can feel like a loss of control, but you have specific rights under U.S. banking laws to stop electronic transfers. Acting before the money leaves your account is a standard financial management step, and most BNPL providers prefer a rescheduled payment over a total default.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for an imminent payment retry. It does not cover long-term debt management, credit repair, or state-specific licensing disputes (such as those under the New York BNPL Act).

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal or financial advice. Federal BNPL-specific protections were largely rescinded in 2025; however, general protections under the Electronic Fund Transfer Act (Regulation E) still apply to bank transfers. State laws, particularly in New York and California, may provide additional protections regarding late fees and disclosures.
