# What to do if… your partner insists on controlling your contraception, appointments, or medication in a way that feels coercive (USA)

## Short answer
Seek a private moment to contact a confidential hotline or a Title X-funded clinic using a device your partner cannot monitor. Request "confidential communications" under HIPAA to ensure your care, billing, and reminders are handled through a safe, alternative contact method.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront your partner about contraceptive tampering or monitoring if you believe it will escalate your immediate physical danger.
- Do not use a phone, computer, or patient portal that your partner has access to or monitors.
- Do not leave physical evidence of your search—such as brochures, clinic business cards, or browser history—in shared spaces or trash cans.
- Do not stop or change essential medications suddenly without medical supervision, even if pressured, unless the medication itself has been tampered with.
- Do not assume you must involve the police or leave the relationship today; focus first on establishing a private line of communication with a healthcare professional.

## What to do now
1. **Find a safe pause and a private device.**
   - Use a trusted friend’s phone, a public library computer, or a work device if you suspect your personal phone is monitored.
   - If you must use your own device, use "Incognito" or "Private" mode and close all tabs immediately after use.
2. **If you are in immediate physical danger, call 911.**
   - If it is unsafe to speak, some jurisdictions allow you to text 911; otherwise, try to reach a public location.
3. **Contact a confidential relationship abuse advocate.**
   - **National Domestic Violence Hotline:** Call **1-800-799-SAFE (7233)**, text **START** to **88788**, or use the secure web chat.
   - **DoD Safe Helpline (for military-affiliated):** Call **877-995-5247**.
4. **Access confidential sexual assault support if needed.**
   - **RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline:** Call **800-656-HOPE (4673)** or text **HOPE** to **64673**.
5. **Contact a clinic and request a "Confidential Communication" visit.**
   - Search for a **Title X-funded clinic** or **Planned Parenthood**; these facilities are specifically trained in handling reproductive coercion and privacy.
   - When calling, state: "I need an appointment where I can be seen alone, and I need to set up confidential communication because my partner monitors my mail and phone."
6. **Invoke your HIPAA rights regarding alternative contact.**
   - Explicitly tell the clinic: "Under **HIPAA (45 CFR 164.522)**, I am requesting that you communicate with me only at [Safe Number/Address] because disclosure of my medical information could endanger me."
   - Request that **Explanation of Benefits (EOB)** forms or billing statements be sent to a safe address or withheld from the primary insurance policyholder if possible.
7. **Identify interference methods to the clinician.**
   - **Boldly state the specific behavior:** "My partner hides my pills," "My partner damages condoms," or "My partner insists on being in the room."
   - Ask for "stealth" contraception options, such as an IUD (with strings trimmed) or an injectable, which are harder for a partner to detect or sabotage.
8. **Create a minimal 24-hour safety plan.**
   - Identify one person you can trust, one place you can go if things escalate, and one way to reach help without using your primary phone.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to end the relationship or file a legal report right now.
- You do not need to have "proof" of coercion; your feeling that something is wrong is sufficient to seek help.
- You do not need to figure out how to pay for long-term care today; Title X clinics offer sliding-scale fees and can assist with privacy-protected billing.

## Important reassurance
If you feel like your choices regarding your body or health are being managed or blocked by someone else, that feeling is valid. Reproductive coercion is a recognized form of abuse, and feeling confused, hesitant, or "not sure if it's serious enough" is a normal response to this type of control. You have a legal and ethical right to private medical care.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only, focused on immediate safety and establishing private healthcare access. Later decisions regarding legal action or relationship changes should be made with the support of a trained advocate or specialist.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or medical advice. Because privacy laws and Title X funding rules are subject to change (including significant updates in early 2026), always verify the current privacy protocols with your specific healthcare provider at the start of your visit.
