# What to do if… your plug-in hybrid enters a fault mode and power is reduced after you have pulled over safely (USA)

## Short answer
Secure the vehicle with hazards and parking brake, evacuate if you detect odors or heat, and request a flatbed tow to avoid damaging the electric drive system.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attempt to merge back into high-speed traffic to test if the power has returned.
- Don’t touch any orange-colored cables, connectors, or high-voltage battery covers.
- Don’t attempt to "check the fluids" or perform repairs if you are on a highway shoulder or in a high-traffic area.
- Don’t ignore sensory cues like gurgling, popping, or eye/throat irritation near the vehicle.
- Don’t allow a tow operator to tow the vehicle with the drive wheels on the ground unless the owner's manual explicitly permits it.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the vehicle immediately to trigger Move Over law protections.** **Shift the vehicle into Park, set the parking brake, and activate your hazard lights to signal to passing drivers that you are a protected disabled vehicle.**
2. **Evaluate your immediate surroundings for secondary hazards.** **If you are on a high-speed road with narrow shoulders, remain buckled inside the vehicle unless there is a fire risk; if you must exit, move to a position upwind, uphill, and well behind a safety barrier.**
3. **Monitor the vehicle for signs of a high-voltage battery event.** **Watch for smoke, hissing, popping sounds, or a strong chemical smell, and evacuate the area immediately if you experience eye, nose, or throat irritation.**
4. **Perform exactly one power cycle if no critical hazards are present.** **Turn the vehicle completely off for at least 60 seconds and then restart it once; if the "Reduced Power" or "Hybrid System Fault" message persists, do not attempt to drive the vehicle.**
5. **Request a flatbed tow from your roadside service provider.** **Inform the dispatcher specifically that the vehicle is a "Plug-in Hybrid" and requires a flatbed to prevent unpowered rotation of the electric motors, which can cause internal damage.**
6. **Provide critical information to emergency responders or the tow operator.** **Alert them to any specific dashboard messages, such as "Stop Safely Now" or "High Voltage Battery Overheat," and point out the location of the charging port if requested.**
7. **Maintain a safe distance from the vehicle's high-voltage components while waiting.** **Keep yourself and any passengers away from the underbody and the rear or center of the vehicle where the high-voltage battery pack is typically located.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the specific diagnostic trouble code (DTC) or cause of the fault right now.
- You do not need to decide which repair shop will handle the long-term fix while you are still on the roadside.
- You do not need to worry about the state of charge or whether the battery is "ruined" in this moment.

## Important reassurance
Reduced power modes, often called "limp mode" or "turtle mode," are intentional safety protocols designed to prevent catastrophic hardware failure. By pulling over and stopping, you have already completed the most important step in preventing an escalation of the fault.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction instructions for the immediate aftermath of a PHEV power reduction. It does not replace professional mechanical diagnosis or emergency services protocols.

## Important note
High-voltage vehicle systems carry a risk of electric shock and chemical exposure if damaged. If you see sparks, flames, or persistent smoke, contact 911 immediately. Roadside safety is subject to local traffic conditions and state-specific "Move Over" laws.
