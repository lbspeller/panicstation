# What to do if… someone says they will tell others you consented unless you stay silent (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all engagement with the person making the threat and reach a safer physical location. Contact a confidential sexual assault service to discuss safety planning and reporting options before taking further action.

## Do not do these things
- Do not promise to stay silent or agree to any demands in an attempt to stop the threats.
- Do not pay money, provide more images, or perform any acts requested by the person.
- Do not delete messages, call logs, or digital evidence, even if they are distressing to look at.
- Do not attempt to negotiate, explain your side, or convince the person to stop.
- Do not meet the person in private or in person to resolve the situation.
- Do not post about the threat on social media while the situation is active.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** If you are in immediate physical danger or the person is outside your location, call **911** or local emergency services.
2. **Cease all communication immediately.** Stop replying to texts, emails, or direct messages. If you must use your phone, block the individual after taking necessary screenshots.
3. **Capture and secure evidence.** Screenshot the threat, the timestamp, the account profile, and the specific platform used.
   - **Store copies securely.** Save these to a private cloud drive or a new email account that the person cannot access.
4. **Utilize image protection tools if applicable.** If the threat involves the sharing of intimate images or videos, use specialized tools to block their distribution.
   - **For adults (18+):** Use **StopNCII.org** to create digital fingerprints (hashes) of the images so participating platforms can proactively block them.
   - **For content involving minors:** Use the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children (NCMEC) **Take It Down** tool.
5. **Contact a confidential specialist.** Reach out to **RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-HOPE (4673)** or use their online chat service. They provide 24 / 7 support and can connect you with local advocates who understand state-specific coercion laws.
6. **Report cyber-enabled extortion.** If the threat occurs online, file a report with the **FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3)**. Use the specific "Sextortion" or "Extortion" categories if prompted.
7. **Consult local law enforcement.** If you wish to pursue criminal charges for harassment, stalking, or extortion, contact your local police department’s non-emergency line. Provide the saved evidence and specify that you are being coerced into silence.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a formal police report today.
- You do not need to figure out the legal definition of what happened before seeking help.
- You do not need to explain the situation to friends, family, or your employer right now.
- You do not need to have a "perfect" or complete record of every interaction to receive support.

## Important reassurance
Threats and coercion are used to create a sense of isolation and powerlessness. Feeling panicked, frozen, or overwhelmed is a common response to this type of intimidation. These reactions are not a sign of weakness or fault. There are professional systems designed specifically to handle these types of threats and help you navigate the next steps safely.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate safety and evidence preservation. It does not replace legal advice or long-term therapeutic support.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding coercion and sexual assault vary by state. If any step in this guide feels unsafe in your specific situation, prioritize your immediate physical safety and contact a confidential advocate or emergency services.
