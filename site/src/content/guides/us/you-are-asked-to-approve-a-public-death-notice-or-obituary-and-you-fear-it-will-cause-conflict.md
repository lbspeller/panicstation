# What to do if… you are asked to approve a public death notice or obituary and you fear it will cause conflict (USA)

## Short answer
Ask for a pause before anything is published. Suggest a short, neutral notice that leaves out contested relationship wording, unnecessary personal details, and any survivor list that is likely to trigger conflict.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t approve a draft you have not read carefully.
- Don’t include accusations, coded insults, or disputed explanations of what happened.
- Don’t publish a home address, full birthdate, middle name, birthplace, mother’s maiden name, personal phone number, or personal email address.
- Don’t try to settle long-running family disputes in a large message thread.
- Don’t assume an ordinary obituary must be public. Ask whether this is an obituary, a family announcement, or a notice connected with a formal process.

## What to do now
1. **Ask where the notice will appear and when a decision is actually needed.**  
   Check whether it is for a newspaper, funeral home website, online obituary page, or social media post. Ask for a brief pause before publication.

2. **Ask what approval is being requested.**  
   Ask the funeral home, publisher, or person managing the notice whether they need approval from you, edits from you, or only a response by a deadline. If someone describes it as a legal or formal notice, ask what process it relates to. If there is a dispute about who has authority to approve wording, do not try to resolve it in the draft itself.

3. **Offer a minimum neutral version.**  
   Start with the deceased person’s name. Add only the details that are needed and safe to publish, such as age, a general location, date of death, or public service details. Leave out disputed family descriptions and explanations.

4. **Remove the survivor list if names or relationships are causing conflict.**  
   The first public notice does not need a survivor line. A general sentence such as “Remembered with care” may be used only if it will not restart the dispute.

5. **Use one contact person for edits.**  
   Ask the funeral home or publisher to take the final draft from one agreed contact. Ask whether visibility or comment settings can be limited if the notice will appear online.

6. **Reduce privacy and scam risks.**  
   Do not publish a home address or details that suggest a home will be empty. If public service details are needed, use the venue address. If someone asks for money or personal information, contact the funeral home directly using a number you already know is real or a number from existing funeral-home paperwork.

7. **Keep a copy of the final text.**  
   Save the version that was approved and the date it was sent.

8. **If threats, harassment, or stalking are involved, stop negotiating.**  
   Keep the messages or voicemails and consider contacting local law enforcement. In an immediate emergency, call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve family hierarchy or old relationship disputes now.
- You do not need to produce a full tribute now. A brief notice can be followed by a longer tribute later or shared privately.
- You do not need to settle a disagreement about legal authority while people are arguing. That can be addressed separately with specialist help.

## Important reassurance
Grief can bring confusion, anger, sadness, and strong reactions to public wording. Choosing a short, neutral, privacy-protecting notice is a reasonable way to prevent an irreversible publication while people have time to think.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce conflict and avoid a regretted public notice. A serious dispute about authority, intimidation, or ongoing harassment may need specialist legal or safety support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Requirements and policies vary by state, publisher, funeral home, and platform. Ask the organisation publishing the notice what it requires and get independent legal advice if authority remains disputed.
