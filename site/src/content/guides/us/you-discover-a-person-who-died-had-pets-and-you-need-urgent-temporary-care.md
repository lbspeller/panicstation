# What to do if… you discover a person who died had pets and you need urgent temporary care (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the pets with water and basic care, then immediately contact the estate executor, administrator, or local animal control to authorize and document a lawful temporary care plan.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t leave pets without checking water, ventilation, and safe confinement, even if the situation appears stable.
- Don’t force entry to a private residence or violate lease agreements to access animals; use official channels for a welfare entry.
- Don’t give pets away informally or rehome them via social media without legal documentation from the estate.
- Don’t assume you have legal ownership because you are providing care; pets are considered personal property under US law.
- Don’t attempt to handle or corner a frightened or aggressive animal; wait for animal control or professional help.
- Don’t administer any human medications or leftover prescription pet meds unless specifically directed by a licensed veterinarian.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the animals immediately.**  
   **Place each pet in a separate, safe enclosed room or a secure crate/carrier to prevent escapes through open doors.** Provide fresh water immediately. If their specific food is visible and accessible, offer a small portion.

2. **Search for emergency pet alert cards and contact info.**  
   **Check the deceased person’s wallet for an "Emergency Pet Alert" card or look for notices on the refrigerator or inside the front/back doors.** These often list designated emergency caregivers, the vet’s name, and specific medical needs.

3. **Identify the legal decision-maker for the estate.**  
   **Contact the named executor or the court-appointed administrator to get authorization for the pets' removal and care.** If there is no will or the executor is unknown, contact the immediate next of kin, but be aware that only the estate representative typically has the legal authority to dispose of "property" (pets).

4. **Verify the existence of a Pet Trust.**  
   **Look through the person’s estate planning documents for a "Pet Trust" or "Letter of Instruction."** Under the laws of all 50 states (as of 2026), a Pet Trust may name a specific "Trustee" or "Enforcer" who has the legal duty and funds to manage the animal’s care.

5. **Establish a legal welfare entry if the home is locked.**  
   **If you cannot gain entry and believe the pets are in immediate danger, call the local police non-emergency line or animal control to request a welfare check.** Do not break windows or pick locks, as this can lead to liability or criminal charges.

6. **Screen for urgent medical emergencies.**  
   **If a pet is non-responsive, bleeding, struggling to breathe, or showing signs of extreme heat/cold stress, contact a 24-hour emergency veterinary hospital.** Inform them the owner is deceased and you are a temporary responder.

7. **Contact local animal control to document the situation.**  
   **Notify the municipal animal services or the local humane society that you have found pets belonging to a deceased owner.** Even if you intend to house them temporarily, reporting the pets' location creates a necessary legal trail for the estate.

8. **Have the pets scanned for microchips.**  
   **Take the animals to a veterinary clinic or shelter to check for a microchip.** If a chip is found, the registry can provide contact details that may lead to a designated backup caregiver or co-owner.

9. **Keep a detailed log of all actions and expenses.**  
   **Record the date and time of discovery, the condition of the pets, all food/water provided, and any money spent on supplies or vet care.** Share this log with the executor to ensure the pets’ history is preserved and to request reimbursement from the estate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on permanent rehoming or adoption today.
- You do not need to settle the deceased person’s wider estate or financial affairs.
- You do not need to clean the property beyond what is required for animal safety.
- You do not need to contact multiple breed-specific rescues until the executor has authorized a long-term plan.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel uncertain when managing animals that do not belong to you, particularly during a crisis. Focusing on immediate containment, hydration, and contacting the authorities (animal control or the executor) fulfills the most critical welfare and legal requirements in the first hours.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only for the initial 24–48 hours. Long-term placement, ownership transfer, and estate reimbursement are governed by state probate laws and may require legal assistance.

## Important note
Pets are legally classified as personal property in the USA. This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal or veterinary advice. If an animal is aggressive or you cannot safely manage the environment, withdraw and wait for animal control or professional responders.
