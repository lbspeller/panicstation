# What to do if… a hospital or facility says belongings will be disposed of unless collected within 24 hours after a death (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the facility to pause any disposal while pickup is arranged. Ask for confirmation in writing if possible, the current storage location, an itemized list or property receipt if available, and the written policy behind the deadline.

## Do not do these things
- Do not share passwords, phone unlock codes, or verification codes.
- Do not assume that the first person you speak to has physical custody of the belongings.
- Do not send someone to collect valuables until the facility confirms whether that person is allowed to collect them and what identification or documents are required.
- Do not spend the first call arguing about the estate. Focus on securing the items, finding out where they are, and recording the deadline.
- Do not treat a verbal confirmation as useless. Write down the name, role, date, time, and exact wording while asking for written confirmation if possible.

## What to do now
1. Call the facility and ask for the office that handles belongings after a death. If the correct office is unclear, ask the operator for the supervisor on duty or Patient Relations if available.
2. Say: “I am asking for a temporary hold on any disposal while pickup is arranged. Please tell me the deadline, where the belongings are stored now, and how I can receive written confirmation if possible.”
3. Ask for an itemized list or property receipt if one is available. Specifically ask about the phone, wallet, keys, identification, payment cards, cash, jewelry, documents, glasses, hearing aids, and any bags or clothing.
4. Ask whether the belongings are still at the facility or have been transferred to another custodian, such as a funeral home, medical examiner, or coroner. Ask for the current custodian’s contact details if the items have been transferred.
5. Ask what identification or proof of authority is required before anyone travels to collect the belongings. Requirements may vary by facility policy, state rules, the type of item, and who currently has custody.
6. If you cannot collect the belongings within the stated time, ask for the next available pickup appointment and whether another person may collect them. Ask the facility to explain its exact requirements for any representative pickup.
7. If the first contact cannot pause disposal or explain the process, ask for the supervisor on duty or the next available escalation contact. Ask for Patient Relations or a Patient Advocate if the facility has one.
8. Keep a simple written log. Record names, roles, callback numbers, dates, times, the stated deadline, the words used about disposal, the listed items, and where the items are said to be stored.
9. If a wallet, payment card, or identification document is missing rather than merely awaiting pickup, contact the relevant card issuer promptly. Use the FTC resources below if identity information may be at risk.
10. If the facility still cannot account for the items, send a short written complaint to the facility after the immediate calls. If that does not resolve the issue, ask whether your state consumer protection office or state attorney general’s office is an appropriate later contact.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle the estate before asking the facility to secure the belongings.
- You do not need to decide funeral arrangements, close accounts, or complete every death-related task during the first calls.
- You do not need to resolve family disagreements before asking where the belongings are and what is required for a safe pickup.
- Broader administrative tasks can wait until the belongings issue is stabilised. The USAGov death checklist below can help with those later tasks.
- External complaints can usually wait until you have asked for a hold, confirmed the storage location, and written down what happened.

## Important reassurance
A short collection deadline can feel overwhelming after a death. It is reasonable to slow the situation down, ask where the belongings are, request a temporary hold, and take one call at a time. The correct contact and release process may vary, so asking for a clear written explanation is a sensible first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce the risk of belongings being lost or discarded. Later decisions may need help from the facility, a funeral home, a medical examiner or coroner, an estate professional, or another specialist depending on who has custody and what is missing.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Facility policies and state rules vary. Ask the facility what applies to the specific belongings and who currently has custody.
