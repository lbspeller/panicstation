# What to do if… a hospital or facility says belongings will be disposed of unless collected within 24 hours after a death (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the facility immediately to request an **administrative hold on disposal**, identify the **specific location of the property** (nursing unit vs. security safe), and request a **written inventory** of the items they are holding.

## Do not do these things
- Don't accept a verbal "24-hour disposal" threat as a legal finality; hospitals are generally required to treat property as "unclaimed" rather than trash.
- Don't provide digital passcodes or biometric access to facility staff who claim they need them to "verify the owner."
- Don't engage in arguments about inheritance or kinship with front-line staff; focus strictly on the physical security of the items.
- Don't assume all items are in the same place; jewelry and cash are often stored in a different safe than clothing or bags.
- Don't allow the facility to release items to anyone not authorized in writing or by state law.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact department holding the items right now.** **Call the hospital main switchboard and ask to be connected to "Security/Valuables" and "Patient Advocacy" or "Patient Relations."**
   - In VA hospitals, ask specifically for the **Decedent Affairs Coordinator**.
   - In private hospitals, ask for the **Nursing Supervisor** (after hours) or **Patient Relations** (business hours).
2. **Issue an immediate formal request for a property hold.** **Tell the facility: "I am the next of kin/legal representative, and I am requesting an immediate administrative hold on the disposal or release of all personal effects for [Patient Name]."**
   - Ask for the name and employee ID of the person acknowledging this hold.
   - Request that the hold be noted in the patient’s electronic medical record (EMR).
3. **Obtain a copy of the property inventory and current location.** **Ask for a written list of all items currently inventoried, including specific descriptions of "valuables" like rings, watches, or wallets.**
   - Confirm if items are on the **nursing unit**, in a **hospital safe**, or with **Security**.
   - Ask if any items were sent to the **morgue** or **funeral home** with the body.
4. **Request the specific written policy regarding the 24-hour deadline.** **Ask the administrator: "Please provide the written policy or the state statute that authorizes the destruction of personal property within 24 hours of a death."**
   - Most facilities will be unable to provide a legal citation for destruction, as state unclaimed property laws usually require 30 to 90 days of storage.
5. **Verify the required documentation for property release.** **Confirm exactly what you need to bring to collect the items (e.g., photo ID, a copy of the death certificate, or "Letters Testamentary").**
   - If you are not yet the court-appointed executor, ask about "Small Estate" affidavits or next-of-kin release forms provided by the hospital.
6. **Propose a specific, alternative pickup window if the deadline is impossible.** **Provide a date and time within 48–72 hours when you or a designated representative will arrive, and ask for written confirmation that the items will be held until that time.**
7. **Escalate to Risk Management if the facility refuses the hold.** **Tell the switchboard: "I need to speak with the Risk Manager or the Administrator on Call regarding the threatened destruction of a decedent's personal property."**
   - Mention that you are documenting this as a potential "loss of property" claim.
8. **Contact your chosen funeral director if items are "on the body."** **Ask the funeral home to collect and safeguard any jewelry, dentures, or clothing that remains with the decedent during transport.**
   - Funeral directors often sign for these items upon pickup and can hold them for you.

## What can wait
- You do not need a final probate court order just to secure a hold on the items.
- You do not need to settle funeral costs before requesting that the hospital protect the property.
- You do not need to identify every single item that might be missing; focus first on securing what they admit to having.

## Important reassurance
It is common for staff to use the "24-hour" phrase to prevent the accumulation of unclaimed bags in busy nursing units. It is rarely a legal mandate for destruction. By identifying yourself and requesting a hold in writing, you shift the facility’s role from "storage" to "legal custodian," which usually triggers more cautious handling.

## Scope note
This guide addresses the immediate stabilization of property at risk of disposal. It does not cover the legal process of probating an estate or filing a lawsuit for lost property.

## Important note
Hospital policies are subject to state laws regarding bailment and unclaimed property. If a facility claims to have already disposed of items, ask for a "Property Disposition Report." For Medicare patients, if grievances are not resolved, you may contact your state's Quality Improvement Organization (QIO).
