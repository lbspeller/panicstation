# What to do if… you are told not to contact certain colleagues while matters are reviewed but you are not suspended (USA)

## Short answer
Get the reversal in writing and immediately compare your employer's PTO policy against state-specific protected leave laws. Under federal and most state laws, employers can legally revoke discretionary vacation time for business reasons, but they cannot revoke leave protected by FMLA, ADA, or USERRA.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the directive and "just go anyway" without clarification, as this is often classified as a no-call/no-show and grounds for termination.
- Do not cancel your travel bookings or non-refundable tickets until you have completed the internal appeal or HR review process.
- Do not resign, threaten to quit, or send aggressive communications to your manager in the heat of the moment.
- Do not invent a medical or family emergency to force the leave, as falsifying leave reasons is a common cause for immediate firing.
- Do not discuss the dispute on social media or public company channels while the matter is unresolved.

## What to do now
1. **Secure all documentation related to the original approval and the subsequent cancellation.** Save copies of the initial approval email, system screenshots showing the "approved" status, and the message revoking it.
   - Note the exact date and time of the cancellation.
   - Record the specific "business need" cited (e.g., staffing shortage, project deadline, or emergency).
2. **Review your employee handbook, union contract (CBA), or employment agreement for specific revocation clauses.** Look for language regarding "blackout periods," required notice periods for cancellation, or "unforeseen business exigencies."
   - Check if your state (e.g., California, Colorado) classifies accrued vacation as "wages" that cannot be forfeited, even if the timing of use is restricted.
3. **Submit a formal written request to HR for a policy-based explanation of the revocation.** Use a neutral tone and ask for the specific section of the company policy that allows the withdrawal of previously approved time off.
   - State: "My PTO for [dates] was approved on [date]. I was notified on [date] that this is being revoked due to business needs. Please confirm which policy section governs this and what the process is for reimbursement of non-refundable costs."
4. **Determine if the time off qualifies as protected leave separate from standard PTO.** If the leave is for a serious medical condition (self or family), bonding with a child, or military service, business needs are generally not a valid reason for denial.
   - If applicable, tell HR: "This time off is for a [medical / military / family] reason. Please provide the necessary forms for FMLA, USERRA, or state-specific Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFML)."
   - Be aware that as of 2026, states like Washington, Minnesota, and others have expanded PFML protections that may override internal company policies.
5. **Present a formal request for reimbursement if you have incurred non-refundable costs based on the prior approval.** Under the legal principle of "promissory estoppel," an employer may be liable for your financial losses if you reasonably relied on their promise (the approval) to your detriment.
   - Provide a summary of non-refundable expenses (flight, hotel, event tickets) but do not provide receipts until specifically requested by HR.
6. **Propose exactly one compromise or alternative schedule to demonstrate "reasonableness."** Offer to move the dates, work remotely for a specific portion of the trip, or cover a high-priority meeting while maintaining the rest of the time off.
   - Example: "I can cover the [specific project] on Monday morning, but I must be away for the remainder of the week as planned."
7. **Document any signs of retaliation if the cancellation occurred after you engaged in a protected activity.** If the revocation happened shortly after you filed a complaint, requested a disability accommodation, or reported a safety issue, start a private log of events, names, and dates.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to file a wage claim or a lawsuit.
- You do not need to resolve the staffing crisis for your manager; that is a management function.
- You do not need to provide extensive medical or personal details in your first communication to HR; wait for the formal leave request process.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel blindsided and frustrated when a planned break is cancelled. This situation is often a result of poor management planning rather than your performance; following a structured, written process is the most effective way to protect your job while seeking a resolution.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. It addresses the immediate conflict between employer discretion and employee expectations. Long-term solutions may require legal consultation or formal union grievance procedures.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and does not constitute legal advice. Employment laws vary significantly by state, and your specific rights depend on whether you are an "at-will" employee, a contract worker, or a union member. For definitive guidance, consult a qualified employment attorney or your state’s Department of Labor.
