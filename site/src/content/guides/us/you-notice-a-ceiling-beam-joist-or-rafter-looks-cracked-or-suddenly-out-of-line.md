# What to do if… you notice a ceiling beam, joist, or rafter looks cracked or suddenly out of line (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the area immediately by moving all people and pets away from the suspect structure. If you observe active movement, hear ongoing cracking, or see significant debris falling, evacuate the building and call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stand under the affected area, climb into the attic for a closer inspection, or apply pressure to "test" the stability.
- Do not cut into, drill, or remove drywall or any framing components to investigate the damage.
- Do not add any weight or load above the area, including attic storage or walking on the floor directly above.
- Do not attempt to install temporary posts, jacks, or shoring unless specifically directed by a licensed structural engineer.
- Do not ignore secondary hazards such as the smell of gas, buzzing electrical fixtures, or water leaks near the crack.
- Do not climb onto the roof to inspect or apply tarps while the internal structure is unstable.

## What to do now
1. **Clear and block access to the hazard zone.** **Immediately move all occupants and pets out of the room containing the damaged beam and any space directly above it.** 
   - Close doors to the area.
   - Use tape or furniture to block entry points if doors are not present.
2. **Determine if immediate evacuation is required.** **Leave the building and call 911 if you observe active movement, worsening sag, falling debris, or loud, persistent cracking sounds.** 
   - If evacuating, maintain a safe distance from the exterior of the building (at least the height of the structure).
3. **Minimize vibrations and mechanical stress.** **Stop all activities that cause vibration in the building, such as running, jumping, or moving heavy furniture in adjacent rooms.** 
   - Ensure no one enters the attic or upper floor until a professional clearance is issued.
4. **Identify and respond to utility emergencies.** **Treat any signs of utility compromise as a primary life-safety threat.** 
   - For gas smells or hissing: evacuate and call 911 from a safe distance.
   - For electrical arcing, burning smells, or buzzing: shut off power at the main breaker only if the panel is in a safe, unaffected zone; otherwise, evacuate and call 911.
5. **Contact local building authorities for a safety assessment.** **Call your local building department or code enforcement office to report a potentially unsafe structure.** 
   - In many US cities, this can be done via 311; in other jurisdictions, use the non-emergency municipal service number.
6. **Hire a licensed structural engineer for an urgent evaluation.** **Engage a Professional Engineer (PE) or Structural Engineer (SE) to conduct a formal inspection and provide a written stabilization plan.** 
   - If you are a renter, notify the landlord in writing immediately that the area has been isolated due to a suspected structural failure.
7. **Notify your insurance provider of "suspected structural damage."** **Contact your insurance carrier to open a claim if the damage followed a specific event like a storm, impact, or plumbing failure.** 
   - Do not authorize permanent repairs until the adjuster and your engineer have coordinated on the scope of work.

## What can wait
- You do not need to remove ceiling finishes or "open up" the structure today.
- You do not need to choose a repair contractor until the engineer's report is finalized.
- You do not need to determine the underlying cause or liability during the initial stabilization phase.
- You do not need to photograph the damage if doing so requires entering the hazard zone.

## Important reassurance
Noticing a structural shift is an alarming experience, but the immediate risk is managed by creating distance. Most residential framing issues can be safely stabilized and repaired once the load paths are professionally assessed. Taking the step to isolate the area prevents the most common cause of injury in these scenarios.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions only. It does not replace a professional engineering assessment or satisfy local building code requirements for structural repairs.

## Important note
This information is for general safety stabilization and does not constitute engineering or legal advice. If you suspect an imminent collapse, prioritize evacuation and contact emergency services.
