# What to do if… a creditor demands an urgent payment to keep your plan active (USA)
## Short answer
Pause before paying. Verify the creditor or debt collector through a phone number or account portal you found independently, then ask how today’s payment affects the plan in writing. Only use an official payment channel if you choose to pay and the amount is affordable.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay with gift cards or cryptocurrency.
- Don’t send a wire transfer to new account details, use an unexpected payment link, or call a number provided only in a message until you have verified the details independently.
- Don’t share verification codes, passwords, account logins, or remote access to your phone or computer.
- Don’t agree to a payment amount you cannot afford.
- Don’t assume an incoming call, text, email, or caller ID display is genuine.
- Don’t ignore court papers if they arrive.

## What to do now
1. **End the pressured contact and verify the company.**  
   Hang up or stop replying. Use the phone number on your statement, the back of your card, the creditor’s official website, or your existing account portal. Ask whether your payment plan is active and whether a payment is due today.

2. **Ask what will happen if you do not pay today.**  
   Ask for an email, letter, or secure account message showing:
   - the amount due,
   - the date it is due,
   - how a missed or partial payment may affect the plan,
   - whether fees, interest, or account status may change,
   - the next step if you cannot pay the full amount.

3. **If the contact is from a third-party debt collector, ask for validation information.**  
   A debt collector generally must provide validation information in the initial communication or within five days. This generally includes the collector’s details, the creditor’s name, the amount claimed, and information about disputing the debt.  
   The written notice should give an end date for the validation period. If you dispute some or all of the debt in writing by that date, the collector generally must pause collection of the disputed amount until it sends verification.

4. **If you can afford a payment, confirm how it will be applied before paying.**  
   Ask for written confirmation of the amount, the official payment channel, how the payment will be applied, and whether the existing plan remains active or changes. Keep the confirmation and receipt.

5. **If you cannot afford the requested payment, say so plainly.**  
   Ask whether the creditor or collector can accept a smaller amount, move the due date, or review the account for a hardship option. Do not promise an amount or date you cannot manage.

6. **Set communication boundaries if the contact is from a debt collector.**  
   Tell the collector if calls at work or at a particular time are inconvenient. If you want a debt collector to stop contacting you, send a written request and keep a copy. After receiving it, the collector may still send limited messages, such as confirmation that contact will stop or notice of a lawful next step.

7. **Write down what happened.**  
   Keep the date, time, phone number, company name, caller name, amount requested, deadline, and stated consequences. Save messages, letters, screenshots, and payment receipts.

8. **Use an official complaint route if needed.**  
   You can submit a complaint to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau about a debt collection or consumer financial services issue. You can also report suspected fraud to the Federal Trade Commission.

9. **If you already paid and now suspect a scam, act now.**  
   Contact the bank, card issuer, payment app, gift card company, wire transfer company, or cryptocurrency platform you used. Say that you suspect fraud and ask whether the transaction can be stopped, reversed, or flagged.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to use debt settlement, consolidation, credit repair, or bankruptcy.
- You do not have to explain the full account history during a pressured call.
- You do not have to accept a new repayment amount before you understand it and know it is affordable.
- You do not have to solve every longer-term debt question today.

## Important reassurance
A same-day demand can make it harder to think clearly. Pausing, verifying the contact independently, and asking for written details are reasonable steps, even if you later decide to make a payment.

## Scope note
These are first steps for stabilizing the situation. Later decisions may need help from a nonprofit credit counseling organization or a lawyer, especially if you receive court papers or need advice about state law.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Rights and procedures can depend on whether the contact is from the original creditor or a third-party debt collector, the type of debt, and state law.
