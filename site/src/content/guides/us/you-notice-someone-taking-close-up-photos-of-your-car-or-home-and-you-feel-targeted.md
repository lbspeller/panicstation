# What to do if… you notice someone taking close-up photos of your car or home and you feel targeted (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer position first: inside, behind a locked door, in a locked vehicle, or near other people. Call 911 if you feel in immediate danger or a crime is happening now; otherwise use your local police non-emergency number to report what happened.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront, chase, block, or try to physically stop the person.
- Do not step outside alone to get proof if you feel unsafe.
- Do not post photos, videos, your address, or your plate details publicly.
- Do not touch an unfamiliar object on your car or property, such as a tag, tracker-looking device, or package; leave it in place and report it.
- Do not argue with the person online or try to identify them publicly.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance and safety.** Go inside and lock doors. If you are in a vehicle, keep doors locked and drive to a busy, well-lit place with people nearby rather than going straight home if that feels safer.
2. **Choose the right reporting route.** Call 911 if the person is approaching you, trying doors, refusing to leave while you are afraid, following you, damaging property, or if you cannot safely leave. If the incident is suspicious but not immediately dangerous, find your local police department’s non-emergency number on your city, county, or police website. Use 311 only if your local government lists it for non-emergency police or city service routing.
3. **Record what you can from safety.** From indoors or a locked vehicle, note the time, exact location, what they photographed, what they looked like, what direction they left, and any vehicle details. A written note is enough if taking a photo or video would draw attention or increase risk.
4. **Save camera evidence after you are secure.** Download or export doorbell, dashcam, or CCTV clips promptly, if your system may overwrite old footage. Keep the original files and write a short timeline of what you saw.
5. **Make your home and car less accessible right now.** Lock doors, windows, garage doors, gates, and outbuildings. Close blinds or curtains, move visible valuables away from windows, and turn on exterior lights. After the person has left, or if you can do it without going outside alone, move your car into a garage, driveway camera view, or a more visible spot.
6. **Make an official record.** When you report, ask whether an incident or case number will be created. Save that number, and use it if the same person, vehicle, or behavior appears again.
7. **Tell one nearby trusted person.** Let household members know not to open the door to strangers right now. If you trust a neighbor, ask them to save any camera footage covering the street or driveway for the same time window.

## What can wait
- Figuring out the person’s motive.
- Trying to identify the person yourself.
- Buying new security equipment or making major changes to your routine.
- Posting warnings online.
- Calling insurance, unless there is damage, theft, or attempted entry to document.

## Important reassurance
This kind of incident can make fear spike quickly. There may be an ordinary explanation, but you do not need to decide that now. The immediate goal is to stay safe, preserve basic details, and create a record in case it repeats.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the behavior repeats, becomes harassment, includes threats, or makes you feel unsafe at home, later decisions may need help from local law enforcement, a victim-support service, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, investigative, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 911. If you are unsure which local route to use, you can report what happened and ask the dispatcher or local agency which route to use.
