# What to do if… your rescue inhaler is not relieving symptoms the way it usually does (USA)

## Short answer
Sit upright and follow the "Red Zone" steps of your asthma action plan; if rescue medicine does **not provide relief within 15 minutes** or you cannot speak, **call 911**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not lie down or bend over; staying upright maximizes your airway capacity.
- Do not wait to "see if it passes" if you are struggling to speak or your skin is pulling in around your ribs.
- Do not perform a "float test" by putting the canister in water to check if it is empty.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you are in significant respiratory distress.
- Do not exceed the maximum number of puffs specified in your written medical plan without emergency assistance.

## What to do now
1. **Sit upright and remain in a "safe pause" position.** Do not move around more than necessary; keep your chest open and your phone within reach.
2. **Check the medicine level immediately.** Look at the dose counter on the canister; if it shows "0," the medicine is gone even if the inhaler still makes a "puff" sound (this is just propellant).
3. **Use your rescue inhaler with a spacer if available.** Follow these specific technique steps:
   - Shake the inhaler for 5 to 10 seconds.
   - Prime the inhaler by spraying 2 to 4 puffs into the air if it has not been used in several days.
   - Take one puff at a time, breathing in slowly and holding your breath for 10 seconds.
   - **Wait at least 1 minute between puffs** of rescue medication to allow the first dose to begin opening the airways.
4. **Follow your written Asthma Action Plan's emergency instructions.** If you do not have your plan, most standard USA protocols (NHLBI/CDC) designate the following as a "Red Zone" emergency requiring an immediate 911 call:
   - You are gasping for air or cannot speak in full sentences.
   - Your quick-relief medicine is not helping or relief lasts less than 4 hours.
   - Your peak flow reading is less than 50 percent of your personal best.
5. **Monitor for physical "Danger Signs" that require calling 911 now.** Do not wait for a doctor to call you back if you observe:
   - Lips or fingernails appearing blue, pale, or gray.
   - The skin between your ribs or at the base of your throat "sucking in" with every breath (retractions).
   - Feeling confused, extremely drowsy, or like you might pass out.
6. **Notify a nearby person of your situation.** Keep the door unlocked if you are alone so emergency responders can enter quickly.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify what triggered the flare-up right now.
- You do not need to clean the inhaler or spacer until you are breathing comfortably.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of the emergency visit; prioritize oxygen intake.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of panic when breathing is difficult, and anxiety can make your chest feel tighter. Focus on sitting still and following the numbered steps. Emergency services are trained to handle asthma emergencies, and calling early is a proactive safety measure, not a failure of management.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute failure of rescue medication. It does not replace a personalized asthma action plan or long-term controller therapy.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only. If you are currently in respiratory distress and your inhaler is ineffective, seek professional emergency medical care immediately by calling 911 or going to the nearest emergency department.
