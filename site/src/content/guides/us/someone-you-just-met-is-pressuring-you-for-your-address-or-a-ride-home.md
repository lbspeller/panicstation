# What to do if… someone you just met is pressuring you for your address or a ride home (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a staffed, public place and tell staff you need help leaving without the person. Use transportation you control, and call 911 if you need immediate police, fire-department, or ambulance assistance.

## Do not do these things
- Do not give your real address just to end the conversation.
- Do not accept transportation controlled by the person.
- Do not let the person accompany you to your car alone.
- Do not move to a quieter place or outside area to talk privately.
- Do not keep debating if ending the conversation and moving away is safer.
- Do not let the person see your phone screen while you arrange transportation or contact someone.
- Do not go straight home if you are concerned that the person may follow you.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause.** Move to a staffed, public area such as a counter, front desk, security desk, or busy lobby. Stay where other people can see you.
2. **Tell staff what you need.** Say, “This person will not stop pressuring me for my address or a ride home. I need help leaving without them.” Ask staff to involve security if available and to help you wait or leave without the person accompanying you.
3. **Use a brief boundary if speaking feels safe.** Say, “I am not sharing my address. I am leaving now.” You do not need to explain or debate.
4. **Arrange transportation the person does not control.** Ask staff to call a taxi, arrange your own rideshare while waiting inside, or contact a trusted person. Before entering a booked car, check that the car and driver match the details shown in the app.
5. **If you are using your own car, ask for company.** Ask staff, security, or someone you trust to walk with you. Get in, lock the doors, and leave. If you think the person is following you, do not drive home. Call 911 if you need immediate assistance and follow the call-taker’s instructions.
6. **Contact someone you trust.** Tell them where you are and what is happening. Share your location only if it is safe and you choose to do so.
7. **If the person follows you on foot or blocks your exit, stay public.** Move toward staff or other people and call 911 if you need immediate assistance.
8. **Once you are somewhere safer, save useful details.** Note the time, place, what happened, the person’s appearance, any name or account handle, and any vehicle details you noticed. Save messages if there are any.
9. **If you already shared your address, take precautions without rushing into a decision.** Consider not going home alone. Go to a staffed public place or another place the person does not know if that feels safer, contact someone you trust, and call 911 if you need immediate assistance.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether the behaviour meets a legal definition.
- Deciding whether to make a report.
- Writing a perfect message back.
- Any confrontation, closure conversation, or follow-up meeting.
- Reviewing the whole interaction to decide what you should have done differently.

## Important reassurance
You are not being rude by ending the interaction. You do not owe someone your address, a ride arrangement, or a longer explanation. It is understandable if you froze, tried to keep the peace, or needed time to decide what to do.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes and hours. If the person continues contacting you, threatens you, follows you, or appears again, you may want specialist support and can ask your local police department about reporting options.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you need immediate police, fire-department, or ambulance assistance, call 911. For a non-emergency police contact route, check the official website for your local police department.
