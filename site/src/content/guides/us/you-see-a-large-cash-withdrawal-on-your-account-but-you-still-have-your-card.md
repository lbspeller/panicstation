# What to do if… you see a large cash withdrawal on your account but you still have your card (USA)

## Short answer
Lock your card immediately via your banking app and contact your bank or credit union to report an "unauthorized electronic fund transfer" to trigger your Regulation E $0 liability protections.

## Do not do these things
- Do not call phone numbers found in a text or email alert; only use the official number on the back of your physical card or the bank’s verified website.
- Do not keep using the card for other purchases until the bank has confirmed the account is secure.
- Do not share your PIN, password, or any "one-time verification codes" with anyone, even if they claim to be from the bank’s fraud department.
- Do not delay reporting the transaction while waiting for it to change from "pending" to "posted."
- Do not attempt to visit the ATM location yourself to investigate or look for cameras.

## What to do now
1. **Freeze or lock the debit card immediately.** Open your bank's mobile app or website and use the "Lock Card" or "Freeze Account" toggle to prevent further withdrawals.
2. **Call the official fraud department number.** Use the number on the back of your card or your monthly statement.
   - Tell the representative: "I am reporting an unauthorized electronic fund transfer."
   - State clearly: "I am in possession of my card, and I did not authorize this withdrawal."
3. **Request a formal "Regulation E" dispute.** Ask the bank to open an official error resolution case.
   - **Ensure you receive a claim or case number.**
   - **Ask for a confirmation email or letter** detailing the dispute.
4. **Change your online banking credentials.** Update your password and enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) from a secure device.
5. **Verify your contact information.** Check that the mobile number, email, and mailing address on your bank profile have not been changed by a third party.
6. **File an identity theft report.** Go to IdentityTheft.gov (FTC) to document the unauthorized access to your financial information.
7. **Note the "10 business day" rule.** Under federal law, the bank generally has 10 business days to investigate or provide a provisional credit while they continue the inquiry.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine how the card was cloned (e.g., skimming or shimming) before reporting.
- You do not need to file a local police report immediately unless the bank specifically requests one for their investigation.
- You do not need to decide whether to close the entire bank account today; focus first on securing the specific card and its access.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel confused when money is withdrawn while the physical card is still in your wallet. This often occurs due to "cloning" or "skimming," where the card's data is copied. Federal protections under Regulation E are designed for this specific situation; if you report the error within 60 days of the statement date, your liability for the unauthorized transfer is typically zero.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate steps to secure a bank account and initiate a federal error dispute for unauthorized ATM withdrawals. It does not cover long-term credit monitoring or legal actions against third parties.

## Important note
This information is for stabilization purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Liability limits and investigation timelines are governed by the Electronic Fund Transfer Act (Regulation E) and may vary based on your specific account agreement and the timing of your report.
