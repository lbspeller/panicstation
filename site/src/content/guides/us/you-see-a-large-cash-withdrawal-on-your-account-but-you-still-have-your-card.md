# What to do if… you see a large cash withdrawal on your account but you still have your card (USA)

## Short answer
Lock the card and contact your bank or credit union through its official app, website, or the number on the back of your card. Report the cash withdrawal as an unauthorized electronic fund transfer and ask the bank to start its Regulation E error-resolution process.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t call numbers from texts, emails, pop-ups, or voicemail messages about the withdrawal; use your bank’s official app, website, or the number on the back of your card.
- Don’t keep using the card to see whether it still works.
- Don’t delay reporting while you try to work out how it happened; timing can affect protections.
- Don’t share your PIN, password, one-time code, or remote-access permission with anyone who contacts you.
- Don’t confront a suspected person while you are panicked; secure the account first.

## What to do now
1. **Lock or block the card now.** Use your banking app if it has a freeze or lock option. If you cannot do that, call the bank or credit union using an official route and ask them to block the card.
2. **Report the transaction in exact words.** Say: “I still have my card, I did not authorize this ATM or cash withdrawal, and I want to report an unauthorized electronic fund transfer.” Ask for the case number and the name of the team handling it.
3. **Ask the bank to start its Regulation E error-resolution process.** Ask what information they need from you, whether the withdrawal is still pending or posted, and whether they need a written confirmation after your phone report.
4. **Ask for immediate protective actions.**
   - Cancel the current debit card and issue a replacement.
   - Disable or lower ATM withdrawal access if the bank can do that.
   - Check for recent card, ATM, debit, transfer, or login activity you do not recognise.
   - Check whether your phone number, email, mailing address, or alert settings were changed.
5. **Write down the basic facts in one place.** Record the amount, date, time, ATM location if shown, whether it is pending or posted, the case number, and the date and time you reported it. Save screenshots of the transaction list before it changes.
6. **Secure online access from a trusted device.** Change your online banking password, turn on login and transaction alerts, and sign out of other sessions if your bank offers that option.
7. **Follow any written-confirmation request carefully.** Ask where to send it and whether there is a deadline, including any 10-business-day deadline for written confirmation. Keep a copy or screenshot of anything you submit.
8. **Escalate if the bank response is unclear or stalled.** Ask for the fraud, debit-card dispute, or electronic funds transfer dispute team. If you still cannot get a clear route, you can submit a complaint to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove how it happened right now, such as skimming, a cloned card, stolen PIN, or account takeover.
- You do not need to decide today whether to close the whole account unless the bank says it is needed to stop more loss.
- You do not need to argue about final liability on the first call; focus on getting the card blocked, the report logged, and the investigation started.
- You do not need to contact the ATM owner before reporting to your own bank.

## Important reassurance
It can feel impossible for cash to leave an account when the card is still in your possession. That confusion is normal, and it does not stop you from reporting the withdrawal as unauthorized. The useful first move is to lock the card, get the bank’s formal process started, and prevent more withdrawals.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce further loss and start the bank or credit union’s formal review. Later decisions, including complaints, account changes, or specialist help, may depend on what the bank finds and on the details of your account.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Rules and procedures can vary by account type and facts. If someone is threatening you, coercing you, or pressuring you for cash, your PIN, or account access, move to safety and call 911 if there is immediate danger.
