# What to do if… a radiator or heating pipe starts leaking water onto the floor (USA)

## Short answer
Shut off the heating system power immediately (if dry to reach), then close the water supply valve feeding the boiler to stop the pressure. Keep all people and pets away from standing water or potential steam spray.

## Do not do these things
- Do not step into standing water to reach an electrical outlet, power switch, or breaker.
- Do not touch a leaking pipe or radiator with bare hands, as it may be hot enough to cause scalding.
- Do not attempt to "tighten" a spraying fitting while the system is under pressure.
- Do not ignore the smell of gas or a carbon monoxide alarm near the boiler.
- Do not use a standard vacuum to suck up water unless it is specifically a wet-dry vacuum plugged into a dry, safe outlet.

## What to do now
1. **Move away from the immediate spray or puddle.** Hot water or steam can cause burns, and wet floors are a slip hazard.
2. **Turn the heating system OFF at the thermostat.** This stops the system from trying to cycle, which reduces the buildup of heat and pressure that drives the leak.
3. **Cut power to the boiler (if safe to reach).** **Locate the service switch—often a red-labeled switch near the unit—and flip it to "OFF."** 
   - If you must walk through water to reach the switch, do not touch it.
   - Go to the breaker panel and shut off the circuit labeled "Boiler" or "Heating" only if the panel area is dry.
4. **Close the water supply to the heating system.** **Locate the "makeup water" or "auto-fill" valve on the pipe leading into the boiler and turn it clockwise until it stops.** 
   - This prevents the system from automatically adding more water to the leak.
   - If you cannot find this valve, shut off the home's main water supply valve.
5. **Isolate the leak locally if possible.** **If the radiator has a manual shutoff valve on the inlet pipe, turn it clockwise to close it.** 
   - Use a heavy glove or cloth if the valve handle is hot.
   - If the valve is stuck, do not force it; move to the next available shutoff.
6. **Contain the water and protect the floor.** **Place a bucket, bowl, or tray under the drip to catch water.** 
   - Use towels to create a "dam" around the edges of the puddle to prevent water from reaching walls or electrical floor outlets.
7. **Notify the appropriate parties for repair.** **If you are a tenant, call your landlord or building maintenance emergency line immediately.** 
   - If you own the home, call a licensed HVAC technician or plumber specializing in hydronic (hot water) heating.
8. **Document the damage for insurance.** **Take photos of the leak source and the extent of the water spread once the situation is stabilized.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific failed component (e.g., air vent, valve packing, or pipe corrosion) right now.
- You do not need to start major floor or drywall demolition in the first hour.
- You do not need to decide on a full system replacement or upgrade while the leak is active.

## Important reassurance
A radiator leak often looks worse than it is because water spreads quickly on hard floors. By turning off the power and the water feed, you have removed the two forces (pressure and supply) that allow the leak to continue. The remaining water in the pipe will eventually stop once the local pressure drops.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for residential hydronic heating leaks. It does not cover permanent repairs, system draining, or the handling of steam-specific high-pressure boiler failures which require immediate professional intervention.

## Important note
This is general safety information. Heating systems involve high temperatures and electrical components; if water has contacted any electrical wiring or if the leak involves steam, evacuate the immediate area and wait for a qualified professional.
