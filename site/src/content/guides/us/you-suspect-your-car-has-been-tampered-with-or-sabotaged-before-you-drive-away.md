# What to do if… you suspect your car has been tampered with or sabotaged before you drive away (USA)

## Short answer
Do not drive the car. Move to a safer place if anything looks attached, altered, or out of place, then call 911 if there is immediate danger and arrange a tow or inspection before the vehicle is driven.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “just drive around the block” to test it.
- Do not confront someone you suspect or try to catch them.
- Do not touch, remove, or fix anything that looks altered.
- Do not lean under the car or put your hands into gaps where you cannot see clearly.
- Do not clean up, throw anything away, or move suspicious items unless emergency responders tell you to.
- Do not share your exact location or suspicions publicly in the moment.

## What to do now
1. **Put your personal safety ahead of the car.**  
   If you are inside the car and it is safe to stay there briefly, lock the doors and keep your phone ready. If you are outside, step back and move to a well-lit, populated place nearby, such as a store entrance, lobby, staffed desk, or security office.

2. **If you see an unfamiliar object or device attached to the car, inside the car, or placed right next to it, move away.**  
   Do not touch it, move it, photograph it close up, or try to identify it yourself. Once you are safely away, call 911 and follow the dispatcher’s instructions.

3. **Call the right level of help based on danger.**
   - Call 911 if you believe you are in immediate danger, the situation is happening now, there is a suspicious object, or you feel unsafe where you are.
   - If it is not an immediate emergency, look up the local law enforcement agency where the car is located and use its non-emergency phone or online reporting route to report suspected tampering.

4. **Do a quick no-touch safety scan from a safe position.**  
   Without kneeling in traffic, reaching under the vehicle, or opening anything that seems unsafe, look for:
   - A suddenly flat tire, severe visible tire damage, or something lodged in a tire
   - Fresh leaks under the vehicle
   - Anything hanging down, dragging, cut, loose, or visibly out of place underneath
   - Signs of forced entry, disturbed doors, damaged locks, broken glass, or anything obviously moved inside the car

5. **If anything seems off, treat the car as unsafe to drive.**  
   Do not drive it to a shop. Call roadside assistance or a reputable tow company and ask for towing to a trusted repair shop for a safety inspection, including tires, brakes, steering, wheels, and the undercarriage.

6. **Document what you can without touching the car more than necessary.**  
   From a safe distance, take photos or video of all sides of the vehicle, the tires, the ground around it, the suspicious area, the parking space, nearby signs, and any visible cameras. Write down the time, exact location, and when you last drove it normally.

7. **If the car is on managed property, tell security or property staff now.**  
   Ask whether they can preserve or flag camera footage for the time your vehicle was parked there. Ask them to make a note of your report and any nearby incidents.

8. **Contact your insurer after the immediate safety steps are handled.**  
   Ask what they need before repairs, towing, or a claim. If police give you a report number or incident number, save it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to figure out who did this right now.
- You do not need to accuse anyone or send messages.
- You do not need to decide every reporting, insurance, repair, or next-step detail now.
- You do not need to do a detailed under-car inspection yourself.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to stop and not drive when something about the car looks wrong. If it turns out to be harmless, you still chose the safer option. If something was actually altered, pausing may prevent a crash or further escalation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: get safer, avoid disturbing possible evidence, and keep the vehicle from being driven until it is checked. Later decisions may need help from a mechanic, police, your insurer, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, mechanical, insurance, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If the car may be unsafe, do not drive it; use towing and a professional inspection.
