# What to do if… a public charger is working but will not accept your payment method and you are low on charge (USA)

## Short answer
Stop attempting the same failed payment, minimize battery drain immediately, and use the station's toll-free support number to request a remote session start or a courtesy charge.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t repeatedly swipe or tap the same card as this can trigger a bank fraud lock or multiple $50 pre-authorization holds.
- Don’t leave the vehicle’s high-power systems (heater, A/C, or seat warmers) running while you troubleshoot.
- Don’t attempt to disassemble or force the card reader or connector.
- Don’t wait until the battery reaches 0% to look for a backup location.
- Don’t give your full credit card number or CVV to anyone over the phone unless you have personally dialed the official number printed on the station.

## What to do now
1. **Reduce the vehicle energy load immediately to preserve range.**
   **Switch the car to Eco, Range, or Max Range mode and turn off the climate control system.** Ensure all doors and windows are closed to maintain cabin temperature without using power.

2. **Verify the physical status of the charging station.**
   **Check that the Emergency Stop (E-stop) button is not partially depressed and look for any "out of order" icons on the screen.** Ensure the connector is fully seated in your vehicle; some chargers will not process payment until a "handshake" with the car is confirmed.

3. **Document the specific identifiers for technical support.**
   **Take a photo or note the Charger ID, the station network name (e.g., Electrify America, ChargePoint, EVgo), and the exact error message shown.** This information is required for the operator to locate your stall in their system.

4. **Attempt the mandatory alternative payment methods required by federal standards.**
   **Try using a different physical card, a mobile wallet (Apple Pay/Google Pay), or the guest checkout option on the station's screen.** Per 2026 NEVI standards, most public fast chargers must also allow you to initiate a session via a toll-free number or SMS displayed on the unit.

5. **Call the network's 24/7 customer support line immediately.**
   **Ask the representative to "remote start" the session from their end.** If your payment is being declined due to a station communication error rather than a bank issue, ask if they can provide a "courtesy charge" to give you enough range to reach a backup site.

6. **Check a third-party app for the real-time status of nearby chargers.**
   **Open a neutral app like PlugShare or A Better Routeplanner (ABRP) to find the nearest functional backup.** Do not rely solely on the car’s built-in map if it does not show recent user comments about station reliability.

7. **Initiate a Plan B move while you still have a safety buffer.**
   **If the session does not start within 10 minutes, drive to the nearest Level 2 charger or a different DC fast charging site.** It is safer to arrive at a backup with 2–5% remaining than to exhaust the battery entirely while troubleshooting a broken station.

8. **Contact roadside assistance if you are at risk of a total shutdown.**
   **Call your automaker’s roadside line or a service like AAA and specify that you are in a "low-power state at a non-functional charger."** Confirm they are sending a flatbed trailer, as most EVs cannot be traditionally towed without damaging the motors.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve the underlying bank decline or app glitch while at the station.
- You do not need to call your credit card company to dispute holds until you have reached your destination.
- You do not need to report the station's failure to local authorities unless the site itself is physically unsafe.

## Important reassurance
Payment terminal failures are common and often have nothing to do with your bank account or vehicle. Modern charging networks have protocols for remote overrides specifically to prevent drivers from being stranded due to software handshaking errors.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for payment-related charging failures. It does not cover mechanical repairs, grid-wide outages, or long-term battery health management.

## Important note
The information provided is for emergency stabilization and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Charging station availability and network policies can change. Always prioritize personal safety and follow official emergency protocols if stranded in a hazardous location.
