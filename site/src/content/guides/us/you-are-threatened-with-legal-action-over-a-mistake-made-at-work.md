# What to do if… you are threatened with legal action over a mistake made at work (USA)

## Short answer
Pause the task, move to a safe distance, and inform your supervisor that you are awaiting confirmation of hazard controls before continuing. Request a temporary reassignment and document the instruction to continue work despite the unresolved incident.

## Do not do these things
- Do not re-enter the affected area or restart a task if you have a reasonable belief it poses an imminent risk of serious injury or death.
- Do not accept vague verbal assurances that the situation is being "looked into" as a substitute for visible physical controls or written safety approvals.
- Do not sign any document stating an area is safe if you have not been shown the specific mitigation steps taken.
- Do not leave the worksite entirely unless staying puts you in immediate physical danger, as this may be classified as job abandonment.
- Do not engage in a verbal confrontation regarding blame or fault; focus exclusively on the absence of safety controls.

## What to do now
1. **Establish a safe pause.** Step away from the immediate equipment, chemical exposure, or structural risk involved in the incident.
2. **State your safety concern in neutral terms.** Inform your supervisor: "I am not comfortable continuing this specific task until the hazard from the recent incident has been mitigated and the controls are confirmed."
3. **Request specific information on hazard controls.** Ask for the "safety basis" for the instruction to continue, including what physical barriers, lock-out procedures, or personal protective equipment (PPE) have been implemented since the incident.
4. **Ask for temporary reassignment.** Request to be moved to a different area or assigned to different duties that do not involve the hazard while the assessment is finalized.
5. **Call 1-800-321-OSHA (6742) for imminent dangers.** If the situation is life-threatening and the employer refuses to pause, contact the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) immediately for an emergency inspection.
6. **Identify if you are in a "State Plan" state.** 
   - Check if your state (such as California, Washington, or Oregon) operates its own safety agency, as you may need to file reports through state-specific portals like Cal/OSHA or WA L&I.
7. **Document the instruction and the environment.** Record the time, the names of supervisors involved, the exact wording of the instruction to continue, and a description of the hazard (including photos if it is safe to take them).
8. **Act with coworkers if possible.** Under the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), two or more employees acting together to protest unsafe conditions have "protected concerted activity" rights, which can provide broader legal protection than acting alone.
9. **File a confidential hazard complaint.** You may file a safety complaint with OSHA online or by phone; you can request that your identity remain confidential from your employer during the safety inspection.
10. **Monitor for retaliation and note the 30-day window.** If you are demoted, threatened, or fired for raising safety concerns, you must file a whistleblower complaint under Section 11(c) of the OSH Act within 30 calendar days of the retaliatory act.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the exact OSHA standard or regulation code that was violated.
- You do not need to decide on long-term legal action or resignation in the immediate aftermath of the incident.
- You do not need to provide a comprehensive, multi-page incident report today; a brief factual summary of the hazard and the instruction to work is sufficient for initial documentation.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel pressured or isolated when a supervisor prioritizes production over safety assessment. Requesting clarity on safety controls is a standard professional procedure in industrial and high-risk environments. Taking a pause to ensure a hazard is controlled is an act of workplace safety, not an act of insubordination.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and harm prevention in the hours following a workplace safety incident. It focuses on federal and state-level safety rights in the United States and does not cover specific union grievance procedures or long-term workers' compensation claims.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. OSHA citations can only be issued for violations that currently exist or existed within the past six months. If you believe there is an immediate threat to life or limb, utilize emergency services by dialing 911. Retaliation complaints under Section 11(c) are not anonymous and involve a formal legal investigation where the employer will be notified of the claim.
