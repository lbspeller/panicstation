# What to do if… your child tells you someone online is asking to keep a “special secret” and it feels sexual (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a child sexual exploitation concern, not a private online problem the child must manage alone. Stop the contact immediately without deleting evidence, and report the interaction to the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children (NCMEC) or the FBI.

## Do not do these things
- Do not shame your child or demand they explain the entire situation perfectly before taking action.
- Do not message the person yourself or attempt to "confront" them via the child's account.
- Do not ask your child to continue the conversation to "gather more evidence" or "catch" the person.
- Do not delete messages, clear browser history, or factory reset the device.
- Do not forward or share sexual content (if found) with friends or family for advice.
- Do not promise that "nothing will happen" next if you are still assessing immediate physical risks.
- Do not rush into punishments regarding device use while the child is in a state of distress or panic.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize immediate belief and safety.** Say clearly: "Thank you for telling me. You are not in trouble. We are going to handle this together."

2. **Cease all contact through the device.** Have the child stop replying and put the device in "Do Not Disturb" mode or airplane mode to stop new notifications from appearing, but do not turn it off or wipe it.

3. **Check for immediate physical danger.** Ask if the person knows your child’s school name, home address, or current location, or if a physical meeting has already been requested or occurred.
   - If there is an immediate threat of physical harm or a meeting is imminent, call **911**.

4. **Identify the platform and handle.** Calmly note the app name, the other person’s username/handle, and any other platforms they have moved the conversation to (e.g., from a game to an encrypted chat app).

5. **Preserve digital evidence without redistributing it.** Take screenshots of the profile, handle, and key messages showing the "secret" request or sexual content.
   - Store these screenshots on a separate, password-protected device or drive.
   - Do not send these images to your own phone or any other person via messaging apps.

6. **Submit a report to the NCMEC CyberTipline.** Go to **cybertip.org** or call **1-800-843-5678** (1-800-THE-LOST) to report suspected online child sexual exploitation. This is the official national reporting mechanism in the USA.

7. **Contact the FBI if federal intervention is needed.** You can also report the incident directly to the FBI at **tips.fbi.gov** or by calling **1-800-CALL-FBI** (1-800-225-5324).

8. **Use "Take It Down" for existing images.** If nude or sexually explicit images or videos of the child (under 18) have already been shared, use the NCMEC tool at **takeitdown.ncmec.org**.
   - This tool creates a digital fingerprint (hash) to help participating platforms remove the content automatically without you having to upload the actual image to them.

9. **Limit further online exposure today.** Keep the child’s device in a common area and pause non-essential apps to reduce incoming "online noise" while you wait for professional guidance.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on new long-term household internet rules tonight.
- You do not need to determine the "real" identity or location of the other person yourself.
- You do not need to extract a full, chronological timeline of the entire relationship in one sitting.
- You do not need to notify the school or other parents until you have spoken with a reporting agency or law enforcement.

## Important reassurance
Secrecy and "special secrets" are standard grooming tactics used to isolate children from their support systems. If a child complies, freezes, or hides contact, it is a normal response to high-pressure manipulation and is never the child's fault.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and reporting instructions only. Ongoing support and investigation will require specialist intervention from law enforcement and trained counselors.

## Important note
This is general information and does not constitute legal or clinical advice. In the USA, suspected online child sexual exploitation should be reported to NCMEC or the FBI. Under the 2026 federal TAKE IT DOWN Act, platforms have specific obligations to remove nonconsensual intimate imagery of minors within 48 hours of a valid request.
