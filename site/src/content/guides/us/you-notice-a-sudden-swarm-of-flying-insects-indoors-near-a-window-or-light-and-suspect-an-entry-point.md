# What to do if… you notice a sudden swarm of flying insects indoors near a window or light and suspect an entry point (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the room immediately by closing doors, turn off the attracting light source, and contact a professional (beekeeper or pest control) to identify the species and find the entry point.

## Do not do these things
- Do not spray pesticides, aerosol "bug bombs," or household cleaners onto a large swarm, as fumes are hazardous indoors and can provoke stinging insects.
- Do not use water or a garden hose to spray a cluster of bees or wasps, as this can cause them to scatter or become defensive.
- Do not swat at or walk through a dense cluster of winged insects if you have not identified them.
- Do not immediately seal the entry hole while insects are actively emerging, as this may force them deeper into wall voids or other rooms.
- Do not ignore discarded wings on windowsills, as these are a primary sign of structural pests.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the swarm by closing all doors to the affected room.** This prevents the insects from spreading to the rest of the house and protects children and pets from potential stings or contact.
2. **Turn off the indoor lights that are attracting the swarm.** Most swarming insects (alates) are drawn to light; darkening the room will help the swarm settle in one place rather than flying throughout the space.
3. **Open an exterior window in the room if the insects are already clustering against the glass.** **Only do this if the window can be opened without you having to reach into the swarm.** If the insects are coming from *outside*, keep the window shut; if they are emerging from *inside* a wall, opening the window may encourage them to exit the building.
4. **Identify the physical characteristics of the insects from a safe distance.** Use a phone camera to zoom in on a settled insect or a dead specimen on a windowsill to check for:
   - **Waist shape:** A "pinched" narrow waist indicates ants; a thick, straight "cigar-shaped" waist indicates termites.
   - **Wings:** Four wings of equal length indicate termites; four wings where the front pair is much longer than the back pair indicate ants.
   - **Antennae:** Straight or beaded antennae indicate termites; bent or "elbowed" antennae indicate ants.
5. **Call a local beekeeper or beekeeping association if the insects appear to be honey bees.** Honey bee swarms are often docile and temporary, but if they have entered a wall, you need a specialist to remove the colony and the honeycomb to prevent structural rot.
6. **Watch for signs of a severe allergic reaction if anyone has been stung.** **Call 911 immediately if you observe difficulty breathing, swelling of the tongue or throat, hives, dizziness, or fainting.** If the person has a known allergy and a prescribed epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen), assist them in using it immediately.
7. **Document the entry point without disturbing the area.** Take a short video or photo of where the insects are emerging, such as a baseboard, vent, or window frame, to show the inspector.
8. **Vacuum up dead or non-stinging insects once the active swarming has ceased.** Empty the vacuum canister or bag into a sealed plastic bag and move it to an outdoor trash bin immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact sub-species of the insect in the first hour.
- You do not need to begin structural repairs or sealing gaps until a professional has confirmed the source.
- You do not need to decide on long-term pest management contracts or chemical treatments during the initial surge.

## Important reassurance
A sudden swarm is often a temporary reproductive event triggered by weather changes. While it can be overwhelming, isolating the room and removing the light source will usually cause the swarm to settle within 20 to 60 minutes, allowing for a calm professional assessment.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Swarms emerging from within a building typically indicate an established colony (such as termites or carpenter ants) that requires a professional structural inspection.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and is not a substitute for professional pest control or medical advice. If you suspect an infestation of wood-destroying organisms or honey bees in a structure, consult a licensed professional.
