# What to do if… you receive a phone call saying a family member has died and you are alone (USA)

## Short answer
Get yourself physically safe and steady first. Then verify the news through a callback you start yourself, using an official hospital, medical examiner, coroner, police, or sheriff’s office number before you notify others publicly or agree to anything.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t drive while you are in shock, crying, numb, dizzy, or confused.
- Don’t send money, gift cards, cryptocurrency, bank details, verification codes, or payment app transfers to anyone who calls about a death.
- Don’t share your Social Security number, scans of ID, or sensitive details about you or the person named in the call with an unsolicited caller.
- Don’t rely on caller ID as proof that the caller is real.
- Don’t announce the death publicly on social media or in large group chats until you have verified it.
- Don’t sign contracts, choose a funeral home, book urgent travel, or make major legal or financial decisions while you are alone and overwhelmed.

## What to do now
1. **Pause before you act.** Sit down or lie down, put both feet on the floor if you can, and take a few slow breaths. Put down anything sharp, hot, heavy, or breakable.
2. **Write down the call details before they blur.** Note the caller’s name, role, agency or organization, phone number shown, callback number they gave, case or incident number, time of call, and exactly what they said happened.
3. **Treat the news as unverified until you confirm it.** It is okay to say, “I need to call back through the official number,” and end the call.
4. **Check for scam pressure.** Be especially cautious if the caller demands secrecy, money, gift cards, cryptocurrency, bank details, passwords, verification codes, or immediate travel to an unfamiliar place.
5. **Verify through a callback you start yourself.**
   - If they said **hospital**, search for the hospital’s official website or use a trusted directory, call the main number, and ask for the relevant unit, department, patient relations, or bereavement office.
   - If they said **medical examiner, coroner, police, or sheriff**, use the official city, county, or state government website to find the public number, then ask whether they can verify the report and any case or incident number.
   - If they gave you a callback number, do not use it as your only source. Compare it with the official public number first.
6. **Get another person with you while you verify.** Call one trusted person and say, “I got a call saying [name] died. I am alone. Can you stay on the phone while I verify it?” If they cannot answer, try a neighbor, nearby friend, or family member.
7. **If the death is confirmed, ask only what you need for the next few hours.**
   - “Where is [name] now?”
   - “Who is the official point of contact?”
   - “What is the case, incident, or reference number?”
   - “What number should I use if I need to call back?”
   - “Is there anything that must be done tonight, or can it wait until morning?”
8. **Use emergency help for immediate danger.** Call 911 if you believe someone may still be in immediate danger, a crime may be happening now, you are being threatened, or you cannot stay physically safe. If you need urgent emotional support, call or text 988, or use 988 chat, and ask for help getting through the next hour.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a funeral home today.
- You do not need to make travel plans while you are still in shock.
- You do not need to notify everyone at once; start with one trusted support person and, if needed, one key family contact.
- You do not need to deal with death certificates, benefits, bank accounts, belongings, debts, or paperwork until the death is confirmed and you have support.
- You do not need to decide what this means for the rest of your life tonight.

## Important reassurance
A call like this can make your mind feel blank, unreal, or scrambled. That reaction does not mean you are doing anything wrong. Your first job is only to slow the situation down, verify through official numbers, and avoid irreversible steps while you are alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours after the call. Later decisions may depend on where the death occurred, whether a hospital, hospice, medical examiner, coroner, police department, or sheriff’s office is involved, and whether you need legal, financial, medical, or bereavement support.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe, prioritize immediate safety and official emergency help.
