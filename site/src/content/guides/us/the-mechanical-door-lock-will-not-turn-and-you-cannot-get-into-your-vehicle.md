# What to do if… the mechanical door lock will not turn and you cannot get into your vehicle (USA)

## Short answer
Stop applying force to the key immediately to prevent snapping it. Move to a safe, well-lit area and call roadside assistance or an automotive locksmith for professional non-destructive entry.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force the key with tools or excessive hand pressure; a snapped key inside the cylinder significantly increases repair costs.
- Do not use "slim jims" or wire hangers on modern vehicles; you risk deploying side-impact airbags or damaging internal wiring and weather seals.
- Do not pour boiling or hot water over the lock or handle; the thermal shock can shatter the door glass and the water will likely refreeze inside the mechanism.
- Do not use WD-40 or multi-purpose oils in the lock cylinder unless it is specifically labeled as a dry lock lubricant.
- Do not leave the vehicle unattended in an active traffic lane or unsafe area while waiting for help.

## What to do now
1. **Verify you are at the correct vehicle and using the correct key.**  
   Confirm the license plate or VIN visible through the windshield. It is common to attempt entry on an identical-looking vehicle in a crowded lot.

2. **Move to a safer pause point.**  
   If you are on a busy roadside or in an unlit area, move to a sidewalk, store, or lobby. If you are in immediate personal danger, call **911**.

3. **Check every potential entry point.**  
   Test the passenger door, rear hatch, trunk, or sliding doors. Mechanical failure often affects only the high-use driver’s side cylinder.

4. **Identify if someone is trapped inside.**  
   If a child, pet, or vulnerable person is inside the vehicle in extreme heat or cold, call **911** immediately. 
   - State that it is a "life-safety lockout."
   - Follow the dispatcher's instructions regarding breaking a window if help is not immediate.

5. **Examine the key for physical damage.**  
   Check for bends, cracks, or worn-down "teeth." If you have a spare key nearby, have it brought to you; a worn key will often fail to engage the cylinder pins properly.

6. **Check for a hidden mechanical key in your fob.**  
   If your vehicle uses a push-button start, look for a release button or slide on the fob to extract the internal mechanical key.
   - Look for a small plastic cap on the door handle that hides the physical keyhole.
   - Only remove this cap if it is designed to pop off (check your vehicle's digital manual or app).

7. **Apply gentle de-icing if the temperature is below freezing.**  
   Use a commercial lock de-icer spray if available.
   - Alternatively, warm the metal part of the key between your palms before inserting.
   - Press firmly against the door frame with your shoulder to break any ice seals on the rubber gaskets before trying the handle.

8. **Perform a single "jiggle" attempt without torque.**  
   Insert the key fully, then pull it back approximately 1 millimeter. Apply very light turning pressure while gently vibrating the key up and down. Stop immediately if there is no movement.

9. **Contact professional roadside assistance or a locksmith.**  
   Call your insurance provider’s roadside line, AAA (**1-800-AAA-HELP**), or a local locksmith.
   - Specifically request "non-destructive entry."
   - Confirm if your membership covers the full cost of a locksmith or only the service call.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine why the lock failed (e.g., salt buildup, mechanical wear, or ice).
- You do not need to replace the lock cylinder or the key immediately.
- You do not need to file an insurance claim for the lockout at this moment.

## Important reassurance
Mechanical lock failures and frozen cylinders are common occurrences that professional technicians are equipped to handle in minutes. Stopping now before the key breaks ensures the repair remains a simple lockout service rather than a complex mechanical replacement.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and safe entry procedures for mechanical vehicle lock failures. It does not provide instructions for ignition lock failures or electronic immobilizer programming.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace the advice of emergency responders or certified automotive technicians. If a life-safety emergency exists, contact 911 immediately. Extreme temperatures can lead to rapid heatstroke or hypothermia for anyone trapped in a vehicle.
