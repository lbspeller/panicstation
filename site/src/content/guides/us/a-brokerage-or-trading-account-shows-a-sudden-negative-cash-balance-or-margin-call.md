# What to do if… a brokerage or trading account shows a sudden negative cash balance or margin call (USA)

## Short answer
Cease all new trading activity and contact your broker’s margin or risk department through a verified phone number to identify the exact cause and the deadline for covering the deficiency. In the U.S., brokerage firms are legally permitted to liquidate your positions without prior notice or your consent to cover a margin debt.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notification; automated liquidation systems can trigger based on real-time price fluctuations.
- Do not place new trades to attempt to recover the balance while the account is in a deficiency state.
- Do not provide account access or security codes to anyone claiming to be "support" who contacted you first.
- Do not use wire transfer instructions received via email or SMS without verifying them inside the broker's secure portal.
- Do not assume a specific "grace period" exists, as house requirements often supersede standard regulatory timelines.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific account status and restrictions currently in place.** Look for labels such as "liquidate-only," "closing-only," or "restricted" in your account dashboard. These indicators mean you cannot open new positions until the balance is corrected.

2. **Document the current state of the account before any automated changes occur.** Take screenshots of the negative balance, the "Buying Power" or "Day Trade Power" figures, and the specific margin call message. Download the "Cash Ledger" or "Transaction History" for the last 48 hours to track exactly when the debit occurred.

3. **Determine the technical cause of the deficiency via the transaction history.** Check for recent options assignments, expired derivatives, or unsettled trades that may have consumed available cash. Review if a specific security’s price drop triggered a "maintenance margin" violation (dropping below the 25 percent FINRA minimum or a higher "house" requirement).

4. **Contact the brokerage firm’s margin desk using a verified official channel.** Use the "Contact Us" number from the official website or the secure app, rather than a number provided in a margin call email. Ask specifically for the "Amount Due," the "Liquidation Deadline," and whether the call is a "Federal/Reg T" call or a "House" call.

5. **Execute a manual trade to reduce risk if you want control over which assets are sold.** If you cannot deposit cash immediately, selling existing positions is the fastest way to reduce the margin requirement. Closing positions yourself allows you to choose which tax lots or assets to exit before the broker's automated system selects them for you.

6. **Secure the account if you suspect the negative balance resulted from unauthorized activity.** If you do not recognize the trades that caused the debit, change your password immediately and notify the broker’s fraud department. Request a temporary "trade freeze" on the account while they investigate the suspicious transactions.

7. **Initiate a formal written dispute if the broker’s explanation is inconsistent with your records.** Send a clear, bulleted message through the broker’s secure message center outlining the error. If the firm does not resolve the issue, you may file an investor complaint via the FINRA Investor Complaint Center or the SEC Tips, Complaints, and Referrals (TCR) system.

8. **Confirm your SIPC coverage status if the concern is the broker’s financial stability.** Note that SIPC protects against the loss of missing assets if a firm fails, up to $500,000 (including $250,000 for cash). As of 2026, money market funds are typically treated as securities ($500,000 limit) rather than cash. SIPC does not protect against market losses or debts you owe the broker.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to close the account permanently.
- You do not need to calculate the long-term tax implications of forced sales until the account is stabilized.
- You do not need to revise your overall investment strategy while the account is in an active margin deficiency.

## Important reassurance
A negative balance or margin call is a standard procedural event in leveraged accounts, often triggered by settlement timing, price volatility, or automated options processing. It does not necessarily indicate a permanent loss of the account if addressed promptly through documented communication with the broker.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate financial shortages in U.S. brokerage accounts. It does not cover long-term debt repayment plans, complex arbitration, or the specifics of non-U.S. jurisdictions.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Margin requirements are subject to the specific terms of your margin agreement and FINRA Rule 4210. Brokerage firms have broad discretion to sell your securities without notice to satisfy a margin deficiency.
