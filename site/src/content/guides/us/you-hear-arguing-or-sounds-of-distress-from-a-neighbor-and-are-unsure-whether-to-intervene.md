# What to do if… you hear arguing or sounds of distress from a neighbor and are unsure whether to intervene (USA)

## Short answer
Do not intervene face-to-face if there is any risk to you. If you think someone may be in immediate danger or violence may be happening, call 911 from a safe place and report what you can hear.

## Do not do these things
- Do not knock on the door, go into the hallway, or try to physically intervene if that could put you at risk.
- Do not shout warnings, threats, or “I’m calling 911” through the wall or door.
- Do not move closer to investigate, record at the door, look through windows, or share audio or video online.
- Do not dismiss screams, repeated crashes, threats, mention of a weapon, or a child sounding unsafe.
- Do not put yourself where you could be trapped, such as outside your unit with the door locked behind you.
- Do not gather other neighbors outside the door.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Go inside your home if you are not already there, lock your door, keep your phone with you, and stay away from the shared door or wall if that feels safer.
2. **Listen only from where you already are.** Do not move closer to confirm what is happening. Notice concrete details you can report, such as the unit, voices, words you heard, crashing sounds, threats, a weapon being mentioned, or a child crying.
3. **Call 911 if it may be an emergency.** Signs that may justify calling 911 include screams for help, sounds of assault, repeated crashing, threats to kill or seriously hurt someone, mention of a weapon, or a child sounding in danger.
4. **Give the dispatcher facts, not guesses.** Say you are a neighbor, give the exact address or unit information you know, describe what you can hear, and follow the dispatcher’s instructions. If you are worried about retaliation, ask how caller information is handled in your area.
5. **If you cannot speak safely, prioritize the address.** If you can whisper, give the address and unit first. If you cannot speak, stay connected and follow any instructions you can. Call if you can; text 911 only if you cannot call. Text-to-911 is not available everywhere, and if it is unavailable you should receive a bounce-back message.
6. **If it is concerning but not clearly an emergency, use the local non-emergency number.** Look it up through an official city, county, or police department website. Say you are hearing a possible domestic disturbance and ask whether they can do a welfare check or another appropriate response.
7. **Use building security only if it improves immediate safety.** If your building has on-site security, a concierge, or a front desk that can contact emergency services or respond safely, contact them from inside your unit.
8. **Make it easier for responders without exposing yourself.** Keep your door locked. If it is safe, be ready to describe the entrance, floor, stairwell, elevator, or unit location from inside your home.
9. **After it quiets down, write a brief factual note for yourself.** Record the date, time, duration, and specific things you heard, such as “loud crash around 10:40pm” or “heard someone say ‘don’t touch me.’”
10. **If you later see the neighbor alone and it feels genuinely safe, keep any check-in simple.** A neutral “Are you okay?” is enough. Do not ask for details, pressure them to report, or mention it where another person may overhear.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide who is right, what the relationship is, or whether it legally counts as domestic violence.
- You do not need to investigate, collect proof, or move closer to confirm.
- You do not need to contact property management unless doing so would improve immediate safety.
- You do not need to make a long-term plan for the neighbor tonight.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, hesitating, or worrying about making things worse is common in this situation. Staying safe and calling for help when you hear signs of possible danger is a reasonable first response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: protect your own safety, report immediate danger, and avoid escalating the situation. Later decisions, especially if this becomes recurring, may need local specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you believe someone is in immediate danger or a violent incident may be happening, call 911.
