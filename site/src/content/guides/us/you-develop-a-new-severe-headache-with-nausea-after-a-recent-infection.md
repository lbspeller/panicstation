# What to do if… you develop a new severe headache with nausea after a recent infection (USA)

## Short answer
A **new severe headache with nausea following a recent infection** requires **immediate medical evaluation today**. If the pain is sudden and reaching peak intensity within seconds, or if you have neurological symptoms, **call 911 or go to an Emergency Department immediately**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if the pain improves if it is **different, more severe, or more sudden** than your usual headaches.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you are experiencing **confusion, dizziness, or vision changes**—call 911 for ambulance transport.
- Do not take additional doses of pain medication if the first dose provided no relief.
- Do not exceed **4,000 mg of acetaminophen in a 24-hour period**, noting that many "multi-symptom" cold and flu medicines already contain this ingredient.
- Do not ignore a **stiff neck, high fever, or sensitivity to light**, as these can indicate a serious secondary infection like meningitis.
- Do not stay alone while waiting for medical care if your mental clarity is decreasing.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 immediately if you experience any "red flag" emergency symptoms.**
   - **Thunderclap headache:** sudden pain that reaches its worst intensity within 60 seconds.
   - **Neurological changes:** face drooping, arm or leg weakness, numbness (especially on one side), or difficulty speaking.
   - **Altered mental state:** sudden confusion, extreme sleepiness, or difficulty staying awake.
   - **Infection signs:** severe headache combined with a stiff neck, high fever, and vomiting.
2. **Seek a same-day medical assessment if you do not have immediate "red flag" symptoms.**
   - Go to an **Emergency Department** if the pain is escalating or you cannot keep fluids down due to vomiting.
   - Contact your **primary care physician** or a **nurse advice line** for an urgent same-day appointment if the headache is new but stable.
3. **Arrange for someone else to stay with you and manage transportation.**
   - Do not attempt to drive if you are nauseated or in severe pain; have a friend or family member drive you or use a taxi / rideshare service.
   - If you are alone and your symptoms are worsening rapidly, call **911** instead of waiting for a private ride.
4. **Prepare a concise summary of your symptoms and recent history for the triage team.**
   - Note the type and timing of your **recent infection** (e.g., sinus, ear, flu, or viral illness).
   - Describe exactly when the **headache began** and how it feels compared to any previous headaches.
   - List all **medications taken today**, including dosages of over-the-counter cold, flu, or pain relief products.
5. **Isolate yourself from bright lights and loud noises while waiting for transport.**
   - Rest in a quiet, dark room to minimize sensory aggravation.
   - Avoid eating or drinking large amounts in case a medical procedure is required upon arrival.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause of the headache (such as migraine versus infection) before seeking help.
- You do not need to wait for lab results or a call back from your doctor if your symptoms are severe.
- You do not need to worry about long-term recovery plans or follow-up appointments until you have been stabilized.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant anxiety when a new, severe pain develops after being sick. Seeking medical attention is the correct safety-first response. Most post-infection headaches are manageable, but professional evaluation ensures that time-sensitive issues are identified and treated early.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions only. Diagnosis and treatment require physical examination and testing (such as imaging or blood work) by qualified medical professionals.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute a medical diagnosis or professional advice. Always err on the side of caution and seek emergency care for any sudden or severe neurological symptoms.
