# What to do if… a last-minute flight reroute adds a transit stop and you are unsure about document requirements (USA)

## Short answer
Before accepting or boarding the rerouted itinerary, identify the added airport and country and ask the airline to check the document requirements for your exact passport and route. If the answer is unclear, ask for a reroute that removes the uncertain stop.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume that a transit stop always avoids border control or extra document requirements.
- Do not accept an overnight stop, airport change, or self-transfer without checking whether you must pass through border control or collect baggage.
- Do not rely on forum posts or unofficial lists when the route has changed at short notice.
- Do not assume an ESTA application will be approved immediately.
- Do not board simply to test whether the connection will work if the airline cannot confirm that your documents cover the rerouted itinerary.

## What to do now
1. **Save the new itinerary.** Take a screenshot showing every airport, country, flight number, connection time, and any terminal or airport change.
2. **Show the airline your passport and the rerouted itinerary.** Ask staff to check the requirements for your exact nationality, passport type, destination, and added transit stop. Ask for written confirmation when available.
3. **Ask whether the reroute changes the physical steps.** Check whether you must pass through border control, collect and re-check baggage, change terminals, change airports, or leave the secure transit area.
4. **For a new U.S. stop, check U.S. permission before travel.** A foreign traveler transiting through the United States may need an appropriate visa. Eligible Visa Waiver Program travelers need valid ESTA approval before travel. Check the rule for your passport and circumstances rather than assuming that a short layover is exempt.
5. **Check any existing ESTA against the passport you will use.** If your passport has changed or the authorization is not approved, do not treat it as confirmed permission to travel.
6. **Ask about checked baggage on a U.S. connection.** Travelers entering the United States from overseas generally need to obtain luggage and bring it through U.S. Customs and Border Protection. Ask the airline what applies to your exact itinerary, including whether preclearance changes the steps.
7. **For a stop outside the United States, use the added country’s official immigration or consular information.** Check the rule for your nationality, passport type, airport, connection type, and whether you must leave the secure transit area.
8. **Ask for a different route when the answer remains unclear.** Request a reroute that removes the uncertain transit stop. If that is not available, ask the airline what options exist before accepting the changed itinerary.

## What can wait
- You do not need to research every possible transit exception now.
- You do not need to make decisions about the rest of the trip until you know whether this rerouted itinerary works for your passport.
- You do not need to argue about the rule at the counter. Focus on the added airport, your passport, your baggage, and whether the airline can confirm boarding eligibility.

## Important reassurance
A changed route can create a real document question, and it is reasonable to pause before accepting it. Work through one added stop at a time: identify the airport, check your passport position, and ask for a different route if the answer remains uncertain.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from the airline, the added transit country’s embassy or consulate, or a qualified immigration professional, especially if you have prior immigration issues, a refugee travel document, dual nationality complications, or a passport mismatch.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, travel, or professional advice. Requirements depend on nationality, passport type, personal circumstances, airport, and itinerary, and they can change. An airline may prevent boarding when the rerouted itinerary requires documents you do not have.
