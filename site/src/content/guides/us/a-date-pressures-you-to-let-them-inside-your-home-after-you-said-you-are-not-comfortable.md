# What to do if… a date pressures you to let them inside your home after you said you are not comfortable (USA)

## Short answer
Do not open the door. Create a physical barrier by locking all entry points and communicate only through the door or a phone to tell them to leave immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door to explain your reasons or to "be polite."
- Do not engage in a back-and-forth negotiation or "debate" your boundary.
- Do not allow them inside for "just one minute" or for any reason like using the bathroom or charging a phone.
- Do not step outside to continue the conversation.
- Do not worry about being "rude" or hurting their feelings; physical safety is the priority.
- Do not ingest alcohol or substances that might impair your ability to stay alert.

## What to do now
1. **Secure your physical space immediately.** Ensure the door is closed and all locks (deadbolts, chains, or bars) are engaged. If you are already at the door, step back and close it firmly.
2. **State your boundary once through the door.** Use a clear, firm voice: “I am not comfortable letting anyone in tonight. Please leave now.” Do not provide excuses or justifications.
3. **Cease all further communication if they continue to push.** You do not owe them a response to their questions or protests. Silence can be a tool to signal that the interaction is over.
4. **Establish a live connection with a trusted person.** Call a friend or family member, put them on speakerphone, and tell them exactly what is happening: “I am at my door, a date is refusing to leave, and I need you to stay on the line with me.”
5. **Call 911 if they refuse to leave or you feel in danger.** If they attempt to turn the knob, lean against the door, shout, or threaten you, contact emergency services immediately. Tell the dispatcher your address and that an unwanted person is attempting to enter or refusing to leave your property.
6. **Alert building security or neighbors if possible.** If you live in an apartment with a front desk or have a roommate, use your phone or an internal intercom to ask them to come to your floor or watch the entrance.
7. **Document the behavior from a safe distance.** Once you are in a secure room away from the door, write down the time, what was said, and any specific behaviors that made you feel unsafe.
8. **Contact a confidential advocate for stabilization.** Call the **National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN) at 800-656-HOPE (4673)** or text **HOPE to 64673**. They can help you process the interaction and plan for your immediate safety without pressure to report.
9. **Seek support for domestic or dating violence if this is a pattern.** Contact the **National Domestic Violence Hotline at 800-799-SAFE (7233)** or text **START to 88788**. They provide specific resources for navigating coercion and boundary-crossing in relationships.
10. **Prioritize medical care if any unwanted contact occurred.** If the person touched you against your will or caused injury, go to an urgent care center or Emergency Room. You can ask for a **Sexual Assault Forensic Exam (SAFE)** or a **SANE (Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner)**; these exams are time-sensitive and typically most effective within 120 hours.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a police report tonight.
- You do not need to "block" them or send a final text message until you are in a calmer state of mind.
- You do not need to justify your reaction to yourself or anyone else.
- You do not need to figure out "what went wrong" with the date.

## Important reassurance
Feeling a "freeze" response, being overly polite to de-escalate, or second-guessing your fear are all natural biological reactions to a perceived threat. These responses do not mean you did anything wrong. Your home is your private space, and you have the absolute right to control who enters it.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate boundary violation at your home. It does not cover long-term legal strategies, restraining orders, or psychological recovery.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional legal or medical advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If you are safe but shaken, a confidential hotline can help you stabilize and decide your next steps without pressure.
