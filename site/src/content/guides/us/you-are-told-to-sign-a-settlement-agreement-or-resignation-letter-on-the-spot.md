# What to do if… you are told to sign a settlement agreement or resignation letter on the spot (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the exact termination date and reason in writing immediately, then contact your insurer and plan administrator to identify if the cancellation was an administrative error that can be corrected retroactively.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the cancellation is final until you have confirmed it with both the employer and the insurance carrier.
- Do not miss the 30-day or 60-day windows for backup coverage while waiting for an error to be fixed.
- Do not stop essential medical treatments or prescriptions; focus on documenting the disruption for later reimbursement.
- Do not provide verbal-only reports; avoid relying on phone calls without recording reference numbers or names.
- Do not sign waivers or "decline coverage" forms provided by HR until you have confirmed your alternative options.

## What to do now
1. **Request a formal written notice of termination from your employer or plan administrator.**
   - Ask for the specific effective date of the cancellation.
   - Ask for the specific reason (such as non-payment, eligibility change, or administrative error).
   - Ask for a copy of the notification sent to the insurer.
   - Verify if the cancellation applies only to the employee or includes all dependents.

2. **Call the insurance company’s member services line to verify their records.**
   - Ask for the "termination date" currently listed in their system.
   - Ask if a "reinstatement" is possible if the employer submits a correction.
   - Request a reference number for the call and the name of the representative.

3. **Check your most recent paystubs for benefits deductions.**
   - Confirm if premiums were still being deducted after the alleged termination date.
   - Save digital or physical copies of these stubs as evidence of payment.

4. **Identify your applicable backup enrollment windows immediately.**
   - **COBRA:** You generally have 60 days from the later of your coverage loss or the date you receive your COBRA election notice to enroll.
   - **Marketplace (HealthCare.gov):** You generally have 60 days from the loss of qualifying coverage to enroll via a Special Enrollment Period (SEP).
   - **New Job-Based Plan:** If you are joining a spouse’s plan, you typically have only 30 days from the loss of prior coverage to notify their HR department.
   - **Medicaid/CHIP:** If you lost Medicaid or CHIP, the standard Marketplace SEP window is 60 days as of 2026.

5. **Prepare for strict documentation requirements if using a Marketplace SEP.**
   - Gather your "Certificate of Creditable Coverage" or termination letter from your previous insurer.
   - Note that as of 2026, most Marketplace applications require document verification before enrollment is finalized.

6. **Manage immediate medical needs as a "self-pay" patient temporarily.**
   - Tell your doctor or pharmacist you have a "temporary coverage disruption."
   - Ask for the "cash price" or "self-pay rate" for essential services.
   - Keep all itemized receipts and invoices for retroactive claims once coverage is restored.

7. **Escalate to the Employee Benefits Security Administration (EBSA) if the error is not fixed.**
   - Contact the U.S. Department of Labor EBSA for assistance with employer-sponsored plans.
   - Contact your State Department of Insurance if the issue involves the insurance company’s conduct or a private individual policy.

## What can wait
- Determining who was specifically at fault for the error.
- Filing formal legal complaints or lawsuits.
- Researching long-term policy changes for next year.
- Deciding on supplemental or secondary insurance.

## Important reassurance
Sudden benefit cancellations are frequently the result of data transmission errors between payroll systems and insurance carriers. These errors are often reversible and coverage can typically be reinstated retroactively to the date it was lost, provided you act within the required administrative windows.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate loss of health coverage. It does not cover long-term legal strategy, specific medical advice, or detailed tax implications of different insurance types.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal, financial, or medical advice. Health insurance regulations and deadlines vary by state and plan type. Always confirm deadlines with your specific plan administrator and the relevant government agencies.
