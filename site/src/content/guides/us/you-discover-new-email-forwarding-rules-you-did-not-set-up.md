# What to do if… you discover new email forwarding rules you did not set up (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the situation as an active account takeover: immediately delete the unauthorized rules and secure the account by changing the password and enabling multi-factor authentication (MFA) through a direct, manual login.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links in any security alerts or emails claiming to fix the issue.
- Don’t assume the issue is a technical glitch; forwarding rules are a primary method used to intercept password resets and financial data.
- Don’t reuse the compromised password on any other service or site.
- Don’t stop at deleting the rule; if the attacker has your password, they will simply recreate the rule until the account is locked down.
- Don’t ignore "connected apps" or "third-party permissions" in your settings, as these can provide persistent access even after a password change.

## What to do now
1. **Access your account through a manual, secure route.** Open a fresh browser window on a trusted device and type the URL of your email provider (e.g., mail.google.com, outlook.com) directly into the address bar rather than using bookmarks or links.
2. **Locate and delete all unauthorized forwarding addresses and rules.** **You must check both the "Forwarding" section and the "Rules" or "Filters" section of your settings.**
   - Remove any email addresses you do not recognize.
   - Look for rules that automatically delete, archive, or mark messages as read, especially those targeting keywords like "security," "password," "bank," "verify," or "invoice."
3. **Terminate all other active sessions and remove unknown devices.** **Navigate to your account security or recent activity page and select the option to "Sign out of all other sessions."**
   - Review the list of "trusted devices" or "connected apps" and remove anything unfamiliar.
4. **Update your password to a unique, 16-character minimum passphrase.** **Ensure this password is not used for any other account and does not contain personal information easily found online.**
5. **Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) using the strongest method available.** **Prioritize passkeys or physical security keys over SMS or email codes if your provider supports them.**
   - If using an authenticator app, ensure you generate and store new backup/recovery codes in a safe, offline location.
6. **Verify and update your account recovery information.** **Check that the recovery phone number and secondary email address listed are current and belong solely to you.**
7. **Inspect your sent, deleted, and archived folders for unauthorized activity.** **Look for outgoing spam, bank notifications, or password reset confirmations that you did not initiate.**
   - If you find evidence of financial accounts being targeted, contact those institutions immediately.
8. **Report the incident to the appropriate federal authorities.** **If money was lost or identity theft is suspected, file a report at IdentityTheft.gov and the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (ic3.gov).**
   - For work or school accounts, notify your IT department or security team immediately to allow for server-side investigation.

## What can wait
- Identifying exactly how the attacker gained access is not necessary for immediate stabilization.
- Replacing the device you are currently using is not required if you can successfully secure the account through a browser.
- Notifying every contact in your address book can wait until you have confirmed the account is no longer sending unauthorized messages.

## Important reassurance
Finding unauthorized rules is a high-confidence signal of a compromise, but it is a manageable situation. By following these steps to isolate the hazard and secure your credentials, you stop the attacker's ability to monitor your communications or hijack other linked accounts.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an email account takeover. It does not cover deep forensic device analysis or the long-term recovery of stolen financial funds.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and is not legal or professional cybersecurity advice. If you suspect your Social Security number or financial identity has been fully compromised, refer to official government resources at IdentityTheft.gov for a comprehensive recovery plan.
