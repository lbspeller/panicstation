# What to do if… you are charged for an expensive purchase you did not make but it is still “pending” (USA)

## Short answer
Lock or freeze the card immediately through your bank's app and call the issuer's fraud department to report the transaction as unauthorized before it posts.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait for the charge to "clear" or "post" before taking action.
- Don’t use phone numbers provided in suspicious text or email alerts about the charge.
- Don’t share images of the pending transaction on social media.
- Don’t unfreeze the card to see if other charges are blocked.
- Don’t assume a "pending" status means the money hasn't already been set aside.
- Don’t contact the merchant using a phone number listed in the transaction description if you suspect fraud.

## What to do now
1. **Lock or freeze the card immediately via your bank’s official mobile app or website.**
   - This prevents new unauthorized charges from being attempted while you handle the current one.
   - If the card is in a digital wallet (Apple Pay, Google Pay), check for any linked devices you do not recognize.
2. **Review your household and authorized users for "friendly fraud" or accidental purchases.**
   - Check with anyone who has access to the card or account to see if they made the purchase.
   - Verify if the merchant name is a parent company for a service you actually use (e.g., "LBI*Services" for a specific subscription).
3. **Call the official customer service or fraud number on the back of your physical card.**
   - If you do not have the card, use the number from your bank’s official website.
   - Tell the representative: "I am reporting an unauthorized pending transaction for [Amount] at [Merchant]."
   - Ask them to "close the compromised card and issue a new account number."
4. **Request the representative to "void the authorization" if possible.**
   - While banks often say they cannot stop a pending charge, a fraud report can sometimes trigger an immediate block on the settlement.
   - Ask for a "Fraud Case Number" and write it down immediately.
5. **Report the specific impact if using a debit card.**
   - If the pending hold has depleted your checking account and will cause other payments to fail, inform the bank.
   - Ask if they can provide a "provisional credit" or expedited release of the hold due to the unauthorized nature of the charge.
6. **Capture a screenshot or record the exact details of the pending entry.**
   - Note the merchant name, date, time, and any "Transaction ID" or "Reference Number" shown.
   - This documentation is required if the charge posts and you must file a formal dispute.
7. **Secure your primary email and banking login credentials.**
   - Change your email password first, as many account takeovers start there.
   - Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) using an authenticator app rather than just SMS if available.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file an IdentityTheft.gov report unless you see evidence of other compromised accounts or new credit lines.
- You do not need to file a police report immediately unless the bank requires it for their investigation or the theft occurred physically.
- You do not need to close the entire bank account; usually, replacing the specific card is sufficient.

## Important reassurance
Seeing a large amount of money "pending" or missing from your balance is stressful, but the "pending" status is actually a window of opportunity to alert your bank. By locking the card and reporting it now, you are following the exact procedure required to protect your rights and limit your legal liability.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate first steps for addressing an unauthorized pending transaction on a US-based credit or debit card. It does not cover long-term identity restoration or credit repair.

## Important note
Federal protections vary by card type. For credit cards, the Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA) limits liability for unauthorized use to $50. For debit cards, the Electronic Fund Transfer Act (EFTA) / Regulation E limits liability to $50 if reported within 2 business days, but this can rise to $500 or more if reporting is delayed. Many major issuers offer "Zero Liability" policies that may exceed these federal minimums.
