# What to do if… a dependent adult has been left without a caregiver due to a sudden death (USA)

## Short answer
Make sure the dependent adult is safe and supervised today. Call 911 if there is immediate danger or an urgent medical emergency; otherwise contact Eldercare Locator, your local Area Agency on Aging, 211, or Adult Protective Services if the person cannot be safely left without care.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave the person alone if they cannot safely manage basic needs such as medications, mobility, toileting, meals, confusion, or wandering.
- Do not assume an emergency room visit is the route to services unless there is a true medical emergency.
- Do not agree in the moment to become the full-time caregiver if you cannot do it safely.
- Do not stop existing services, such as home health, aides, meal delivery, equipment, or alarm systems, until you know what is already in place.
- Do not let inheritance, funeral, or family conflict issues take priority over immediate care and supervision.

## What to do now
1. **Check immediate safety right now.**  
   Check whether the person is alone, whether the home is safe enough for the moment, whether they have food and safe temperature control, and whether essential medications are due today. If they are in immediate danger or there is an urgent medical emergency, call **911**.

2. **Make a same-day supervision plan.**  
   Identify who can be physically present for the next few hours and overnight if needed. If nobody can safely stay, treat the situation as urgent and move to the next steps now.

3. **Contact local help using the fastest route you can reach.**  
   - Call **Eldercare Locator at 1-800-677-1116** to be connected with local aging services, including the local Area Agency on Aging.
   - Call **211** for local referrals, such as caregiver resources, respite options, disability supports, food, transportation, or home care referrals.
   - If 211 does not connect from your phone, use the 211 local search page or ask Eldercare Locator for the right local route.

   Say: **“Their primary caregiver died, and they cannot be safely left alone. We need urgent help today.”**

4. **Contact Adult Protective Services if the person may be without necessary care.**  
   If the person is at risk of neglect, self-neglect, exploitation, or being left without essential support, contact Adult Protective Services in the person’s county or state. Eldercare Locator or 211 can usually help you find the right APS contact. If danger is immediate, call **911**.

5. **Look for existing services before making new decisions.**  
   Check paperwork, fridge notes, phone contacts, pill packs, calendars, or emails for a Medicaid plan, managed care case manager, home health agency, disability services contact, meal delivery, transport service, senior center, or medical alert provider. Call any listed coordinator or provider and ask what emergency coverage, reassessment, or short-notice support is available.

6. **Make one essentials list for every agency call.**  
   Write down or photograph:
   - The person’s name, address, phone number, and how to get into the home
   - What they cannot safely do alone
   - Known diagnoses, mobility limits, confusion, falls, wandering, oxygen, diabetes, swallowing problems, or other immediate risks
   - Medication names if available, and what is due today
   - Names and phone numbers of relatives, neighbors, aides, doctors, case managers, or agencies already involved
   - Who has keys, a lockbox code, or safe access to the home

7. **Use clear safety language if tonight is the problem.**  
   Say plainly: **“I do not think they can be safely left alone overnight.”** This gives services the clearest information to screen for crisis respite, urgent in-home support, welfare checks, APS involvement, or other local options.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide long-term living arrangements today.
- You do not need to settle guardianship, power of attorney, estate matters, or financial authority before asking for urgent help.
- You do not need to choose a permanent caregiver today.
- You do not need to resolve family disagreements before making a safe plan for tonight and tomorrow.

## Important reassurance
A sudden caregiver death can create a real care gap very quickly. The immediate task is not to solve the whole future; it is to keep the person safe, avoid unsafe promises, and get local services involved.

## Scope note
These are first steps for immediate safety and rapid connection to local services. Later decisions about care arrangements, benefits, housing, authority, or estate issues may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If anyone is in immediate danger, call 911.
