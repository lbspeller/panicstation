# What to do if… you suspect your partner is interfering with your contraception or pregnancy choices (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause before you act, then contact confidential support or a clinic using a device and account your partner cannot access. If pregnancy is possible, ask for medical advice as soon as you safely can, because some options are time-sensitive.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront your partner right away if it could lead to retaliation, violence, forced sex, or tighter control.
- Do not use a shared phone, shared computer, shared cloud account, or shared location app to look for help if monitoring is possible.
- Do not assume a promise or explanation is enough if your contraception, pregnancy choices, or sexual consent are being pressured or interfered with.
- Do not stop or change prescribed contraception in a panic; ask a clinician, pharmacist, or clinic what is safest for your situation.
- Do not use insurance, portals, email, or mailed paperwork without asking about privacy first if your partner may see bills, statements, messages, or explanations of benefits.
- Do not gather evidence if doing so could put you at more risk.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Move, if you can, to somewhere you can think and communicate without being overheard or trapped, such as a public place, a clinic, a trusted person’s home, work, school, or another place where you can leave. If you are in immediate danger, call **911**.
2. **Use safer communication.** Use a friend’s phone, a work phone, a library computer, or another device your partner cannot access. Avoid shared Apple, Google, phone-plan, email, browser, and location-sharing accounts if you think monitoring is possible.
3. **Contact domestic violence support.** Ask the National Domestic Violence Hotline for help with the next safe steps: safer communication, where to go if things escalate, and how to keep essentials accessible. If calling is unsafe, use chat or text from a safer device.
4. **Contact a sexual or reproductive health provider.** Tell the clinic, pharmacist, or provider: “I’m worried a partner is interfering with contraception or pregnancy decisions, and I need confidential options.” Ask about pregnancy testing, emergency contraception, contraception that is harder for a partner to tamper with, and STI testing if condoms were interfered with or sex was pressured.
5. **Ask about emergency contraception promptly if there has been recent unprotected sex, condom interference, missed pills, or forced sex.** Emergency contraception generally works best as soon as possible. A clinic or pharmacist can tell you what options are available now and what timing applies.
6. **Protect privacy before booking or paying.** Before giving insurance details, ask the clinic what could appear on bills, portals, mailed statements, texts, calls, or explanations of benefits. Ask whether confidential communications, self-pay, sliding-scale care, or referral to another provider is safer for you.
7. **Tell one trusted person if it is safe.** Keep it practical: where you are, how to reach you safely, whether you need a ride, and what they should do if you use a code word.
8. **If you may want a record later, keep it simple and safe.** Only if it will not increase risk, write down dates and brief facts such as missing pills, damaged condoms, threats, pressure, or refusal to use agreed contraception. Store it somewhere your partner cannot access.
9. **If what happened may have been sexual assault, or you are unsure, contact sexual assault support.** RAINN can help you talk through options and find local services. You do not have to decide about reporting to get support.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report to police, seek a protective order, leave the relationship, or tell family.
- You do not need proof before contacting a hotline, clinic, advocate, pharmacist, or sexual assault service.
- You do not need to decide today what contraception you want long term.
- You can decide later whether you want legal advice, more documentation, or a longer safety plan.

## Important reassurance
Feeling unsure, scared, embarrassed, or conflicted does not mean you are overreacting. Your wishes about pregnancy, contraception, and sex matter, and you can ask for help without committing to any major decision today.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: reduce immediate risk, protect your privacy where possible, and connect with confidential support and medical care. Later decisions may need help from advocates, clinicians, or legal specialists.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If pregnancy, sexual assault, STI exposure, injury, or other health harm is possible, seek clinical care as soon as you safely can.
