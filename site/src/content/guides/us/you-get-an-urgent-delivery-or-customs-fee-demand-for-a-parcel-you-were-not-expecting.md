# What to do if… you get an urgent delivery or customs fee demand for a parcel you were not expecting (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the message as suspicious and do not interact with any links or numbers provided; instead, verify the status through the carrier’s official website or official customer service line found independently.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, scan QR codes, or download "tracking apps" from the message.
- Don’t reply to the sender or call numbers included in the notification.
- Don’t provide personal identifiers like Social Security numbers or bank logins to "verify" a delivery.
- Don’t pay fees using gift cards, cryptocurrency, or wire transfers.
- Don’t trust "official" logos or "tracking numbers" contained within the message body.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the message and avoid interaction.**
   - Do not click anywhere in the message.
   - Take a screenshot that captures the sender’s number or email address, the date, and the content.

2. **Verify the shipment through a known official channel.**
   - Open a new browser window and go directly to the carrier’s official site (e.g., usps.com, ups.com, fedex.com).
   - Enter the tracking number manually into the carrier’s official search tool.
   - If the number is not recognized or the details do not match, the message is likely fraudulent.

3. **Confirm if you or your household are expecting an international shipment.**
   - Review your order history from trusted retailers.
   - Note that legitimate carriers rarely text regarding customs fees unless you have specifically signed up for that carrier's tracking alerts for a specific package.

4. **Verify customs demands through official government services if CBP is cited.**
   - Use only official U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) payment platforms such as Pay.gov.
   - Contact the carrier’s international department using a verified number to ask if duties are outstanding.

5. **Mitigate immediate financial or data exposure if you interacted.**
   - **If you entered payment details:** Contact your bank or card issuer immediately using the number on the back of your card to report fraudulent activity.
   - **If you entered a password:** Change the password for that account immediately, and update it on any other site where you reuse the same credentials.
   - **If you shared a Social Security number:** Visit IdentityTheft.gov to begin a recovery plan.

6. **Report the incident to the relevant authorities.**
   - **Forward suspicious texts to 7226 (SPAM):** This alerts your mobile provider to the sender.
   - **Email USPS-related smishing to spam@uspis.gov:** Include your name and the screenshot showing the sender's number and date.
   - **File a report at ReportFraud.ftc.gov:** This helps the Federal Trade Commission track and investigate fraud patterns.
   - **Report to the FBI at ic3.gov:** Use this for any incident involving financial loss or identity theft.

## What can wait
- You do not need to figure out how your number was obtained today.
- You do not need to respond to the sender to "opt out" or "stop" the messages.
- You do not need to immediately initiate a full credit freeze unless you are certain sensitive identity data (like an SSN) was successfully transmitted.

## Important reassurance
These messages use high-pressure language to provoke a quick response. Pausing to verify through an official website is a standard safety procedure and will not cause you to lose a legitimate package.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for responding to suspicious delivery demands. If you have already lost funds or personal data, you will need to follow your financial institution's specific fraud recovery protocols.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. If you suspect a financial loss is in progress, contact your bank immediately using their verified official contact methods.
