# What to do if… a leak from the unit above is damaging your home and the responsible party is not responding (USA)

## Short answer
Move away from standing water, electrical hazards, and any sagging ceiling. Contact the building’s emergency maintenance or management route immediately so the source can be shut off without waiting for the person upstairs.

## Do not do these things
- Do not step into standing water to reach a breaker panel or use an electrical device.
- Do not touch a wet breaker panel.
- Do not guess which breaker to switch off if you are unsure.
- Do not stand under a bulging or sagging ceiling.
- Do not puncture a bulging ceiling.
- Do not force entry into the unit above.
- Do not keep relying only on calls to the upstairs occupant if the leak is continuing.
- Do not discard damaged items before documenting them unless keeping them would be unsafe.
- Do not use fans or other powered equipment until electrical safety is established.
- Do not handle water that may contain sewage or other contamination without professional guidance.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer area.**
   - Keep people and pets away from standing water, wet electrical fixtures, and any bulging or sagging ceiling.
   - If there is sparking, smoke, rapid flooding, or an immediate risk of ceiling collapse, leave the affected area and call 911 or the fire department from a safe location.

2. **Only switch off power if you can do so safely.**
   - If the breaker panel is dry, you can reach it without entering standing water, and you know which switch controls the affected area or the main power, switch it off.
   - If the panel is wet, the route is unsafe, or you are unsure which switch to use, do not touch the panel or guess. Call building maintenance or a licensed electrician.

3. **Contact the building route that can arrange shutoff and authorized access.**
   - If you rent, call your landlord, property manager, or emergency maintenance contact.
   - If you are in a condominium or other managed building, call the association or building management emergency contact.
   - Say that an active leak from the unit above is entering your home and ask them to arrange the appropriate water shutoff and authorized access procedure.
   - You can contact the upstairs occupant once if it is safe, but do not wait for a reply before escalating.

4. **Call a licensed emergency plumber if building help is unavailable.**
   - Explain that the leak is coming from another unit in a multi-unit building.
   - Ask whether they can safely stop the source or advise on the correct shutoff route.
   - They may need building authorization or access from management.

5. **Limit damage only from a safe position.**
   - Move electronics, furniture, rugs, and valuables away from the drip area if you can do so without entering standing water or standing under a damaged ceiling.
   - Use buckets or towels only where it is safe.
   - If the water may contain sewage or other contamination, avoid contact and tell building management, your insurer, or a restoration professional.

6. **Document the situation.**
   - Take photos or video of the leak, affected ceiling and walls, standing water, and damaged belongings from a safe position.
   - Keep a simple log of when the leak started, who you contacted, and any replies.
   - Save messages, emails, and receipts.

7. **Notify your insurer promptly.**
   - Contact your renters or homeowners insurer and report water damage from the unit above.
   - Ask what temporary steps they want documented, what may be covered, and whether they have a preferred restoration provider.
   - Keep receipts for reasonable emergency supplies and professional visits.

8. **Start safe drying after the leak is stopped.**
   - Once electrical safety is established and there is no reason to suspect contamination, begin drying wet areas promptly with ventilation, fans, or a dehumidifier.
   - If you are unsure whether the water may be contaminated, ask a restoration professional before cleanup.
   - Aim to dry wet or damp materials within 24–48 hours where possible.
   - Ask a restoration professional about soaked drywall, hidden moisture, extensive damage, or water that may be contaminated.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove fault while the leak is active.
- You do not need to argue with the upstairs occupant.
- You do not need to decide on permanent repairs immediately.
- You do not need to understand every insurance detail before reporting the damage.
- You do not need to decide today who will ultimately pay.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to solve responsibility or repairs all at once. Reaching a safe area, getting the building response started, documenting the damage, and beginning safe drying are enough for the first stage.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repairs, responsibility, insurance coverage, and building procedures may need help from your property manager, insurer, a licensed contractor, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, insurance, electrical, plumbing, or other professional advice. If there is an immediate danger, leave the affected area and call 911 or the fire department from a safe location.
