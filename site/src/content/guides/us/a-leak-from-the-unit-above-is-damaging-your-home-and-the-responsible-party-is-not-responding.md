# What to do if… a leak from the unit above is damaging your home and the responsible party is not responding (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate electrical hazards immediately, then contact building management or an emergency plumber to execute a building-wide or unit-line water shut-off.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t enter standing water to reach an electrical panel or outlet.
- Don’t attempt to force entry into the neighboring unit yourself.
- Don’t use light switches or ceiling fans if water is dripping through the fixture.
- Don’t discard damaged items until they are photographed and your insurer provides clearance.
- Don’t wait for the neighbor to return before escalating to building maintenance.

## What to do now
1. **Clear the area and check for electrical contact.**
   - **Move people and pets away from the drip zone immediately.**
   - **Check if water is contacting outlets, power strips, or light fixtures.**
   - **Shut off power to the affected room at the breaker only if the panel and the path to it are completely dry.**
   - **If the floor is wet near the electrical panel, do not touch it; call the utility company or an electrician to pull the meter or disconnect service.**
2. **Contact the building's 24-hour emergency response line.**
   - **Call the property manager, HOA emergency line, or building superintendent immediately.**
   - **State clearly: "Active water intrusion from the unit above; the resident is not responding; emergency entry and water shut-off required."**
   - **Request a "master shut-off" if unit-level valves are inaccessible.**
3. **Locate your unit's internal shut-off valves if applicable.**
   - **Check for shut-off valves behind your own toilets or under sinks if the leak appears related to a shared vertical stack.**
   - **Look for a main unit shut-off valve, typically located near the water heater or in a utility closet, to see if it affects the flow.**
4. **Mitigate secondary damage in the next 15 minutes.**
   - **Place buckets or waterproof bins under drips to catch falling water.**
   - **Lay towels inside the buckets to prevent splashing and further floor damage.**
   - **Move furniture, rugs, and electronics to a dry room.**
   - **If the ceiling is bulging or sagging, stay clear of the area as this indicates a collapse risk.**
5. **Document the scene for insurance and liability.**
   - **Take video of the water entering your home, including the ceiling, walls, and floor.**
   - **Photograph individual damaged items, focusing on serial numbers for electronics.**
   - **Log the exact time the leak was discovered and every attempt made to contact the neighbor and building management.**
6. **Begin moisture extraction within the first hour of stopping the leak.**
   - **Use a wet/dry vacuum to remove standing water from floors if safe to use the equipment.**
   - **Open windows and run fans or dehumidifiers only if the electrical system has been cleared by a professional.**
   - **Contact your renters or homeowners insurance company to file a "water intrusion" claim.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the exact cause of the leak (e.g., a burst pipe vs. an overflowing tub) right now.
- You do not need to negotiate repair costs with the upstairs neighbor today.
- You do not need to hire a permanent reconstruction contractor until the building has certified the leak is stopped and the structure is dry.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a loss of control when a hazard originates outside your home. Building management and HOAs have established protocols for emergency entry and water shut-offs when a resident is unresponsive. Following these steps ensures you have prioritized safety and fulfilled your "duty to mitigate" for insurance purposes.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for active water intrusion in multi-unit buildings. It does not cover long-term mold remediation, subrogation legalities, or permanent structural repairs.

## Important note
Water contacting electrical systems creates a high risk of lethal shock or fire. If you see sparks, hear buzzing, or smell ozone, evacuate the unit and call 911. Under 2026 NEC standards, any electrical components submerged or saturated by water typically require professional replacement.
