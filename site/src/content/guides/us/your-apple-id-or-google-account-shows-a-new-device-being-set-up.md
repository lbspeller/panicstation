# What to do if… your Apple ID or Google Account shows a new device being set up (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as urgent until you confirm it was you. Deny the sign-in if you can, then use a trusted device to change the password and remove any unfamiliar devices, sessions, recovery details, and connected apps.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t approve any sign-in prompt you did not personally start.
- Don’t tap links in alert emails or texts to “secure your account”; go through device Settings or the official account security page.
- Don’t type your password into pop-ups, ads, search-result pages, or lookalike sites.
- Don’t reuse an old password, or a slightly changed version of one.
- Don’t stop after changing the password; access can sometimes continue through recovery details, signed-in sessions, forwarding settings, or connected apps.
- Don’t contact the unknown device or person.

## What to do now
1. **Use a device you trust.**
   - Use your own phone, tablet, or computer if you still control it.
   - Avoid making account changes from a public computer or a link in a message.

2. **Deny the sign-in alert if it is still on screen.**
   - **Apple:** choose **Don’t Allow** if the Apple Account sign-in is not yours.
   - **Google:** choose the option that says it was not you, such as **No, it wasn’t me**, if the activity is not yours.

3. **Change the password from official settings.**
   - **Apple Account:** on iPhone or iPad, go to **Settings → your name → Sign-In & Security → Change Password**. On Mac, use **System Settings → your name → Sign-In & Security → Change Password**.
   - **Google Account:** go to your Google Account security settings and change the password.
   - Use a brand-new password you do not use anywhere else.

4. **Remove unfamiliar devices and sessions.**
   - **Apple:** review the device list under your Apple Account and remove any device you do not recognize.
   - **Google:** go to **Security → Your devices → Manage all devices** and sign out of any device or session that is not yours.
   - If the same unfamiliar device type appears more than once, review each session and sign out of any you are unsure about.

5. **Check recovery details and sign-in methods.**
   - **Apple:** check that trusted phone numbers, trusted devices, email addresses, and reachable-at details are yours.
   - **Google:** check recovery phone, recovery email, alternate email, two-step verification methods, and apps with account access.
   - Remove anything unfamiliar, then save the changes.

6. **Check for quiet access in email and connected services.**
   - **Gmail:** look for unfamiliar forwarding, filters, mail delegation, scheduled emails, POP or IMAP access, and “send mail as” addresses.
   - **Google:** remove apps or services with account access that you do not recognize.
   - **Apple:** check recent purchases, payment methods, and any Apple Account details that changed without you.

7. **Do a quick damage check if money or identity information could be exposed.**
   - Check recent Apple, Google Pay, Google Play, and saved-card activity if you use them.
   - If you see an unauthorized charge, contact your card issuer or bank using the number on your card or in your banking app.
   - If you see a SIM swap, number change, or sudden loss of mobile service, contact your mobile carrier from another phone or secure channel.

8. **Use official recovery or reporting routes if you cannot regain control quickly.**
   - Use Apple or Google’s official recovery steps from a trusted device.
   - Avoid search-result ads and unofficial “recovery” services.
   - If identity theft is involved, use IdentityTheft.gov.
   - If money was stolen or there was a serious internet-fraud attempt, consider reporting it to the FBI’s IC3.
   - Keep a simple note of the time of the alert, what you denied or changed, and screenshots if safe.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out how this happened before securing the account.
- You do not need to make long-term decisions about every device, app, or password right now.
- You do not need to factory reset your phone unless official support or a trusted technical professional advises it.
- You do not need to report anything before you have taken the immediate account-security steps, unless money is actively being moved or you are locked out.

## Important reassurance
A new-device alert can feel alarming, but it is also an opportunity to block or limit access. The useful goal right now is narrow: stop this sign-in, close off easy re-entry, and check for obvious damage.

## Scope note
These are first steps for an Apple Account or Google Account sign-in alert. Later decisions about identity theft, financial loss, repeated compromise, malware, or device replacement may need help from Apple, Google, your financial institution, your mobile carrier, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. If you are unsure whether a prompt or page is real, close it and go directly through device Settings or an official account page.
