# What to do if… you smell a strong fuel or solvent odour in a garage or utility area and cannot identify the source (USA)

## Short answer
Treat a strong unidentified fuel or solvent odor as a possible fire, explosion, or inhalation risk. Avoid sparks, get everyone into fresh air, and call 911 from a safe location if natural gas, propane, or a strong unknown vapor is possible.

## Do not do these things
- Do not flip light switches, use fans, unplug or plug in devices, press doorbells, or use anything electrical in the affected area.
- Do not use a phone while you are still in or beside the odor; move to clean air first.
- Do not smoke, light matches, use lighters, or use candles.
- Do not start vehicles, use power tools, run appliances, or operate equipment in or near the fumes.
- Do not use a powered garage door opener or press a remote while you are near the odor.
- Do not search for the source by opening containers, moving fuel cans, sniffing close up, or checking behind appliances.
- Do not re-enter if anyone feels dizzy, sick, confused, short of breath, or has a headache.

## What to do now
1. **Leave the affected area calmly.** Step out of the garage, utility room, or nearby indoor space without turning anything on or off.
2. **Move people and pets to fresh air.** Go outside if you can, and stay away from the garage door, utility room door, windows, and vents where fumes may come out.
3. **Avoid creating sparks on the way out.** Leave lights, fans, appliances, thermostats, garage door openers, and vehicles exactly as they are.
4. **Ventilate only if it does not delay leaving.** If an exterior door or window is already on your way out and can be opened without using electricity, you may leave it open. Do not stay inside to air the area out.
5. **If the odor could be natural gas, propane, or you are not sure, call 911 from a safe place.** After that, call your gas utility emergency line if you know it and can do so without going back near the odor.
6. **If it seems more like gasoline, paint thinner, fuel cans, or solvent fumes, still stay out.** Call 911 if the smell is strong, spreading, near a furnace or water heater, near a vehicle, or you cannot identify the source quickly from outside.
7. **If anyone has symptoms, keep them in fresh air.** Call 911 for trouble breathing, fainting, confusion, or severe, worsening, or concerning symptoms. For possible poisoning advice after everyone is in fresh air, you can also contact Poison Help at 1-800-222-1222.
8. **Wait for responders outside.** Tell them where the odor is strongest, whether fuel or solvents are stored there, whether there are gas appliances nearby, and whether anyone has symptoms.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact source right now.
- You do not need to clean a spill, move containers, start the car, check appliances, or sort stored fuel cans.
- You do not need to decide about repairs, landlord messages, cleanup, or insurance until the fire department, gas utility, or a qualified person says the area is safe.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to treat an unexplained fuel or solvent smell as serious even if you are not sure what it is. Leaving, avoiding sparks, and calling from clean air are steady first steps that reduce the risk while someone qualified checks the area.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Finding the source, clearing the fumes, repairing equipment, handling a spill, or deciding when the space is safe to use again may need the fire department, gas utility, Poison Help, a qualified technician, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, environmental, engineering, or other professional advice. Follow instructions from 911, emergency responders, your gas utility, Poison Help, and local authorities.
