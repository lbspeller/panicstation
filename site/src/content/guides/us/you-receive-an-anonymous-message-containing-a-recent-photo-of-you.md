# What to do if… you receive an anonymous message containing a recent photo of you (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the message as a safety signal and a possible harassment, stalking, or extortion attempt. Do not engage with the sender; move to a safer place, save the message if you can do so safely, and call 911 if you feel in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply, argue, threaten, or “bait” the sender for more information.
- Do not click links, open attachments, scan codes, or install anything they send.
- Do not pay money, send gift cards, send passwords, or send more photos; this may not stop contact and can lead to more demands.
- Do not post the photo publicly or ask strangers online to identify the sender.
- Do not delete the conversation before saving what you may need.
- Do not assume you have to decide today whether to make a police report, seek a protection order, or change your number.

## What to do now
1. **Check immediate safety first.**  
   If the photo seems to have been taken very recently, shows your home, workplace, car, route, or current location, or the sender says they are nearby, move to a safer place if you can. Go toward other people, lock doors if you are inside, and call 911 if you feel in immediate danger.

2. **Stop feeding the contact.**  
   Do not reply. Mute the chat if you need a moment to save it. Turn off read receipts if the app allows it. After saving evidence, use the safest available option: report, restrict, mute, or block the sender.

3. **Save the message in a simple way.**  
   If you can do so safely, screenshot the full thread, including the sender’s number, username, profile, message text, photo, and timestamps. Save the photo as received without cropping or editing it. Write down the date and time received, the platform, and any location clues in the photo, such as signs, your vehicle, your workplace, or the area where it was taken.

4. **Report it on the platform.**  
   Use the app or website’s report tools for harassment, threats, stalking, impersonation, or extortion. If there are threats, demands for money, sexual pressure, or a warning not to tell anyone, include that in the report.

5. **Report threats, demands, or cyber-enabled crime through the right route.**  
   If you are in immediate danger, call 911 or local police first. If the message involves internet-based threats, fraud, extortion, account compromise, or demands for money or gift cards, file a report with the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center. IC3 is not an emergency service.

6. **Reduce what the sender can learn today.**  
   Turn off live location sharing and recent location tags on social apps. Check who can see your stories, posts, map location, and profile details. Remove recent posts that show your current location, route, home, workplace, school, or vehicle.

7. **Secure the account the message came through.**  
   If there is any sign your account was accessed, change the password for that account and your email account. Sign out of other sessions if the service offers that option. Turn on two-factor authentication, and check recovery email addresses, phone numbers, linked devices, and recent logins.

8. **Tell one trusted person what happened.**  
   Send them the screenshots or keep a copy somewhere safe. Ask them to check in with you soon. If you need to leave now, ask them to stay with you, meet you, or know where you are going.

9. **If the photo may have been taken at work, school, housing, or a building with cameras, contact onsite security.**  
   Share only what they need to identify the time and place. Ask whether any relevant camera footage can be preserved before it is overwritten.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the sender right now.
- You do not need to keep chatting to collect evidence.
- You do not need to post publicly, warn everyone, or explain the situation to people who do not need to know.
- You do not need to delete accounts, change your number, move, or make major decisions today.
- You do not need to decide right now whether to pursue charges or any legal process.

## Important reassurance
This is a frightening message to receive, and taking it seriously is reasonable. The safest first response is calm and limited: do not engage, protect your immediate safety, save what you can, and report through channels that create a record.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the contact continues, escalates, involves someone you know, or suggests in-person stalking, later decisions may need help from local law enforcement, a victim advocate, campus security, workplace security, or a lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, call 911.
