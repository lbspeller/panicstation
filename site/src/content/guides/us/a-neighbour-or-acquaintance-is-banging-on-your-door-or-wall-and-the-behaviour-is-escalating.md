# What to do if… a neighbour or acquaintance is banging on your door or wall and the behaviour is escalating (USA)

## Short answer
Stay behind a locked door and move away from the area being struck. Call **911** if there is immediate danger, the person is trying to enter, threats of harm have been made, or urgent police assistance is needed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door or step into the hallway to talk while the person is escalated.
- Do not yell back, threaten, or bang back.
- Do not follow the person if they move away from your door.
- Do not display or mention a weapon as a warning.
- Do not move closer to film or inspect damage while the person is still nearby.
- Do not open the door merely to check what is happening. Move only if another immediate hazard requires it or a responder directs you to do so.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance and keep a locked barrier between you and the person.**  
   Move away from the door or wall being struck. Go to another room with your phone if that gives you more distance or a second locked barrier. Use a peephole, doorbell camera, or security camera only if you can do so without opening the door.

2. **Call 911 if urgent police assistance is needed.**  
   Tell the call-taker your exact address and unit number, that someone is banging on your door or wall, and whether the person is trying to enter, damaging property, or making threats. Answer the call-taker’s questions and follow any instructions given.

3. **Keep any necessary communication brief.**  
   If you need to speak through the closed door, say: “I can’t open the door. Please leave.” Stop engaging after that. Do not argue or negotiate through the door.

4. **Contact building support without leaving your locked space, if available.**  
   Call on-site security, a front desk, or property management. Tell them where the person is and that you do not want to open the door.

5. **Use the local non-emergency route later if urgent police assistance is not needed.**  
   Look for the law-enforcement non-emergency contact route on the relevant agency or local-government website. Ask whether a report can be made and whether an incident or case number is available.

6. **Record the basic facts safely.**  
   Write down the time, what you heard, any threats, any attempted entry, and any witnesses. Save doorbell or security footage already captured. Photograph damage after the person has gone and it is safe to do so.

7. **Use 988 only when there is no immediate danger and you are concerned about a possible crisis.**  
   If you are concerned that the person may be in emotional distress or another behavioral health crisis, you can contact **988** for crisis guidance. Use **911** instead if urgent police, fire, or ambulance assistance is needed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to confront the person, move home, or start a court process.
- You do not need perfect evidence before calling 911 or using a local non-emergency route. Report what you directly observed.
- You can organize notes, saved footage, and photographs after the immediate situation has settled.
- If incidents continue, you can later ask your local court or a local legal-help service what protective-order or restraining-order options may apply in your state and situation.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to open the door to prove that the situation is serious. Keeping a locked barrier between you and an escalating person is a reasonable first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation only. Repeated incidents or later decisions may need help from local law enforcement, building management, a court self-help service, or a legal professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Call **911** if urgent police, fire, or ambulance assistance is needed.
