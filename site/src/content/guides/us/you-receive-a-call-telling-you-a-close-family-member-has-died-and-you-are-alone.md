# What to do if… you receive a call telling you a close family member has died and you are alone (USA)

## Short answer
Take a moment to steady yourself physically, then initiate a separate call to an official agency number you have looked up yourself to verify the report before taking any other action. Do not provide information or money to any caller who reached out to you first.

## Do not do these things
- Don't drive a vehicle if you feel shaky, numb, or confused—wait for a support person or use a car service.
- Don't send any form of payment—including wire transfers, gift cards, or cryptocurrency—to anyone claiming it is required for transport or morgue fees.
- Don't provide personal details like your Social Security number or the deceased person’s banking information to the caller.
- Don't assume the voice on the phone is genuine based on sound alone; AI voice cloning can mimic familiar relatives or authorities.
- Don't post the news on social media or in group chats until you have independently verified the facts.
- Don't sign any digital contracts for funeral services or transport while you are in the immediate state of shock.

## What to do now
1. **Stabilize your physical safety immediately by sitting down and drinking water.** If you feel lightheaded, lie down on your side to prevent a fall.
2. **Write down every detail you can remember from the call while it is fresh.** Include the caller's name, their stated job title, the agency they claim to represent, any case or incident numbers provided, and the specific location where the death allegedly occurred.
3. **End the call if you feel pressured or if the caller asks for money or personal data.** Official agencies like police departments, hospitals, and coroners will not demand immediate payment over the phone for "processing" a death.
4. **Locate the official public number for the agency mentioned in the call using a trusted government website.**
   - If they said **hospital**: call the main switchboard and ask for the nursing supervisor on the relevant ward or the patient relations/bereavement office.
   - If they said **police, medical examiner, or coroner**: call the department's public non-emergency line and ask to speak with the officer or investigator assigned to the case number you were given.
5. **Request that a support person stay on the phone with you while you verify the news.** Call a trusted friend or family member and explain that you received a distressing call and need them to stay on the line until you have confirmed the facts.
6. **Ask for the location of the deceased person once the report is verified.**
   - "Is [name] currently at the hospital, the medical examiner's office, or a morgue?"
   - "Who is the official point of contact for this case?"
   - "What is the official case number for my records?"
7. **Contact 911 if you believe you are in immediate physical danger or have been targeted by a crime in progress.** If you suspect the call was a coordinated attempt to lure you out of your home, do not leave until police arrive.
8. **Dial 988 if the emotional shock is making it difficult for you to stay safe.**
   - If you are a Veteran, call 988 and **press 1** for the Veterans Crisis Line.
   - If you need a Spanish-speaking counselor, call 988 and **press 2**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a funeral home or cemetery in the next few hours.
- You do not need to book travel or inform your employer immediately.
- You do not need to handle life insurance, bank accounts, or social security notifications today.
- You do not need to decide on cremation or burial services until you have a support person physically present.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for your brain to feel "scrambled," blank, or slow after receiving news of a death. This is a protective shock response. You do not have to solve everything in the next hour; your only job is to verify the facts and get a support person with you.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hours following a notification call. It does not cover the legalities of estate management or long-term funeral planning.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal or medical advice. Procedures vary by state and county; always follow the instructions of local law enforcement or medical examiners once their identity has been verified through official channels.
