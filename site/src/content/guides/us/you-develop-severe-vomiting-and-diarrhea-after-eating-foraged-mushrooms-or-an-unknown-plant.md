# What to do if… you develop severe vomiting and diarrhea after eating foraged mushrooms or an unknown plant (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the situation as a potential poisoning emergency and **call Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222** or **call 911** immediately if you have trouble breathing, seizures, or loss of consciousness. Do not wait for symptoms to worsen, as early intervention is critical for preventing organ failure.

## Do not do these things
- Do **not** attempt to induce vomiting (make yourself throw up) unless specifically instructed by a medical professional.
- Do **not** assume that because the mushroom or plant was cooked, it is safe; many toxins are heat-stable and survive boiling, frying, or drying.
- Do **not** take anti-diarrhea medications like loperamide (Imodium) without professional advice, as these can trap toxins in the digestive tract.
- Do **not** consume alcohol, which can irritate the stomach and mask or worsen certain toxic effects.
- Do **not** discard any remaining samples of the plant or mushroom, even if they are in the trash.
- Do **not** wait for symptoms to disappear before seeking help; a "false recovery" period is common before serious liver or kidney damage occurs.

## What to do now
1. **Contact emergency services or Poison Control immediately based on symptom severity.** 
   - Call 911 if the person is unconscious, having seizures, struggling to breathe, or extremely confused. 
   - Otherwise, call 1-800-222-1222 to reach the National Poison Help hotline for free, 24/7 expert guidance.
2. **Rinse the mouth out with water without swallowing.** 
   - Use a damp cloth to wipe out the mouth of a child or someone unable to rinse themselves. 
   - This removes residual fragments or juices that have not yet been swallowed.
3. **Secure a sample of the ingested material for professional identification.** 
   - Place any remaining mushrooms or plant parts in a paper bag or a dry, ventilated container; do not use plastic bags, which cause rapid decay. 
   - If no fresh samples remain, retrieve scraps from the trash or take clear photos of the top, gills/underside, stem, and base of similar specimens nearby.
4. **Document the specific details of the ingestion for the medical team.** 
   - Record the exact time the item was eaten, the amount consumed, and the time the first symptoms (nausea, vomiting, or cramps) began. 
   - Note if others who shared the meal are also ill or if anyone consumed alcohol with the meal.
5. **Position the person on their side in the recovery position.** 
   - This prevents choking if vomiting occurs while the person is drowsy or lying down. 
   - If alone, unlock your front door and notify a neighbor or emergency contact of your location.
6. **Follow hydration instructions exactly as provided by Poison Control.** 
   - If instructed, take very small, frequent sips of water or an oral rehydration solution. 
   - Report immediately if you cannot keep any fluids down, as this indicates a high risk of severe dehydration.

## What can wait
- Identifying the mushroom or plant species using mobile apps or online forums.
- Determining if the illness is "just food poisoning" versus a toxic ingestion.
- Cleaning the kitchen or disposal of food scraps before they are sampled.
- Contacting foraging groups or experts for general advice.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel fear or embarrassment after a foraging mistake. Poison Control centers manage thousands of similar calls every year and are focused entirely on your physical safety, not on judgment. Early reporting significantly increases the chance of a full recovery, even if the toxin is severe.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions only. It is not a substitute for professional medical diagnosis or hospital treatment.

## Important note
Mushroom and plant toxins can cause permanent liver or kidney damage that may not be symptomatic for several days. Always follow the specific clinical plan provided by Poison Control or emergency department staff, even if you begin to feel better.
