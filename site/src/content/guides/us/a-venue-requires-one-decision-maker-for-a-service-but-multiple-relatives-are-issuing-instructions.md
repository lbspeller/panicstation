# What to do if… a venue requires one decision-maker for a service but multiple relatives are issuing instructions (USA)

## Short answer
Freeze all new instructions and identify the legally recognized “authorizing agent” based on your state’s Right of Disposition hierarchy or written designations. Direct the venue in writing to only accept instructions from that specific individual until a court order or unanimous agreement is reached.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign cremation or burial authorization paperwork if you are not the legally recognized authorizing agent.
- Do not allow multiple relatives to contact the funeral director with conflicting requests, as this usually triggers an immediate halt to all services.
- Do not pay non-refundable deposits until the authorizing agent is legally confirmed and documented by the venue.
- Do not assume a general Power of Attorney remains valid; most POA authority ends at the moment of death unless it includes specific "durable" disposition rights.
- Do not attempt to "vote" on decisions unless the specific state statute allows for majority rule in that kinship category.

## What to do now
1. **Request the venue’s written "Right of Disposition" policy and state-specific hierarchy.**  
   **Ask the funeral director to provide the exact statute or internal checklist they use to identify the primary decision-maker.** This documentation should include the priority list (e.g., spouse, adult children, parents, siblings) and their requirements for "same level" relatives.

2. **Search for a state-recognized "Appointment of Agent" or "Advance Directive" form.**  
   **Check the deceased’s records for a signed document that specifically names a person to control disposition.**  
   - A pre-need funeral contract may already name an agent.
   - A health care power of attorney or a will may contain a "Disposition of Remains" clause that overrides the standard kinship hierarchy in many states.

3. **Identify if "majority rule" applies to your specific kinship tier.**  
   **Determine if your state law (such as in California, Washington, or Georgia) allows a majority of a group—like adult children—to authorize services.** If it does, ask the venue for the specific affidavit or majority-consent form required to proceed without unanimous agreement.

4. **Formally pause all non-essential arrangements in writing.**  
   **Send an email to the funeral home or venue manager stating that a dispute exists and requesting a temporary "administrative hold" on all discretionary decisions.** This prevents irreversible actions like cremation or burial while authority is clarified.

5. **Designate a single liaison to the venue via a formal notice.**  
   **Submit a written statement to the venue identifying one person as the "temporary point of contact" for communication only.** Include:
   - Deceased’s full name and case number.
   - A statement that "No further instructions should be implemented unless signed by [NAME] or verified by legal counsel."

6. **Verify mandatory state waiting periods.**  
   **Confirm with the funeral director how many hours must pass before a cremation can legally occur (often 24–48 hours).** Use this time window to gather the necessary signatures or legal documentation rather than rushing a decision during the first few hours.

7. **Consult the State Funeral Board if the venue is not following state hierarchy.**  
   **Contact the regulatory agency in the state where the death occurred if the funeral home refuses to identify the legal hierarchy or is accepting signatures from a lower-priority relative.** 

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle disagreements over music, readings, or obituary text.
- You do not need to decide on the final resting place for ashes (cremated remains can be held in a temporary container).
- You do not need to resolve long-term family conflicts or "estrangement" claims, as these often require court intervention to prove.
- You do not need to finalize the date of the service until the authorization form is signed and filed.

## Important reassurance
It is common for funeral homes to stop all activity when family members disagree. They do this to protect themselves legally and to ensure they do not perform an irreversible act, like cremation, without proper authority. This pause is a standard procedural safety measure, not a personal judgment on your family.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying legal decision-making authority. If relatives in the same priority tier remain deadlocked and the state does not allow majority rule, you may need a court order from a probate judge to proceed.

## Important note
Funeral laws are governed by the state where the death occurred, not the state where the deceased lived. Always follow the written instructions provided by the licensed funeral director regarding authorizing agent paperwork, as they are bound by specific state licensing regulations.
