# What to do if… your connection becomes an overnight layover and you are unsure if you can enter the country (USA)

## Short answer
Do not walk toward Arrivals, Exit, baggage claim, or a landside hotel plan unless airline, airport, or CBP staff direct you there and you know you have the U.S. permission needed to request entry. Ask the airline now whether you can stay airside, must clear U.S. immigration and customs, or need a rebooking that does not require U.S. entry.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume “transit” means you can stay in the airport without U.S. entry permission.
- Do not head to Arrivals, Exit, baggage claim, or a hotel outside security unless airline, airport, or CBP staff direct you there.
- Do not assume an ESTA, visa, or boarding pass guarantees admission; CBP decides admission at the port of entry.
- Do not assume your checked bag will stay checked through after arriving from overseas.
- Do not rely on advice from other travellers; U.S. entry and transit rules depend on your passport, status, route, and documents.
- Do not let your phone die; keep access to your airline app, booking reference, onward itinerary, and messages.

## What to do now
1. **Ask whether the airport route will force you landside.**
   - Ask airline staff: **“Because this is now an overnight layover, do I have to clear U.S. immigration and customs tonight, or can I stay airside until my onward flight?”**
   - Ask: **“Does this terminal stay open airside overnight for connecting passengers?”**
   - If the answer is unclear, ask for the airline’s transfer desk, duty manager, or irregular operations desk.

2. **Check whether you have the U.S. permission needed for transit or entry.**
   - If you are travelling under the Visa Waiver Program, you need a valid ESTA or a visa to transit the United States.
   - If you are not eligible for the Visa Waiver Program, you may need an appropriate visa for transit, such as a C transit visa, unless another valid route applies to you.
   - If you are unsure, say to the airline: **“I may not have permission to enter the U.S. Please check whether this rebooking requires U.S. entry.”**

3. **Check your baggage before you move.**
   - Ask: **“Will I have to collect my checked baggage here before tomorrow’s flight?”**
   - For many overseas arrivals into the United States, travellers must collect checked baggage and bring it through CBP before rechecking it.
   - Some airports and routes may handle this differently, so follow instructions for your exact flight rather than guessing.

4. **If you do not have the right ESTA or visa, make the airline solve the route problem.**
   - Say: **“I cannot assume I can enter the U.S. I need an airside option or a rebooking that I can legally take.”**
   - Ask the airline to note the document issue in your booking.
   - Keep your passport, booking reference, original itinerary, disruption notice, and onward flight details ready.

5. **If you are sent to CBP, keep it factual and brief.**
   - Say: **“My connection became an overnight layover. I am trying to continue to my onward destination and I am not sure what my documents allow.”**
   - Show your passport, onward itinerary, and any airline rebooking message.
   - Do not argue about admission; if there is a problem, ask the airline again for rerouting or instructions.

6. **If you are admitted landside and need somewhere to sleep, keep the plan simple.**
   - Choose the closest practical airport hotel or rest area.
   - Keep your passport, boarding pass, medication, chargers, and valuables with you.
   - Set more than one alarm and plan to return early enough for check-in, document checks, and security.

7. **If you can stay airside, protect tomorrow’s departure.**
   - Find the gate area, transfer desk, quiet seating area, or airside hotel if one exists.
   - Confirm when your airline desk and security checkpoint reopen.
   - Keep documents on your person and keep your phone charged.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to claim compensation, refunds, expenses, or insurance.
- You do not need to fix the whole trip now; the immediate job is to avoid moving into a process your documents may not support.
- Seat changes, sightseeing, loyalty points, and nonessential planning can wait until you have a confirmed route for the morning.

## Important reassurance
This is a confusing situation, especially because U.S. transit can still involve U.S. entry checks. Pausing before you move toward Arrivals, Exit, baggage claim, or a landside hotel is a sensible way to reduce the risk of getting stuck.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours only. Later decisions about rebooking, complaints, expenses, insurance, or immigration options may need help from the airline, your travel provider, your insurer, or a qualified adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, travel, immigration, or professional advice. U.S. entry and transit requirements depend on your nationality, passport, immigration history, route, and current rules. If you are unsure you have the right U.S. permission, ask the airline for a route or instruction you can legally follow before you choose a landside option.
