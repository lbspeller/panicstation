# What to do if… your sent folder shows emails you do not recognise sent to many recipients (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the email account as likely compromised. Regain control through the provider’s official recovery or security settings, sign out other sessions, change the password, remove hidden forwarding or rules, and turn on multi-factor authentication.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t reply from the affected account to explain what happened until you have checked security settings.
- Don’t click links, open attachments, or copy payment details from the suspicious sent emails.
- Don’t assume changing the password alone is enough; forwarding, filters, app passwords, or connected apps may still give access.
- Don’t delete all traces immediately; keep the time, subject lines, recipient list, and any suspicious rules or forwarding addresses.
- Don’t send codes, documents, money, or payment instructions from the account to “test” whether it is safe.
- Don’t make a public post before warning the people most likely to be harmed.

## What to do now
1. **If you cannot log in, use the provider’s official recovery flow.** Go directly to the email provider’s website or app, not through a link in an email. After you regain access, continue with these steps.
2. **Use a trusted, updated device.** If your usual device is acting strangely, use another device you trust. If you think malware may be involved, run an up-to-date security scan before changing passwords on that device.
3. **Sign out other sessions.** In the account security area, use “sign out of all devices,” “log out everywhere,” or the closest available option.
4. **Change the password.** Use a new, unique password that is not used on any other account.
5. **Turn on multi-factor authentication.** Use an authenticator app or security key if available. Use the strongest option you can set up now; do not delay the rest of the steps while choosing.
6. **Remove hidden mailbox access.** Check email settings for:
   - auto-forwarding you did not set;
   - filters or rules that delete, archive, forward, or hide messages;
   - unfamiliar connected apps or third-party access;
   - unfamiliar app passwords, if your provider shows them;
   - automatic replies or signatures changed without your approval.
7. **Secure recovery options.** Confirm the recovery email and phone number are yours. Remove anything unfamiliar and regenerate backup codes if the provider offers them.
8. **Look for signs other accounts were targeted.** Search inbox, deleted, archive, trash, and spam folders for “password reset,” “security alert,” “verification code,” “new device,” and “payment.” Start with banking, payment apps, shopping, cloud storage, and social media.
9. **Warn likely targets using another channel.** Text or call close contacts, coworkers, customers, or anyone named in the sent emails. Tell them to ignore recent links, attachments, payment requests, or code requests from your email unless you confirm by phone.
10. **If money, identity details, threats, or workplace data are involved, report and escalate.** Contact your bank or payment provider if money moved or card details were exposed. Use the FBI internet crime reporting portal for cyber-enabled crime, fraud, extortion, identity theft, or business email compromise. If personal information was misused, use the federal identity theft recovery site.
11. **If this is a work or school account, contact IT or security support now.** Ask them to check sign-in logs, mailbox rules, forwarding, connected apps, and any suspicious approval prompts.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the attacker today.
- You do not need to prove exactly how the account was accessed before securing it.
- You do not need to notify every recipient at once; start with people most likely to click, pay, send codes, or share sensitive information.
- You usually do not need to decide right now whether to replace, reset, or wipe devices unless your provider, IT team, or security scan gives a clear reason.

## Important reassurance
Seeing many unfamiliar emails in Sent is alarming, but it is a known pattern in email account compromise. The most useful first steps are to remove current access, block hidden persistence, protect accounts that depend on this email, and warn people who may be targeted.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from your email provider, IT or security support, your bank, an identity theft recovery service, or law enforcement reporting channels.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Follow your provider’s current instructions and get prompt specialist help if money, workplace systems, threats, or sensitive personal information are involved.
