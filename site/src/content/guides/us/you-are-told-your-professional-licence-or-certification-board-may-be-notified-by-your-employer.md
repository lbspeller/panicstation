# What to do if… you are told your professional licence or certification board may be notified by your employer (USA)

## Short answer
Do not click any links or buttons within the emails and contact your organization's IT or security department immediately through an official internal channel. Treat a "flood" of reset emails as a potential distraction for a real account takeover or an MFA fatigue attack.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click any links, "Unsubscribe" buttons, or "Report this" links inside the suspicious emails.
- Do not use any phone numbers, helpdesk links, or chat options provided within the email body.
- Do not approve any Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) prompts on your phone that you did not personally trigger.
- Do not enter your current credentials into any page that opened from one of these emails.
- Do not forward the emails to your personal account or other colleagues, as they may contain malicious tracking pixels or scripts.

## What to do now
1. **Stop interacting with the emails and close your mail client if the notifications are overwhelming.** **Taking a moment to pause prevents an impulsive click that could compromise your credentials.**
2. **Report the activity to your company IT or security team using a trusted, out-of-band method.** **Use your internal company chat, a known-good helpdesk portal, or a verified internal phone number rather than anything found in the emails.**
   - Tell them which specific account is receiving the emails (e.g., Microsoft 365, Slack, Salesforce).
   - Provide the frequency and timestamps of the emails.
   - Mention if you are also receiving unrequested MFA push notifications or SMS codes.
3. **Verify your account's security settings only through the official organization login portal.** **Manually type the web address of your company's login page into your browser or use your IT-approved single sign-on (SSO) dashboard.**
4. **Check for "hidden" changes in your email settings if IT confirms it is safe to do so.** **Look for unfamiliar inbox rules that move messages to the "Archive" or "Deleted Items" folders, or auto-forwarding addresses you did not set up.**
5. **Immediately inform your manager if your role involves financial transfers, payroll, or sensitive data access.** **Alerting your team ensures that any urgent requests seemingly "from you" during this period are verified via phone or video call before action is taken.**
6. **Ask your IT department to "revoke all active sessions" for your account.** **This forces every device currently logged in as you—including those belonging to a potential attacker—to re-authenticate with your verified credentials.**
7. **Document the sender address and email headers without clicking links if IT requests evidence.** **Many "reset" emails are sent from legitimate services being abused by attackers, while others are spoofs; headers help security teams distinguish between the two.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to change your password until IT confirms the specific nature of the attack; resetting it too early can sometimes interfere with their investigation.
- You do not need to contact the FBI or IC3 yourself unless your company’s legal or security team instructs you to do so or if there is a confirmed financial loss.
- You do not need to perform a factory reset of your work computer or phone unless security identifies active malware on the device.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a password reset email does not mean your account has been hacked; it only means someone knows your email address and is requesting a reset. If you have strong, phishing-resistant MFA enabled and you do not click the links, your account usually remains secure while IT mitigates the attempt.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active surge of reset notifications. It does not replace your organization's specific Incident Response (IR) plan or cybersecurity policies.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional cybersecurity advice. Security protocols vary significantly between organizations; always prioritize the direct instructions provided by your employer's authorized IT security staff.
