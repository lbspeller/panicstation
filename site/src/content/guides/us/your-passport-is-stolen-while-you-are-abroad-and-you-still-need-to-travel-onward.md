# What to do if… your passport is stolen while you are abroad and you still need to travel onward (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately report the theft to the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate and apply in person for an emergency (limited-validity) passport. Do not attempt to cross borders or board international flights until you have this physical replacement document in hand.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to travel using a photocopy or digital image of your stolen passport.
- Do not try to use your passport if you find it after reporting it stolen, as it will be electronically cancelled.
- Do not book non-refundable onward travel until you have a confirmed appointment and timeline from the consular section.
- Do not pay third-party "expediting" services while abroad; only the U.S. embassy or consulate can issue a replacement.
- Do not delay reporting the theft, as this increases the risk of identity fraud and slows down the replacement process.

## What to do now
1. **Find a secure location with reliable communication and internet access.** **Moving to a safe, quiet space like a hotel lobby or a government building allows you to organize your next steps without immediate pressure.** Ensure you have a way to make international calls or access the U.S. Department of State website.

2. **Locate the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate and check their emergency hours.** **U.S. missions abroad have specific procedures for emergency passport services, often involving morning walk-in hours or dedicated emergency appointment slots.** Use the official "Usembassy.gov" portal to find the contact details and physical address of the nearest Consular Section.

3. **Gather any available evidence of your identity and U.S. citizenship.** **Even if the originals were stolen, having digital scans, photocopies, or other forms of ID like a driver's license will significantly speed up the verification process.** 
   - Look for a Social Security Number (SSN), as you are required to provide this on the application.
   - Collect any proof of onward travel, such as flight or train itineraries.

4. **Prepare the mandatory passport application forms (DS-11 and DS-64).** **You must complete Form DS-11 for the replacement and Form DS-64 to formally statement the loss or theft of the original document.** You can fill these out using the online form filler or download the PDFs, but do not sign them until you are in front of a consular officer.

5. **Obtain new passport photos that meet U.S. specifications.** **U.S. passport photo requirements (2x2 inches) often differ from local foreign standards, so verify the dimensions before paying for them.** Many embassies have photo booths nearby or on-site, but it is safer to arrive with them ready.

6. **Attend the embassy or consulate in person to submit your application.** **You must appear physically to be interviewed by a consular officer and to pay the required passport fees.** 
   - Be prepared to pay the standard passport fee (typically via credit card or U.S. dollars / local currency equivalent).
   - Explain your onward travel deadlines clearly to the officer.

7. **Verify if your onward destination and transit countries accept emergency passports.** **While a limited-validity (emergency) passport is valid for travel, some countries have specific entry or transit restrictions for non-biometric or temporary documents.** Check the "International Travel" section of the State Department website for your specific route.

8. **Contact your airline or transport provider to update your booking details.** **Once you have the new passport number, you must update your Advanced Passenger Information (API) with the carrier to avoid delays at check-in.** 

9. **File a local police report for the theft if it is safe and practical to do so.** **While not strictly required by the U.S. government for a replacement, a police report is often necessary for travel insurance claims and may help explain the lack of an entry stamp to local immigration.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to apply for a full-validity 10-year passport immediately if you have an urgent need to move; the emergency version is designed for this gap.
- You do not need to replace other stolen items (like secondary IDs or credit cards) through the embassy; they only handle passport and consular matters.
- You do not need to pay for a second passport fee if you replace your emergency passport within one year; the government fee is waived for the exchange to a full-validity book.

## Important reassurance
Losing a passport abroad is a common emergency that consular staff are trained to handle daily. While the process requires an in-person visit and paperwork, it is a routine administrative procedure. Once you are inside the embassy or consulate, you are in a regulated environment designed to restore your ability to travel.

## Scope note
This guide focuses on the immediate steps to obtain a replacement travel document (emergency passport) for onward travel. It does not cover the replacement of foreign visas, long-term identity theft monitoring, or insurance claim procedures.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Requirements for entry into foreign countries and airline boarding policies are subject to change without notice. Always follow the specific instructions provided by the U.S. consular officer at the time of your application.
