# What to do if… your passport is stolen while you are abroad and you still need to travel onward (USA)

## Short answer
Report the passport lost or stolen and contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate before trying to travel onward. You usually need a replacement passport before international travel, and an emergency passport may not be accepted by every destination or transit country.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to travel on a passport after you have reported it lost or stolen; it is cancelled and is no longer valid for international travel.
- Do not pay unofficial “fixers” who claim they can get a passport or visa quickly.
- Do not book expensive non-refundable onward travel until you know what the embassy or consulate can issue and whether your route will accept it.
- Do not post passport photos, passport numbers, or personal documents publicly while trying to recover them.
- Do not hand original documents to private strangers. Use official embassy, consulate, carrier, hotel, insurer, or local authority routes.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safe, staffed place.**
   - Move to hotel reception, an airport help desk, a police station lobby, or another calm public place.
   - Check your bags carefully and ask your hotel, transport operator, or venue to check lost property.
   - If cards, phone, or identity documents were stolen too, lock cards, accounts, and your SIM as soon as you can.

2. **Write down the basic facts before they blur.**
   - Note the date, time, place, what was stolen, and any local report or incident number.
   - Save any passport scan or photo you already have, but keep it private.

3. **Report the passport lost or stolen through an official route.**
   - Use the official State Department lost or stolen passport page, or report it in person when you apply for the replacement if the embassy or consulate tells you to do that.
   - After it is reported, do not use the passport even if it is found later.

4. **Contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate now.**
   - Say that your U.S. passport was stolen abroad and that you still need to travel onward.
   - Give your route, transit countries, travel dates, and where you can be reached.
   - Ask whether you need an appointment, what local entry rules affect you, and what they can issue in time.

5. **Prepare for an in-person passport replacement appointment.**
   - Bring one passport photo if you can get one locally; the usual size is 2x2 inches or 5x5 cm.
   - Bring any identity or citizenship evidence you still have, such as a driver’s license, expired passport, birth certificate, or a photocopy or scan of the missing passport.
   - Bring your travel itinerary, police report or reference number if you have one, and a way to pay passport fees if required.

6. **Ask specifically about an emergency or limited-validity passport.**
   - If there is not enough time for a regular passport, the consular section may offer an emergency passport.
   - Emergency passports issued abroad may be valid for up to 1 year, but some foreign destinations may not accept a limited-validity passport.

7. **Check the whole route before you rebook.**
   - Ask each airline, train, ferry, or bus operator what document they will accept for boarding.
   - Check destination and transit entry rules, especially if you will use an emergency passport.
   - If a visa or residence permit was inside the stolen passport, contact the issuing authority before attempting onward travel.

8. **File a local police report if you can do it safely and without delaying the passport appointment.**
   - A police report is not mandatory for the U.S. passport replacement process, but it can help confirm what happened.
   - Ask for a written report or reference number if the local system provides one.

9. **Tell your travel insurer early.**
   - Ask what proof they need for stolen documents, rebooking costs, emergency accommodation, or replacement travel.
   - Keep receipts and written messages in one folder or email thread.

## What can wait
- You do not need to replace every other document right now unless it affects safety, money access, or travel.
- You do not need to decide today whether to exchange an emergency passport for a full-validity passport later.
- You do not need to argue at check-in before you have replacement documentation and clear carrier instructions.
- You do not need to solve every visa, insurance, and identity-protection task at once; first stabilize your documents and lawful travel route.

## Important reassurance
This is stressful, but it is a standard consular problem with a recognised process. Once you are in contact with the embassy or consulate, you are no longer trying to solve it alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps for replacing a stolen U.S. passport abroad and reducing immediate travel harm. Later decisions about visas, full-validity replacement, insurance, identity protection, or missed travel may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information only, not legal, financial, travel, insurance, or other professional advice. Embassy and consulate procedures, destination entry rules, transit rules, and carrier checks can change, so follow the current instructions from the U.S. Department of State, the embassy or consulate, and your carrier.
