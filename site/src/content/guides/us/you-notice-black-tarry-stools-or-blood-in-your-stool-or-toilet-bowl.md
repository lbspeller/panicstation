# What to do if… you notice black, tarry stools or blood in your stool or toilet bowl (USA)

## Short answer
Black, tarry stool or visible blood in the toilet can be a sign of gastrointestinal bleeding, so get medical advice urgently. Call 911 now if bleeding is heavy or continuous, you feel faint, weak, confused, short of breath, or clammy, you have severe pain, or you vomit blood or material that looks like coffee grounds.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore black, tarry stool or visible blood because you “feel okay.”
- Do not drive yourself if you feel dizzy, weak, short of breath, faint, confused, or clammy; call 911 or ask someone else to drive you.
- Do not stop prescribed blood thinners or antiplatelet medicines on your own; tell the urgent-care or emergency team exactly what you take.
- Do not take aspirin, ibuprofen, or naproxen for pain unless a clinician has told you to use them.
- Do not use laxatives, enemas, or “cleanse” products to try to clear the bowel.
- Do not keep repeatedly wiping or checking if it is irritating or painful; get assessed instead.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency warning signs. Call 911 now if any apply:**
   - Large amounts of blood, bleeding that does not stop, or clots in the toilet.
   - Fainting, severe lightheadedness, confusion, very bad weakness, shortness of breath, cold or clammy skin, or looking very pale.
   - Severe abdominal pain, chest pain, vomiting blood, or vomit that looks like coffee grounds.
2. **If the stool is black and tarry, or the blood is dark red or maroon:** go to an emergency department now, or follow your clinician’s urgent line if they can direct you immediately. Tell triage staff the stool is black and tarry, dark red, or maroon.
3. **If it is a small amount of bright-red blood only, such as on toilet paper or streaking the stool, and you otherwise feel well:** call your primary care clinician promptly for advice. Go to emergency care instead if bleeding becomes heavy or continuous, pain starts, you feel dizzy or weak, or you cannot get timely advice.
4. **If you take blood thinners, antiplatelet medicines, aspirin, or have liver disease or a bleeding disorder:** treat this as more urgent. Tell the clinician or emergency team the exact medicine and last dose, such as warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, clopidogrel, or aspirin.
5. **Write down the details before you are seen, if you can do it without delaying care:**
   - When it started and how many times it happened.
   - Whether it was black and tarry, bright red, dark red, maroon, mixed into stool, in the bowl, or only on paper.
   - Any dizziness, weakness, shortness of breath, vomiting, fever, abdominal pain, diarrhea, constipation, or straining.
   - Recent medicines or supplements, especially ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin, steroids, blood thinners, iron, or bismuth products such as Pepto-Bismol.
6. **Do a quick look-alike check, but do not use it to dismiss symptoms:** iron tablets, bismuth products, black licorice, blueberries, beets, and some food dyes can change stool color. New black, tarry stool or visible blood still needs medical advice.
7. **While waiting for care or transport:** rest, avoid alcohol, and have someone stay with you if you feel weak. If symptoms worsen, switch to 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause on your own.
- You do not need to start a restrictive diet, cleanse, supplement, or home treatment today.
- You do not need to decide about colonoscopy, endoscopy, or longer-term treatment right now.
- You can save prevention questions until after a clinician has checked whether you need urgent care.

## Important reassurance
Blood or black, tarry stool is alarming, but clinicians deal with this symptom often and have clear ways to check what is happening. Getting help quickly is the safest move, and it does not mean the cause is automatically life-threatening.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about tests, medicines, procedures, or specialist care may need help from an emergency clinician, primary care clinician, or gastroenterology specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information and is not medical advice, a diagnosis, or a substitute for professional care. If bleeding is heavy, continuous, or you feel faint, weak, confused, short of breath, clammy, or have severe pain, call 911 or seek emergency care.
