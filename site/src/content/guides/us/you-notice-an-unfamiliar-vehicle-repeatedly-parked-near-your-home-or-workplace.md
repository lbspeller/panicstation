# What to do if… you notice an unfamiliar vehicle repeatedly parked near your home or workplace (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safe pause indoors or in a staffed public place, and do not approach the vehicle. If you feel in immediate danger or believe a crime is happening now, call 911; otherwise contact your local police non-emergency route with a clear description and the repeated pattern.

## Do not do these things
- Do not walk up to the vehicle, knock on the window, or try to see who it is.
- Do not block the vehicle in, follow it, or try to prove anything by investigating.
- Do not introduce a weapon or make threats; back away, get to safety, and contact help.
- Do not post the license plate or photos publicly.
- Do not make sudden, dramatic routine changes in a panic; make small, practical safety adjustments instead.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Go indoors, lock doors, and keep distance. If you are at work, move to reception, security, or a busy area rather than staying outside or alone.
2. **Tell a real person nearby.**
   - Home: alert someone you live with, or call or text a friend and stay connected.
   - Apartment or building: notify building management, a concierge, or front desk staff so they can note the vehicle and watch access points.
   - Work: notify your manager, building security, or reception and ask them to watch entrances and parking areas.
3. **Write down the key details without getting close.** Record:
   - exact location, dates, times, how long it stayed, and what changed over time
   - make, model, and color, license plate, and distinguishing marks
   - what you observed, such as watching doors, photographing, following people, or trying to talk to staff
   If safe, take a photo or video from indoors or from a distance in a well-populated area.
4. **Use existing home or work systems without escalating.**
   - Save doorbell or camera clips before they overwrite; note the exact timestamps.
   - At work, ask security to keep an incident or visitor log entry with the plate and to check whether it matches expected contractors, deliveries, visitors, or permitted parking.
5. **Choose the right contact route based on immediate risk.**
   - **Call 911** if you feel in immediate danger, someone is approaching you, trying doors, making threats, following you, or you believe a crime is in progress.
   - **Otherwise, contact your local police non-emergency number or online reporting route** and report the repeated pattern and your recorded details. The correct route varies by city or county, so use your local police or city website.
6. **Ask for a call or report number and keep one running timeline.** Each new sighting is easier to explain when it is tied to an existing report.
7. **Make small safety changes for the next day.**
   - Do not walk to or from your car alone if you can avoid it; ask a coworker, security escort, neighbor, or friend.
   - Use well-lit, busier routes and entrances.
   - Park near lights, cameras, or staffed areas rather than isolated spots.
   - Have your phone ready before you step outside and avoid being distracted while walking.
8. **If it feels targeted at you, broaden awareness carefully.** Tell one trusted neighbor or coworker what the vehicle looks like and ask them to notify you, and to call police if needed, not to confront.
9. **If you think it could involve a serious crime or threat, still report locally first.** In an emergency call 911; otherwise notify local law enforcement. You can also submit a separate FBI tip for serious federal crimes, but do not investigate on your own.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the driver or owner right now.
- You do not need to confront anyone or gather proof.
- You do not need to buy security gear immediately; saving existing footage and reporting a repeat pattern is more important today.
- You do not need to decide what this means yet. Focus on distance, documentation, and the right reporting route.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel unsettled by a repeated, odd-hours pattern near places you rely on. Many situations turn out to be ordinary parking, deliveries, shift work, or waiting for someone, but calm safety steps reduce risk without forcing you to overreact.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the pattern continues, you may need local, situation-specific guidance from law enforcement, workplace security, building management, or a victim support service.

## Important note
This guide is general information for immediate safety and decision support, not legal, security, investigative, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 911.
