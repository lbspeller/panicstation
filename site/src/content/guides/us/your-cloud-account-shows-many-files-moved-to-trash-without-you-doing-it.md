# What to do if… your cloud account shows many files moved to trash without you doing it (USA)

## Short answer
Stop syncing and do not permanently delete anything. Secure the cloud account and the email account tied to it, then restore files carefully from the provider’s website.

## Do not do these things
- Do not empty Trash, Recently Deleted, Recycle bin, or Deleted files.
- Do not use “delete forever” or any permanent delete option while you are still checking what happened.
- Do not let several devices keep syncing and changing the same files at once.
- Do not click links in security-alert emails or texts; open the provider’s site or app yourself.
- Do not pay someone who contacts you claiming they can recover the files outside the provider’s official support.
- Do not assume the cloud account is the only problem; the email account used for password resets may also need securing.

## What to do now

1. **Pause cloud sync on every device you can reach.**  
   Quit or pause the cloud app on computers and phones. Do not edit, rename, move, or delete affected files while you are checking, because more synced changes can make recovery harder.

2. **Open the provider’s website directly and check the deleted-items area.**  
   Use a saved bookmark, the official app, or type the provider’s address yourself. Check the area called Trash, Recently Deleted, Recycle bin, or Deleted files, and look for the date or time the files were moved there.

3. **Check recent activity before restoring everything.**  
   Look for an activity log, file history, version history, audit log, recent events, or security activity page. Note any unfamiliar device, app, user, shared-folder action, or timestamp.

4. **Secure the cloud account.**  
   In the account security settings, sign out other devices or sessions if the provider offers that option. Change the password to a new, unique password. Turn on multi-factor authentication. Remove third-party apps, browser extensions, or connected services you do not recognize or no longer need.

5. **Secure the email account used for this cloud account.**  
   Change that email password, turn on multi-factor authentication, review recent sign-ins, and check that recovery email addresses and phone numbers are yours. Check for email forwarding rules or filters you did not create.

6. **Tell the shared-folder owner or admin, if this involves shared files.**  
   Ask whether they made a bulk change, changed permissions, removed a shared folder, or can see an admin audit log. If this is a work, school, or team account, report it through the normal internal support or security route.

7. **Make a brief incident note.**  
   Write down when you noticed the problem, which folders were affected, the approximate number of files involved, any unfamiliar sign-ins or devices, and any account changes you made. Keep it simple.

8. **Restore a small batch first.**  
   Restore a few non-critical files from the provider’s deleted-items area and watch whether they stay restored. If they move back to trash, stop restoring and re-check paused sync clients, shared users, connected apps, and automation rules.

9. **Ask the provider’s official support about bulk restore and logs.**  
   Use the provider’s official help page or in-account support. Ask whether they can show which user, device, app, or sync client moved the files, whether bulk restore is available, and how long deleted files remain recoverable for your account type.

10. **Report cyber-enabled crime only if it seems relevant.**  
   If you suspect account takeover, fraud, extortion, or other cyber-enabled crime, you can report it to the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center. If anyone is in immediate danger, call 911 or local police instead.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide right now whether to wipe or reinstall devices. You do not need to restore every file today if the account is still changing. A full root-cause investigation, new backup system, or long-term security plan can wait until the account is stable and the most important files are protected.

## Important reassurance
Finding many files in trash is alarming, but many cloud services keep deleted files recoverable for a limited time, depending on the provider, plan, permissions, and whether anything was permanently deleted. The safest first order is to stop more changes, secure access, then restore carefully.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce further loss and regain control. Later decisions, especially for workplace data, large data loss, suspected fraud, or repeated compromise, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, cybersecurity, forensic, financial, or professional advice. If this involves workplace, school, client, or regulated data, follow the organization’s incident response process and report internally right away.
