# What to do if… you are locked out of a storage unit or safe deposit box due to unpaid fees (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the storage facility or bank now. Ask for the exact current status, the amount and payment method needed to restore access if that is still possible, and any approaching deadline in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force entry or tamper with locks. This can create additional legal or practical problems.
- Do not assume you still have plenty of time. The next step can depend on your state, your agreement, and the current account status.
- Do not rely only on a verbal promise. Ask for written confirmation of the status and any required action.
- Do not ignore notices sent by mail, email, text message, or an online account message.
- Do not make a payment without keeping a receipt or screenshot.
- Do not assume that filing a complaint automatically stops a scheduled self-storage sale.

## What to do now
1. **Identify which type of storage is involved.**
   - For a **self-storage unit**, the next step can depend on state law, your rental agreement, and whether a sale has been scheduled.
   - For a **bank safe deposit box**, the bank may still hold the contents, or it may have transferred unclaimed contents to a state treasurer or unclaimed-property office after the applicable state process.

2. **Gather the minimum useful details.**
   - The facility or bank name and location
   - The unit or box number
   - The account-holder name
   - Any notices you received
   - Any recent payment receipts or failed-payment messages
   - Your current phone number, email address, and mailing address

3. **Contact the facility or bank and ask for written confirmation of the status.**
   Ask:
   - “What is the current status of my unit or box?”
   - “What amount, if any, is needed to restore access?”
   - “What payment methods are accepted, and does the payment need to clear before access is restored?”
   - “Is there a deadline or scheduled event that I need to know about?”
   - “When would access be restored after I complete the required step?”

4. **If this is a self-storage unit, ask whether a sale has been scheduled.**
   If a sale is pending, ask for the exact deadline and the action required to stop it. After completing that action, ask for written confirmation that the sale will not go ahead. If essential items such as medication, identification, or keys are inside, ask whether the facility can allow a limited supervised visit to retrieve them.

5. **If this is a bank safe deposit box, ask where the contents are now.**
   Ask whether the box is still at the bank, whether it has been drilled or opened, and whether the contents have been transferred to a state treasurer or unclaimed-property office. If the contents were transferred, use the official state program listed through NAUPA to search or start a claim.

6. **If you think the charges or payment history are wrong, start a simple paper trail.**
   Request an itemized ledger of charges and payments. Save notices, receipts, screenshots, and a brief note of each call. Contact the facility or bank first and state what you believe needs correcting.

7. **Use an outside complaint route only if the issue remains unresolved.**
   - For a self-storage dispute, use the USAGov consumer-complaint guidance and state consumer-protection directory.
   - For a bank safe-deposit-box dispute, check whether the OCC regulates the bank before using the OCC complaint route. If it does not, follow the regulator options listed on that page.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to keep the unit or box long-term.
- You do not need to build a perfect dispute file before making the first call.
- You do not need to resolve every fee disagreement before asking about the next deadline.
- You do not need to choose a long-term plan until you know whether the valuables are still being held, a self-storage sale is pending, or safe-deposit-box contents were transferred.

## Important reassurance
Being locked out can feel final, but the current status may still leave a practical next step. Confirming where the valuables are and what deadline applies can make the situation more manageable.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. State law, your agreement, and the type of storage can affect what happens next. A local consumer-protection office or legal adviser may be useful if a sale is close, a notice appears wrong, or valuables cannot be located.
