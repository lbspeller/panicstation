# What to do if… you receive a vague meeting invite asking you to bring a colleague or representative (USA)

## Short answer
Reply promptly and ask in writing what the meeting is about, who will attend, and what “colleague or representative” means in your workplace. If you are union-represented and reasonably believe an investigatory interview could lead to discipline, clearly request your representative before answering substantive questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the wording tells you exactly what kind of meeting this is.
- Do not ignore the invite or miss the meeting without confirming what you are doing.
- Do not assume that “representative” means a lawyer, coworker, union representative, or silent observer; ask what is allowed.
- Do not assume that every workplace gives non-union employees the same representation rights.
- Do not sign a document or agree that meeting notes are accurate before you have read them carefully.
- Do not send emotional or speculative messages while you still lack basic information.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for basic clarity in writing.**  
   Ask:
   - what type of meeting this is
   - the topic or topics they expect to discuss
   - who will attend and who will lead the meeting
   - whether the meeting may involve questions about your conduct or performance
   - whether discipline is a possible outcome
   - what they mean by “colleague or representative” in your workplace

   The employer may not provide every detail in advance. A short written request still creates a clear record of what you asked.

2. **If you are union-represented, contact your union promptly.**  
   Under current NLRB guidance, a union-represented employee may request a representative for an investigatory interview that the employee reasonably believes could lead to discipline. Make the request personally.

   A simple script is:
   - “If this is an investigatory interview that could lead to discipline, I request my representative before answering questions.”

   For a covered request, the employer may delay the interview until a representative is available, end the interview, or offer you the choice whether to continue without representation.

3. **Ask what the representative may do.**  
   Ask whether the person may attend, take notes, speak, ask for clarification, or meet with you briefly before questioning. For a covered Weingarten interview, the representative may be a union steward, union agent or officer, or fellow employee, subject to reasonable limits. The representative may provide advice and active assistance.

4. **If you are not union-represented, or you are unsure about coverage, check the workplace rules.**  
   Ask whether a coworker or other support person may attend under a handbook, contract, collective bargaining agreement, or public-sector rule. If the answer is no, ask whether you may:
   - receive a short written outline of the topics
   - take notes during the meeting
   - have a brief pause to review any new document before responding
   - confirm your understanding in writing after the meeting

5. **If the meeting starts and the purpose is still unclear, ask before substantive questions begin.**  
   Say:
   - “Before we start, please confirm the purpose of the meeting and whether you expect to ask questions about my conduct or performance.”
   - “Please confirm whether discipline is a possible outcome.”
   - “Please confirm the role of the colleague or representative mentioned in the invite.”

   If a new issue or document appears during the meeting, ask for a brief pause to read it and understand what response is expected.

6. **Keep a factual record.**  
   Save the invite and your replies. Note the date, time, attendees, requests you made, and any response you received. Keep the note in a place you can lawfully access. After the meeting, write a short factual summary while the details are fresh.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately what the meeting means for your job.
- You do not need to send a long explanation before you know the topic.
- You can ask for clarification before giving a detailed response where possible.
- You can ask for a brief review period before signing a document or responding to new material.
- Broader decisions about external steps can usually wait until you understand the meeting and any deadline, unless a deadline is already close.

## Important reassurance
A vague invite can feel alarming, especially when it mentions bringing someone. Asking for the purpose, the attendees, and the role of the other person is a reasonable first step. Take the meeting one step at a time and avoid rushing into a detailed response before you understand what is being asked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on whether the meeting is investigatory, disciplinary, performance-related, or something else, and on your union status, contract, workplace policy, and whether public-sector rules apply.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. National Labor Relations Act coverage is not universal. Public-sector employees and some other workers may be covered by different rules. If you are unsure, ask your union, check your workplace documents, or use the official NLRB resources below.
