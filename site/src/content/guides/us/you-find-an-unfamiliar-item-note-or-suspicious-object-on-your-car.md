# What to do if… you find an unfamiliar item, note, or suspicious object on your car (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a safer place before you inspect anything. If the item looks attached, bulky, wired, hidden, leaking, or deliberately placed where you would not expect it, do not touch the item or the car; move away and call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not touch, open, move, peel off, or kick anything that looks attached, taped, magnetic, boxed, wired, hidden, leaking, or out of place.
- Do not get into the car if you feel watched, followed, blocked in, or unsafe.
- Do not confront anyone you suspect placed it.
- Do not reply to a note with your name, phone number, home address, workplace, routine, or other personal information.
- Do not drive the car away if you suspect something has been attached underneath, near the wheels, near the engine area, or near the fuel door.
- Do not post photos online that show your license plate, home street, workplace, regular parking spot, or other identifying details.

## What to do now
1. **Make yourself safer first.**  
   Go into a nearby store, building, lobby, staffed office, security desk, or well-lit busy area. Ask staff, security, or another safe person to stay nearby while you decide what to do.

2. **Look from a distance for only a few seconds.**  
   Decide whether it appears to be:
   - a clearly loose paper note, flyer, tag, or card resting on the car or under a wiper; or
   - something attached, installed, hidden, bulky, wired, taped, magnetic, leaking, wedged under the car, near a wheel, near the bumper, near the fuel door, or not quickly identifiable.

3. **If it is attached, hidden, bulky, wired, leaking, or suspicious, treat it as unsafe.**  
   Move away from the car, keep other people back, and call 911 from a safer distance. Say your exact location and that there is an unknown item on your vehicle that appears attached, hidden, or suspicious.

4. **If it is clearly a loose note, flyer, or tag and nothing suggests immediate danger, document it before moving it.**  
   Take a wide photo showing where it was placed, then a close photo of the item. Write down the time, location, who was with you, and anything unusual nearby. If you are at a store, garage, workplace, school, hotel, apartment complex, or managed lot, ask staff or security to preserve camera footage for the time window and write down who you spoke to.

5. **Only if the item is clearly loose and safe to handle, preserve it simply.**  
   Use a tissue, clean bag, envelope, or gloves if available. Handle it as little as possible, place it in a bag or envelope, and keep it somewhere you can find later if you decide to report.

6. **Check the outside of the car before getting in.**  
   From a safe position, look once around the car for anything new on the windshield, door handles, mirrors, wheel wells, bumpers, underside edges, fuel door, roof, trunk, or under the wipers. If anything looks attached or deliberately hidden, step back and follow Step 3.

7. **Check your phone for unwanted tracker alerts.**  
   If you received an unknown tracker, AirTag, Find My, or Android tracker alert, take screenshots of the alert and any map before changing settings. On an iPhone, follow the alert or Find My instructions for more information. On Android, use the unknown tracker alert or manual scan options if your phone offers them. These checks may not find every possible tracker.

8. **If a tracker alert or found tracker makes you feel unsafe, move to a safe public place and contact law enforcement.**  
   If you find a tracker and you are not in immediate danger, consider asking local law enforcement what they want you to preserve before disabling or discarding it.

9. **If you think you are being targeted, use the safest reporting route available.**  
   If you are being followed, threatened, blocked in, or in immediate danger, call 911. If the danger is not immediate, call the local police non-emergency number to ask about making a report and getting a case or report number.

10. **If you suspect your phone or accounts may be monitored, avoid sudden account changes in the moment.**  
   Use a safer phone, a trusted person’s device, a library computer, or an in-person support route if possible. Making changes on a monitored device can sometimes alert the person watching it.

11. **If you want confidential support before deciding what this is, contact victim support.**  
   VictimConnect can help with confidential support and referrals in the United States by phone, text, or online chat.

## What can wait
- You do not need to figure out who did it or why right now.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether it counts as stalking, harassment, a prank, or a crime.
- You do not need to confront anyone, post about it, or send photos to friends before you are in a safer place.
- You do not need to make major life changes in this moment unless there is immediate danger and you have support.

## Important reassurance
It makes sense to feel alarmed. Many items on cars are harmless flyers, mistakes, or ordinary notes, but it is reasonable to treat an unfamiliar attached object, hidden object, tracker alert, or targeted message as a safety concern until you have help and a safer pause.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes only. If this becomes a pattern, later decisions may need help from local law enforcement, victim-support services, a qualified safety advocate, or another specialist familiar with your situation.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. If you believe you or someone else is in immediate danger, call 911.
