# What to do if… someone is sending rapid messages demanding you respond immediately and the tone turns controlling (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause first. Mute the message thread, stop the rapid reply loop, and contact a trusted person. If you are in immediate danger or the person is nearby and escalating, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep replying quickly just to reduce the pressure. Rapid replying can keep the pressure loop going.
- Do not share your location, plans, passwords, or who you are with to placate the person.
- Do not agree to meet in person to resolve the messages if you feel unsafe.
- Do not delete the original messages before deciding whether you may want them later.
- Do not make sudden account, device, or location-sharing changes if you think the person may be monitoring you and the changes could raise risk.
- Do not take screenshots inside an app until you have checked whether the app may notify the other person.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause if it is safe to move.**  
   Go somewhere you feel safer, such as a room with other people, a public place, or a staffed area. If the person is nearby, trying to reach you in person, or making you feel in immediate danger, call 911.

2. **Reduce the immediate message pressure.**  
   Mute the thread or contact. Turn on Do Not Disturb if that is safe. You do not need to keep reading each new message as it arrives.

3. **Tell one trusted person what is happening.**  
   Send a brief message such as: “Someone is sending controlling rapid messages. Can you stay available?” If you are at work or school and the person may show up, tell security, the front desk, or another responsible person.

4. **Preserve the messages only if it is safe.**  
   Keep the original messages. Note the date, time, account or phone number, and any threats or attempts to control where you are. If screenshots are safe in the app, capture the number or account, timestamps, and relevant messages. If the app may alert the other person, consider using a second safer device to photograph the screen instead.

5. **Choose whether any reply is safer than silence.**  
   You do not owe an immediate response. If a single neutral message is likely to reduce risk, you could send: “I am not available to text right now.” Do not continue a back-and-forth conversation. If any reply feels unsafe, do not reply.

6. **Use block or report tools only when it feels safe.**  
   If you decide to block or report the account, consider preserving the messages first. Delay this step if you think it could increase risk or lead the person to try other ways to reach you.

7. **Contact specialist support if this may be connected to relationship abuse or monitoring.**  
   You can contact the National Domestic Violence Hotline for confidential support and safety planning:
   - Call **1-800-799-SAFE (7233)**
   - Text **START** to **88788**
   - Use the chat option at **thehotline.org**

8. **Use a safer device for sensitive help-seeking if you think your phone is monitored.**  
   Consider using a trusted person’s phone or another device the person has not accessed. Avoid major device or account changes until you can think through safety and evidence preservation with support.

9. **Consider local help if the messages include threats or persistent harassment.**  
   When there is no immediate danger, you may contact your local police non-emergency line or a local support service to ask about options. You do not have to decide now whether to make a formal report.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide now whether to end a relationship, seek a protective order, or make a formal report.
- You do not have to write a detailed explanation or the perfect response.
- Password changes, location-sharing reviews, account checks, and changing your phone number can wait until you are at a safer pause and can consider whether changes might raise risk.
- You do not have to work out whether the behavior fits a particular label before asking for support.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to feel anxious, guilty, frozen, or unsure when someone is demanding immediate access to you. Rapid demands and a controlling tone are reasons to slow the situation down, protect your privacy, and involve support. You are allowed to take time before responding, or not respond.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate pressure and buy time. Later decisions may need support from a domestic violence service, a stalking support resource, local law enforcement, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
