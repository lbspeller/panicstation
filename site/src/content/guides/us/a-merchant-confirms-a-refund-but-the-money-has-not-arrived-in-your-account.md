# What to do if… a merchant confirms a refund but the money has not arrived in your account (USA)

## Short answer
Save the merchant’s refund confirmation and contact the provider for the original payment method now. Ask the merchant for the refund date, amount, destination, and any available trace or reference number; for a card refund, ask whether an acquirer reference number (ARN) is available.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume a merchant confirmation means the money is available today.
- Don’t wait to check your statement, transactions, and provider instructions. Deadlines can depend on how you paid.
- Don’t ignore your credit-card bill while the refund is being traced. Pay undisputed charges on time and follow the issuer’s instructions.
- Don’t request another refund until the merchant has checked the destination and status of the existing refund.
- Don’t assume a replaced or closed card means the refund is lost. Tell the issuer what changed and ask how incoming credits are handled.

## What to do now
1. Write down the merchant name, refund amount, refund date, purchase date, and how you paid. Save the merchant’s refund email, chat, or screenshot.
2. Check the original payment route:
   - Look at your recent transactions and latest statement.
   - Check whether the merchant sent store credit instead of money.
   - Check any wallet or BNPL account used for the purchase.
   - If the card or account was replaced or closed, note that for the issuer or bank.
3. Ask the merchant to confirm the refund amount, date, destination, and the last four digits of the card if relevant. Ask for any available trace or reference number. For a card refund, ask whether an ARN is available.
4. Contact the provider for the original payment method. Say that the merchant confirmed a refund but the credit has not appeared. Ask the provider to check for the refund, tell you whether it can open a case, and explain any deadline or written-document requirement.
5. If you paid by credit card and the statement that should show the credit does not show it, use the issuer’s billing-dispute instructions promptly. Federal billing-error rules generally require the issuer to receive written notice no later than 60 days after it transmitted the statement on which the credit should have appeared. Use the billing-inquiries or billing-dispute address, or an electronic method the issuer says it accepts. Include your name, account-identifying information, amount, dates, and a clear explanation. Keep a copy.
6. If you paid by debit card or ACH, tell the bank promptly that an expected refund or credit is missing. Ask whether it can investigate through its electronic-fund-transfer error-resolution process. If the bank treats your phone report as an error notice, it may require written confirmation within 10 business days and should tell you where to send it.
7. If you paid through a wallet or BNPL provider, use that provider’s official dispute or error process and ask about its deadline. If a linked card or bank account is also affected, ask that issuer or bank which route applies.
8. If a bank, card issuer, or other financial company is not following its process, you may submit a complaint to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide who is ultimately responsible before the payment route has been checked.
- You don’t need to threaten legal action while the refund is being traced.
- You don’t need perfect records before making the first call. Start with the merchant confirmation, amount, dates, and payment method.
- You don’t need to decide whether to use the merchant again right now.

## Important reassurance
A missing refund is a reasonable thing to ask about. A clear record and any available reference number can help the merchant, issuer, bank, or payment provider investigate what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps to locate the missing refund and avoid avoidable deadline problems. Later decisions may need help from the payment provider or a specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Processes and deadlines can depend on the payment method, account type, provider, and facts of the transaction.
