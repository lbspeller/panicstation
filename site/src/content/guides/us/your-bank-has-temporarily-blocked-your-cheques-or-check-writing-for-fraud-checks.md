# What to do if… your bank has temporarily blocked your cheques or check writing for fraud checks (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the notice through your bank’s app or the number on the back of your card, ask exactly which check payments are affected, and move any urgent bills to a safe alternative payment method.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t call phone numbers or click links in an unexpected text, email, or pop-up about “fraud”; contact your bank through the app or the number on your card.
- Don’t keep writing checks hoping they will go through; they may be delayed, declined, or returned unpaid.
- Don’t send a replacement payment until you know whether the original check may still clear.
- Don’t share one-time codes, online banking passwords, card PINs, or full Social Security numbers with anyone who contacted you first.
- Don’t let anyone “help” by remote access to your phone or computer.
- Don’t close the account before asking how that would affect pending checks, deposits, fees, and the fraud review.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the notice is real.** Open your bank’s app, use its official website typed in directly, or call the number on the back of your debit card. If someone contacted you first, end that contact and start a new one through a trusted channel.
2. **Ask the bank exactly what is restricted.** Ask whether only check writing is blocked, or whether cards, ACH transfers, bill pay, wires, deposits, or withdrawals are also affected.
3. **Ask what happens to checks already written.** For each recent check, ask whether it has cleared, is pending, is likely to be returned unpaid, or needs a stop-payment request.
4. **Write down a check inventory.** List each outstanding check number, date, payee, amount, and purpose. Mark any missing, unfamiliar, altered, or stolen check as urgent when you speak to the bank.
5. **Stabilize urgent payments due today or this week.** Contact essential payees such as rent, utilities, childcare, insurance, loan, or tax payment offices. Ask for their safest alternative payment route, such as their official online portal, ACH, card payment, money order, cashier’s check, or another method they confirm directly.
6. **Avoid double-paying by accident.** Before sending a replacement payment, ask the payee and the bank how to document that the original check is stopped, voided, returned, or not yet paid.
7. **If a check may be lost, stolen, altered, or fraudulent, ask about stop payment right away.** Ask the bank what information it needs, whether there is a fee, how long the stop payment lasts, and whether you need written confirmation.
8. **If identity misuse is possible, use the official identity theft route.** IdentityTheft.gov can help you report identity theft and create a recovery plan. If someone may be opening accounts in your name, you can consider a fraud alert by contacting one of the three credit bureaus, or a credit freeze by contacting each of the three credit bureaus.
9. **Escalate if you cannot get a clear answer.** If the bank will not explain the restriction, will not say what happens to pending checks, or the delay is causing serious payment harm, submit a complaint through the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and keep copies of notices, messages, check details, and payment attempts.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to permanently stop using checks.
- You do not need to switch banks while you are still finding out what is blocked.
- You do not need to argue blame with payees right now; focus on keeping essential payments current and documented.
- You do not need to complete every identity-theft step unless there are signs your personal information or account access has been misused.

## Important reassurance
A temporary check block can be a protective step while a bank reviews possible fraud. The immediate goal is not to solve everything at once; it is to verify the notice, prevent missed essential payments, and make sure suspicious checks are handled safely.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the bank confirms fraud, account takeover, identity theft, or repeated returned payments, later decisions may need help from the bank’s fraud team, a consumer protection agency, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Bank policies, fees, timelines, and account restrictions vary by institution and state. If anything feels pressured or suspicious, end the interaction and re-contact your bank using a trusted channel.
