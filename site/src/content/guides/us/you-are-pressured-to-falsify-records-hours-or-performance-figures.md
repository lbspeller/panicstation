# What to do if… you are pressured to falsify records, hours, or performance figures (USA)

## Short answer
Avoid resigning or refusing the transfer immediately. Request all reassignment terms in writing and create a factual timeline of the conflict and the transfer to preserve your legal options and professional standing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign or walk off the job site while in a state of shock or anger.
- Do not send aggressive or emotional emails to the people involved in the conflict.
- Do not sign any acknowledgement of performance issues or separation papers on the spot.
- Do not refuse the transfer verbally before understanding your legal rights or union protections.
- Do not rely on verbal assurances that your pay or duties will remain identical.

## What to do now
1. **Request a brief pause before responding.** If you are told in person, state: "I need a moment to process this information; please send the details of this transfer to my email so I can review them." 
2. **Obtain the specific terms of the new role in writing.** Ask Human Resources (HR) or your current manager to provide a document covering:
   - Your new job title and the name of your new direct supervisor.
   - The effective start date and whether the move is permanent or temporary.
   - Any changes to your pay rate, hours, schedule, or physical work location.
   - A list of updated job duties and expectations for the first 30 days.
   - Handover instructions for your current projects and deadlines.
3. **Document the sequence of events immediately.** Write a private log (not on a company device) that includes:
   - The date, time, and nature of the original conflict.
   - Names of any witnesses to the conflict or the transfer notification.
   - The exact time elapsed between your report/conflict and the transfer notice.
   - Any comments made by management that linked the transfer to the conflict.
4. **Identify the nature of the underlying conflict.** Determine if the incident you were involved in is "protected activity" under federal law:
   - **Discrimination/Harassment:** Reporting issues related to race, sex, age (40+), disability, or religion.
   - **Safety/Whistleblowing:** Reporting safety hazards or legal violations (e.g., OSHA, environmental laws).
   - **Concerted Activity:** Acting with coworkers to improve working conditions (Note: Supervisors are generally not covered by the NLRA).
5. **Verify critical filing deadlines.** Be aware that different types of retaliation have strict time limits for external reporting:
   - **OSHA (Safety):** Complaints must typically be filed within 30 days of the transfer.
   - **Federal Employees:** Must contact an EEO counselor within 45 days.
   - **EEOC (Discrimination):** Generally 180 days, but may be 300 days if a state agency also covers the claim.
   - **NLRB (Group Activity):** Generally 6 months.
6. **Communicate your concerns professionally via email.** Send a neutral follow-up to HR: "I am confirming receipt of the transfer notice. I am concerned about the timing of this move following my report/incident on [Date]. Please clarify the business necessity for this change."
7. **Maintain standard performance in the new role.** Meet all deadlines and follow new protocols to prevent the employer from citing "poor performance" or "insubordination" as a secondary reason for the transfer.
8. **Consult your union representative or steward.** If you are in a unionized workplace, contact your rep immediately to determine if the transfer violates your Collective Bargaining Agreement (CBA).

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to file a formal lawsuit or external charge.
- Confronting the manager or coworker involved in the original dispute.
- Cleaning out your old desk or returning equipment until handover is confirmed.
- Determining if you should look for a new job entirely.

## Important reassurance
A sudden transfer can feel like a punishment, but it is often a procedural step used by employers to separate parties while an investigation occurs. Taking a moment to document facts and stay professional does not mean you are accepting the situation; it means you are building a record that protects your future interests.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for employment-related moves following a conflict. It focuses on immediate documentation and harm prevention. Later steps may require an employment attorney or specific agency filings.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment laws vary significantly by state and specific job sector. If you believe your physical safety is at risk at work, contact local law enforcement or your site security immediately.
