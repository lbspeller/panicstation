# What to do if… a medical provider says services will be paused unless an unexpected balance is paid immediately (USA)

## Short answer
Prioritize medical evaluation at an emergency department if your condition is urgent. If the situation is not an emergency, request an itemized bill and a formal billing hold while you verify the charges with your insurer or request a Good Faith Estimate.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay a disputed balance immediately out of fear of care interruption without obtaining an itemized breakdown.
- Do not provide credit card or bank details over the phone to unsolicited callers claiming to be from the billing office.
- Do not sign new financial responsibility waivers or medical loan applications under immediate physical or emotional duress.
- Do not assume a verbal quote of a balance is accurate or that your insurance has already processed it correctly.
- Do not ignore the demand; proactive documentation is required to prevent the account from progressing to collections.

## What to do now
1. **Determine if the situation requires immediate emergency care and act accordingly.** **If you are experiencing a medical emergency, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency department immediately.**
   - At Medicare-participating hospitals, the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (EMTALA) requires a medical screening exam and stabilization regardless of ability to pay.
   - Hospitals cannot delay this screening or treatment to ask for payment or insurance information.

2. **Request an itemized bill and the specific codes for the services rendered.** **Ask the provider for a printed or digital itemized statement that includes CPT (procedure) and ICD-10 (diagnosis) codes.**
   - Request the account number and the specific date of service for the balance.
   - Ask for a "plain-language summary" of the provider's Financial Assistance Policy (FAP).

3. **Request a formal billing hold while you investigate the charges.** **Inform the billing department that you are disputing the balance and request that the account be placed on "dispute status" to prevent further escalation.**
   - Ask for a written confirmation or a reference number for this hold.
   - Request that the account not be sent to collections or have late fees applied while the review is pending.

4. **Verify the status of the claim with your insurance provider.** **Contact your insurer to confirm if a claim was submitted and what your specific "member responsibility" (copay, coinsurance, or deductible) is for that date of service.**
   - Ask if the provider is in-network and if "Prior Authorization" was required but missing.
   - If the claim was denied due to a coding error, ask the provider to resubmit a corrected claim.

5. **Request a Good Faith Estimate (GFE) if you are uninsured or paying out-of-pocket.** **Ask for a written Good Faith Estimate for the services being provided or for the expected costs of future care.**
   - Under the No Surprises Act, if your final bill is at least $400 higher than the GFE for a specific provider, you have 120 calendar days from the date of the bill to initiate the Patient-Provider Dispute Resolution (PPDR) process.

6. **Apply for financial assistance or charity care programs.** **Submit a request for the provider’s financial assistance application to see if you qualify for a balance reduction or waiver.**
   - Tax-exempt nonprofit hospitals are required by Section 501(r) of the Internal Revenue Code to have a written financial assistance policy.
   - Eligibility is often based on your household income relative to the Federal Poverty Level.

7. **Document all interactions regarding the payment demand.** **Keep a log of every phone call, portal message, and in-person conversation, including dates, names of representatives, and exact quotes.**
   - Save copies of any notices stating that care will be paused.
   - Store these records in a dedicated physical or digital folder.

## What can wait
- You do not need to pay the full balance today to begin the dispute process.
- You do not need to apply for medical credit cards or high-interest financing products immediately.
- You do not need to file a formal complaint with state or federal regulators until you have received a written response to your initial dispute.
- You do not need to resolve insurance appeals or charity care applications in a single day; these processes often take weeks.

## Important reassurance
It is common for medical billing and insurance processing to contain errors. Requesting documentation and a pause on payment is a standard administrative step and does not mean you are refusing to pay a valid debt. Billing departments are accustomed to holds while insurance claims are reprocessed.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for unexpected medical billing demands. It does not cover long-term medical debt management, legal defense for lawsuits, or the specifics of state-level surprise billing laws which may offer additional protections beyond federal rules.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal or medical advice. Jurisdictional rules regarding medical billing, credit reporting, and patient rights may change. Always prioritize emergency medical needs over financial disputes.
