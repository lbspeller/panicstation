# What to do if… your social media is being targeted by a hostile surge and you fear for your safety (USA)

## Short answer
Stop engaging and switch to safety mode: reduce what strangers can learn about you, lock down account access, and tell one real-world support person what is happening. If you believe someone may come to your location, you are being followed, or you received a credible threat of immediate harm, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t argue or try to “set the record straight” while the attention is surging; it can intensify attention and reveal personal details.
- Don’t post your location, schedule, travel plans, workplace details, or photos that show identifiable surroundings.
- Don’t delete the most serious threats, doxxing, impersonation, or harassment before saving limited evidence.
- Don’t move the conflict to direct messages with strangers, even if they sound calm.
- Don’t make threats back, joke about violence, or post “if you come here” messages.
- Don’t meet anyone who claims they want to talk or resolve this.
- Don’t assume online attention is only online if people are sharing your address, workplace, school, regular routes, or threats to come near you.

## What to do now
1. **Take a short safety pause and check your surroundings.**  
   If you are in public and feel watched or unsafe, move into a well-lit, staffed place. Decide for now: no posting, no replying, and no live updates while you stabilize.

2. **Tell one trusted person and set check-ins.**  
   Send a quick message with what is happening, where you are, and when you will check in again. If you live alone and feel exposed, consider staying with someone you trust or having someone stay with you tonight.

3. **Lock down account access.**  
   Change passwords on the affected social accounts and the email account connected to them. Turn on two-factor authentication where available. Check active sessions and log out devices you do not recognise. Make sure recovery email addresses and phone numbers are yours.

4. **Reduce access and discoverability.**  
   - Switch accounts to private where possible.
   - Limit who can comment, reply, mention, tag, or DM you.
   - Remove public contact buttons, personal email, phone number, and link-in-bio pages that point to personal information.
   - If the platform supports it, filter keywords, hide replies, restrict new followers, or pause notifications temporarily.

5. **Reduce your real-life trace, especially if doxxing is possible.**  
   - Remove or limit visibility of posts showing your home exterior, street signs, car or license plate, children’s school identifiers, workplace entrance, regular venues, or commute.
   - Turn off location access for social apps and stop posting in real time.
   - Ask friends not to tag you, post your location, or reply publicly “in support” with personal details about you.

6. **Preserve focused evidence.**  
   Save the most serious examples first: threats, doxxing, impersonation, attempts to contact your workplace or school, and messages naming your address or schedule. Capture usernames, timestamps, platform names, and links or URLs where possible, then step away.

7. **Use platform reporting tools deliberately.**  
   Report specific posts or accounts for threats, doxxing, impersonation, harassment, or sharing private information. Block or mute after you have saved the most important evidence.

8. **Contact the right help for immediate risk.**  
   - Call 911 if you believe someone may come to your location, you are being followed, or you received a credible threat of immediate harm.
   - If there is no immediate danger, consider calling your local police non-emergency line to make a report and ask what they recommend for your area.
   - If you are worried about a false emergency response being sent to you, sometimes called swatting, contact local police non-emergency and ask how they handle that concern locally.
   - If the harassment includes fraud, extortion, account compromise, or other internet-enabled crime, you can also submit a report to the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center.

9. **Create a short buffer for the next 24 hours.**  
   If your address may be circulating, keep doors locked, do not open the door to unexpected visitors, and consider postponing non-essential outings. If your workplace or school could be targeted, discreetly alert a supervisor, reception desk, or security contact that hostile online attention is happening and ask them not to confirm your schedule or personal details to callers or visitors.

## What can wait
- Posting a statement, apology, final word, or long explanation.
- Reading everything being said about you, especially quote-posts and comment threads.
- Trying to identify the original instigator or prove your case to strangers.
- Major decisions like deleting all accounts, changing your name, or relocating.
- Deciding whether to pursue longer-term legal steps, unless a professional tells you something is urgent.

## Important reassurance
A sudden pile-on can make your body react as if danger is already in the room. Shaking, nausea, racing thoughts, and the urge to fix the narrative are common stress reactions. Right now, your task is narrower: reduce access to you, save the most serious evidence, and put real people and local systems around you.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the initial hours or days of sudden hostile attention. If threats continue, personal details are posted, people contact you repeatedly, or anyone follows or approaches you offline, later decisions may need specialist help, victim support, cybersecurity support, legal advice, or tailored safety planning.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, mental health, cybersecurity, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in danger or a crime is occurring, contact emergency services or local law enforcement.
