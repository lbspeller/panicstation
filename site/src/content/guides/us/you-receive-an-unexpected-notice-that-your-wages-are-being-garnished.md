# What to do if… you receive an unexpected notice that your wages are being garnished (USA)

## Short answer
Get the court or agency name and case number from payroll, then verify the order directly through the official court or issuing agency. If you were never properly served, the debt is not yours, or you may qualify for protections, ask the court or agency what you must file to object, claim exemptions, or request a temporary pause.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t call numbers, click links, or pay anyone using only the details in the notice until you have verified the court or agency independently.
- Don’t rely on a debt collector to explain deadlines or tell you the only way to fix it.
- Don’t ignore any deadline shown on official paperwork; missing it can make the garnishment much harder to stop or challenge.
- Don’t quit your job, empty accounts, or make panic payments before you know what the order is.
- Don’t give your Social Security number, bank details, or payroll login information to a caller just because they mention court, garnishment, or legal action.

## What to do now
1. **Ask payroll for the exact papers they received.** Ask for copies of the notice or order, including:
   - the court or agency name,
   - the case number,
   - the document type, such as writ, order, income withholding order, levy, or garnishment,
   - the plaintiff, creditor, agency, attorney, or collection firm listed,
   - the date withholding is supposed to start,
   - any page that explains objections, exemptions, hearings, or deadlines.

2. **Verify the case through an official source, not the sender.**
   - Use the official court website, court clerk, or issuing agency contact details you find yourself.
   - Ask for the judgment, garnishment paperwork, docket entries, and proof of service.
   - Ask what address was used for service and when the court record says service happened.

3. **Identify what kind of garnishment it is before responding.**
   - A private creditor usually needs a court judgment before wages are garnished.
   - Child support withholding, tax levies, federal agency debts, and some student-loan collections may use different procedures.
   - Use the issuer named on the document to decide which court, agency, or office to contact.

4. **If you did not know about the lawsuit, ask about the local procedure to challenge the judgment.**
   - Say: “I did not receive notice, or I may not have been properly served. What forms or procedure does this court use to ask to set aside or vacate a default judgment?”
   - Ask whether there is a way to request a stay or pause of garnishment while that request is pending.
   - Ask where to file, whether there is a fee, and how to serve or send copies to the other side.

5. **If the debt may be yours but the garnishment will cause hardship, ask about exemptions or objections.**
   - Use the exact wording from your notice or court website, because forms and deadlines vary by state.
   - Ask: “Where is the exemption or objection form, what is the deadline, and do I need to request a hearing?”
   - Ask whether you must send a copy to the creditor’s attorney, agency, or other listed party.

6. **Check quickly for wrong-person, wrong-debt, or identity-theft signs.**
   - Compare the full name, middle initial, date of birth if shown, address history, employer, and any account information.
   - If something does not match, tell the court or issuer in writing that you may be the wrong person and are requesting the underlying documents.
   - Do not explain more than necessary until you have the court or agency record.

7. **Get help quickly if time is short.**
   - Contact Legal Aid, a local legal clinic, a courthouse self-help center, or a consumer-law attorney and say: “I need help with wage garnishment, a default judgment, possible improper service, or mistaken identity.”
   - Have the notice, case number, payroll copy, and any proof of your address history ready.

8. **Make a record if a debt collector may be misleading you.**
   - Save voicemails, letters, emails, texts, envelopes, screenshots, and payment demands.
   - A CFPB complaint can help create a record about debt-collection conduct, but it usually does not automatically stop a court order or wage withholding.

9. **Know the basic federal job-protection point.**
   - Federal law limits wage garnishment amounts in many situations and generally protects an employee from being fired because wages are garnished for one debt.
   - If your employer hints that you may be fired only because of a single garnishment, write down what was said, save related messages, and get legal help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to settle, declare bankruptcy, or hire a private attorney before you verify the issuer, case number, judgment, and deadline.
- You do not need to argue the whole debt by phone. The immediate task is to get the record and file the correct objection, exemption claim, or request for relief on time.
- You do not need to share personal financial details with payroll beyond what they need to identify the order and give you copies.

## Important reassurance
A surprise wage-garnishment notice can happen because papers went to an old address, a default judgment was entered, the debt was misidentified, or an agency process started without you understanding the notice. That does not mean you are powerless. The stabilizing move is to verify the official record, find the exact deadline, and use the correct court or agency procedure.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Garnishment procedures, exemptions, deadlines, service rules, and forms vary by state, court, agency, and debt type, so later decisions may need help from a legal aid office, courthouse self-help center, or qualified attorney.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Use official court or agency contact information, and seek qualified legal help if you believe you were never served, the debt is not yours, the notice names the wrong person, or withholding is about to start.
