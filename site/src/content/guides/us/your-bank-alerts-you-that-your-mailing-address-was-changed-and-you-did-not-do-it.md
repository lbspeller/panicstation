# What to do if… your bank alerts you that your mailing address was changed and you did not do it (USA)

## Short answer
Assume an account takeover is in progress: call your bank via a trusted number to revert the address and restrict high-risk activity, then freeze your credit and contact the U.S. Postal Inspection Service to block mail diversion.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click "verify" links or call phone numbers provided in the alert itself; use the number on the back of your physical card or the bank’s official website.
- Don’t just change the address back and log out; you must speak to the fraud department to ensure no other "backdoor" changes (like email or phone) were made.
- Don’t assume the bank caught everything; a changed address is often a precursor to a thief requesting a new debit or credit card to be mailed to themselves.
- Don’t reuse your banking password on any other site, especially your email account.
- Don’t ignore your physical mailbox; if mail stops arriving, a "Hold Mail" or "Change of Address" order may be active with the USPS.

## What to do now
1. **Call your bank’s fraud or security department using the number on your card.**  
   **Ask the representative to:**
   - **Revert the mailing address** to your correct physical location.
   - **Check for other profile changes**, specifically the "primary" phone number and recovery email address.
   - **Place a temporary hold** on new card reissues, wire transfers, Zelle payments, and check orders.
   - Review the "paperless" settings to see if the thief disabled mail alerts to hide their activity.
2. **Secure the email account linked to your bank.**  
   **Log in to your email, change the password to something unique, and enable app-based multi-factor authentication (MFA).** This prevents the thief from using "Forgot Password" links to regain access to your bank after you kick them out.
3. **Change your bank login credentials and check for "Authorized Users."**  
   **Update your bank password and review the list of people authorized to use the account or cards.** Sign out of all active sessions or "trusted devices" within the bank’s security settings.
4. **Place a security freeze on your credit reports with Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.**  
   **Execute a freeze at each of the three bureaus to prevent the thief from opening new lines of credit using your identity.** This is the most effective step to stop secondary identity theft.
5. **Place a ChexSystems security freeze.**  
   **Go to the ChexSystems website to freeze your consumer sub-profile.** Most banks use this system to screen new checking and savings account applications; a freeze stops a thief from opening new bank accounts in your name.
6. **Check your USPS status for fraudulent mail diversion.**  
   **Log in to your USPS Informed Delivery dashboard or visit your local post office to verify if a "Change of Address" or "Hold Mail" order has been filed.**
   - If you find a fraudulent order, report it to the **U.S. Postal Inspection Service (USPIS) at 1-877-876-2455** or via their online reporting portal.
7. **File an official report at IdentityTheft.gov.**  
   **Create a Federal Trade Commission (FTC) Identity Theft Report to generate a recovery plan and official documentation.** This document is often required by banks to permanently remove fraudulent activity or close unauthorized accounts.
8. **Contact the Identity Theft Resource Center (ITRC) for guidance.**  
   **Call or text 888-400-5530 to speak with a specialist who can provide a free, customized recovery plan.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to close the account permanently until the bank completes its internal investigation.
- You do not need to file a local police report immediately unless your bank or the FTC specifically requires one for a specific dispute.
- You do not need to figure out exactly how your data was stolen (e.g., which data breach) before taking protective action.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an alert means the bank's security systems are functioning. By acting within the first few minutes or hours to revert the address and freeze your credit, you significantly limit the thief's ability to intercept physical cards or open new accounts.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and harm prevention for an unauthorized financial address change. It does not cover long-term credit repair, legal action against banks, or forensic computer cleaning.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Processes for specific financial institutions may vary based on their internal security protocols and state regulations.
