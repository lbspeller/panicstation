# What to do if… your bank alerts you that your mailing address was changed and you did not do it (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as possible account takeover or identity theft. Contact your bank through a trusted route, ask them to restore your correct mailing address and restrict risky account changes, then protect your credit and mail to make it harder for the problem to spread.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links or call phone numbers from the alert unless you are sure it is genuine.
- Don’t only change the address back and move on.
- Don’t give a caller a one-time code, password, PIN, card number, or Social Security number.
- Don’t reuse a password that has been used for email, banking, shopping, or social accounts.
- Don’t ignore missing mail, unexpected paperless settings, or a replacement card you did not request.
- Don’t wait for another alert before asking the bank to review the account.

## What to do now
1. **Contact your bank through a trusted route.**  
   Use the number on the back of your card, the number printed on a statement, or the bank’s official app or website that you type in yourself. Tell them you did not change your mailing address.

2. **Ask the bank to secure the account before discussing details.**  
   Ask them to:
   - restore the correct mailing address;
   - check whether phone, email, username, password, paperless delivery, card delivery, or security questions were changed;
   - add stronger verification for future profile changes;
   - review recent logins, new devices, failed login attempts, new payees, Zelle, ACH transfers, wires, card reissues, and check orders;
   - block or pause high-risk activity where the bank can do that while they investigate.

3. **Secure the email account that can reset your bank login.**  
   Change your email password, turn on multifactor authentication, and review recovery phone numbers, recovery email addresses, forwarding rules, and signed-in devices.

4. **Secure your bank login.**  
   Change the bank password or passcode using a trusted app or website. Turn on login, transfer, payee, card, and profile-change alerts. Remove unfamiliar devices or ask the bank to sign out other sessions if you cannot do it yourself.

5. **Make an identity theft report if personal information may have been used.**  
   Use the federal identity theft recovery site to create a report and recovery plan. Save the report number or confirmation.

6. **Protect your credit.**  
   Consider placing a credit freeze with Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. A fraud alert can also be used; for many fraud alerts, contacting one of the three credit bureaus starts the alert process with the others. Review your credit reports for unfamiliar addresses, inquiries, or accounts.

7. **Consider a ChexSystems security freeze.**  
   This may make it harder for someone to open some new bank accounts in your name.

8. **Check for mail diversion.**  
   Look for mail you expected but did not receive, USPS confirmations you did not request, unexpected hold-mail notices, or changes to Informed Delivery. If you suspect mail theft or mail-related identity theft, use the U.S. Postal Inspection Service reporting route.

9. **Save a simple record.**  
   Keep screenshots of the bank alert, the date and time you contacted the bank, who you spoke with, confirmation numbers, and any unfamiliar calls, texts, login prompts, or mail changes.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to close every account, switch banks, buy monitoring, or work out exactly how the address was changed. First secure bank access, email access, credit, and mail; disputes and longer follow-up can come after the immediate risks are contained.

## Important reassurance
An unauthorized mailing address change is serious, but it is also a known fraud pattern that banks and identity theft services deal with. Acting quickly through trusted routes is a normal and useful response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, such as disputes, replacing accounts, police reports, extended fraud alerts, or specialist help, may depend on what the bank and your reports show.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Bank procedures and credit reporting processes can differ, so use official routes and ask each organization what they can do on your account.
