# What to do if… you are asked to sign a document by an airline, hotel, or official and you do not understand it (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign any document that you cannot read or explain in your own words. Request a written translation or a professional interpreter, secure a copy of the unsigned document, and contact Overseas Citizen Services if you are being detained or pressured by officials.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a document "just to move things along" if you cannot identify the legal obligations it creates.
- Do not sign blank pages, incomplete forms, or documents where sections have been obscured or hidden from view.
- Do not accept verbal summaries as a substitute for a readable text; verbal promises rarely override written terms in foreign jurisdictions.
- Do not assume that U.S. consular officers can act as your personal interpreter or provide legal advice on the specific meaning of a foreign contract.
- Do not escalate the situation with physical gestures or aggressive language; maintain a calm, repetitive request for translation.

## What to do now
1. **Clearly state that you cannot sign a document you do not understand.** Use a calm voice and repeat this phrase as your primary boundary if you are being pressured to hurry.
2. **Request an English-language version of the document immediately.** 
   - Ask if a translated version is available on the organization’s website or via their corporate office.
   - Ask for a staff member who is fluent in English to provide a line-by-line explanation, but do not sign based solely on this verbal explanation.
3. **Capture a high-quality photograph or scan of every page of the document before any marks are made.** Ensure the edges of the pages, any official stamps, and the document title are clearly visible. If they refuse to let you photograph the document, treat this as a high-risk indicator and do not sign.
4. **Use a mobile translation tool to perform a preliminary check of the text.** 
   - Scan for keywords such as "liability," "waiver," "penalty," "admit," "fault," "consent," or specific currency amounts.
   - Look for clauses regarding "jurisdiction" or "venue," which dictate where a legal dispute would be heard.
5. **Request a supervisor or a duty manager if the request comes from an airline or hotel staff member.** Explain that you are willing to cooperate once the terms are clear, and ask for the specific company policy that requires this signature.
6. **Ask for an official interpreter if you are dealing with police, customs, or immigration officials.**
   - State: "I need an interpreter to understand this official document."
   - If you feel you are not free to leave, ask the authorities to notify the nearest U.S. Embassy or Consulate of your situation.
7. **Contact the U.S. Department of State's Overseas Citizen Services (OCS) for guidance if you are being detained.** 
   - Call +1 202-501-4444 from abroad or 1-888-407-4747 from the U.S./Canada.
   - Request a list of local English-speaking attorneys who can review the document for you.
8. **Document the circumstances of the request in a separate notebook or digital file.** Record the time, the names or badge numbers of the people involved, the location, and exactly what they told you would happen if you did not sign.
9. **Refuse any immediate notarization if you do not understand the document's purpose.** Consular notaries and local notaries only verify that you signed the document and that your identity is confirmed; they do not verify that the document is "safe" or "fair" for you to sign.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the document is "legal" or "valid" under local law right now.
- You do not need to decide if you want to file a formal complaint or lawsuit against the entity requesting the signature.
- You do not need to argue about the fairness of the terms; you only need to focus on the fact that you do not understand them.

## Important reassurance
It is standard and expected for travelers to hesitate when presented with complex paperwork in an unfamiliar language. Taking time to seek clarity is a protective action, and the pressure you feel from others is often a tactic to bypass your normal caution rather than a reflection of an actual emergency.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for document-related pressure while traveling. It does not replace the need for professional legal counsel or formal translation services in cases involving arrests, significant financial transactions, or complex litigation.

## Important note
This information is for immediate harm-reduction only and does not constitute legal advice. Requirements for signatures vary by country and institution. U.S. Consular officers cannot provide legal representation or force a foreign entity to waive its signature requirements.
