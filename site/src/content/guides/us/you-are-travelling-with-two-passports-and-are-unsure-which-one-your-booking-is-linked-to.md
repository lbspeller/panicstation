# What to do if… you are travelling with two passports and are unsure which one your booking is linked to (USA)

## Short answer
Check the name on the booking, keep both passports with you, and ask the airline which passport details it needs for each relevant part of the itinerary. If you are a U.S. citizen, use your U.S. passport to enter and leave the United States.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess a passport number or expiry date to make an online form accept your entry.
- Do not assume that the passport details already stored by the airline are correct or unchangeable.
- Do not hide either passport from airline staff when you are unsure which document is relevant.
- Do not leave a known name or passport-detail mismatch until boarding if you can ask the airline about it earlier.
- Do not share photos of your passports, boarding passes, or booking reference online.

## What to do now
1. Check the passenger name on the confirmation or e-ticket against both passports. If there is a meaningful difference, contact the airline and ask whether the booking needs to be corrected.

2. Put both passports in your hand luggage. When you speak to airline staff, say that you have two passports and ask them to check which passport details should be recorded for each relevant part of the itinerary.

3. If you are a U.S. citizen, including a dual national, use your U.S. passport to enter and leave the United States.

4. For travel to another country where you hold nationality, check whether that country requires you to enter or leave using its passport. If a visa or other travel permission is involved, check which passport it is connected to.

5. If you are not a U.S. citizen and you are travelling to the United States under the Visa Waiver Program, check that your approved ESTA is for the passport you plan to use for that travel.

6. Look briefly in the airline app or website for passport or travel-document details. For international travel involving the United States, airlines may need to send passenger information to U.S. Customs and Border Protection. If the details are unclear, wrong, or not editable, contact the airline or use a staffed airline check-in desk. Ask staff to review both passports and tell you what can be confirmed or corrected before travel.

7. If the ticket was issued by a travel agent or another booking provider, ask the airline whether that provider also needs to help with any change.

8. At the airport, show the passport requested for the relevant check. Keep the other passport available and explain that you are a dual national if staff ask for it or if the route involves both nationalities.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide which passport you will use for every future trip.
- You do not need to update every airline profile today.
- You do not need to solve unrelated account details unless they affect this booking.
- You do not need to work out the airline’s internal system yourself. Staff can check the itinerary and documents.

## Important reassurance
Travelling with two passports is not unusual. The useful next step is to keep both documents available and ask the airline to review the booking before travel rather than guessing or making rushed changes.

## Scope note
These are first steps for reducing uncertainty around check-in and boarding. Later decisions may need help from the airline, the relevant embassy or consulate, or an immigration specialist, especially where a visa, ESTA, transit rule, or document-name difference is involved.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, or professional advice. Entry, exit, transit, visa, and airline requirements can depend on your nationality, route, destination, and documents.
