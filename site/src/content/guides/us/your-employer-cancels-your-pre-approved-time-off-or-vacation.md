# What to do if… your employer cancels your pre-approved time off or vacation (USA)

## Short answer
Ask for the cancellation in writing, save the original approval, and check the written PTO policy, any employment agreement, and any union agreement. If the time off may relate to a medical, qualifying family, disability, or uniformed-service reason, contact HR or your employer’s designated leave contact promptly and ask for the appropriate process.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the original approval still applies after you have been told it was canceled.
- Do not take the time off without clarifying your status; the absence may be treated as an attendance issue.
- Do not quit, threaten to quit, or send messages written in anger.
- Do not rely only on a verbal conversation.
- Do not invent a protected reason or say you are sick if that is not true.
- Do not post about the dispute on social media or in public work channels.

## What to do now
1. **Save the approval and the cancellation.** Keep the approval email, app record, calendar entry, or screenshot. Save the cancellation message. Note the dates, who contacted you, and the reason given, such as business needs, coverage, or a blackout period.
2. **Ask for a written confirmation.** Send a short message: “My time off for [dates] was approved on [date]. I understand that it is now being canceled because of business needs. Please confirm the decision in writing and tell me which policy applies.” If the cancellation was only verbal, send your own written recap.
3. **Check the written rules.** Review the PTO policy, employee handbook, any employment agreement, and any union agreement. Look for rules about revoking approval, blackout periods, notice, exceptions, and reimbursement. State or local rules may also matter.
4. **Use the appropriate route if the reason may be protected.** Tell HR or the designated leave contact promptly if the time off may relate to your own serious health condition, care for an eligible family member with a serious health condition, bonding with a new child, certain military-family needs, or a disability-related need for leave. Ask for the appropriate leave or accommodation process and the next steps. You do not need to provide unnecessary medical detail in an initial message, but respond to reasonable requests for information or documentation. If the time off is for your own uniformed service, tell your employer promptly and ask how to record the absence; separate rules may apply.
5. **Ask what options are available.** Ask whether the original dates can be kept, whether an exception is available, or whether another arrangement would work. You may offer one workable alternative, such as changing part of the dates or covering one specific task, but you do not need to solve staffing.
6. **Raise booked costs briefly.** If you relied on written approval and have non-refundable costs, ask whether the employer has an exception or reimbursement process. Keep receipts available.
7. **Keep a private timeline if something feels connected to a protected reason or protected activity.** Record dates, names, messages, and what was said. Keep your communications factual and professional. You can decide later whether to seek help from a union representative, a state labor office, a government agency, or an employment lawyer.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to contact a government agency or a lawyer.
- You do not need to write a long argument.
- You do not need to solve the employer’s staffing problem.
- You can check booking cancellation deadlines now, but you do not need to cancel bookings before asking what options are available.

## Important reassurance
Being told that approved time off has been canceled can feel destabilizing, especially when plans or costs are involved. A short written record and a prompt check of the applicable policy can make the next step clearer.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and reduce avoidable mistakes. Later decisions may depend on your employer’s written rules, your state or locality, any union agreement, and whether a protected leave or accommodation process applies.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Eligibility for protected leave or an accommodation depends on the facts. For tailored advice, consider contacting your union representative, a state labor office, an appropriate government agency, or an employment lawyer.
