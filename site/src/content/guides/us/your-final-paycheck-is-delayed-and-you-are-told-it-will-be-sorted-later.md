# What to do if… your final paycheck is delayed and you are told it will be sorted later (USA)

## Short answer
Ask payroll or HR today for the exact pay date, payment method, and itemized breakdown in writing. If the regular payday for your last pay period has passed, contact your state labor office or the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division to ask which unpaid-wage complaint route applies.

## Do not do these things
- Do not rely on “we’ll fix it later” without a specific date and written confirmation.
- Do not sign a release, “paid in full” form, or severance paperwork you do not understand just to get your paycheck.
- Do not accept off-the-books cash without a pay stub or written earnings statement.
- Do not threaten, exaggerate, or post publicly; keep the record factual and document-based.
- Do not let an overdue final check stay informal; ask the state labor office or WHD what route applies.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether the regular payday has passed.**  
   Federal law does not require an employer to give a final paycheck immediately. Some states require faster payment. If the regular payday for your last pay period has passed and you have not been paid, treat it as overdue and move to written follow-up.

2. **Check the rule for your state and separation type.**  
   Final-pay timing can depend on state law and on whether you quit, were fired, or were laid off. Use your state labor office’s final paycheck or unpaid wages page, or call the state labor office if the page is unclear.

3. **Send one clear written request to payroll or HR.** Include:
   - Your name, employee ID if any, last day worked, and the pay period dates
   - “My final paycheck has not been paid” or “My final paycheck is short”
   - A request for the pay date, payment method, and itemized breakdown
   - The wages you believe are missing, such as regular hours, overtime, commissions, bonus pay, reimbursements, or approved expenses
   - A question about whether accrued PTO or vacation is being paid out, because state law and company policy vary

4. **Remove easy payment excuses.**  
   Confirm your current mailing address, direct deposit status, and whether the employer claims a paper check was issued. If direct deposit is no longer reliable, ask for a paper check by pickup or mailing and ask them to confirm the date it was sent.

5. **Save the proof you can gather quickly.** Save:
   - Offer letter, contract, and handbook sections about pay, commissions, bonuses, PTO, or vacation payout
   - Time records, schedules, punch reports, timesheets, and supervisor approvals
   - Recent pay stubs and direct deposit records
   - Bank history showing the missing deposit, with unrelated transactions hidden if you prefer
   - Separation paperwork, resignation email, termination letter, or exit checklist
   - Any payroll or HR messages saying payment will be “sorted later”

6. **If it is overdue and the employer still gives no firm answer, contact an official wage office.**  
   Start with your state labor office to ask about the state final-pay rule, any deadline, and whether it accepts wage claims for your type of missing pay. If the issue involves minimum wage, overtime, or federal wage-and-hour coverage, contact the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division and ask about filing a complaint.

7. **If essentials are at risk, buy time without giving up your pay claim.**  
   Tell your landlord, utility, lender, or other essential creditor that your final paycheck is delayed and you are pursuing it. Ask for a short extension, payment arrangement, or fee waiver while the pay issue is being corrected.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to hire a lawyer.
- You do not need to calculate the exact penny before you start gathering information and asking the wage office what it needs.
- You do not need to argue about why payroll delayed it; focus on when it will be paid, how it will be paid, and what is included.
- You do not need to decide today whether to take a settlement, severance, or release.

## Important reassurance
You are not overreacting by asking for a written pay date and an itemized breakdown. A calm written request, followed by the correct wage office if needed, gives you a record and stops the delay from staying vague.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stop an open-ended final-pay delay and preserve your options. Later decisions may need help from a state labor office, the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division, a worker support organization, or an employment lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Final paycheck rules vary by state and by the type of pay owed, including wages, overtime, commissions, bonuses, reimbursements, PTO, or vacation pay.
