# What to do if… you have sudden severe pain in your upper abdomen with nausea that will not settle (USA)

## Short answer
Treat sudden, severe upper abdominal pain with persistent nausea as a medical emergency and go to an Emergency Room (ER) or call **911** immediately. This combination of symptoms can indicate a serious condition like acute pancreatitis, a gallbladder obstruction, or a heart attack.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if the pain is severe, makes you feel faint, or is accompanied by vomiting.
- Do not take aspirin, ibuprofen, or other pain medications until a doctor has evaluated you, as these can worsen certain causes like ulcers.
- Do not eat or drink anything, including antacids or "stomach-settling" liquids, as you may require surgery or urgent imaging.
- Do not wait for a primary care appointment or "see how you feel" in the morning if the pain is unrelenting or worsening.
- Do not apply a heating pad directly to the skin, which can mask worsening symptoms or cause burns.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 immediately if you have any "red flag" symptoms that indicate a life-threatening crisis.**  
   These symptoms include:
   - Pain or pressure in the chest, neck, or jaw lasting 2 minutes or more
   - Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
   - Fainting, sudden dizziness, or a rapid, racing heart rate
   - Vomit that contains blood or looks like coffee grounds
   - Stool that is bloody, black, or tarry
   - A rigid, hard, or severely bloated abdomen that is painful to touch
   - Sudden, sharp pain in (or between) your shoulder blades
   - Pain that began suddenly after a physical injury to the abdomen
2. **Go to the Emergency Room (ER) now if you are in high-risk categories even without chest pain.**  
   You must seek immediate emergency assessment if:
   - You are over age 45 and have new, severe upper abdominal pain
   - You are pregnant or could be pregnant
   - You have had recent abdominal surgery or a gastric bypass
   - You have a history of heart disease or diabetes and cannot stop vomiting
3. **Isolate yourself from further physical strain while waiting for transport or help.**  
   **Sit upright or lean forward over your knees if this helps reduce the intensity of the pain.** Do not lie flat if it increases the pain or makes you feel more nauseated.
4. **Identify the exact location and nature of the pain to tell emergency responders.**  
   **Note if the pain is in the upper right, upper middle, or radiating through to your back.** Check if you have a fever or if the pain started specifically after a meal.
5. **Gather your essential medical information and medications into one place.**  
   **Bring a list of all current prescriptions, supplements, and known allergies to the ER.** If you have a history of gallstones or pancreatitis, tell the triage staff immediately.
6. **Alert a friend, family member, or neighbor that you are heading to the ER.**  
   **Ask them to meet you there or stay on the phone with you until help arrives.** If your condition worsens suddenly, they can provide critical information to responders.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the cause of the pain (such as food poisoning vs. gallstones) before seeking help.
- You do not need to pack a bag for a long hospital stay; focus only on immediate departure.
- You do not need to wait for a return call from your regular doctor’s office.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant anxiety or a sense of dread when experiencing sudden, severe internal pain. Emergency departments are equipped to quickly differentiate between non-emergency issues and serious conditions through vitals, blood tests, and imaging. Taking action now is the standard protocol for these symptoms.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency identification only. It does not replace a clinical diagnosis or suggest long-term treatment plans.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute medical advice. If you are experiencing a medical emergency, call **911** or go to the nearest emergency room immediately.
