# What to do if… you receive an urgent notice demanding identity documents within 48 hours (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before sending identity documents. Verify the request through contact details you find independently, not through the notice, then use only a verified secure submission method if the request is real.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, open “secure upload” buttons, download attachments, or scan QR codes from an unexpected notice before verification.
- Don’t email photos or scans of your driver’s license, passport, Social Security card, or proof of address to a new or unverified address.
- Don’t pay “processing fees,” “expedite fees,” “verification fees,” or “failure-to-comply penalties” because of the deadline.
- Don’t trust caller ID, sender names, logos, reference numbers, or official-looking wording on their own.
- Don’t stay on the line with a caller who pressures you to act immediately; end the call and use a trusted callback route.
- Don’t send extra identity information “to be safe.”

## What to do now
1. **Preserve the notice as received.** Save the email, text, letter, attachments, envelope, screenshots, sender address, phone number, and the date and time you received it.
2. **Identify who is supposedly asking.** Note whether it claims to be from a federal agency, state agency, court, police department, employer, bank, landlord, platform, payment provider, or private company compliance team.
3. **Check the contact route without using the notice.**
   - For a government agency, type the official agency website address yourself or search for the official .gov page, then use the published contact or case process.
   - For a bank, card issuer, employer, platform, or service provider, use the phone number in your app, on your card, on a prior official statement, or in a portal you already know is real.
   - For a court or local office, use the official court or government website for that location, not the contact details in the message.
4. **Ask the verified contact to confirm the request.** Use calm, specific wording: “I received a notice asking for identity documents within 48 hours. Can you confirm whether this request is real, what file or account it relates to, and how I should respond?”
5. **Ask for the minimum required documents and the allowed method.** Confirm exactly which documents are needed, whether copies are required, whether front and back are needed, and whether submission must be through a portal, in person, by mail, or another verified route.
6. **If the request is real, submit only through the verified route.** Prefer a known account portal reached by typing the website yourself or using the official app. Do not use a link from the original notice unless the verified organization specifically confirms that exact route.
7. **Reduce document exposure.** Send only what the verified organization says is required. Do not add your full Social Security number, full account numbers, or extra documents unless the verified process specifically requires them. If you want to mark a copy with a purpose note, ask first and do not obscure required information.
8. **If the deadline is close, ask for a note on the file.** On the verified channel, say you are checking authenticity before sending identity documents and ask whether they can record that you contacted them before the stated deadline.
9. **If you already clicked, replied, paid, or sent documents, stop using that channel.** Contact the real organization through verified details, change passwords for any account involved, turn on multi-factor authentication where available, and watch for unauthorized activity.
10. **If you think it was a scam or identity theft risk, report it.** Use ReportFraud.ftc.gov for scams. Use IdentityTheft.gov if personal information or identity documents may have been misused.

## What can wait
- You don’t have to gather every possible identity document right now; first confirm that the request is real.
- You don’t have to argue with the sender or prove it is a scam.
- You don’t have to decide today whether to close accounts, move money, replace documents, or make a legal complaint unless a verified organization tells you there is an immediate account risk.
- You don’t have to answer every question in the notice before you know who is actually asking.

## Important reassurance
Urgent deadlines are often used to make people act before checking. Taking a few minutes to verify through a trusted route is a normal protective step, and legitimate organizations generally have a way to confirm whether a request is real.

## Scope note
These are first steps for verifying and safely responding to an urgent identity-document request. Later decisions may need help from the real organization, a qualified local adviser, a lawyer, or an identity theft recovery service, depending on what the notice turns out to be.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Requirements vary by agency, state, court, employer, bank, platform, and company, so use verified official contact routes and avoid sending identity documents through unverified links or email.
