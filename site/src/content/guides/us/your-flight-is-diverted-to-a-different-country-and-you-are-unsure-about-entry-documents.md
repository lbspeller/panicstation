# What to do if… your flight is diverted to a different country and you are unsure about entry documents (USA)

## Short answer
Do not leave the secure transit area until you know whether you are required, and allowed, to enter that country. Ask the airline to check the document rules for your passport and the diversion country, and contact U.S. consular help if you are stuck, missing documents, or being told to enter without clear permission.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go landside “to figure it out” if you are not sure you are allowed to enter that country.
- Do not hand over your passport to be kept by anyone except uniformed border or immigration officers. Airline staff may need to inspect it.
- Do not sign paperwork you do not understand, especially anything that says you knowingly tried to enter without permission.
- Do not throw away boarding passes, bag tags, rebooking messages, hotel vouchers, or diversion paperwork.
- Do not assume a short stop means visas or entry rules do not matter. An overnight stay, baggage reclaim, or terminal closure may change what you are required to do.
- Do not photograph staff, security areas, immigration desks, or documents if signs or officials say not to.

## What to do now
1. **Find out whether you are staying airside or clearing immigration.**  
   Ask airline staff: “Are we remaining in international transit, or are we being required to clear immigration here?” If the answer is unclear, ask for a supervisor or the airline’s ground handling manager.

2. **Ask for a travel-document check before you move.**  
   Say: “Please check the entry or transit document rules for my passport nationality, this airport, and this diversion country. Do I need a visa, electronic authorization, or other document to enter or transit?” Ask what source they are using, such as the airline’s Timatic-based document system, and ask them to note the answer on your booking record if they can.

3. **If you may not qualify to enter, say that clearly and early.**  
   Say: “I do not think I have the required visa or authorization to enter. I need to remain in international transit if possible. Please coordinate this with border control before I go landside.”

4. **If officials require immigration processing, ask what permission you are being given.**  
   Calmly ask the border officer:
   - “Am I being admitted, allowed to transit, given temporary landing permission, or something else?”
   - “How is that recorded?”
   - “How long does it last, and where am I allowed to go?”
   
   Have ready your passport, your original itinerary, and proof of onward travel such as a boarding pass, rebooking email, or written note from the airline.

5. **Pause before accepting a hotel, leaving the terminal, or collecting checked bags.**  
   A hotel voucher, baggage reclaim, or escorted transfer may require you to clear immigration. Ask: “Will doing this require entry clearance, and can you confirm I am eligible before I leave the transit area?” If you are not eligible, ask for an airside option or an escorted arrangement that border control has approved.

6. **Create a simple paper trail.**  
   Keep screenshots or photos of your flight status, rebooking messages, airport name, time of arrival, and written airline instructions. Keep all paper slips. Where allowed, write down staff names, roles, desk locations, and times instead of photographing people or controlled areas.

7. **If your passport is lost, stolen, damaged, or held up, contact U.S. consular help.**  
   Contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate, or use the State Department’s published emergency numbers for calls from the U.S. and Canada or from overseas. Tell them where you are, your flight number, what the airline or officials are requiring, whether you are airside or landside, and what document problem you have.

8. **If the diversion country is the United States and you are not a U.S. citizen, treat it as a real U.S. entry or transit issue.**  
   You may need a U.S. transit visa, visa waiver authorization, or another valid basis to transit or enter, depending on your nationality and situation. Ask the airline to check your documents before you clear immigration, and ask U.S. border officials what process applies if the diversion was outside your control.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle reimbursement, complaints, compensation, or blame right now.
- You do not need to choose the perfect rebooking immediately if your document status is unclear.
- You do not need to decide whether to leave the airport until you know whether you have permission to enter.
- Focus only on three things for now: whether you are airside or landside, what permission you have if any, and how you will continue travel without creating a document problem.

## Important reassurance
A diversion can put you in a country you never planned to enter. Asking staff and officials to confirm your status before you move is normal and protective, not difficult or suspicious.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours after a flight diversion. The exact documents and permissions depend on the diversion country, your citizenship, your passport, your visa or authorization status, and whether you must clear border control. Later decisions may need help from the airline, immigration officials, a consular officer, or a qualified immigration professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, travel, immigration, or professional advice. Entry and transit rules can change and may be applied differently during disruptions. Follow instructions from official border or immigration officers, and use U.S. consular support if you are stuck, missing documents, or unsure what is being required.
