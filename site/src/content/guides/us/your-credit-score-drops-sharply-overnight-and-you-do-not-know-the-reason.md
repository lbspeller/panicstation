# What to do if… your credit score drops sharply overnight and you do not know the reason (USA)

## Short answer
Do not apply for new credit yet. Get your credit reports from all three credit bureaus, look for the specific item or change, and if anything looks suspicious, place a credit freeze or fraud alert right away.

## Do not do these things
- Do not submit new credit applications to “test” what happens; more hard inquiries can lower scores further.
- Do not pay a debt you do not recognize just to make it go away until you confirm it is yours.
- Do not give sensitive personal information or upfront payment to any company that pressures you to act immediately.
- Do not close multiple accounts at once in a rush; it can create new score changes and confusion.
- Do not rely on the score number alone; identify what changed on the credit report.

## What to do now
1. Stop and capture what you are seeing. Note the date, time, score service, score model if shown, and the size of the drop. Screenshot any “what changed” alerts.
2. Get your reports from all three credit bureaus, not just one score. Use AnnualCreditReport.com, the federally authorized site for free credit reports, and compare Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion side by side.
3. Scan for the most common sudden-drop triggers and write down the details:
   - a new late payment or collection
   - a new account you do not recognize
   - a new hard inquiry
   - a large balance increase or credit limit decrease
   - unfamiliar personal information, such as a name variation or address
4. If anything looks suspicious, put a barrier in place immediately:
   - Credit freeze: contact each of the three credit bureaus. A freeze can help stop new credit being opened in your name, but you may need to temporarily lift it before legitimate new credit checks.
   - Fraud alert: contact any one of the three credit bureaus to place an initial fraud alert. That bureau must tell the other two.
   Choose the option you can complete fastest right now. A freeze is generally the stronger immediate block against new accounts.
5. Contact the company tied to the suspicious entry. Use contact information from an official statement, your existing account portal, your credit report, or the company’s official website. Ask for the fraud department, say you do not recognize the account, inquiry, or reporting entry, and ask what they need to investigate.
6. Dispute incorrect items with each credit bureau that shows them. Dispute the exact line item, identify the account or inquiry if one is listed, include the report confirmation number if available, explain clearly why it is wrong, and attach copies of proof. Keep copies of everything you send.
7. If the company that reported the information is known, also dispute with that company. Ask where to send a written credit reporting dispute and keep a copy of your message or letter.
8. If identity theft is likely, create an official recovery record at IdentityTheft.gov. It can help you make an FTC Identity Theft Report and recovery plan for dealing with credit bureaus and companies.

## What can wait
- You do not need to rebuild your credit today; first identify the entry or change that may have caused the drop.
- You do not need to close all cards or change banks unless you confirm a specific account compromise.
- You do not need to make big borrowing decisions, such as a mortgage, auto loan, or consolidation loan, until you have checked all three reports and taken protective steps.
- You do not need to pay for credit repair before you know whether the drop came from an error, fraud, or accurate reporting.

## Important reassurance
A sharp overnight drop may come from one or more report changes, such as a late payment, collection, hard inquiry, balance change, or newly reported account. Finding the exact entry or change is the fastest way to regain control and avoid making the situation worse.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the initial shock and the first protective actions. If you find fraud, repeated inaccuracies, or you have an urgent housing or loan deadline, later decisions may need help from the credit bureaus, the creditor involved, a consumer regulator, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Credit reporting rules, score effects, and response times can vary by bureau, company, and situation. If you feel rushed or pressured, slow down, keep copies, and use official contact routes.
