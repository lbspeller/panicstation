# What to do if… you faint briefly and wake up feeling confused about what happened (USA)

## Short answer
Lie flat on your back and remain still for several minutes to prevent another fall. Call **911** immediately if you do not wake up within 60 seconds, have chest pain, are struggling to breathe, have a severe headache, or remain confused for more than a few minutes.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stand up or walk until the feeling of "faintness" has completely passed.
- Do not drive or operate machinery until you have been medically cleared.
- Do not put anything in your mouth (or the mouth of someone else) if you suspect a seizure.
- Do not consume food, water, alcohol, or medications until you are fully alert and steady.
- Do not ignore the event even if you feel "fine" now; a first-time or unexplained faint requires a medical check.

## What to do now
1. **Stay on the ground and lie flat.** If you are already on the floor, do not try to get up. Lie on your back with your head flat.
   - If you feel like you might faint again, stay lying down. 
   - You may prop your feet up about 12 inches if you are certain there are no injuries.
2. **Check for immediate danger signs.** **Assess yourself for any of the following symptoms:**
   - Severe head, neck, or back pain.
   - Difficulty speaking or "thick" tongue.
   - Weakness or numbness on one side of the body.
   - Significant bleeding from a fall.
3. **Call 911 immediately if emergency triggers are present.** **Activate emergency services if:**
   - The loss of consciousness lasted more than **1 minute**.
   - Confusion or disorientation lasts longer than **2 to 5 minutes** after waking.
   - You experience **chest pain**, palpitations, or extreme shortness of breath.
   - The faint occurred during **physical exercise** or while you were already **lying down**.
   - You have a history of heart disease or are pregnant.
   - There was a seizure-like event (shaking, jerking, or tongue biting).
4. **Place yourself or the person in the recovery position if not fully alert.** **If you are assisting someone who is breathing but still groggy, roll them onto their side.**
   - This keeps the airway clear in case of vomiting.
   - Do not move the person if a neck or spinal injury is suspected.
5. **Request a witness account of the event.** **If anyone saw you fall, ask them to describe specific details:**
   - Did you turn pale, blue, or red?
   - Did you go limp or did your muscles get stiff/jerky?
   - How long exactly were you "out"?
   - Did you make any unusual sounds or have foam at the mouth?
6. **Log the circumstances for a clinician.** **Write down the following as soon as you are steady:**
   - The time of day and your last meal/hydration.
   - Exactly what you were doing (standing up, coughing, seeing blood, etc.).
   - Warning signs (tunnel vision, "aura" or strange smell, nausea, sweating).
7. **Contact a healthcare provider today.** **Even if you feel normal, you must be evaluated if this is your first faint or if the cause is unknown.**
   - Call your primary care physician for a same-day appointment.
   - Go to an Urgent Care or Emergency Department if your doctor is unavailable.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the medical cause (syncope vs. seizure) yourself.
- You do not need to resume your normal schedule, work, or chores today.
- You do not need to decide if you are "overreacting"; medical evaluation is the standard protocol for unexplained loss of consciousness.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel frightened, shaky, or briefly disoriented after losing consciousness. These physical sensations are common as your body restores blood flow and your brain re-orients to its surroundings. Taking the time to lie still is the most effective way to prevent a secondary injury from a second fall.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency triggers for a transient loss of consciousness. It is not a substitute for professional diagnostic testing like an EKG or EEG.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute medical advice. Any sudden loss of consciousness should be treated as a potential emergency until a qualified healthcare professional determines otherwise.
