# What to do if… your charging cable won’t release from the car or the charger and you need to leave (USA)

## Short answer
Terminate the charging session via the app or station screen, unlock the vehicle, and perform a "push-in then pull-out" maneuver to relieve latch tension. If software methods fail, locate the physical manual release cable inside the trunk or under the hood as specified in the vehicle owner's manual.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use tools, pry bars, or excessive force to yank the connector, as this can damage the high-voltage pins.
- Do not drive or attempt to move the vehicle while the cable is still physically attached to the port.
- Do not pull the emergency release cable while the vehicle is actively charging or if you see smoke or sparks.
- Do not attempt to dismantle the charging station or open any electrical panels.
- Do not leave the connector lying on the ground in a high-traffic area if it does eventually release.

## What to do now
1. **Ensure the vehicle is in Park and the parking brake is engaged.** This ensures the vehicle's onboard computer can safely process the command to release the electronic port lock.
2. **Verify the charging session has fully ended on the station screen or app.**
   - Press "Stop" or "End Session" on the charger’s physical display.
   - Check the network app (e.g., Electrify America, ChargePoint, EVgo) to confirm the session status is "Summary" or "Finished."
3. **Unlock all vehicle doors using the key fob.** Many electric vehicles (EVs) automatically lock the charging connector whenever the doors are locked to prevent theft or accidental disconnection.
4. **Relieve mechanical tension on the latch by pushing the connector inward.**
   - Push the handle firmly into the car’s port.
   - While holding it pushed in, press the release button or trigger on the handle.
   - Pull the connector straight out in one smooth motion without twisting.
5. **Execute a "Tesla Handle Release" if operating a compatible Tesla vehicle.**
   - Ensure the vehicle is unlocked.
   - Pull and hold the rear-left door handle (the one closest to the port) for exactly three seconds to trigger a hardware-level port unlock.
6. **Perform a ChargePoint "Unlock Cycle" if using a J1772 or CCS connector.**
   - Press the unlock button on your vehicle key fob five times in rapid succession.
   - If it remains locked, toggle the lock and unlock buttons three times each.
7. **Call the charging network’s 24/7 support line for a remote unlock command.**
   - Electrify America: 1-833-632-2778
   - ChargePoint: 1-888-758-4389
   - EVgo: 1-877-494-3833
8. **Locate and pull the manual emergency release cable only if charging has stopped.**
   - Consult the owner's manual for the exact location (typically behind a trim panel in the trunk or a pull-ring under the hood).
   - Pull the ring or cable once to mechanically override the electronic pawl.
   - **Warning:** Do not touch the connector pins or the port interior during this process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the fault lies with the car's software or the charger's hardware immediately.
- You do not need to file a formal damage report until you are in a safe, non-congested location.
- You do not need to wait for a technician to arrive if the manual release successfully disconnects the vehicle.

## Important reassurance
A stuck charging cable is a common occurrence usually caused by a simple communication "handshake" error between the vehicle and the station. In the vast majority of cases, the physical manual release or a remote command from the network provider will resolve the issue without requiring a tow or causing permanent damage to the vehicle.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a physically stuck charging connector in the USA. It does not cover repair of damaged charging ports or long-term electrical troubleshooting for home charging stations.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and does not replace professional mechanical or electrical services. High-voltage charging systems pose a risk of electric shock if components are damaged. If you observe sparks, smell burning plastic, or see exposed orange high-voltage wiring, move at least 20 feet away and contact emergency services.
