# What to do if… you receive an official notice to appear for an alleged violation and are unsure what to do (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the notice and appearance date through an official court or agency source, then preserve records before anything disappears. Do not explain the allegation, contact the other side, or send evidence around until you have legal advice or an official self-help route.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice or miss the appearance date; in many places, missing an appearance may lead to a warrant, fines, default, or additional penalties.
- Do not rely only on the phone number or link printed on the notice if you are unsure it is real; use an official court or agency website or directory first, then compare the details.
- Do not post about the case, argue with the other party, or send “your side” by text, email, or social media.
- Do not delete messages, photos, location data, account records, receipts, or device data to “clean things up.”
- Do not edit, crop, filter, rename, or overwrite original photos, videos, files, or screenshots.
- Do not answer questions about the allegation without legal advice, especially from police, investigators, code officers, agency staff, or the other side.
- Do not contact or pressure witnesses before getting legal advice.

## What to do now
1. **Identify what the document says it is.** Look for words such as “summons,” “notice to appear,” “subpoena,” “citation,” “arraignment,” “initial appearance,” “hearing,” “complaint,” or “violation.” Write down the case number, court or agency name, address, date, time, and whether it says criminal, civil, traffic, municipal, code, housing, school, workplace, or administrative.
2. **Verify the notice through an official source.** Find the court or agency website yourself, using a government site, court directory, or trusted search result. Ask only basic confirmation questions: “Can you confirm this case number?”, “What is the appearance date and location?”, and “What type of matter is this?”
3. **Save the notice exactly as received.** Photograph or scan every page, envelope, email header, attachment, QR code, and delivery record. Keep the original paper notice in one place and do not write on it.
4. **Ask what help route fits the case type.**
   - **If it is criminal, or could involve jail or imprisonment:** ask the court how to request counsel. If you cannot afford a lawyer, ask about court-appointed counsel or the public defender process and what financial forms are required.
   - **If it is civil, traffic, municipal, code, housing, school, workplace, or administrative:** public defender help usually may not apply. Ask the court or agency about self-help, legal aid, lawyer referral, pro bono, or clinic resources.
5. **If police or investigators want to talk, pause.** Miranda warnings generally relate to custodial questioning, but you do not need to guess whether they apply. You can say, “I do not want to answer questions about this without an attorney.”
6. **Create one evidence folder.** Put these in it without editing the originals:
   - the notice, attachments, envelope, and proof of delivery;
   - texts, DMs, emails, voicemail records, call logs, and full message threads;
   - photos and videos, with the original files kept unchanged;
   - receipts, bank or card records, invoices, delivery confirmations, parking records, tickets, or travel records;
   - work, school, housing, appointment, calendar, sign-in, or timecard records;
   - screenshots that show dates, usernames, URLs, and surrounding context.
7. **Make a one-page timeline.** Use short factual entries only: date, time, place, who was present, what happened, and where any record is stored. Mark anything uncertain as “not sure” rather than filling gaps from memory.
8. **Make a witness list, but do not contact witnesses yet.** List names, contact details if you already have them, how each person relates to the event, and what they may have seen or recorded.
9. **Write down questions for a lawyer, legal-aid worker, or official self-help appointment.** Ask what the allegation is, what rule or law is claimed to be violated, whether there are reports, recordings, body-camera footage, witness statements, photographs, or agency records, and how evidence requests usually work in this type of case.
10. **If you cannot attend on the listed date, act early through the official route.** Follow the court or agency instructions for asking about a continuance, postponement, remote appearance, interpreter, disability access, or address correction. Get any change in writing if possible, and do not assume a voicemail or unanswered email changes your duty to appear.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your full strategy, plea, defense, settlement position, or explanation today.
- You do not need to prove everything immediately.
- You do not need to contact witnesses, gather character letters, or write a long personal statement right now.
- You do not need to send evidence to the other side unless a lawyer or official court or agency instruction tells you how and when.
- You do not need to sign a statement, accept a deal, pay a disputed penalty, or admit anything before getting legal advice where possible.

## Important reassurance
Official notices can feel frightening because they use formal language and deadlines. The safest early moves are simple: verify the notice, protect the date, preserve records, avoid impulsive explanations, and get the right help route for the type of case.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce avoidable mistakes and buy time. Evidence rules, court procedures, deadlines, counsel appointment, discovery, subpoenas, and continuances vary by state, court, agency, and case type, so later decisions may need a lawyer, legal aid, court self-help staff, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Laws and procedures vary widely across the USA. If you may be arrested, have an imminent appearance date, or are being questioned by law enforcement, seek urgent legal help.
