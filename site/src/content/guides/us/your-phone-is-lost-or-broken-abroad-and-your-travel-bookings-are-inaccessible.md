# What to do if… your phone is lost or broken abroad and your travel bookings are inaccessible (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a staffed, safe place with Wi-Fi, recover your booking details from email or the airline or booking site, and go early to the airline counter with your passport. If the phone may be stolen, also lock the device or account access and contact your mobile carrier to suspend the line.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume you cannot board just because the boarding pass was on your phone; airline staff can often locate a reservation after checking your identity.
- Do not keep guessing passwords or two-factor codes until the account locks.
- Do not hand your email, airline account, Apple Account, Google Account, or bank login to a stranger.
- Do not use a public computer for banking unless there is no safer option.
- Do not remove a stolen iPhone from Find My just to “clean up” your account; that can remove Activation Lock.
- Do not follow texts, emails, or calls claiming your stolen phone was found unless you verify them through the official Apple, Google, carrier, airline, or bank website yourself.
- Do not go to an unsafe location to retrieve a phone shown on a map.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a stable, staffed place with Wi-Fi.** Use hotel reception, an airline service desk, an airport information desk, or a staffed business center rather than an unattended computer or a stranger’s phone.

2. **Write down what you already know.** On paper, note your full name as booked, flight date, route, airline, approximate departure time, booking site, payment card used, frequent flyer number, hotel name, and any confirmation or record-locator fragments you remember.

3. **Recover the booking from email or the booking site.**
   - From a safer computer or borrowed device, go directly to your email provider, airline site, hotel site, or booking site.
   - Search your email for the airline name, “confirmation,” “itinerary,” “e-ticket,” “receipt,” “record locator,” “booking,” and the city names.
   - If you booked through a third party, open its “Trips” or “Manage booking” area and write down the airline record locator if shown.

4. **If two-factor authentication blocks you, use recovery options instead of repeated attempts.**
   - Try backup codes, a recovery email, a trusted device, or the provider’s account-recovery flow.
   - Keep a simple written list of what you tried so you do not repeat failed attempts while stressed.
   - If you can reach a trusted person at home, ask them to read out a booking reference from your email or printed travel papers without sending passwords.

5. **Go to the airline counter early with your passport.**
   - Say: “My phone is lost or broken, and my boarding pass and booking are on it. Can you locate my reservation and print or re-issue my boarding pass?”
   - Give the staff your passport, route, travel date, destination, and any booking details you recovered.
   - Ask whether you need counter check-in, a kiosk printout, a gate reprint, or help from the airline’s ticketing desk.

6. **If an app or online check-in is causing the problem, ask airline staff for the manual path.** Processes vary by airline and airport, but staff may be able to check you in, verify documents, print a pass, re-send a confirmation, or tell you the fastest alternative.

7. **Lock or secure the missing phone.**
   - For an iPhone, use iCloud Find Devices or another trusted Apple device to mark it as lost if Find My is available.
   - For an Android phone, use Google Find Hub or another trusted Android device to find, secure, mark as lost, or erase the device if recovery is not realistic.
   - If the phone appears stolen, do not add personal contact details to the lock screen unless you are comfortable with that information being used in scam attempts.

8. **Contact your mobile carrier.** Ask the carrier to suspend the line, block the SIM or eSIM where available, discuss a replacement SIM or eSIM, and note any device identifier they ask for, such as an IMEI, if you can find it from your Apple or Google account records.

9. **Protect the accounts that control everything else.**
   - Start with your email account, because it controls password resets.
   - Then check airline, booking, bank, payment-card, Apple, Google, and messaging accounts.
   - Change passwords from a safer device when you can, and sign out of sessions you do not recognize.

10. **Use shared computers only with extra caution.** A private or incognito window may reduce saved-session risk, but it does not make a shared computer safe. Log out of every account, close the browser, and change important passwords later from a safer device.

11. **Secure cards and money access if the phone had wallet or banking apps.** Use the official bank or card-issuer website, the official app on a trusted replacement device, or numbers printed on physical cards if you have them. Freeze or replace cards if you see suspicious activity or if the phone was stolen while unlocked.

12. **If the phone was stolen, make a local report only if it is safe and practical.** A police report or reference number may help with insurance, carrier claims, device claims, or documentation, but do not miss a flight or put yourself at risk just to get it.

13. **If your U.S. passport is also lost or stolen, treat that as a separate urgent problem.** Contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate for replacement steps before trying to travel internationally on that passport.

## What can wait
You do not need to buy a new phone immediately if you can reach your booking details and the airline can print or re-issue travel documents. You do not need to rebuild every app, file every insurance claim, or perfect all security settings right now. First handle the flight, your mobile line, email access, booking access, and money access.

## Important reassurance
This feels worse than ordinary travel disruption because the phone held the passes, bookings, passwords, maps, and messages. It is usually still workable: airlines can often find reservations from your identity and trip details, and Apple, Google, carriers, banks, and booking sites have recovery routes even when the device is gone.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours: reach a safer pause, recover booking access, get airline help, secure the missing device, and reduce fraud risk. Later decisions about replacement devices, insurance, identity-theft recovery, passport replacement, or disputed charges may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, insurance, travel, or professional advice. Airline, airport, carrier, bank, Apple, Google, and consular processes vary by country and provider, so use official channels and ask staff for the current local procedure.
