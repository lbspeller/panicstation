# What to do if… your passport is damaged and you are worried it will be rejected for travel (USA)

## Short answer
Do not rely on hope at check-in: compare the damage with official U.S. passport guidance, contact your airline, and start the official replacement route now. If you travel internationally in less than 2–3 weeks, check urgent passport agency or center appointment options immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not tape, glue, heat, trim, re-laminate, or otherwise repair the passport yourself.
- Do not scrape off marks, remove pages, or try to make damage look less obvious.
- Do not wait until the airport to find out whether the passport will be accepted.
- Do not report the passport lost or stolen unless it is actually lost or stolen; a reported lost or stolen passport is no longer valid.
- Do not pay a third-party site before checking official U.S. passport options and what that company can and cannot do.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether the damage matches official “damaged passport” examples.**  
   U.S. passport guidance says examples of damage include water damage, mold, stains, significant tears, unofficial markings, or missing pages. Normal wear, such as folded pages or small bends, generally does not count as a damaged passport. If the photo page, personal details, pages, cover, or laminate are affected, treat it as a real travel-document problem.

2. **Keep the passport as it is and make a simple record.**  
   Put the passport somewhere dry and flat. Take clear photos of the photo page and the damaged area. Write a short signed and dated statement saying what happened, such as when it got wet, torn, stained, or damaged.

3. **Contact the airline or travel operator before going to the airport.**  
   Describe the exact damage: where it is, whether any details are hard to read, whether pages are missing, and whether the photo page or laminate is affected. Ask whether their document-check team is likely to refuse boarding, and write down the date, time, and what you were told.

4. **Start the damaged-passport replacement route.**  
   A damaged valid U.S. passport usually means applying in person using Form DS-11, submitting the damaged passport, and including a signed statement explaining the damage, along with the required identity, citizenship, photo, and fee materials. Use an official passport acceptance facility or passport agency route, depending on how soon you travel.

5. **If international travel is soon, check urgent appointment timing now.**  
   If you are traveling in less than 2–3 weeks, make an appointment at a passport agency or center when you are within 14 calendar days of your international travel date. If you need a foreign visa, check the 28-day appointment window. Appointment slots may not be available right away.

6. **If you are not in the urgent window, use the standard in-person process promptly.**  
   Find an official passport acceptance facility and prepare the damaged passport, DS-11, your signed statement, passport photo, ID, citizenship evidence if needed, photocopies, and fees. Acceptance facilities may include post offices, libraries, or local government offices.

7. **If you are outside the United States, contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate.**  
   Tell them your passport is damaged and may not be usable for travel. Follow their instructions, and have the damaged passport, travel details, identification, and any photos of the damage ready if you have them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to cancel the whole trip.
- You do not need to collect every online opinion about damaged passports.
- You do not need to write a long explanation of the damage; a short factual signed and dated statement is enough to start.
- You do not need to solve refunds, insurance, or itinerary changes before checking the document risk and urgent passport options.

## Important reassurance
A damaged passport is stressful because the problem may only become clear at check-in or border control. Acting through the official replacement or urgent-travel route gives you the best chance of avoiding a last-minute refusal.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about travel changes, refunds, visas, insurance, or disputes with a carrier may need help from the airline, the passport office, a consulate, an insurer, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, travel, financial, or professional advice. Final acceptance can depend on the airline, border officials, the destination, the route, and the condition of the passport.
