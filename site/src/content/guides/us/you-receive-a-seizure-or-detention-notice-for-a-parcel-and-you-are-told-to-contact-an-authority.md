# What to do if… you receive a seizure or detention notice for a parcel and you are told to contact an authority (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the notice's authenticity through official government channels before responding or providing payment. Genuine U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) seizures arrive via physical mail and include an "Election of Proceedings" form with specific legal deadlines.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links or call numbers provided in a text message or email alleging a "detained package"—legitimate seizure notices are sent via U.S. Mail.
- Do not pay any "customs fees," "fines," or "release charges" using gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfers, or third-party payment apps (Venmo, Zelle).
- Do not provide sensitive personal data (Social Security Number, bank details, or ID photos) over the phone to anyone claiming to be "Customs" or "Federal Agents."
- Do not ignore a physical Notice of Seizure or Notice of Forfeiture; missing the 30-day or 35-day deadlines can result in permanent loss of the property.
- Do not attempt to "explain away" the situation to an agent on the phone if there is any mention of criminal violations; request a lawyer immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Determine if the notice is a physical letter or a digital message.**
   - Physical letters from U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP) or the U.S. Postal Inspection Service (USPIS) on official letterhead are typically legitimate.
   - Text messages or emails demanding immediate payment or "verification" via a link are almost always scams.
2. **Locate the specific "Seizure Number" and "Case Number" on the mailed notice.**
   - A genuine notice will include a unique identification number for the seizure.
   - If you received a text, forward it to **7726** (SPAM) to report it to your carrier, then delete it.
3. **Verify the agency using an independent .gov source.**
   - Look up the local CBP "Fines, Penalties, and Forfeitures" (FP&F) office for the port of entry mentioned in your letter.
   - Call the official number found on a **.gov** website to confirm the case number exists before sending any documents.
4. **Identify the "Election of Proceedings" form in your physical mail packet.**
   - This form lists your legal options, such as:
     - **Petition for Remission or Mitigation:** Asking the agency for the item back based on your lack of knowledge of the violation.
     - **Offer in Compromise:** A settlement offer.
     - **Abandonment:** Voluntarily giving up the item.
     - **Judicial Forfeiture:** Requesting the case be moved to Federal Court (requires a "Claim" form).
5. **Note the exact deadlines for response (30 and 35 days).**
   - You generally have **30 days** from the date of the notice to file an administrative petition.
   - You generally have **35 days** from the mailing date to file a CAFRA (Civil Asset Forfeiture Reform Act) claim for judicial action.
6. **Preserve all original shipping labels and purchase receipts.**
   - Keep the envelope the notice arrived in, as the postmark may be needed to prove you responded within the legal timeframe.
7. **Cease questioning if an agent contacts you directly.**
   - If an agent asks about the contents of the package or your intent, state: **"I will not answer any questions without an attorney present."**
8. **Report financial fraud immediately if you already paid a scammer.**
   - Contact your bank or credit card issuer to report the transaction as fraud.
   - File a report at **ic3.gov** (FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center).

## What can wait
- You do not need to contact the seller or sender until you have verified the seizure is legitimate.
- You do not need to decide between a "Petition" and a "Court Claim" in the first hour; you have approximately 30 days to choose your legal path.
- You do not need to provide a written statement explaining the situation until you have decided which legal option on the Election of Proceedings form you will use.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an official seizure notice is a civil administrative process, not an immediate arrest. Scammers use the "authority" of CBP and USPS to create artificial urgency; taking 24 hours to verify a notice's case number through an official portal is a safe and standard procedure.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for receiving a notice of parcel seizure or detention. It focuses on distinguishing between scams and legitimate civil forfeiture processes. It does not cover legal strategy for criminal defense or complex trade compliance litigation.

## Important note
This information is for situational stabilization and does not constitute legal advice. Government deadlines are strict; failure to respond to a legitimate notice within the timeframe listed on the document will result in the permanent forfeiture of your property. If the notice mentions controlled substances, weapons, or high-value currency, consult a qualified attorney before submitting any forms.
