# What to do if… someone in a public place begins hovering close and watching your phone or bag (USA)

## Short answer
Create immediate physical distance and move toward a staffed or high-traffic area while securing all valuables against your body. If you are being actively touched, threatened, or if a theft is in progress, call 911 immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don't continue using your phone or looking at your screen while the person is within arm's length.
- Don't open your bag, purse, or wallet to "check" if items are still there while the person is watching.
- Don't engage in a verbal confrontation, argument, or physical blocking maneuver that could escalate the situation.
- Don't follow or chase the individual if they begin to move away from you.
- Don't set your bag down on a floor, counter, or chair back while addressing the situation.
- Don't hand your phone to any stranger offering "help" with directions, photos, or finding information.

## What to do now
1. **Secure your phone and bag against your body immediately.**  
   Stop scrolling, lock the screen, and place the phone in a secure, zipped internal pocket or hold it firmly with both hands against your chest. If carrying a bag, move it to the front of your body and ensure all zippers are closed and held under your arm or hand.
2. **Move to a "safe pause" location to break their line of access.**  
   Step sideways or change direction abruptly to put a physical barrier, such as a pillar, a store counter, or a group of people, between you and the individual. Avoid corners or dead-ends where your movement could be restricted.
3. **Locate and move toward a staffed point of assistance.**  
   Go directly to a security desk, a checkout counter, a transit employee booth, or a manager's station. If you are on a transit platform, look for an emergency intercom or move toward the conductor's car (usually marked by a black-and-white "zebra" board).
4. **Protect your "blind side" by using physical architecture.**  
   Stand with your back against a wall or solid pillar to eliminate the possibility of someone approaching you from behind. Keep your bag in front of you and maintain a 360-degree awareness of others in your immediate radius.
5. **Watch for team-based distraction cues and maintain your grip.**  
   Be alert for "accidental" bumps, people dropping items near you, or strangers asking probing questions, as these are often used to distract targets for a second person to commit the theft. Do not loosen your grip on your belongings if a commotion occurs nearby.
6. **Set a clear physical boundary if the person continues to close in.**  
   If the individual persists in crowding you, turn your shoulders to face them and say in a firm, neutral voice: "Please step back." Immediately resume moving toward a staffed area or a crowd of people without waiting for a response.
7. **Contact emergency services if the threat escalates or theft occurs.**  
   **Call 911** if the person attempts to grab your property, follows you after you move to a staffed area, or makes a physical threat. If the behavior is suspicious but not an immediate emergency, report the incident to venue security or the local police non-emergency line.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the person's intent was "definitely" criminal before taking safety actions.
- You do not need to provide a perfect physical description of the individual in the first few seconds of moving to safety.
- If no theft occurred, you do not need to file a formal police report immediately, though notifying staff helps them monitor the area.
- Administrative tasks like changing passwords or canceling cards can wait until you are in a fully secured, private location.

## Important reassurance
Feeling uneasy or "hyper-aware" when someone invades your personal space is a natural survival response. Moving to a safer area and securing your belongings is a proactive, reasonable step that reduces your status as a target, regardless of the other person's actual intentions.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a potential theft or harassment situation in a public space. It does not cover long-term legal reporting, insurance claims, or self-defense tactics.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. Safety procedures may vary depending on the specific laws of your city or the policies of the venue. Always prioritize your physical safety over the protection of property.
