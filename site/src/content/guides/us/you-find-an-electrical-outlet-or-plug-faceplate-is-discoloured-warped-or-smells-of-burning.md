# What to do if… you find an electrical outlet or plug faceplate is discoloured, warped, or smells of burning (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using that outlet immediately. If there is smoke, flame, active sparking, or immediate danger, get out and call 911. If it is safe, turn off power to that outlet at the breaker panel, keep it unused, and arrange for a licensed electrician to check it.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using the outlet to finish charging or running something.
- Don’t touch the outlet, cover plate, or plug if it feels hot, looks softened or melted, smells burnt, smokes, sparks, or crackles.
- Don’t remove the cover plate or try to tighten wires yourself.
- Don’t plug in another device to test it.
- Don’t ignore warmth, discoloration, a burning or rubbery smell, buzzing, crackling, sparks, smoke, or frequent breaker trips.
- Don’t keep resetting a breaker that trips. Leave the circuit off until it has been checked.
- Don’t move the same heavy load to another outlet with extension cords as a workaround.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If you see smoke, flames, active sparking, or arcing, get everyone out and call **911**.
2. **Stop the load only if it is safe.** If the outlet and plug are cool, dry, not smoking, not sparking, not crackling, and not stuck, unplug the device by holding the plug body, not the cord.
   - If anything is hot, stuck, softened, smoking, sparking, crackling, or smells like it is burning, don’t touch it.
3. **Shut off power at the breaker panel only if the panel area seems normal.** Turn off the circuit breaker that feeds that outlet.
   - If you can’t identify the right breaker, you may turn off the main breaker only if you can do so safely and quickly and there is no heat, smoke, burning smell, or crackling at the panel.
   - If the panel area seems hot, smoky, or crackling, don’t open it or stand close. Leave and call **911**.
   - If someone relies on powered medical equipment, prioritize safety and call **911** for guidance before shutting off the main.
4. **Mark the outlet as not safe to use.** Put tape or a note over the outlet and tell others not to use it. Keep the device, charger, plug, or cord that was connected out of service too.
5. **Reduce nearby fire risk.** Move paper, fabric, cleaning chemicals, aerosols, and other easy-to-burn items away from the outlet if you can do this safely. If there is odor but no smoke, flames, sparking, or heat, open a window briefly only if it does not delay leaving or calling for help.
6. **Get the right help.**
   - **If you rent:** notify your landlord or property manager right away. Say the outlet or plug faceplate is discolored or warped, you suspect overheating, and you have shut off the breaker if you were able to do that safely.
   - **If you own:** contact a licensed electrician promptly. Tell them what you saw, smelled, or heard, whether the breaker tripped, and what was plugged in.
7. **Keep the circuit off if the problem continues.** If the breaker will not stay on, or other outlets or lights on that circuit flicker, dim, buzz, or smell odd, leave the breaker off and treat it as urgent until evaluated.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to diagnose whether the problem is the outlet, wiring, breaker, appliance, charger, or cord right now.
- You don’t need to replace the outlet yourself today.
- You don’t need to decide who is responsible for the repair before making the area safer.
- You don’t need perfect photos or notes before calling for help. Keeping the outlet off and unused matters more.

## Important reassurance
Discoloration, warping, heat, buzzing, crackling, and burning smells are real warning signs, not something you have to downplay. Stopping use and cutting power when it is safe to do so are practical first steps that reduce the chance of the problem escalating.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to reduce risk and buy time. A licensed electrician may need to inspect the receptacle, faceplate, wiring connections, breaker, and the appliance, charger, plug, or cord that was involved.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, electrical, fire-safety, insurance, medical, or other professional advice. If you see smoke, flames, active sparking, or believe there is immediate danger, leave and call emergency services.
