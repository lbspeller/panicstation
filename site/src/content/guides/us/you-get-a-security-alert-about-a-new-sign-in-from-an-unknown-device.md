# What to do if… you get a security alert about a new sign-in from an unknown device (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the account by accessing the service directly through an official app or bookmarked URL, revoke all active sessions immediately, and change your password to a unique 16-character string while enabling phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click any links or buttons inside the alert email or text message to "confirm" or "deny" the sign-in.
- Don’t call phone numbers provided in the notification or reply to the sender.
- Don’t ignore the alert even if it looks like a common mistake; assume the account is compromised until you verify the session logs yourself.
- Don’t attempt to "investigate" the attacker's location or IP address through the alert's links.
- Don’t reuse any part of your old password or use a password you use for any other service.
- Don’t approve any "push" notifications on your phone that you did not personally trigger.

## What to do now
1. **Access the account through a trusted, independent channel.**  
   **Open the official app on your device or type the website address directly into your browser rather than following the alert link.** Navigate to the **Security**, **Privacy**, or **Recent Activity** tab to view all active sign-ins.

2. **Verify the session details against your own activity.**  
   **Compare the time, device type, and approximate location shown in the account settings with your actual movements.** Note that some cellular networks or VPNs may show locations in nearby cities or states, but an entirely unknown device (e.g., an "iPhone 15" when you use Android) is a definitive sign of compromise.

3. **Force a "Sign Out of All Sessions" immediately.**  
   **Look for a button labeled "Sign out of all other sessions," "Remove all devices," or "Logout everywhere" and trigger it.** This is critical because modern attackers (2026) often use stolen session tokens that allow them to stay logged in even if you change the password.

4. **Update your password to a high-entropy string.**  
   **Create a new password that is at least 16 characters long and completely unique to this account.** Use a password manager to generate and store this to ensure it is not guessable or reused from other breaches.

5. **Enable or upgrade your Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).**  
   **Activate MFA and prioritize phishing-resistant methods like hardware security keys (FIDO2) or passkeys over SMS-based codes.** If you already had MFA enabled, verify that no new "backup" codes were generated or new "trusted devices" were added by an intruder.

6. **Review and purge account recovery settings.**  
   **Check the recovery email address, phone number, and "security questions" to ensure they have not been modified.** Remove any unfamiliar entries immediately, as these are used by attackers to regain access after you have changed the password.

7. **Inspect email rules and forwarding instructions.**  
   **Look for hidden inbox filters or auto-forwarding rules that might be sending copies of your sensitive emails (like bank alerts) to an external address.** Delete any rules that move mail to the "Trash" or "Archive" automatically, which is a common tactic to hide unauthorized activity.

8. **Contact financial institutions if the account is linked to payments.**  
   **Call your bank or credit card issuer using the number on the back of your card to report a potential digital compromise.** 
   - Ask for a "Hold Harmless" or "Letter of Indemnity" if fraudulent transfers are detected.
   - Request a temporary freeze on the account if you cannot yet verify the extent of the access.

9. **File official reports if personal or financial data was exposed.**  
   **Use IdentityTheft.gov to create a personalized recovery plan and place a free one-year fraud alert on your credit reports.** If the takeover involved fraud, file a complaint at **ic3.gov** and specifically include the phrases "Account Takeover" and "SEO poisoning" in your description to assist with 2026 federal tracking priorities.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific person or group who accessed the account.
- You do not need to replace your hardware (phone/computer) unless the compromise continues after all sessions are revoked and MFA is upgraded.
- You do not need to notify all your contacts unless you have evidence the attacker sent messages on your behalf.
- You do not need to close the account permanently if you have successfully regained control and secured the recovery methods.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a security alert means the system's protective triggers are working. While the notification is designed to create a sense of urgency, taking a deliberate and methodical approach—securing the account first and reviewing the data second—is the most effective way to prevent further loss.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a suspected unauthorized sign-in. It does not cover deep forensic device cleaning or long-term legal recovery for identity theft.

## Important note
This information is for situational stabilization and does not constitute legal or professional cybersecurity advice. If you suspect your Social Security number or financial accounts have been fully compromised, follow the official recovery paths provided by the FTC and your financial institutions.
