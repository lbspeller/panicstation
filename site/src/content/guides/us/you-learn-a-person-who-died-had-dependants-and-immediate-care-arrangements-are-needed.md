# What to do if… you learn a person who died had dependants and immediate care arrangements are needed (USA)

## Short answer
Ensure all dependants are physically supervised and safe; if any child or vulnerable adult is currently alone, call 911 immediately for a welfare check and emergency placement assistance.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t leave a child or vulnerable adult alone while you search for paperwork or relatives.
- Don’t assume "someone" is coming to get them without verifying the person's identity and arrival time.
- Don’t transport the dependant to a different state or jurisdiction without consulting child/adult welfare agencies first.
- Don’t delay calling emergency services because you are worried about the dependant "entering the system" if they are currently unsafe.
- Don’t give medications to the dependant unless you have clear, labeled instructions or professional medical guidance.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 immediately if any dependant is currently unsupervised or in an unconfirmed location.**  
   State clearly that a primary caregiver has died and there are minors or vulnerable adults who may be alone or in danger.
2. **Contact the state or county Child Protective Services (CPS) hotline for minors.**  
   Explain that you are calling regarding a "caregiver death" and require an immediate emergency response for placement.
   - Ask for the 24-hour intake worker.
   - Provide the current physical address where the children are located.
3. **Contact Adult Protective Services (APS) for vulnerable or elderly dependants.**  
   Use the local APS directory to find the emergency reporting line for the county where the adult resides.
   - Report that the primary caregiver is deceased.
   - Specify if the adult has immediate medical, mobility, or cognitive needs.
4. **Locate the "Emergency Care" or "Standby Guardian" documents if possible.**  
   Check the deceased person’s fridge, nightstand, or digital files for a Will, Standby Guardianship form, or Power of Attorney.
   - These documents may name a specific person authorized to take immediate custody.
5. **Notify the school, daycare, or home health agency immediately.**  
   Inform them of the death so they do not release the dependant to unauthorized persons or leave them at a bus stop alone.
   - Ask for the facility's social worker or crisis coordinator to assist with a safe handoff.
6. **Gather a "care kit" of essential items for the dependant.**  
   If the dependant must be moved, try to secure:
   - Prescription medications and medical devices (CPAP, monitors).
   - Identification, insurance cards, and Social Security numbers.
   - A comfort item (blanket, toy) and several days of clothing.
7. **Document all known allergies and immediate medical schedules.**  
   Write down the names of doctors, current pharmacies used, and the last time any medication was administered to prevent dosing errors.
8. **Contact the Social Security Administration (SSA) to report the death if dependants may be eligible for benefits.**  
   Call 1-800-772-1213 (TTY 1-800-325-0778) between 8:00 a.m. and 7:00 p.m. local time.
   - Minor children and some disabled adult dependants may be eligible for immediate survivor assistance.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve permanent legal custody or adoption today.
- You do not need to handle the deceased person’s estate, house, or vehicle right now.
- You do not need to notify extended family members who are not involved in immediate care.
- You do not need to coordinate the funeral or memorial services until dependants are placed in a safe environment.

## Important reassurance
It is a normal and protective response to feel overwhelmed when discovering dependants left behind after a death. Calling 911 or welfare agencies is a responsible action to ensure the safety of those who cannot care for themselves; it is the standard procedure for these crises and ensures professional resources are activated.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hours following a caregiver's death. It does not cover long-term guardianship litigation, probate law, or permanent foster care placement.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and is not legal or medical advice. Procedures for emergency guardianship vary by state. If anyone is in immediate physical danger, contact 911.
