# What to do if… you get an alert that a garage door, gate, or secure entry opened unexpectedly and you did not do it (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the alert as potentially real until you know otherwise. Get to a safer place and call 911 if someone may be on the property now or anyone could be in danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go into the garage, yard, driveway, hallway, or entry area to “take a look” if you are not sure whether someone is there.
- Don’t confront a person, chase a vehicle, or try to clear your home yourself.
- Don’t re-enter a home, garage, or secured area that you suspect was entered.
- Don’t keep opening and closing the door or gate to test it.
- Don’t touch, tidy, or move things in an area that may have been accessed, unless you need to do so for immediate safety.
- Don’t post real-time details about your location, cameras, travel, or whether the property is empty.

## What to do now
1. **Get to, or stay in, a safer position.**
   - **If you’re inside:** move away from the alerted entry point, go to a room that locks if you can, gather others quietly, and keep your phone with you.
   - **If you’re outside or arriving:** do not enter. Move to a neighbor’s home, a staffed public place, or a locked vehicle at a safe distance.
   - **If you’re away from home:** stay where you are and do not race back into a possible active situation.

2. **Check only what you can see without approaching.**
   - Look at camera, doorbell, alarm, or app notifications from your current safe spot.
   - Notice whether the alert names a garage door, gate, shared entry, user account, keypad, remote, or sensor.
   - If you can do it safely from where you are, turn on lights remotely or from inside your safe area.

3. **Call 911 if someone may be there now, if you hear or see anything suspicious, or if you cannot rule out immediate danger.**
   - Say you received an alert that a garage door, gate, or secure entry opened and you did not open it.
   - Give the exact address, your location, whether anyone is inside, and what the app, alarm, camera, or sounds show.
   - Follow the call-taker’s instructions and do not hang up until they tell you to.

4. **If you are away and it does not seem immediate, use the safest reporting route.**
   - If there may be danger now, call 911 from where you are.
   - If you are confident there is no immediate danger, use the local police non-emergency number from your city, county, police department website, or a saved contact.
   - If you are unsure whether there may be danger now, call 911.

5. **If building staff, a neighbor, or security can help, keep their role limited.**
   - Ask them to observe from a safe distance or check a camera, lobby, gate area, or exterior only if they can do so safely.
   - Do not ask anyone to enter your unit, garage, yard, or home to look for a person.

6. **If you have a monitored alarm, contact the monitoring company once you are safe.**
   - Ask what zone, sensor, user, keypad, or timestamp triggered the alert.
   - Ask whether emergency response was already requested.

7. **Only if it is clearly safe, reduce immediate exposure once.**
   - If a live camera view clearly shows the door or gate area is empty and closing it will not put anyone at risk, you may choose to close it from the app one time.
   - If you cannot see the area clearly, do not operate the door or gate. Stay safe and wait for help.

8. **Preserve details without going in.**
   - Write down the alert time, what opened, what you saw or heard, and what you did next.
   - Save camera clips, screenshots, app notifications, access logs, and alarm messages so they are not overwritten.
   - Note any people, vehicles, license plates, direction of travel, or unusual sounds only if you can do that safely.

9. **When officers or other help arrive, let them take over.**
   - Stay visible, keep your hands empty, and follow instructions.
   - Do not enter the home, garage, yard, or secured area until you are told it is safe.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this was a break-in, a device malfunction, a delayed alert, a mistaken user, or a shared-access issue.
- You do not need to search every room, check every item, or prove what happened while you are still unsafe or shaken.
- You do not need to reset devices, change locks, remove users, call an installer, or redesign your security setup in the moment.
- You do not need to decide about insurance, repairs, complaints, or long-term prevention until the immediate situation is stable.

## Important reassurance
An unexpected entry alert can make your body react fast. Taking it seriously, staying out of the area, and calling for help when danger is possible are reasonable first steps, even if it later turns out to be a glitch or a confusing notification.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to reduce immediate risk and prevent irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about access logs, shared users, locks, devices, reports, repairs, or insurance may need help from police, building management, an alarm company, an installer, an insurer, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. If someone may be on the property or anyone could be in danger, call 911.
