# What to do if… your car door is frozen shut and you cannot get inside (USA)
## Short answer
Do not yank the handle. Try another door first, then gently break the ice bond by pushing the stuck door inward and using de-icer, or a small amount of alcohol-based hand sanitizer if the lock is frozen and you do not have de-icer.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pull hard on the handle or pry at the door edge; handles, trim, and weatherstripping can break.
- Don’t pour hot or boiling water on the door, lock, or windows; it can damage glass or locks and may refreeze.
- Don’t use an open flame on the key, lock, handle, paint, or rubber seals.
- Don’t chip at the paint, seal, or lock with a screwdriver, knife, or metal scraper.
- Don’t keep struggling outside if your fingers are numb, you are shaking, or you feel confused or weak; get to warmth and ask for help.

## What to do now
1. **Try every entry point first.** Check the passenger door, rear doors, trunk, or hatch. One door may open even when the driver door is frozen shut.
2. **Confirm it is unlocked.** Use the fob or key if available. If the lock cylinder is frozen, do not force the key.
3. **Push the stuck door inward before pulling.** Press the flat of your hand against the door near the frozen edge and push it slightly more closed in short, gentle pulses. This can crack the ice bond at the seal without stressing the handle.
4. **Use de-icer on the door seam.** Spray along the top and sides where the door meets the body. Wait briefly, repeat if needed, then try the handle gently.
5. **If the lock cylinder is frozen, use lock de-icer if you have it.** Aim it into the lock opening, wait briefly, then insert the key gently. Do not twist hard.
6. **If you do not have lock de-icer, use a small amount of alcohol-based hand sanitizer.** Put a little on the key and around the lock opening, insert the key gently, wait briefly, and try turning without force. Wipe the key afterward.
7. **Use warm water only as a last resort.** Use warm, not hot, water along the rubber seal area only. Avoid window glass, electronics, and the lock if you can. Dry the seal and door edge immediately so it does not refreeze worse.
8. **If you get in through another door, warm the stuck area from inside.** Start the car only outdoors or with full ventilation, check that the tailpipe is clear of snow, and do not run the engine in a closed garage.
9. **If you are stranded in dangerous cold or an unsafe location, stop trying to fix the door outside.** Move to a warm public place if you can and call roadside assistance, your insurer’s roadside number, or someone who can come to you. If there is immediate danger to life or safety, call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide how to winterproof the car right now.
- You do not need to keep working on the driver door if another door or the hatch gets you inside safely.
- You do not need the perfect method; gentle pressure and targeted melting are safer than force.
- You can deal later with cleaning the seals, checking drainage, replacing damaged weatherstripping, or buying lock de-icer.

## Important reassurance
A frozen car door is a common cold-weather problem, and feeling rushed is understandable. Damage is more likely when you force the handle or pry at the seal, so slowing down and switching to gentle, targeted steps is usually the safer move.

## Scope note
These are first steps for getting access safely and reducing damage. If the door, lock, handle, or seal is still stuck or damaged after thawing, later decisions may need help from roadside assistance, a mechanic, or a locksmith.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional, mechanical, medical, legal, financial, or emergency-services advice. If you feel unwell from cold exposure, cannot get to warmth, or are in immediate danger, seek emergency help.
