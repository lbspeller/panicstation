# What to do if… you cannot find the important documents or will of a person who has died (USA)

## Short answer
Secure the home and the mail first, then make a simple list of what you have found and who you have asked. Most missing documents can usually be found through mail, records, the person’s attorney or bank, or replaced through official offices once you have the right authority.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t shred, discard, or “tidy away” mail, folders, envelopes, notebooks, or old statements yet.
- Don’t post or mass-text photos of IDs, bank letters, account numbers, passwords, or death certificates.
- Don’t try to access accounts by guessing passwords, using saved logins, or pretending to be the person who died.
- Don’t hand out valuables, documents, cards, keys, or checkbooks until you know who is legally allowed to manage them.
- Don’t assume the will is already at the courthouse; in many places, it may only appear in court records after a probate case is opened or a filing is made.
- Don’t pay large bills from your own money unless you have checked whether you are responsible and whether the estate can pay.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the obvious places without reorganising everything.**  
   Lock the home if you can, collect spare keys, and put obvious document containers in one controlled place: folders, envelopes, filing boxes, safes, binders, desk drawers, bedside drawers, briefcases, and car glove compartments. Take a photo of where important items were found before moving them.

2. **Make a one-page documents map.**  
   Write down the places searched, documents found, people contacted, and next follow-ups. Use plain labels such as “desk drawer searched,” “insurance letter found,” “called bank,” and “attorney name unknown.”

3. **Look for clues before looking for every document.**  
   Prioritise anything that points to other records: an attorney’s name, bank name, insurance company, tax preparer, employer, union, veterans paperwork, safe deposit box key, password manager note, address book, email printouts, or recent mail.

4. **Ask for certified death certificates through the right vital records office.**  
   If you do not already have certified copies, check the state or local vital records office in the state where the death occurred. Ask how many certified copies you can get and order only what you reasonably need now; banks, insurers, courts, and government offices may each have their own requirements.

5. **Protect the mail stream.**  
   Keep mail together because bills, statements, tax forms, insurance notices, and renewal letters often reveal missing accounts. If you shared the address, USPS says you may open and manage the deceased person’s mail as needed. If mail needs to be forwarded to a different address, USPS says the appointed executor or administrator must make the request in person at a Post Office with documented proof of authority; a death certificate by itself is not enough.

6. **Contact any attorney or tax preparer you can identify.**  
   If you find letterhead, business cards, invoices, emails, or tax returns naming a law office or tax professional, call and ask what they need before they can confirm whether they hold a will, trust, tax records, or estate-planning documents.

7. **Ask the local probate court whether anything has been filed.**  
   If you suspect a probate case has already started, contact the probate court in the county where the person lived or owned property. Ask what information the clerk needs to check whether an estate case or will filing exists. Do not assume the court can advise you on what to do next.

8. **Contact banks and insurers cautiously.**  
   Call customer service or the bereavement department, say the account holder has died, and ask what documents they require before they can discuss anything. Account details, safe deposit boxes, and records may be restricted until an executor, administrator, beneficiary, joint owner, or other authorised person provides the required paperwork.

9. **If the person received Social Security or Medicare, check that the death was reported.**  
   Funeral homes generally report deaths to Social Security when given the person’s Social Security number. If no funeral home was involved, or you are not sure the report was made, SSA says reports are made by phone or in person, not online or by email. The SSA main number is 1-800-772-1213, and TTY is 1-800-325-0778.

10. **Keep found documents in a simple temporary bundle.**  
   Use separate envelopes or folders for death certificates, identity documents, will or trust papers, bank and insurance papers, tax papers, property papers, vehicle papers, debts and bills, digital-access clues, and contact names. Keep originals separate from copies.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find every document today.
- You do not need to decide who receives property or personal items until you know whether there is a will and who has authority to act.
- You do not need to close every account now; focus first on preventing lost mail, missed urgent notices, fraud risk, and loss of property.
- You do not need to solve every password or digital account unless it blocks a specific urgent task.
- You do not need to know probate law today before making a careful list and asking offices what documents they require.

## Important reassurance
This is a common situation, even in close families. Important papers are often spread across mail, filing cabinets, banks, attorneys, tax preparers, email printouts, and official records, and many documents can be replaced once the right person has the required proof.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Probate rules, who can act for the estate, access to bank records, safe deposit box rules, and document replacement processes vary by state, county, institution, and family situation; later decisions may need help from a probate attorney, tax professional, bank bereavement team, or court clerk.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If there is family conflict, suspected fraud, unclear authority, missing valuables, or disagreement about a will, slow down and get qualified local guidance before signing forms, moving assets, distributing property, or handing over original documents.
