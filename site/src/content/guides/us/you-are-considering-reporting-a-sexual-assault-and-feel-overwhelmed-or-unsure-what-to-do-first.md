# What to do if… you are considering reporting a sexual assault and feel overwhelmed or unsure what to do first (USA)

## Short answer
You usually do not need to decide whether to report right now. Start with one small step: contact a sexual assault hotline or advocate, or ask a healthcare provider about sexual assault medical care and any privacy or reporting limits that apply.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force yourself to make every decision while you feel overwhelmed.
- Do not let anyone pressure you to report, stay silent, confront the person, or make a formal statement before you are ready.
- Do not assume that support is unavailable because time has passed, you have showered, or you are unsure what to call what happened.
- Do not confront the person who harmed you while you feel overwhelmed or unsafe.
- Do not feel that you must give a detailed, perfectly ordered account before asking for help.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause.**  
   Move to a place where you feel safer, if you can. If you are in immediate danger or need emergency help, call **911**.

2. **Contact a sexual assault hotline or advocate.**  
   RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline can connect you with support and local services. You can call **800-656-4673** or use the online hotline. Before sharing identifying details, ask about any privacy limits that may apply.

3. **Ask about medical care and local rules.**  
   You can ask a hospital, clinic, hotline, or local sexual assault service where to receive sexual assault medical care. You can also ask whether a sexual assault medical forensic exam, sometimes called a SAFE exam, is available and what local reporting or confidentiality rules apply.

4. If you may want a forensic exam and it feels manageable, set aside the unwashed clothes you were wearing in a paper bag; you can still seek care and support if you have already changed, washed, or waited.

5. **Ask one safe person to help with the next contact.**  
   A trusted person can sit with you, help you call a hotline, arrange a ride, or help you find a local service. If no one feels safe, ask the hotline or advocate to help you choose the next step.

## What can wait
- You usually do not need to decide now whether to report to law enforcement or participate in any process. Ask an advocate or provider about local rules if you are unsure.
- You do not need to decide what label to use for what happened.
- You do not need to give a detailed account before asking for support or medical care.
- You do not need to investigate, gather information yourself, or confront anyone.
- You can wait to decide what to tell family, friends, your employer, or your school.

## Important reassurance
Feeling overwhelmed, numb, unsure, or unable to think clearly can happen after sexual assault. You are allowed to take one small step at a time. You can ask about support and options before deciding whether to make a report.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the moment and reduce pressure. Later decisions about medical care, reporting, school or workplace processes, or legal options may need help from a trained advocate, healthcare professional, or other appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This guide provides general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If you feel unable to keep yourself safe, seek urgent help through emergency services or an emergency department.
