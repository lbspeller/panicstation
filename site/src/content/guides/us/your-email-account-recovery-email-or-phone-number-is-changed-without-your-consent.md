# What to do if… your email account recovery email or phone number is changed without your consent (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as possible account takeover. Use the email provider’s official recovery or “secure a hacked account” process now, then remove any recovery details, forwarding, rules, devices, or connected apps you do not recognize.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click security-alert or reset-password links from messages you were not expecting; open the provider’s official app or type the provider’s address yourself.
- Do not call support numbers from search ads, popups, texts, or social media replies.
- Do not pay anyone who claims they can recover the account for you.
- Do not keep guessing passwords or codes; repeated failures can slow recovery or add verification hurdles.
- Do not assume a new password is enough; attackers may leave forwarding, inbox rules, recovery details, devices, or connected apps behind.
- Do not delete the account while you are still trying to regain control, because you may lose access to messages and security records you need.

## What to do now
1. Use a safer device if you can. Choose a device you normally control. If you think that device may be infected, run its built-in security scan or use another trusted device before changing passwords.
2. Start with the provider’s official recovery flow. Look for the provider’s own “account recovery,” “secure a hacked account,” or “can’t sign in” page. If you can still sign in, go straight to the account security settings.
3. If you can get into the account, secure it in this order:
   - Change the password to a strong password you have not used anywhere else.
   - Sign out of other sessions and devices if the provider offers that option.
   - Remove any recovery email address or phone number you do not control.
   - Add recovery details you control.
   - Turn on the strongest sign-in protection the provider offers, such as two-step verification, an authenticator app, passkeys, or backup codes.
4. Check the mailbox for ways the attacker could keep seeing your messages:
   - Turn off forwarding you did not set.
   - Remove filters or inbox rules that auto-forward, auto-delete, archive, or mark messages as read.
   - Review connected apps and third-party access, then revoke anything you do not recognize.
   - Check recent sign-in activity and remove unknown devices where the provider allows it.
5. Protect accounts that can be reset through this email. Start with banking, payment apps, mobile carrier, cloud storage, retailer accounts with saved cards, social media, and work or school accounts. Check that each account’s recovery email and phone number are yours.
6. If you suspect a SIM swap or phone-number takeover, contact your mobile carrier using the number on your bill, statement, official app, or official website. Ask whether there were recent SIM, eSIM, port-out, or line changes, and ask what extra account protections are available.
7. If identity theft may be involved, use IdentityTheft.gov for a recovery plan. If money was lost or the incident was part of an online scam or cybercrime, use the official IC3.gov site to file a complaint.
8. Write down the main facts while they are fresh: when you noticed the change, what changed, any unknown recovery details, any suspicious sign-ins, and any accounts that may have been affected.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove exactly how the attacker got in before securing the account.
- You do not need to message everyone immediately; first stop forwarding, remove rules, and regain control.
- You do not need to fix every online account at once; prioritize accounts tied to money, phone service, identity documents, cloud storage, work, or password resets.
- You do not need to decide today whether to abandon the email address; that can wait until the account is stable or recovery has clearly failed.

## Important reassurance
This is a common account-takeover pattern, and it is normal to feel rushed. The most useful first move is not to investigate everything at once; it is to use official recovery, remove the attacker’s access, and protect the accounts that depend on this email.

## Scope note
These are first steps to limit damage and buy time. Later decisions, such as provider support options, identity-theft recovery, workplace notification, or moving important accounts to a new email address, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, or you believe a crime is actively in progress, contact local authorities.
