# What to do if… you expect a volatile person may show up at a funeral or memorial service and you are worried about disruption (USA)

## Short answer
Set up a clear access and escalation plan with the funeral home, cemetery, place of worship, or other venue now. Choose one point person so the closest family do not have to negotiate at the door.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t try to talk them down yourself at the entrance or during the service.
- Don’t send threats, insults, or public posts about banning them.
- Don’t share the schedule or location more widely than necessary.
- Don’t ask grieving family members to act as security without venue support.
- Don’t physically block, grab, or remove them yourself.
- Don’t debate a protective order at the door; keep a copy available and let law enforcement handle interpretation.

## What to do now
1. **Call the funeral home or venue and state the concern plainly.**  
   Say: “We are worried that a specific person may arrive and disrupt the service. Who on staff can handle entry concerns, and what is your process if someone refuses to leave?”

2. **Choose the access plan you want staff to use.**  
   Ask what the venue can realistically support. Options may include:
   - allowing entry only if the person is calm and not threatening
   - treating the service as private and asking the person to leave if they are not invited
   - seating the person separately, with staff nearby, if that feels safer than a confrontation at the entrance

3. **Name one point person.**  
   The point person speaks with staff, police, or security. Close family should not have to explain, argue, or negotiate during the service.

4. **Give staff practical identifying information.**  
   Provide the person’s name, a recent photo if you have one, a vehicle description if known, and the specific concerns, such as yelling, intoxication, threats, or targeting a particular mourner.

5. **Agree on a simple signal.**  
   Use a plain phrase such as “Please start the safety plan.” Make sure the point person, funeral director or venue contact, and one backup person know what it means.

6. **Plan arrival, seating, and exit to reduce contact.**  
   Ask whether close family can use a side entrance, whether staff can be near the main entrance before guests arrive, and where someone can step away quietly if distressed.

7. **Save the right phone numbers before the service.**  
   If there is immediate danger, violence, weapons, or an active threat, call 911. For planning ahead or non-emergency concerns, look up the local law enforcement agency for the venue’s city or county and save its 10-digit non-emergency number.

8. **If there is a protective or restraining order, make it usable on the day.**  
   Give the point person a copy or a clear photo of the order. Tell the venue that an order exists, but do not rely on staff to interpret it; call law enforcement if the person appears and you believe the order or safety plan is being violated.

9. **Reduce direct contact on the day.**  
   Put phones on Do Not Disturb except for the point person and essential contacts. Forward any last-minute messages from the person to the point person instead of replying while emotions are high.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to pursue longer-term legal action, family mediation, or a permanent communication plan.
- You do not need to write the perfect message to extended family about boundaries.
- You do not need to solve the underlying conflict before the service.
- You only need a clear access plan, one point person, and a way to get help if safety changes.

## Important reassurance
Feeling on edge before a service with family volatility is understandable. A simple plan with the venue can reduce the chance of disruption and help the closest family focus on mourning rather than managing conflict.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate service period only. Later decisions about boundaries, legal options, venue complaints, or family communication may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, safety, mental health, or professional advice. Laws, protective orders, police procedures, and venue policies vary by state, county, and property type, so confirm the plan directly with the venue and local law enforcement where the service will take place.
