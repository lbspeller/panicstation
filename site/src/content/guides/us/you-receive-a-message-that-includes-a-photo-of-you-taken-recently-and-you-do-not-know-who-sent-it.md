# What to do if… you receive a message that includes a photo of you taken recently and you do not know who sent it (USA)

## Short answer
Assume the message represents a credible threat to your physical or digital safety: do not respond, isolate yourself from the sender, and move to a secure location if the photo suggests the sender is nearby. Call 911 immediately if you believe you are being actively watched or followed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply, acknowledge the message, or attempt to "bait" the sender into revealing their identity.
- Do not click any links, download files, or open further attachments from the sender.
- Do not pay any money, provide gift card codes, or send additional photos to stop the contact.
- Do not attempt to investigate the sender's identity or confront them yourself.
- Do not delete the message or thread until you have preserved the necessary evidence.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a secure or public location immediately if the photo appears to have been taken in your current vicinity.** **Go to a police station, a staffed public building, or a locked residence and call 911 if you feel in immediate physical danger.**
2. **Stop all interaction with the sender account or phone number.** **Do not send a final message or explanation; simply stop responding and mute the conversation to prevent new notifications from causing further distress while you preserve evidence.**
3. **Capture comprehensive screenshots of the entire communication thread.** **Ensure the screenshots include the sender's profile name, phone number or handle, the photo itself, the date and time of the message, and any accompanying text.**
   - Do not crop or edit the screenshots.
   - Note down any specific location clues visible in the photo, such as street signs, building numbers, or your vehicle's license plate.
4. **Report the account using the specific platform's reporting tools.** **Select the options for "stalking," "harassment," or "extortion" to alert the service provider's safety team.**
5. **Block the sender once evidence is preserved and the platform report is filed.** **This prevents the sender from seeing your "active" status or continuing to send direct messages to that specific account.**
6. **File an official report with the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3).** **Visit ic3.gov to submit a complaint regarding cyber-enabled extortion or harassment, providing the specific details captured in your screenshots.**
7. **Disable location-sharing services on all mobile devices and social media accounts.** **Turn off "Live Location" in messaging apps and ensure your "Significant Locations" or "Location History" settings are set to private or off.**
8. **Contact a trusted individual to inform them of the situation and your current location.** **Ask them to check in with you at a specific time and avoid traveling alone for the remainder of the day.**
9. **Notify onsite security if the photo was taken at your workplace, school, or residential complex.** **Provide them with the time the photo was likely taken so they can attempt to preserve relevant surveillance footage from the area.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify who the sender is or why they have the photo right now.
- You do not need to delete your social media accounts or change your phone number today.
- You do not need to decide whether you want to press criminal charges immediately.
- You do not need to check your other devices for malware right this second; prioritize physical safety first.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a photo of yourself from an unknown source is a high-stress event, and feeling a sense of alarm is a normal reaction to a boundary violation. These messages often rely on the element of surprise to force a victim into making quick, ill-advised decisions. By stopping contact and documenting the incident, you are taking the most effective steps to regain control of the situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions. If the harassment continues from different accounts or if you suspect physical stalking, you may need to consult with law enforcement for a long-term safety plan.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, contact 911 or your local law enforcement agency. Reporting to the IC3 is for documenting cybercrime and does not replace an emergency response.
