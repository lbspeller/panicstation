# What to do if… an officer asks you to write down your account of events “from memory” immediately (USA)

## Short answer
Do not write or sign any statement at the scene. Verbally state that you are remaining silent and that you want a lawyer, then stop providing any information other than basic identification if required by your state.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attempt to “clear things up” by providing a quick written summary—any discrepancy between this and later testimony can be used to impeach your credibility.
- Don’t guess at times, distances, or sequences of events to be helpful.
- Don’t sign any documents, even if the officer tells you it is “just to acknowledge you were here” or “just a formality.”
- Don’t physically resist if an officer tries to place a pen or paper in your hand; simply leave the items on the surface provided.
- Don’t explain *why* you are refusing to write; providing excuses gives the officer more information to use in their report.

## What to do now
1. **Determine your current legal status by asking if you are free to leave.** **Ask the officer: “Am I free to go, or am I being detained?”**
   - If the officer says you are free to go, leave the area immediately and calmly.
2. **State your intention to remain silent clearly and out loud.** **Say: “I am going to remain silent and I want a lawyer.”**
   - You must speak these words; remaining silent without explicitly invoking the right may allow police to continue questioning you.
3. **Provide basic identification if your state law requires it during a detention.** **If you are being detained, provide your name, date of birth, and address if requested, but do not provide any other details.**
   - In some "Stop and Identify" states, refusing to provide a name during a lawful detention can lead to arrest.
4. **Refuse to use any writing materials provided by law enforcement.** **If an officer places a form or paper in front of you, do not write anything on it, including your name or an invocation of rights.**
   - Anything you write on a police-provided form may be categorized as a "voluntary statement" or used as evidence of a waiver of rights.
5. **Maintain your silence even if the officer continues to talk or ask questions.** **Wait for the officer to finish speaking and then repeat: “I am remaining silent and I want a lawyer.”**
   - Officers are permitted to use various conversational tactics to encourage you to speak; your only responsibility is to repeat your invocation.
6. **Request an interpreter or parent if you do not understand the proceedings.** **If English is not your first language or if you are a minor, state that you need an interpreter or your parent/guardian present before any further interaction.**
7. **Take mental notes of the interaction for your own future records.** **Note the officer’s name, badge number, agency, and the approximate time the request for a written statement was made.**
   - Do not write these notes down in the presence of the officer; wait until you are in a private, safe location.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide right now whether you will ever provide a statement; that is a decision for you and your lawyer later.
- You do **not** need to worry about "looking guilty" by exercising your constitutional rights.
- You do **not** need to correct any perceived errors or lies the officer tells you in the moment.
- You do **not** need to provide a timeline or narrative to the police today.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant pressure to comply when a person in authority asks for "your side of the story." Feeling anxious, confused, or pressured does not mean you have done anything wrong. Using a simple, pre-planned phrase to protect your rights is a standard procedure and is what legal professionals recommend.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate pressure of an on-scene request for a written statement. It does not cover specific state-level nuances for witnesses vs. suspects or the long-term legal strategy required for court proceedings.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding police encounters vary by jurisdiction and can change. Always consult a qualified attorney as soon as possible following any interaction with law enforcement where a statement was requested.
