# What to do if… you think you are being followed or stalked after repeated coincidental encounters (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer, public place and tell one trusted person where you are. If you feel in immediate danger, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to “confirm” the stalking by leading the person somewhere isolated or putting yourself at risk.
- Do not confront the person alone or agree to a private meeting to “clear it up.”
- Do not post your location, route, or routine in real time.
- Do not delete messages, voicemails, photos, screenshots, notes, gifts, or call logs that may show a pattern.
- Do not assume you must wait until it “gets worse” before asking for help.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** Go inside a busy store, café, workplace, campus building, or other staffed public place. If you are driving, do not go straight home if you think you are being followed; go to a well-lit, staffed public place or an open police station lobby if one is nearby.
2. **Use emergency help if danger feels immediate.** Call 911 if you think you may be harmed, blocked, chased, threatened, or unable to leave safely. If you cannot safely speak, call and stay on the line; text 911 only if you cannot safely call, and include your exact location because text-to-911 is not available everywhere.
3. **Tell one person now.** Send or say where you are, what you noticed, what the person or vehicle looks like, and what you are doing next. Ask them to stay available, meet you, or help you get home safely without being alone.
4. **Ask nearby staff or security for a practical barrier.** Ask for an escort to your car, transit stop, or rideshare; ask staff not to let an unknown visitor through; and point out any person or vehicle description you are worried about.
5. **Start a simple incident log.** Record the date, time, location, what happened, what made it feel connected to earlier encounters, the person or vehicle description, witnesses, and any nearby cameras. Keep the log somewhere the person cannot access, and ask staff or security to preserve relevant camera footage if they can.
6. **Save evidence without engaging.** Save messages, DMs, emails, voicemails, call logs, photos, screenshots, notes, gifts, and delivery packaging. Do not reply just to create more proof.
7. **Make today’s route harder to predict.** Stay on main roads, avoid isolated shortcuts, and ask someone to meet you or stay on the phone while you travel. Use a staffed location as your next stop if you are unsure whether you are still being followed.
8. **Reduce easy digital tracking.** Turn off unnecessary location sharing, stop posting real-time updates, review social media privacy, and secure your email and key accounts. If you think the person can see your phone, accounts, or device activity, use a safer device or account first to contact help.
9. **Consider a non-emergency report or victim advocate.** If the immediate danger has passed, contact local law enforcement, campus security, workplace security, or a local victim service provider and explain the repeated “coincidental” encounters and why they make you fear for your safety. Ask how to submit your log and how to get a report or incident number.
10. **Ask locally about legal distance if you want it.** Protective order names and rules vary by state and county. A practical next step is to ask a local victim advocate, court self-help center, court clerk, or legal aid program what type of order may exist where you live.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to move, change jobs, change schools, or relocate permanently.
- You do not need to prove the person’s intent before seeking help or making a record.
- You do not need to contact the person, get closure, or explain yourself to them.
- You do not need a perfect timeline today; start with the incidents you remember and add details later.

## Important reassurance
A series of “small” encounters can feel frightening because each one may seem explainable on its own. Writing things down, telling someone, and creating barriers around your normal places are calm protective steps that do not require you to solve the whole situation today.

## Scope note
These are first steps to help you reach a safer pause, reduce exposure, and create a usable record. Later decisions about advocacy support, workplace or school measures, housing, technology safety, police reports, or court options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel at risk right now, prioritize immediate safety and contact emergency services.
