# What to do if… you are suddenly moved to a different team or manager after a conflict at work (USA)
## Short answer
Pause before resigning, refusing the transfer, or sending an angry response. Ask for the reassignment details in writing and make a factual timeline of what changed and what happened immediately before the transfer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign, walk out, or refuse the transfer on the spot while you are shocked or angry.
- Do not send heated emails, threats, or posts about the conflict.
- Do not sign an acknowledgment, warning, or separation document before reading it carefully and requesting a copy.
- Do not rely on verbal promises about your role, pay, schedule, or reporting line.
- Do not confront the person involved alone if the conversation could become unsafe or escalate the conflict.
- Do not remove confidential workplace files or records you are not allowed to access or keep.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the transfer details in writing.** You can say: “Please send me the reassignment details in writing so I can review them.”
2. **Ask HR or the appropriate employer contact to confirm the practical changes.** Ask about:
   - the new supervisor or reporting line and the start date
   - whether the transfer is temporary and whether there is a review date
   - your job title, core duties, priorities, and evaluation criteria
   - your pay, classification, hours, schedule, location, and remote-work status
   - handover expectations, deadlines, and ownership of current projects
3. **Write a dated timeline for yourself.** Record the conflict briefly and factually, including dates, witnesses, any complaint or report you made or participated in, and the reassignment or other changes that followed. Keep copies of notices, emails, calendar invitations, and messages that you can lawfully access and keep.
4. **Ask for the reason and process in writing.** Request a short meeting with HR or the appropriate employer contact. Ask whether the reassignment is temporary, performance-related, part of a review, or based on another stated business reason. After the meeting, send a brief factual email confirming your understanding.
5. **Raise a timing concern carefully if it applies.** If the reassignment followed a discrimination or harassment report, participation in an investigation, a request such as a disability or religious accommodation request, group action about working conditions, or a safety report, you can write: “I am concerned that the timing of this reassignment may be connected to my report or participation on [date]. Please confirm the business reason for the change.”
6. **Check any applicable deadline promptly.** Do not assume that an internal meeting, grievance, or review pauses an outside filing deadline. Depending on what happened, the relevant route may be the EEOC, a state or local fair-employment agency, an OSHA-administered whistleblower process, or the NLRB for some covered workers acting together about working conditions. NLRB coverage varies, and protected concerted activity can apply with or without a union. Federal employees generally have a different EEO process, so check the agency process promptly.
7. **Flag any immediate hardship caused by the transfer.** Tell HR or the appropriate employer contact promptly if the change creates an urgent schedule, accessibility, location, remote-work, or safety problem. Ask what options are available while the situation is reviewed.
8. **Contact your union representative if you have one.** Ask the representative to review the reassignment notice and any meeting request before you respond.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether the transfer proves retaliation or unfair treatment. First get the written details and timeline in order.
- Writing a long narrative or sending a large bundle of records. Start with a short factual timeline and keep the originals safe.
- Negotiating a return to the old team, an apology, or another long-term outcome.
- Deciding whether to stay in the job long term.
- Filing a detailed external complaint today, unless a deadline is close. Checking the applicable deadline should not wait.

## Important reassurance
A reassignment after a conflict does not by itself establish why the decision was made or what will happen next. You can slow the situation down, get the practical details in writing, and preserve your options without deciding everything immediately.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need advice from an employment attorney, union representative, or another appropriate specialist, depending on the facts and the applicable deadline.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or need urgent police, fire department, or ambulance assistance, call 911.
