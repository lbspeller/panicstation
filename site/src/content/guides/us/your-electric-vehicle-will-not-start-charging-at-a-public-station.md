# What to do if… your electric vehicle will not start charging at a public station (USA)

## Short answer
Make the situation safe first, then try one clean restart of the charging session: stop the session, unplug, wait briefly, plug in firmly, and start again. If it still will not charge, call the support number shown on the charger or in the network app; they may be able to remote-start, reset, or direct you to another working stall.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force the connector, pry the latch, or put anything into the charge port.
- Do not touch damaged charging equipment, exposed wires, melted parts, or a connector that is heat-damaged, giving off heat, or was unusually hot when you already handled it.
- Do not keep rapid-fire retrying; repeated failed attempts can make the session harder to untangle.
- Do not stay in a traffic lane, isolated spot, or unsafe area just to keep troubleshooting.
- Do not assume it is your vehicle, but only try another stall if it is safe and the equipment looks intact.
- Do not ignore smoke, sparks, crackling, burning smell, or signs of fire.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If you are exposed to traffic, poorly lit, or too close to damaged equipment, move yourself and the vehicle to the safest nearby position before troubleshooting.

2. **Check for immediate danger.** If there is smoke, sparks, crackling, unusual heat, melted plastic, exposed wiring, or a burning smell, step back, keep others away, do not touch the equipment, and call **911** if there is fire or immediate danger.

3. **Do one clean charging reset.**
   - Stop or end the session on the charger screen or in the app.
   - Unplug the connector.
   - Wait 15–30 seconds.
   - Plug in again firmly until the connector is fully seated.
   - Start the session again and watch whether the charger and car both show charging, not just “ready” or “available.”

4. **Check the simple vehicle-side blockers.**
   - Put the car in Park.
   - Unlock the vehicle once, because some cars lock or release the connector through the central locking system.
   - Check the dash or app for a charge limit already reached, scheduled charging, departure timer, battery temperature warning, or charge-port error.
   - If your vehicle has a “restart charging” or charge-port reset option, try it once.

5. **Check the simple station-side blockers.**
   - Confirm you selected the connector you are actually using, such as J1772, CCS, NACS, or another available connector for your vehicle.
   - Confirm payment or account authorization has completed and the session has moved beyond “authorized,” “ready,” or “available.”
   - Try one other normal activation method if available, such as the app, card reader, RFID card, or vehicle-based automatic start.

6. **Try one controlled swap if it is safe.**
   - Move to another stall at the same site if one is available.
   - Use a different cable or connector only if it is meant for your vehicle.
   - If your battery is low and another compatible charger is nearby, choose the shortest safe move rather than spending too long at one failed unit.

7. **Call the charging network’s support number early.**
   - Use the number on the charger, in the app, or on the network’s website.
   - Give the site address, charger or stall ID, connector type, vehicle make and model, screen message, and any error code.
   - Ask whether they can remote-start the session, reset the stall, confirm whether the stall is out of service, or direct you to a working charger.

8. **Preserve range while you sort it out.**
   - Reduce heavy cabin heat or air conditioning if you can do so safely.
   - Avoid unnecessary driving around the site.
   - Use your map or charging app to identify the nearest compatible backup charger before moving.

9. **Arrange recovery if charging still will not start.**
   - Call roadside assistance through your vehicle manufacturer, insurer, auto club, rental company, or fleet contact.
   - Say that the vehicle will not accept a charge after a reset and a safe stall swap, and say how much range remains.
   - Ask for EV-capable towing or mobile charging if available in your area.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the onboard charger right now.
- You do not need to buy an adapter, cable, or replacement part while you are stranded.
- You do not need to dispute a failed authorization or billing hold on the spot.
- You can report the station, request a refund, review receipts, or book a vehicle inspection after you are safe and have enough charge or recovery arranged.

## Important reassurance
This can happen even when a charger appears to be working. Many failures are caused by the session start, payment, connector seating, vehicle settings, or one bad stall, so a calm reset, one safe stall swap, and a support call are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, such as a refund request, station report, warranty claim, or vehicle repair check, may need help from the charging network, vehicle manufacturer, roadside assistance provider, or a qualified EV technician.

## Important note
This guide is general information for immediate first steps, not professional electrical, mechanical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or medical advice. If you suspect electrical danger, fire, vehicle damage, flooding, smoke, sparking, or a burning smell, stop troubleshooting and prioritize distance, safety, and emergency help.
