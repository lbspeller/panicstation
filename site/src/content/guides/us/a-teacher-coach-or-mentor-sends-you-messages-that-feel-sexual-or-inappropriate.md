# What to do if… a teacher, coach, or mentor sends you messages that feel sexual or inappropriate (USA)
## Short answer
Move the conversation out of private contact and reach a safe person. You do not need to work out the sender’s intent before asking for support.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep replying because you feel pressured to be polite.
- Do not meet them alone to discuss the messages.
- Do not move the conversation to a hidden or disappearing-message app.
- Do not share screenshots widely or post them on social media.
- Do not feel that you have to protect their reputation or manage their reaction.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Stop replying if you can. Do not meet the teacher, coach, or mentor alone. If contact is needed for school, sport, or another activity, use an official channel and include another adult where possible.
2. **Tell one safe person.** This could be a parent, guardian, relative, another trusted adult, or a support person who can stay with you while you decide what to do next.
3. **Ask about privacy before sharing details if that matters to you.** Some school staff and other adults may need to share child-safety concerns under state law or policy.
4. **Keep only what is already there if it feels safe and easy.** You may keep the messages you already received or save a screenshot, then stop engaging.
5. **Choose a support route when you are ready.** At a school or college, you can ask for the Title IX Coordinator or another designated support contact. For a club or sports program, you can ask for its safety or misconduct contact. If the activity is part of the U.S. Olympic and Paralympic Movement, the U.S. Center for SafeSport has a report-a-concern route.
6. **Use urgent help for an emergency.** If anyone is in immediate danger or needs urgent police, fire, or ambulance help, call 911. For a child-safety concern that is not an immediate emergency, a safe adult can use the official state reporting-number directory or contact Childhelp for help finding the right local route.
7. **Use specialist support if it would help.** RAINN offers hotline support from trained support specialists. Ask about privacy before sharing identifying information if that matters to you. Childhelp can also help someone talk through a child-safety concern and work out where local reporting options are.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to make a formal report.
- You do not have to work out whether the sender meant the messages as a joke, flirting, or something else.
- You do not have to write a detailed statement or gather more messages.
- You do not have to make a long-term decision about the school, team, activity, or relationship right now.

## Important reassurance
Sexualized, secretive, or uncomfortable messages from an adult in a position of trust are a valid reason to step back and ask for support. You are not responsible for managing the adult’s boundaries, reputation, or response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: create a safer pause, bring in support, and avoid private contact. Later decisions about school processes, organization processes, or other options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes and privacy limits can vary by state, school, college, and organization.
