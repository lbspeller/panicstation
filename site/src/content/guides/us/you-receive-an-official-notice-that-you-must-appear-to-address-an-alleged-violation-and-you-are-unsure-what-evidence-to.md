# What to do if… you receive an official notice that you must appear to address an alleged violation and you are unsure what evidence to gather (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the notice through an official court or agency website, preserve all digital and physical records immediately, and do not discuss the allegations with anyone until you have secured legal representation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice or miss the date; missing a court appearance can result in an immediate warrant for your arrest (bench warrant) or a default judgment against you.
- Do not call the phone number provided on the paper if you suspect a scam; use an independent search to find the official clerk of court's office.
- Do not open attachments or click links in an "official notice" received via email, as courts typically serve notice via certified mail or professional process servers.
- Do not delete any messages, call logs, location history, or social media posts, even if you believe they are irrelevant or make you look bad.
- Do not contact the "other side" or the alleged victim to explain your side or try to "work it out" privately.
- Do not post about the notice or the underlying incident on any social media platform or public forum.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific document type and the issuing authority.** Look for the header to see if it is from a Federal Court (U.S. District Court), a State/County Court, or an administrative agency (e.g., DMV, IRS, or Immigration Court/EOIR).
   - Locate the Case Number (or Docket Number).
   - Locate the specific date, time, and courtroom/location for the appearance.
2. **Verify the document's legitimacy through an independent channel.** Search for the official ".gov" website of the court named on the notice.
   - Use the court’s online case search tool (or PACER for federal cases) to see if the case number exists.
   - Call the Clerk of the Court directly using the number on the official website to confirm your name is on the docket.
3. **Establish an immediate "Evidence Hold" on all relevant data.** Stop any auto-delete settings on your phone, email, or messaging apps.
   - **Take screenshots of all digital communications** related to the time period mentioned in the notice, including timestamps.
   - **Save original copies of photos or videos** without applying filters, cropping, or editing the metadata.
   - **Locate financial records** such as bank statements, receipts, or transaction confirmations that place you at a specific location or prove a transaction occurred.
4. **Determine if you are eligible for court-appointed counsel.** In criminal cases where jail time is a possibility, you have a right to a lawyer under the 6th Amendment.
   - **Contact the Clerk’s Office** or check the court's website for "Indigency Guidelines" or "Public Defender Application."
   - **Prepare financial documentation** (tax returns, pay stubs, or proof of public benefits) required to prove you cannot afford a private attorney.
5. **Create a factual, chronological timeline for your future lawyer.** Write down exactly what happened, including dates, times, and locations, in a private document (labeled "Confidential - For Attorney").
   - Include a list of potential witnesses with their names and contact information if known.
   - Do not share this document with anyone other than your legal counsel.
6. **Gather your "Day One" court materials.** Prepare a physical folder to bring to your first appearance.
   - Include the original notice and any attachments.
   - Include a valid government-issued photo ID (driver's license or passport).
   - Include any financial documents required for your public defender application.
7. **If the notice is for Immigration Court (EOIR), check the automated system.** Use the EOIR Case Status portal or call the automated hotline (800-898-7180) using your Alien Registration Number (A-Number) to confirm hearing details.

## What can wait
- You do not need to gather "character letters" or testimonials from friends and family today.
- You do not need to decide whether you will plead "guilty" or "not guilty" before speaking to your assigned lawyer.
- You do not need to hire an expert witness or private investigator immediately.
- You do not need to respond to the substance of the allegations in writing before your first appearance.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a formal notice is a high-stress event, and feeling a sense of panic or confusion is a standard physiological response. The legal system is procedural; by verifying the notice, preserving evidence, and remaining silent until you have a lawyer, you are taking the most effective steps to protect your rights.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and evidence preservation for individuals who have received a formal notice to appear in a U.S. court or agency. It does not provide long-term legal strategy or address specific state-level nuances of evidence admissibility.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding "Notice to Appear" and evidence preservation vary significantly by state and case type (criminal, civil, or administrative). If you are facing criminal charges, you should consult with a licensed attorney immediately.
