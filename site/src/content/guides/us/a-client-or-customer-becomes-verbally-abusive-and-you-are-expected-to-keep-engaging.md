# What to do if… a client or customer becomes verbally abusive and you are expected to keep engaging (USA)

## Short answer
Do not provide full medical records or portal screenshots to your direct manager. State that you will provide the legally required documentation—focused on work restrictions rather than a diagnosis—directly to Human Resources or the designated leave administrator.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hand over an unredacted medical file, full history, or "everything from the doctor" in the heat of the moment.
- Do not feel obligated to disclose a specific diagnosis label if the workplace need can be met by describing functional limitations and expected duration.
- Do not engage in a detailed health discussion in a public space or hallway; move the conversation to a private, written format.
- Do not ignore the request entirely, as this can be interpreted as a failure to cooperate with the interactive process or leave requirements.
- Do not assume "HIPAA" prevents an employer from asking; HIPAA regulates your health provider, while the ADA and FMLA govern what an employer can specifically require and how they must store it.

## What to do now
1. **Request the specific purpose of the inquiry in writing.**  
   **Ask the manager to clarify if the request is for an FMLA leave certification, an ADA reasonable accommodation, or a "fitness-for-duty" safety assessment.**
2. **Redirect the information flow to Human Resources (HR).**  
   **Inform your manager that you are happy to cooperate with the request but will be submitting the medical documentation directly to HR to protect your privacy.**
   - If your company lacks an HR department, ask for the person designated to handle confidential medical and leave records.
3. **Obtain the correct forms for the specific situation.**  
   **Ask HR for the official FMLA certification form (e.g., WH-380-E) or the standard ADA accommodation request paperwork used by your organization.**
4. **Instruct your health care provider to focus on functional limitations.**  
   **Ask your provider to complete the paperwork by detailing what job tasks you cannot perform and any necessary accommodations, rather than providing a narrative history of your condition.**
   - For FMLA, the provider must include "sufficient medical facts" to establish a serious health condition, but a specific diagnosis is not strictly required.
5. **Verify your right to a "cure" period if documentation is rejected.**  
   **If the employer claims your documentation is "insufficient," remind them that under 2026 regulations, they must specify in writing what is missing and give you at least 7 calendar days to fix it.**
6. **Confirm that your information will be stored in a separate file.**  
   **Remind the employer (if necessary) that the ADA requires all medical information to be kept in a confidential medical file that is separate from your general personnel file.**
7. **Maintain a personal log of all interactions.**  
   **Save copies of every email and note the date, time, and content of every verbal conversation regarding your health information.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to file a formal complaint or lawsuit.
- You do not need to disclose the specific name of your medication or doctor.
- You do not need to agree to the first accommodation the employer suggests; the interactive process is a dialogue.
- You do not need to provide "extra" information beyond what is requested on the official DOL or company forms.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of panic or intrusion when your employer asks for health details. These laws exist precisely because there is a power imbalance. By moving the process to HR and using official forms, you are not "being difficult"—you are following the standard professional protocol designed to protect both the company and your privacy.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling an immediate request for medical data. It does not cover the specifics of long-term disability claims, complex litigation, or the nuances of state-specific "Paid Family Leave" programs.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and is not legal advice. Rights and procedures vary significantly based on company size (e.g., FMLA requires 50+ employees within 75 miles) and state law. If you feel you are facing immediate retaliation or termination, consult an employment attorney or your union representative.
