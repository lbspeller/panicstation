# What to do if… you have a fever and your heart rate feels unusually fast for the temperature (USA)

## Short answer
**If your heart is racing at rest while you have a fever and you feel severely unwell, treat it as a medical emergency and call 911 immediately.** For stable symptoms, seek same-day medical triage via an urgent care center or your primary clinic's nurse line to rule out sepsis or severe dehydration.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "sweat out" the fever through exercise, saunas, or excessive layers of blankets; this increases the risk of dehydration and cardiovascular strain.
- Do not exceed the maximum daily dosage of acetaminophen or ibuprofen as listed on the product packaging.
- Do not take multiple medications without checking the ingredients; many "all-in-one" cold and flu remedies already contain acetaminophen.
- Do not ignore a rapid pulse simply because your temperature is only slightly elevated; the combination of a high heart rate at rest and feeling "extremely ill" is a significant warning sign.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you are experiencing dizziness, confusion, or chest pain; call 911 for transport.

## What to do now
1. **Take immediate readings if you are able to do so safely.**
   - **Measure your temperature** and note the exact time.
   - **Check your resting heart rate** by counting your pulse for 15 seconds and multiplying by 4.
   - **Assess yourself for red flags** including new confusion, shortness of breath, chest pain, inability to stay awake, or blue-tinted lips and skin.
2. **Call 911 if emergency symptoms are present.**
   - **Request emergency medical services immediately** if you have chest pressure, severe difficulty breathing, fainting, or are unable to interact normally.
   - **Explicitly state "I am concerned about sepsis"** to the dispatcher if these symptoms followed a known or suspected infection (such as a UTI, skin wound, or cough).
3. **Go to an Emergency Room if you are rapidly worsening.**
   - **Seek emergency care** if you feel "extremely ill," have severe shaking chills (rigors), or have significantly decreased urine output.
   - **Ask a household member or neighbor to drive you** if you are stable enough for a car but too unwell to drive yourself.
4. **Stabilize your body while waiting for care.**
   - **Sit or lie down immediately** to reduce the workload on your heart and prevent falls from dizziness.
   - **Move slowly** if you must change positions; avoid standing up quickly as this can cause a sudden drop in blood pressure.
   - **Take small, frequent sips of water** or an electrolyte drink if you are not vomiting; do not gulp large amounts.
5. **Prepare a concise summary for the medical team.**
   - **Write down your current temperature and heart rate** to show the nurse or paramedic upon arrival.
   - **Identify the suspected source of infection** if known, such as "I have had a painful cough for three days" or "I have a wound that looks red."

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the specific cause of the fever (viral vs. bacterial) right now.
- You do not need to continue monitoring your vitals once you have contacted emergency services or reached a clinic.
- You do not need to decide on long-term work or child-care arrangements until you have been medically stabilized.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for the heart rate to increase slightly with a fever, but it is also normal to feel alarmed when your heart is pounding or racing while you are resting. Noticing that "something feels wrong" is a reliable cue to seek professional assessment.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions. It is not a substitute for clinical diagnosis or treatment for sepsis, tachycardia, or underlying infections.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute medical advice. Symptoms such as a high heart rate with fever can indicate life-threatening conditions like sepsis which require immediate hospital intervention.
