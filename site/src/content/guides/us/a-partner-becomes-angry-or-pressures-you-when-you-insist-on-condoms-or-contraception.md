# What to do if… a partner becomes angry or pressures you when you insist on condoms or contraception (USA)

## Short answer
Pause anything sexual. Take the anger or pressure seriously, and move toward a safer pause before deciding anything else.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to sex without the protection you want just to keep the peace.
- Do not keep negotiating alone if you feel afraid or intimidated.
- Do not hand over your contraception, phone, or medical information to prove anything.
- Do not dismiss escalating behavior such as threats, grabbing, blocked exits, or a taken phone.
- Do not blame yourself if you freeze, comply, or need to leave without explaining.

## What to do now
1. Stop the sexual situation if you can do so safely. Move toward a safer pause or a place with other people if that feels safer.
2. If you are in immediate danger, call 911 and follow the call-taker’s instructions.
3. Contact someone you trust and ask them to stay on the phone, collect you, or help you get to a safer place. You may use any brief reason to step away if that feels safer.
4. Take your contraception, phone, identification, and essential medication with you if you can do so safely and without delaying your move to safety. If you are worried your phone is monitored, use a safer device to contact support when possible.
5. If sex happened without the protection you wanted, a condom was refused or removed, or contraception may have been interfered with, contact a clinic or pharmacy as soon as possible. Emergency contraceptive pills should generally be taken as soon as possible within 5 days, and a clinic can explain whether an emergency contraceptive pill or a copper IUD may be appropriate.
6. Seek medical care promptly if you have an injury, pregnancy concern, or sexually transmitted infection concern. If sexual contact happened without your consent and you might want a forensic exam, consider avoiding washing or changing clothes before medical care, only if that feels safe.
7. Contact RAINN or the National Domestic Violence Hotline for confidential support and help thinking through the next safe step. You do not need to decide whether to report anything before contacting support.
8. If you feel overwhelmed or unsafe with your own thoughts, call, text, or chat with 988.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to call the situation assault, abuse, or coercion.
- You do not need to confront your partner or get them to admit anything.
- You do not need to make a major relationship decision today.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to report anything.

## Important reassurance
Wanting condoms or contraception is reasonable. Anger, intimidation, threats, or pressure about your sexual boundaries are not your fault. Freezing or going along with something to reduce danger does not mean you caused the situation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about safety, health care, reporting, or the relationship may need support from a specialist service or healthcare professional.

## Important note
This guide provides general first-step information for the USA. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
