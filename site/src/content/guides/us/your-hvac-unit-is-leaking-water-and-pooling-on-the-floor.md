# What to do if… your HVAC unit is leaking water and pooling on the floor (USA)

## Short answer
Turn the HVAC system off at the thermostat and keep people, pets, plugs, cords, outlets, and electrical panels away from the water. If electrical equipment or circuits are wet or close to the water, shut power off only from a dry, safe position; otherwise keep away and get qualified help.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep the HVAC system running while water is leaking or pooling.
- Don’t touch switches, plugs, the air handler, the condensate pump, or the breaker panel while standing in water or with wet hands.
- Don’t remove covers or access panels while there is active leaking, dampness, or possible wet electrical equipment.
- Don’t enter standing water to reach a shutoff, breaker, or service switch.
- Don’t push wire, coat hangers, or sharp objects into the condensate line.
- Don’t pour harsh chemicals into the drain line.
- Don’t use a regular household vacuum around water unless it is designed for wet pickup.

## What to do now
1. **Switch the HVAC system off at the thermostat.** Set cooling and heating to off so the unit stops making more condensate.

2. **Deal with electrical risk before cleanup.**
   - If you can reach it from a dry, safe place, turn off the HVAC breaker or service switch. It may be labelled “Furnace,” “Air Handler,” “AC,” or “HVAC.”
   - If water is near outlets, cords, the breaker panel, the air handler’s electrical area, or the condensate pump cord, do not touch them.
   - If you cannot shut power off safely, keep people away and call an electrician. Call your utility only if outside service equipment or a utility-directed shutoff is involved.

3. **Contain the water without touching the unit’s electrical parts.**
   - Put a bucket or tray under the drip if you can do that safely.
   - Use towels to slow the spread away from outlets, cords, walls, stored items, and finished flooring.
   - Move plugged-in items and valuables only if you can do it without stepping in water or touching wet electrical equipment.

4. **Make a quick no-panel visual check.**
   - Look for water under or around the air handler, furnace, indoor AC coil area, or condensate pump.
   - If the condensate line is visible, check only the easy-to-see end, such as where it exits outside, reaches a floor drain, or connects near a condensate pump.
   - If a condensate pump is present, do not handle its cord or tubing if anything is damp. Just note whether the pump reservoir looks full, overflowing, unplugged, tilted, or disconnected.

5. **Call the right help.**
   - Call an HVAC technician for a suspected clogged condensate line, overflowing drain pan, loose drain tube, float switch trip, or failed condensate pump.
   - Call an electrician before restoring power if water contacted outlets, cords, wiring, the breaker panel area, or the unit’s electrical compartment.

6. **If you rent, notify the landlord or property manager after the immediate electrical risk is controlled.** Say the HVAC condensate line or drain area is leaking and water is collecting nearby.

7. **If someone may have been shocked, or there is sparking, smoke, burning smell, or fire, call 911.** Keep everyone away from the wet area while you wait.

8. **Take photos after the system is off and the area is safe.** Photograph the pooling water, the visible drain line or pump, and any affected floor, wall, or belongings.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to identify the exact clog, pump fault, or drain pan problem right now.
- You don’t need to restart the system briefly to test it.
- You don’t need to remove panels, take the unit apart, or clear the line yourself.
- You don’t need to decide on repairs, replacement, insurance, or long-term cleanup until the leak is stopped and the area is electrically safe.

## Important reassurance
Condensate leaks are a common HVAC problem, and many are caused by a blocked drain, overflowing pan, loose tubing, or pump issue. The useful first steps are simple: stop the system, avoid wet electrical hazards, contain the water, and get the right repair help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and limit property damage. Later decisions about repair, cleanup, electrical inspection, landlord responsibilities, or insurance may need help from an HVAC technician, electrician, property manager, or insurer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, electrical, HVAC, insurance, medical, or other professional advice. If you cannot make the area safe without touching water or electrical equipment, keep away and use qualified help.
