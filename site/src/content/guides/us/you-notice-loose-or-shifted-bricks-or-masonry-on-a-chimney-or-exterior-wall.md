# What to do if… you notice loose or shifted bricks or masonry on a chimney or exterior wall (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as a falling-debris hazard: keep people and pets out of the area underneath and call a qualified professional, licensed where your area requires it. Call 911 now if bricks or mortar are actively falling, the area cannot be kept clear, or the hazard could hit someone soon.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t climb onto the roof, use a ladder, or touch bricks to test whether they move.
- Don’t remove a loose brick or chip out mortar to “check behind it”.
- Don’t make a quick glue, caulk, foam, or mortar patch to hold it in place.
- Don’t use the fireplace, wood stove, or appliance venting through that chimney or flue if the chimney may be involved.
- Don’t let anyone stand, park, sit, or walk directly below the loose or shifted area.
- Don’t pressure-wash, hammer, drill, or chisel near the area; vibration can dislodge more material.

## What to do now
1. **Make a no-go zone.** Keep people and pets away from the area under and next to the loose or shifted masonry, including entrances, walkways, decks, patios, driveways, and side yards. Use cones, chairs, tape, or another clear marker if you can do that without going under the hazard.
2. **Move objects out of the drop zone only if it is safe.** Move cars, trash bins, patio furniture, grills, and anything breakable away from the area without walking under the loose masonry.
3. **Stop using the chimney or flue.** Do not light a fire, use a wood stove, or use any appliance venting through that chimney or flue until a qualified person says it is safe. If a carbon monoxide alarm sounds, leave the building and call 911 from outside.
4. **Decide if this is an emergency right now.** Call 911 if bricks or mortar are actively falling, the chimney or wall is visibly leaning more, an exit is affected, or falling debris could hit someone on a sidewalk, road, shared driveway, or neighboring property.
5. **If the public could be affected, contact local authorities.** If the hazard reaches a public sidewalk, roadway, alley, shared access, or neighboring property and there is not an immediate 911-level danger, contact your local building department or code enforcement. Some places use 311 for this, but not all do.
6. **Get an urgent professional assessment.** For chimney masonry, call a qualified chimney professional or mason, licensed where required locally. For a bulging, bowing, separating, leaning, or cracking exterior wall, ask a structural engineer or your local building department what kind of assessment is needed.
7. **Ask for the first safety action, not the full repair plan.** Say: “Bricks or masonry have shifted and may fall. I need to know whether the area must be shored, blocked off, removed, or made safe before repairs.”
8. **Document from a safe distance.** Take photos or video from the ground without using a ladder. Note when you first noticed it and any recent trigger, such as a storm, freeze-thaw weather, impact, nearby construction, or earthquake.
9. **If you rent, notify the landlord or property manager in writing now.** Describe it as loose or shifted chimney or exterior-wall masonry and a possible falling-debris hazard. Keep the no-go zone in place while you wait for a response.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now, such as moisture, mortar failure, settlement, corroded ties, storm damage, or freeze-thaw damage.
- You do not need to choose the final repair today; first priority is making the area safe.
- Matching brick, finish, paint, trim, repointing appearance, and other cosmetic decisions can wait until after the safety assessment.

## Important reassurance
Loose or shifted masonry is alarming, but you do not have to solve the whole repair in the first few minutes. The safest first move is simple: keep people out of the drop zone, stop using any affected chimney or flue, and get the hazard assessed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need a qualified mason, chimney professional, structural engineer, local building department, landlord, insurer, or other specialist depending on the building and the damage.

## Important note
This is general information, not engineering, legal, emergency-service, insurance, or professional advice. If you believe falling debris or collapse is an immediate hazard, call emergency services.
