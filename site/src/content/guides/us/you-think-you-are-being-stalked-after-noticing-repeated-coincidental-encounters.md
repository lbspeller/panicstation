# What to do if… you think you are being stalked after noticing repeated “coincidental” encounters (USA)

## Short answer
Move immediately to a crowded, well-lit public space or a police station and notify a trusted contact of your location. If you are in immediate danger, call 911 or text 911 if calls are unsafe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not lead a suspected stalker to your home, workplace, or an isolated area to "test" if they are following you.
- Do not confront the person directly or attempt to "clear things up" in a private setting.
- Do not delete any evidence including unwanted gifts, letters, emails, texts, or digital messages.
- Do not post your current location, future plans, or daily routines on social media.
- Do not assume you must wait for a physical threat before seeking help or starting a record.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause immediately.** Go inside a busy store, restaurant, or a public building with security personnel. If you are driving, do not go home; instead, drive to a police station lobby or a highly populated, well-lit business parking lot and stay in your vehicle with doors locked until help arrives or you can safely enter.
2. **Contact emergency services if you feel threatened.** Call 911 if you are in immediate danger. If you cannot safely speak, text 911 with your exact location and the nature of the emergency; if texting is unavailable in your area, you should receive a "bounce-back" message, in which case you must attempt a voice call or find another way to signal for help.
3. **Notify one trusted person of your situation.** Tell them exactly where you are, who you suspect is following you, and what your next move will be. Ask them to stay on the phone with you while you move between locations or wait for assistance.
4. **Initiate an incident documentation log.** Record the date, time, location, and a description of the person or vehicle involved in every encounter. 
   - Note any witnesses who were present.
   - Describe exactly what the person did or said.
   - Keep this log in a secure location the stalker cannot access, such as a password-protected digital file or with a trusted friend.
5. **Vary your travel routes and routines for the remainder of the day.** Avoid using your standard path home or to work if you believe you are being watched. Use main roads with heavy traffic and avoid shortcuts through alleys or quiet residential streets.
6. **Inform security at your "fixed" locations.** Tell building management, workplace security, or gym staff about the repeated encounters. Provide a description of the individual and any vehicle they use, and ask for an escort to your car or transit stop.
7. **Secure your digital presence.** Disable location-sharing services on all mobile devices and social media apps. Change passwords for your primary email and social media accounts using a device you believe is secure and unmonitored.
8. **Contact the VictimConnect Resource Center for guidance.** Call or text 855-484-2846 (855-4-VICTIM) to speak with a specialist who can provide confidential referrals and help with safety planning.
9. **File a non-emergency police report to establish a paper trail.** Visit a local precinct to report the pattern of "coincidental" encounters. Request a copy of the report and the officer’s name and badge number for your documentation log.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on permanent lifestyle changes, such as moving or quitting a job, right now.
- You do not need to have a "perfect" explanation for why the encounters are suspicious; the pattern itself is the evidence.
- You do not need to seek legal representation or file for a protective order in the first hour.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel uncertain or to wonder if you are overreacting when encounters seem coincidental. Recognizing a pattern and taking steps to increase your distance and documentation are practical safety measures, not signs of panic.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions. Long-term safety planning and legal remedies should be pursued with the help of professional advocates or law enforcement.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Stalking laws vary significantly by state; consult a local victim advocate or legal professional to understand specific statutes in your jurisdiction.
