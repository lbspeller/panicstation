# What to do if… you receive an unexpected collect call from a detention facility and you do not know who it is (USA)

## Short answer
Do not accept the call and listen for an automated prompt to block the number or simply hang up. Verify the caller's identity using official government inmate locators before providing any information or money.

## Do not do these things
- Don't press "1" or follow any prompt to "Accept" the call if you do not recognize the person's name.
- Don't provide personal details such as your Social Security Number, date of birth, or home address to "verify" your identity to the caller.
- Don't send money via gift cards, cryptocurrency, or wire transfers, even if told there is a medical or legal emergency.
- Don't trust the caller ID alone, as scammers can spoof facility numbers.
- Don't call back a phone number provided by the caller or the recording; only use numbers from official ".gov" or ".org" websites.

## What to do now
1. **Hang up or decline the call immediately to avoid connection charges.**  
   If you listen to the automated recording, many systems provide a specific digit (often "9" or "7") to press if you wish to block all future calls from that facility or inmate.
2. **Note the specific details provided in the automated announcement.**  
   Write down the name of the facility mentioned, the name of the Incarcerated People’s Communications Services (IPCS) provider (e.g., ViaPath/GTL, Securus, ICSolutions), and the date/time.
3. **Verify the inmate's status through an official locator tool.**  
   - For federal facilities, use the **Federal Bureau of Prisons (BOP) Inmate Locator**.
   - For state and local facilities, use the **VINE (Victim Information and Notification Everyday)** system or the specific state Department of Corrections (DOC) website.
   - Search by the name provided in the call to see if a person by that name is actually in custody at that location.
4. **Contact the facility’s main switchboard directly using a verified number.**  
   Find the facility's official phone number on a ".gov" website and ask to speak with the "Records" or "Booking" department to confirm if a specific person is currently housed there.
5. **Contact the IPCS provider to request a permanent block on your number.**  
   Identify the company handling the calls (usually stated in the recording or on your phone bill) and use their official website or customer service line to place a "Recipients Block" on your phone line.
   - Common providers include Securus (800-844-6591), ViaPath/GTL (877-650-4249), and ICSolutions (888-506-8407).
6. **File a complaint with the FCC if you suspect a scam or see unauthorized charges.**  
   Use the **FCC Consumer Complaint Center** (consumercomplaints.fcc.gov) and select "Phone" then "Incarcerated People" as the sub-issue to report billing fraud or predatory calling practices.
7. **Review your phone bill for third-party billing charges.**  
   Check your cellular or landline statement for "Premium" or "Collect" charges and contact your carrier to dispute any fees for calls you did not intentionally accept.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to provide bail or legal help right now.
- You do not need to respond to "urgent" requests for money to "keep the inmate safe."
- You do not need to worry about being arrested for refusing a collect call.
- You do not need to explain to the facility why you are blocking a number.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a call from a detention facility can be startling, but it does not mean you are in legal trouble. Scammers frequently use the high-pressure environment of the justice system to bypass logical checks; hanging up and using official search tools is the safest way to differentiate a real friend or family member from a fraud attempt.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and identity verification only. It does not provide legal advice for bail, criminal defense, or long-term communication management with an incarcerated individual.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is based on 2026 US federal regulations. Facility-level procedures vary significantly by county and state. If you are being harassed or threatened, contact your local law enforcement agency.
