# What to do if… you receive an unexpected collect call from a detention facility and you do not know who it is (USA)

## Short answer
Do not accept the call, or hang up if you already did. Verify independently through an official jail, prison, or court-related website or main switchboard before you share information, pay anything, or agree to help.

## Do not do these things
- Do not give your Social Security number, date of birth, address, employer, card number, bank details, passwords, or verification codes.
- Do not send money by gift card, cryptocurrency, wire transfer, payment app, or any urgent method the caller chooses.
- Do not trust a call-back number given by the caller, recording, text, or voicemail until you verify it on an official website.
- Do not rely on caller ID, a local-looking number, or official-sounding wording as proof that the call is real.
- Do not stay on the line if you feel pressured, threatened, rushed, or confused.
- Do not agree to receive money, move money, pass on messages, or do a “favor” for someone you cannot identify.

## What to do now
1. **End the call first.**  
   If you already accepted, say only: “I do not know who this is. I will verify independently.” Then hang up.

2. **Write down the details you already have.**  
   Note the date and time, the number displayed, any facility name, any inmate-calling company name, the caller’s stated name, and any booking number or register number mentioned.

3. **Check the facility claim through an official route.**  
   Search for the official website of the county jail, state corrections department, or federal Bureau of Prisons. Use only the main switchboard, official facility page, or official inmate locator you find yourself.

4. **Ask limited verification questions through the official number.**  
   If you reach a facility switchboard, ask whether they can confirm whether the named person is there, what information they can give to the public, and how call blocking works for unwanted inmate calls.

5. **Use a hard stop for suspicious payment demands.**  
   If anyone says you must pay by gift card, cryptocurrency, wire transfer, payment app, or immediately to avoid arrest, treat that as a scam sign and end contact.

6. **Check your phone account for charges.**  
   Look for collect-call, inmate-call, or third-party billing charges. Ask your phone carrier how to block collect calls, inmate calls, and third-party billing to your number.

7. **If the calls continue, use the provider or facility block process.**  
   Use the inmate-calling company name from the recording or bill to ask for a block and ask what dispute or refund process is available. If you do not know the provider, ask the facility’s main number who handles inmate calls.

8. **If it appears real and you may know the person, slow down.**  
   Before sending money, sharing personal details, accepting repeated calls, or doing any favor, consider speaking with a lawyer or another trusted person who is not caught up in the urgency.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to help the person.
- You do not need to pay anything before you verify who called and where they are.
- You do not need to call back immediately.
- You do not need to explain yourself to the caller.
- You do not need to solve the person’s legal, housing, money, or family situation right now.

## Important reassurance
An unexpected detention-facility call can feel alarming, especially when a recording sounds official. Hanging up and checking through official channels is a normal, safe way to protect yourself while leaving room to help later if the call is genuine.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The goal is to prevent fraud, avoid surprise charges, and verify identity safely; later decisions may need legal advice, family support, carrier help, or the facility’s formal process.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, professional, or safety advice. Practices vary by state, county, facility, phone carrier, and calling provider. If you feel threatened or in immediate danger, call 911.
