# What to do if… you return to where you parked and your vehicle is missing (USA)

## Short answer
First, make sure you are safe and check whether the vehicle was moved nearby. Then use posted tow information, an official local tow or impound lookup, 311 if available, or the local police non-emergency route before treating it as stolen.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t call 911 unless there is an immediate safety emergency.
- Don’t report it stolen until you have checked likely towing and relocation routes, unless there is clear evidence of theft or an immediate safety issue.
- Don’t pay or give personal information to someone who approaches you offering to “track it” unless you independently confirm they are the actual tow company or public agency involved.
- Don’t leave the area without photos of signs, curb markings, the lot entrance, and your exact parking location if it is safe to take them.
- Don’t delay once you confirm the tow yard or impound location; storage or administrative fees may increase with time.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safe pause and confirm the exact spot.**  
   Step away from traffic or conflict. Check that you are on the right block, level, entrance, or lot, especially in large garages, apartment complexes, hospitals, campuses, and shopping centers.

2. **Look nearby for a relocation.**  
   Walk the nearest legal parking areas if it is safe. Vehicles are sometimes moved nearby for construction, filming, crashes, street events, emergency access, temporary no-parking zones, or street work.

3. **Photograph the parking area before you leave.**  
   Take photos of curb paint, parking meters, temporary notices, tow-away signs, the lot entrance, posted rates or rules, and the place where the vehicle was parked.

4. **Use any posted tow information first.**  
   If there is a tow sign, call the posted company number. Ask for the vehicle location, release hours, total amount due, accepted payment types, required documents, and whether a police, parking-enforcement, or agency release is needed.

5. **Use official local lookup routes if there is no clear sign.**  
   Search your city or county’s official website for a towed vehicle or impound lookup. If your area has 311, ask for help locating a towed or relocated vehicle. Otherwise, call the local police non-emergency number or the precinct or station covering the place where you parked and ask whether a tow or relocation was reported.

6. **If it was on private property, contact the property controller.**  
   Call the apartment office, building management, lot operator, store, venue, campus security, or parking office. Ask which towing company they use and whether they authorised a tow from that exact location.

7. **Before travelling to the tow yard, confirm what to bring.**  
   Requirements vary. They often include photo ID and proof that you own or are authorised to collect the vehicle, such as registration, title, lease documents, rental agreement, or written authorisation. Some police-ordered or agency-ordered impounds may require a separate vehicle release before the tow company can release the vehicle.

8. **At the tow yard, keep the interaction simple and document it.**  
   Ask for the stated tow reason and an itemised receipt or written bill. Before driving away, photograph the vehicle from several sides and photograph any damage you believe is new.

9. **If official checks still cannot locate it, use the local stolen-vehicle process.**  
   If the tow company, property controller, official lookup, 311 where available, and local police non-emergency route cannot locate the vehicle, and you have reason to suspect theft, make a stolen-vehicle report through local law enforcement and contact your insurer.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to dispute the tow, complain, sue, or request a hearing.
- You do not need to argue about whether the tow was fair while you are trying to find the vehicle.
- You do not need to gather every possible document yet; first confirm the exact release requirements.
- You do not need to solve the whole problem at once. Focus on locate, confirm requirements, retrieve if possible, and keep records.

## Important reassurance
A missing vehicle is alarming, and it can feel like theft at first. Many situations turn out to be a tow, impound, or nearby relocation, and a calm sequence of checks usually gives you a clearer answer quickly.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to help you locate the vehicle, avoid scams, reduce fees where possible, and keep useful records. Later decisions about disputes, refunds, hearings, complaints, insurance, or legal options may need local specialist help because rules vary by state, city, county, property type, and tow type.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, insurance, professional, or law-enforcement advice. Follow current instructions from local authorities, the tow yard, your insurer, and any relevant court or agency process.
