# What to do if… your replacement bank card has not arrived and your current card is deactivated (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank or card issuer using a trusted route, report the replacement card as not received, and ask what they can do today to keep you able to pay for essentials or access cash if this is a deposit account.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t share one-time passcodes, online banking passwords, card security codes, or PINs with anyone who contacts you first.
- Don’t click “fix your delivery” links in unexpected texts or emails about your card.
- Don’t update your address through a link in a message. Use the bank’s app, statement phone number, or official website.
- Don’t keep retrying declined transactions over and over if the card is blocked or deactivated.
- Don’t assume a missing replacement card is just a mail delay. Treat it as a possible security issue until the issuer checks it.

## What to do now
1. **Contact the issuer through a trusted route.**
   - Use the phone number inside your banking app, on your statement, or on the issuer’s official website.
   - Say: **“My replacement card has not arrived, my current card is deactivated, and I need a safe way to pay for essentials today.”**

2. **Ask them to secure the missing card.**
   - Ask them to mark the replacement card as **not received**.
   - Ask whether they should cancel, block, or reissue the missing card.
   - Confirm the mailing address and ask whether any recent address, phone, email, device, or online banking changes were made.
   - Ask them to remove or review any mobile-wallet links, new devices, or access you do not recognise.

3. **Ask for the fastest safe access option they support.**
   - For a debit card or bank account, ask about cardless ATM access, in-branch cash withdrawal with government photo ID, or another same-day way to access your money.
   - For a credit card, ask whether a temporary digital card number or instant mobile-wallet card is available.
   - If card access is not available today, ask whether you can use bill pay, ACH transfer, or another bank-supported transfer route from an account you control.
   - Only ask a trusted person for temporary help if you are comfortable doing that and you can keep the arrangement simple.

4. **Protect essential payments for the next day or two.**
   - Focus first on housing, utilities, phone service, transport, medication, food, and childcare.
   - Move only the urgent payments you need to move now.
   - If a card-based payment is due today, contact that merchant and ask whether they can take ACH, bank bill pay, another card you already control, or a short extension.

5. **Check for fraud while you are still in the bank app or on the call.**
   - Review recent transactions, pending charges, alerts, new payees, and profile changes.
   - If you see an unauthorized electronic transfer from a bank account, report it to the bank or credit union immediately. Prompt notice can affect your protections under federal electronic transfer rules.
   - If this is a credit card, tell the issuer about any unauthorized charges and ask for their credit-card dispute process.

6. **Write down the essentials.**
   - Record the date and time you noticed the problem.
   - Write down how you contacted the issuer, the case or reference number, and what they said they would do.
   - Note any temporary access method they enabled and when the new replacement is expected.
   - If you suspect interception, ask for the fraud or security team. If the issue is a delivery or system problem and you are stuck, ask for a supervisor.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to solve the mail problem yourself before contacting the issuer.
- You don’t need to decide today whether to change banks or close accounts.
- You don’t need to file reports with multiple agencies unless you see fraud, identity-theft signs, or the issuer tells you a report is needed.
- You don’t need to update every subscription at once. Handle only the payments that protect your safety, housing, communication, and essentials.

## Important reassurance
A deactivated card with a missing replacement can feel like everything has stopped, but banks and card issuers deal with this situation often. The first safe move is to make the missing card unusable, check for signs of fraud, and get one temporary way to pay or access money.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the initial hours or day. If there are unauthorized transactions, identity-theft signs, or repeated access problems, you may need the issuer’s formal dispute process, a regulator complaint route, or specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Bank features, card replacement rules, branch withdrawal rules, digital card availability, and dispute procedures vary by institution and account type.
