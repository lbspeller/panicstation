# What to do if… police contact you asking you to come in for a “voluntary” interview (USA)

## Short answer
Do not attend the interview or answer questions until you have consulted with a private criminal defense lawyer. Politely decline the immediate request and state that you are invoking your right to remain silent.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t "just go in to clear things up"—statements made in voluntary interviews are admissible in court even without a Miranda warning.
- Don’t believe that being "not under arrest" means you are safe; police often use voluntary interviews to gather enough evidence for an arrest warrant.
- Don’t explain your side of the story over the phone, via text, or through social media messages.
- Don’t consent to searches of your phone, vehicle, or home "to show you have nothing to hide."
- Don’t bring family members or friends to the station to speak for you, as they can be subpoenaed as witnesses.
- Don’t guess or speculate if you do choose to speak; any inaccuracy can be charged as a separate crime (false statements).

## What to do now
1. **Identify the official and the agency making the request.** Ask for the officer's full name, badge number, department, and a direct desk phone number.
2. **Note the specific nature of the contact.** Document whether they called from a blocked number, left a business card at your residence, or approached you in plainclothes.
3. **State clearly that you are not answering questions without legal counsel.** Use a direct phrase such as: "I am exercising my right to remain silent and I will not answer questions without my lawyer present."
4. **Determine if you are currently being detained or if you are free to go.** If the contact is face-to-face, ask "Am I free to leave?" 
   - If the answer is yes, walk away immediately and do not engage in further "small talk."
   - If the answer is no, stop speaking entirely except to repeat "I want a lawyer."
5. **Verify the officer's identity through official channels.** Call the main non-emergency line of the police department or agency they claimed to be from and ask to be connected to that specific officer to confirm the request is legitimate.
6. **Contact a private criminal defense attorney immediately.** Provide them with the officer's contact information so the lawyer can handle all future communications on your behalf.
   - Use the American Bar Association (ABA) or your specific State Bar Association’s lawyer referral service if you do not have a lawyer.
7. **Secure your digital and physical privacy.** Ensure your phone and electronic devices are locked with a passcode (not biometric/face ID) and do not discuss the police contact on any digital platform.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you will eventually give a statement.
- You do not need to look for evidence or "alibi" documents before speaking to a lawyer.
- You do not need to worry about "looking guilty" by requesting a lawyer; this is a protected constitutional right that cannot be used as evidence of guilt in a trial.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel an intense urge to explain yourself or "fix" the situation by talking. Police are trained to build rapport and make the interview feel like a low-stakes conversation. Choosing to remain silent and wait for a lawyer is the standard, safest procedural response and does not indicate that you have done anything wrong.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate minutes and hours following a request for a voluntary interview. It does not cover grand jury subpoenas, witness testimony under immunity, or procedures once formal charges have been filed.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Jurisdiction-specific laws (such as "stop and identify" statutes) vary by state. Consult a licensed attorney in your jurisdiction for advice specific to your case.
