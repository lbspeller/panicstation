# What to do if… you are asked to sign a confidentiality agreement connected to a sexual misconduct complaint (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign the document immediately; request a physical or digital copy to take home for independent legal and specialist review. Federally enacted laws such as the Speak Out Act may limit the enforceability of such clauses, but professional advice is required to confirm your specific rights before you commit.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any document during the initial meeting under pressure or a "sign-now" deadline.
- Do not accept verbal assurances that the agreement "is just standard" or "won't be used against you."
- Do not assume that federal protections (like the Speak Out Act) automatically cancel out all parts of the agreement.
- Do not rely on your employer’s HR department or company lawyers for an explanation of your personal legal rights.
- Do not delete or move your personal notes about the incident to company-owned servers or devices.

## What to do now
1. **Request a copy of the full document and ask for a specific period to review it.** **Say: "I need to take this home to review it and consult with an advisor before I can consider signing."** 
   - If you are over age 40, federal law (OWBPA) generally requires that you be given at least 21 days to consider a severance agreement that waives age claims, and 7 days to revoke it after signing.
2. **Identify the exact nature of the document provided.** **Ask the representative to clarify in writing if this is a pre-dispute agreement (like an initial employment NDA), a settlement agreement for an active complaint, or a severance package.**
3. **Scan the text for high-consequence keywords.** **Look specifically for terms including "Confidentiality," "Non-Disclosure," "Non-Disparagement," "Arbitration," "Liquidated Damages," "Waiver of Claims," and "No Cooperation."** 
   - These terms indicate that you are being asked to limit your future speech or legal options.
4. **Determine if the agreement was presented before or after a dispute arose.** **Verify if the conduct you are concerned about occurred before or after you were asked to sign.** 
   - Under the federal Speak Out Act (2022), non-disclosure and non-disparagement clauses are judicially unenforceable if they were signed *before* a sexual assault or sexual harassment dispute arose.
5. **Check for arbitration clauses that might force your claim out of court.** **Note if the document requires you to resolve all disputes through private arbitration rather than a public trial.** 
   - The Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act (2022) allows you to choose to go to court for these specific claims even if you previously signed a "pre-dispute" arbitration agreement.
6. **Ensure the document does not attempt to block your access to medical or legal support.** **Verify that there is an explicit exception allowing you to discuss the matter with your attorney, medical professionals, or licensed counselors.** 
   - Legal agreements generally cannot lawfully prevent you from cooperating with law enforcement or administrative agencies like the EEOC.
7. **Contact a specialist support service for grounding and resource referrals.** **Call the RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-4673 or use their online chat for confidential guidance on navigating the emotional and procedural aspects of the situation.**
8. **Document the circumstances of the request to sign.** **Keep a private record of who asked you to sign, the date/time of the request, any deadlines mentioned, and exactly what was said about the consequences of not signing.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you want to file a lawsuit or a formal EEOC charge.
- You do not need to agree to "standard" language that you do not fully understand.
- You do not need to negotiate the dollar amount or specific terms of a settlement while in a state of high stress.
- You do not need to worry about the immediate enforceability of a "pre-dispute" NDA you signed years ago, as federal law may now protect you from its gag provisions regarding sexual misconduct.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed or intimidated when presented with legal paperwork following a sexual misconduct report. Requesting time and outside counsel is a standard, professional response to such a request and does not imply "uncooperativeness." Federal laws have changed significantly since 2022 to provide specific protections for people in your exact situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and information on federal protections only. State laws (such as those in California, New York, or Washington) may offer additional protections that are not covered here.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding non-disclosure agreements and sexual misconduct vary by state and are subject to change. Consult a qualified employment attorney for advice specific to your case.
