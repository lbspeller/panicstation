# What to do if… you receive a notice that your visa or travel permission has been cancelled while you are abroad (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the notice as urgent, but verify it through the issuing country’s official immigration website or official contact route before you click links, pay money, or send documents. Until you have written clarification, act as if there may be an immediate deadline.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore the notice or assume it will not be enforced.
- Don’t assume it is a scam just because it is sudden; verify it before dismissing it.
- Don’t click payment links or send passport scans through contact details that came only from the message.
- Don’t argue with airline, border, or immigration staff; calmly ask for the reason and next step in writing.
- Don’t miss a stated reporting appointment, departure deadline, or instruction while you are trying to verify the notice.
- Don’t hand your passport to a private person or unofficial agent. If an official authority must hold it, ask for a receipt or written record if possible.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a calm, safe pause before acting.** Sit somewhere private enough to read the notice, stop forwarding it around, and keep your passport, phone, charger, and any immigration documents with you.

2. **Read the notice once for the urgent facts.** Write down the issuing authority, date and time, your name and document number, reference number, and any instruction to report, depart, attend an appointment, stop working, or avoid travel.

3. **Verify through official details, not the message links.** Type the issuing government’s immigration website into your browser yourself, use an official status checker if there is one, or call an official number found separately. If you cannot verify it quickly, continue as if it may be real.

4. **Ask the issuing authority what it affects.** A cancellation may affect boarding, entry, a visa sticker, an electronic travel permission, or your current permission to remain. Ask: “What is my lawful status today?”, “Do I have a deadline?”, and “Do I need to report, leave, apply, or wait for a written decision?”

5. **Ask for written clarification.** Request a case reference, the name or office of the person you spoke to, and written instructions by email, portal message, or paper notice if possible.

6. **If you have a flight or border crossing soon, contact the airline before going to the airport.** Ask whether their system shows you as eligible to board, what document check they will apply, and whether they need updated authorization or written confirmation from immigration.

7. **Make a small proof pack.** Save offline copies or photos of the notice, passport photo page, entry stamp or entry record, visa or permit card, official portal screenshots, flight bookings, accommodation details, and notes of who you spoke to and when.

8. **If you are a U.S. citizen and you are stuck, detained, or the situation is escalating, contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate.** They cannot act as your lawyer, but they can help within consular limits, including emergency support, general process information, and information about local attorneys.

9. **If you are detained or taken for questioning, ask for consular contact.** Ask the detaining authorities to notify the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate, and ask to contact the embassy or consulate yourself as soon as you can.

10. **Use the State Department emergency numbers if you cannot reach the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate in an emergency.** The published numbers are:
    - From overseas: +1 202-501-4444
    - From the U.S. and Canada: 1-888-407-4747

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether to appeal, reapply, or change your long-term travel plans. First confirm your current status and any immediate deadline.
- You do not need a perfect document bundle before making first contact. Start with the notice, passport, entry record, travel bookings, and any official portal screenshots.
- You do not need to tell every friend, employer, host, or travel contact right now. Prioritise the issuing authority, your airline if travel is close, and consular support if you are a U.S. citizen and need help.
- You do not need to pay anyone who pressures you to act immediately through an unofficial route.

## Important reassurance
A cancellation notice abroad can feel destabilising, but the safest first move is not to panic or guess. Verifying the notice, getting the deadline in writing, and avoiding scam links can prevent the most common immediate harms.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Immigration options, appeals, reapplications, detention procedures, and deadlines depend on the country you are in and may need help from a qualified local immigration lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Follow written instructions from the relevant authority and use official government contact routes.
