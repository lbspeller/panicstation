# What to do if… you receive a demand to return company equipment with threats of action (USA)

## Short answer
Ask for the specific dates and source records in writing, then submit a factual, documented rebuttal for your personnel file to ensure the record reflects your side before the warning leads to further discipline.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a disciplinary form if the text states you "agree" with the findings; instead, note that you are acknowledging receipt but dispute the accuracy.
- Do not attempt to alter, delete, or recreate digital or physical time records yourself.
- Do not ignore a deadline to respond or fail to attend a scheduled meeting, as this may be interpreted as a separate policy violation.
- Do not post details of the dispute or the warning on social media or internal workplace chat platforms.
- Do not resign abruptly, as this may forfeit your right to unemployment benefits or internal appeal processes.
- Do not provide a full medical diagnosis; keep health information limited to what is required for leave or accommodation eligibility.

## What to do now
1. **Request the exact details of the alleged violation in writing.** **Ask Human Resources or your supervisor for the specific dates, times, and the section of the employee handbook or attendance policy they believe was violated.**
   - Request copies of the primary records used, such as time-clock punches, GPS logs, badge swipes, or manager exception reports.
2. **Review your state-specific rights regarding personnel records.** **Verify if you are in a state that legally guarantees you the right to inspect your personnel file and insert a written rebuttal (e.g., California, Illinois, or Massachusetts).**
   - Check the "Workplace Know Your Rights" notice or similar annual posters required in many states as of 2026 for specific local protections.
3. **Assemble your supporting documentation for the disputed dates.** **Collect any external or internal evidence that places you at work or proves you were on approved leave.**
   - Gather timestamps from sent emails, call logs, or work product submissions.
   - Take screenshots of scheduling apps, approved time-off requests, or text messages with supervisors regarding schedule changes.
   - Obtain badge/access logs or job-site dispatch records if accessible.
4. **Invoke your Weingarten rights if you are in a unionized workplace.** **If you are a union member and are called into a meeting that you reasonably believe could lead to discipline, explicitly state that you want your union representative present before answering questions.**
   - If you are not in a union, ask if your company policy allows a support person or witness to attend the meeting.
5. **Submit a formal written rebuttal for your file.** **Draft a concise, bulleted statement identifying the errors in the warning and attach your supporting documentation.**
   - Avoid emotional language or long narratives; stick to "Record A says X, but Evidence B shows Y."
   - Request that this rebuttal be permanently attached to the warning in your personnel file.
6. **Trigger formal leave or accommodation processes if health issues are involved.** **If the absences were due to a medical condition or caregiving, inform HR that you are requesting a "reasonable accommodation" under the ADA or initiating the FMLA process.**
   - Ask for the specific forms and the deadline for medical certification.
7. **Address pay discrepancies as a separate wage-and-hour issue.** **If the attendance error resulted in unpaid time, send a written request to payroll asking for a correction of hours worked to ensure compliance with the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA).**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file an external complaint with the EEOC or Department of Labor immediately.
- You do not need to hire an attorney in the first 24 hours of receiving a warning.
- You do not need to identify who made the data entry error; focus on correcting the data itself.
- You do not need to provide a complete history of your past performance or "good" attendance to fix a specific date error.

## Important reassurance
Attendance tracking systems frequently experience technical errors, such as software sync failures, missed manager overrides, or incorrect coding of protected leave. Initiating a calm, written correction process is a standard professional step and does not necessarily mean your job is in immediate jeopardy.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for factual attendance disputes. It does not cover legal strategies for wrongful termination or long-term litigation.

## Important note
Employment laws and record-access rights vary significantly by state and employer size. This guide is for informational purposes and is not legal advice. If you suspect your warning is retaliatory or discriminatory, consult with an employment attorney or your state’s labor department.
