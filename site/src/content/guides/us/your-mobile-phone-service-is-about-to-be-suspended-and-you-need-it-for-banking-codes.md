# What to do if… your mobile phone service is about to be suspended and you need it for banking codes (USA)

## Short answer
Before the suspension happens, use your current access to add a non-SMS way into your bank, then contact your carrier to keep the line or number active long enough to finish that change.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay a carrier bill through a link in a random text or email; use the carrier’s official app, official website, or the number on your bill.
- Don’t keep trying bank logins until you trigger a security lockout.
- Don’t change your phone number, cancel your line, or switch SIMs before your bank verification is updated, unless the bank has already given you a recovery path.
- Don’t cancel your current phone line while trying to keep the number; number transfers usually work better when the old service is still active.
- Don’t share one-time passcodes with anyone, including anyone claiming to be from fraud prevention.
- Don’t assume the bank or carrier can fix this instantly after the number is inactive; recovery may take longer once texts stop.

## What to do now
1. **Log into your bank while SMS still works and add a non-SMS verification method.**  
   Look for security, two-factor authentication, verification methods, trusted devices, or account recovery. Add an option that does not depend on text messages, such as in-app approval, an authenticator app, email backup, a security key you already use, or backup codes if the bank offers them. Store any backup codes somewhere you can reach without that phone number.

2. **Call your bank using a trusted number and ask for account access recovery today.**  
   Use the number on the back of your card, on a statement, or inside the bank’s official app or website. Say: “My mobile service may be suspended soon. I need to change my verification method and make sure I can still access my account.” Ask whether a branch visit is possible, what ID is needed, and whether they can mark your account so you do not get locked out while you update verification.

3. **Contact your carrier now and ask for the fastest way to keep the line active.**  
   Ask whether there is a same-day payment arrangement, extension, hardship option, or reduced plan that keeps texts working. Ask the exact date and time suspension starts, whether calls, texts, and data will still work during suspension, and how long before the number may be terminated or reassigned. Write down the answers, the time of the call, and the representative’s name or ID.

4. **If you may need to leave the carrier, ask how to keep your number before service ends.**  
   Start the number transfer with the new provider; do not cancel the old service first. Ask your current carrier what is needed for port-out, usually the account number and a Number Transfer PIN or port-out PIN. Make sure the name, address, and account details match what the carrier has on file. If the port is delayed, ask both providers what exact mismatch or block is causing it.

5. **Set one backup contact route for money-related accounts.**  
   Choose one route you can use today: a branch visit, secure message inside the bank app, a verified bank phone number, or a trusted person’s phone for outgoing calls only. Do not save passwords, bank details, or one-time codes on a borrowed device.

6. **If you need a temporary phone plan, avoid making the bank problem worse.**  
   A prepaid SIM or eSIM may help with calls and data, but do not make it your bank verification number until the bank has confirmed the change. If your old number is still the bank’s text-code number, focus first on keeping or porting that number.

7. **Check Lifeline only after the immediate lockout risk is under control.**  
   Lifeline may lower the monthly cost of phone or internet service if you qualify, but it is not usually the fastest fix for a same-day suspension. Use the official Lifeline application route, not ads or messages that ask for bank details.

8. **Escalate if you are stuck after trying the direct route.**  
   For carrier billing, suspension, number porting, or unlocking problems, use the FCC consumer complaint route. For bank account access or verification problems that the bank is not resolving, use the CFPB complaint route. A complaint may help create a formal record, but it may not restore access immediately.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to choose the best long-term phone plan today; the goal is to keep a working path into your bank.
- You don’t need to update every website, subscription, or shopping account right now; focus only on accounts that affect money, rent, bills, medicine, travel, or work.
- You don’t need to argue every fee on the first call; stabilize access first, then dispute charges later.
- You don’t need to decide whether to stay with the carrier permanently today.

## Important reassurance
This is a common failure point because many banks still rely on SMS codes. Once you have one working non-SMS route into the bank, a verified branch recovery option, or a successful number transfer, the situation becomes much easier to handle step by step.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent immediate loss of banking access and reduce the chance of losing your phone number. Later choices about carriers, bills, disputes, budgeting, or account security may need help from the bank, carrier, a consumer agency, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not financial, legal, telecoms, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Bank and carrier policies vary. Use trusted contact routes only, and do not give passcodes, passwords, payment details, or personal information to anyone who contacted you first.
