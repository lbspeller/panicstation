# What to do if… you develop facial swelling from a tooth problem that seems to be spreading (USA)

## Short answer
Seek in-person medical or dental evaluation immediately. If you experience difficulty breathing, trouble swallowing, or swelling extending into the neck or under the tongue, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if the swelling subsides if it is rapidly expanding or moving toward the neck.
- Do not attempt to drain, puncture, or lancing any swelling in the mouth or on the face.
- Do not apply heat packs to the swollen area, as this can increase blood flow and potentially worsen the spread of infection.
- Do not lie flat if you have swelling near the airway / neck; stay in an upright, seated position.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics or medication prescribed for a different person or condition.
- Do not exceed the maximum daily dose listed on the label for over-the-counter pain relievers.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate emergency triggers for 911 or the ER:**
   - Difficulty breathing or noisy, labored breathing (stridor).
   - Inability to swallow saliva, excessive drooling, or a muffled "hot potato" voice.
   - Firm, painful, or woody-feeling swelling under the tongue or on the floor of the mouth.
   - Swelling that has reached the eyelid, is closing the eye, or is causing vision changes.
   - Significant restriction in opening the mouth (trismus).
   - Confusion, extreme weakness, or a fever over 103°F (39.4°C).
   **If any of these are present, go to an emergency room or call 911 immediately.**
2. **Contact a dentist for a same-day urgent appointment if you are currently stable:**
   - State clearly: "I have facial swelling from a tooth infection that is spreading."
   - If a dentist cannot see you within a few hours and you have a fever (100.4°F / 38°C or higher) or the swelling is worsening, proceed to the emergency room.
   - Do not rely on Urgent Care centers unless they have a dentist on-site; they often provide temporary antibiotics but cannot treat the source of the infection.
3. **Monitor the speed of spread while waiting for care:**
   - Observe if the swelling moves from the jawline into the neck or toward the eye over the course of an hour.
   - Check if your voice becomes muffled or if it becomes harder to swallow.
   - If the infection spreads rapidly, do not wait for your scheduled appointment; go to the ER.
4. **Manage symptoms safely until seen by a professional:**
   - **Remain upright** to help reduce pressure and keep the airway clear.
   - **Apply a cold compress** to the outside of the face for 15 minutes at a time to reduce discomfort.
   - **Record the time** you first noticed the swelling and when it reached its current size to assist the provider.
5. **Prepare for admission or a procedure:**
   - Bring a list of current medications and any known allergies to antibiotics (especially Penicillin or Sulfa drugs).
   - Mention if you have a compromised immune system, diabetes, or are currently pregnant.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific tooth causing the issue before seeking help.
- You do not need to decide on permanent treatments like a root canal or extraction right now; the priority is stabilizing the infection.
- You do not need to wait for business hours if red-flag symptoms are present.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard physiological response to feel significant concern when facial swelling occurs. Seeking professional help when swelling spreads is the standard protocol for managing dental infections and preventing complications. Once the infection is addressed by a professional, the physical pressure and associated symptoms typically begin to resolve.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage guidance only. It is intended to help the user identify when a dental infection requires emergency hospital intervention versus urgent dental care. It does not replace a clinical diagnosis or definitive dental treatment.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and is not medical advice. Spreading dental infections can lead to serious conditions such as Ludwig’s Angina or sepsis, which are life-threatening. If you have any doubt about your ability to breathe or swallow, seek emergency care immediately.
