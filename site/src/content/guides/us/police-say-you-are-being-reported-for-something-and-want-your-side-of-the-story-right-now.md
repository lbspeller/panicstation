# What to do if… police say you are being reported for something and want “your side of the story” right now (USA)

## Short answer
Do not provide any statement or explanation immediately. Verbally invoke your right to remain silent, state that you want a lawyer, and verify the caller's identity through an official department number before continuing any interaction.

## Do not do these things
- Don't attempt to "clear your name" or explain the situation to the officer, as even innocent statements can be used as evidence.
- Don't speculate, guess, or provide names of other people during the initial contact.
- Don't agree to a "quick chat" or "informal meeting" without an attorney present.
- Don't pay any money, fines, or "settlement fees" over the phone; police do not collect money to resolve reports.
- Don't consent to any searches of your person, vehicle, home, or digital devices.
- Don't provide passwords, PINs, or biometric access (fingerprints/face ID) to your phone.

## What to do now
1. **Stop and pause before responding.** Taking a few seconds to orient yourself prevents impulsive or panicked speech that cannot be retracted.
2. **Verify the caller is actual law enforcement.** Ask for the caller’s full name, badge number, department, and a case or incident number. Hang up and call that department's main publicly listed non-emergency number. Ask the operator or dispatch to confirm that the specific officer is employed there and is currently trying to reach you regarding that case number.
3. **Determine if you are legally detained.** If the contact is in person, ask: "**Am I free to leave?**" If the contact is by phone, ask: "**Am I free to end this call?**"
   - If you are free to leave/end the call, do so immediately without further explanation.
   - If you are not free to leave, you are being detained or arrested.
4. **Verbally invoke your rights using a clear script.** You must state your intent out loud for it to be legally effective. Say:
   - "**I am going to remain silent. I want a lawyer.**"
5. **Stop talking immediately after invoking your rights.** Do not answer follow-up questions, "small talk," or clarifications. If the officer continues to ask questions, repeat the same script: "I am remaining silent and I want a lawyer."
6. **Route all future interview requests through an attorney.** If they ask to schedule a time to talk, say:
   - "**I do not answer questions without an attorney. Please contact my lawyer to schedule any further communication.**"
   - If you do not have a lawyer yet: "**I am obtaining counsel. I will have them contact you once they are retained.**"
7. **Document the interaction immediately after it ends.** Write down the date, time, officer names, badge numbers, the alleged department, and exactly what they said or asked for while the details are fresh.
8. **Preserve existing digital evidence.** Do not delete messages, emails, or call logs in a panic, as this can be interpreted as tampering with evidence. Simply stop using the devices for communication regarding the incident.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove your innocence today.
- You do not need to gather witnesses or evidence right now.
- You do not need to decide whether you will ever tell your story; that decision is made later with legal counsel.
- You do not need to explain why you are choosing to remain silent.

## Important reassurance
The urge to explain yourself when accused is a natural response. However, the legal system treats spontaneous explanations as evidence, not just "your side." Choosing to remain silent and requesting a lawyer is a standard procedural protection used by people in all circumstances and does not imply guilt.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the minutes and hour immediately following sudden police contact. It does not cover long-term legal defense, trial strategy, or how to handle specific types of criminal allegations.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice or an attorney-client relationship. Laws regarding identification (Stop and Identify statutes) vary by state. If you are in immediate physical danger, call 911.
