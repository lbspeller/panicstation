# What to do if… a person you know becomes increasingly angry during a conversation and you think it could turn physical (USA)

## Short answer
Create immediate physical distance and move toward a public area or exit. If you are being blocked from leaving or feel violence is imminent, call **911**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay to "finish" the argument or get the last word.
- Do not use physical touch, such as a hand on their arm, to try and calm them down.
- Do not move into confined spaces like bathrooms, walk-in closets, or vehicles.
- Do not turn your back on the person while you are still within their reach.
- Do not block the person’s own exit or corner them.
- Do not reach for objects that could be perceived as weapons, even if intended for defense.
- Do not ignore your instincts if you feel a "gut" sense of danger.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate sensory warning signs.**
   - Look for clenched fists, pacing, or a "squared-off" stance.
   - Listen for a sudden drop in volume (hissing) or repetitive yelling.
   - Notice if they are moving to stand between you and the door.
2. **Increase your physical distance immediately.**
   - **Stand up if you are sitting and take several steps back.**
   - **Position yourself so there is a large object (table, sofa) between you and the person.**
3. **Set a short, non-negotiable boundary and stop talking.**
   - **Use a neutral voice to say: "I am stopping this conversation now" or "I am leaving."**
   - **Do not respond to further insults or questions; additional talk often increases escalation.**
4. **Move toward the nearest viable exit.**
   - **If the primary door is blocked, move toward a different room with an exit or a window.**
   - **If you are in a public building, head toward security, reception, or a crowded area.**
5. **Call for help if you cannot leave safely.**
   - **Dial 911 if they are preventing you from leaving or have made a specific threat.**
   - **If you cannot speak, leave the line open so the dispatcher can hear the background.**
6. **Use a discrete signal if others are nearby.**
   - **Text a friend or neighbor a pre-arranged code word or your location.**
   - **In a workplace, use an emergency alert button if available at your desk.**
7. **Go to a "safe zone" once you have exited.**
   - **Do not just go to your own car or a nearby room; go to a police station, a 24-hour business, or a trusted neighbor’s house.**
   - **If you believe you are being followed, drive to a police station or a busy fire station.**
8. **Contact support once the immediate threat has passed.**
   - **Call the National Domestic Violence Hotline at 1-800-799-SAFE (7233) or text "START" to 88788.**
   - **For hearing impaired support, use TTY 1-800-787-3224.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the future of the relationship or the friendship right now.
- You do not need to collect all your belongings before leaving.
- You do not need to explain your departure to the person later today.
- You do not need to file a police report immediately unless there has been a physical assault or a direct threat of one.

## Important reassurance
Physical sensations like a racing heart, shaking hands, or a dry mouth are normal "fight or flight" responses to a threat. Choosing to leave a conversation that feels unsafe is a valid safety measure, regardless of whether the other person thinks you are overreacting.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an escalating interpersonal conflict. It is not a substitute for a comprehensive safety plan or professional legal advice regarding harassment or domestic abuse.

## Important note
911 is the primary emergency number in the USA for immediate physical threats. For non-emergency reports after you are safe, you can dial 311 in many cities or contact your local police department's business line.
