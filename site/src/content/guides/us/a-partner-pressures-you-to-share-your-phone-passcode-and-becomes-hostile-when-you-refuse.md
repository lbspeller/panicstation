# What to do if… a partner pressures you to share your phone passcode and becomes hostile when you refuse (USA)

## Short answer
Do not hand over your passcode solely to de-escalate an argument if you feel unsafe or controlled. Focus immediately on creating physical distance and contacting a domestic violence advocate for a safe, confidential consultation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to explain your legal or moral right to privacy while the other person is actively hostile or aggressive.
- Do not make sudden, obvious changes to your security settings or delete apps while the partner is watching, as this may trigger further escalation.
- Do not unlock the phone to "prove" you have nothing to hide; this rarely satisfies a controlling demand and establishes a dangerous precedent.
- Do not assume the situation is minor because no physical hitting has occurred; tech-based control and hostility are significant red flags.
- Do not "test" the partner by bringing up the phone again once the immediate tension has dropped.
- Do not use the compromised device to search for help or shelters if you believe the device is already being monitored.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer physical location immediately to create a safe pause.** If you are indoors, move toward an exit or a room with a lock; if you are being followed or blocked, move toward a public space, a neighbor's home, or a police station. 
2. **Contact the National Domestic Violence Hotline from a device they cannot access.** **Call 1-800-799-7233 or text "START" to 88788 to reach a trained advocate who can help you build a safety plan.**
   - Use a library computer, a friend's phone, or a work device if you suspect your personal phone is being tracked.
3. **Keep your passcode and biometric data private during the escalation.** **Maintain your boundary by stepping away from the conflict rather than engaging in a debate about the code itself.**
4. **Identify one trusted person and send a brief, non-detailed alert.** **Send a message such as "I am having a difficult situation at home and need you to stay reachable for the next hour" to someone you trust.**
5. **Observe and note sensory cues of escalation without engaging.** **Notice if the person is blocking your path, raising their volume, hovering over your shoulder, or using physical intimidation to get the code.**
6. **Call 911 if the person attempts to take the phone by force or prevents you from leaving.** **State your location clearly and inform the dispatcher that you are being prevented from leaving or are being threatened.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you are ending the relationship right now.
- You do not need to figure out how to "fix" the person's jealousy or trust issues today.
- You do not need to perform a full factory reset or change all your passwords this minute.
- You do not need to justify why you want privacy.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel startled, confused, or guilty when a partner treats a standard boundary like a passcode as a personal attack. Wanting digital privacy is a healthy and standard boundary in any relationship. The hostility you are experiencing is a reflection of the other person's behavior, not a failure of your own.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate digital and physical safety. Long-term safety planning, legal options regarding harassment, and domestic violence support services require ongoing coordination with specialists.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice or formal crisis counseling. In the United States, coercive control and technology-facilitated abuse are recognized patterns of domestic violence. If you are in immediate danger of physical harm, contact emergency services.
