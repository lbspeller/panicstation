# What to do if… you suspect someone is impersonating police or an official to intimidate you (USA)

## Short answer
Create a safer pause and verify through 911 or an independently found agency number. If the person is present, following you, threatening you, or it is happening now, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go with them, let them into your home, or hand over your keys or phone just because they claim authority.
- Don’t pay anything on the spot to “avoid arrest”, “clear a warrant”, “pay a fine”, or “protect your money”.
- Don’t use cash, wire transfer, payment app, gift card, cryptocurrency, or a Bitcoin ATM because a caller or visitor tells you to.
- Don’t trust caller ID, a badge shown briefly, a uniform, a seal, a case number, or a link sent to you as proof by itself.
- Don’t give your Social Security number, bank details, login codes, one-time passcodes, ID copies, or device access because they demand “verification”.
- Don’t argue, chase, block, or confront them if you can safely create distance and verify instead.
- Don’t call back a number they gave you or use a link they sent you to “confirm” who they are.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause first.** If they are at your door, keep it locked and speak through the door if you speak at all. If you are outside, move toward a public, well-lit place with other people nearby. If you are in a business, ask staff to stay nearby while you call.
2. **If you feel threatened or it may be an active crime, call 911 immediately.** Say: “I believe someone is impersonating law enforcement or an official authority and intimidating me. I am at [location].” Be ready to give your location, your phone number, what is happening, and any vehicle description.
3. **If you are driving and unsure it is a real stop, make your intention to comply visible.** Slow down, signal, turn on hazard lights if you can, and move safely toward a well-lit public place or police station. Do not speed up, drive erratically, or try to elude the vehicle.
4. **Use 911 to verify a suspicious traffic stop.** Tell the dispatcher your location, direction of travel, your vehicle description, and the vehicle behind you. Ask whether an officer is trying to stop you and ask for a marked unit if you still feel unsafe. Follow the dispatcher’s instructions.
5. **Ask for identifying details from a safe distance.** If it is safe, ask for their name, agency, badge number, and supervisor’s name. A real officer or official should not object to you verifying through dispatch or an independently found agency number.
6. **End remote contact that uses threats or urgency.** If it is a call, text, email, or message demanding money, personal information, secrecy, or immediate action, stop replying. Do not click links or open attachments they sent.
7. **Preserve the evidence without engaging further.** Save voicemails, screenshots, emails, texts, phone numbers, usernames, payment instructions, vehicle details, descriptions, times, locations, and any case number or badge number they gave.
8. **Treat any “pay now or be arrested” demand as highly suspicious.** Real law enforcement does not call or email to threaten arrest unless you pay, and does not demand gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfers, cash, or payment apps as a way to avoid arrest.
9. **Report through the right channel once you are safe.** For an immediate threat or incident in progress, call 911. For a non-emergency report, search for your local police department’s official website and use its non-emergency number. If the contact involved phone, text, email, online fraud, attempted payment, account access, cryptocurrency, or data theft, file a report with IC3 and report the scam to the FTC.
10. **If you shared sensitive information or sent money, do one containment step now.** Contact your bank or card issuer using the number on your card or in the official app, lock cards if available, change any exposed account password, and use IdentityTheft.gov if your Social Security number, ID, or other identity details were exposed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to press charges, pursue an investigation, or make a formal complaint.
- You do not need to prove they are fake before calling 911; uncertainty plus fear is enough to ask dispatch to verify.
- You do not need to organize every screenshot or write a full timeline while the person is present or while you feel unsafe.
- You can make additional reports, contact your bank again, or seek identity-theft help once you are away from the immediate pressure.

## Important reassurance
Impersonators use fear, authority, and urgency to make you comply before you can think. Pausing, creating distance, and verifying through 911 or an independently found official number is a normal and appropriate response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to reduce immediate harm and prevent irreversible mistakes. Later decisions may need help from local law enforcement, a victim assistance service, your bank, an identity-theft resource, or a qualified legal professional.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, safety, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
