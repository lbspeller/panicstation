# What to do if… you are asked to decide about moving a relative to comfort-focused care and you feel unprepared (USA)

## Short answer
Pause the conversation to confirm who the legal decision-maker is, request an immediate palliative care consultation, and ask for a specific list of which treatments will stop and which comfort measures will start.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to "comfort-focused care" until you can explain in your own words what specific curative treatments are being stopped.
- Do not assume that being the "next of kin" automatically gives you legal authority; hospitals must follow specific state surrogate hierarchies if a Health Care Proxy is not named.
- Do not feel forced to decide on hospice enrollment in the first few minutes of a medical crisis.
- Do not allow the medical team to combine the "comfort care" decision with "code status" (DNR / DNI) into a single "yes or no" question; they are separate decisions.
- Do not have this discussion in a high-traffic area; request a quiet, private room for a formal "goals of care" meeting.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the patient’s capacity and the legal spokesperson.**  
   **Ask the doctor: "Does my relative have the capacity to make this decision right now?"**
   - If no, ask: "Who is documented as the legally recognized surrogate—do you have a Health Care Proxy on file, or are you using the state default hierarchy?"

2. **Request a formal Palliative Care consultation immediately.**  
   **Say: "I want a palliative care team to join this discussion to help us manage symptoms and clarify our options before we decide."**
   - Palliative care is not the same as hospice; it can be provided alongside curative treatment to manage pain and stress.

3. **Check for existing "Portable Orders" (POLST / MOLST).**  
   **Look for a bright green, pink, or yellow form in the patient's bedside papers or wallet.**
   - These are medical orders (called POLST, MOLST, POST, or MOST depending on your state) that may already document your relative's wishes regarding life-sustaining treatment.

4. **Ask for the "stop / start" list in plain language.**  
   **Ask: "If we move to comfort-focused care today, exactly which medications or procedures will stop, and exactly which medications will be added for pain or breathing?"**

5. **Clarify the hospice eligibility requirements if hospice is mentioned.**  
   **Ask: "Is a physician ready to certify that my relative has a life expectancy of 6 months or less if the illness runs its normal course?"**
   - This is the standard requirement for Medicare hospice benefits; if the doctor cannot say this yet, the focus should remain on palliative care.

6. **Inquire about the location of care.**  
   **Ask: "Where would this comfort care take place—can it happen here in the hospital, or are you recommending a move to a facility or home?"**

7. **Verify the reversibility of the decision.**  
   **Ask: "If we choose comfort care now, can we change our minds and return to curative treatment if the situation changes?"**
   - In most cases, hospice election can be revoked at any time.

8. **Contact the Patient Advocate if you feel pressured.**  
   **If you feel a decision is being forced before you understand it, ask to speak with "Patient Relations" or a "Patient Representative."**

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a specific hospice agency in the next hour.
- You do not need to reconcile long-standing family disagreements about the patient’s life choices right now.
- You do not need to understand every detail of the patient's insurance policy before asking for a palliative care consult.
- You do not need to feel "at peace" with the decision before it is made; focus on whether the plan aligns with the patient's known values.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when asked to make high-stakes medical decisions without prior warning. Clinical teams are trained to wait while you process information and verify legal authority. Hesitating to ensure you have the full facts is a protective action, not a failure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for medical decision-making in a hospital setting and does not replace legal or medical advice.

## Important note
Medical surrogate laws and portable order forms (POLST / MOLST) vary significantly by state. Medicare hospice eligibility requires specific physician certification. Always confirm the identity of the attending physician and ask for the agreed plan to be documented in the patient's electronic health record.
