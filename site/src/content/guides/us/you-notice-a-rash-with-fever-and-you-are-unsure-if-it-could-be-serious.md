# What to do if… you notice a rash with fever and you are unsure if it could be serious (USA)

## Short answer
Call 911 immediately if the person is confused, has trouble breathing, or has a stiff neck with light sensitivity. If the person is stable but has a rash that looks like tiny bruises or does not fade when pressed, seek emergency care at a hospital right away.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for the rash to spread or the fever to break if emergency signs are present.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel dizzy, confused, or are having trouble breathing.
- Do not go into a clinic or hospital for a suspected contagious rash like measles without calling ahead first.
- Do not apply new creams or take unprescribed antibiotics until a clinician has viewed the rash.
- Do not ignore "pinprick" red spots or purple blotches even if they do not itch or hurt.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate life-safety triggers and call 911 if any are present.**
   - Difficulty breathing, gasping, or blue-tinted lips and nails.
   - Sudden confusion, extreme sleepiness, or inability to wake the person.
   - A seizure or sudden collapse.
2. **Identify meningitis or sepsis red flags for immediate ER transport.**
   - **Check for a stiff neck** and ask if bright light hurts their eyes (photophobia).
   - **Check for cold hands and feet** while the rest of the body feels hot with fever.
   - **Look for a non-blanching rash** by pressing a clear glass firmly against the spots; if the spots do not fade or disappear under the glass, seek emergency care.
3. **Isolate the person if measles or chickenpox is suspected.**
   - If there is a known local outbreak or recent travel, keep the person in a separate room away from others, especially pregnant people or the immunocompromised.
   - **Call the urgent care or ER before arriving** so they can provide a mask and a separate entrance to prevent spreading the virus to other patients.
4. **Locate and note the timing of any new medications.**
   - **Identify if any new drugs were started** in the last 14 days, particularly antibiotics or anti-seizure medications.
   - **Stop the new medication only if instructed** by a clinician, unless the reaction is an immediate emergency like throat swelling.
5. **Document the rash characteristics for the clinician.**
   - **Identify where the rash started** (e.g., face, trunk, or extremities) and if it is currently moving.
   - **Note if the rash is flat, raised, blistered, or feels like sandpaper.**
   - **Check the mouth** for sores, white spots, or a very red "strawberry" tongue.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific virus or bacteria yourself.
- You do not need to notify school, work, or contacts until a professional diagnosis is made.
- You do not need to worry about the specific temperature reading if the person is showing emergency behavioral signs.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel alarmed when a fever and rash appear together. Taking immediate action to seek professional triage—whether through 911 for severe symptoms or a same-day clinical call for stable ones—is the standard safety protocol for these symptoms.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency identification only. It does not provide medical diagnosis or treatment plans, which require a physical examination by a healthcare professional.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional medical advice. If you believe a situation is life-threatening, contact 911 or your local emergency services immediately.
