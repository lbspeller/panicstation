# What to do if… you develop sudden ringing in your ear with dizziness or nausea (USA)

## Short answer
Seek **immediate emergency evaluation** if these symptoms are accompanied by facial drooping, weakness, or trouble speaking. If you have sudden hearing loss with ringing and vertigo, contact an **Ear, Nose, and Throat (ENT) specialist or emergency room immediately**, as treatment for hearing recovery is most effective within the first **72 hours**.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not drive yourself** if you are experiencing vertigo or imbalance.
- **Do not wait more than 24 hours** to seek help if your hearing has changed or vanished in one ear.
- **Do not put anything into the ear canal**, including drops, oils, or cotton swabs.
- **Do not attempt self-treatment maneuvers** (like the Epley maneuver) if you have a severe headache, vision changes, or numbness.
- **Do not ignore the symptom** even if the ringing is faint, as the window for effective steroid treatment for sudden hearing loss is narrow.

## What to do now
1. **Stabilize your physical position to prevent injury.**
   - Sit or lie down on a firm surface immediately.
   - Support your head with pillows but keep it slightly elevated.
   - If you feel like the room is spinning, fix your gaze on a single, non-moving object.
2. **Perform a rapid stroke and neurology screen.**
   - Call 911 immediately if you notice:
   - Facial drooping or a "crooked" smile.
   - Weakness or numbness in one arm or leg.
   - Slurred speech or difficulty finding words.
   - A "thunderclap" headache (the worst pain of your life).
3. **Check for sudden hearing loss using the "Hum Test."**
   - Close your mouth and hum a steady tone.
   - Note if the sound is louder in one ear or centered. 
   - If the sound is much louder in the "good" ear, the symptomatic ear likely has sensorineural hearing loss.
   - Cover one ear at a time while listening to a constant sound (like a faucet or fan) to confirm if hearing is muffled.
4. **Identify if the ringing is "pulsatile."**
   - Listen to the ringing to see if it pulses in time with your heartbeat.
   - If it is rhythmic/pulsatile, inform the medical provider immediately, as this requires specific vascular imaging.
5. **Contact an emergency department or ENT clinic.**
   - State clearly: "I have sudden onset tinnitus, vertigo, and suspected hearing loss."
   - Specifically ask: "Is there an ENT specialist on call who can evaluate me for sudden sensorineural hearing loss?"
   - If an ENT is not available, go to the nearest Emergency Room.
6. **Note the exact time of onset.**
   - Record exactly when the symptoms began (e.g., "woke up with it" or "started at 2:00 PM").
   - List any other symptoms like ear pressure, "popping" sounds, or recent viral illnesses.

## What can wait
- Identifying the exact underlying cause (Meniere’s, vestibular neuritis, or idiopathic loss) does not need to happen right now; the priority is stabilization and starting the treatment window.
- Deciding on long-term hearing aids or tinnitus management strategies can wait until the acute phase is over.
- You do not need to notify your employer or insurance until you are physically stable and have been triaged.

## Important reassurance
Sudden ear symptoms and vertigo are highly disorienting and often trigger intense anxiety. This reaction is a normal physiological response to a balance-system disruption. Most cases can be managed effectively when addressed within the first few days. Focus only on the next physical step of getting to a medical evaluation.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and triage only. It addresses the acute onset of symptoms where time-sensitive treatments (like steroids for hearing loss) are required.

## Important note
This information is not a medical diagnosis. If you are unable to walk, have lost consciousness, or suspect a stroke, call 911. Sudden hearing loss is a recognized medical emergency in the United States.
