# What to do if… you find signs of sexual activity in your room and do not remember consenting (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer place first. Then contact RAINN or go to an ER and ask for sexual-assault-informed medical care; you can ask about options without deciding right now whether to report to police.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront a person you suspect while you are shaken or unsafe.
- Do not pressure yourself to remember, explain, or label exactly what happened right now.
- Do not post about it publicly or send details widely in the first hours.
- Do not throw away clothes, bedding, cups, wrappers, messages, or anything else you think might matter while you are still deciding what you want.
- If you might want evidence-preservation options later and it feels manageable, avoid showering, bathing, cleaning your body, or changing clothes; if you already changed, place the clothes you were wearing in a clean paper bag or clean dry container.

## What to do now
1. **Make a safer pause first.**  
   If you feel in immediate danger or think someone may still be nearby, leave if you can do so safely and call **911**. Go to a public place, a locked room away from the person, or a trusted person.

2. **Bring in one safe person.**  
   Call or message someone you trust. Ask them to stay with you, help you get to care, and not push you to make decisions.

3. **Get medical care even if you are unsure what happened.**  
   Go to an ER, urgent hospital service, or sexual-assault clinic and say: “I may have been sexually assaulted and I do not remember consenting.” Ask whether they have a **Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE)**, **Sexual Assault Forensic Exam (SAFE)**, or **Sexual Assault Response Team (SART)**, or whether they can send you to a place that does.

4. **Ask about your local reporting choices before deciding.**  
   In some places, adults can receive medical care or have evidence collected without making an immediate police report. Rules vary, and mandatory reporting can apply in some situations, including if the person is under 18, a vulnerable adult, or otherwise covered by state law, so ask the hospital or an advocate what applies where you are.

5. **Treat severe symptoms as urgent.**  
   Call **911** or go to the ER now if you have trouble breathing, chest pain, seizures, repeated vomiting, passing out, serious injury, heavy bleeding, or confusion that is getting worse.

6. **Contact confidential specialist support.**  
   Contact **RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline** by phone, text, or online chat. They can help you find local medical care, an advocate, and support options without pressure.

7. **Keep immediate notes simple.**  
   If you can, write or voice-note only what you actually remember: your last clear memory, when you woke up or noticed the signs, who may have had access to the room, and any messages, calls, or location information you can see. Do not fill gaps or guess.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide what exact label fits: assault, drugging, break-in, misunderstanding, or something else.
- You do not need to decide right now whether to make a police report.
- You do not need to contact a landlord, employer, school, family member, or anyone you do not trust.
- You do not need to clean, reorganize, or explain the room immediately if you are safe leaving it as it is.
- You do not need to make long-term housing, relationship, work, or legal decisions today.

## Important reassurance
Confusion, numbness, memory gaps, freezing, or delayed feelings can happen after fear, shock, intoxication, sleep, or possible drugging. Not remembering, not fighting, or being unsure does not mean you agreed. You deserve care and support even if the details are unclear.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, urgent health needs, and keeping choices open. Later decisions about reporting, housing, security, legal options, or formal statements may need help from a local advocate, medical professional, attorney, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, therapeutic, forensic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or need urgent medical help, call **911**. If you are overwhelmed and need immediate emotional crisis support, call or text **988** or use 988 chat.
