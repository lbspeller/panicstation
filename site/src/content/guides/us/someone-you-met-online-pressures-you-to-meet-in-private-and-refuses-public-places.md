# What to do if… someone you met online pressures you to meet in private and refuses public places (USA)

## Short answer
Do not meet them in private. Stop arranging the meeting, contact someone you trust, and call 911 if you are in immediate danger or need emergency help.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to meet in a home, hotel, car, or isolated place to keep the conversation calm.
- Do not let them pick you up or learn your address.
- Do not keep negotiating after they refuse safer public options.
- Do not send more personal details, intimate images, money, or account access because they pressure or threaten you.
- Do not blame yourself if you already shared information or images.
- Do not meet them to test whether your concern is justified.

## What to do now
1. **Stop arranging the meeting.** You do not owe them another reply. If replying feels safe and useful, send one brief message such as: “I am not meeting in private.” Then disengage.
2. **Block and report the account on the platform.** Use the platform’s reporting tools for pressure, harassment, threats, or unsafe behavior. Avoid continuing the argument in messages.
3. **Keep a small record only if it feels safe.** If you may want support or to report later, save a few relevant messages or screenshots somewhere safe before blocking.
4. **Reduce immediate exposure.** Turn off any location sharing with them, remove visible address details from your profiles, and tell someone you trust that you are not meeting this person.
5. **Respond to immediate danger.** If they threaten to harm you, show up, follow you, or you otherwise need emergency help, move to a safer place if you can and call 911.
6. **Use specialist guidance for blackmail or intimate-image threats.** Do not send more images, money, passwords, or account access. The FTC and RAINN resources below explain options for blocking, platform reporting, support, and optional reporting routes.
7. **Get confidential support when relevant.** RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline can provide confidential support and help you consider options if sexual threats, abuse, or intimate-image concerns are involved.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether the situation fits a particular label.
- You do not need to confront them, warn them, or manage their feelings.
- You do not need to make every privacy change at once.
- You do not need to decide about a non-emergency report today. Call 911 if you need emergency help.

## Important reassurance
Refusing safer public options is enough reason to stop arranging the meeting. You do not need proof that something worse would have happened. You are allowed to end contact without explaining or defending your decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Ongoing threats, stalking, repeated contact attempts, blackmail, or intimate-image abuse may need specialist support and a tailored safety plan.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or need emergency help, call 911.
