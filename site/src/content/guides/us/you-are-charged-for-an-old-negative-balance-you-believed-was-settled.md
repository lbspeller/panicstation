# What to do if… you are charged for an old negative balance you believed was settled (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate your current funds from the charging institution and submit a formal written dispute immediately to trigger federal consumer protections (Regulation E for bank debits or Regulation Z for credit cards).

## Do not do these things
- Don't make a partial payment "just to show good faith," as this can restart the statute of limitations on old debt in many states.
- Don't acknowledge that you owe the debt in writing or on recorded lines until you have received a formal itemization and validation.
- Don't assume a phone call to customer service is enough; federal law typically requires a written notice to preserve your full legal rights.
- Don't ignore legal summons or court dates even if the debt is clearly incorrect or already paid.
- Don't provide your current bank account or routing numbers to a debt collector to "verify" your identity.

## What to do now
1. **Secure your primary income source immediately.**
   - If the charge happened in your main checking account, move any remaining balance to a separate institution if possible.
   - Redirect upcoming direct deposits (paychecks/benefits) to a different account to ensure you can cover rent and food while the dispute is pending.

2. **Identify the specific legal track for your dispute.**
   - **For bank account debits (ACH/Debit Card):** Send a written "Notice of Error" to your bank within 60 days of the statement showing the charge. Under Regulation E, the bank must generally investigate within 10 business days (20 for new accounts) or provide provisional credit while they continue.
   - **For credit card charges:** Send a written "Billing Error Notice" to the specific address for billing inquiries (not the payment address) within 60 days of the statement. This triggers Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA) protections which prevent the issuer from reporting the item as delinquent while investigating.
   - **For third-party debt collectors:** Send a "Debt Validation Request" within 30 days of their initial contact. This forces them to stop collection activity until they provide proof of the debt and its current owner.

3. **Gather "extinguishment" evidence from your records.**
   - Locate the settlement agreement, the "Paid in Full" letter, or the bank statement showing the original $0 balance or the settlement payment.
   - Save copies of these documents; do not send your only original copy to the creditor.

4. **Send a formal dispute letter via Certified Mail.**
   - **Draft a concise statement:** "I dispute the validity of this charge. This account was settled/closed on [Date]. I request a full itemization of the alleged debt and proof of your authority to collect it."
   - Request that they cease all reporting to credit bureaus and pause interest/fee accrual during the investigation.
   - Use Certified Mail with Return Receipt Requested to create a legal paper trail of their receipt.

5. **Review your credit reports for "Zombie Debt" reporting.**
   - Pull your reports from AnnualCreditReport.com to see if the item is listed as "past due" or "in collection."
   - If found, file a dispute with the credit bureau (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion) and the company reporting the data simultaneously.

6. **File a regulatory complaint if the institution is unresponsive.**
   - Submit a complaint through the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) website if the bank or collector fails to follow the 10/30/60-day legal windows for acknowledging or investigating your dispute.

## What can wait
- Negotiating a new settlement (do not do this until the original settlement is proven invalid).
- Closing the account entirely (sometimes keeping it open with a $0 balance is necessary for the investigation).
- Explaining your emotional or financial distress to the collector (this does not change the legal dispute process).

## Important reassurance
Sudden charges for "zombie debt" or settled balances are frequently the result of automated system errors, bulk debt sales where records are incomplete, or misapplied payments. Feeling startled or frustrated is a normal response to an unexpected financial threat. Following the formal written dispute process is the most effective way to force a correction.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for disputed financial charges under federal law. It does not cover specialized commercial/business debt, tax liens, or specific state-level statutes of limitations which may provide additional protections.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Laws such as the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) and Electronic Fund Transfer Act (EFTA) have strict deadlines; consult with a consumer protection attorney if you are served with a lawsuit.
