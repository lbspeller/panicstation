# What to do if… you find your front door unlatched when you are certain you closed it (USA)

## Short answer
Do not go in to check. Move to a safer spot outside, and call 911 if someone could be inside, there are signs of forced entry, or you feel unsafe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not step inside “just to check fast”.
- Do not call out from the doorway or try to draw someone out.
- Do not touch the door, handle, lock, frame, or anything that looks disturbed unless you must move away safely.
- Do not try to “clear the house” yourself.
- Do not confront anyone you might see or hear.
- Do not reset, delete, or overwrite doorbell, camera, alarm, or smart-lock history.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Step away from the doorway. Go somewhere less exposed, such as inside a locked car, behind a solid barrier, at a trusted neighbor’s door, or to a staffed building area.
2. **Check only from that safer spot.** Without entering, look and listen for signs such as a damaged lock or frame, broken glass, unusual lights or sounds, movement inside, missing items visible from outside, or anything clearly out of place.
3. **Call 911 if there is any immediate concern.** Call if you think someone may be inside, you see signs of forced entry, you hear movement, or you feel in danger. Give your exact address, say your front door is unlatched or ajar, say you are outside, and follow the call-taker’s instructions.
4. **Use camera, alarm, or smart-lock history only if you can do it safely from outside.** Check recent events from your phone, but do not delete, reset, or change settings yet.
5. **Get another person present outside.** Ask a neighbor, friend, household member, doorman, building security, or property manager to come to you outside. Do not ask them to go in and check.
6. **If it is suspicious but not an immediate emergency, use your local official non-emergency police or emergency communications contact.** Many areas publish a non-emergency number on the local police department, sheriff, county, or emergency communications website. Do not call 911 just to ask for the non-emergency number.
7. **Document what you can see before touching anything.** From outside, take a few photos of the door position, lock area, frame, and any visible marks or damage. Do not delay calling 911 to take photos if danger may be present.
8. **Do not enter while anything still feels wrong.** If there are signs of entry, disturbance, or someone inside, stay out and wait for police instructions. If it appears to be a simple latch issue and you decide to go in, keep your phone in hand, keep an exit available, and leave immediately if anything seems disturbed.

## What can wait
- Troubleshooting the latch, strike plate, hinges, weather stripping, or door alignment.
- Calling a locksmith, landlord, property manager, or repair person, unless you cannot secure the door after the immediate safety concern is over.
- Reviewing camera footage in detail.
- Posting about it online or messaging a group chat.
- Making an inventory of missing items, contacting insurance, or planning security upgrades.

## Important reassurance
A door can fail to latch because the latch did not catch, the frame shifted, the strike plate is misaligned, or the door was pulled by wind or pressure. It is still reasonable to treat the moment carefully until you know you are safe.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first minutes after noticing the door is not fully latched. Later decisions about repairs, police reports, insurance, locks, cameras, or tenant issues may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, insurance, or professional advice. In the U.S., 911 is for emergencies needing immediate police, fire, or medical assistance; if you think there may be an intruder, a crime in progress, or immediate danger, call 911 right away.
