# What to do if… you have diarrhea and are starting to show signs of dehydration (USA)

## Short answer
Start replacing fluid and salts now with small, frequent sips, preferably an oral rehydration solution. Get medical help now if you are confused, fainting, hard to wake, unable to keep liquids down, or showing worsening dehydration signs.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore dehydration signs such as dark urine, peeing much less than usual, dry mouth, strong thirst, weakness, or dizziness when standing.
- Do not drink a large amount all at once if you feel nauseated; it may trigger more vomiting.
- Do not rely on alcohol or lots of caffeine while you are dehydrated.
- Do not use anti-diarrhea medicine to push through bloody diarrhea, black or tarry stool, fever, severe pain, or feeling very unwell without medical advice.
- Do not prepare food for other people or do close caregiving while you are having vomiting or diarrhea.

## What to do now
1. **Start oral rehydration.**
   - Use an oral rehydration solution if you have one.
   - Take small sips every few minutes.
   - If you are keeping it down, slowly increase the amount.

2. **If you are vomiting, make the sips smaller.**
   - Try 1–2 teaspoons at a time.
   - If you vomit, pause briefly, then restart with smaller sips.
   - Sucking on ice chips may help if drinking feels difficult.

3. **Check the dehydration signs that matter right now.**
   - Notice whether you are peeing less than usual or not at all.
   - Check whether urine is dark.
   - Notice dry mouth, strong thirst, weakness, or dizziness when standing.
   - If these are worsening or not easing after steady sipping, call a clinician, urgent care, or an emergency department now for advice.

4. **Escalate quickly for red flags.**
   - Call **911** if someone is confused, fainting, hard to wake, breathing fast, has a very fast heartbeat, or seems to be in shock.
   - Seek urgent medical help now if you cannot keep liquids or oral rehydration solution down.
   - Seek medical help now for bloody diarrhea, stool with pus, black or tarry stool, severe abdominal or rectal pain, or fever over 102°F.
   - Adults should contact a health care provider if diarrhea lasts more than 2 days.

5. **Get help earlier for higher-risk people.**
   - Call a clinician promptly for infants, young children, older adults, pregnancy, weakened immune system, significant chronic illness, or current antibiotic use.
   - For babies or young children, do not wait if there are no wet diapers for 3 hours or more, no tears when crying, unusual sleepiness, sunken eyes, or signs of dehydration.

6. **Reduce spread while you are sick.**
   - Wash hands well with soap and water, especially after using the bathroom and before touching food.
   - Avoid preparing or handling food for others, and avoid close caregiving if possible.
   - Wait at least 48 hours after vomiting and diarrhea stop before preparing food for others or caring closely for others.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to force food; fluids and salts come first.
- You do not need to decide about tests, stool samples, or medicines until you have either improved with rehydration or spoken with a clinician.
- You do not need to make work, school, travel, or social plans while dehydration signs are still active.

## Important reassurance
Diarrhea can make you feel weak, shaky, thirsty, or dizzy because fluid and salts can drop quickly. Small, steady sips are a useful first step, and getting help early is reasonable when dehydration signs are starting.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours only. Ongoing diarrhea, worsening dehydration, high-risk situations, or red-flag symptoms may need medical assessment and treatment.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical diagnosis or professional medical advice. If you are unsure whether symptoms are becoming severe, seek urgent medical evaluation.
