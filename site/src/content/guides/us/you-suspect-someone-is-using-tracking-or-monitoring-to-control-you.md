# What to do if… you suspect someone is using tracking or monitoring to control you (USA)

## Short answer
Focus on safety first: get to a safer place and contact sexual violence or domestic violence support using a device or account they cannot access. Avoid confronting them or making sudden tech changes that could alert them or increase danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront them, accuse them, or try to “catch them” while you are still within their reach.
- Do not immediately factory reset your phone or change all passwords if that could alert them and increase danger.
- Do not let them “check your phone,” “help you change settings,” or pressure you to share accounts.
- Do not plan safety steps using a device you suspect is being monitored.
- Do not search for a physical tracker alone in an isolated place if you feel unsafe.
- Do not assume you need proof before asking for support.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If you feel in danger right now, call **911**. If you can, move to a public place, a trusted person, or another place where they cannot easily isolate you.
2. **Use safer communications.** Use a friend’s phone, a work device, a public computer, or a device they have never handled to look up resources and contact help. Keep messages neutral until you are safer.
3. **Contact specialist support; you control what you share and what options you explore.** Before giving identifying details, you can ask what is confidential and whether there are any reporting limits.
   - **RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline: 800-656-HOPE (4673)**, online chat, or text **HOPE** to **64673**.
   - **National Domestic Violence Hotline: 800-799-SAFE (7233)**, chat, or text **START** to **88788** for safety planning, including technology-related abuse.
4. **Use survivor technology-safety resources before making changes.** NNEDV’s Safety Net Project and TechSafety resources can help you understand stalkerware, location sharing, physical trackers, account access, and safer order-of-operations. A hotline or local advocate can help you decide what changes are safest before you toggle settings, remove devices, reset a phone, or change passwords.
5. **Reduce the most dangerous leak: live location access.** From a safe device or account, check whether your location may be shared through phone settings, Apple or Google accounts, family-sharing features, social apps, car systems, or small trackers in belongings. If you find sharing turned on, pause before changing it if they might notice quickly; write down what you find and decide with an advocate how to change it safely.
6. **Create a clean contact route if it is safe.** Use a device they have never handled to create a new email address and a new passcode. Use that contact route only for help-seeking, such as advocates, healthcare, housing, or legal advice.
7. **If you suspect a physical tracker, check with support.** Ask a trusted person, advocate, mechanic, or law enforcement officer to help you check a car, bag, coat, or other belongings if that feels safe. If you find something, prioritize your immediate safety and consider reporting only if you feel safe doing so.
8. **Write down a brief log for yourself.** On paper or a safe device, note what happened, what the person knew, what alerts you saw, and any connection to sexual pressure, threats, coercion, or control. This can help you explain the situation without having to remember everything under stress.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report to police, end the relationship, confront them, or remove every possible tracking route.
- You do not need to fix all the technology immediately; careful steps with support are often safer than fast changes.
- You do not need to prove exactly how they know where you are before you seek help.

## Important reassurance
If someone is using monitoring or tracking to sexually control you, it is understandable to feel panicked, unsure, or constantly on edge. Taking this seriously is reasonable, and support should respect your choices and pace.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety and harm prevention. More detailed device checks, account changes, evidence decisions, and reporting decisions may need specialist help because sudden changes can sometimes increase risk.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If you want confidential support, a sexual assault hotline, domestic violence hotline, or local advocacy program can help you explore options without pressure to report.
