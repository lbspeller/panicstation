# What to do if… your online banking shows a zero balance and you suspect a system outage (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the zero balance as unverified until you confirm it through an official route. Do not make panic transfers or duplicate payments; check another access method and contact the bank or credit union using the number on your card, statement, app, or official website.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click bank-support links from texts, emails, search ads, or social media posts about the outage.
- Do not move all your money, take an emergency loan, or cancel automatic payments just because the app shows zero.
- Do not keep resetting your password repeatedly; lockouts can make access harder.
- Do not share screenshots publicly or by direct message, because they may show account details.
- Do not decide it is fraud based only on the displayed balance; do report any unfamiliar transaction, payee, alert, or transfer promptly.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and name the problem.** Say to yourself: “This could be a display or access outage.” Your next job is to verify the real balance, protect essential payments, and avoid scams.
2. **Save a private record of what you see.** Take a screenshot of the zero balance, any error message, and the date and time. Keep it private, and do not post or send it unless your bank asks for it through a secure channel.
3. **Check for an official outage notice.** Look in the bank app, the bank’s official website, a status page if the bank has one, or a recorded message on the customer service line. If you look at social media, use only a profile linked from the bank’s official website, and do not send account information in posts or direct messages.
4. **Try a different official access method.** Use one or more of these:
   - an ATM balance inquiry, without withdrawing more than you need;
   - automated phone banking or a live representative using the number on your card or statement;
   - a branch visit, if one is open and reachable;
   - a recent statement or transaction history you already have, to compare expected activity.
5. **Ask the bank to confirm the real account position.** Use a trusted contact route, not a search ad or link from a message. Ask:
   - what your actual or ledger balance is;
   - what your available balance is;
   - whether there is a known outage affecting displayed balances, cards, ACH, direct deposit, bill pay, or transfers;
   - whether any holds, pending payments, or scheduled withdrawals are involved;
   - whether the bank will refund outage-related fees, such as overdraft, insufficient funds, withdrawal, or late-payment fees it caused.
6. **Protect essentials due today.** Until the balance is confirmed, pause non-essential spending. If rent, utilities, insurance, a loan payment, or another essential bill may fail, contact the payee and say your bank access is affected by a suspected outage. Ask for a short extension or fee waiver, and avoid sending a second payment unless the first one is clearly cancelled or failed.
7. **If you see unfamiliar or incorrect electronic transfers, report them as an error or unauthorized transfer.** Use the bank’s dispute route and record the date, time, method, representative name if available, and reference number. If you report by phone, ask whether written confirmation is required within 10 business days and where to send it.
8. **If the bank does not resolve the problem, escalate with your records.** Start with the CFPB complaint process if you are unsure where to complain. A direct regulator complaint can wait until you confirm who supervises your bank or credit union; national banks and federal savings associations may involve the OCC, some state-chartered banks may involve the FDIC or Federal Reserve, and credit union complaints may involve the NCUA.

## What can wait
- You do not need to close accounts, move all your money, or choose a new bank right now.
- You do not need to prove the exact technical cause of the outage today.
- You do not need to choose the perfect regulator immediately; you can start with the CFPB if you are unsure where to complain.
- You do not need to sort out every non-essential payment until you have confirmed the real balance and protected anything due today.

## Important reassurance
A sudden zero balance in an app can be a display, sync, or access problem, especially when other customers are reporting trouble too. Using a second official access method and avoiding message links protects you while the bank confirms what is actually happening.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to stabilize the situation and prevent irreversible mistakes. If money is missing, payments fail, fees appear, or access problems continue, you may need help from the bank’s dispute team, a complaint process, or another qualified source.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not financial, legal, professional, or consumer-rights advice. Protections, time limits, and complaint routes can depend on the account, transaction type, institution, and facts. Contact your bank or credit union promptly through verified contact details if you see unauthorized activity or risk missing essential payments.
