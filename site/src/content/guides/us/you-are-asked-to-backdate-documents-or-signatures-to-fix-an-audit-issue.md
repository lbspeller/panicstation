# What to do if… you are asked to backdate documents or signatures to fix an audit issue (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign, approve, or alter anything you believe would be inaccurate or misleading. Pause the request, ask for a truthful correction method, and use a safe internal route or independent advice before taking another step.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use a date that suggests a signature, approval, or review happened earlier than it did.
- Do not alter timestamps, version history, logs, emails, chats, or audit trails.
- Do not delete messages, drafts, or files related to the request.
- Do not send confidential work documents to a personal email account, cloud account, or device.
- Do not accuse or threaten the requester while you are trying to clarify the instruction.
- Do not assume that an internal report pauses any outside deadline.

## What to do now
1. **Pause before signing or editing.** Ask what document is involved, which date you are being asked to use, and what audit issue the change is meant to address.
2. **Ask for the approved correction method.** Say that you can help correct the record, but you cannot sign or enter a date that would be inaccurate or misleading.
3. **Keep the real signature date.** Ask whether the approved option is to sign with the current date and add a truthful explanation of when the underlying work or decision occurred and why the documentation is being completed now.
4. **Create a factual note.** Record who made the request, when it happened, the document involved, the date requested, who was present, and how you responded. Keep the note in a lawful, secure place without copying confidential work material.
5. **Choose a safe escalation or advice route.** An internal route may be Compliance, Legal, Internal Audit, Risk, or an ethics hotline. If the request involves senior leadership or an internal route does not feel safe, get independent legal advice before sending a detailed written follow-up.
6. **Preserve records without exporting them.** Do not delete or alter related material. Ask Legal, Compliance, or an independent attorney how to preserve relevant information lawfully before copying or moving documents.
7. **Check retaliation deadlines promptly if you are worried about consequences.** Protections and filing routes vary by law, industry, and state. Some federal whistleblower complaint deadlines administered by OSHA are as short as 30 days.
8. **Get prompt advice if regulated reporting or government money may be involved.** This includes situations involving government contracts, grants, healthcare reimbursement, certifications, public-company reporting, or other regulated records.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, sue, or make an external report.
- You do not need to investigate motives or prove what anyone intended.
- You do not need to solve the audit issue yourself.
- You do not need to choose an outside reporting route this minute, but get prompt independent advice if retaliation, regulated reporting, government claims, or a deadline may be involved.

## Important reassurance
You are allowed to slow this down. Asking for an accurate, traceable correction process is a reasonable response when your signature, approval, or work record is involved.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help based on your state, industry, employer, role, and the type of record involved.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Follow workplace policies where lawful and safe, and seek qualified independent advice if you are unsure about your risks or deadlines.
