# What to do if… you are asked to backdate documents or signatures to fix an audit issue (USA)

## Short answer
Do not send money, buy equipment through a provided link, or deposit checks sent by the recruiter. Treat any request for upfront payment as a high-probability scam or a violation of federal wage-and-hour laws.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money via wire transfer, cryptocurrency, gift cards, or peer-to-peer payment apps (e.g., Venmo, CashApp, Zelle).
- Do not deposit a check sent by a "new employer" with instructions to use the funds to purchase equipment from a specific vendor.
- Do not share your Social Security Number, bank account details, or copies of your ID until you have verified the employer through an independent, trusted channel.
- Do not assume that funds "available" in your bank account after a deposit mean the check has cleared; banks are required to make funds available before they are fully verified.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all financial transactions and communications immediately.** **Refuse to send any personal funds or deposit any checks until the employer's legitimacy is confirmed through an independent channel.**
2. **Verify the company and the recruiter using official contact information.** **Search for the company's official website independently—do not use links provided in emails—and call the main HR or corporate office number to confirm the job offer and the recruiter's identity.**
   - Confirm if the company's standard policy involves asking new hires for upfront payments (legitimate U.S. employers almost never do this).
   - Check the sender's email domain; scammers often use slightly altered domains or free services (e.g., @gmail.com or @outlook.com) while pretending to be from a large corporation.
3. **Notify your bank immediately if you have already deposited a check.** **Explain that you believe you are the target of a "check overpayment scam" and ask the bank to flag the transaction before the check bounces.**
   - Be aware that when the check is eventually identified as fraudulent, the bank will likely reverse the entire deposit, leaving you responsible for any money you spent or sent elsewhere.
4. **Demand standard onboarding documentation if the situation appears to be a legitimate but non-compliant business.** **Ask for a formal offer letter, an IRS Form W-4, an I-9 form, and a written policy regarding equipment and training reimbursements.**
   - If the employer refuses to provide these or insists on payment before documentation, cease interaction.
5. **Evaluate the request against federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) rules.** **Understand that federal law generally prohibits employers from requiring employees to pay for "tools of the trade" or uniforms if the cost brings the employee's net pay below the federal minimum wage ($7.25 per hour as of 2026).**
   - Note that many states (e.g., California, New York, Illinois) have much stricter reimbursement laws that require the employer to pay for all necessary business expenses regardless of the wage level.
6. **Confirm the compensability of mandatory training.** **Clarify that under 29 C.F.R. § 785.27, training is considered compensable work time unless it is voluntary, outside regular hours, not job-related, and involves no productive work.**
   - If an employer requires "pre-employment training" that is mandatory and job-specific, they must generally pay you for that time; charging you for it is a major red flag.
7. **Report the incident to federal authorities.** **File a report with the Federal Trade Commission at ReportFraud.ftc.gov and, if a digital check or online communication was involved, file a complaint with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) at ic3.gov.**
   - If you shared your Social Security Number, visit IdentityTheft.gov to start a recovery plan and consider placing a credit freeze.

## What can wait
- You do not need to worry about "missing out" on the job; legitimate employers will understand a request for verification.
- You do not need to argue legal points with the recruiter; simply stating "I do not pay upfront costs" is a complete response.
- You can wait to file a formal wage-and-hour claim with the Department of Labor until you have confirmed the company is a real entity and not a scammer.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressured or excited about a new job can make these requests seem reasonable. However, it is a standard business practice in the USA for the employer to provide the necessary tools or reimburse expenses after you have started work. Pausing to verify is a sign of professional diligence, not a reason for a legitimate offer to be rescinded.

## Scope note
This guide addresses the immediate response to requests for upfront payment during the hiring process. It covers both fraudulent "check overpayment" scams and potential wage-and-hour violations by legitimate companies. It does not provide legal representation for ongoing labor disputes.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Employment laws and fraud tactics can change. If you have lost significant funds, contact local law enforcement and a qualified employment attorney in your jurisdiction.
