# What to do if… a lender or subscription service pulls more money than you authorised from your account (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank or card issuer promptly, say that the amount was not what you authorized, and ask them to open the correct dispute route. Ask what control is available to prevent another payment from the same company while the issue is reviewed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for the next billing cycle if the debit affects rent, food, utilities, or another essential cost.
- Do not assume that cancelling a subscription automatically stops a bank-account automatic debit.
- Do not assume that one payment-control method works for every transaction type. Bank-account debits, debit-card transactions, and credit-card charges can be handled differently.
- Do not close the account or replace a card as your first step unless your bank or card issuer recommends it or you suspect wider compromise.
- Do not discard the transaction details, cancellation confirmation, payment authorization, emails, screenshots, or reference numbers.
- Do not ignore fees caused by the disputed payment. Ask the bank to review them.
- If you suspect card compromise beyond this company, do not rely only on a merchant dispute. Use the issuer’s card-freeze option if available and contact the issuer promptly.

## What to do now
1. **Check how the money was taken.**
   - Look at the transaction details and identify whether it was a bank-account automatic debit, a debit-card transaction, or a credit-card charge.
   - Save the date, amount, company name, transaction description, and any reference number.
   - Save any record showing the amount you agreed to, the date you cancelled, or the date you revoked permission.

2. **Contact your bank or card issuer now.**
   - Say: “This company took more than I authorized” or “This payment was taken after I cancelled or revoked authorization.”
   - Ask the bank or card issuer to open the correct dispute for that transaction type.
   - Ask what control is available to prevent another payment from the same company and whether you need to complete a form or take any further step.

3. **For a bank-account automatic debit, revoke permission and ask about stop payment.**
   - Tell the company in writing that you revoke authorization for future automatic debits from your bank account. Keep a copy.
   - Tell your bank or credit union that you revoked authorization and ask it to stop future automatic debits from that company.
   - If another preauthorized debit is scheduled, ask for a stop-payment order as early as possible. For a scheduled preauthorized electronic fund transfer, notice given to the bank at least three business days before the transfer is important.
   - Ask whether the bank requires written confirmation and where to send it. If written confirmation is required after an oral stop-payment request, send it within 14 days.

4. **Start the dispute promptly and preserve the formal route.**
   - For a bank-account or debit-card electronic-transfer error, tell the financial institution promptly. Explain the type, date, and amount of the error. To use the Regulation E error-resolution process, the institution generally must receive your notice no later than 60 days after it sends the periodic statement that first shows the error.
   - The financial institution may require written confirmation within 10 business days after an oral electronic-transfer error notice. Send the confirmation promptly using the address or process it gives you.
   - For a credit-card billing error, use the issuer’s dispute process promptly and send a written billing-error notice to the billing-inquiries address shown by the issuer. To preserve the Regulation Z billing-error route, the creditor generally must receive the written notice no later than 60 days after it sends the first periodic statement showing the alleged error.
   - Keep a copy of every message and note the date, time, and reference number for every call.

5. **If the payment was from a lender, arrange the next correct payment.**
   - Ask the lender in writing for the correct amount due.
   - Ask how to pay manually while the automatic-payment issue is being handled.
   - Keep paying any amount you still owe using the agreed method. Stopping an automatic debit does not cancel the debt.

6. **Protect essential spending.**
   - Turn on transaction and low-balance alerts if they are available.
   - If the extra debit has affected essential costs, tell the bank and ask it to review any related overdraft or returned-payment fees.
   - Explain which fees followed from the disputed debit and keep a note of the bank’s response.

7. **Escalate if the bank, card issuer, lender, or subscription service does not resolve the problem.**
   - For a bank, card issuer, or consumer-lender problem, consider submitting a complaint to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau.
   - For a subscription cancellation problem or a subscription charge you did not agree to, consider reporting it to the Federal Trade Commission.
   - You can also find your state attorney general through the USAGov directory.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to keep the service or lender relationship long-term.
- Replacing every automatic payment or moving to a different bank unless your bank recommends an urgent change.
- Writing a long account of what happened. A short timeline and the key records are enough for the first contact.
- Negotiating anything beyond stopping repeat payments, disputing the incorrect amount, protecting essential costs, and arranging any correct loan payment.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to solve the full dispute immediately. The useful first steps are to stop repeat payments where possible, keep the key records, and start the correct dispute route promptly.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Contract terms, unresolved account problems, debt disputes, and any credit-reporting issue may need later specialist help.
