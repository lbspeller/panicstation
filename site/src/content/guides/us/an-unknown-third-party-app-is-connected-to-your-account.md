# What to do if… an unknown third-party app is connected to your account (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the connection as a possible account compromise. Use the account provider’s real website or app to remove the unfamiliar connection, then review sign-in security and recent account changes.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use links in an alert email or text. Open the provider’s real website or app yourself.
- Don’t dismiss an unfamiliar connection only because a permission label sounds limited.
- Don’t approve new prompts or share verification codes while you are checking the account.
- Don’t make unnecessary purchases, password resets, or other sensitive changes until you have removed the unfamiliar connection. Use the provider’s official recovery steps when needed.
- Don’t delete relevant alerts or notifications before saving a simple screenshot or note, when practical.
- Don’t remove every legitimate connection in a rush. Focus on the unfamiliar one first.

## What to do now
1. **Open the account through a trusted route.**  
   Type the provider’s website address yourself, use a saved bookmark, or open the provider’s official app. Do not sign in through the alert.

2. **Remove the unfamiliar connection.**  
   Look for an area such as **Security**, **Privacy**, **Connected apps**, **Linked apps**, **Third-party access**, or **App permissions**. Select the app you do not recognise, note the permissions shown, and remove or revoke its access.

3. **Review recent sessions and devices.**  
   Remove devices or sessions you do not recognise. If the provider offers a **sign out of all devices** or **sign out of all sessions** option, use it.

4. **Review recovery and sign-in settings.**  
   Check the recovery email address, phone number, MFA methods, and trusted devices. Remove anything you did not add. If the provider shows app passwords, remove any unfamiliar ones.

5. **Change the password when needed.**  
   Change it if you did not authorize the app connection, you see other unfamiliar changes, or the provider’s recovery instructions tell you to do so. Use a new, unique password. If the old password was reused elsewhere, change it on those accounts too, starting with your email and password manager.

6. **Turn on multi-factor authentication if it is not already enabled.**  
   If the provider offers several methods, use an authenticator app or security key when available. These are safer than text-message or email codes.

7. **Check email rules if this is an email account or your recovery email.**  
   Review forwarding, filters, rules, and delegated access if your provider offers them. Remove anything unfamiliar.

8. **Check for signs of wider account use.**  
   Review recent security alerts, sent messages, deleted messages, and recent account activity where available. Save a simple record of changes you did not make.

9. **Run a device security check if the connection may have followed phishing or malware.**  
   Update the device’s security software and run a scan. Use a different trusted device for account recovery if unfamiliar changes continue.

10. **Use official support if you cannot fully remove access.**  
   Use the provider’s official recovery or support route if the app reappears, settings keep changing, or you cannot remove unfamiliar sessions.

11. **Use IdentityTheft.gov if personal information is being misused.**  
   If someone used your information to open accounts, make purchases, or impersonate you, follow the official recovery steps at IdentityTheft.gov. Contact your bank or card issuer through its official app or a number from your card or statement if there are unfamiliar transactions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to delete the account.
- You do not need to review every old connected app before removing the unfamiliar one.
- You do not need to change every password in one sitting. Secure the main account and any reused password first.
- Decisions about wider monitoring or a deeper cleanup can wait until access is stable.

## Important reassurance
Removing the unfamiliar connection can block future access through that connection. It does not undo actions already taken or data already copied, so take the next checks one step at a time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A business, school, or managed account may need help from its administrator or provider support team. Wider fraud or identity theft may need specialist recovery steps.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, forensic, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Use official provider support and official reporting routes for your situation.
