# What to do if… a chimney pot, cap, or top section looks shifted after high winds and could fall (USA)

## Short answer
Evacuate the area immediately beneath the chimney and call 911 if the unstable structure threatens a public walkway, driveway, or street. Do not use any connected fireplaces or heating appliances until a professional inspection is completed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not climb onto the roof or use a ladder to inspect or secure the chimney.
- Do not stand directly under the chimney to take photographs or videos.
- Do not use any fireplace, wood stove, or gas appliance that vents through the damaged chimney.
- Do not attempt to move debris that has already fallen if it is located directly under an unstable section.
- Do not ignore the situation even if the wind has died down, as shifted masonry can fall at any time.

## What to do now
1. **Establish an exclusion zone around the base of the chimney.**  
   **Keep all people and pets at a distance of at least 1.5 times the height of the chimney away from the potential fall area.**
   - Ensure no one enters the "no-go" zone, including delivery drivers or neighbors.
   - Use caution near exterior doors that exit directly into the fall zone.
2. **Call 911 if the unstable chimney threatens a public area.**  
   **Contact emergency services immediately if the chimney is leaning toward a sidewalk, a shared driveway, a public street, or a neighbor's property.**
   - Tell the dispatcher there is an "unstable structural hazard with a risk of falling debris onto a public right-of-way."
3. **Shut down all appliances connected to the chimney flue.**  
   **Turn off fireplaces, wood stoves, and any gas furnaces or water heaters that utilize that specific chimney for venting.**
   - A shifted top can cause a flue blockage or "backdrafting," which forces carbon monoxide into the living space.
4. **Move vehicles out of the fall zone only if it is safe to do so.**  
   **Do not enter the exclusion zone or walk under the chimney to reach a vehicle.**
   - If the car is already in the danger zone, leave it there until the structure is stabilized.
5. **Contact a certified chimney professional or roofing contractor.**  
   **Request an urgent "make-safe" visit to stabilize the structure and prevent it from falling.**
   - Specify that you need an inspection following storm damage, which typically requires an NFPA 211 Level 2 or Level 3 evaluation.
   - For rental properties, notify the landlord or property management company of the "urgent structural safety hazard" immediately.
6. **Monitor for symptoms of carbon monoxide exposure.**  
   **If anyone in the home experiences sudden headaches, dizziness, or nausea, evacuate the building immediately and call 911.**
   - If a carbon monoxide alarm sounds, do not assume it is a malfunction; treat it as a life-threatening emergency.
7. **Document the hazard from a safe distance.**  
   **Take clear photos of the shifted chimney section using a zoom lens from a position well outside the fall zone.**
   - Note the time of the storm and any specific noises (grinding, cracking) heard from the chimney.

## What can wait
- Deciding on permanent aesthetic repairs or masonry repointing.
- Filing a detailed insurance claim beyond the initial notification of the hazard.
- Removing fallen bricks or debris from the ground until the overhead structure is professionally secured.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel anxious when a part of your home's structure appears unstable. By clearing the area and stopping the use of the chimney, you have already taken the most critical steps to prevent injury or fire. Professional stabilization is a standard procedure following high-wind events.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. A shifted chimney often indicates internal flue damage that is not visible from the ground. A comprehensive inspection by a certified professional is required before you can safely use your heating system again.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance only and does not constitute structural engineering or legal advice. If a structure is in immediate danger of collapse or poses a risk to the public, contact your local emergency services (911).
