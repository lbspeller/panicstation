# What to do if… you receive notice that wages may be garnished because of a court order you did not expect (USA)

## Short answer
Obtain the court name and case number from your payroll department and verify the case directly with the court clerk to check for improper service. If you were never served or the debt is not yours, you must file a motion to vacate the judgment and request an immediate stay of the garnishment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use contact information provided in the notice (phone numbers or links) until you have verified the court or agency independently through a government directory.
- Do not rely on a debt collector’s explanation of your rights or deadlines; follow only the instructions provided by the court clerk or official government forms.
- Do not ignore the notice or delay action; missing a filing deadline can finalize a default judgment and make it much harder to recover funds already taken.
- Do not give sensitive personal information, such as your Social Security number or bank details, to anyone calling about a "pending garnishment" without a verified case number.
- Do not quit your job or change employers as an immediate response; federal law provides specific protections against termination for a single garnishment.

## What to do now
1. **Request a complete copy of the garnishment order and all supporting documents from your HR or payroll department immediately.** Ensure you receive the name of the court or issuing agency, the case number, the specific document type (such as a "Writ of Garnishment"), the creditor’s name, and the listed attorney or collection firm.
2. **Contact the clerk of the court listed on the documents to verify the existence and status of the case.** Provide the case number and ask the clerk for the "Affidavit of Service" or "Proof of Service" to see when and where the creditor claims you were notified of the lawsuit.
3. **Determine the type of debt involved, as government and private debts follow different legal paths.** Private consumer debts (credit cards, medical bills) typically require a court judgment first, whereas government-related debts (IRS levies, child support, or federal student loans) may use administrative orders without a prior lawsuit.
   - Note: As of early 2026, most involuntary collections for defaulted federal student loans remain temporarily paused under the Working Families Tax Cuts Act reforms.
4. **Inform the court clerk if you were never served with the original lawsuit and ask for the procedure to file a "Motion to Vacate" or "Motion to Set Aside" a default judgment.** Ask specifically for the forms required to request a "Stay of Garnishment" (a temporary pause) while your motion to vacate is being considered by a judge.
5. **Request the local "Claim of Exemption" or "Objection to Garnishment" forms from the court if the debt is yours but the amount taken will cause extreme hardship.** Check if you qualify for state or federal exemptions, such as those for Social Security benefits, veterans' benefits, or income falling below the federal minimum threshold ($217.50 weekly disposable income as of 2026).
6. **Perform a name and identifier check to rule out mistaken identity or "same name" errors.** Compare your full name, middle initial, and past addresses against the information in the court file; if they do not match, file a written notice with the court and the creditor's attorney stating you are the wrong person.
7. **Locate a local Legal Aid office or a recognized consumer law clinic if you are unable to navigate the filing process alone.** Ask for assistance with "improper service" or "default judgment defense" to ensure your motions are filed correctly within the strict local timelines.
8. **Document any communication from your employer regarding your job security in relation to the garnishment.** Federal law (the Consumer Credit Protection Act) prohibits an employer from firing you because your wages are garnished for any one debt, regardless of how many proceedings are brought to collect that specific debt.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a long-term debt settlement or bankruptcy filing in the first hour; focusing on the immediate motion to stay the garnishment is the priority.
- You do not need to provide payroll with an explanation of your financial history; they are generally only required to follow the instructions on the court order or a subsequent release.
- You do not need to argue the merits of the original debt over the phone with a collector; those arguments belong in your written court filings.

## Important reassurance
It is a common occurrence for legal papers to be sent to old addresses or for "sewer service" (improper service) to result in a surprise judgment. Receiving this notice does not mean you have lost your rights; the court system has specific, standard procedures for reopening cases where the defendant was not properly notified. Taking the step of verifying the court record is the standard way to regain control of the process.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and verification only. Garnishment laws, exemption amounts, and the specific forms required to vacate a judgment vary significantly by state and type of debt.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Wage garnishment involves strict legal timelines and procedural rules that vary by jurisdiction. You should consult with the court clerk or a qualified legal professional to ensure you meet all local filing requirements.
