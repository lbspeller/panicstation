# What to do if… you start vomiting blood or see coffee-ground material in your vomit (USA)

## Short answer
If you are vomiting blood or coffee-ground material, get emergency medical care now. Call 911 if it is ongoing, more than a small streak, you feel faint, confused, short of breath, very weak, clammy, or you cannot get to the ER safely. Do not drive yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to drive yourself to the ER, because you could faint or choke if you vomit again.
- Do not lie flat on your back if you might vomit again.
- Do not take aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen, or other NSAIDs for pain unless a clinician specifically told you to.
- Do not drink alcohol or take “stomach settling” remedies.
- Do not assume it is food, supplements, or dye if it looks like blood or coffee grounds.
- Do not delay care to see if it stops, especially if it is more than a trace, happens again, or you feel weak or dizzy.

## What to do now
1. Choose the safest urgent route:
   - Call 911 if you feel faint, confused, short of breath, very weak, clammy, have chest pain or severe belly pain, are actively vomiting blood or coffee-ground material, it is more than a small streak, or you are alone without safe transport.
   - If you are fully alert and stable and a responsible adult can take you safely, have them take you to the ER now.
2. Position yourself to reduce choking risk:
   - Sit upright and lean forward slightly, or lie on your side with your head turned.
   - Keep a bowl, bag, or bin nearby.
3. Ask someone to stay with you if possible:
   - Have them keep you sitting upright or side-lying.
   - Ask them to unlock the door for EMS if you are calling 911.
   - If children or pets are nearby, ask them to manage immediate safety and keep the space clear.
4. Get key details ready for EMS or the ER:
   - What it looked like: bright red, dark red, brown, black, grainy, or like coffee grounds.
   - About how much there was: streaks, spoonfuls, mouthfuls, or more.
   - Whether you have black or tarry stool, severe belly pain, chest pain, fainting, shortness of breath, confusion, or weakness.
   - Your medicines, especially blood thinners such as warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban, or dabigatran; aspirin; steroids; or frequent NSAID use.
   - Any history of ulcers, liver disease, heavy alcohol use, or recent forceful vomiting.
5. If it is safe and does not delay care, keep what clinicians may need to see:
   - Keep the container you vomited into, or take a quick photo.
   - Do not delay calling 911 or going to the ER to do this.
6. Do not eat or drink unless a dispatcher or clinician tells you to:
   - If your mouth is very dry, rinse and spit rather than swallowing.
7. If you worsen at any point, call 911 or tell the dispatcher immediately if you are already on the line.

## What can wait
- Working out the cause yourself.
- Calling primary care first or waiting for an appointment.
- Cleaning up properly, beyond making the area safe from slips.
- Deciding whether it is “enough blood” to matter.
- Searching symptoms online once emergency care is already the next step.

## Important reassurance
Seeing blood or coffee-ground material in vomit is frightening, and it is normal to second-guess yourself. Getting urgent emergency evaluation is a safety-first response, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and get you to urgent evaluation. Diagnosis and treatment decisions should be made by clinicians after assessment.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical advice or a diagnosis. If you vomit blood or material that looks like coffee grounds, seek emergency medical care immediately.
