# What to do if… you find pets left alone after their owner has died and you need urgent care for them (USA)

## Short answer
Make sure the pets have a safe temporary space and fresh water without putting yourself at risk. Then contact an authorized family or estate contact if known, and ask local animal control, a shelter, or a veterinary clinic for the local short-term care process.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t force entry into a home or put yourself at risk to reach an animal.
- Don’t chase, corner, or grab a frightened or aggressive animal.
- Don’t break up an animal fight with your hands.
- Don’t give away, sell, or permanently rehome the pets informally.
- Don’t assume that taking the pets in makes you their owner. Local reporting or custody rules may apply.
- Don’t give human medication, sedatives, or leftover pet medication unless a veterinarian directs you to.
- Don’t try to make an animal vomit unless a veterinarian or poison-control service directs you to.
- Don’t mix the pets with your own animals until you have advice about safe separation.

## What to do now
1. **Contain the pets safely if you can.**  
   Put each animal in a secure room, crate, carrier, or other suitable space away from open doors and hazards. Keep unfamiliar animals separate from each other and from your own pets where possible. Provide fresh water. Offer their usual food only if you can identify it.

2. **Get help with access rather than forcing entry.**  
   If the pets are inside a locked property and you are not authorized to enter, contact an authorized family or estate contact if known. You can also ask local animal control and, where relevant, building management or the landlord what access route is available. A police non-emergency line may also be able to direct you to the appropriate local service.

3. **Get urgent veterinary advice for emergency signs or possible poisoning.**  
   Call a veterinary clinic or emergency veterinary hospital promptly if an animal is struggling to breathe, is unresponsive, has seizures, is bleeding heavily, has experienced known trauma, or may have swallowed or touched something toxic. For a possible toxic exposure, you can also call ASPCA Poison Control at (888) 426-4435. A consultation fee may apply.

4. **Check visible pet-care information if you are authorized to be in the property.**  
   Look for the veterinarian’s details, feeding notes, medication instructions, carriers, leashes, identification tags, microchip information, and warnings about bites, escapes, or animals that must be kept apart.

5. **Contact the person who can approve short-term care if known.**  
   This may be a family contact, executor, administrator, or another authorized estate contact. Ask who can approve a temporary caretaker or boarding arrangement. Request written confirmation by text or email where possible.

6. **Ask local animal control or a local shelter about the local process.**  
   Explain that the owner has died and the pets need urgent temporary care. Ask whether you should file a report, where the pets can stay temporarily, and whether any local reporting or temporary-custody steps apply.

7. **Arrange a microchip scan when practical.**  
   A veterinary clinic or shelter can scan for a microchip. They can help identify the relevant registry and advise how the registered contacts can be approached.

8. **Ask what temporary care is available if nobody can take the pets today.**  
   A veterinary clinic may offer boarding or suggest a provider. Local animal control, a municipal shelter, a humane society, or an SPCA may be able to explain available intake, foster, or referral options. Tell them the species, number of animals, and any urgent needs when you call.

9. **Write down the essentials.**  
   Record when the pets were found, where they are staying, what food or medication was found, what care was provided, and who was contacted. Share this with the authorized family or estate contact when possible.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide permanent ownership or rehoming today.
- You do not have to settle estate questions before arranging basic temporary care.
- You do not have to contact many organizations at once. Start with one local animal-control or shelter contact and one veterinary clinic if needed.
- You do not have to sort the home or belongings beyond the visible pet-care information needed for immediate care.
- You do not have to make long-term decisions while you are overwhelmed.

## Important reassurance
Providing water, safe containment, and a documented handover is a useful first response. It is reasonable to ask local services for help rather than trying to solve every decision alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation. Later decisions about ownership, estate authority, costs, boarding, or rehoming may need local animal-services guidance and specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, veterinary, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If an animal appears unwell, contact a veterinary clinic. If you cannot safely approach or contain an animal, step back and contact local animal control or a shelter.
