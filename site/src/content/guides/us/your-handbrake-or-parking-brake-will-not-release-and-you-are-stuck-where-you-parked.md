# What to do if… your handbrake or parking brake will not release and you are stuck where you parked (USA)

## Short answer
Keep the vehicle stopped and visible. Try the correct release sequence once or twice for your brake type; if it still will not release, stop forcing it and call roadside assistance or a tow company.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to “drive through it” or use heavy throttle to break it free.
- Do not repeatedly force the lever, pedal, or switch if it feels jammed.
- Do not get under the vehicle in a parking lot, garage, shoulder, or roadside area to pull cables or pry parts.
- Do not use open flames, torches, improvised heat, or boiling water around wheels or brakes.
- Do not keep trying to move the vehicle if you notice a burning smell, smoke, loud grinding, unusual heat near a wheel, or strong resistance.

## What to do now
1. **Make the vehicle visible and reduce immediate risk.**
   - Turn on hazard lights if other vehicles or pedestrians may approach.
   - If you are in a travel lane, on a blind curve, on a highway shoulder, or at high risk of being hit, stay belted if that is the safer place and call 911.

2. **Use the release method that matches your brake type.**
   - **Hand lever:** hold the release button, lift the lever slightly, then lower it fully while still holding the button.
   - **Foot parking brake pedal:** press the main brake pedal, then press the parking brake pedal fully again to release it.
   - **Electronic parking brake:** with the ignition on or the engine running, press the main brake pedal firmly and operate the parking brake switch as shown by your vehicle’s dash prompt or owner’s manual.

3. **Make one clean restart attempt.**
   - Put the vehicle in Park for an automatic, or neutral with the main brake pressed for a manual.
   - Turn the vehicle off, wait a short moment, restart, press the main brake pedal firmly, and try the correct release once more.
   - If a brake-system warning message stays on or the parking brake still will not release, treat the vehicle as not safe to drive.

4. **Stop if the brakes seem to be dragging or seized.**
   - Burning odor, smoke, grinding, strong resistance, or unusual heat near a wheel means stop trying to move the vehicle.
   - Tell roadside assistance or the tow company: “The parking brake is stuck on and the vehicle will not move.”

5. **If cold weather may have frozen the brake, keep it gentle.**
   - If there is snow or slush around the vehicle, make sure the tailpipe is clear before idling the engine.
   - Let the vehicle warm normally for a short period, then try the correct release sequence once more.
   - If it does not release quickly, stop and call for help rather than adding heat, chemicals, or force.

6. **Get help early if you are blocking, unsafe, or still stuck.**
   - Call your roadside assistance provider, insurer, manufacturer assistance line, or a local tow company.
   - If you are in a managed lot or parking garage, tell security or management that the vehicle is disabled and help is on the way.

7. **While waiting, keep the vehicle stable and easy to find.**
   - Keep the vehicle stationary: Park for an automatic, or 1st gear or reverse for a manual if you are parked and waiting.
   - Note the exact location: garage level, section, nearest pillar number, address, cross-streets, or parking-space number.
   - Keep your phone charged and stay in a safe, visible place.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the problem is a cable, caliper, drum shoe, switch, battery, or electronic module right now.
- You do not need to choose a repair shop or approve repairs while you are still stuck.
- You do not need to try multiple DIY fixes after the safe release sequence and one restart attempt.

## Important reassurance
A stuck parking brake can feel alarming, but it can happen after cold, wet, rusty, or long parking conditions. The safer path is to avoid forcing the vehicle, keep the situation controlled, and bring in roadside or tow help if the brake will not release quickly.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk and avoid damage. Later decisions may need a mechanic, roadside professional, manufacturer support, insurer, or other specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information for immediate first actions, not mechanical, legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger where you are parked, prioritize safety and emergency services.
