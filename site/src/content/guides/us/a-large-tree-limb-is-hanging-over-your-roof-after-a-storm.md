# What to do if… a large tree limb is hanging over your roof after a storm (USA)

## Short answer
Keep everyone out of the fall zone and off the roof immediately. If the limb is within 10 feet of a power line or utility pole, stay at least 35 feet away and report it as a life-safety hazard.

## Do not do these things
- Don't climb onto the roof or use a ladder to inspect the damage.
- Don't attempt to cut, pull, or move the limb yourself.
- Don't touch any wires, cables, or metallic objects contacting the tree.
- Don't enter or touch vehicles parked under or near the hanging limb.
- Don't stand directly underneath the limb to take photographs or video.
- Don't assume a line is "just a phone line" or "dead" because the power is out.

## What to do now
1. **Establish a safe perimeter around the potential fall zone.** **Keep people and pets inside and block off any paths, driveways, or doors that lead into the area where the limb could drop or swing.**
2. **Identify electrical hazards from a safe distance of at least 35 feet.** **If the limb is touching a line, sparking, or hanging within 10 feet of utility infrastructure, call 911 immediately and then notify your electric utility provider.**
   - Do not approach the area even if the ground is dry, as electricity can travel through the root system or wet wood.
3. **Inspect the interior of the home for structural compromise without entering the rooms directly under the limb.** **Look for visible signs of damage such as new cracks in the ceiling, sagging drywall, daylight visible through the roof, or the sound of popping and creaking.**
   - If these signs are present, relocate all occupants to a different part of the building or evacuate if the structure appears unstable.
4. **Take photographs only from a safe, distant vantage point once the immediate area is secured.** **Document the position of the limb and any visible roof impact for your insurance record, noting the time and weather conditions.**
5. **Contact your homeowners insurance company to report the "hanging limb hazard."** **Ask specifically if they require an inspection by their own adjuster before you authorize "make safe" emergency removal and if they have preferred licensed arborists in your area.**
6. **Hire a professional tree service that carries both liability and workers' compensation insurance.** **Verify they follow current ANSI Z133 safety standards for storm-damage operations before they begin work on your property.**
7. **Notify neighbors briefly if the tree is rooted on their property.** **Send a simple, factual message (text or email) stating that a hazardous limb from their tree is currently threatening your roof and that you are contacting your insurer.**

## What can wait
- You don't need to determine who is "at fault" or liable for costs in the first few hours.
- You don't need to seek multiple competitive bids for emergency "make safe" work if your insurer authorizes immediate stabilization.
- You don't need to begin permanent roof repairs until the tree has been professionally removed and the site is cleared.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of urgency when your home is threatened, but storm-damaged trees are physically unpredictable and often under extreme tension. Taking a safe pause and waiting for utility and tree professionals is the most effective way to prevent injury.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for hanging tree limbs. It does not cover technical rigging, permanent structural repairs, or the legal resolution of property line disputes.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and is not professional legal or arboricultural advice. Always prioritize instructions from emergency responders and utility workers on-site.
