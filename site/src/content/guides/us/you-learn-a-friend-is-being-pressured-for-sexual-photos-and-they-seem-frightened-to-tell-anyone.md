# What to do if… you learn a friend is being pressured for sexual photos and they seem frightened to tell anyone (USA)

## Short answer
Connect your friend to one safe adult or a specialist support service immediately to ensure they are not facing this situation alone. Prioritize their physical safety and emotional stabilization over investigating the details or confronting the person pressuring them.

## Do not do these things
- Do not blame the friend for any previous messages, photos, or interactions they had with the person.
- Do not pressure the friend to make a police report immediately if they are not in physical danger and are not ready.
- Do not contact, message, or threaten the person who is pressuring your friend.
- Do not ask to see the images or ask the friend to describe the sexual content in detail.
- Do not download, save, or share the images yourself, as possessing or distributing images of minors is a crime.
- Do not promise total secrecy if you believe the friend is a minor in danger or if other children are at risk.
- Do not tell the friend to delete all evidence immediately, as it may be needed for future reporting or takedown requests.

## What to do now
1. **Verify their immediate physical safety before taking other steps.**  
   Confirm that they are not in the same physical location as the person pressuring them and that there is no immediate threat of physical violence. If they are in immediate danger or believe the person is coming to their home, call **911** immediately.

2. **Help them identify one safe, trusted adult to speak with today.**  
   Suggest a parent, a school counselor, a healthcare provider, or a campus Title IX coordinator. If they are struggling to find the words, help them draft a simple message: "I am being pressured online and I’m scared to handle it alone. Can we talk?"

3. **Contact a specialist support service for confidential guidance.**  
   Help them reach out to the **National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN)** at **800-656-HOPE (4673)**, via online chat at rainn.org, or by texting "HOPE" to **64673**. These services are available 24/7 and can provide support without requiring an immediate police report.

4. **Report the situation to the appropriate federal authorities if the friend is under 18.**  
   Use **NCMEC’s CyberTipline** (report.cybertip.org) or call **1-800-843-5678** to report suspected online child sexual exploitation. You can also contact the **FBI** at **1-800-CALL-FBI (225-5324)** or submit a tip at tips.fbi.gov, as they treat sextortion of minors as a high-priority crime.

5. **Utilize official takedown tools to stop the spread of images.**  
   If the friend is under 18, use the **Take It Down** tool (takeitdown.ncmec.org) on the device where the images are stored; do not send or upload the images to another device first. For those 18 and older, use **stopncii.org** to help prevent the sharing of non-consensual intimate images.

6. **Exercise federal rights regarding image removal from platforms.**  
   Under the **Take It Down Act**, online platforms are required to remove non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) within 48 hours of a valid request. If a platform refuses to remove the content or does not have a clear reporting process, you can report the violation to the **FTC** at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.

7. **Secure accounts and limit further contact without deleting evidence.**  
   Encourage the friend to block the individual and set all social media profiles to private. Save basic information such as the person’s username, the platform used, and the nature of the threats in a secure place (like a written note or a single screenshot), then stop all further communication.

## What can wait
- They do not need to decide right now whether they want to press criminal charges.
- They do not need to provide a full chronological history of the relationship or interactions.
- They do not need to resolve how this will impact their long-term social standing or school status today.
- They do not need to explain the situation to their entire friend group or family.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for people in this situation to feel intense shame, panic, or the belief that they have "ruined" their lives. These feelings are the exact tools used by those who pressure others. Reaching out for help is the most effective way to break the cycle of pressure, and there are established legal and technical systems designed specifically to protect people from this type of exploitation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions. Long-term recovery, legal proceedings, or school-based disciplinary actions will require ongoing support from professionals.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal or clinical advice. Because laws regarding digital privacy and sexual exploitation vary by state and are subject to federal updates, involving a trusted adult or a specialist service early is the safest course of action.
