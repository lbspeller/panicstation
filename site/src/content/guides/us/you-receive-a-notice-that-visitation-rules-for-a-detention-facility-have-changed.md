# What to do if… you receive a notice that visitation rules for a detention facility have changed (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the notice directly with the facility or corrections department using official contact details, not links or phone numbers inside the notice. Then respond through a confirmed official channel with only the information needed to confirm your next step.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t travel to the facility until you have confirmed the current visiting status, your approval status, and any booking requirement.
- Don’t send your Social Security number, full ID scan, payment details, or passwords through an email, text, QR code, or link you have not verified from an official site.
- Don’t click attachments or links in a suspicious notice before checking the facility’s official website.
- Don’t argue, threaten, or accuse staff in writing; keep the response calm and factual.
- Don’t assume one rule applies everywhere. Federal prisons, state prisons, county jails, city jails, immigration detention facilities, and other detention settings may use different visiting systems.
- Don’t try to get around new rules about visitor limits, identification, dress code, minors, items, or scheduling; it may lead to a longer restriction.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the person’s current location.**  
   Check whether the person is still at the same facility. A transfer can change the visiting rules, the approved visitor list, the visiting days, or the booking process.

2. **Identify what type of facility it is.**  
   Write down whether it is a federal prison, state prison, county jail, city jail, immigration detention facility, or another detention setting. If you are unsure, use the facility name from the notice and confirm it on an official government or facility website.

3. **Verify the notice without using the notice’s links.**  
   Go separately to the official facility, sheriff, corrections department, or federal agency website. Use the phone number, visitor information page, or official portal listed there. Ask:
   - “Is this notice legitimate and currently in effect?”
   - “Does it apply to all visitors, or only to me?”
   - “Does it cancel an existing visit, require rebooking, or require a new visitor application?”
   - “What is the safest official way for me to respond?”

4. **Check whether you are still approved to visit.**  
   Many facilities require approval before a visit. For federal prisons, the Bureau of Prisons says a person generally must place you on their visiting list and you must be cleared before visiting. If the notice says your status changed, ask what specific step is needed to restore or confirm approval.

5. **Pull out the immediate action items.**  
   Make a short list of only what matters now:
   - new visiting days or hours
   - whether an existing visit is cancelled
   - whether you must rebook or reconfirm
   - any new identification requirement
   - visitor limits, minor visitor rules, or dress code changes
   - whether the visit is now video-only
   - any stated response deadline

6. **Respond through a confirmed official method.**  
   Use only the official portal, official email address, postal address, or phone route you verified. Keep the message short:
   - acknowledge that you received the notice
   - state the person’s name and facility identifier only if the facility says that is needed
   - confirm the deadline you are following
   - ask only the questions you need answered now
   - state your intended next step, such as rebooking, resubmitting a visitor application, or switching to video visitation

7. **Save proof of what you were told.**  
   Keep the notice, screenshots of the official visiting page, confirmation numbers, emails, portal messages, and a simple call log with the date, time, number called, name or role of the person you spoke to, and what they said.

8. **If the notice may be phishing, pause before doing anything else.**  
   Do not open attachments, scan QR codes, pay fees, or send personal documents until the facility confirms the request through an official route. If you already clicked a link or entered a password, change that password from the real website and check the account for suspicious activity.

9. **If access is restricted, ask what alternative is available now.**  
   Ask whether there is a current video visitation process, a way to reschedule under the new rules, or a facility process for disability-related or medical-access needs.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to complain, escalate, or seek legal help unless there is an urgent deadline you have confirmed.
- You do not need to pay for travel, hotels, rides, or childcare until the visit is confirmed under the current rules.
- You do not need to gather extra documents until the facility tells you exactly what is required and how to submit them safely.
- You do not need to solve the whole visitation problem at once; first confirm whether the notice is real, whether the person is still there, and what action is due next.

## Important reassurance
A visitation notice can feel alarming because it affects contact with someone you care about. A careful check through official channels is a steady first step and can prevent travel, money, or identity mistakes.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on the facility type, the reason for the change, your visitor status, and whether you need help from the facility, a corrections department, an attorney, or another specialist.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and practical first steps. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Visitation rules are facility-specific and can change quickly, including short-notice suspensions.
