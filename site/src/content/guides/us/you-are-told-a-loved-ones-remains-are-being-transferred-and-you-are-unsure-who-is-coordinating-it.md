# What to do if… you are told a loved one’s remains are being transferred and you are unsure who is coordinating it (USA)
## Short answer
Call the office or facility said to be holding your loved one’s remains. Ask who currently has custody, who has authority to release the remains, where any transfer is going, who the intended recipient or provider is, and whether a temporary pause is available while you verify the details.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign or send a release authorization until you have verified the destination, the intended recipient or provider, and who requested the authorization.
- Do not pay a transport or handling fee until you have independently verified who is requesting it, what it is for, and how the official office or provider accepts payment.
- Do not send identification documents or signatures to an unfamiliar email address or phone number without calling back through an official main number.
- Do not assume that physical custody and authority to release the remains belong to the same office.
- Do not let several relatives send different instructions without first checking who is authorized to make decisions.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the details you were given.**  
   Note your loved one’s full name, date of birth, date and place of death, the caller’s name, organization, phone number, and the transfer details they gave you.

2. **Confirm who currently has custody.**  
   Call the facility said to be holding the remains using a number from its official website or main switchboard. If the situation is unclear, start with the hospital main line or the medical examiner or coroner office said to be handling the case.

3. **Ask four direct questions.**
   - “Do you currently have custody of the remains?”
   - “Who has authority to release the remains in this case?”
   - “Has a recipient, funeral home, or other provider been designated?”
   - “Is any transfer scheduled, and where is it going?”
   
   Ask for the case number, if there is one, and the staff member’s name.

4. **Ask what local authorization is required.**  
   Ask who the office recognizes as the person authorized to give instructions, whether a written form is required, and how the form should be submitted. Use only the method confirmed by the official office.

5. **If no recipient or provider has been confirmed, ask what is needed next.**  
   Ask whether a funeral home, another locally accepted provider, or another recipient must be designated. If you need time to verify the situation, ask whether a temporary pause is available and what the office needs from the authorized person.

6. **If someone says transport is already arranged, verify both sides.**  
   Ask for the recipient or provider’s name and the destination. Call the office holding the remains and the intended recipient or provider through independently verified numbers to confirm that they have matching instructions.

7. **Use one family contact for updates.**  
   Nominate one person to collect information and relay updates while you confirm who is authorized to make decisions. Ask each office to note that contact person if its process allows.

8. **If relatives disagree about who can decide, say so clearly.**  
   Tell the office holding the remains that there is a dispute about authorization. Ask it to note the dispute, explain what documentation it requires, and tell you whether it can pause release while the issue is checked.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide every detail of burial or cremation, a service, an obituary, or memorial arrangements right now.
- You do not need to complete insurance, estate, or bank steps before confirming custody and the intended recipient or provider.
- You do not need to settle every family disagreement immediately. Focus on verifying the holder, the release authority, and the intended recipient or provider.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to ask for the case number, custody status, release status, destination, and intended recipient or provider before sending documents or making payments. You only need to establish the next verified step.

## Scope note
These are first steps to clarify custody, authorization, and who is coordinating a transfer. Later decisions may need help from the local office handling the case, a funeral provider, or an appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Procedures vary by state, county, and office. Ask the official office handling the case what applies locally before signing documents or making payments.
