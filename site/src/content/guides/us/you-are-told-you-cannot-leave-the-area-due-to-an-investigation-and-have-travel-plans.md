# What to do if… you are told you cannot leave the area due to an investigation and have travel plans (USA)

## Short answer
Pause your travel plans until you know the exact source and wording of the restriction. A formal court or supervision condition can lead to a warrant, arrest, or other case consequences if it is violated, so get written clarification and legal advice before leaving any area that may be restricted.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume that a verbal statement is definitely a court order or definitely harmless.
- Do not leave any area named in your paperwork or instruction, including for an airport connection, until you have checked whether that is allowed.
- Do not make repeated calls or give new statements about the investigation while trying to clarify travel. Route questions through a lawyer when possible.
- Do not rely only on a verbal approval if your paperwork requires permission.
- Do not post about the investigation or your travel plans on social media.
- Do not buy new nonrefundable tickets while the restriction is unclear.

## What to do now
1. **Write down exactly what you were told.**
   - Note who told you, their organization, the date, the exact words used, and any case number or contact details.
   - Save any message, letter, release paperwork, bond or bail paperwork, supervision paperwork, or court notice.

2. **Check whether there is a formal document.**
   - If you were arrested, released, charged, or placed under supervision, look for a release order, bond or bail conditions, probation conditions, parole conditions, or pretrial supervision instructions.
   - A verbal statement alone does not show the legal status. If no document was provided, ask in writing which document, order, case, or supervision condition contains the restriction.
   - The relevant court clerk’s office can tell you how to obtain filed documents. It cannot give legal advice.

3. **Read the exact geographic boundary and approval route.**
   - Look for wording about a county, state, federal judicial district, another named area, international travel, passport surrender, or travel only with permission.
   - Check whether permission must come from the court, an assigned supervision contact, or both.
   - Do not assume that permission to travel within one area also covers another area or an airport outside that area.

4. **Contact your lawyer promptly and send the itinerary.**
   - Ask whether the restriction is binding, whether the planned route is allowed, and what written approval is needed.
   - If you do not have a lawyer and there is a court case, ask the court clerk how to obtain the filed order and whether a lawyer has been assigned. Seek advice from a qualified criminal defense lawyer.

5. **Use the assigned supervision channel if one applies.**
   - If you are on pretrial supervision, probation, parole, or supervised release, contact the officer or office named in your paperwork.
   - Ask what information is required, how much lead time applies, whether court approval is also needed, and what written proof you will receive.
   - Keep copies of the request and any written approval or amended order. Keep the approval accessible during travel if permission is granted.

6. **Make the booking reversible.**
   - Ask the airline, hotel, or other provider about date changes, credit, or a temporary hold.
   - Share only the information the provider says it needs.

7. **Check for related conditions before leaving.**
   - Check your paperwork for court dates, reporting duties, check-ins, curfew, passport surrender, location monitoring, and any instruction to report after returning.
   - Follow any communication method and deadlines stated in your paperwork or given by your assigned supervision contact.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the long-term legal strategy today.
- You do not need to cancel every booking before checking whether written permission is possible.
- You do not need to tell friends, relatives, or an employer the details of the investigation. You can say that an official restriction may affect travel.

## Important reassurance
The phrase “do not leave the area” can refer to different things, including a request, a court order, or a supervision condition. Checking the exact wording and the correct approval route is a practical way to reduce the risk of an accidental violation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about statements, interviews, evidence, court applications, or the investigation itself may need advice from a qualified lawyer in the relevant state or federal system.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. State and federal procedures vary, and the official resources below are federal examples. Follow the actual wording of your paperwork and obtain legal advice before traveling while the restriction is unclear.
