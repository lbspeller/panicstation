# What to do if… you find water pooling along a wall and it seems to be spreading (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as an active leak with a possible electrical hazard: keep people away from wet electrical items, shut off the water if you can do that safely, then contact urgent repair help.

## Do not do these things
- Do not walk into pooled water if it may be touching outlets, power strips, appliances, cords, or wet electrical equipment.
- Do not operate the breaker panel if you would have to stand in water or reach across wet surfaces.
- Do not touch wet cords, wet appliances, or devices sitting in or near the water.
- Do not keep running sinks, toilets, showers, washing machines, dishwashers, or other water-using fixtures to “test” the leak.
- Do not open walls, pull flooring, or cut drywall while water is still spreading or the electrical situation is unsafe.
- Do not stand under a bulging, sagging, or cracking ceiling.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Move children and pets away from the wet area. Put on shoes. If you see sparks, smell burning, see smoke or fire, or think someone has been shocked, call **911**.
2. **Reduce electrical risk.**
   - Do not touch outlets, cords, appliances, or power strips near the water.
   - If you can reach the breaker panel without stepping in water or touching wet surfaces, shut off power to the affected area, or shut off the main breaker.
   - If you cannot reach the panel safely, keep people out of the area and call a qualified electrician or building emergency maintenance.
3. **Stop the water if you can do it safely.**
   - Shut off the home’s main water valve if you know where it is and can reach it without going through the wet area.
   - Common locations include where the water line enters the home, near a water meter, in a basement, garage, utility room, crawlspace access area, or outside meter box.
   - If one fixture or appliance is clearly causing the leak and its local shutoff valve is dry and reachable, shut that valve too.
4. **If you are in an apartment or condo, report it immediately.** Contact building management, emergency maintenance, or your HOA contact. If water may be coming from above, alert the neighbor above without entering unsafe areas.
5. **Contain the spread from the dry side.** Place towels on the dry edge of the water, put a bucket or container under active drips, and move rugs, paper, cardboard, electronics, and soft items away from the wet edge.
6. **Treat the water as possibly contaminated if you are not sure.**
   - Likely cleaner water may come from a supply pipe or some appliance supply lines.
   - Possibly contaminated water may come from a toilet overflow, drain backup, sewage line, outdoor floodwater, or an unknown source.
   - Avoid splashing, keep pets away, and wash exposed skin if you touched questionable water.
7. **Call the right repair help.**
   - If you rent, contact your landlord or property manager using the emergency maintenance route if available.
   - If you own, call a plumber. If sewage, drain backup, or widespread soaking is involved, ask whether a water-damage or restoration professional is needed.
8. **Document briefly.** Take a few photos or a short video of the pooling, the wall line, the wet baseboard, any dripping, and any visible source before you move items, if it is safe to do so.
9. **After the leak is stopped and the area is electrically safe, start drying.** Ventilate if practical, remove standing water you can reach safely, and dry wet materials quickly, ideally within 24–48 hours if possible.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether this means major renovation.
- You do not need to determine fault, insurance coverage, or blame before stopping the water and reporting the problem.
- You do not need to diagnose the exact hidden path of the water before calling repair help.
- You do not need to open walls or remove flooring yourself while the leak is active or electricity may be unsafe.

## Important reassurance
Water can travel behind baseboards, inside wall cavities, and along floors before it appears, so the visible pooling may not show the true source. The useful first steps are simple: stay out of wet electrical areas, stop the water if you safely can, report it, contain what is spreading, and document enough for the people who need to fix it.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to stabilize the situation and reduce avoidable harm. Repairs, drying, restoration, mold cleanup, insurance, and landlord or HOA issues may need qualified professional help later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, electrical, plumbing, insurance, or other professional advice. If water is rising quickly, you cannot stop it, the ceiling is sagging, there is fire or shock risk, or the structure seems unsafe, keep people out and contact emergency services and qualified professionals.
