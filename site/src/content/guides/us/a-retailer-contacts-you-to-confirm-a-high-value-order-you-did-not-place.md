# What to do if… a retailer contacts you to confirm a high-value order you did not place (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the contact as unverified and end the interaction immediately without confirming any personal or financial details. Log in to the retailer’s official website or app independently to check your order history and contact your bank if you see unauthorized charges.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t stay on the line or reply to the message if you feel pressured or suspicious.
- Don’t click any links or call any phone numbers provided in the unsolicited message.
- Don’t provide a "one-time password," "verification code," or "security PIN" to the person contacting you.
- Don’t download any software or allow "remote access" to your device to resolve the issue.
- Don’t pay any "cancellation fees" or "refund processing fees" via gift cards, wire transfers, or cryptocurrency.
- Don’t confirm your full Social Security number, credit card number, or banking login credentials.

## What to do now
1. **End the communication immediately and do not engage with the sender.** **Hang up the phone or close the message without clicking any links or buttons.**
2. **Verify your account status through official channels only.** **Open a new browser window or the retailer's official mobile app and log in directly to your account.**
   - Navigate to the **Order History** or **My Orders** section.
   - Check for any recent transactions, even if they are marked as "Pending" or "Canceled."
   - Review your **Account Settings** for any new shipping addresses, phone numbers, or linked payment methods you do not recognize.
3. **Contact the retailer using a verified phone number.** **Locate the official customer service number on the retailer’s primary website or the back of a physical membership card.**
   - Ask the representative to confirm if an order was actually placed.
   - If a fraudulent order exists, request that they **cancel the order**, **freeze the account**, and **remove unauthorized information**.
4. **Review your financial statements for unauthorized activity.** **Log in to your online banking or credit card app to check for charges matching the retailer or the mentioned amount.**
   - If you see a suspicious transaction, call the **number on the back of your payment card** immediately.
   - Report the transaction as **unauthorized fraud** and ask to have the card replaced with a new number.
5. **Secure your primary digital accounts.** **Change the passwords for your email account and the retailer account immediately using unique, strong passwords.**
   - Enable **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)**—such as an authenticator app or SMS code—on all financial and shopping accounts.
6. **Report the scam attempt to the authorities.** **Submit a report at ReportFraud.ftc.gov if you received a scam contact but did not lose money.**
   - If your personal information was compromised or money was stolen, go to **IdentityTheft.gov** to create an official recovery plan.
7. **Monitor your credit reports for signs of identity theft.** **Visit AnnualCreditReport.com to request a free credit report from Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.**
   - Consider placing a **Credit Freeze** on your files at all three bureaus to prevent new accounts from being opened in your name.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a local police report immediately unless your bank or the FTC recovery plan specifically requires it for a dispute.
- You do not need to cancel all of your credit cards if only one specific card or account was mentioned or used.
- You do not need to sign up for paid identity theft monitoring services right now; the free tools provided by the government and credit bureaus are the priority.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of urgency when someone claims a large amount of money is being spent in your name. However, legitimate retailers will not pressure you to stay on a call or provide secret codes to cancel a mistake. Taking a moment to disconnect and verify the situation independently is the most effective way to protect yourself.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for responding to suspicious retail contacts. It focuses on immediate harm prevention and account security. If you determine your identity has been fully stolen, follow the long-term recovery steps provided by the Federal Trade Commission.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Regulations regarding fraud liability may vary by state and by the terms of your specific financial institution.
