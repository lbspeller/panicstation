# What to do if… your bank blocks your card while you are abroad (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank or card issuer using a trusted method, such as the banking app, the number on the back of the card, or the issuer’s official website. Do not use a phone number or link from an unexpected text, email, or call.

## Do not do these things
- Do not call a number or click a link from a surprise fraud message. Find the issuer’s contact details yourself.
- Do not share one-time verification codes, PINs, passwords, or full card details with anyone who contacts you unexpectedly.
- Do not move money to a safe account because someone tells you to do so.
- Do not keep retrying payments or ATM withdrawals at multiple places while you are unsure whether the card is temporarily blocked, closed, or affected by another issue.
- Do not hand your card to an unexpected helper or allow anyone to photograph it to help.
- Do not assume the problem will resolve without contacting the issuer if you need to pay for accommodation, transportation, food, or medication.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for a brief pause if a payment is urgent.** If you are at a hotel, airport counter, restaurant, or checkout, say that your issuer has flagged the card and you are contacting them. Ask whether they can hold the booking, bill, or purchase briefly while you sort it out.
2. **Check your banking app.** Your issuer may show a verification prompt, fraud alert, card-status message, or secure support option. Confirm only activity that you recognize.
3. **Contact the issuer safely.** Use the banking app, the number on the back of the card, a number on your statement, or the issuer’s official website typed into your browser.
4. **Ask what has happened to the card.** Ask whether the card is temporarily blocked pending verification, affected by a merchant hold, restricted for another reason, or closed and being replaced.
5. **Review the flagged activity with the issuer.** Tell them which transactions are yours and which are not. If you see activity you do not recognize, ask how to report it and whether the card should be closed or replaced.
6. **Ask what access is available now.** Ask whether the issuer can restore card use, allow a specific payment, provide a digital replacement card, arrange an emergency replacement card, or offer another secure option. Ask whether any replacement can be delivered to a secure address, such as your hotel, and whether ATM use will be available.
7. **Contact the card network shown on your card if you cannot resolve it with the issuer.** Visa and Mastercard provide cardholder support routes that may help with issuer contact, emergency cash, or replacement-card options. Available help varies by card, issuer approval, location, and circumstances.
8. **Cover immediate essentials with a backup route if needed.** Use another card if you have one. You may also ask an independently chosen trusted travel companion to cover an immediate bill or ask a trusted person at home about a reputable money-transfer method that you can collect with identification. Do not transfer money because an unexpected caller or message tells you to do so.
9. **Contact U.S. consular support if you have no workable access to money and no safe place to stay.** The nearest U.S. embassy or consulate can explain emergency financial-assistance options for U.S. citizens abroad. You can also contact the U.S. Department of State Office of Overseas Citizens Services:
   - In the U.S.: **1-888-407-4747**
   - From abroad: **+1-202-501-4444**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to change banks, replace every card, or redesign your travel setup.
- You do not need to prepare a detailed fraud timeline before speaking to the issuer. A short note of the time, the declined payment, and any activity you do not recognize is enough for the first call.
- You can review passwords, recurring payments, and longer-term account security after you can pay for immediate essentials.

## Important reassurance
A declined or blocked card can have several causes, including suspected fraud, unusual activity, or a merchant hold. Slow down, use a trusted contact route, and focus first on safe access to accommodation, transportation, food, and medication.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the issuer confirms fraud, closes the card, or cannot restore access, later decisions may need help from the issuer, the card network, consular staff, or a qualified adviser.

## Important note
This guide provides general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe, move to a safer place and ask venue or accommodation staff for time while you contact the issuer through a trusted channel.
