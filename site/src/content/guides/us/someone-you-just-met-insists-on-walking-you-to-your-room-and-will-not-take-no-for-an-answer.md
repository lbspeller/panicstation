# What to do if… someone you just met insists on walking you to your room and will not take no for an answer (USA)

## Short answer
**Do not go to your room.** Immediately move to a well-lit, staffed public area such as the lobby, front desk, or security station and request staff assistance.

## Do not do these things
- Don't unlock your door or enter your room if the person is standing with you.
- Don't lead the person to your floor or reveal your room number if they do not already know it.
- Don't enter elevators, stairwells, or empty hallways with them.
- Don't attempt to "be polite" by debating or explaining your refusal; "No" is sufficient.
- Don't accept compromises, such as letting them wait "just outside" the door.
- Don't worry about causing a scene or appearing rude.

## What to do now
1. **Move immediately to a public, staffed location.** Turn away from your room and head to the lobby, a campus security desk, or a busy hotel bar/restaurant where there are cameras and witnesses.
2. **State your boundary once and stop engaging.** Use a clear, firm statement: "I am not going to my room with you." Do not answer follow-up questions or enter a debate.
3. **Notify staff or security that you are being followed.** Tell the front desk, RA, or security officer: "This person is following me and will not leave me alone. I do not feel safe going to my room."
4. **Ask for a specific safety intervention.** Depending on your location:
   - **Hotels:** Request that security ask the person to leave the premises or provide an escort once the person is gone.
   - **Dorms/Campus:** Call campus security or the on-call Resident Assistant (RA) and ask to be met in the common area.
   - **Apartments:** Ask the concierge or doorman to deny the person entry to the elevator or hallway.
5. **Call an emergency contact or the VictimConnect Resource Center.** If you are not in immediate physical danger but need guidance on local resources, call or text 855-4-VICTIM (855-484-2846). 
   - If the person becomes aggressive or threatens you, call 911 immediately.
6. **Narrate the situation if you are on the phone.** If you are calling a friend, say loudly: "I am at the front desk of [Place]. This person I just met is refusing to leave me alone and trying to follow me to my room. Stay on the line with me."
7. **Request a room change or secure escort.** If you are at a hotel and believe the person knows your room number, ask management to move you to a different floor or room for the remainder of your stay.
8. **Stay in the public area until the person has left the building.** Do not return to your room until staff confirm the individual is no longer on the property or in the immediate vicinity.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a formal police report or campus complaint right now.
- You do not need to explain "why" you are uncomfortable to the person following you.
- You do not need to decide if you want to block or delete the person's contact information immediately; focus only on physical separation first.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a social pressure to be polite, but someone who refuses to respect a "no" has already violated social norms. Taking protective steps is a proportional response to someone who is ignoring your boundaries. You are not being dramatic; you are prioritizing your physical safety.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only. These steps are intended to create immediate distance and involve official oversight. Future decisions regarding reporting or long-term safety may require consultation with specialized support services.

## Important note
This guide provides general safety information and is not a substitute for legal advice or professional security services. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. 

If you have experienced harassment or assault, confidential resources such as RAINN (800-656-HOPE) or the VictimConnect Resource Center can provide support and help you understand your options for medical care or reporting.
