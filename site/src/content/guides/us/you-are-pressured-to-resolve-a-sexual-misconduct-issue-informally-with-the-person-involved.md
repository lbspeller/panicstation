# What to do if… you are pressured to resolve a sexual misconduct issue informally with the person involved (USA)

## Short answer
You can pause before agreeing to an informal process or a direct meeting. Say that you are not agreeing to meet the person one-on-one and ask for your options in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree on the spot to meet the person alone or take part in mediation.
- Do not sign an agreement while you feel pressured or overwhelmed.
- Do not keep replying directly to the person if contact feels unsafe or destabilizing.
- Do not let someone rush you into deciding whether to make a formal complaint or a police report.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause if you feel at risk.**  
   Leave the room, end the call, or stop replying. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.

2. **Set a clear boundary in writing if that feels safe.**  
   You can write: “I am not agreeing to meet with them directly or take part in an informal resolution at this time. Please send me my options and the relevant policy in writing.”

3. **If this is at a school or college, contact the Title IX coordinator.**  
   Ask for the current written policy and ask whether Title IX applies. When Title IX applies, the 2020 rule now used for Office for Civil Rights enforcement means the school must not require participation in an informal resolution process and must obtain the parties’ voluntary, written consent before using that process. Ask about available supportive measures even if you are not ready to make a formal complaint.

4. **If this is at work, ask for the written harassment-reporting policy.**  
   Ask whether there is an available reporting route that does not require direct contact with the person involved, such as HR, a designated reporting channel, or another manager if your usual manager is involved.

5. **Ask about immediate arrangements that reduce contact.**  
   At a school, this may include available supportive measures. At work, ask whether temporary changes can reduce direct contact. If a meeting is proposed, ask whether a support person can attend and whether you can receive the purpose of the meeting in writing.

6. **Choose support before making further decisions.**  
   You can contact a trusted person, a union representative if relevant, a campus advocate, an employee assistance program if available, or a sexual assault support service. Ask about privacy limits before sharing details if confidentiality matters to you.

7. **Consider medical support if the issue involved physical contact or you want medical care.**  
   A medical provider or sexual assault support service can explain available local care. Ask what options and privacy limits apply before deciding whether you want to report anything.

8. **Keep your options open.**  
   If you may want to refer back to them later and it feels safe, keep existing messages or emails somewhere you can access them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a formal complaint, use an informal process, or make a police report.
- You do not need to meet the person involved to show that the issue matters.
- You do not need to give a detailed account while you are overwhelmed.
- You do not need to decide the outcome you want right now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling unsure or pressured does not mean you have to agree quickly. It is reasonable to ask for time, written information, support, and less direct contact while you decide what feels manageable.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about institutional processes, workplace options, medical care, or reporting may need support from an appropriate specialist or service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Policies, procedures, privacy limits, and available support can vary by institution, employer, state, age, and circumstances. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
