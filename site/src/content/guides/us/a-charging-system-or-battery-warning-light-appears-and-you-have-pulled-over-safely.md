# What to do if… a charging-system or battery warning light appears and you have pulled over safely (USA)

## Short answer
Keep the vehicle safely stopped, use hazard flashers if they are working, and arrange roadside assistance or a tow. A charging-system or battery warning may indicate a charging or electrical-system problem, so driving farther could leave the vehicle unable to continue or restart.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pull back into fast traffic solely to see whether the vehicle will keep running.
- Do not repeatedly restart the vehicle to test it.
- Do not stand in a travel lane or between the vehicle and moving traffic.
- Do not open the hood or attempt roadside repairs on a highway shoulder.
- Do not touch exposed wiring, leaking fluids, damaged electrical components, or orange high-voltage cables on an electric or hybrid vehicle.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the stopped vehicle.**  
   Secure the vehicle against movement and set the parking brake. Turn on the hazard flashers if they are working. Use essential lights when needed for visibility.

2. **Check for an immediate danger.**  
   If you see sparks, smoke, or flames, smell burning, or are stopped in a travel lane or another immediately dangerous position, call **911**. Move away from the roadway and the vehicle only when you can do so safely without entering or crossing travel lanes. If an electric or hybrid vehicle has been damaged, flooded, or involved in a crash, avoid exposed components and leaking fluids, and tell the dispatcher what type of vehicle is involved.

3. **Use the least exposed waiting place.**  
   There is no single safe option for every roadside location. If remaining inside the vehicle appears safer, keep seat belts fastened. If you can safely reach a protected area away from traffic, such as behind a guardrail, move there without entering or crossing travel lanes. Follow dispatcher instructions.

4. **Arrange roadside assistance or a tow.**  
   If you are safely off the travel lanes and there is no immediate danger, call your roadside assistance provider, insurer, or a towing service. Give the road name or route number, direction of travel, nearest exit or mile marker, nearby signs, vehicle make and model, and the warning message. Ask whether a tow is appropriate; a jump-start alone may not resolve a charging-system problem.

5. **Preserve remaining electrical power.**  
   Turn off non-essential electrical loads, such as heated seats, the rear defroster, and charging accessories. Keep hazard flashers and any essential visibility lights on when needed for safety.

6. **Record the warning without troubleshooting.**  
   When it is safe, take a photo of the dashboard warning or write down the exact message. Check the owner’s manual for vehicle-specific instructions, but leave roadside inspection and repairs to a professional.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide whether the fault is the battery, alternator, belt, wiring, or another component while you are roadside.
- You do **not** need to test the vehicle with repeated restarts.
- You do **not** need to choose parts, compare repair costs, or settle on a repair shop before you are safely away from traffic.
- Checking for recalls or reporting a possible safety problem can wait until after the immediate roadside situation is stable.

## Important reassurance
You have already taken the most important first step by pulling over safely. Asking for a tow instead of trying to continue is a normal and sensible response to an uncertain charging-system warning.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the roadside moment only. Diagnosis, repairs, recall checks, and later decisions may need help from a qualified roadside professional, repair shop, or vehicle manufacturer.

## Important note
This is general information, not mechanical, legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911 and follow dispatcher instructions.
