# What to do if… you are suspended from work pending an investigation (USA)

## Short answer
Do not agree to the change immediately. Request the proposal in writing (including the new rate, schedule, and effective date) and check your eligibility for partial unemployment or COBRA benefits before responding.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any documents or agree to new terms during the initial meeting if you feel pressured or have not read them.
- Do not assume that an "at-will" status allows an employer to ignore federal or state wage-and-hour laws.
- Do not perform any "off the clock" work to compensate for reduced hours; all work must be recorded and paid.
- Do not quit or resign in the heat of the moment, as this may forfeit your right to unemployment benefits or severance.
- Do not ignore the $684 per week federal threshold for exempt employees; if a pay cut drops you below this (or a higher state threshold), your "exempt" status may be legally lost.

## What to do now
1. **Request the full proposal in writing before providing any verbal or written agreement.** 
   - Ask for the specific new hourly rate or weekly salary.
   - Confirm the new weekly schedule and expected start date.
   - Ask if the change is temporary or permanent.
   - Request a summary of how this affects health insurance, PTO accrual, and retirement contributions.
2. **Determine your current pay classification as either hourly (non-exempt) or salaried exempt.** 
   - Non-exempt workers must be paid at least the minimum wage and 1.5x overtime for hours over 40.
   - Salaried exempt workers generally must receive their full weekly salary for any week in which they perform work, though employers can reduce salary prospectively for future weeks.
3. **Confirm that the pay reduction is not being applied retroactively to hours you have already worked.** 
   - Employers generally cannot legally reduce pay for work already completed.
   - Check your most recent pay stub against your logged hours to ensure no unauthorized deductions were made for past work.
4. **Send a brief, neutral written message to acknowledge receipt without accepting the terms.** 
   - Example: "I have received the proposal regarding my pay and hours. I am currently reviewing the details and will respond once I have clarified how this affects my benefits and classification. Please confirm the exact date this change is intended to take effect."
5. **Begin a private log to document every interaction regarding this change.** 
   - Record the date, time, and names of people involved in pay-cut discussions.
   - Save copies of emails, schedule changes, and memos to a personal device or printed file.
   - Keep a precise daily record of your actual start and end times.
6. **Check your state’s eligibility requirements for partial unemployment benefits immediately.** 
   - Many states allow workers to collect a portion of unemployment insurance if their hours are reduced through no fault of their own.
   - Visit your state's Department of Labor or Unemployment Agency website to view the specific "earnings disregard" rules for 2026.
7. **Ask HR for a formal statement on health insurance continuity if your hours drop below the full-time threshold.** 
   - If your employer has 20 or more employees, a reduction in hours that causes a loss of coverage is a "qualifying event" for federal COBRA.
   - Check if your state offers "mini-COBRA" if your company has fewer than 20 employees.
8. **Contact your union representative immediately if you are a member of a collective bargaining unit.** 
   - Wages and hours are mandatory subjects of bargaining, and your employer may be prohibited from changing them without negotiating with the union first.
9. **Identify the correct agency for reporting specific violations if the cut seems illegal.** 
   - Report minimum wage or overtime issues to the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division.
   - Report potential union-rights violations to the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB).

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to look for a new job or stay long-term today.
- You do not need to threaten legal action or file a formal complaint in the first 24 hours.
- You do not need to resolve complex financial budgeting for the next six months immediately; focus only on the next pay cycle.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of panic or frustration when your income is suddenly threatened. Taking a few hours to gather facts and verify your rights is a standard professional response and does not mean you are being difficult or uncooperative.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hours following a pay or hours reduction notice. It does not cover long-term employment litigation, contract negotiation, or specific state-level tax implications.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Wage-and-hour laws, including salary thresholds and unemployment eligibility, vary significantly by state and are subject to change. Always consult current 2026 filings from the U.S. Department of Labor or your state’s labor agency for the most accurate local rules.
