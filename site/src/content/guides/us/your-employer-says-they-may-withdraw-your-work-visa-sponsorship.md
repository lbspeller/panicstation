# What to do if… your employer says they may withdraw your work visa sponsorship (USA)

## Short answer
Get the facts in writing: what may be withdrawn, whether your employment is ending, and the exact dates involved. Save your I-94 and approval notices, and contact a qualified US immigration attorney promptly before you guess about staying, working, travelling, or filing anything.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume your visa stamp expiration date is the same as how long you may stay in the United States; your I-94, approval notices, status category, and case facts matter.
- Do not keep working if your employment has ended or you are not sure you are still authorized to work for that employer.
- Do not quit impulsively, sign separation papers immediately, or accept a retroactive termination date without understanding possible immigration consequences.
- Do not leave the United States or make travel plans until you have checked how withdrawal or termination may affect re-entry and status.
- Do not rely on friends’ timelines or online anecdotes. Some categories may have an up-to-60-day discretionary grace period after employment ends, but it may be shorter, category-specific, and dependent on exact dates.
- Do not ignore any notice from USCIS, CBP, the Department of State, an immigration court, or another government agency.

## What to do now
1. **Find out exactly what is being withdrawn, and when.** Ask HR, and the employer’s immigration counsel if they are involved, for written answers to:
   - Is your employment ending, and on what exact date?
   - Are they withdrawing an I-129 petition or another filing?
   - When do they expect to send the withdrawal to USCIS?
   - Can they confirm in writing once the withdrawal is sent?
   - Will they provide a written letter confirming your last day of employment and last paid day?

2. **Save the documents that show your current dates.**
   - Retrieve and save your most recent I-94.
   - Save your most recent I-797 approval notice if you have one.
   - Save your visa stamp page, passport identity page, offer letter, employment agreement, recent pay stubs, and any emails about withdrawal or termination.
   - Put the key dates in one short note: date you were told, last day worked, last paid day, I-94 admit-until date, approval notice end date, and any stated withdrawal date.

3. **Pause anything that could become unauthorized work.**
   - If your employment has ended, do not keep working informally, answering work messages, or doing transition tasks until counsel confirms what is allowed.
   - If employment is still ongoing but unclear, ask for written confirmation that you remain employed and authorized to perform duties.
   - If the employer cannot confirm that, ask whether duties can be paused in writing while you get legal advice.

4. **Contact a qualified US immigration attorney as promptly as you can.** Share:
   - Your status category, such as H-1B, H-1B1, L-1, O-1, TN, E-1, E-2, or E-3.
   - Your I-94 admit-until date and any approval notice dates.
   - The employer’s written statement about withdrawal, termination, or last day.
   - Whether you have dependents in related status.
   - Whether you have any separate employment authorization document, and what it is based on.

5. **Ask counsel the time-sensitive questions directly.**
   - Whether your category may qualify for an up-to-60-day discretionary grace period, and what date it may start from.
   - Whether the grace period could be shorter because your authorized validity period ends sooner.
   - Whether you can work during any gap. In many work-visa situations, you cannot work during the grace period unless you have separate authorization.
   - Whether a new employer petition, change of status, extension, departure, or another option must be filed before a specific date.
   - Whether portability is available in your category, and what must be filed or approved before you can start work for a new employer.

6. **Keep communication clean and written.**
   - Use calm, factual emails.
   - Do not argue about immigration law with HR.
   - Ask for dates, copies, and confirmation of what has or has not been filed.
   - Save screenshots or PDFs of important messages in a place you can access outside work systems.

7. **Keep your address reliable.**
   - If you move while this is happening, check the USCIS address-change rules for your status.
   - If the address-change requirement applies to you, file the USCIS change of address within 10 days and keep proof of submission.
   - Make sure you can receive mail safely and quickly while any immigration issue is open.

8. **If you receive any government notice, treat it as urgent but not as something to handle alone.**
   - Scan or photograph every page.
   - Save the envelope if it came by mail.
   - Send it to your attorney before replying, travelling, or making a filing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to leave the United States, change status, change employers, or pursue a longer-term immigration strategy.
- You do not need to post online, explain widely at work, or tell coworkers more than necessary.
- You do not need to negotiate every severance detail immediately.
- You do not need to rush-file something without legal review; the wrong filing or wrong date can create new problems.

## Important reassurance
This can feel frightening because dates matter, but the first steps are manageable. Get the facts in writing, save the documents that show your dates, pause risky work activity, and get immigration advice before making irreversible moves.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate first steps only. Later decisions may need a qualified US immigration attorney, and possibly employment-law or tax advice, depending on your situation.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. US immigration status can depend on exact dates, status category, filings, travel history, and individual facts.
