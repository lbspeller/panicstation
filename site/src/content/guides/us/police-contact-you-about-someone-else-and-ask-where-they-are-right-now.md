# What to do if… police contact you about someone else and ask where they are right now (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the identity of the person contacting you, state clearly that you are not answering questions without a lawyer, and do not provide any speculative or "estimated" information.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t lie or give a "best guess" about where the person might be; false statements to law enforcement can lead to felony charges.
- Don’t confirm or deny any details about the person's habits, plans, or relationships before speaking with legal counsel.
- Don’t invite officers into your home or hand over your phone to show them messages or location history without a warrant.
- Don’t provide personal financial information, Social Security numbers, or passwords to "verify" your identity over the phone.
- Don’t argue about the legality of the request or the person's innocence; maintain silence instead of debating.

## What to do now
1. **Verify that the contact is legitimate law enforcement.**  
   Scams involving police impersonation are highly prevalent.
   - **Ask for the officer's name, badge number, agency, and case number.**
   - **Hang up and call the agency's official, publicly listed non-emergency number.**
   - **Request to speak to the specific officer or their supervisor to confirm the inquiry is real.**

2. **Identify high-pressure tactics or sensory cues of an investigation.**  
   Recognize if the officer is using specific techniques to elicit a fast response.
   - **Rapid-fire questioning or "interrupting" your answers.**
   - **Promises that "helping now" will make things easier for you or the other person.**
   - **Threats of "obstruction of justice" or "harboring" charges if you do not cooperate immediately.**
   - **Friendly, informal "off the record" conversation styles meant to lower your guard.**

3. **State your intent to remain silent clearly and out loud.**  
   Under U.S. law, you must generally invoke your right to silence verbally rather than simply staying quiet.
   - **Say: “I am exercising my right to remain silent. I will not answer questions without a lawyer present.”**
   - **Stop talking immediately after making this statement, regardless of the officer's follow-up remarks.**

4. **Provide only legally required identifying information if applicable.**  
   In many "stop and identify" states, you may be required to provide your name or ID if an officer has reasonable suspicion of a crime.
   - **State your full legal name if the encounter is in-person and you are being detained.**
   - **Be aware that in some jurisdictions (e.g., Alabama 2026 rules), refusal to provide physical ID or a "satisfactory" verbal identification can lead to arrest for interfering with government functions.**

5. **Protect yourself from "Federal False Statement" charges (18 U.S.C. § 1001).**  
   If the contact is from a federal agent (FBI, DEA, HSI), any "materially false" statement—even if not under oath—is a felony.
   - **Do not say "I haven't seen them" if you saw them five minutes ago.**
   - **Do not say "I don't know" if you actually do know; instead, use the "right to remain silent" script.**

6. **Manage the interaction if it occurs at your door.**  
   - **Keep the door closed or speak through a window/intercom.**
   - **Ask: “Do you have a warrant?”**
   - **If they have a warrant, ask them to hold it up to the glass or slide it under the door so you can check the address, date, and scope.**
   - **If they do not have a warrant, say: “I do not consent to a search or entry.”**

7. **Record the details of the contact immediately afterward.**  
   - **Write down the time, date, and method of contact (phone, in-person, email).**
   - **Note exactly what you said and what the officer claimed (e.g., "They said it was a welfare check").**
   - **Save any caller ID logs or business cards provided.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to cooperate with the investigation today.
- You do not need to contact the person they are looking for until you have assessed your own legal risk.
- You do not need to explain your relationship with the person or why you may or may not know their location.
- You do not need to respond to subpoenas or legal requests for records until you have reviewed them with an attorney.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel panicked or intimidated when law enforcement asks for the location of another person. Choosing to remain silent is a constitutionally protected act, not an admission of guilt or a sign of obstruction. Taking a moment to verify the caller and request a lawyer is the standard way to protect your rights in a high-stakes encounter.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization during the initial contact only. It does not provide legal defense strategies for obstruction charges or advice on handling ongoing surveillance.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding "stop and identify" and "obstruction" vary significantly by state and have been subject to court changes as recently as 2026. For specific legal needs, consult a qualified criminal defense attorney in your jurisdiction.
