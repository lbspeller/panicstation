# What to do if… you receive a letter saying you must attend court as a juror and you cannot make the date (USA)

## Short answer
Request a postponement immediately via the court's online portal or automated phone system, as most jurisdictions allow one "no-questions-asked" deferral if done before the deadline. Do not ignore the summons, as failure to appear can result in a "show cause" order or fines for contempt of court.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume missing the date is acceptable as long as you have a "good excuse" — most courts require approval in advance.
- Do not show up at the courthouse in person just to request a postponement or excuse.
- Do not pay any "jury excuse service" or provide your Social Security number/credit card info to anyone calling you about jury duty.
- Do not ignore the summons if you have moved; you must still notify the court that you are no longer a resident of that jurisdiction.
- Do not book non-refundable travel or confirm work changes until you receive a formal notice (online or by mail) that your date has been moved.

## What to do now
1. **Locate your "Juror Participant Number" or "Badge Number" on the summons.** This is the unique identifier you will need for any online or phone system.
2. **Access the official court portal URL or automated phone line printed on the letter.** Online portals are the fastest way to request a change and often provide an immediate automated response.
3. **Select the "Request Postponement" or "Deferral" option if you can serve at a later time.** 
   - Most courts allow you to choose a new date within a 3-to-6-month window.
   - Postponements are granted more easily than full excusals.
4. **Identify if you qualify for a "Permanent Excusal" or "Disqualification" based on local rules.**
   - Common categories include being over a certain age (often 70 or 75), active duty military, non-citizenship, or no longer residing in the county.
   - Medical excuses almost always require a signed statement from a healthcare provider.
5. **Submit a request for an "Undue Hardship" excuse only if a later date will not solve the problem.** 
   - Use specific language required by the court, such as "extreme financial hardship" or "sole caregiver for a dependent."
   - Be prepared to provide documentation, such as a letter from an employer or a birth certificate for childcare issues.
6. **Check your reporting status the evening before your original date.** Many summonses are for "standby" service, meaning you may not actually be required to report even if your postponement wasn't processed yet.
7. **Take a screenshot or save the confirmation number once your request is submitted.** Do not assume the request is granted until the system shows "Approved" or "Postponed."
8. **Contact the jury office immediately if you have already missed your reporting date.**
   - Call the number on the summons and explain that you missed the date.
   - Ask for instructions to "clear" the missed appearance to avoid a contempt citation.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to hire a lawyer to handle a jury summons scheduling conflict.
- You do **not** need to notify your employer of the specific case details; you only need to provide proof of the summons or the eventually confirmed dates.
- You do **not** need to provide detailed financial records unless the court specifically requests them after a hardship claim.

## Important reassurance
Courts manage thousands of jurors and expect scheduling conflicts. Most systems are designed to accommodate a single postponement request without requiring a judge's intervention. As long as you interact with the official system before your reporting date, you are generally not in any legal jeopardy.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only. Every court (Federal, State, and Municipal) has its own specific deadlines and portal requirements. The instructions on your physical summons are the final authority for your specific case.

## Important note
This guide provides general procedural information and does not constitute legal advice. Requirements for excusals and the penalties for non-attendance vary by jurisdiction. Always use the contact information provided on your official court documents to ensure you are speaking with authorized court personnel.
