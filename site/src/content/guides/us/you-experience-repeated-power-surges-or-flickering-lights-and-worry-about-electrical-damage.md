# What to do if… you experience repeated power surges or flickering lights and worry about electrical damage (USA)

## Short answer
Treat repeated brief surges as a possible equipment-damage and electrical-safety issue. Unplug sensitive electronics, reduce electrical load, and shut off power at the main breaker only if you can do it safely and there are signs of heat, smoke, sparking, or a burning smell.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep repeatedly resetting breakers or trying to force a circuit to stay on.
- Don’t remove the cover of the electrical panel or touch service conductors.
- Don’t ignore burning or plastic smells, buzzing, crackling, warm outlets, discoloured switches, scorch marks, sparks, or smoke.
- Don’t keep using a surge protector or power strip if its protection light is off, it looks damaged, it feels hot, or it smells hot.
- Don’t run multiple high-draw appliances, such as a space heater, dryer, oven, air conditioner, or microwave, while power is unstable.
- Don’t assume the situation is harmless just because the lights come back quickly.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger signs.** If you see smoke, sparks, a burning smell, crackling or buzzing at an outlet, warmth, scorch marks, or a discoloured switch, shut off power at the main breaker only if you can reach it safely. Do not go through smoke, water, fire, sparking, or visible electrical damage to reach it. If there is fire, smoke, or you cannot safely cut power, leave and call 911.
2. **Unplug sensitive and high-value electronics.** Unplug computers, TVs, game consoles, modems, routers, chargers, and anything that keeps rebooting. Do not pull plugs from an outlet that is hot, sparking, wet, smoking, or visibly damaged.
3. **Reduce demand while the power is unstable.** Turn off non-essential lights and avoid high-power loads, including air conditioners, space heaters, washers, dryers, ovens, and microwaves, until the issue has been checked.
4. **Check whether it is only your home.** From a safe place, see whether neighbours’ lights, nearby homes, or streetlights are flickering too. If multiple homes are affected, it is more likely to be a utility-side problem. If it is only your home, it may be an internal wiring, panel, meter, or connection problem.
5. **Contact the right help.**
   - If multiple homes are affected, call your electric utility and report repeated voltage fluctuations or flickering lights.
   - If you see arcing at the meter, a damaged service drop, a downed line, or burning outside, stay well away, use the utility’s emergency or hazard line, and call 911 if there is fire, smoke, or immediate danger.
   - If it seems limited to your home, or you noticed warning signs indoors, call a licensed electrician for a safety check.
6. **If a breaker tripped, leave it off if the problem returns.** If you reset a breaker once and it trips again, or anything smells hot or burnt, leave it off, unplug what you can safely unplug on that circuit, and wait for an electrician.
7. **Verify anyone you hire.** Check your state or local licensing authority’s verification tool where available before authorising electrical work.
8. **Make a simple log for the utility or electrician.** Write down times, what happened, whether lights dimmed or brightened, which devices rebooted, what large appliances were running, and whether neighbours were affected. Take photos of scorch marks, damaged outlets, or tripped breakers only from a safe distance.
9. **If you rent, report it in writing right away.** Tell the landlord or property manager what happened, mention any burning smell, heat, flickering, tripping breaker, or damaged outlet, and ask for a licensed electrician. Avoid temporary DIY fixes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to replace the breaker panel, rewire, or buy whole-home surge protection.
- You do not need to choose replacement electronics until the power is stable and the cause has been checked.
- You can wait to make a detailed insurance decision; for now, keep notes and safe-distance photos if anything seems damaged.
- You do not need to diagnose whether the cause is utility equipment, a loose connection, a breaker, an outlet, or an appliance yourself.

## Important reassurance
Repeated flickering or surges can be frightening, but the first steps are straightforward: protect people first, reduce load, unplug vulnerable electronics when safe, and bring in the utility or a licensed electrician depending on what is affected. You do not have to solve the electrical cause in the moment.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety and preventing avoidable damage. Diagnosis, repair, code compliance, insurance decisions, and any permanent surge protection choices may need help from a licensed electrician, your electric utility, your landlord, or your insurer.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional electrical, fire-safety, insurance, legal, or other professional advice. If there are signs of fire, smoke, sparking, heat, a downed line, or immediate danger, prioritize getting away from the hazard and contacting emergency services.
