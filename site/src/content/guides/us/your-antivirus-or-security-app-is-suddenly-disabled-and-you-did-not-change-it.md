# What to do if… your antivirus or security app is suddenly disabled and you did not change it (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the browser as compromised by unauthorized software: stop using it for sensitive transactions, remove unknown extensions and programs, and use the browser’s built-in reset tools to restore control.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t enter passwords, credit card numbers, or MFA codes into the affected browser until it is cleaned.
- Don’t download "fix-it" tools from pop-ups or unfamiliar websites.
- Don’t click "Allow" on any browser notifications or permission requests that appear during the redirect.
- Don’t ignore a "Managed by your organization" message on a personal, non-work device.
- Don’t assume the issue is fixed just because you manually changed the homepage back once; find the underlying cause.

## What to do now
1. **Stop using the affected browser for any sensitive activity immediately.** Close all tabs and windows related to banking, email, or personal accounts to prevent session or credential theft.
2. **Identify the source of the control by checking for management policies.** 
   - In Chrome, type `chrome://policy` into the address bar to see which extensions or external programs are enforcing settings.
   - If the browser says "Managed by your organization" on a personal device, this confirms an unauthorized policy is active.
3. **Remove suspicious extensions and add-ons from your browser settings.** 
   - Open the Extensions menu (Chrome/Edge: three dots > Extensions; Firefox: three lines > Add-ons and Themes; Safari: Safari Menu > Settings > Extensions). 
   - Toggle off and then click **Remove** on any items you do not recognize or did not intentionally install.
4. **Use the browser's internal reset or refresh feature to wipe unauthorized configurations.** 
   - In Chrome, go to Settings > Reset settings > **Restore settings to their original defaults**. 
   - In Firefox, type `about:support` and click **Refresh Firefox**. 
   - In Safari, go to Settings > General to reset the **Homepage**, and Settings > Search to pick your **Search engine**.
5. **Uninstall recently added programs from your computer's operating system.** 
   - On Windows, go to Settings > Apps > Installed Apps and sort by date to **Uninstall** unfamiliar software. 
   - On Mac, open Applications and move unknown programs to the Trash; also check System Settings > General > **Login Items** for suspicious background tasks.
6. **Execute a full system scan using your device's integrated security software.** 
   - Open **Windows Security** (Windows) or use a reputable, pre-installed security suite to scan for and quarantine potentially unwanted programs (PUPs).
7. **Update your operating system and all browsers to the latest versions.** Ensure all security patches are applied to close vulnerabilities that may have allowed the hijacker to gain persistence.
8. **Secure your high-value accounts from a clean device if possible.** Change passwords for your primary email and financial accounts, and verify that your **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** settings have not been altered.
9. **File a report with official consumer protection and cybercrime agencies.** 
   - Submit a report to the **Federal Trade Commission (FTC)** at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.
   - If you suspect a serious data breach or financial loss, file a complaint with the **FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3)**.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to identify the exact name or "family" of the malware before you start cleaning.
- You don’t need to format your hard drive or perform a factory reset if the browser reset and extension removal successfully stop the redirects.
- You don’t need to call "tech support" numbers that appear in browser pop-ups; these are typically part of the scam.

## Important reassurance
Browser hijacking is a common issue often caused by bundled software or malicious extensions rather than a direct hack of your hardware. Most of these incidents can be resolved by systematically removing the unauthorized software and resetting browser defaults. Taking these steps quickly prevents the situation from escalating into identity theft.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for browser-level hijacking. If redirects persist after a full reset and scan, or if your device shows signs of deeper infection (such as disabled security software), professional technical support is required.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute professional cybersecurity or legal advice. If you are a victim of identity theft or significant financial fraud, contact your local law enforcement and your financial institutions immediately.
