# What to do if… you receive a last-minute notice to provide evidence or documents before a hearing (USA)

## Short answer
Read the notice from top to bottom, write down the deadline and hearing details, then contact the court clerk right away to confirm the filing method and how to ask for more time if you need it. Keep working on filing what you can; a phone call or request for more time usually does not change the deadline unless the court orders it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice because it arrived late.
- Do not skip the hearing unless the court clearly tells you the hearing has been moved, cancelled, or changed.
- Do not assume a call to the clerk extends the deadline; a judge or court order usually must approve an extension or continuance.
- Do not falsify, alter, recreate, or “clean up” documents to make them look like originals.
- Do not file private identifiers publicly without checking redaction rules. In federal civil cases, FRCP 5.2 requires redaction of certain personal information; state and local courts may have their own rules.
- Do not send documents only to the court if the rules also require service on the other side.
- Do not send documents only to the other side if the notice requires filing with the court.

## What to do now
1. **Copy the critical details into one note.**  
   Write down the case number, court name, judge if listed, hearing date and time, due date and time, exactly what evidence or documents are requested, and how the notice says to file or deliver them.

2. **Check whether the notice is an order, a scheduling notice, or a request from the other side.**  
   Use the exact words on the notice. A judge’s order may need faster action than a letter or email from another party, but all of them should be handled promptly.

3. **Call the clerk’s office using the number on the court website or notice.**  
   Ask procedural questions only, because clerks generally cannot give legal advice:
   - “What filing method does this court accept for this deadline?”
   - “Is there a local form or local rule for asking for an extension or continuance?”
   - “Do I need to serve the other side, and what proof or certificate of service does this court require?”
   - “If I cannot get all documents in time, how should I file a request explaining that?”

4. **Check the court’s local rules and forms page.**  
   If this is federal court, the federal timing rules may be relevant, including FRCP 6 for civil cases and Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 45 for criminal cases. The court’s order, local rules, and judge’s instructions may still control, so use the court’s own website or ask the clerk where the local rule or form is posted.

5. **If you need more time, file a short written request as soon as possible.**  
   Use the court’s form if one exists. If there is no form, make the request brief and include:
   - the current deadline and hearing date;
   - what documents or evidence were requested;
   - the specific reason you cannot provide everything in time, such as late notice or a records holder not responding yet;
   - what you can provide now;
   - the new deadline or hearing date you are asking for;
   - your signature and contact information;
   - proof or certificate of service if required.

6. **File what you can now, if the court allows or requires it.**  
   Label each document clearly. If the packet is incomplete, say plainly what is missing and what steps you have taken to get it. Do not hide gaps or guess at missing information.

7. **Keep proof of every filing and delivery.**  
   Save e-filing confirmations, email receipts, fax confirmations, stamped copies, screenshots, tracking numbers, and a copy of exactly what you sent. Keep a separate note of the date, time, method, and recipient.

8. **Request missing records immediately and document the request.**  
   Contact the records holder, such as an employer, school, hospital, jail, police records unit, landlord, bank, or agency. Make the request in writing where possible and save proof that you asked.

9. **If this is a criminal case or you could face jail, detention, or a warrant, contact defense counsel urgently.**  
   Send the notice to your lawyer or public defender immediately. If you do not have counsel and think you may qualify for appointed counsel, ask the clerk or the court’s self-help office how to contact the public defender or assigned counsel office for that court.

10. **Assume the hearing still happens unless the court confirms otherwise.**  
   Keep preparing to appear. Bring or have ready the notice, your filed request for more time, proof of filing, proof of service, and copies of any documents you were able to provide.

## What can wait
- You do not need to make a perfect trial packet right now.
- You do not need to decide settlement, plea, appeal, or full legal strategy today.
- You do not need to organize every possible exhibit before handling the immediate deadline.
- You can sort deeper evidence review, witness planning, and long-term case strategy after you have filed what you can or asked the court for more time.

## Important reassurance
A last-minute evidence or document notice is stressful, but the safest first move is simple: act quickly, use the court’s required filing method, ask for more time in writing if needed, and keep proof of every step. Courts often look for clear, honest, documented efforts rather than panic or silence.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for a sudden evidence or document deadline before a hearing. Court rules vary by federal, state, county, court type, case type, local rule, and judge, so later decisions may need help from a lawyer, legal aid office, public defender, court self-help center, or other qualified local specialist.
