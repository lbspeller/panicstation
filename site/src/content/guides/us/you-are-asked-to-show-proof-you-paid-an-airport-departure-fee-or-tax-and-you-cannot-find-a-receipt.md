# What to do if… you are asked to show proof you paid an airport departure fee or tax and you cannot find a receipt (USA)

## Short answer
Show your itemized e-ticket or airline booking confirmation immediately, as federal law requires most U.S. taxes to be included in the total ticket price.

## Do not do these things
- Don't pay any individual who approaches you away from a marked airline or government counter.
- Don't hand over your phone or passport to anyone claiming they need to "take it to a back office" to check the record.
- Don't leave the immediate area or security line without first requesting a supervisor or "Red Coat" / lead agent.
- Don't pay a second time unless you are at an official cashier desk and have exhausted digital search options.
- Don't delete any emails or browser history while trying to find the document.

## What to do now
1. **Show your itemized e-ticket or "Receipt for Purchase" in your email or airline app.** 
   - Department of Transportation (DOT) rules require the advertised price to include all government-imposed taxes. 
   - Point to the line items for "US International Departure Tax," "September 11 Security Fee," or "Passenger Facility Charge."
2. **Search your email for specific keywords to retrieve the digital backup.**
   - Use search terms: **receipt**, **itinerary**, **tax**, **breakdown**, **confirmation**, or **invoice**.
   - Look for the 13-digit ticket number (starting with the airline's 3-digit code).
3. **Verify if the request is actually for the 2026 TSA ConfirmID fee.** 
   - If you are traveling without a REAL ID-compliant document, security may ask for proof of the $45 identity verification payment.
   - Access the TSA ConfirmID portal on your mobile device to show the transaction history or QR code.
4. **Request an official system check at the check-in counter.**
   - Ask the agent: **"Please check the 'Tax' status in the PNR or GDS record for this ticket number."**
   - Most modern systems flag "TE" (Tax Exempt) or "TP" (Tax Paid) directly in the passenger manifest.
5. **Show the bank or credit card transaction on your mobile banking app.** 
   - Match the date of the ticket purchase with the amount paid to the airline or travel agency.
   - While not a formal receipt, it serves as evidence of a completed transaction to a legitimate merchant.
6. **Escalate to a supervisor if the frontline agent insists on a paper receipt.**
   - State clearly: **"I am requesting a supervisor to verify my digital proof of payment as per DOT total-price requirements."**
   - If at security, ask for the TSA Lead Transportation Security Officer (LTSO).

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a formal DOT complaint while at the airport.
- You do not need to contact your bank to initiate a chargeback for the original ticket.
- You do not need to locate physical paper receipts if you have a digital e-ticket or mobile boarding pass with a "confirmed" status.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for travelers to lose paper receipts, and airport staff are trained to look up records digitally. In the U.S., taxes are almost never paid as a separate physical transaction at the gate, so a confirmed ticket is usually all the proof required.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for document loss during airport transit. It does not cover legal disputes regarding tax law or long-term refund processes for incorrectly collected fees.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Requirements for international travel from the U.S. may involve foreign taxes not covered by U.S. DOT rules. If you suspect a scam or feel physically threatened, contact airport police immediately.
