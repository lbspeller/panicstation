# What to do if… someone claims to be a partner after a death and demands access to information or plans (USA)
## Short answer
Do not share access, documents, or private details directly in response to the request. Ask for the request in writing and route it through the person handling the estate, an estate attorney, or the relevant provider’s formal process.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send copies or photos of the will, funeral plan, financial plan, insurance papers, passwords, account numbers, or identity documents directly in response to the request.
- Do not confirm extra personal information about the person who died, such as their Social Security number, date of birth, prior addresses, or account providers.
- Do not let urgency, repeated messages, or requests for secrecy push you into sharing information.
- Do not meet the person alone or let them into the home if you feel unsafe or unsure who they are.
- Do not access, change, or close accounts that belonged to the person who died unless you have confirmed authority to do so.

## What to do now
1. **Send one brief boundary message.**  
   Say: “I am not sharing plans or documents directly. Please send your request in writing. It will be passed to the appropriate person or provider.”

2. **Route the request without deciding whether the claim is genuine.**  
   If a personal representative or estate attorney is already handling matters, send the written request to them. For a funeral plan, insurance policy, medical record, or other provider-held document, use the provider’s formal process rather than disclosing information yourself.

3. **Ask for written details, not informal proof sent to your phone.**  
   Ask the person to state what they are requesting and why. Do not judge relationship claims, inheritance claims, or authority documents yourself while you are under pressure.

4. **Preserve the plans and records you already control.**  
   Put physical documents somewhere secure. Save digital files without forwarding them. Change credentials only for accounts you personally own, or for accounts you are formally authorized to administer.

5. **Keep a simple record of the contact.**  
   Save texts, emails, and voicemails. Note the date, what was requested, and the reply you sent.

6. **Use the appropriate official reporting route if the contact appears suspicious.**  
   Report suspected scams or fraud through ReportFraud.ftc.gov. Use IdentityTheft.gov if personal information has been stolen or used. Call 911 if there is an immediate threat or someone is trying to enter the home.

7. **Use the provider’s formal process for medical or care details.**  
   Medical information may remain protected after death. Do not disclose medical records or care information informally.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether the person was genuinely a partner.
- You do not need to decide inheritance rights, access rights, or who should receive documents.
- You do not need to argue, investigate the person, or prove that you are cooperating.
- You do not need to answer repeated messages after you have sent one boundary response.
- You do not need to make changes to accounts that belonged to the person who died.

## Important reassurance
Using a formal route is a neutral response. It protects the person who died, the estate, and anyone with a genuine claim without requiring you to make a rushed decision while grieving.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the initial contact. Probate rules, relationship claims, and provider processes vary by state and situation. Later decisions may need help from an estate attorney, the relevant provider, or the appropriate court.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Prioritize immediate safety if you feel threatened.
