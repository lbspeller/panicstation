# What to do if… water starts dripping through your ceiling and you cannot see the source (USA)

## Short answer
Assume an **electrical hazard** and hidden water spread. **Move away from the drip, shut off electricity to the affected area at the breaker panel if safe, and turn off the main water supply valve immediately.**

## Do not do these things
- Don't touch light fixtures, switches, or outlets near the moisture.
- Don't stand in pooled water to reach an electrical panel or water valve.
- Don't use a ladder to inspect the ceiling if the floor is wet.
- Don't assume the leak is minor because the drip is slow; water may be pooling above the drywall.
- Don't use "wait and see" as a strategy; hidden water can cause structural collapse or mold within 24 to 48 hours.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance and isolate the zone.**  
   **Keep people and pets away from the area where water is visible.** Moisture inside a ceiling often follows electrical conduits, creating a risk of energized water or surfaces.
   - Close the door to the room if the leak is localized.
   - Move furniture or valuables only if you can do so without entering the wet area.

2. **Shut off electrical power if safe.**  
   **Locate your circuit breaker panel and switch off the breakers for the affected room.** If you are unsure which breaker controls the area, or if water is near the panel itself, turn off the **Main Breaker** (usually a large switch at the top or bottom of the panel).
   - **Do not touch the panel if your hands are wet or you are standing on a damp floor.**
   - If you cannot reach the panel safely, leave the building and call your electric utility provider to report a water-related electrical hazard.

3. **Contain the water flow.**  
   **Place a bucket or plastic bin under the drip once the power is off.**
   - Place a towel at the bottom of the bucket to prevent splashing and reduce noise.
   - If the ceiling is bulging or sagging but not yet breaking, you may use a screwdriver to poke a small "relief hole" in the center of the bulge to drain the water into a bucket; this prevents the weight of the water from causing a large-scale ceiling collapse.

4. **Kill the water supply.**  
   **Close the main water shut-off valve for your home.** 
   - In houses, this is typically located in a basement, crawlspace, or near the water heater.
   - In apartments or condos, it may be under a sink or in a utility closet; if you cannot find it, you must move to the next step immediately.

5. **Contact property management or an emergency plumber.**  
   **Call for professional help to identify the source.**
   - **Apartments:** Call the emergency maintenance line immediately.
   - **Condos/HOAs:** Notify the building manager, as the leak may originate from a common pipe or an upstairs neighbor.
   - **Houses:** Call a licensed emergency plumber.

6. **Coordinate with neighbors above (if applicable).**  
   **Alert the occupant of the unit directly above the leak.** Ask them to stop using all water (sinks, showers, toilets, dishwashers, and washing machines) and check for visible pooling on their floors.

7. **Call 911 for life-safety threats.**  
   **Dial 911 only if you see sparks, smell burning (like ozone or plastic), or see smoke.** You should also call if the ceiling collapses and creates an immediate physical danger or entrapment.

## What can wait
- Determining financial liability or insurance deductibles.
- Professional water restoration and drying (this happens after the source is stopped).
- Permanent drywall or paint repairs.
- Filing a formal written claim (verbal notification to insurance is sufficient for now).

## Important reassurance
Not seeing the source is normal. Water frequently travels horizontally along joists, pipes, or electrical wires before gravity pulls it through a seam in the drywall. Taking the immediate steps of cutting power and water is the standard and most effective way to stabilize the situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active, unknown ceiling leak. It does not cover diagnostic leak detection, long-term mold remediation, or structural engineering assessments which may be required after the leak is stopped.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only and is not professional electrical or plumbing advice. Always prioritize physical safety over property protection. If you suspect an electrical hazard and cannot safely access your breaker, evacuate the premises and contact a professional.
