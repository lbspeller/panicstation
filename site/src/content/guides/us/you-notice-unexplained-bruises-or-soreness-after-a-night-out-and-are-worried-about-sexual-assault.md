# What to do if… you notice unexplained bruises or soreness after a night out and are worried about sexual assault (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safe place and contact RAINN, a local sexual assault crisis center, or medical care as soon as you can. You usually do not have to decide about reporting right now; you can ask an advocate or clinician what must be shared in your state.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pressure yourself to prove what happened before getting help.
- Do not confront anyone you suspect while you are alone, unsafe, or in shock.
- Do not delete texts, photos, call logs, location history, or rideshare receipts if you may want options later.
- Do not throw away clothing, underwear, bedding, cups, or other items from the night if you may want options later.
- Do not delay emergency care if you have significant pain, bleeding, injuries, trouble breathing, vomiting, confusion, or you feel seriously unwell.
- Do not investigate by yourself or message people from the night if doing that could put you at risk.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer place and bring in one safe person.** Move away from anyone who may be involved. Ask a trusted person to stay with you or help you get to medical care. If you are in immediate danger or need urgent medical help, call **911**.
2. **Contact sexual assault support.** RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline is available 24/7:
   - Call **800-656-HOPE (4673)**
   - Chat through RAINN’s hotline page
   - Text **“HOPE” to 64673**
3. **Seek medical care today if you can.** An ER or a sexual-assault care clinic can check bruises, soreness, bleeding, injury, pregnancy risk, and STI risk. Tell them you are worried about possible sexual assault and ask whether a SANE, SAFE, or other trained sexual-assault clinician is available.
4. **Say clearly if you think you may have been drugged or spiked.** Ask whether toxicology testing is appropriate and time-sensitive. You do not need to know what substance it might have been, and a negative test does not prove nothing happened.
5. **Make a brief memory snapshot.** In your phone notes or on paper, write only the basics: where you were, who you were with, when you last remember feeling okay, where you woke up, what hurts, what bruises you see, and whether clothing or underwear was missing or changed.
6. **If you may want the option of a forensic exam, preserve what you can without panicking.** If you can, avoid showering, bathing, brushing your teeth, changing clothes, or washing bedding until you speak to a clinician or advocate. Keep clothing or bedding from the night in a clean, dry bag; paper is best if you have one, but use the cleanest dry bag or container you can. If you already showered or changed, it is still okay to seek care.
7. **Protect existing digital details, then stop.** If it is easy, screenshot or save texts, call logs, rideshare receipts, location history, photos, or payment records from the night. Do not keep scrolling or trying to solve everything while you are overwhelmed.
8. **Ask about privacy and reporting before you start.** Tell the advocate or clinician what you want to know: “Do I have to report to police?” “What are you required to share?” “Can I get medical care without making a report?” Rules can vary by state and by age or safety situation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report to police, seek a protective order, make a complaint to a venue, or take legal action.
- You do not need to decide what to call what happened.
- You do not need to contact the bar, club, host, friends from the night, or anyone you suspect unless it affects your immediate safety or your ability to get care.
- You do not need to write a full timeline, collect proof yourself, or answer every message today.
- You do not need to decide what this means for your relationships, work, school, or future.

## Important reassurance
Bruises, soreness, numbness, shock, and patchy memory can happen for different reasons, including trauma, alcohol, drugs, injury, or a mix of things. Your concern is enough reason to seek support and medical care. You deserve calm help, clear choices, and control over what happens next wherever possible.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: get safer, get support, check your health, and keep options open. Later decisions about reporting, evidence, medical follow-up, school, work, or legal issues may need help from specialist advocates, clinicians, or legal services.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical, legal, investigative, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or need urgent medical attention, call 911. If you are unsure what to do, contacting RAINN or going to an ER is a reasonable first step.
