# What to do if… your employer stops reimbursing your approved work expenses (USA)

## Short answer
Pause avoidable new out-of-pocket spending, gather the approvals and receipts, and send one written request asking why payment stopped and when it will be paid. If your pay may have dropped below required minimum wage or overtime, contact the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division; if it is mainly a state reimbursement issue, ask your state labor agency what process applies.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep paying new work costs personally when reimbursement is unclear, unless you have no safe practical alternative.
- Do not rely only on verbal promises such as “next payroll” without a written date and confirmation.
- Do not mix personal and work spending without clear records, separate receipts, and notes.
- Do not threaten, accuse, or argue in the first message; keep it factual and documented.
- Do not assume federal law requires reimbursement for every work expense in every situation; state rules vary.

## What to do now
1. **Pause avoidable new spending and ask for a company-funded option.**  
   Write to your manager: “I have approved reimbursements still unpaid, so I need a company-funded method before I incur more required expenses.” Ask for one of these:
   - a company card or virtual card
   - travel booked directly by the company
   - a cash advance or per diem
   - a vendor invoice paid directly by the company

2. **Make a simple proof bundle today.**  
   Put these in one folder:
   - approval emails, chat messages, tickets, or written OKs
   - receipts and invoices
   - submitted expense reports, with dates and report IDs
   - the reimbursement policy or handbook language you relied on
   - pay stubs or payment records showing what was and was not reimbursed
   - any messages showing similar expenses were paid before

3. **Send one written request for clarity.**  
   Email payroll or finance, copying your manager if that is normal at your workplace. Include:
   - the total unpaid amount
   - a short itemised list of expenses
   - the dates submitted
   - who approved them
   - this request: “Please confirm why reimbursement stopped and the payment date.”
   - this boundary: “Until this is resolved, I need the company to provide a company-funded method for any required expenses.”

4. **Check whether this may be a minimum-wage or overtime problem.**  
   Federal law generally does not require reimbursement for every business expense. For covered nonexempt workers, though, employer-required costs generally cannot cut into required minimum wage or overtime pay. For each affected workweek, write down:
   - gross pay for the week
   - hours worked
   - unpaid required expenses for that week
   - whether those expenses brought your pay below the required minimum wage or reduced overtime pay

5. **Check whether your state has an expense-reimbursement rule.**  
   Some states have specific rules for necessary business expenses. California, for example, has Labor Code § 2802, which addresses necessary expenditures or losses connected to an employee’s duties or employer directions. If you are not sure about your state, use the official state labor office contact route and ask: “Does my state have a complaint process for approved, necessary business expenses that remain unpaid?”

6. **Escalate through the route that matches the problem.**
   - If your pay may have dropped below required minimum wage or overtime, contact the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division and ask about filing a complaint.
   - If your state may require reimbursement for necessary business expenses, contact your state labor agency and ask what complaint or wage-claim process applies.
   - If the company says the reimbursement was denied, ask for the denial reason in writing and which policy they are relying on.
   - Keep the proof bundle ready; agencies usually ask for dates, amounts, employer details, pay information, and copies of records.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, hire a lawyer, or sue.
- You do not need to prove bad intent before taking first steps; focus on amounts, approvals, dates, and written records.
- You do not need to contact every internal person at once; start with one clear written request, then escalate if needed.
- You do not need to solve future travel or supply arrangements today beyond asking for a company-funded method.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to feel panicky when you paid work costs yourself and reimbursement suddenly stops, especially after approval. You are not being difficult by asking for a written explanation, a payment date, and a safer way to handle future required expenses.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about complaints, legal options, repayment timing, or retaliation concerns may depend on your state, your pay status, the amount unpaid, and what the company required you to spend.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Rules differ by state and workplace facts. If the unpaid amount is large, there may be a deadline, or you are worried about retaliation, consider getting state-specific advice before taking irreversible action.
