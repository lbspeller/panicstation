# What to do if… someone threatens to spread false claims about consent unless you stay silent (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause and reduce contact with the person if that feels safer. Contact confidential sexual assault support so you do not have to handle the threat alone.

## Do not do these things
- Do not meet the person privately to discuss the threat.
- Do not keep replying because you feel pressured to defend yourself.
- Do not feel you have to decide now whether to reply, comply, challenge the threat, or disclose anything.
- Do not post publicly while you feel overwhelmed.
- Do not make yourself choose a label or decide whether to report right now.

## What to do now
1. Get to a safer place or safer pause. If you are in immediate danger or afraid you may be harmed, call 911.
2. Reduce contact for now if that feels safer. Silence notifications or move away from the conversation if that helps. You do not have to answer immediately. If reducing contact could increase risk, focus on the safest next pause and contact support.
3. If you may want options later and can do so safely, keep any existing messages without replying, gathering more material, or trying to handle this perfectly.
4. Contact RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline for confidential support at 800-656-4673, by online chat, or by texting `HOPE` to `64673`.
5. Tell one trusted person who can stay calm with you. A short message is enough: “Someone is threatening me to stay silent about consent. I need support.”
6. Use RAINN’s local service-provider directory if you want support near you.
7. If the person is a partner or ex-partner, The National Domestic Violence Hotline can also help you think about safety. VictimConnect is another confidential referral option; check its page for current availability.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to report anything.
- You do not have to confront the person or respond to rumours now.
- You do not have to write a detailed account while you feel overwhelmed.
- You do not have to decide what label fits before asking for support.
- You do not have to make longer-term relationship, workplace, school, or legal decisions today.

## Important reassurance
The threat is not your fault. Fear, uncertainty, or feeling frozen can happen after someone pressures you to stay silent. You deserve support, and you do not have to manage this alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce pressure and help you reach a safer pause. Later decisions may need support from a specialist advocate or another qualified professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Prioritize immediate safety. Skip any step that could increase your risk.
