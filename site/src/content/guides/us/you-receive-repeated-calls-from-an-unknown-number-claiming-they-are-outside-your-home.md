# What to do if… you receive repeated calls from an unknown number claiming they are outside your home (USA)

## Short answer
Stop engaging, stay inside with doors locked, and treat the claim as potentially real until you can rule it out safely. If you feel unsafe or think someone may actually be outside, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go outside to check, meet the caller, or open the door because of the call.
- Don’t confirm your address, whether you are alone, or who is with you.
- Don’t argue, negotiate, threaten, or try to outsmart the caller.
- Don’t rely on caller ID as proof of who is calling.
- Don’t post about it in real time on social media.
- Don’t keep calling the number back.

## What to do now
1. **End the call and stop engaging.** Let further calls go to voicemail. Use silence-unknown-callers, call screening, or do-not-disturb settings while allowing trusted contacts through if your phone supports it.
2. **Make your location safer.** Lock doors and accessible windows. Turn on exterior lights if you can do that without exposing yourself. Keep children, pets, and other people away from doors and ground-floor windows.
3. **Check without opening or stepping outside.** Use a peephole, door camera, security camera, or a view from an interior window. Avoid standing in a lit doorway or window where you are easy to see.
4. **If you feel in immediate danger or think someone may be outside, call 911.** Tell the dispatcher you are receiving repeated calls from an unknown number, the caller claims to be outside your location, and you cannot confirm whether that is true. Give your exact location and follow the dispatcher’s instructions.
5. **If there is no immediate danger but the calls are threatening or harassing, contact local police through the non-emergency number.** Use your local agency’s online reporting option only if it is offered and you are safe enough to do so. Save voicemails and screenshots of the call log.
6. **Assume the number could be spoofed.** Do not trust what appears on caller ID, and do not verify personal details to the caller. If the caller claims to be from an organization, hang up and use an official number you find separately.
7. **Use your phone and carrier tools.** Block the number, enable spam or unknown-call filtering, and ask your carrier what reporting options they offer for suspected scam or spoofed calls.
8. **Bring in a calm third party without creating confrontation.** Call or text a trusted neighbor, friend, building security desk, or front desk to stay in contact. Ask them to check only if they can do it safely, and do not ask anyone to confront the caller.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to change your phone number.
- Buying new security equipment.
- Writing a detailed account; saving the call log and voicemails is enough for now.
- Filing non-urgent federal reports with the FCC, FTC, or National Do Not Call Registry.
- Trying to identify the caller tonight.

## Important reassurance
A caller may be trying to create panic so you open the door, step outside, or confirm personal details. Staying inside, reducing contact, saving evidence, and calling 911 when you feel at risk is a reasonable response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on stabilizing the moment and preventing irreversible mistakes. If the calls continue, later decisions may need help from your phone carrier, local police, building security, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you believe you are in danger or someone may be outside and you feel unsafe, call 911.
