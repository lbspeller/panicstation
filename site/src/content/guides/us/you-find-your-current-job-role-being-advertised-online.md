# What to do if… you find your current job role being advertised online (USA)

## Short answer
Pause, save what you saw safely, and ask for a calm, direct explanation before you react. Do not resign, accuse anyone in writing, or sign anything while you still do not know what the posting means.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign just to regain control of the situation.
- Do not send a hostile email or message accusing anyone of replacing you.
- Do not post about it on social media or turn it into a public workplace conflict.
- Do not copy, download, forward, or remove confidential company information.
- Do not secretly record meetings or calls without checking your state law and workplace policy.
- Do not sign a resignation letter, severance release, warning, or new contract immediately if you need time to read it.
- Do not assume the posting is illegal. Many U.S. workers are at-will, but there are important exceptions.

## What to do now
1. **Save proof of the posting safely.** If the listing is public, screenshot or print the full posting on a personal device. Include the job title, duties, location, pay range if shown, date, and URL. If it is only visible in an internal or restricted system, do not copy restricted content; write down non-confidential facts such as where you saw it, the date and time, the reference number if allowed, and why it appears to match your role.
2. **Check whether it truly matches your job.** Compare the posting with your job title, duties, location, reporting line, schedule, and team. It may be a replacement role, but it may also be a parallel role, expansion, backfill, reclassification, or a posting kept open for recruiting.
3. **Create a short factual timeline.** Write down when you found the posting, what it said, any recent performance discussions, changes in duties, conflicts, complaints, leave requests, accommodation requests, safety concerns, wage concerns, or other protected activity. Keep the timeline factual and off work systems.
4. **Gather only records you are allowed to keep.** Put personal copies of your offer letter, employment agreement, handbook acknowledgement, performance reviews, pay records, written praise, warnings, and benefits information in one place. Do not take confidential files, customer data, trade secrets, or internal documents you are not allowed to remove.
5. **Ask for a short private meeting.** Use calm, specific wording such as: “I saw a posting that looks like my role. Can you help me understand what it is for, and whether it affects my position?”
6. **Take brief notes during or right after the meeting.** Record the date, who was present, what was said about the posting, whether your role is affected, and any next steps. Keep the notes neutral.
7. **Send a short written recap if anything important was said.** For example: “Thank you for speaking with me today. My understanding is that the posting is for [reason given], and that [my role is/is not/has not yet been] affected. Please let me know if I misunderstood.” Do not add accusations or legal threats.
8. **If you are told your job may be ending, ask for the basics in writing.** Ask for the effective date, stated reason, final-pay information, benefits information, unemployment-related paperwork if applicable, and whether any severance documents will be provided. Ask for time to review anything you are asked to sign.
9. **If you suspect an illegal reason, preserve the record and use the right route for that issue.** At-will employment generally does not allow firing for illegal reasons. Depending on what happened, that may mean the EEOC or a state or local fair-employment agency for discrimination or related retaliation; OSHA’s whistleblower program for some safety-related retaliation; the U.S. Department of Labor or your state labor office for wage, overtime, final-pay, or leave-related pay problems; or the NLRB for many private-sector protected concerted activity issues such as acting with coworkers about pay or working conditions.
10. **Keep your work communications steady while you clarify.** Meet deadlines, keep messages factual, and avoid creating a side issue while you are trying to understand what the posting means.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, threaten legal action, or announce anything to coworkers.
- You do not need to collect everything from company systems.
- You do not need to accept severance terms or sign a release immediately; you can ask for time to review.
- You do not need to decide your whole job-search plan before you get basic clarity.
- You do not need to prove the employer’s intent before asking a simple factual question.

## Important reassurance
Finding a posting that looks like your job can feel frightening and humiliating. A careful first response can protect your options: save the public facts, avoid irreversible moves, and get the employer’s explanation on record.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if your state law, a contract, a union agreement, public-sector rules, discrimination, retaliation, wages, leave, or severance documents are involved.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employment rules vary by state, workplace, contract, and job type.
