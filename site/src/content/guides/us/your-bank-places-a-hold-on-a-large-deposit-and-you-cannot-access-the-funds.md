# What to do if… your bank places a hold on a large deposit and you cannot access the funds (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your financial institution immediately to confirm if the hold is a standard Regulation CC funds availability delay (affecting only the deposit) or an account-level security restriction. Ask for the specific "exception reason" and the exact date the funds will be released for withdrawal.

## Do not do these things
- Do not transfer or spend any portion of the held deposit if it was received from a third party you do not know personally, as this may be a "check overpayment" or "fake check" scam.
- Do not attempt to bypass the hold by opening new accounts and immediately moving other funds; this can trigger anti-money laundering (AML) alerts and further freezes.
- Do not provide sensitive account information or secondary passwords to anyone calling you about the hold; only speak to the bank through their verified customer service number.
- Do not take out high-interest emergency loans until you have explicitly asked the bank for a "hardship release" of the first $275 or other partial funds.

## What to do now
1. **Determine if this is a Regulation CC hold or a security freeze.** **Ask the representative whether the hold is specifically a "funds availability hold" on a specific check or a "security restriction" on the entire account.**
   - If it is Regulation CC, federal law dictates specific timelines for release.
   - If it is a security freeze (often for BSA/AML compliance), the bank may be legally restricted from disclosing the specific reason.

2. **Verify the 2026 dollar thresholds for your specific deposit.** **Under current Regulation CC rules, ensure the bank has made the first $275 of a non-next-day check available by the next business day.**
   - For deposits over $6,725 (the 2026 "large deposit" threshold), the bank can hold the amount exceeding $6,725 for a "reasonable" extra period, typically 5 to 7 business days.

3. **Demand the written exception-hold notice if one was not provided.** **Ask the bank to provide the "exception-hold notice" required by law, which must state the reason for the hold and the date the funds will be available.**
   - Common legal reasons include: "Large deposit" (over $6,725), "New account" (open <30 days), "Repeatedly overdrawn," or "Reasonable cause to doubt collectability."

4. **Prepare documentation to prove the source of the funds.** **Collect and offer to provide the "source of funds" documentation that addresses the bank’s risk concern.**
   - This may include a settlement statement (for real estate), an insurance claim letter, a signed contract/invoice, or a payroll statement.
   - Ask for a secure portal link or a branch appointment to submit these; avoid sending them via standard email.

5. **Request a partial release for immediate essentials.** **Explicitly state that the hold is causing a financial hardship and ask for a manual release of funds needed for housing, food, or utilities.**
   - Ask the bank to "provisionally credit" a portion of the funds while the check clears.
   - Ask for a waiver of any overdraft or non-sufficient fund (NSF) fees caused by the hold.

6. **Notify essential payees if a delay is unavoidable.** **Contact your landlord or utility provider today if the hold prevents a scheduled payment, rather than waiting for the payment to fail.**
   - Request a "one-time payment extension" due to a bank processing delay.
   - Document the date and time of these calls and any reference numbers provided.

7. **Escalate to the bank's internal "Office of the President" or "Executive Communications" team.** **If the standard customer service line cannot provide a release date, ask to speak with the "Deposit Operations" or "Risk Management" department.**
   - Record the name and employee ID of every representative you speak with.

8. **Initiate an external complaint if the hold exceeds 7 business days without explanation.** **Submit a complaint to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) if the bank fails to provide a legal notice or refuses to release funds after the standard holding period.**
   - For credit unions, contact the National Credit Union Administration (NCUA).
   - Note that for certain disputes, these agencies may require you to have received a "final response" letter from the bank first.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to close your account or move your primary banking relationship.
- You do not need to hire legal counsel for a standard 7-day hold; regulatory complaints are the standard first path for resolution.
- You do not need to resolve the underlying reason for a "reasonable cause" hold today if the bank has already provided a firm release date.

## Important reassurance
Bank holds on large deposits are automated risk management procedures designed to ensure a check is valid before the money is withdrawn. While the lack of access is stressful, these holds are usually temporary and do not indicate a permanent loss of funds or a failure on your part. Following the bank's verification steps typically resolves the issue within 1 to 7 business days.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for funds availability holds under U.S. federal regulations. It does not cover long-term legal disputes, permanent account closures, or funds seized by law enforcement via a warrant.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal or financial advice. Banking regulations like Regulation CC have specific exceptions for new accounts and suspected fraud that may extend hold times beyond standard limits. Always verify communication from your bank using the phone number on the back of your debit card or your official monthly statement.
