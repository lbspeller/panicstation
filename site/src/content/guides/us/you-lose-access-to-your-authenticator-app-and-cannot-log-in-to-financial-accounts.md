# What to do if… you lose access to your authenticator app and cannot log in to financial accounts (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your financial institution's official fraud or customer service line to verify your identity and request a manual reset of your multi-factor authentication (MFA) settings. Secure your secondary recovery methods, such as email and mobile carrier accounts, to prevent unauthorized access during the reset period.

## Do not do these things
- Avoid approving any unexpected "sign-in," "add device," or "verification" push notifications on your device.
- Avoid using phone numbers or links provided in unsolicited emails or texts claiming to help with "account recovery."
- Avoid sharing one-time passcodes (OTP), backup codes, or temporary passwords with anyone, including individuals claiming to be bank employees.
- Avoid repeated failed login attempts that may trigger a permanent security lockout of your profile.
- Avoid making major transfers or opening new accounts until your primary access is fully restored and secured.

## What to do now
1. **Initiate contact through verified official channels.**  
   **Call the customer service or fraud department number found on the back of your physical debit / credit card or your most recent official paper statement.**  
   - Explain that you have lost access to your 2FA / authenticator device.
   - Request that they "flag the account for heightened security" while access is being restored.
   - Verify that your contact information (mobile number, email, and physical address) has not been changed recently.

2. **Request a multi-factor authentication (MFA) reset.**  
   **Ask the representative specifically to de-provision the lost authenticator app and initiate the identity verification process for a new device.**  
   - Be prepared to provide secondary identification, which may include answering knowledge-based authentication questions or visiting a local branch in person.
   - Inquire about the "cool-down period" for the reset, as some banks require a 24 to 72-hour wait before a new 2FA method becomes active.

3. **Secure your mobile carrier account against SIM swapping.**  
   **Contact your mobile provider and request that they enable "Port-Out Protection" or "Number Lock" on your line.**  
   - Check if any unauthorized SIM changes or eSIM transfers have occurred in the last 48 hours.
   - Ensure your carrier account is protected by a unique PIN or passcode that is not shared with your banking passwords.

4. **Audit and secure your primary recovery email.**  
   **Log in to the email account associated with your financial profiles and check for unrecognized sign-in activity or new forwarding rules.**  
   - Review the "Sent" or "Trash" folders for any confirmation emails regarding password changes you did not initiate.
   - Update your email password and ensure it also uses a secure, functioning 2FA method (such as a hardware key or a different backup phone).

5. **Place protective alerts on your credit files if you suspect a compromise.**  
   **Contact one of the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion) to place a free one-year fraud alert on your credit report.**  
   - Under US law, the bureau you contact must notify the other two automatically.
   - For higher security, contact each of the three bureaus individually to place a "Credit Freeze," which prevents new credit accounts from being opened in your name.

6. **Monitor for unauthorized transactions via alternative means.**  
   **Ask the bank representative to read back the last five transactions and any "pending" authorizations while you are on the phone.**  
   - If any transaction is unrecognized, request an immediate "Fraud Claim" and ask for the specific card or account to be restricted.

## What can wait
- Evaluating different third-party authenticator apps for future use.
- Updating security settings on non-financial accounts (social media, shopping).
- Reviewing long-term credit report history beyond the last few days.
- Closing or switching your primary bank account entirely.

## Important reassurance
Losing a 2FA device is a standard technical issue that financial institutions are equipped to handle daily. While the verification process may feel repetitive or slow, these delays are intentional security barriers designed to protect your assets from unauthorized recovery attempts.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for regaining access to US-based financial accounts. It does not cover the recovery of cryptocurrency private keys or specialized corporate banking systems which may require different legal protocols.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal, financial, or cybersecurity advice. Individual bank policies and federal regulations may change; always prioritize the specific security instructions provided by your financial institution's official fraud department.
