# What to do if… you are in a hotel or apartment building and someone is trying multiple doors in the corridor (USA)

## Short answer
Keep the door locked, move away from the entryway, and call 911 or building security immediately. Do not open the door or acknowledge the person verbally unless they attempt to force entry.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door to investigate or confront the individual.
- Do not shout through the door that you are alone or ask what they want.
- Do not turn on lights that are visible from the hallway (e.g., under-door gaps).
- Do not look through the peephole if you see light from the hallway being blocked; stay away from the door.
- Do not leave the door unlocked for security; they have their own keys/access.

## What to do now
1. **Engage every physical lock and secondary security device immediately.** **Verify that the deadbolt, swing-bar, or security chain is fully seated.**
   - If you have a portable door wedge or security bar, install it now.
   - Cover the peephole with a piece of paper or your finger to prevent "reverse peephole" viewing or light leaking.
2. **Move to a location in the room away from the door and out of the line of sight.** **Position yourself in a bathroom or behind a secondary interior door if possible.**
   - Put on sturdy shoes and keep your phone, ID, and car keys on your person.
   - Keep the room quiet; silence your phone’s ringer and vibration.
3. **Call 911 directly from your mobile phone or the room phone.** **State your exact location including the hotel name, floor number, and room or unit number first.**
   - Federal law (Kari’s Law) requires hotel phones to dial 911 directly without a prefix like "9."
   - If you cannot speak safely, keep the line open. If the dispatcher asks "Yes/No" questions, you can tap once for "Yes" and twice for "No" on the mouthpiece or use the keypad if instructed.
4. **Contact the front desk or building security after calling emergency services.** **Inform them that a suspicious person is testing door handles on your floor.**
   - **Hotel:** Use the "Emergency" or "Security" button on the room phone.
   - **Apartment:** Use the concierge or after-hours security line.
   - Request that they monitor hallway cameras and send personnel to your floor immediately.
5. **Monitor for sounds of forced entry or movement.** **Listen for the direction the person is traveling and whether they are using tools or keys.**
   - Do not approach the door to listen; stay back. 
   - Note distinctive sounds like a heavy cart, multiple voices, or the use of a master keycard.
6. **Identify responders before opening the door.** **Keep the door locked until you have confirmed the identity of the person on the other side.**
   - If someone knocks and claims to be security or police, call the front desk or 911 back to verify that personnel were sent to your specific room number.
   - Do not rely on a uniform or a verbal claim alone.
7. **Request a relocation if you are in a hotel.** **Ask security to escort you to the lobby or a different room once the hallway is cleared.**
   - If you move rooms, ensure the old keycard is deactivated and the new room is on a different floor.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the person or determine their intent.
- You do not need to gather your luggage or pack your belongings before calling for help.
- You do not need to review your own security footage or doorbell camera until you are in a secure location.
- You do not need to decide whether to press charges or file a formal management complaint right this second.

## Important reassurance
Hearing someone testing doors is a significant security indicator that justifies an immediate emergency response. Your reaction is a standard safety protocol designed to prevent unauthorized entry. Taking these steps is a proactive measure to ensure your physical boundaries remain intact.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate security threat. It does not cover long-term legal actions, lease breaks, or hotel refund policies.

## Important note
This information is for immediate safety stabilization and does not constitute legal advice. If you are in active danger, follow all instructions provided by the 911 dispatcher or responding law enforcement officers.
