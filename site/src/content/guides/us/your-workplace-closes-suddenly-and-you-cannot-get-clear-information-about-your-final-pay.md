# What to do if… your workplace closes suddenly and you cannot get clear information about your final pay (USA)

## Short answer
Save proof of what you worked and what you think you are owed, then create a written record today if you can. Final paycheck timing is often a state-law issue, but the U.S. Department of Labor and state labor offices can point you to official wage-claim routes.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume federal law guarantees an immediate final paycheck; final pay timing often depends on state rules.
- Do not sign any severance, release, resignation, or “paid in full” statement until you can verify the amount and what it covers.
- Do not return company equipment without getting a dated receipt or keeping dated proof of what you returned.
- Do not hand over original pay records; keep copies, photos, or screenshots.
- Do not send Social Security numbers, bank details, or identity documents to a new contact unless you can verify the contact through an official company, court, agency, or government route.
- Do not rely only on phone calls or verbal promises; make at least one written request you can save.
- Do not start with public accusations online; focus first on evidence, dates, and official channels.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the core pay facts while they are fresh.**  
   Note your last day worked, last day paid, pay rate, usual payday, unpaid dates and hours, and what you think is still owed, such as regular wages, overtime, earned commissions, tips, bonuses, or PTO or vacation.

2. **Save records before access disappears.**  
   Save pay stubs, timecards, schedules, tip records, commission plans, PTO or vacation policy pages, offer letters, closure messages, emails or texts about hours worked, and bank deposits from earlier paydays.

3. **Send one calm written request to the employer contact you have.**  
   Ask for your separation date, the final paycheck issue date, where the check or deposit will be sent, and a written breakdown of wages, overtime, commissions, tips, deductions, and PTO or vacation if applicable. Keep proof that you sent it.

4. **Check your state labor office or wage-claim route.**  
   Use the state where you worked, not just where you live, unless the official unemployment or labor office tells you otherwise. State labor offices vary, so look for “wage claim,” “unpaid wages,” “final paycheck,” or “labor standards” on the state office site.

5. **Contact the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division if minimum wage, overtime, tips, or hours are involved.**  
   WHD can take complaints about covered federal wage issues. Have your employer name, work location, manager contact details, dates worked, pay rate, and unpaid-hours estimate ready if you have them.

6. **If there is a bankruptcy notice, case number, or court mailing, follow that track carefully.**  
   Save the notice, look for the claim deadline, and use the official U.S. Courts Proof of Claim form if the court notice tells creditors to file claims. Attach copies of pay records, not originals. Missing a bankruptcy deadline may make recovery harder or impossible.

7. **Check Workers Owed Wages.**  
   If the Department of Labor has already recovered wages from your employer, the Workers Owed Wages tool may show money that can be claimed.

8. **If this was a large closing or mass layoff, check WARN information but do not assume it applies.**  
   WARN only applies in covered situations. Save closure dates, layoff messages, and any notice you received, because those dates may matter later.

9. **Apply for unemployment through the state where you worked as soon as you reasonably can.**  
   Unemployment insurance is state-run. A missing final paycheck does not have to be solved before you start an unemployment claim.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to sue, hire an attorney, or join a group claim.
- You do not need a perfect dollar calculation before starting an official complaint or claim; use your best estimate and keep updating your records.
- You do not need to negotiate severance while basic final-pay information is still missing.
- You do not need to solve every benefits issue today; focus first on saving records, starting unemployment, and finding the right wage-claim route.

## Important reassurance
Sudden closures are confusing, and vague or conflicting answers are common when payroll, HR, and management are disrupted. A saved record, a written request, and official wage or unemployment channels can give you a steadier path without needing to solve everything at once.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on stabilizing the situation, preserving pay evidence, and finding credible official routes. Later decisions about hearings, settlements, bankruptcy claims, group action, or legal advice can come after you have created a clear record.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Final paycheck timing, PTO or vacation payout, wage-claim processes, unemployment rules, and bankruptcy procedures vary by state, employer policy, and court case. Use official agency or court information for your situation and keep your records factual and organized.
