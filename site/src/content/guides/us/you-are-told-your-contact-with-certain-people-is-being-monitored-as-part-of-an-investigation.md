# What to do if… you are told your contact with certain people is being monitored as part of an investigation (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all communication regarding the investigation with anyone except a licensed attorney and contact a criminal defense lawyer immediately. Assume all standard phone calls, facility emails, and text messages are being recorded or reviewed by law enforcement.

## Do not do these things
- Do not call or message involved parties to "clear things up" or coordinate statements.
- Do not delete any digital evidence, including texts, Signal/WhatsApp chats, or call logs, as this can trigger obstruction of justice charges.
- Do not use facility email systems (like TRULINCS or CorrLinks) for legal strategy, as these are typically not privileged.
- Do not attempt to "test" monitoring by sending fake or "bait" messages.
- Do not ask third parties to relay messages or hold devices for you.
- Do not use "patching" or three-way calling services to reach people, as this violates facility rules and does not bypass monitoring.

## What to do now
1. **Cease all case-related communication immediately.**
   **Stop discussing facts, timelines, or people involved in the investigation via any non-privileged channel.**
   - Non-privileged channels include standard cell phones, facility phones, social media, and inmate email systems.
   - Keep necessary communications (e.g., family logistics) strictly practical and brief.

2. **Document the source of the monitoring notice.**
   **Write down exactly how you learned about the monitoring while the details are fresh.**
   - Note the name and agency of the person who told you.
   - Note the date, time, and specific warning provided (e.g., "all calls to [Name] are being recorded").
   - Keep this written note in a secure place, ideally with your legal papers.

3. **Secure a qualified criminal defense attorney.**
   **Contact a lawyer before responding to any further investigative outreach.**
   - If law enforcement attempts to interview you, state: "I am exercising my right to remain silent and I want to speak to an attorney."
   - Do not engage in "informal" or "off-the-record" conversations with investigators.

4. **Verify the terms of any "No Contact" orders.**
   **Review your bond paperwork, pretrial release conditions, or protective orders for specific communication bans.**
   - Check if the monitoring is a result of a "No Third-Party Contact" order.
   - If you are unsure of the terms, ask your lawyer to pull the official court order rather than guessing.

5. **Establish protected legal communication channels.**
   **Request unmonitored legal calls or in-person attorney visits through official facility procedures.**
   - In federal facilities, unmonitored calls must be specifically requested and scheduled through staff (e.g., a Counselor or Executive Assistant).
   - Ensure all legal mail is clearly marked: "Special Mail - Open only in the presence of the inmate" to protect privilege.

6. **Manage external pressure with a consistent script.**
   **Use a neutral, repetitive phrase if friends or family press you for information.**
   - Example: "My lawyer has instructed me not to discuss any details of the case right now."
   - Do not provide "just a little bit" of information to satisfy others.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the technical method of the monitoring (e.g., wiretap vs. pen register) right now.
- You do not need to gather or organize evidence folders today; wait for your attorney’s specific instructions on what is relevant.
- You do not need to "go dark" or destroy devices; standard behavior is less likely to be interpreted as consciousness of guilt.
- You can wait to decide whether to challenge the legality of the monitoring until after your counsel has reviewed the warrants or orders.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a notice of monitoring is a standard part of many investigations and does not mean a conviction is inevitable. Your immediate reaction—feeling watched or anxious—is a normal response to an intrusive situation. By following a "stop talking" protocol, you are taking the most effective step possible to protect your legal interests.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for individuals notified of communication monitoring in the USA. It is not a substitute for individual legal strategy. Monitoring rules vary significantly between federal (BOP) and state/local facilities, and between grand jury investigations and post-arrest proceedings.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding surveillance, the Sixth Amendment, and attorney-client privilege are complex and subject to change. If you are under investigation or incarcerated, contact a qualified attorney to discuss your specific rights and obligations under 2026 legal standards.
