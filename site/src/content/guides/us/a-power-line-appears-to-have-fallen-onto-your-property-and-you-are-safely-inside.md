# What to do if… a power line appears to have fallen onto your property and you are safely inside (USA)

## Short answer
Stay inside your home, avoid touching anything metal or running water, and immediately **call 911** to report the downed line. Assume the wire and the ground around it are energized and potentially lethal.

## Do not do these things
- Do **not** go outside to investigate, even if there is no sparking, smoke, or noise.
- Do **not** touch faucets, showers, or metal pipes, as electricity can travel through plumbing if the line is touching the house.
- Do **not** use corded landline phones or touch appliances that are plugged into wall outlets.
- Do **not** touch any metal objects connected to the exterior, such as window frames, door handles, or gutters.
- Do **not** approach windows or glass doors on the side of the house where the line is located.
- Do **not** attempt to move the wire with a broom, stick, or any other object, as even non-conductive materials can carry current if they are damp or the voltage is high.

## What to do now
1. **Move everyone to a room on the opposite side of the house.** Ensure children and pets are kept away from the area where the line is down.
2. **Call 911 immediately.** State clearly that there is a "downed power line on the property" and provide your exact location and any visible hazards like fire or sparking.
3. **Notify the electric utility company.** Use a cell phone to report the specific location of the line (e.g., "on the roof," "across the driveway") once you have finished the emergency call.
4. **Assume the ground is energized for at least 35 feet.** High-voltage transmission lines may require a distance of **100 feet or more**; stay as far away as possible if you can see the line from a safe window.
5. **Warn others to stay away.** If you can safely communicate with neighbors or people approaching the property from a distance (via phone or shouting through a far-side window), tell them to stay back at least 35 feet.
6. **If you are forced to evacuate due to fire or smoke inside the house:**
   - Use the exit **farthest** from the downed power line.
   - Do **not** walk normally; shuffle your feet without lifting them off the ground, keeping them in constant contact with each other.
   - Alternatively, hop with both feet together so they land at the same time; this prevents "step potential," where electricity flows through your body between two points on the ground.
   - Move at least 35 feet away (approximately three car lengths) and do not return until a utility professional confirms it is safe.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to identify if the wire is a power line, phone line, or cable; treat all downed wires as live power lines.
- You do **not** need to take photos or document the damage for insurance while the area is still active.
- You do **not** need to worry about clearing debris or fallen branches until the utility company has officially de-energized the lines.

## Important reassurance
Remaining indoors is the safest action you can take. Most modern homes provide significant protection as long as you avoid the specific paths electricity might take, such as metal pipes or wired electronics. Help is dispatched immediately for downed line reports because they are high-priority safety hazards.

## Scope note
This guide covers initial stabilization while you are inside a structure. Follow all specific directions from emergency dispatchers or utility workers on-site regarding evacuation or power shut-offs.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and does not replace professional guidance from emergency services or utility technicians. Electrical hazards are invisible and can change instantly; always prioritize instructions from on-site first responders.
