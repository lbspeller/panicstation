# What to do if… someone claims to be the partner of a person who died and starts requesting access to plans (USA)

## Short answer
Withhold all information and direct the individual to the court-appointed personal representative of the estate. Do not verify the relationship or provide any documents until a legal authority confirms their status through the probate process.

## Do not do these things
- Do not share copies or photos of the will, funeral contracts, insurance policies, or financial statements.
- Do not provide sensitive personal data such as the Social Security Number, birth date, or account numbers of the deceased.
- Do not allow the individual to pressure you into immediate decisions or "emergency" document transfers.
- Do not permit the individual to enter the home of the deceased or meet them alone if their identity is unverified.
- Do not engage in arguments or attempts to "disprove" the relationship yourself.
- Do not confirm which banks or providers held the deceased person's accounts.

## What to do now
1. **Establish a single, repetitive boundary statement.**  
   **Inform the person that all requests for documents or plans must be handled through the formal estate process.**  
   - Use a neutral phrase: "I cannot provide access to these records. Please direct your inquiry to the estate's personal representative or the probate court."  
   - Do not offer explanations or justifications for the boundary.
2. **Identify the person with legal authority over the estate.**  
   **Determine if a personal representative (executor or administrator) has been appointed by a probate court.**  
   - In the USA, authority is proved via a court-sealed document called "Letters Testamentary" (if there is a will) or "Letters of Administration" (if there is no will).  
   - If a representative exists, provide their contact information once and cease further direct communication with the claimant.
3. **Notify the Social Security Administration (SSA) to protect the identity of the deceased.**  
   **Call the SSA at 1-800-772-1213 (TTY 1-800-325-0778) to ensure the death is officially recorded.**  
   - This prevents the Social Security Number from being used for fraudulent credit applications or benefit claims.  
   - Note that reports must be made by phone or in person; the SSA does not accept reports online.
4. **Secure physical and digital records immediately.**  
   **Gather all original documents and move them to a locked or secure location.**  
   - Collect the original will, life insurance policies, and funeral pre-payment contracts.  
   - Secure digital access by logging out of shared devices, but do not attempt to change passwords on accounts owned solely by the deceased unless you are the court-appointed representative.
5. **Log every instance of contact from the claimant.**  
   **Maintain a chronological record of all messages, calls, and physical visits.**  
   - Save copies of emails and screenshots of text messages.  
   - Record the specific information requested and the exact wording of your response.  
   - This documentation is necessary if the situation escalates to harassment or requires evidence in probate court.
6. **Protect medical information under HIPAA guidelines.**  
   **Refuse requests for medical records or care history.**  
   - Federal HIPAA protections remain in effect for 50 years after an individual's death.  
   - Only a legally authorized personal representative can access or authorize the release of medical records.
7. **Report suspected fraud to the appropriate agencies.**  
   **If you believe the claim is an attempt at identity theft, use official reporting channels.**  
   - If you are the personal representative, file a report at IdentityTheft.gov.  
   - If you are being harassed or feel physically unsafe, call 911 or your local non-emergency police line to document the behavior.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the relationship claim is true or false today.
- You do not need to decide on inheritance or asset distribution now.
- You do not need to respond to "deadlines" set by the claimant.
- You do not need to hire a private investigator to look into the individual's background.

## Important reassurance
In the immediate aftermath of a death, the legal requirement for "Letters Testamentary" or "Letters of Administration" acts as a protective shield. Insisting on these documents is not an act of unkindness or a denial of a relationship; it is the standard legal procedure designed to protect the deceased person's wishes and the rights of all legitimate heirs.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling unexpected claims and protecting estate privacy. It does not cover the full probate process or the specific laws of intestate succession, which vary by state and may require an estate attorney.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Probate laws are jurisdiction-specific. If you are being threatened with legal action or feel the estate is at risk, consult a qualified probate or estate attorney in the state where the deceased resided.
