# What to do if… someone tries to steer you toward a quieter exit or side street and you feel pressured (USA)

## Short answer
Stop walking immediately and move back toward a well-lit, staffed, or crowded area. If the person refuses to let you go or continues to pressure you, call 911 and state that someone is attempting to isolate you.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t prioritize politeness over your physical safety by continuing to walk to avoid a scene.
- Don’t agree to go to a quieter area to “talk,” “find a ride,” or “look at something” once you feel uneasy.
- Don’t allow yourself to be led to a vehicle, a private residence, or a secluded alley.
- Don’t offer long explanations or negotiate; use short, firm refusals to maintain your boundary.
- Don’t look down at your phone or get distracted if the person is still within arm’s reach.
- Don’t assume you must handle the situation alone if there are bystanders or employees nearby.

## What to do now
1. **Break the physical momentum of the interaction immediately.** Stop walking, plant your feet, and step backward or sideways to create a gap of at least three feet. Use a firm, audible voice to say: **“No. I am staying here.”** Do not provide a reason or an excuse, as this invites negotiation.
2. **Move directly toward the nearest "witnessed" or staffed location.** Head for a bright, busy area such as a cashier counter, a security desk, a bouncer at the door, or a hotel front desk. If you are outside, **go inside** the nearest open business and stay in the line of sight of employees.
3. **Engage bystanders or staff with specific instructions.** Look for one person and say: **“I need help. This person is trying to lead me away and I don’t know them. Please stay here with me.”** If you are in a venue, alert staff that you feel unsafe and ask them to stand between you and the individual.
4. **Call 911 immediately if the person becomes aggressive or continues to follow you.** Provide the following information to the dispatcher:
   - Your current location (business name, address, or nearest intersection).
   - The specific nature of the threat (“Someone is pressuring me to move to a secluded area and won’t leave me alone”).
   - A physical description of the individual and their last known direction of travel.
5. **Use your phone to create an immediate connection to a witness.** If it is safe to do so, call a trusted friend or family member, put them on speakerphone, and state your location and the situation clearly. Use a location-sharing app to send your live "pinned" location to a contact immediately.
6. **Avoid going to your home or car if you are still being followed.** Continue moving between well-lit, staffed public spaces rather than entering quiet residential streets or parking structures. Stay in a lobby or a busy store until help arrives or you have a safe, escorted way to leave.
7. **Document the details of the incident once you are behind a locked door or with security.** Record the time, the specific words used by the individual, their physical appearance, and any vehicle details if applicable. Ask business staff if they have security footage of the interaction and if they can note the incident in their logs.
8. **Contact a national support service for further guidance and resource referrals.** If you need assistance with safety planning or navigating your rights, contact **VictimConnect** (a program of the National Center for Victims of Crime). You can **call or text 1-855-4-VICTIM (855-484-2846)**; in 2026, this service provides 24/7 confidential support and referrals for all victims of crime.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to determine if your reaction was "justified" or "proportionate" right now; safety is the priority.
- You don’t need to provide a detailed statement to the police until you have reached a secure location and recovered from the immediate shock.
- You don’t need to confront the person or explain why their behavior was inappropriate.
- You don’t need to keep moving if you have reached a safe, staffed building; it is okay to stay there for as long as necessary.

## Important reassurance
The feeling of pressure or "gut instinct" is a biological response to boundary-crossing. Choosing to stop, make a scene, or move back to a crowd is a standard and effective safety response when someone is attempting to isolate you.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate public safety threat. If this person is known to you or if you are being targeted repeatedly, you may require long-term safety planning and legal assistance.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and is not legal advice. In an emergency, always call 911.
