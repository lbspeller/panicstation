# What to do if… you receive a court claim or summons about an unpaid debt unexpectedly (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the case directly with the court and respond by the deadline shown in the court papers; missing the deadline can allow the case to move ahead without your side being heard.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the summons, complaint, court claim, or hearing notice.
- Do not assume the papers are fake just because the debt is old, unfamiliar, or from a company you do not recognize.
- Do not call the collector first to negotiate before you have checked the court deadline and case details.
- Do not admit the debt, agree to payments, or give bank details if you are not sure the debt is yours or the amount is correct.
- Do not pay anyone demanding gift cards, crypto, wire transfer, payment apps, or unusual same-day payment methods to “stop the lawsuit”.
- Do not rely on QR codes, short links, or phone numbers printed on suspicious papers; find the court’s official contact details independently.
- Do not miss any court date listed, even if you plan to dispute the case.

## What to do now
1. **Save every page and capture the key facts.**  
   Photograph or scan the papers, then write down:
   - the court name
   - the case number
   - the deadline to respond
   - any hearing, appearance, or trial date
   - the plaintiff and any law firm listed
   - the amount claimed and what the debt is said to be for

2. **Verify the case directly with the court.**
   - Search for the court’s official website yourself, or call the clerk of court using a number found independently.
   - Ask whether the case exists, what the response deadline is, where to file, whether there is a hearing date, and whether you must send a copy of your response to the other side.
   - If there is an online docket, compare the case number, names, and deadline with the papers you received.

3. **Identify what counts as a response in your court.**
   The response may be called an Answer, appearance, motion, or another filing, and some courts also require you to attend a hearing. Follow the summons and the court’s official instructions rather than guessing.

4. **File a timely response if the case is real.**
   Your immediate goal is to avoid default. Use the court’s official forms or self-help instructions if they are available, or contact legal aid, a court self-help center, or a lawyer for help with the correct local filing.

5. **If you do not recognize the debt, keep the issue clear and in writing.**
   - Compare the claim with old creditors, medical bills, utilities, phone plans, loans, addresses, and account records.
   - Note anything unclear, such as no account number, vague dates, an unknown original creditor, unexplained fees, or the wrong name or address.
   - Keep all collection letters, validation information, envelopes, emails, texts, and payment records.

6. **If you suspect identity theft, document it without skipping the lawsuit response.**
   You can use IdentityTheft.gov to create an identity theft report and recovery plan. That may help you organize records, but it does not replace filing a court response or attending a required court date.

7. **If service seems wrong, still act now.**
   If the papers went to an old address, were handed to someone else, arrived late, or were found after the deadline, tell the court or a legal aid adviser exactly what happened and ask what filing is needed to raise the service issue.

8. **Get local help focused on your court.**
   If you cannot afford a lawyer, look for legal aid in your state or county. If a deadline or court date is soon, try to contact legal aid, a court self-help center, or a consumer-debt lawyer today.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to settle, fight to the end, or pay the full amount.
- You do not need to give the plaintiff or collector your bank details, employer details, or new payment promise right now.
- You do not need to prove everything immediately; first, verify the case, protect the deadline, and preserve the papers.
- You do not need to understand every legal term before asking the court clerk where filing instructions and self-help resources are located.

## Important reassurance
A surprise debt lawsuit can feel frightening, but the first protective step is narrow: verify it and respond on time. Taking one concrete step can reduce the chance of the case moving ahead without your side being heard.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The right form, deadline, defense, fee waiver, service rule, and court process depend on your state and court, so later decisions may need help from local legal aid, a court self-help center, or a lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice, financial advice, or professional advice. Deadlines and required filings differ by state and court, so verify the case with the court, follow the summons instructions, and get local help if you are unsure.
