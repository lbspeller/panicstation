# What to do if… your flight is diverted to a different country and you are unsure what documents you need to enter temporarily (USA)

## Short answer
Do not leave the secure transit area or pass through "Arrivals" until you have confirmed your legal right to enter that country. Request an immediate Timatic check from the airline to verify transit requirements and contact the U.S. State Department's emergency line at +1 202-501-4444 if you are denied entry or forced to move landside without documents.

## Do not do these things
- Do not cross the immigration/customs boundary ("Landside") just to find a service desk or "figure it out."
- Do not surrender your passport to anyone other than uniformed immigration officers or official airline staff at a marked desk.
- Do not sign any "Entry Refused" or "Voluntary Departure" forms without speaking to a supervisor or consular official.
- Do not discard your original boarding passes or baggage tags; these prove you are a diverted passenger.
- Do not assume a short stay or "airline-provided hotel" exempts you from visa or biometric requirements.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm if you are required to clear immigration or remain airside.**  
   **Ask the gate agent or transit desk staff: "Are we remaining in international transit, or is the airport requiring us to clear border control?"** If the terminal is closing or you are being moved to a hotel, you will likely be asked to clear immigration.

2. **Request an immediate "Timatic check" for the diversion country.**  
   **Provide your passport and say: "Please run a Timatic check for my nationality for [Current Country] to see if I need a transit visa or entry permit for an unscheduled stop."** This database is the industry standard used by airlines to verify real-time entry requirements.

3. **Enroll in or update your status in the Smart Traveler Enrollment Program (STEP).**  
   **Go to step.state.gov and update your current location to the city/country where you have landed.** This ensures the nearest U.S. embassy knows you are in the jurisdiction during a disruption.

4. **Identify yourself as lacking documents if entry is mandated.**  
   **If directed toward immigration but you know you lack a visa, tell the airline supervisor: "I do not have legal permission to enter this country; I need you to coordinate a 'controlled transit' or 'gate pass' with border officials."** 

5. **Be prepared for biometric registration if diverted to Europe.**  
   **Note that as of 2026, many countries (including the Schengen Area) require biometric data (Entry/Exit System - EES) even for diverted passengers clearing the border.** Follow official instructions but keep a record of the time and officer name.

6. **Contact U.S. Consular Affairs if you are detained or denied re-boarding.**  
   **Call the State Department emergency line at +1 202-501-4444 (from abroad) or 1-888-407-4747 (from the U.S./Canada).** Tell them your location, flight number, and that you are "traveling on a diverted flight and lack required entry documents for the current location."

7. **Verify if collecting checked luggage triggers formal entry.**  
   **Ask: "Does reclaiming my bags require me to pass through customs and immigration?"** If yes, and you lack a visa, request that the airline hold the bags in bond or provide an airside alternative.

## What can wait
- Seeking compensation for the delay or filing a formal complaint against the airline.
- Rebooking your entire itinerary; the immediate priority is your current legal status in the diversion country.
- Deciding which hotel to stay in; focus first on whether you are legally allowed to reach the hotel.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel disoriented when landing in an unplanned destination. Immigration officials deal with flight diversions regularly; as long as you remain in the designated areas and follow official instructions, you are not "illegal." Asking for clarification before crossing a border is a responsible and protective action.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the first two hours after an unscheduled international landing. It does not cover long-term visa applications or specific local laws which vary by country.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Entry and transit requirements are subject to immediate change by sovereign governments. Always prioritize instructions from uniformed border and immigration officers.
