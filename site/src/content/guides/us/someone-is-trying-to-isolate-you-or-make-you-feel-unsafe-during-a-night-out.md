# What to do if… someone is trying to isolate you or make you feel unsafe during a night out (USA)

## Short answer
Move into a busy, staffed area and ask venue staff or security to stay with you. If you feel in immediate danger or need emergency police, fire, or medical help, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go somewhere quieter, such as outside, a parking lot, a hallway, another venue, or a car, to talk or end the awkwardness.
- Do not let the person take your phone, hold your drink, or control your transportation.
- Do not accept a ride arranged or controlled by the person making you feel unsafe.
- Do not get pulled into a private argument. Keep moving toward staff and other people.
- Do not wait for proof before asking for help.
- Do not leave alone if you feel suddenly unwell or unusually intoxicated.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a staffed area.** Go to the bar, front desk, security desk, entrance, or another visible area with staff and other people. Keep a clear route away from the person.
2. **Tell staff what you need.** Say: “I feel unsafe. Please stay with me and help keep this person away from me.” You do not need to give a detailed explanation first.
3. **Reconnect with someone you trust.** Call or message a friend or coworker: “I feel unsafe. Please meet me with staff at the bar or entrance now.” Ask staff to stay nearby while you make contact.
4. **Wait somewhere safer.** Ask staff or security whether there is a supervised place where you can remain inside until a trusted person arrives or a safer pickup is ready.
5. **Call 911 for immediate danger.** Call 911 if you feel in immediate danger, including if the person is threatening you, blocking you, following you, or trying to force you to leave. Be ready to give your location and answer the call-taker’s questions. If you cannot safely call yourself, ask staff or someone nearby to call while you stay in the staffed area.
6. **Text 911 only if you cannot safely call.** Text-to-911 is not available everywhere. If texting is not working or you receive a bounce-back message, make a voice call as soon as you safely can or ask someone nearby to call.
7. **Ask for medical help if you feel suddenly unwell.** Tell staff and a trusted person immediately if you feel unusually intoxicated, confused, dizzy, very drowsy, or otherwise suddenly unwell. Do not leave alone. Ask for medical help, and call 911 or ask someone nearby to call if you need emergency medical assistance.

## What can wait
- You do not need to confront the person or explain the situation in detail right now.
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report what happened.
- You do not need to collect proof before moving to safety.
- You can decide later whether to write down the time, location, and a description of what happened.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to be certain about the other person’s intentions before moving toward staff and asking for help. Taking a safer pause in a public, staffed area is a reasonable response.

## Scope note
These are immediate first steps only. Later decisions, including whether to report what happened, arrange non-urgent follow-up care, or contact a specialist support service, can be made once you are safer and have support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel in immediate danger, prioritize moving into a staffed public area and contacting emergency services.
