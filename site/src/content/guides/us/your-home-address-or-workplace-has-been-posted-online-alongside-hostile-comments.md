# What to do if… your home address or workplace has been posted online alongside hostile comments (USA)

## Short answer
Prioritize physical safety first: if you believe anyone may be in immediate danger, call 911. Then document enough to show what was posted, report it for removal, and alert workplace security, HR, your manager, front desk staff, or building management so they can help reduce real-world risk.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t argue with the posters or reply to the thread.
- Don’t post more personal details to defend yourself.
- Don’t delete posts, messages, emails, or account data before saving the minimum record you need.
- Don’t try to identify, confront, visit, or threaten the poster.
- Don’t assume it is only an online problem when your home address or workplace is involved.
- Don’t ask friends to pile onto the post; that can spread it further.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger.**  
   If the post includes threats, tells people to come to your home or workplace, you see suspicious activity nearby, you are being followed, or you believe someone may show up soon, call 911 and move to a safer place if you can do so safely.

2. **Create a safer pause.**  
   If you are at the exposed address or workplace and feel unsafe, go near other people, a staffed front desk, a security desk, a trusted neighbor, or another public or secure place. If leaving would put you at greater risk, stay inside, lock doors if appropriate, and call for help.

3. **Preserve the minimum evidence before reporting.**  
   Save the URL, platform or website name, usernames, date, time, and enough of the post or thread to show your address or workplace and the hostile context. Take screenshots if doing so will not alert the poster; otherwise, photograph the screen from another device. Do not share the screenshots publicly.

4. **Report the post to the platform or website.**  
   Use the closest reporting category, such as harassment, threats, or sharing private personal information. If the content is on a standalone website, look for an abuse, safety, privacy, or contact route and ask for removal of your address, workplace details, and threatening or harassing content.

5. **Ask search engines to reduce visibility.**  
   You may be able to request removal of doxxing content or personal contact information, such as a home address, from Google Search results. This may reduce how easily strangers find it, but it usually does not remove the original webpage or remove the information from every search engine.

6. **Notify your workplace or building support today.**  
   Tell HR, security, your manager, the front desk, reception, or building management that your workplace or address has been posted online alongside hostile comments. Ask them to avoid confirming your schedule or contact details to unknown callers, watch for unusual visitors or calls, keep a copy of any related messages, and follow visitor controls closely for now.

7. **Make a local report when there are threats, stalking, repeat targeting, or interference at home or work.**  
   Use 911 for immediate danger. Otherwise, contact your local police department’s non-emergency route and explain that your address or workplace was posted publicly with hostile comments, threats, or calls for harassment.

8. **Use IC3 as an additional route if the situation is cyber-enabled crime or fraud.**  
   If the posting is tied to hacking, account takeover, extortion, impersonation, fraud, or coordinated cyber-enabled harassment, you can file a report with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center. This is not a substitute for 911 or local police when there is immediate danger.

9. **Reduce easy address lookups only if you have capacity.**  
   Start with major people-search sites that list your address or phone number and use their opt-out routes. This can be slow and incomplete, so do one or two at a time. If you are a California resident, you can also look at the state’s DROP system; data brokers are scheduled to start processing DROP deletion requests on August 1, 2026, so it is not an immediate safety fix.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to move, change jobs, pursue a restraining order, hire a lawyer, pay for a removal service, or wipe every online account. Full privacy cleanup, search alerts, and long-term monitoring can wait until the immediate safety, documentation, reporting, and workplace steps are handled.

## Important reassurance
Feeling alarmed when your address or workplace is exposed is understandable. Taking practical safety steps, asking others not to confirm your whereabouts, and reporting threats or stalking behavior are reasonable responses.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours or day. If the posts continue, spread, or escalate, later decisions may need help from workplace security, local law enforcement, a victim advocate, a privacy professional, or a lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If anyone may be in immediate danger, call 911. If you are unsure whether the situation counts as a crime or stalking, you can still report what happened and ask what local options exist.
