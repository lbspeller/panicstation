# What to do if… you receive an urgent message saying you may be in breach of supervision conditions and must respond today (USA)

## Short answer
Do not click links or provide personal data; verify the message by calling your supervision office directly using a known, official number from your paperwork or a government website.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click any links, open attachments, or download files contained in the message.
- Do not reply with "STOP," "UNSUBSCRIBE," or any other automated prompt if you suspect a scam, as this confirms your number is active.
- Do not pay any "fines," "fees," or "bonds" via gift cards, cryptocurrency, or payment apps (CashApp, Venmo, Zelle) to avoid arrest.
- Do not provide your Social Security number, banking details, or one-time passcodes (OTP) over text or email.
- Do not call the phone number provided in the message if it differs from the one on your official supervision paperwork.
- Do not delete the message until you have documented it.

## What to do now
1. **Document the message immediately.** Take a screenshot of the entire message, ensuring the sender’s phone number, email address, and the timestamp are visible.
2. **Identify your specific supervision authority.** Determine if you are under Federal (U.S. Probation and Pretrial Services), State (Parole Board or Department of Corrections), or County / Municipal supervision.
3. **Locate your official supervision paperwork.** Find the most recent document provided by the court or your officer that contains a verified office address and phone number.
4. **Call the main office line using a verified number.** Do not use a number provided in the suspicious message; instead, use the number from your paperwork or search the official `.gov` website for your local court or probation department.
   - If calling after hours, leave a message stating your name, case number, and that you are responding to a message received at [time].
5. **Request to speak with your assigned officer or the officer on duty.** State clearly that you received a message regarding a potential breach and wish to verify its legitimacy and provide a formal response.
6. **Provide a brief, factual update once legitimacy is confirmed.** If the message is real, confirm that you have received the notice and ask for specific instructions on how to remedy the situation or when to report in person.
7. **Report scam attempts to the appropriate federal agency.** If the message is determined to be a scam, file a report at ReportFraud.ftc.gov or via the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3.gov).
8. **Contact your defense attorney or public defender.** Inform them of the message and your efforts to verify it, especially if the breach appears to be a legitimate technical violation.
9. **Check for official court records if a warrant is mentioned.** Use the official court "Case Lookup" or "Registry" on the relevant `.gov` court website to see if any new filings or warrants have been issued in your name.
10. **Follow official instructions only after verification.** If told to report to a specific office, confirm the address is an official government building before traveling.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a detailed defense or explanation for the alleged breach until you have verified the contact and spoken with counsel.
- You do not need to pay any alleged fines immediately; legitimate court-ordered payments are processed through specific clerk offices or official online portals, never via text-link transfers.
- You do not need to change your phone number or contact information until the situation is resolved.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a notice of a supervision breach is a high-stress event, and scammers rely on this panic to bypass your judgment. A legitimate supervision officer will prioritize establishing clear, verified communication over demanding immediate untraceable payments or sensitive data via text.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying and responding to urgent supervision-related communications. It does not provide legal defense strategies for actual violations or representation in revocation hearings.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Supervision requirements vary significantly by jurisdiction (Federal vs. State); always refer to your specific court orders and consult with a qualified attorney regarding your legal rights and obligations.
