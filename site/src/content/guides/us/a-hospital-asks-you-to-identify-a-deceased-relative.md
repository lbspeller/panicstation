# What to do if… a hospital asks you to identify a deceased relative (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the call through the hospital’s published main number before traveling. Ask who is handling the case and whether any in-person attendance is actually required; identification does not always involve viewing the remains, and local practice varies.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t travel based only on an incoming call, voicemail, or text. Call the hospital’s published main number first.
- Don’t assume that “identify” means you must view your relative’s remains. Ask exactly what staff need from you.
- Don’t agree to a viewing before asking what it involves and whether another accepted method is available.
- Don’t sign a form you do not understand. Ask what it is for, whether it is required now, and whether you can have time to read it.
- Don’t drive yourself if you are too shaken, exhausted, or overwhelmed to travel safely. Ask someone to take you or arrange a ride.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the call.**  
   Find the hospital’s published main number and call it. Ask the switchboard to connect you to the department handling the call. Write down the staff member’s name, department, callback number, and any case or reference number.

2. **Ask who is handling the identification request.**  
   Ask whether the request is coming from the hospital or from a medical examiner or coroner office. Ask where your relative is and which office should answer your questions.

3. **Ask whether you need to attend in person.**  
   Say: “I was told I need to come in to identify my relative. Is an in-person visit required, and what do you need from me?”  
   Depending on the circumstances, the office may confirm identity remotely or may use identifying information, fingerprints, medical or dental records or radiographs, or DNA comparison. Ask what is needed in this case.

4. **Ask what an appointment would involve before traveling.**  
   Ask:
   - “Will any viewing be involved?”
   - “Can you explain what will happen before I arrive?”
   - “Is an appointment required?”
   - “What should I bring?”
   - “May a support person come with me?”
   - “Can another family member or designee assist under your policy?”

5. **Ask for the immediate next step only.**  
   Ask whether the office needs a funeral-home choice now for release coordination or whether that can wait. Ask who you should call back and request any essential instructions in writing if possible.

6. **Make the visit safer if you are asked to attend.**  
   Arrange transport if you are too distressed to travel safely alone. Tell staff if you need a pause, a slower explanation, or information repeated before you decide what to do next.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today about a service, obituary, estate steps, or notifying everyone.
- You do not need to choose a funeral home immediately unless the handling office tells you that the choice is needed for release coordination.
- You do not need to answer every call or message. One trusted person can relay updates when you are ready.
- You do not need to make sense of everything now. Focus on verifying the request and finding out the next required step.

## Important reassurance
Being asked to identify a relative does not automatically mean you must view their remains. You can ask what is required, what alternatives are accepted, what can wait, and who will explain the process.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours. Later decisions about arrangements, documents, benefits, property, or legal questions may need help from the relevant office or an appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Processes vary by state, county, office, and the circumstances of the death. The hospital, medical examiner, or coroner office handling the case can tell you what is required in this situation.
