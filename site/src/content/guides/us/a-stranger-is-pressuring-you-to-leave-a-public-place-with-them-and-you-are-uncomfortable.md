# What to do if… a stranger is pressuring you to leave a public place with them and you are uncomfortable (USA)

## Short answer
Stay in the public area, move immediately to a staffed service point, and say “No” clearly. If the person persists, blocks your path, or makes you feel in danger, call 911 immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave the current public area to go to a "second location" such as a car, hallway, or side street.
- Do not engage in a long conversation or attempt to explain why you are declining.
- Do not prioritize politeness over your physical safety or boundary-setting.
- Do not head toward isolated areas like restrooms, stairwells, or empty parking lots to "hide."
- Do not go directly to your vehicle or home if you believe you are being followed.
- Do not accept any items, drinks, or physical assistance that requires you to move away from the crowd.

## What to do now
1. **Move immediately to a staffed service point or security desk.** Head to a cashier, host stand, or reception area where employees are visible.
   - Put a physical barrier, such as a counter or display rack, between yourself and the stranger.
2. **State clearly to staff that you are being pressured and do not feel safe.** Use direct language: "I need help. This person is pressuring me to leave with them and I am uncomfortable." 
   - Request that they call a manager or security to stand with you.
3. **Engage a specific bystander if staff are not immediately available.** Point to one person and give a direct instruction: "You in the blue shirt—please stand here with me while I call for help."
4. **Call 911 if the person follows you, touches you, or prevents you from moving.** Provide your exact location first (business name and street address). 
   - State: "I am in a public place and a stranger is attempting to force or coerce me to leave with them."
5. **Request a safety escort if you must eventually leave the building.** Do not walk to your car or public transit alone if the person is still in the vicinity. 
   - Ask security or police to walk you to your vehicle or wait with you until a trusted ride arrives.
6. **Use Text-to-911 only if speaking aloud would increase your immediate danger.** Enter "911" in the "To" field and send a message with your location and the nature of the emergency.
   - If your local area does not support Text-to-911, you will receive a "bounce-back" message; if this happens, you must make a voice call.
7. **Record descriptive details only once you are in a secured, staffed location.** Write down or voice-memo the person’s height, clothing, hair color, and any identifying marks or vehicle details.
   - Do not stop moving or look away from your surroundings to record these details while the hazard is still present.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to press charges or file a formal police report right this second.
- You do not need to "figure out" if the person was actually dangerous or just "confused."
- You do not need to respond to any follow-up messages or social media pings if the person obtained your information.
- You do not need to justify your reaction to anyone, including the stranger or bystanders.

## Important reassurance
Feeling a "gut instinct" or physical discomfort is a valid and sufficient reason to seek help. A "freeze" or "fawn" response—where you feel paralyzed or overly polite—is a common biological reaction to pressure. Moving toward other people and involving authority figures is the most effective way to disrupt coercion.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate personal safety in public. If the person continues to stalk or harass you after this encounter, you will need to consult local law enforcement regarding protective orders or long-term safety planning.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. In any situation involving a suspected crime or immediate threat to life, contact 911 or local emergency services.
