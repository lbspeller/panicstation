# What to do if… you learn the rent or mortgage for a person who died is due and you do not know what to do next (USA)

## Short answer
Notify the landlord or mortgage servicer of the death immediately and request their specific "successor in interest" or "estate representative" documentation requirements. Do not use your own personal funds to pay these debts unless you were already a co-signer or co-borrower on the legal agreement.

## Do not do these things
- Don't use your own personal money to cover the payment unless you are legally a co-borrower, co-signer, or joint tenant.
- Don't ignore written notices or phone calls from the servicer or landlord, as silence can trigger automated eviction or foreclosure processes.
- Don't sign any new lease agreements or "assumption" documents for a mortgage until you have verified your legal status and the estate's capacity to pay.
- Don't assume the mortgage lender can "call the loan" immediately; federal law generally prevents the "due-on-sale" clause from being triggered by death if a relative is inheriting.
- Don't clear out the property or change the locks until you are legally authorized to act for the estate.

## What to do now
1. **Document the current status of the account and the property.** **Identify whether the account is for rent or a mortgage, the exact amount due, the next due date, and whether the property is currently occupied.**
   - Locate the most recent statement or lease agreement.
   - Note if there are any roommates, co-tenants, or co-borrowers still living in the home.

2. **Verify your personal legal relationship to the debt.** **Check the original lease or mortgage documents to see if your name is listed as a co-borrower, co-signer, or "joint tenant with right of survivorship."**
   - If your name is not on the contract, you generally do not have personal liability for the debt, and it must be paid by the deceased person's estate assets.
   - If you live in a community property state (e.g., California, Texas), consult the specific rules regarding spousal debt.

3. **Provide formal notice of the death to the landlord or mortgage servicer.** **Contact the company to inform them of the death and ask for their specific list of required documents to recognize you as an authorized party.**
   - For mortgages, specifically ask for the "Confirmed Successor in Interest" process.
   - For rent, ask the landlord for a "written pause" while the estate representative is being formally appointed by the court.

4. **Identify the person authorized to act for the estate.** **Determine if there is a named executor in a will or if a court needs to appoint an administrator or personal representative.**
   - Only the authorized representative can legally pay bills from the deceased person's bank accounts or estate assets.
   - If you are not the representative, provide the representative's contact information to the landlord or bank.

5. **Request a summary of the account status in writing.** **Ask the servicer or landlord for a statement showing the current balance, any late fees, and a breakdown of the monthly charges.**
   - Ensure all communication is logged with the date, time, and the name of the person you spoke with.
   - Follow up every phone call with a brief email summarizing what was discussed.

6. **Check for state-specific protections against early termination fees.** **Research whether your state (such as Colorado under 2026 laws) limits the estate's liability for rent after a tenant dies.**
   - Some jurisdictions prohibit landlords from charging "early termination fees" or rent beyond the month the unit is vacated and notice is given.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you will keep, sell, or vacate the home.
- You do not need to settle the entire probate process before making the initial notification to the bank or landlord.
- You do not need to use your own credit to "refinance" the home today.
- You do not need to pay off the entire balance of the debt immediately.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel pressured when housing bills arrive after a death, but these institutions have established legal processes for handling "successor" interests. A single notification of death and a request for the "successor in interest" application is a standard first step that often stops immediate aggressive collection.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Long-term housing decisions involve complex probate, tax, and property laws that vary significantly by state and require specific legal or financial evaluation.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal or financial advice. Laws regarding "successor in interest" and estate liability vary by state. If you receive a "Notice of Default," "Summons," or "Notice to Quit," contact a qualified attorney or your local legal aid office immediately.
