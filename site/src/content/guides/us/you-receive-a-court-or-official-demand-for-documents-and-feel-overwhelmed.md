# What to do if… you receive a court or official demand for documents and feel overwhelmed (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before sending anything. Note the deadline, preserve anything that might be covered, and verify what the paper is before you hand over documents, especially sensitive or privileged material.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the paper because it feels overwhelming.
- Do not delete, shred, edit, rename, or “clean up” emails, texts, files, photos, chat logs, metadata, or paper records that might be covered.
- Do not send unrelated personal data just to feel safer.
- Do not send communications with your lawyer, legal advice, or work-product material without legal advice.
- Do not call the issuing attorney or agency and explain the whole situation while panicking.
- Do not assume every demand has the same deadline, court rule, or response process.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the basics from the paper.**  
   On one page, copy: what the paper is called, who issued or signed it, the case number or agency reference, exactly what documents are requested, the date range, the deadline, and how production is requested.

2. **Check what kind of demand it is.**  
   Look for words such as subpoena, summons, court order, administrative subpoena, agency demand, notice to produce, or request for records. Treat the exact label as important, because different rules can apply.

3. **Verify the source safely.**  
   Use official court, agency, state bar, or lawyer referral websites and phone numbers you find independently. For a federal court case, check the court or PACER record if you can. Do not rely only on phone numbers, email addresses, payment links, or upload links printed on the paper.

4. **Start a preservation hold now.**  
   Stop deletion, shredding, editing, or auto-delete for anything that might match the request. If the records are on a work system, tell the appropriate internal person, such as a manager, legal contact, compliance contact, or IT contact, that a document demand has arrived and records may need to be preserved.

5. **Make a clean working folder.**  
   Save a scan or photo of the paper. Create one folder for possible responsive documents and one short list called “questions, unclear items, sensitive items, need more time.”

6. **Separate documents before producing anything.**  
   Sort only the documents that appear connected to the demand into three groups: clearly requested, unclear or too broad, and sensitive or possibly privileged. Pause before producing the sensitive or unclear group.

7. **Check the deadline for objections or extra time.**  
   If it is a federal civil subpoena under Rule 45, written objections are generally due before the earlier of the compliance time or 14 days after service. State court subpoenas and agency demands can have different rules, so use the earliest deadline as the point by which you need legal advice.

8. **Ask for help quickly.**  
   A lawyer may be able to check validity, object, narrow the request, protect privileged material, or ask for more time. If you do not have a lawyer, use a state or local bar lawyer referral service, legal aid, or a recognised pro bono service.

9. **If the request is too broad or impossible, ask early and keep proof.**  
   Contact the issuing lawyer, court contact, or agency contact, or have a lawyer do it, to ask for clarification, narrowing, an extension, or staged production. Keep the request and any agreement in writing.

10. **Produce only in a controlled way if you are ready to produce.**  
   Follow any stated format instructions. Do not give up originals unless the paper specifically requires originals or inspection, or a lawyer tells you to. Keep a list of what you produced and use a delivery method that gives proof, such as a portal confirmation, trackable mail, or written receipt.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a full story of what happened today.
- You do not need to organise every document you own.
- You do not need to decide the whole legal strategy right now.
- You do not need to answer informal questions beyond dealing with the document demand.

## Important reassurance
Legal paperwork can feel urgent and confusing, but the first steps are manageable. Preserving records, checking the source, and getting advice before sending sensitive material can prevent the most common early mistakes.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Court subpoenas, agency demands, civil cases, criminal matters, and state rules can differ, and later decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, immigration, therapeutic, or professional advice. If compliance could expose you to criminal risk, immigration risk, professional discipline, loss of a licence, or major penalties, get legal advice before sending documents or making informal explanations.
