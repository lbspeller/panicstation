# What to do if… you return home and find a window screen removed or a window left open (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible break-in until you know otherwise. Do not go inside; move to a safer place and call 911 if someone may still be inside, a crime may be in progress, or you feel unsafe.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t enter to check quickly or shout into the home.
- Don’t touch the window, screen, latch, frame, door handle, or nearby items if you may report this.
- Don’t confront anyone, search the home, or try to secure the window first.
- Don’t move closer to the window or door to get a better look.
- Don’t walk around the home if it means going through tight side yards, back gates, blind corners, or poorly lit areas.
- Don’t post your address or live situation publicly while you are outside the property.

## What to do now
1. Back away from the window or door. Go to a safer place, such as a trusted neighbor’s home, a public place, or inside your locked car if that is safer than standing outside.
2. Call 911 if someone might still be inside, you saw or heard anything suspicious, a crime may be in progress, another person inside may be at risk, or you feel in danger.
3. If you are safe and are confident this is not happening right now, call your local police non-emergency number. In some places, non-emergency requests may go through 311 or another local service line.
4. Tell the dispatcher exactly what you noticed from a distance: the removed screen, the slightly open window, which side of the home it is on, whether any door is open, whether lights or sounds are unusual, and whether anyone who should be inside is unaccounted for.
5. If a roommate, family member, cleaner, landlord, or pet sitter could be inside, call or text them from your safe location. If you cannot confirm they are safe, tell dispatch.
6. From your safe place only, look for anything obvious that may help dispatch, such as another open window, a broken latch, an unfamiliar vehicle, or movement inside. Do not move closer to check.
7. If you have an alarm app, doorbell camera, or camera feed, check it from where you are safe. Do not approach the property to improve the signal, angle, or view.
8. Wait for dispatcher or officer instructions. If police respond, do not enter until they say it is safe or tell you what to do next.
9. When it is safe, write down what you remember: when you arrived, what looked changed, whether the window had been closed before, and anything you noticed nearby.
10. If police take a report, ask for the report or case number before you leave or end the call.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it was definitely a burglary, an accident, weather, an animal, or someone with permission.
- You do not need to repair the screen, close the window, clean up, change locks, or make insurance calls until you are safe and police have given any needed guidance.
- You do not need to inventory every item immediately. Safety, reporting, and basic notes come first.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to want to explain it away and get back inside. Stepping back, calling for help, and waiting before entering is a cautious response to an uncertain situation, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on the next few minutes and the first safe pause. Later decisions about repairs, security, insurance, tenancy, or legal follow-up may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Emergency and non-emergency procedures vary by location; follow dispatcher instructions and local law enforcement guidance.
