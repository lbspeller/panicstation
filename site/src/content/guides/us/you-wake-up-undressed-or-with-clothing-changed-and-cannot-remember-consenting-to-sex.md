# What to do if… you wake up undressed or with clothing changed and cannot remember consenting to sex (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a place where you are physically safe, then contact a sexual assault advocate or go to an emergency department for care. If you are in immediate danger or have urgent medical symptoms, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront the person involved while you feel shocked, alone, or unsafe.
- Don’t delete texts, DMs, photos, call logs, rideshare records, or location history in a panic.
- Don’t assume you must report to police before you can get medical care or support.
- Don’t blame yourself for memory gaps, freezing, drinking, using substances, or not fighting back.
- Don’t delay urgent medical care because you already washed, changed, used the bathroom, or are unsure what happened.

## What to do now
1. **Create immediate safety first.**  
   Move away from the person or place if you can do that safely. Go to a trusted person, a locked room, a staffed public place, or an emergency department. If you are in immediate danger, call **911**.

2. **Contact specialist support without needing to decide what happened.**  
   Contact **RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline** for confidential support and local options:
   - Call **800-656-HOPE (4673)**
   - Text **HOPE** to **64673**
   - Use RAINN’s online chat

3. **Get medical care and ask about a SAFE or SANE option.**  
   You can go to a hospital emergency department or a local sexual assault service and say: “I woke up undressed or changed and I cannot remember consenting to sex. I want medical care and to understand my options.” Ask whether a **Sexual Assault Forensic Exam (SAFE)** or **Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE)** is available. You can usually pause, stop, or decline parts of an exam.

4. **Ask about reporting choices before assuming you must decide now.**  
   In many places, adults can receive medical care and may be able to have evidence collected without deciding immediately whether to report to police. Rules can vary by state and situation, especially if the person is under 18, a vulnerable adult, or someone is in immediate danger, so ask the advocate or medical provider what applies where you are.

5. **Keep one simple option open if it is easy and safe.**  
   If you may want an exam and it does not make you less safe, keep the clothing and underwear you woke up in, ideally in a clean paper bag; if you only have plastic or already changed, bring what you have and still get help.

6. **Tell one trusted person for practical support.**  
   Ask someone safe to come to you, stay on the phone, drive you, wait with you, or help you contact a hotline or hospital. You do not have to tell them more detail than you want to.

7. **Treat possible drugging or serious symptoms as urgent.**  
   If you feel hard to wake, very confused, faint, short of breath, badly injured, in severe pain, or you are vomiting repeatedly, go to an emergency department or call **911**. If you think you were drugged, tell hospital staff as soon as you can.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide right now what label fits, whether it “counts,” or whether you will report.
- You do **not** need to confront anyone today.
- You do **not** need to make a public post, explain yourself to friends, notify work or school, or answer messages from the person involved.
- You do **not** need to collect proof yourself before getting care.

## Important reassurance
Waking up confused, undressed, changed, sore, ashamed, numb, angry, or unsure can be frightening. A memory gap does not mean you did anything wrong. You deserve care and support even if you are not sure what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps to help you get safe, get care, and keep choices open. Later decisions about reporting, follow-up care, school or work processes, or legal options may need specialist support.

## Important note
This guide provides general first-step information, not medical, legal, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe or have urgent medical symptoms, use emergency services. You can seek sexual assault support even if you are uncertain about what happened.
