# What to do if… you wake up undressed or with clothing changed and cannot remember consenting to sex (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a physically safe location immediately, then contact a confidential sexual assault advocate or go to a hospital emergency department. You do not need to decide whether to report to the police to receive medical care or a forensic exam.

## Do not do these things
- Don't confront the person or people involved while you are alone, in shock, or unsure of your immediate safety.
- Don't wash your hands, shower, bathe, brush your teeth, or douche if you are considering a forensic exam.
- Don't change your clothes yet; if you have already changed, do not throw away the clothes you woke up in.
- Don't delete any communication, such as texts, DMs, or call logs, even if they are distressing.
- Don't blame yourself for memory loss, "freezing," or a lack of physical struggle; these are common biological responses to trauma or sedation.
- Don't assume that a lack of physical injury means an assault did not occur.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a place where you feel physically safe and can lock the door.**  
   If you are currently in a space with the person you suspect was involved, leave immediately if it is safe to do so. Go to a friend's home, a staffed public building, or a room where you can control access. If you are in immediate danger, **call 911**.

2. **Contact the National Sexual Assault Hotline for confidential guidance.**  
   **Call 800-656-HOPE (4673)** or use the online chat at **hotline.rainn.org** to connect with a trained advocate. They can help you find a local hospital with a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) and explain your rights regarding anonymous evidence collection.

3. **Go to a hospital Emergency Department that provides forensic exams.**  
   **Ask for a Sexual Assault Forensic Exam (SAFE) or a SANE nurse.** These exams are best performed within 72 hours, though many facilities collect evidence up to 120 hours (5 days) after an incident. You can request that an advocate from a local crisis center stay with you during the process.

4. **Preserve physical evidence in a paper bag only.**  
   **If you must change your clothes, place each item—including underwear—into separate paper bags.** Do not use plastic bags, as they trap moisture and can destroy DNA evidence. If you have no paper bags, bring the items loosely to the hospital.

5. **Seek urgent medical attention for suspected drugging symptoms.**  
   **If you are experiencing seizures, extreme difficulty breathing, or cannot stay awake, tell emergency staff immediately.** Some substances used in drug-facilitated assault leave the system within 12 to 24 hours, so early testing of blood and urine is critical for toxicology.

6. **Create a factual "memory snapshot" on your phone.**  
   **Write down only what you know for certain without trying to fill in the gaps.** Include the last thing you remember drinking or eating, who you were with, the location, and the time you woke up. Save screenshots of any rideshare receipts or messages from the previous night.

7. **Identify one trusted person to assist with logistics.**  
   **Ask a friend or family member to drive you to the hospital or sit in the waiting room with you.** You do not have to explain every detail; you can simply state that you are unwell and need support at the hospital.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to press charges or file a formal police report today.
- You do not need to define exactly what happened or use specific labels for the event.
- You do not need to reply to any messages from the people involved.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of the forensic exam; under the Violence Against Women Act (VAWA), the exam itself must be provided free of charge to the survivor.

## Important reassurance
Waking up with no memory of the previous hours is a deeply disorienting and frightening experience. Gaps in memory or "blackouts" are often the result of physiological responses to trauma or the presence of sedatives, and they do not mean you consented. Your priority right now is your physical safety and medical well-being.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and evidence preservation advice only. Long-term medical follow-up, legal options, and psychological support should be managed in coordination with professional advocates and healthcare providers.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or medical advice. Procedures for forensic exams and mandatory reporting (especially for minors or vulnerable adults) vary by state. Consult a local advocate to understand the specific laws in your jurisdiction.
