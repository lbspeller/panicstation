# What to do if… a social media platform asks for proof of death to memorialise an account and time feels urgent (USA)

## Short answer
Submit the platform's official "Deceased User" webform immediately using a link to an online obituary or a digital scan of a funeral program. Most major platforms (Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn) accept these public records for initial memorialization even if you do not yet have a certified death certificate.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not mail original physical documents** to platform headquarters; use digital uploads only.
- **Do not log into the deceased person's account** using their saved passwords, as this can trigger security locks or violate terms of service during a legal review.
- **Do not wait for a certified death certificate** to start the request if an obituary or memorial card is already available.
- **Do not submit multiple "duplicate" tickets** for the same account, as this often resets your position in the support queue.
- **Do not choose "Account Deletion"** if other family members may want to preserve photos or messages later; memorialization is usually permanent and cannot be reversed to "active" status easily.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific "Deceased User" form for the platform involved.** 
   - Facebook and Instagram use a "Memorialization Request" form.
   - X (Twitter) uses a "Deactivation" form (they do not offer memorialization).
   - LinkedIn offers "Memorialization" (anyone can report) or "Closure" (authorized only).
   - Google uses a "Deceased User" troubleshooter for all services (Gmail, YouTube, Drive).
2. **Gather the minimum required identifiers for the submission.**
   - The exact profile URL (e.g., facebook.com/username).
   - The full name as it appears on the account.
   - The approximate date of death.
3. **Upload the fastest available proof accepted by that specific service.**
   - For Facebook or Instagram memorialization, provide a link to a verified online obituary or a scan of a memorial card.
   - For LinkedIn memorialization, a link to an obituary is typically sufficient.
   - For Google or X deactivation, you will likely need to wait for a digital scan of the death certificate.
4. **Determine if the situation requires an "Impersonation" or "Hacked" report instead.**
   - If the account is currently posting new content or sending messages after the death, file a "Report Impersonation" or "Report Hacked Account" ticket alongside the deceased-user report.
5. **Request a "Short-Form" or "Informational" death certificate if the platform insists on a certificate.**
   - Contact the local or state Vital Records office where the death occurred; informational copies are often processed faster than certified copies and are accepted by many digital support teams.
6. **Capture a screenshot of the submission confirmation page.**
   - Note the case number or reference ID immediately, as confirmation emails from automated systems can be delayed or filtered into spam.

## What can wait
- **Accessing the content of the account** (messages, private photos) can wait; platforms rarely grant login access to family members and often require a court order for data downloads.
- **Closing minor or secondary accounts** (niche forums, shopping sites) can wait until you have the final certified death certificates.
- **Deciding on a long-term "Legacy Contact"** does not need to happen during the initial memorialization request.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for these forms to feel cold or overly technical during a period of grief. Automated systems are designed to prevent identity theft and fraudulent account takeovers. Providing a public obituary is a standard, recognized step and does not mean you are failing to provide "real" proof.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and account locking only. It does not cover legal procedures for obtaining digital assets, private messages, or managing financial accounts linked to social media.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Platform policies and US state vital records procedures vary by jurisdiction and are subject to change. For legal authority over an estate's digital assets, consult a qualified attorney in the relevant state.
