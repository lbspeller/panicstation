# What to do if… you are being pressured to resign instead of being dismissed (USA)

## Short answer
Do not resign or sign separation paperwork on the spot. Slow it down: ask for the proposal and reason in writing, document what happened, and get advice before making anything official.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign “to keep it friendly” if you feel pressured or threatened.
- Do not sign a severance, separation, release, waiver, or “voluntary resignation” form in the meeting.
- Do not rely on verbal promises such as “you can still get unemployment” or “we’ll say you resigned for personal reasons.”
- Do not delete emails, texts, calendar invites, performance records, or notes that show what changed.
- Do not take confidential company, client, patient, customer, trade-secret, or private data to prove your case.
- Do not write statements you do not believe, such as “I resign for personal reasons,” just to end the conversation.
- Do not secretly record a meeting unless you already know your state’s recording rules allow it.

## What to do now
1. **Buy time in plain language, then follow up by email.** Say: “I’m not resigning today. Please email me what the company is proposing and the reason for it.”
2. **Ask them to identify what is happening.** Ask: “Are you asking me to resign, or are you terminating my employment?” If they will not answer, write down who was present, when you asked, and that they declined to clarify.
3. **Ask for a copy of anything they want signed.** Say: “I need a copy to review before I respond.” Do not sign anything that says you resign, release claims, waive rights, admit misconduct, or accept final terms unless you have had time to review it.
4. **If they say you only need to acknowledge receipt, slow that down too.** Ask whether the signature is only to confirm you received the document. If you do not understand the wording, do not sign it in the room.
5. **Document the pressure right away.** On a personal device or account, write the date, time, who was present, and exact phrases used, such as “resign or we fire you,” “sign this now,” or “this is voluntary.”
6. **Preserve your own employment documents lawfully.** Keep copies of documents you are allowed to have, such as your offer letter, handbook acknowledgments, job description, pay or commission plans, schedules, recent evaluations, written warnings, and any performance improvement plan or performance emails.
7. **If discrimination, harassment, or retaliation may be part of this, make the record specific.** Note what protected issue may be involved, what changed after you raised it, and who knew about it. Under laws enforced by the EEOC, forcing someone to resign by making the work environment so intolerable that a reasonable person could not stay may be treated as constructive discharge, but this is fact-specific.
8. **If you are a federal employee or federal job applicant, check the federal-sector EEO route quickly.** Federal-sector discrimination complaints have a different process and generally require contacting an agency EEO Counselor within 45 days.
9. **Protect unemployment options by being accurate, not strategic.** Unemployment insurance is handled by states, and rules vary. If you later apply, describe what happened truthfully and specifically, such as “I was told to resign or be terminated,” and keep written proof of that pressure.
10. **If a severance or release is offered, move into review mode.** Ask for the full agreement to take away and review. If they insist on a same-day signature, document that pressure in writing.
11. **Use the right outside channel if protected rights may be involved.** If the pressure involves discrimination or harassment, consider contacting the EEOC to learn filing steps and deadlines. If it involves employees acting together about pay or working conditions, with or without a union, the NLRB may be relevant where the workplace and activity are covered.
12. **Do not delay if discrimination may be involved.** EEOC filing deadlines are generally 180 calendar days from the discriminatory act, sometimes 300 calendar days depending on location and circumstances. If you are unsure, check sooner rather than later.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, accept severance, or contest the situation.
- You do not need to write a detailed rebuttal before you have the stated reasons and proposal in writing.
- You do not need to negotiate references, job title wording, payout timing, or future job-search explanations right now.
- You do not need to explain your emotions, defend your whole work history, or answer every accusation in the meeting.

## Important reassurance
Pressure to resign is often aimed at making the separation look voluntary and fast. Pausing, asking for written clarity, and documenting events are normal, steady steps that help preserve your options.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to prevent irreversible mistakes and keep options open. Later decisions about unemployment, severance, discrimination, retaliation, or a possible forced resignation may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employment and unemployment rules vary by state, workplace, and situation, and some deadlines can be short.
