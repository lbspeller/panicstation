# What to do if… someone pressures you to go to a private room at a party and you feel the situation escalating (USA)

## Short answer
Move immediately to a public space with other people and involve an ally, host, or staff member to break the isolation. You do not need to be polite or provide an explanation when you feel unsafe.

## Do not do these things
- Don't go to a bedroom, back room, car, or "quiet spot" to avoid a scene or to keep the person from getting upset.
- Don't keep explaining or negotiating your reasons—repeat "No" and physically move toward a crowd.
- Don't accept a drink, vape, or edible from the person if it has been out of your sight.
- Don't let the person separate you from your phone, your bag, or your friends.
- Don't assume you have to handle the situation alone to avoid "drama" or social awkwardness.

## What to do now
1. **State your boundary once and move toward a crowd immediately.**  
   Say: **"No. I am staying out here with everyone else."**  
   Move toward a well-lit, populated area without waiting for the person to agree or respond.
2. **Recruit a specific person as an immediate safety ally.**  
   Find someone you know or a person who looks approachable and say:  
   - **"I feel uncomfortable—can I stay with you for a few minutes?"**  
   - **"Can you walk with me to the bathroom or the bar?"**
3. **Involve the host or venue staff to create a barrier.**  
   At a house party: find the host and state: **"I need help; this person is pressuring me to go into a room and won't leave me alone."**  
   At a bar or club: go to the bartender or security and say: **"I feel unsafe. Please help me get to my ride and keep that person away from me."**
4. **Create a physical exit from the location.**  
   Text or call a trusted person and ask them to stay on the line while you leave.  
   If you do not have a ride, request a ride-share while standing next to staff or in a highly visible public area.
5. **Find a safer pause point to wait for your transport.**  
   Stay near staff, in a well-lit area with high foot traffic, or in a locked restroom stall if you need a moment to breathe and message for help.
6. **Call 911 if you are in immediate danger or being physically blocked from leaving.**  
   If you cannot speak safely, you can text 911 in many US jurisdictions (call if you can, text if you can't).  
   If texting 911 is unavailable, you will receive a "bounce-back" message; in that case, find a way to place a voice call or signal to someone nearby to call for you.
7. **Contact confidential support if you have experienced unwanted contact or pressure.**  
   You can reach **RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline** at **800-656-HOPE (4673)** or text **"HOPE" to 64673**.  
   This service is confidential and does not require you to report the incident to the police.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether you will report the person or tell others the details of what happened.
- You do not need to figure out the "right" way to handle the person's reaction or the potential social fallout.
- You do not need to determine if the situation was "serious enough" to justify your reaction—your safety is the priority.
- You do not need to analyze whether you were "clear enough" in your refusal.

## Important reassurance
The feeling of escalating pressure or "bad vibes" is a valid reason to leave or seek help immediately. Stress responses like freezing or being "too polite" are common and do not mean you consented to the situation. Prioritizing your physical safety over social harmony is the correct action.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance focused on immediate physical stabilization and harm reduction. If you have been touched without consent or feel traumatized, professional advocates can help you navigate your next steps at your own pace.

## Important note
This guide provides general safety information and is not a substitute for legal advice or emergency medical care. If you are in immediate danger or have been injured, contact 911 or visit the nearest emergency room. You have the right to be safe regardless of what you were wearing, drinking, or doing before the situation escalated.
