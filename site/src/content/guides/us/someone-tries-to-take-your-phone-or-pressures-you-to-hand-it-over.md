# What to do if… someone tries to take your phone or pressures you to hand it over (USA)

## Short answer
Keep your phone if you safely can, create distance, and move toward other people or a staffed place. If the person is trying to take it or you feel threatened, call 911 as soon as you safely can.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hand your phone over because the person is pressuring you.
- Do not unlock your phone to show them something or prove anything.
- Do not let the person steer you toward a quieter area, your car, an ATM, or behind a corner.
- Do not struggle over the phone if moving away is the safer option.
- Do not chase the person if they leave with the phone.
- Do not stay to explain after you have said no.

## What to do now
1. **Move toward a safer pause.**  
   If you safely can, put the phone in a pocket or bag, step away, and move toward other people, employees, security staff, or an open business.
2. **Use one short phrase.**  
   Say: “No.”, “Back up.”, or “I can’t help.” You do not need to explain.
3. **Make the situation visible.**  
   In a clear, steady voice, say: “I don’t know you. Please step back.” If needed, ask a specific person: “Please call 911. This person is trying to take my phone.”
4. **Call 911 if you feel threatened or the person is trying to take the phone.**  
   Be ready to give your location and answer the call-taker’s questions. If you cannot safely call, ask an employee, security guard, or nearby person to call.
5. **If the phone is taken, lock it remotely when you are somewhere safer.**  
   For an iPhone, go to `iCloud.com/find` and mark it as lost. For an Android phone, use Find Hub and mark it as lost. If the phone was stolen or appears at a location you do not recognise, do not try to recover it yourself.
6. **Contact your mobile carrier when you can.**  
   Report the phone as stolen or missing and ask about suspending service and protecting your phone number and SIM or eSIM.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to make a non-urgent police report after the immediate danger has passed.
- Writing down the time, place, description, direction of travel, and any vehicle details.
- Reviewing email, banking, payment, and social-media accounts for unfamiliar activity.
- Changing passwords if the phone was unlocked, the person may have seen your passcode, or you notice unfamiliar activity.
- Deciding whether to erase the phone remotely after reading the platform guidance. Remote erasure can be irreversible.
- Replacing the phone, dealing with insurance, and restoring backups.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to work out why the person wants the phone. An insistent or physical request is enough reason to say no, move away, and seek help. Your safety matters more than keeping hold of the device.

## Scope note
These are first steps for reaching a safer pause and limiting immediate device access. Later decisions about account security, reports, replacement, or recovery may need support from your carrier, device provider, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or a crime is in progress, call 911. Do not take risks to recover a phone yourself.
