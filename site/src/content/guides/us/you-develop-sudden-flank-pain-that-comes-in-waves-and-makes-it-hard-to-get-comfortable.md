# What to do if… you develop sudden flank pain that comes in waves and makes it hard to get comfortable (USA)

## Short answer
Seek immediate medical assessment at an Emergency Room (ER) if you have a fever, chills, cannot urinate, or have uncontrolled pain; otherwise, contact a primary care provider or go to Urgent Care for same-day evaluation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore a fever or chills if you have side pain, as this can indicate a life-threatening infection behind a blockage.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if the pain is severe, as sudden spasms or nausea can make driving dangerous.
- Do not take Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs) like ibuprofen or naproxen if you have known kidney disease or are severely dehydrated, unless directed by a doctor.
- Do not force large amounts of water if you are vomiting or in extreme pain, as this can increase pressure in the kidney.
- Do not wait for the pain to "pass" if you are unable to urinate at all.

## What to do now
1. **Assess your symptoms for emergency "red flags" and go to the ER immediately if any are present.**
   - Fever (over 101.5°F) or shaking chills.
   - Total inability to pass urine.
   - Uncontrollable vomiting or inability to keep fluids down.
   - Pain that is so severe you cannot walk or sit still.
   - You are pregnant, have a transplant, or have only one functioning kidney.
2. **Call 911 if you are alone, cannot drive safely, or feel faint.**
3. **Check your temperature and look for blood in your urine.**
   - Use a thermometer to verify if a fever is present.
   - Note if your urine is pink, red, or tea-colored.
4. **Choose an Urgent Care center only if you have no "red flags" and your pain is manageable.**
   - Confirm the facility has on-site imaging (like a CT scan or Ultrasound) before going.
   - If their wait is long or your pain escalates while waiting, move to the nearest ER.
5. **Manage pain with heat or labeled doses of over-the-counter medication if safe for you.**
   - A heating pad on the painful side may provide temporary relief.
   - If you have no kidney or stomach issues, NSAIDs (like ibuprofen) are often first-line for this pain, but must be taken exactly as the label directs.
6. **Prepare for your clinical evaluation by gathering essential information.**
   - List the exact time and dosage of any medications you took in the last 4 hours.
   - Be prepared to provide a urine sample immediately upon arrival.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose yourself with a kidney stone versus an infection right now; a clinician must rule out other causes like an aneurysm or appendicitis.
- You do not need to worry about "passing" the stone or surgical options until imaging confirms the size and location.
- You do not need to change your long-term diet or hydration habits today.

## Important reassurance
Sudden, intense flank pain is extremely distressing, and the "pacing" behavior you may feel—where no position is comfortable—is a classic symptom that medical teams recognize immediately. Seeking professional help is the most effective way to manage the pain safely and prevent complications.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and triage for acute flank pain. It does not replace a clinical diagnosis or cover long-term management of chronic kidney disease or recurrent stones.

## Important note
Flank pain combined with a fever is a potential medical emergency (obstructive pyelonephritis) that can lead to sepsis. This condition requires rapid intervention by medical professionals.
