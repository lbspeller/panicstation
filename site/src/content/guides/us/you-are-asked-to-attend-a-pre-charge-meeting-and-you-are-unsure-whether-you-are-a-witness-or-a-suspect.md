# What to do if… you are asked to attend a pre-charge meeting and you are unsure whether you are a witness or a suspect (USA)

## Short answer
Assume you are a suspect or target until a lawyer confirms otherwise, and do not attend any meetings or answer any substantive questions without legal counsel present.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attend a meeting to "clear things up" or "explain your side" without an attorney.
- Do not answer "preliminary" questions about your location, associates, or timeline.
- Do not guess, speculate, or provide approximate details, as inaccuracies can lead to obstruction or false statement charges.
- Do not sign any documents, waivers, or written summaries provided by investigators.
- Do not consent to a search of your person, phone, vehicle, or home.
- Do not discuss the investigation with friends, family, or other involved parties.

## What to do now
1. **Clearly invoke your right to remain silent and your right to an attorney immediately.** Use the specific phrasing:
   - "I am invoking my right to remain silent."
   - "I want to speak with a lawyer before answering any questions."
   - After stating this, stop talking and do not engage in further conversation, even if the investigator continues to speak.

2. **Ask for the request and the investigator’s contact information in writing.** Tell them that your attorney will contact them to discuss the matter.
   - Note the officer’s name, badge number, agency, and the case/reference number if provided.

3. **Verify if you are being contacted as a witness, a subject, or a target.**
   - A "witness" is generally someone with information but no suspected culpability.
   - A "subject" is someone whose conduct is within the scope of the investigation (common in federal cases).
   - A "target" is someone the prosecutor intends to indict.
   - Note that law enforcement is not always required to be truthful about your status during an investigation.

4. **Determine if the contact is a voluntary request or a legal requirement.**
   - Ask: "Am I free to leave?" or "Is my appearance voluntary?"
   - If it is voluntary, you may decline the interview or reschedule it through your lawyer.
   - If you are served with a subpoena, you must comply with the date and location, but you still retain your right to remain silent and your right to counsel during the proceeding.

5. **Provide basic identification only if required by your specific state law.** In "Stop and Identify" states, you may be required to provide your legal name; check your local state regulations to confirm if this applies to you.

6. **End the contact immediately if you feel pressured or threatened.** If investigators persist after you have invoked your rights, repeat: "I will not speak without my lawyer," and then remain silent.

7. **Create a private log of the encounter for your attorney.** Write down exactly what was said, the time of the call or visit, and any specific documents the investigators mentioned or requested. Keep this log in a secure place and do not share it with anyone except your lawyer.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you will eventually cooperate with the investigation.
- You do not need to provide any documents, passwords, or physical evidence right now.
- You do not need to justify your decision to involve a lawyer to the investigators.
- You can wait to review any subpoenas or letters with a qualified criminal defense attorney before responding.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard and protected legal procedure in the U.S. to refuse questioning without an attorney. Doing so is not an admission of guilt; it is the most effective way to prevent self-incrimination or accidental procedural errors. Feeling anxious or startled by an investigation is a normal response and does not mean you have done anything wrong.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for initial contact with law enforcement. It does not provide legal strategy or long-term defense planning, which must be handled by a licensed attorney familiar with your specific jurisdiction and case.

## Important note
This information is general and not legal advice. Laws regarding police questioning and "Stop and Identify" requirements vary significantly between states and between local and federal jurisdictions (e.g., 18 U.S.C. § 1001 for federal agents). Always consult with a qualified attorney to understand your specific rights and obligations.
