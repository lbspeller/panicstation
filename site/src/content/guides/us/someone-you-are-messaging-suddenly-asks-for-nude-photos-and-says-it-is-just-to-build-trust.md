# What to do if… someone you are messaging suddenly asks for nude photos and says it is “just to build trust” (USA)

## Short answer
Stop messaging the person immediately and do not send any images. Document the request by taking screenshots of the conversation and the person's profile, then block the account and report it to the platform's safety team.

## Do not do these things
- Don't send any images to "prove" you are trustworthy or to de-escalate the situation.
- Don't negotiate, argue, or try to explain why you are uncomfortable.
- Don't delete the conversation history until you have captured screenshots of the request and the user's account details.
- Don't pay any money or send additional images if the person threatens to share what you have already sent.
- Don't provide further personal information, such as your full name, workplace, or home address, to "verify" your identity.

## What to do now
1. **End the interaction immediately.** **Do not reply to the request or any follow-up messages.**
   - If you feel you must say something, send one final message: "I am not sending that. Do not contact me again."
2. **Capture evidence of the request and the account.** **Take screenshots of the specific messages where the request was made.**
   - Ensure you capture the person's username, profile URL, and any identifying details (photos, bio).
   - Save these screenshots to a secure folder or a device that is not the primary messaging device.
3. **Report the user to the platform.** **Use the "Report" or "Help" function within the app or website where the messaging occurred.**
   - Select categories such as "Harassment," "Sexual Content," or "Extortion" if available.
4. **Block the account.** **Prevent further contact by using the platform’s blocking tool.**
5. **Protect your images if they have already been sent.** **Use established industry tools to prevent the spread of intimate images.**
   - If you are 18 or older, use **StopNCII.org** to create "hashes" (digital fingerprints) of the images, which participating platforms use to block them from being uploaded.
   - If you are under 18, use **NCMEC Take It Down** to proactively search for and remove images from participating online services.
6. **File an official report if threats are involved.** **Contact law enforcement if the person attempts to blackmail or extort you.**
   - For adults being extorted, file a report with the **FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3)** at ic3.gov.
   - For situations involving a minor, report the incident to the **NCMEC CyberTipline** at report.cybertip.org or call 1-800-843-5678.
7. **Contact specialist support for tech-enabled abuse.** **Speak with trained advocates who understand online sexual coercion.**
   - Call the **Cyber Civil Rights Initiative (CCRI) Crisis Helpline** at 844-878-2274 for specific guidance on image-based abuse.
   - Contact the **RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline** at 800-656-HOPE for confidential support.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to tell friends or family right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to pursue a full criminal case immediately; capturing the initial evidence is sufficient for now.
- You do not need to figure out the "real identity" of the person if they were using a fake profile.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sudden sense of panic, shame, or confusion when a conversation turns sexual or coercive. Framing a request for nudes as a "trust exercise" is a known manipulation tactic used to make targets feel obligated to comply. You have the right to stop any interaction at any time, regardless of how long you have been talking.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for responding to sudden requests for intimate images and potential online coercion. It does not cover long-term legal strategies or recovery.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. If you believe you are in immediate physical danger, contact 911. Federal laws regarding image-based abuse and the Take It Down Act (2026) provide specific protections that may vary based on your circumstances.
