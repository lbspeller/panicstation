# What to do if… your credit card is suddenly cancelled or your credit limit is reduced and bills are due (USA)

## Short answer
Use only the card issuer’s official app, website, or the number on the back of the card to confirm what happened, then move any bills due in the next 48 hours to another payment method or contact the biller before the due date.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t call numbers from texts or emails about the cancellation; use the number on the back of the card, the issuer’s official app, or the issuer’s official website.
- Don’t keep retrying the same declined payment in a panic; repeated attempts may add holds, fees, or confusion with the biller.
- Don’t ignore due dates while waiting for the issuer to explain; contact each biller early and ask what can be noted or arranged.
- Don’t apply for several new credit cards on the same day in a panic.
- Don’t use payday loans, cash advances, or other high-cost emergency borrowing unless you understand the cost and have checked safer options first.

## What to do now
1. **Check the issuer’s official app or online account.** Look for whether the account is closed, the card is suspended, the credit limit was lowered, or there is a security hold. Note the time, the displayed status, and any unfamiliar charges.

2. **Call the issuer using an official contact route and ask three things.**
   - What is the reason shown on the account: fraud review, security block, missed payment, inactivity, account review, or something else?
   - Can the card or limit be reinstated after verification, or is the change final?
   - What payment routes are available right now for making at least the minimum payment on time, such as bank transfer, debit card, mail, phone payment, or another method the issuer accepts?

3. **Ask about written notice.** Credit card issuers generally can lower limits or close accounts, and they usually must send an adverse action notice for certain unfavorable account changes, such as lowering a credit limit or terminating an account. Ask whether a notice has been sent, where it will arrive, and how to request the specific reasons if the notice does not list them.

4. **Make a short list of bills due in the next 7 days.** Start with housing, utilities, insurance, phone, childcare, loan minimums, and anything that could trigger a late fee or service interruption. For each biller, ask for the safest alternate payment method, a due-date extension, a temporary arrangement, or a late-fee waiver if the failed card payment has already caused a fee.

5. **Move essential autopays off the affected card.** Log in with each biller and replace the card with a bank transfer, debit card, bill pay, or another payment method the biller accepts. For merchant recurring payments, update the payment method directly with the merchant.

6. **If you see unfamiliar activity, treat it as possible fraud or account takeover.** Tell the issuer you suspect fraud, follow their verification process, and change the password on the issuer account from a secure device. Consider a credit freeze or fraud alert, and check your credit reports for unfamiliar accounts or addresses.

7. **Create a simple record.** Write down dates, times, names, confirmation numbers, and what each issuer or biller said. If the issuer does not resolve the issue or you need a formal record, submit a complaint through the CFPB complaint portal.

## What can wait
You don’t need to decide today whether to close other accounts, switch banks, rebuild your credit score, or apply for new credit. You also don’t need to fully diagnose why this happened before preventing late fees, missed minimum payments, and service interruptions.

## Important reassurance
A sudden card cancellation or credit-limit cut can feel personal and urgent, but the first job is smaller: keep essential bills paid or formally arranged, then get the issuer’s explanation in writing. One clear call to the issuer and one short list of near-due bills is enough to start stabilizing the situation.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few days. Later decisions about credit rebuilding, debt help, complaints, budgeting, or legal questions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not financial, legal, or professional advice. If you may lose housing, utilities, food access, or another essential service, contact the company immediately and ask about hardship options. You can also try 211 or use 211.org to look for local referrals for help with bills, rent, utilities, food, and other essentials.
