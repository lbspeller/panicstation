# What to do if… you have lost your phone and cannot receive verification codes for your accounts (USA)
## Short answer
From a trusted device, try to locate and remotely lock the missing phone. Then contact your mobile carrier and ask how to suspend or deactivate the lost SIM and move your number to a replacement SIM or eSIM.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep requesting verification codes repeatedly. Extra requests can add confusion and some services may limit repeated attempts.
- Don’t sign in to sensitive accounts on a shared or public computer unless you have no safer option. If you must use one, use a private browsing session, do not save passwords, sign out fully, and remove the session from your account afterward where the service allows it.
- Don’t follow links in unexpected texts or emails claiming that your phone was found or asking for your password, passcode, or verification code. Open the official service yourself.
- Don’t remove an iPhone from Find My while you are dealing with the loss. Removing it from Find My removes Activation Lock.
- Don’t erase the phone while you are still trying to locate or recover it unless you have decided that the trade-off is appropriate.

## What to do now
1. **Try to locate and lock the phone from a trusted device.**
   - For an iPhone, go to **iCloud.com/find**, select the device, and choose **Mark as Lost**. Apple says you do not need a verification code to sign in there and mark a device as lost.
   - For an Android device, use **Find Hub** and choose **Mark as lost**. If the phone is nearby, use the sound option before assuming it is gone.
   - If the map shows an unfamiliar location and you think the phone was stolen, do not try to retrieve it yourself.

2. **Contact your mobile carrier.**
   - Tell the carrier that the phone is missing and that you cannot receive verification codes.
   - Ask what it can suspend or deactivate on the missing phone or SIM.
   - Ask how to move your existing number to a replacement SIM or eSIM.
   - Set or confirm a carrier account PIN or password. Ask what protections are available against an unauthorized SIM change or number transfer.

3. **Use any account-recovery options already available to you.**
   - Start with your primary email account, password manager, and Apple Account or Google Account.
   - Look for a recovery email, backup codes, or another trusted device where you are already signed in.
   - Follow the provider’s recovery instructions rather than trying many different routes at once.

4. **After you regain access, secure the accounts that matter most.**
   - Prioritize your primary email, password manager, Apple Account or Google Account, banking, and payment apps.
   - Review unfamiliar sign-ins, devices, recovery details, and recent changes.
   - Where a service offers a choice, consider using an authenticator app or a security key instead of relying only on text-message codes.

5. **If the phone was stolen, consider reporting the theft when it is safe to do so.**
   - Local law enforcement may ask for a serial number or IMEI.
   - For an Android phone, the IMEI may be available in Find Hub. For an iPhone, you may be able to find the serial number through your Apple Account information. Your carrier account, purchase records, or packaging may also help.

6. **If you notice identity theft or account takeover, use the federal recovery flow.**
   - Use **IdentityTheft.gov** if someone appears to be opening accounts, taking over accounts, or misusing your identity.

7. **Contact your bank or card provider if you see suspicious financial activity.**
   - Tell them the phone is missing and ask for help securing mobile access and reviewing unfamiliar transactions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to erase the phone. Locking it and contacting your carrier can come first.
- You do not need to reset every password at once. Start with your email, carrier account, password manager, and financial accounts.
- You do not need to choose every future security improvement today.

## Important reassurance
You can handle this in stages. Lock the phone, contact the carrier, and regain access to the accounts that control the rest.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, including device replacement, account recovery problems, or suspected identity theft, may need help from the relevant provider or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, or professional advice. If you see signs of active fraud, such as number-transfer alerts, password resets you did not request, or unfamiliar financial activity, contact your carrier, bank, and relevant account providers promptly.
