# What to do if… you notice swelling in your face or hands that develops over a day and your urine output drops (USA)

## Short answer
Get medical evaluation today; with swelling plus clearly reduced urine, the ER is usually the safest place to be assessed. Call 911 now if you have lip, tongue, throat, or mouth swelling, trouble breathing, fainting, confusion, severe chest pain, or you cannot urinate at all.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait until tomorrow if your urine output has clearly dropped or the swelling is getting worse.
- Do not take extra NSAIDs, such as ibuprofen or naproxen, or start new supplements to treat swelling while you are peeing less, unless a clinician has told you to.
- Do not take someone else’s diuretics or water pills.
- Do not stop prescribed medicines unless a clinician tells you to, or your existing written medical plan says to.
- Do not rapidly drink large amounts of fluid to force urine, especially if you are short of breath or your swelling is worsening.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel faint, very weak, confused, or significantly short of breath.

## What to do now
1. Check for emergency signs. Call 911 now if any of these are happening:
   - swelling of your lips, tongue, throat, or inside your mouth
   - tight throat, trouble swallowing, wheezing, choking feeling, or trouble breathing
   - fainting, severe weakness, severe chest pain, new confusion, or extreme drowsiness
   - no urine at all, especially with severe lower belly pain or fullness
2. If this could be a serious allergic reaction and you have a prescribed epinephrine auto-injector that you have been told to use for this situation, use it, then call 911.
3. If you are pregnant or gave birth within the past year, contact your OB/GYN, postpartum care team, Labor and Delivery triage line, or the number on your discharge paperwork now. If you cannot reach them quickly, or you feel very unwell, go to the ER or call 911.
4. Choose same-day care. Go to the ER if your urine output is much lower than normal, swelling is spreading or worsening, you feel short of breath, you feel generally very unwell, or you are not sure how serious it is. Urgent care is only reasonable if symptoms are mild, breathing is normal, and they can see you promptly and send you to an ER if needed.
5. Write down key details to bring with you:
   - when the swelling started and how fast it changed
   - how much you have peed compared with normal, such as “only once since waking”
   - whether your urine is darker than usual
   - any shortness of breath, cough, fever, vomiting, diarrhea, rash, hives, lip swelling, throat tightness, chest pain, belly pain, or new confusion
   - any recent infection, insect sting, new food, new prescription, over-the-counter medicine, workout supplement, herbal product, ibuprofen, or naproxen
6. Take a photo of the swelling if it is safe and quick to do. This can help clinicians see how it changed.
7. If you have a home blood pressure cuff, take one reading after resting for 5 minutes and write it down. Do not delay going to care to repeat readings.
8. While you are heading to care, sip fluids normally if you can tolerate them and avoid alcohol. If throat, mouth, or breathing symptoms start, switch to 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the cause yourself.
- You do not need to decide whether this is kidney-related, allergic, pregnancy-related, dehydration, or something else before getting help.
- You do not need to start diet changes, detoxes, or over-the-counter water-loss products.
- You do not need to make long-term decisions today. Focus on getting assessed safely.

## Important reassurance
Visible swelling and peeing much less than usual can feel frightening because the change is obvious and fast. Getting checked today is the right first step; clinicians can look for urgent causes and start treatment if needed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to help you get safely assessed. Tests, treatment, and follow-up depend on what clinicians find and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, or a substitute for professional medical care. If you have trouble breathing, throat or mouth swelling, confusion, fainting, severe chest pain, or cannot urinate, call 911.
