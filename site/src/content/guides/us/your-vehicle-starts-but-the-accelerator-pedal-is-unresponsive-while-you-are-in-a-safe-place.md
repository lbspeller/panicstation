# What to do if… your vehicle starts but the accelerator pedal is unresponsive while you are in a safe place (USA)

## Short answer
Do not enter traffic. Secure the vehicle, check for an obvious gear or pedal obstruction issue, and get roadside or mechanic help if it does not immediately resolve.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pull out “to see if it clears up.”
- Do not floor the accelerator repeatedly to test it.
- Do not keep restarting the vehicle if you smell burning, see smoke, see fluid pooling, or the vehicle tells you to stop safely.
- Do not reach into the pedal area while the vehicle is in gear or able to roll.
- Do not ignore warning lights or messages that appear at the same time as the problem.
- Do not let someone stand in front of or behind the vehicle while you test the pedal.

## What to do now
1. **Keep the vehicle still**
   - Keep your foot on the brake.
   - Put an automatic vehicle in **Park**. Put a manual vehicle in **Neutral**.
   - Set the **parking brake**.
   - Turn on **hazard lights** if another driver or pedestrian might not expect you to be stopped.

2. **Check the simple gear and brake issues**
   - For an automatic, confirm the gear selector is fully in **Drive** before any gentle test, and confirm the parking brake is released.
   - For a manual, confirm you are in the intended gear and that the clutch is being released normally.
   - If the engine revs but the vehicle does not move, stop testing and treat it as a possible transmission, clutch, or drivetrain problem.

3. **Check for something blocking the pedal**
   - Only check the pedal area after the vehicle is secured in **Park** or **Neutral** with the parking brake set.
   - Look for a floor mat, bottle, bag, cable, or other loose item near or under the accelerator.
   - Remove or reposition anything that could interfere, then do only one gentle re-test while still in the safe place.

4. **Stop immediately if there are danger signs**
   - If you see smoke, smell burning, see fluid pooling, or get a red warning light or dashboard message telling you not to drive, shut the engine off.
   - Step away from the vehicle if there is smoke, fire risk, or leaking fluid.
   - If the vehicle shows a reduced-power or throttle-control warning, treat it as not safe for normal driving until it has been checked.

5. **Try one calm restart only if there are no danger signs**
   - Turn the engine off.
   - Pause briefly.
   - Restart and gently test the accelerator once while still in the safe place.
   - If the problem remains, stop testing.

6. **Get help instead of driving**
   - Call roadside assistance, your insurer’s roadside line, an auto club, a tow company, or a mechanic.
   - Ask for a tow or mobile inspection rather than attempting a short drive.
   - If you are at immediate risk, blocking traffic dangerously, involved in a crash, or there is a fire or injury risk, call **911**.

7. **After you are safe, consider a safety report**
   - If the accelerator problem is unexpected, repeatable, and feels like a possible safety defect, you can report it to NHTSA.
   - You can also check whether your vehicle has an open recall before arranging repairs.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact fault right now.
- You do not need to decide on a repair shop, repair cost, or replacement vehicle while you are stressed.
- You do not need to prove the problem by attempting a short drive.
- You do not need to file a safety complaint before the vehicle and people around it are safe.

## Important reassurance
Finding this while you are still in a safe place is a good time to stop. A vehicle that starts but will not respond normally to the accelerator is a reason to pause, secure it, and bring in help rather than trying to force an answer.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about diagnosis, towing, repairs, warranties, recalls, insurance, or complaints may need help from roadside assistance, a qualified mechanic, the manufacturer, your insurer, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information and is not mechanical, legal, financial, emergency-services, or professional safety advice. Follow instructions from emergency services, roadside assistance, or a qualified mechanic for your specific vehicle and situation.
