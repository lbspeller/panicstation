# What to do if… you get a blackmail-style message demanding you respond immediately and you worry it could escalate in person (USA)

## Short answer
Do not reply, negotiate, or pay. Move to a physically secure location with other people, preserve all evidence exactly as received, and contact law enforcement immediately if you believe a physical confrontation is imminent.

## Do not do these things
- Don't respond to the message to "buy time" or explain yourself — engagement often leads to increased pressure.
- Don't pay the demand via cryptocurrency, gift cards, or wire transfers — payment rarely stops the threat and often leads to higher demands.
- Don't agree to meet the sender in person or go to an isolated location to "resolve" the issue.
- Don't click any links or download any files attached to the message.
- Don't delete the message thread or the account where it was received until you have preserved the evidence.
- Don't post about the threat on social media in a way that confirms your current location or routine.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize your immediate physical safety by moving to a populated or secure area.** **If you are currently alone and believe someone is on their way to your location, call 911 immediately.**
   - Move to a room with a lock, a neighbor's home, or a staffed public place like a library or police station lobby.
   - Inform someone you trust of your location and the nature of the threat.
2. **Preserve the digital evidence before blocking the sender.** **Take screenshots of the entire message, including the sender's username, email address, phone number, and any timestamps.**
   - Ensure you capture the full text of the demand and any specific personal details they used to threaten you.
   - Do not alter or "clean up" the messages.
3. **Report the threat to the correct authorities based on the level of danger.** **For immediate physical threats, call 911; for cyber-based extortion without immediate physical danger, use official federal reporting portals.**
   - File a report at **ic3.gov** (FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center).
   - Submit a tip at **tips.fbi.gov** or call **1-800-CALL-FBI** (1-800-225-5324).
   - If the threat involves a minor, report it to the **NCMEC CyberTipline** at **report.cybertip.org** or call **1-800-THE-LOST** (1-800-843-5687).
4. **Harden your digital and physical privacy settings.** **Block the sender on all platforms and set your social media profiles to private.**
   - Change your passwords if the threat included an old or current password of yours.
   - Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on your primary email and financial accounts.
   - Use the "Take It Down" tool at **takeitdown.ncmec.org** if the threat involves intimate images of a minor.
5. **Establish a short-term safety plan for your home and workplace.** **Tell your workplace security or a trusted supervisor that you have received a threat and they should not admit unauthorized visitors.**
   - Vary your route to work or school for the next few days.
   - Ensure your home doors and windows are locked and your outdoor lighting is functioning.
   - Set up a "family code word" with your household members to signal an emergency without alerting an intruder.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the sender's true identity right now; let law enforcement handle the investigation.
- You do not need to decide on long-term life changes, such as moving or quitting a job, in the first few hours.
- You do not need to have a "perfect" or complete file of evidence before making the initial police report.
- You do not need to respond to clarify any "misunderstandings" or prove your innocence to the blackmailer.

## Important reassurance
Threats of this nature are specifically designed to trigger a high-stress "fight-or-flight" response to force you into making a mistake. Feeling a sense of panic, isolation, or urgency is a common biological reaction to this type of manipulation. Moving to a safe place and involving authorities are the standard procedures for de-escalating these situations.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute threat. It does not replace legal advice or long-term stalking advocacy. If the threats persist or evolve into stalking, you may need to seek a protective order through your local court system.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional legal or law enforcement advice. Always follow the direct instructions of emergency dispatchers or police officers. If you feel your life is in danger, contact 911 immediately.
