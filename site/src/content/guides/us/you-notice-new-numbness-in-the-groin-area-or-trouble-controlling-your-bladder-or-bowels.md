# What to do if… you notice new numbness in the groin area or trouble controlling your bladder or bowels (USA)

## Short answer
Go to the emergency room now, or call 911 if you cannot get there safely. New numbness in the groin or saddle area with bladder or bowel changes can be a sign of serious spinal nerve compression that needs urgent assessment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait it out to see whether the numbness or bladder or bowel symptoms settle.
- Do not use urgent care or a routine appointment as your only plan for these symptoms.
- Do not drive yourself if your legs feel weak or numb, you feel faint, pain is severe, or you are unsure you can travel safely.
- Do not drink alcohol or take extra sedating medication to push through the symptoms.
- Do not do stretches, heavy lifting, or repeated movement tests to check your back.

## What to do now
1. Call 911 now if you cannot pass urine, cannot walk safely, have rapidly worsening leg weakness or numbness, have severe pain, feel faint, or are alone and unsafe getting to the emergency room.
2. If you can travel safely without an ambulance, go to the nearest emergency room now. Have another adult drive you if possible.
3. At triage or registration, say plainly: “I have new numbness in the groin or saddle area and new trouble controlling my bladder or bowels. I am worried about cauda equina syndrome or spinal nerve compression.”
4. Be specific about bladder or bowel changes: trouble starting to pee, being unable to pee, leaking urine, not feeling when your bladder is full, bowel leakage, or not feeling when wiping.
5. Write these details in your phone if you can do it without delaying care:
   - When the numbness and bladder or bowel symptoms started.
   - Whether the symptoms are getting worse, improving, or coming and going.
   - Any leg weakness, leg numbness, back pain, or pain going down one or both legs.
   - Any recent fall, back injury, spinal injection, epidural, spinal procedure, infection symptoms, or known disc problem.
6. If easy to grab, bring ID, insurance information, a medication list or medication photos, allergy information, a phone charger, and a contact number for someone who can be reached. Do not delay leaving to find these.
7. If you are alone, call or text someone now with where you are going and ask them to stay reachable.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause before you are seen.
- You do not need to keep checking the numb area or force yourself to pee or poop to prove the symptom.
- You do not need to decide about imaging, surgery, or treatment options right now; the first step is urgent assessment.
- You do not need to feel embarrassed about the words bladder, bowel, groin, genitals, or wiping. These are important medical details.

## Important reassurance
These symptoms can feel awkward to say out loud, but emergency room teams are used to hearing them and take them seriously. Going now is a protective step, even if the final cause turns out to be less serious than spinal nerve compression.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to help you get urgent assessment and describe the symptoms clearly. Later decisions may need emergency clinicians, imaging, and specialist medical advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not a diagnosis or personal medical advice. If these symptoms are happening now, seek urgent in-person medical assessment immediately.
