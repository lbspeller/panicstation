# What to do if… your website suddenly shows a certificate or security warning and you did not change anything (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the message as a SIM-swap attempt; **do not scan the QR code** or click any links, and **call your wireless carrier immediately** from a trusted phone to lock your account and block any pending transfers.

## Do not do these things
- Don't scan the QR code or follow prompts to "Add eSIM" on your device.
- Don't click "cancel," "verify," or any links provided in the notification.
- Don't reply to the message or provide account PINs or verification codes to anyone who calls or texts you.
- Don't use the phone number provided in the message to contact the carrier.
- Don't assume the message is a glitch; delay can allow an attacker to intercept your calls and texts.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the message and stop the activation.**  
   **Close any "Add eSIM" pop-ups or activation screens that appeared automatically.** Do not interact with the message or QR code further.

2. **Call your wireless carrier's official fraud or customer service line.**  
   **Use a different phone if yours has already lost service, or dial 611 if your service is still active.** Tell the representative: "I received an unauthorized eSIM activation request. I need to block this change and secure my account."
   - Ask to enable a **Number Lock**, **Port-Out Protection**, or **Wireless Account Lock** (names vary by carrier).
   - Ask for a **SIM Protection** lock to prevent internal SIM/eSIM swaps.
   - Confirm if any **eSIM has already been issued** or if a **port-out request is pending**.
   - Request that a **high-security note** be added to your account requiring in-person ID verification for any future SIM changes.

3. **Verify your current service status.**  
   **Check the signal indicator on your phone.** If the device displays "No Service," "SOS Only," or you are unable to receive text messages, notify the carrier immediately that a SIM swap has likely already occurred and your line must be reclaimed.

4. **Secure your primary email and financial accounts.**  
   **Log in to your primary email account from a trusted computer and change the password.** This is the most critical step after securing your phone line, as attackers use hijacked numbers to reset email passwords.
   - Change passwords for **banking apps**, **investment accounts**, and **cryptocurrency exchanges**.
   - Update your **Apple ID** or **Google Account** security settings.

5. **Update your two-factor authentication (2FA) methods.**  
   **Switch from SMS-based codes to an authenticator app (like Google Authenticator or Authy) or a physical security key.** If an attacker has your number, they can bypass SMS-based security; authenticator apps do not rely on your phone number.

6. **Monitor your accounts for unauthorized activity.**  
   **Review your bank and credit card statements for any "test" transactions or login alerts you did not trigger.** If you find suspicious activity, call the financial institution's fraud department to place a hold on the account.

7. **Document the incident for your records.**  
   **Save a screenshot of the unauthorized eSIM message and note the exact time it was received.** Keep a log of the names of carrier representatives you speak with and any case or reference numbers provided.

## What can wait
- You don't need to change your actual phone number immediately.
- You don't need to factory reset your phone unless you suspect a separate malware infection.
- You don't need to notify all your contacts unless you have evidence that the attacker is messaging people using your identity.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a message is often a "warning shot" that gives you a window to act. If you have not scanned the code and your phone still has signal, you are likely in a position to block the attack before any data or funds are compromised. Carriers are required by the FCC to provide these locks and investigate these reports.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an attempted or in-progress SIM swap. It does not cover long-term identity theft recovery or legal proceedings against service providers.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Security features and their specific names (e.g., "Number Lock" at Verizon or "Account Takeover Protection" at T-Mobile) are subject to change by individual carriers. Always use verified contact information from your official billing statement or the carrier's verified website.
