# What to do if… you are contacted by media or strangers about a family member’s death and want privacy (USA)

## Short answer
Avoid responding while you feel overwhelmed. Ask one trusted person to handle contact for now, use one short boundary message if helpful, and save any threatening messages or posts that share private identifying information.

## Do not do these things
- Do not answer questions impulsively to try to make the contact stop. Details shared in a reply may be copied or reposted.
- Do not send photos, documents, your address, or location details to someone who contacted you unexpectedly.
- Do not debate strangers in comments or direct messages.
- Do not assume someone is genuine because they name a news outlet.
- Do not post funeral arrangements or live location details while you are trying to reduce attention.
- Do not click unexpected links or open attachments from people asking for a statement or verification.

## What to do now
1. **Choose one trusted contact person for now.**  
   Ask family members to forward media requests, messages, and voicemails to that person rather than replying individually.

2. **Use one boundary message if it would help.**  
   You can say: “Our family is grieving and does not want publicity. Please do not contact us again.”  
   You do not need to explain further. Save anything important first, then mute, filter, or block contact where helpful.

3. **Verify any media contact independently before replying.**  
   If you are considering a response, find the outlet’s official website yourself and use its published contact details. Do not rely on links, phone numbers, or email addresses sent in the original message.

4. **Reduce publicly visible personal information.**  
   Review social-media privacy settings. Remove or limit posts that show your home address, workplace, school, regular locations, travel plans, or funeral arrangements. Ask friends and relatives not to tag you or share identifying details without checking first.

5. **Save records when contact may matter for safety or reporting.**  
   Keep screenshots, profile links, voicemails, dates, and times for threats, suspected impersonation, repeated unwanted messages, or posts sharing private identifying information. Avoid engaging while collecting them.

6. **Reduce unwanted calls and texts.**  
   Silence unknown callers if that is practical. Use your phone’s filtering tools or ask your phone carrier about call-blocking options. For repeated robocalls, unwanted texts, or spoofing patterns, you can use the FTC and FCC resources below.

7. **Escalate based on the immediate risk.**  
   - If someone is at your home and will not leave, and you feel unsafe or need immediate police help, call **911**. Call **911** if you need immediate fire or ambulance help.
   - If contact includes threats, stalking behavior, or a safety concern without immediate danger, you can contact local law enforcement about your options using a non-emergency route.
   - If you suspect internet-enabled fraud, scam impersonation, phishing, or another online crime, you can consider reporting it to IC3.
   - Use the platform’s reporting tools for accounts or posts that share private information, impersonate someone, or send abusive messages.

8. **Ask about victim assistance if the death is connected to a criminal case.**  
   Ask the investigating agency or prosecutor’s office whether a victim-assistance contact is available. The Office for Victims of Crime state directory below can help you find local support routes.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide today whether to speak publicly, correct rumors, or issue a detailed statement.
- You do **not** need to chase every mention. Prioritize anything that reveals private identifying information or encourages targeting.
- You do **not** need to negotiate with a reporter or stranger online.
- Longer-term decisions about takedown requests or legal advice can wait until the immediate contact slows.

## Important reassurance
Feeling overwhelmed, protective, or angry after a death is understandable. Wanting privacy is reasonable. You can say no, pause contact, and ask someone you trust to handle messages without giving an explanation.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce contact, avoid irreversible oversharing, and create a safer pause. Later decisions may need help from victim services, law enforcement, a platform’s support team, or a lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Laws and available options vary by state and by what is happening. If you need immediate emergency help, call **911**.
