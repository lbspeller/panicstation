# What to do if… you receive repeated unwanted messages that suddenly become threatening (USA)

## Short answer
If you believe there is an immediate risk of physical harm, call **911** now. Otherwise, stop all interaction, preserve copies of the messages as evidence, and report the threats to local law enforcement.

## Do not do these things
- Do not respond to the sender to negotiate, explain, or attempt to de-escalate.
- Do not delete the original messages, call logs, or metadata until you have documented them.
- Do not agree to meet the sender in person to "settle things" or "talk it out."
- Do not click links or download attachments sent by the harasser.
- Do not post the threats on social media to "expose" the sender, as this can escalate the risk.
- Do not block the sender until you have confirmed you have sufficient records of the pattern of behavior.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize your immediate physical safety by moving to a secure location.** 
   If you are in a public space, stay in well-lit areas with other people. If you are at home, ensure all points of entry are locked. Inform a trusted person of your location and the nature of the threats.

2. **Contact emergency services if the threats indicate immediate action.** 
   Call **911** if the sender mentions your current location, describes what you are wearing, or states they are coming to find you right now. For threats that are not immediate, call your local police department’s non-emergency line to file an official report.
   - Request an incident or case number for your records.
   - Ask for the name and badge number of the officer taking the report.

3. **Capture and preserve all evidence of the communication immediately.** 
   Do not rely on the app to save the data, as messages can be unsent or accounts deleted.
   - Take full-screen screenshots that show the sender's handle, phone number, or email address.
   - Ensure the date and time are visible in each screenshot.
   - Copy any relevant metadata, such as email headers or specific URL links to profiles.
   - Forward threatening SMS messages to **7726** (SPAM) if your carrier supports this for reporting.

4. **Report the violation to the platform or service provider where it occurred.** 
   Use the internal reporting tools on apps like Instagram, X, or WhatsApp to flag the account for "violent threats" or "harassment." This creates a secondary digital record with the service provider.

5. **Enhance your digital privacy settings to limit the sender's access.** 
   Set all social media profiles to private and disable location-sharing features (e.g., Snap Map, "Find My" shared locations). Remove your phone number and email address from public-facing directories or bios.

6. **Reach out to specialized support services if the sender is known to you.** 
   If the individual is a current or former intimate partner, contact the **National Domestic Violence Hotline** at 1-800-799-SAFE (7233) or text "START" to 88788. For stalking or harassment not involving a partner, contact the **VictimConnect Resource Center** at 1-855-484-2846.

7. **Submit a federal report if the threats involve cyber-extortion or interstate crime.** 
   File a report with the **FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3)** at ic3.gov if the harassment involves hacking, financial extortion, or threats sent across state lines. This is a supplement to, not a replacement for, local police action.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to change your phone number or delete your accounts permanently right now.
- You do not need to pursue a civil protection order or restraining order in the next few minutes; focus on physical safety first.
- You do not need to determine the identity of the sender if the messages are anonymous; law enforcement can handle the investigation.

## Important reassurance
Receiving sudden threats can trigger a "freeze" response or intense anxiety; these are normal physiological reactions to a safety breach. Taking the immediate step of documenting the evidence and involving authorities is a logical way to regain control over the situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions only. It does not cover long-term legal strategies, civil litigation, or ongoing investigative procedures.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Procedures and response times for law enforcement may vary by jurisdiction. If you are in immediate danger, contact **911**.
