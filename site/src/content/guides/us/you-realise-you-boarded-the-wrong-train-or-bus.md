# What to do if… you realise you boarded the wrong train or bus (USA)

## Short answer
Stay on board until the next safe stop or until staff tell you what to do. Tell the conductor, driver, station agent, or terminal staff at the next safe moment so you can correct the trip without creating a safety or ticket problem.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t force doors, leave between stops, enter tracks, walk on a road shoulder, or open luggage bays.
- Don’t pull emergency equipment just because you are on the wrong service.
- Don’t distract a bus driver while the bus is moving unless there is an immediate safety emergency.
- Don’t hide the mistake from staff if your ticket does not match the service you are on.
- Don’t delete tickets, booking emails, app screens, payment receipts, or delay messages.
- Don’t argue about blame at the doorway or fare check; keep it short and ask for the next safe correction step.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the mistake quickly:** check the onboard screen, announcement, route number, train or bus number, your e-ticket, and your phone’s map location.
2. **Stay physically safe first:**
   - **Train:** stay in the passenger area and ask the conductor which upcoming stop is safest and most useful for correcting the trip.
   - **Bus:** stay seated until a normal stop, terminal, rest stop, or other safe stopping point unless the driver tells you otherwise.
3. **Tell staff using one clear sentence:**
   - **Rail:** “I boarded the wrong train by mistake at [station]. My ticket is for [destination or train]. What stop should I use to fix this?”
   - **Bus:** when the bus is stopped or the driver says it is safe, say: “I think I am on the wrong bus. What is the safest stop or terminal to get off and correct it?”
4. **Keep your ticket details ready:** have your reservation number, last name, origin, destination, e-ticket, and payment confirmation visible on your phone or printed ticket.
5. **Use official change tools only when it is safe to do so:**
   - **Amtrak:** check My Trip or the Amtrak app if a change option is available, or ask the conductor or a station agent what to do next.
   - **Greyhound:** check Manage My Booking if you can access it, but do not rely on the driver to change the ticket for you; use official tools or terminal/customer care after reaching a safe stop.
6. **Make a one-minute note:** write down the time, where you boarded, the train or bus number if visible, the next stop, and any staff instruction. This may help if you need to explain a fare issue, missed connection, or charge later.
7. **Re-plan only the next leg:** once you are safely off or have staff instructions, check the next correct service, return service, or staffed help point before buying anything new.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, request a refund, or argue about responsibility.
- You do not need to solve the whole itinerary at once; focus on the next safe stop and one official correction step.
- You do not need a long explanation. “I boarded the wrong service by mistake and I am trying to correct it” is enough.
- You do not need to buy a replacement ticket while moving, panicking, or unsure which stop you will use next.

## Important reassurance
Boarding the wrong train or bus can happen in busy stations, terminals, gate changes, and unfamiliar routes. Staff deal with wrong-service mistakes regularly, and explaining it early usually gives you more options than trying to fix it alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, aimed at immediate safety, keeping your ticket proof intact, and getting you to the next practical correction point. Fare adjustments, refunds, missed connections, complaints, or accessibility needs may need carrier or station staff help once you are no longer rushing.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, transport, or professional advice. Policies vary by carrier, local transit agency, ticket type, route, and safety conditions. If you feel unsafe or threatened during a trip, move toward staff or other passengers if you can, alert the driver or conductor when safe, or call 911 in an emergency.
