# What to do if… a staffing agency changes your assignment end date with little explanation (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the staffing agency to confirm your current last day, any remaining shifts, and your timecard instructions in writing. Save records you are allowed to keep, then ask about reassignment and check your state’s unemployment instructions if your work or earnings will drop.

## Do not do these things
- Do not walk off the assignment or stop reporting without clarifying whether you are expected to work any remaining shifts.
- Do not rely on a phone call alone when you can follow up with a brief email or text summary.
- Do not resign or sign a release, resignation, or document describing a voluntary quit while the situation is unclear. Ask for time to review it.
- Do not keep client property or access client systems after your authorization ends. Follow the return instructions for badges, equipment, and other property.
- Do not copy confidential client material. Save only personal employment records and messages you are allowed to keep.
- Do not post accusations publicly while you are still trying to clarify your work status, pay, and paperwork.

## What to do now
1. **Ask the staffing agency for written confirmation.** Ask for:
   - your current last day and time
   - whether you should work any remaining shifts
   - whether only the client assignment is ending or whether your status with the staffing agency has also changed
   - a brief reason, if they can provide one
   - the timecard process and the expected pay process

2. **If you are told not to return to the client site, ask for that in writing.** A simple message is enough: “Please confirm that I should not report to the client site after [date and time].” Follow any instructions for returning badges, equipment, or other property.

3. **Save the records you are allowed to keep.** Keep your assignment confirmation, schedule messages, time punches, submitted timecards, agency contact details, and messages about the date change. Write down who contacted you, when they contacted you, and what they said.

4. **Submit your hours and check the pay process.** Confirm that your final timecard for the assignment was received. Federal law does not require an immediate final paycheck, and state rules may differ. If the regular payday for the last period you worked passes without payment, contact the staffing agency’s payroll team in writing and consider contacting the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division or your state labor office.

5. **Ask about reassignment as soon as you reasonably can.** Say that you remain available for work and ask whether the agency has another assignment. Ask whether the agency expects any check-ins, app updates, or other steps while you are available.

6. **Check your state’s unemployment instructions promptly if your work or earnings will drop.** Each state manages its own unemployment program and decides eligibility. Follow your state agency’s instructions about applying and any ongoing claim requirements.

7. **If you think discrimination or related retaliation may be involved, preserve the record.** Save messages and write down dates, exact words, and witnesses. You do not need to debate the issue in the moment. You can check official Equal Employment Opportunity Commission information and seek state-specific advice when you are ready.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign from the staffing agency, contact the client’s leadership, or threaten legal action.
- You do not need to work out the full reason for the date change before securing your written instructions, records, timecard, and pay details.
- Broader job-search planning and negotiations can wait until the immediate details are clearer.

## Important reassurance
Assignments can change for reasons unrelated to your performance. Asking for written instructions, protecting your pay records, and checking the reassignment process are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours and days. Later decisions about missing pay, unemployment, discrimination, or contract terms may need state-specific or specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Pay rules and unemployment requirements can vary by state. Contract terms and agency policies can vary by assignment and circumstances.
