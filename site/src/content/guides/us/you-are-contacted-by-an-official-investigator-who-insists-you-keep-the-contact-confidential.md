# What to do if… you are contacted by an official investigator who insists you keep the contact confidential (USA)

## Short answer
Stop the interaction immediately to verify the investigator's identity using an independently sourced, official government phone number. Do not share any sensitive information or agree to any requests until you have confirmed their credentials through a verified agency switchboard.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide personal identifiers (Social Security number, bank details, or passwords) over the phone or email.
- Do not send any form of payment, including gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfers, or physical cash/gold.
- Do not follow instructions to download software or click links to "view a case file."
- Do not accept a caller's claim that you are legally prohibited from speaking to an attorney.
- Do not use a phone number provided by the investigator or shown in your caller ID to "verify" them.
- Do not meet the person at any location other than a verified, official government office.

## What to do now
1. **Request specific identification details from the caller.** Ask for their full name, their specific agency or department, their badge or credential ID, and a case or reference number.
2. **End the contact to "Take a Beat" and verify.** Tell the caller: "I am going to hang up and call back through your agency's official public number to confirm this contact."
3. **Locate the agency’s official contact information independently.**
   - Search for the agency name (e.g., "FBI [City] Field Office" or "DCSA Personnel Vetting") on a `.gov` website.
   - Call the main switchboard number found on that `.gov` site.
4. **Ask the agency switchboard to confirm the person’s employment.**
   - Request to be transferred to the specific individual or their supervisor.
   - Ask the official representative to confirm that the case or reference number you were given is valid.
5. **Invoke your right to legal counsel if you are a suspect or feel pressured.**
   - State clearly: "I will not answer questions or participate in an interview without my attorney present."
   - If you are currently incarcerated, tell facility staff you require a verified legal visit or call to confirm the investigator's identity through official channels before any movement.
6. **Report suspected impersonation immediately.** 
   - If the caller threatened arrest or demanded money, report the interaction to the **Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3)** or the **DOJ Division for National Fraud Enforcement**.
   - If you provided financial information, contact your bank to freeze your accounts.
7. **Document the specific sensory cues and contact details.**
   - Write down the exact time of the call, the caller's phone number as it appeared, and any specific names or titles used.
   - Save copies of any emails or text messages received without clicking internal links.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to cooperate with the investigation right now.
- You do not need to provide a statement or an explanation of your actions today.
- You do not need to sign any non-disclosure agreements or confidentiality forms presented during an unsolicited contact.
- You do not need to worry about "missing a deadline" or "avoiding arrest" while you are performing a legitimate identity verification.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard safety protocol to verify the identity of any official who contacts you unexpectedly. Real federal or local investigators will understand and allow you to verify their identity through their agency's main office. Feeling startled or anxious is a common reaction to these calls, and taking a pause is the most effective way to protect yourself.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying legitimate official contact and preventing impersonation fraud. It does not provide legal strategy for active criminal investigations or advice on how to handle a confirmed subpoena or warrant.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. If you are involved in a federal or state investigation, contact a qualified attorney to discuss your rights and obligations.
