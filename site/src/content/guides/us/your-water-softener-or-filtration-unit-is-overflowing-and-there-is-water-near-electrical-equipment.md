# What to do if… your water softener or filtration unit is overflowing and there is water near electrical equipment (USA)

## Short answer
Keep people and pets away from the water. Shut off electricity only if you can reach the main breaker from a dry, safe spot; if you would have to stand in water or the panel may be wet, do not touch it and contact your electric utility.

## Do not do these things
- Do not step into standing water to reach outlets, power strips, cords, appliances, or the electrical panel.
- Do not touch breakers, switches, plugs, cords, or appliances with wet hands or while on a wet floor.
- Do not turn on lights, pumps, fans, or appliances to check whether they still work while water is present.
- Do not reset tripped breakers or GFCIs until the area is dry and a qualified person has checked anything that may have been wet.
- Do not start cleanup with a wet/dry vacuum, pump, fan, or powered tool near the wet area until the affected area is electrically safe.
- Do not ignore sparking, smoke, buzzing, crackling, or a burning smell.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safe pause.** Keep everyone, including pets, out of the wet area. If there is fire, smoke, sparking, shock, injury, or anyone is unresponsive, call **911**.
2. **Decide whether you can safely shut off power.**
   - If you can reach the **main breaker panel** from a dry location, with no water underfoot and no dampness on or around the panel, switch the **main breaker OFF**.
   - If you would have to stand in water, touch a wet surface, or reach toward a panel, outlet, cord, or switch that may be wet, **do not touch it**.
3. **If it is not safe to shut off power yourself, contact the utility.** Call your electric utility and say there is water near electrical equipment and you need power disconnected safely.
   - If you have **solar panels, a battery system, or a generator**, assume some parts may still be energized. Do not open panels, covers, disconnects, or equipment boxes; ask the utility and a licensed electrician what to do next.
4. **Stop the water only from a dry, safe position.**
   - Close the softener or filter **inlet and outlet shutoff valves** if you can reach them without stepping into water.
   - If you cannot reach those valves safely, shut off the home’s **main water valve** if it is in a dry, safe place.
   - If the unit has a **bypass valve**, set it to bypass only if the valve is dry and safe to touch.
5. **Once the affected area is electrically safe, limit the spread.**
   - Use towels or barriers to slow water from reaching outlets, cords, the breaker panel area, the furnace or air handler, sump pump controls, or other equipment.
   - Move dry portable electronics away from the area only if you can do it from a dry position.
   - Do not handle wet cords, wet power strips, wet appliances, or anything visibly damaged.
6. **Document and get the right help.**
   - Take a few photos from a safe position showing where the water traveled, any affected outlets or equipment, and the softener or filtration unit.
   - Call a plumber, installer, or service company to stop the overflow and repair the unit.
   - If water may have reached outlets, wiring, the breaker panel, appliances, power strips, or connected equipment, arrange for a licensed electrician to inspect before power is restored or affected items are used.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact cause, such as a stuck valve, clogged drain line, failed float, cracked hose, or loose fitting, right now.
- You do not need to decide on repair or replacement, warranty claims, upgrades, or insurance wording today.
- You do not need to fully clean up immediately. First keep people away, make the area electrically safe, and stop the water if you can do that safely.

## Important reassurance
This is a situation where slowing down is safer than rushing. If you are unsure whether something is safe to touch, staying back and getting the utility, a plumber, or a licensed electrician involved is a reasonable choice.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate shock, fire, and water-spread risk. Later decisions about repairs, replacement, insurance, wiring, appliances, or restoration may need help from a plumber, licensed electrician, utility, insurer, or other qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional electrical, plumbing, legal, insurance, or emergency advice. Call 911 for fire, smoke, sparking, shock, injury, or an unresponsive person; contact your electric utility if power cannot be shut off safely from a dry position.
