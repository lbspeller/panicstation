# What to do if… your car locks or windows stop working and you cannot secure the vehicle (USA)

## Short answer
Move the vehicle to the safest nearby place you can reach, take valuables and access items with you, and arrange roadside, locksmith, repair, or towing help so the car is not left exposed.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t leave the vehicle unattended in an isolated or poorly lit place if it cannot be locked or closed.
- Don’t leave keys, a spare fob, wallet, ID, mail, electronics, bags, or visible valuables in the car.
- Don’t force a window, lock, trunk, or door latch with improvised tools if it could break glass, jam the mechanism, or leave the vehicle harder to secure.
- Don’t confront anyone you think is tampering with the vehicle; step away and get help.
- Don’t use 911 unless there is immediate danger, a crime in progress, or you need emergency police, fire, or medical help.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer parking spot if you can do so safely.** Prefer a busy, well-lit area near an open business, staffed garage, repair shop, hotel entrance, or monitored lot rather than an isolated street.
2. **Remove theft targets now.** Take every key, fob, spare key, and garage remote you can reasonably remove. Remove bags, electronics, loose cash, tools, mail, and spare papers that show your home address. Keep any vehicle documents your state requires available.
3. **Make the problem visible to helpers, not thieves.** Check which part has failed: door lock, trunk lock, window, key fob, door latch, or power locks. Do not keep testing it over and over if it is getting worse.
4. **Use a simple deterrent if you already have one.** Put on a steering wheel lock, brake lock, alarm, or other visible device. If a window is stuck open or a door will not latch, staying with the vehicle may be safer than leaving it exposed only if the place feels safe.
5. **Choose the right help route.**
   - Call 911 if you are threatened, someone is breaking in now, the vehicle is being stolen now, or you are in immediate danger.
   - If there is suspicious activity but no immediate danger, contact the local police non-emergency number for the city or county where the car is parked.
   - If there is no crime or danger, call roadside assistance if you have it, your insurer’s roadside line if covered, an auto locksmith, or a repair shop.
6. **Ask for the car to be secured or moved.** Say clearly: “The car cannot be locked” or “The window is stuck open.” Ask whether they can close the window, secure the latch, unlock or reprogram the fob, or tow the vehicle.
7. **Tow it if you cannot make it secure.** If the car must be left for hours or overnight, ask for a tow to a locked garage, repair shop, staffed lot, home garage, or other safer storage location.
8. **Document only after the immediate risk is handled.** Take photos of the stuck window, failed lock, open trunk, damaged latch, parking location, and any visible damage for repair, police, or insurance records.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether this is a fuse, battery, fob, actuator, regulator, or wiring issue right now.
- You do not need to decide on a full repair plan at the roadside.
- You do not need to start an insurance claim immediately unless there has been theft, damage, or your insurer tells you a prompt report is needed.
- You do not need to argue over repair prices before the vehicle is secured or moved.

## Important reassurance
A car that will not lock can feel urgent and exposed, but the first goal is small and practical: make the vehicle a harder target, keep yourself safe, and get it to a place where it is less vulnerable.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Repairs, police reports, insurance claims, warranty questions, and longer-term theft prevention may need help from a repair shop, insurer, roadside provider, locksmith, or local law enforcement.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, insurance, mechanical, safety, or other professional advice. Follow instructions from emergency dispatchers, local law enforcement, roadside assistance, your insurer, or a qualified repair professional.
