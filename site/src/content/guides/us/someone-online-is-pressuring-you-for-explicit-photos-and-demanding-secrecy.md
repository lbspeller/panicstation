# What to do if… someone online is pressuring you for explicit photos and demanding secrecy (USA)

## Short answer
Stop replying and do not send anything else or pay anyone. Tell one safe person now; if you are under 18, ask a trusted adult to help you.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send another image, video, or message to try to make the pressure stop.
- Do not pay money, cryptocurrency, gift cards, or removal fees.
- Do not negotiate, threaten the person back, or try to investigate them yourself.
- Do not delete your profile or messages in a panic.
- Do not keep this secret because the person demanded it.
- Do not pay an unsolicited cleanup or takedown company, or agree to services under pressure.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause.** Stop replying. Put the phone down briefly or mute notifications while you tell someone safe. If you are in immediate physical danger, call **911**.
2. **Tell one safe person.** If you are under 18, tell a parent, guardian, school counselor, or another trusted adult. If you are 18 or older, tell a trusted person who can stay with you while you take the next steps.
3. **Report the account on the platform, then block it.** Use the platform’s safety or report feature where possible. Block the account after reporting it, and block new accounts if the person contacts you again.
4. **Use the child-safety reporting route when relevant.** If you are under 18, an explicit image was taken before age 18, or you think a child may be being sexually exploited online, ask a trusted adult to help you contact the **NCMEC CyberTipline**. You can also make a report yourself.
5. **Use an image-removal tool only when it fits your situation.** For an image or video taken before you were 18, **Take It Down** may help limit online sharing. Do not send, share, or download an image in order to use it. If you are currently over 18, the image is of you, it was taken when you were 18 or older, and you still have it on your device, **StopNCII** may be an option.
6. **Consider an extortion report.** If the person is demanding money or threatening you, you can also report the incident through the FBI’s **Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3)**. If a child may be involved, keep the **NCMEC CyberTipline** route as the priority.
7. **Preserve only basic details if it feels safe.** If you may seek help later, avoid deleting your profile or messages and note the account name, platform, and threats; do not download or resend explicit files.
8. **Secure an account only if access may be compromised.** If you think the person has your password, accessed an account, or taken over a profile, change the password and turn on two-factor authentication.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to make any reports beyond the immediate options above, unless someone is in immediate danger.
- Writing a full account of what happened.
- Trying to identify or track the person.
- Contacting your school, employer, or wider social circle.
- Responding to paid-help offers.

## Important reassurance
The pressure and secrecy are not your fault. You do not have to handle this alone, and sending something earlier does not mean you have to send anything else now.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need support from a trusted adult, a specialist support service, or an appropriate reporting organisation.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call **911**.
