# What to do if… police contact you about a death and you are not sure what to say (USA)

## Short answer
Slow the contact down. For an unexpected call, get callback details and say you need to return the call; for an in-person contact, ask whether you are free to leave or end the conversation. If you may be implicated or are unsure, say that you do not want to answer questions about the death or your actions without a lawyer; comply with lawful instructions, and be aware that some states may require you to give your name during a lawful stop.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess, speculate, or try to fill gaps in your memory while you are shocked or grieving.
- Do not lie, delete messages, edit records, or ask other people to agree on a version of events.
- Do not voluntarily hand over a phone, provide passwords, or consent to a search just because you feel pressured to help.
- Do not physically interfere if officers say they have a warrant or another legal basis to enter, search, or take an item. You can state clearly that you do not consent.
- Do not assume that the absence of Miranda warnings means your words cannot be used. Miranda warnings relate to custodial interrogation, not every conversation with police.
- Do not sign or adopt a statement that you have not read carefully or that is not accurate.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the contact.** For an unexpected call, say: “I am upset and I want to be accurate. Please give me your details so I can call back.” For an in-person contact, say: “Before I answer questions, am I free to leave or end this conversation?”
2. **Verify who contacted you.** Ask for the officer’s name, badge number, agency, callback number, and a case number if one is available. For an unexpected call, look up the agency’s publicly listed number and call the agency back through that number before sharing information.
3. **Ask about the purpose and your status.** Ask: “What is this about, in one sentence?” and “Are you contacting me as a witness, or do you think I may be involved?” The officer may not answer, and your status may change.
4. **If you may be implicated or you are unsure, decline questions about the death or your actions clearly.** Say: “I do not want to answer questions or do an interview without a lawyer.” If you are in custody and being questioned, say: “I am invoking my right to remain silent. I want a lawyer.” Do not keep explaining. Follow lawful instructions and do not physically resist. Some states may require you to give your name during a lawful stop.
5. **If you choose to answer a narrow factual question, keep it narrow.** Share only what you personally know. Use “I do not know,” “I do not recall,” or “I need to check my records” instead of guessing.
6. **Treat a request for a longer interview as a separate decision.** For an informal voluntary request, say: “I am not able to do a full interview right now. I need to speak with a lawyer before scheduling one.” Do not ignore or physically resist a warrant, subpoena, court order, or detention.
7. **If police are at your door, slow the moment down.** You can ask to speak through the closed door unless officers lawfully require something else. If they ask to enter or search, say: “I do not consent to a search.” If they say they have a warrant, ask to see it if it is safe to do so. Do not physically interfere. Police may sometimes enter or search without consent under a warrant or another legal exception.
8. **Make a brief written record.** Note who contacted you, when they contacted you, what they asked, what you answered, and whether anyone else was present. Save call logs, voicemails, texts, and emails. Do not delete, edit, or discard records connected with the contact.

## What can wait
- You do not need to reconstruct a full account while you are in shock or grieving.
- You usually do not need to decide immediately whether to agree to a voluntary interview, sign a statement, hand over a device voluntarily, provide passwords voluntarily, or consent to a search.
- You do not need to make a final decision about longer conversations with police before getting individual legal advice.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to slow the conversation down. Stress and grief can make it harder to recall details clearly, and asking for time or legal advice can help prevent avoidable misunderstandings.

## Scope note
These are first steps for an unexpected police contact about a death. Later decisions may need advice from a lawyer who can consider the facts, your state, and any documents or requests you receive.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Laws and procedures vary by state and situation. If you are detained, searched, served with a legal document, or concerned that you may be implicated, seek advice from a lawyer.
