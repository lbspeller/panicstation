# What to do if… your child says an adult is asking for private photos or video calls and offering gifts (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a child sexual exploitation risk, not misbehavior. Get your child away from the contact, do not investigate the adult yourself, and report suspected online exploitation to the CyberTipline; call 911 now if there is immediate danger, a planned meeting, blackmail, or fear the adult may reach your child soon.

## Do not do these things
- Do not blame, shame, or punish your child for telling you.
- Do not ask your child to keep chatting with the adult to collect more evidence.
- Do not message, threaten, pay, bargain with, or try to identify the adult yourself.
- Do not delete messages, usernames, gift offers, payment requests, account details, or screenshots if they still exist.
- Do not send, copy, forward, post, or show sexual images or videos of your child to other adults unless a platform, CyberTipline report, or investigator specifically requires it.
- Do not make your child repeat the whole story to several people if it can be avoided.
- Do not promise that you can keep it entirely private; say that some safe adults may need to help protect them.

## What to do now
1. Move your child to a calmer, private place and say clearly: “You are not in trouble. Thank you for telling me. This is not your fault.”
2. Stop the contact in the safest practical way. Help your child stop replying, block the account, leave the chat, turn off live video and location sharing, and put the device aside if new messages are still coming in.
3. Preserve what already exists without spreading it. Write down or screenshot usernames, profile names, app names, message threads, gift offers, payment requests, threats, dates, and any meeting suggestions, but do not make your child keep engaging with the adult.
4. Check the immediate risk level: whether any photo or video was sent, whether a live call happened, whether money, gift cards, games, rides, or deliveries were offered, whether the adult knows your child’s real name, school, phone number, address, or plans, and whether an in-person meeting was suggested.
5. Report it. In the USA, suspected online enticement or child sexual exploitation can be reported to the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children’s CyberTipline. If there is immediate danger, a planned meeting, blackmail, or fear the adult may find your child soon, call 911 or local police now. You can also contact your local FBI field office, call 1-800-CALL-FBI, or report online at tips.fbi.gov.
6. If sexual images or videos of your child may already be online, use NCMEC’s Take It Down if the material is already on a device you control, and make a CyberTipline report. Do not download, forward, or send sexual images or videos between adults to try to solve this yourself.
7. If the adult is linked to your child’s school, sports club, faith group, tutoring, childcare, or another youth activity, tell the responsible supervisor or child-safety lead enough to keep your child away from that person while reports are being made.
8. Stay close for the next several hours. Check with your child for backup accounts, new friend requests, gaming messages, gift cards, delivery messages, payment-app messages, or threats across other platforms so they are not managing this alone.
9. Arrange calm support for your child tonight. A familiar adult presence matters. If you need help thinking through child abuse concerns or where to report, contact the Childhelp National Child Abuse Hotline by calling or texting 800-422-4453.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide every device rule, school consequence, family conversation, or long-term internet plan today. You also do not need a perfect written statement from your child right now. The urgent priorities are safety, ending contact, preserving what already exists without spreading it, and getting reporting and support started.

## Important reassurance
Adults who target children often use attention, secrecy, gifts, pressure, flattery, fake identities, or threats. Children may freeze, comply, hide it, or worry they will be blamed. None of those reactions make this their fault, and telling you was a protective step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for a highly sensitive situation. Later decisions about police follow-up, school, devices, platform reports, and emotional support may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, law-enforcement, medical, mental-health, therapeutic, financial, or professional advice. If you think there is any immediate danger, coercion, blackmail, or risk of an in-person meeting, call 911 now.
