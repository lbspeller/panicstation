# What to do if… an officer asks you to write down your account of events immediately (USA)
## Short answer
Do not start writing a detailed account just because you feel rushed. Ask whether you are free to leave. Say clearly that you do not want to answer questions about events or write an account before speaking with a lawyer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess, fill gaps, or invent details because you feel pressure to produce a complete account.
- Do not write or sign a narrative merely to end the conversation quickly.
- Do not edit, initial, or sign a statement you do not fully understand.
- Do not lie.
- Do not argue, physically resist, or obstruct an officer.

## What to do now
1. **Ask whether you are free to leave.** Say: “Am I free to leave?” If the officer says yes, leave calmly. If you need emergency help, give the minimum immediate safety information needed to get that help.
2. **Say that you do not want to write an account now.** Use one clear sentence: “I do not want to answer questions about events or write a statement before speaking with a lawyer.”
3. **If you are in custody and being questioned, or you are unsure whether that applies, be explicit.** Say: “I am invoking my right to remain silent. I want a lawyer.” Then do not answer questions about events or start writing a narrative. Rules about identifying information vary by state and situation.
4. **If questioning about events continues, repeat the same sentence.** Do not try to explain your reasons, correct the officer’s wording, or fill silence with details.
5. **If paper or a form is placed in front of you, pause.** Do not fill in, edit, initial, or sign a written account merely because you feel rushed. Repeat that you want to speak with a lawyer first.
6. **Ask for help understanding what is happening when needed.** Say that you need an interpreter if you do not understand the language being used. If you are a minor, clearly ask to speak with a lawyer and a trusted adult; rules vary by state.
7. **Ask for medical care if you need it.** Say: “I need medical attention.”
8. **Make a private note later, when safe.** Record the time, location, agency, and any officer name or badge number you remember. Keep the note for your lawyer rather than handing it over as a statement.

## What can wait
- You do not need to produce a complete written narrative, timeline, or explanation immediately.
- You do not need to resolve memory gaps, estimated times, or possible contradictions in the moment.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to provide a fuller account later.
- Rules about identifying information are separate from writing a narrative and may vary by state and situation.
- You do not need to work out your legal position without qualified legal help.

## Important reassurance
Feeling rushed, frozen, or unsure can make it harder to remember details accurately. Using one calm sentence and pausing before writing is a reasonable way to reduce avoidable mistakes.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate moment only. Later decisions may depend on whether you are a witness, victim, or suspect, whether you are free to leave, and the rules in your state. A qualified lawyer can help with those later decisions.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Laws and procedures vary by state and situation. If questioning about events continues after you clearly say that you are invoking your right to remain silent or that you want a lawyer, do not answer questions about events; repeat your clear sentence and seek legal help.
