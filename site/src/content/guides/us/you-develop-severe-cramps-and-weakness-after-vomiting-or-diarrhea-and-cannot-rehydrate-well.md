# What to do if… you develop severe cramps and weakness after vomiting or diarrhea and cannot rehydrate well (USA)

## Short answer
Seek immediate medical evaluation if you cannot keep fluids down or experience severe weakness, confusion, or significantly decreased urination. Call 911 for life-threatening symptoms like fainting or chest pain; otherwise, go to an emergency room or urgent care for potential IV fluid replacement.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to drink large volumes of fluid rapidly, as this often triggers further vomiting.
- Do not ignore muscle twitching, severe cramps, or a heart rate that remains high while resting.
- Do not consume alcohol, caffeinated coffee, or energy drinks, which can increase fluid loss.
- Do not drink undiluted fruit juice or full-strength soda, as high sugar content can worsen diarrhea.
- Do not take over-the-counter anti-diarrheal medication if you have a high fever or bloody stool without clinician approval.
- Do not drive yourself to a medical facility if you feel lightheaded, dizzy, or confused.

## What to do now
1. **Identify emergency warning signs and call 911 immediately if they occur.**
   - Fainting, loss of consciousness, or inability to stay awake.
   - New or worsening confusion, delirium, or slurred speech.
   - Vomiting that is bright green (bile), bright red (blood), or looks like dark coffee grounds.
   - Severe, localized abdominal pain or chest pain.
   - Seizures or uncontrollable muscle twitching.
2. **Escalate to professional care if you cannot maintain hydration at home.**
   - If you have not urinated in 8 hours (or have very dark, brown-colored urine) and cannot keep liquids down, **go to the emergency room**.
   - If symptoms are persistent but not life-threatening, **visit an urgent care center** or contact your primary clinician for a same-day appointment.
   - Inform the triage staff immediately if you have underlying conditions like diabetes, kidney disease, or are pregnant.
3. **Attempt oral rehydration using very small, measured amounts.**
   - Use a commercially prepared **oral rehydration solution** (ORS) such as Pedialyte or a store-brand equivalent.
   - Take **1 to 2 teaspoons (5 to 10 ml) every 2 to 5 minutes** rather than drinking from a glass.
   - If you use sports drinks, **dilute them with equal parts water** (1:1 ratio) to reduce sugar concentration.
4. **Monitor your physical response to position changes.**
   - Pay close attention if you feel **faint, dizzy, or if your vision dims** when moving from lying down to sitting or standing.
   - If these symptoms occur, stay lying down and arrange for transport to a medical facility; do not attempt to "walk it off."
5. **Minimize physical exertion and external heat.**
   - Rest in a cool environment and avoid any unnecessary movement.
   - If vomiting has paused for more than two hours, you may try small bites of bland foods like saltine crackers or dry toast, but **prioritize fluids over solids**.
6. **Track your output to provide data to medical staff.**
   - Note the approximate time of your last urination and the frequency of vomiting or diarrhea episodes.
   - This information helps clinicians determine the severity of your dehydration and the volume of IV fluids required.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the specific virus or bacteria causing the illness right now.
- You do not need to consume a full meal or meet caloric goals while unstable.
- You do not need to decide on returning to work or school for the next 24 to 48 hours.
- You do not need to try multiple different home remedies or "stomach-settling" supplements.

## Important reassurance
Severe weakness and cramping are common physiological responses when the body’s electrolyte and fluid levels are depleted. These symptoms are signals from your nervous and muscular systems that professional intervention, such as IV hydration, may be necessary to stabilize your condition.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for suspected acute dehydration. It is not a substitute for clinical diagnosis; severe dehydration is a medical emergency that requires professional assessment and treatment.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute medical advice. Dehydration can escalate rapidly, particularly in children, older adults, and those with chronic health conditions; always default to professional medical evaluation if you are concerned.
