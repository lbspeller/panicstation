# What to do if… you realise your passport was left in an airport security tray after you walk away (USA)

## Short answer
Go straight back to the same TSA checkpoint and tell a TSA officer that your passport was left in a screening bin. Ask for the checkpoint supervisor or the TSA lost-and-found process for that airport.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave the airport before trying the checkpoint supervisor and the TSA or airport lost-and-found route.
- Do not push back through screening without staff direction; ask to be escorted or directed.
- Do not post passport photos online or give passport details to unofficial “helpers.”
- Do not report the passport lost or stolen to the State Department while staff are actively checking and may be able to hand it back quickly.
- Do not board an international flight without confirming you still have the passport you need for that trip.

## What to do now
1. **Turn around and go back to the TSA checkpoint immediately.** Go to the same lane if you can. Say: “I left my passport in a bin at this checkpoint a few minutes ago.”
2. **Ask for the TSA checkpoint supervisor.** Ask them to check the bin-clearing area, the screening lane, and wherever documents found at that checkpoint are secured.
3. **If you cannot get back to the checkpoint, go to the nearest airport information desk, gate agent, or uniformed airport staff member.** Ask them to contact TSA lost and found or the checkpoint supervisor for that terminal.
4. **Submit the TSA lost-and-found form for that airport if the passport is not handed back quickly.** Include the airport, terminal, checkpoint if known, lane if known, time, passport description, your name, and contact details.
5. **Also check the airport’s own lost-and-found route if TSA tells you to.** TSA generally handles items left at security checkpoints, while the airport authority may handle items left elsewhere in the airport.
6. **Write down the next step before you move on.** Note any case or reference number, the office holding the item, when you can collect it, and what identification they require.
7. **If you have an imminent international flight, tell the airline gate agent what is happening.** Ask what your timing options are before the boarding or document-check deadline.
8. **If TSA or the airport cannot confirm it is found and being returned soon, treat it as genuinely lost or stolen.** Use the U.S. State Department process to report a valid passport lost or stolen. Once reported, it is cancelled and cannot be used for international travel even if you later find it.
9. **If you are outside the United States and need to travel urgently, contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate.** Ask about replacing a lost or stolen passport abroad or getting an emergency passport if there is not enough time for a regular one.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide about a full replacement application while TSA or airport staff are still actively checking and you may recover the passport immediately.
- You do not need to make a police report right now unless there is clear theft, local staff instruct you to, or you need it for insurance.
- You do not need to cancel cards or freeze credit unless you also lost other sensitive items or you see suspicious activity.
- You do not need to explain everything to every person nearby; one clear report to the right official route is enough for this moment.

## Important reassurance
This is a common airport mistake, and items left at TSA checkpoints are often moved into a controlled lost-and-found process. Acting quickly, staying with official staff, and avoiding premature cancellation gives you the best chance of getting the passport back without creating a bigger problem.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: recover the passport if it is nearby, avoid unsafe shortcuts, and avoid cancelling it too early. Later decisions about replacement, flights, insurance, or identity protection may need official help from TSA, the airport, the State Department, the airline, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, travel, security, or professional advice. Airport procedures vary, and official staff can tell you the process for that airport and your situation.
