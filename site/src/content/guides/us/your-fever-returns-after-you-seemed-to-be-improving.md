# What to do if… your fever returns after you seemed to be improving (USA)

## Short answer
If your fever returns after you seemed to be improving, contact a clinician today, such as your primary care office, nurse line, telehealth service, or urgent care. Call 911 or go to an emergency department now if you have trouble breathing, chest pain, confusion, seizures, severe weakness, signs of serious dehydration, or symptoms that are rapidly worsening.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume you are in the clear because you had a better day; a returned fever can sometimes signal a complication or a new problem.
- Do not exceed label doses of fever medicine.
- Do not stack cold, flu, or sleep products without checking the ingredients, because more than one product may contain acetaminophen.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics or someone else’s medicine.
- Do not restart or stop prescribed antibiotics without asking the prescriber or another clinician.
- Do not push through work, school, travel, or errands while the fever is back and you are arranging care.
- Do not ignore symptoms that feel severe, unusual, or rapidly worse.

## What to do now
1. Re-check and write down the fever clearly. Use a digital thermometer if you have one. Write the temperature, time, how it was taken, and any fever-reducing medicine you took, including the name, dose, and time.

2. Check for emergency warning signs. Call 911 or go to an emergency department now for trouble breathing, persistent chest pain or pressure, new confusion, inability to wake or stay awake, seizures, fainting, severe weakness or unsteadiness, not urinating, severe dehydration, a stiff neck with a bad headache, or any symptom that feels severe or rapidly worsening.

3. Treat “better, then fever or cough came back” as a reason to get medical advice today. This is especially important if you have flu-like or COVID-like symptoms, shortness of breath, chest discomfort, pregnancy, immune suppression, diabetes, heart or lung disease, are an older adult, or are caring for a baby or young child.

4. Get urgent medical help for a baby under 3 months old with any measured fever. For a child of any age, seek care promptly if there is trouble breathing, unusual sleepiness, no tears when crying, very few wet diapers, a stiff neck, a rash, repeated vomiting, or symptoms that are getting worse again.

5. Contact care using the fastest available route. Call your primary care office and ask for same-day advice, use your health plan’s nurse line, use a telehealth service, or go to urgent care. Say: “My fever improved, then returned,” and give your highest temperature, when it came back, and what symptoms changed.

6. Reduce spread while you arrange care if a respiratory virus is possible. Stay home and away from others where possible. If you must be around other people or go for care, consider wearing a well-fitting mask, avoid close contact with high-risk people, and wash or sanitize your hands.

7. Keep fluids going in small amounts. Take frequent sips of water or an oral rehydration drink. If you cannot keep fluids down, are repeatedly vomiting, feel faint, have a very dry mouth, or are peeing very little or very dark urine, seek urgent advice or emergency care depending on severity.

8. Prepare a short timeline for the clinician. Include when the illness started, when you improved, when the fever returned, your highest temperature, current symptoms, any COVID or flu test results, medical conditions, pregnancy status if relevant, and all medicines or supplements you have taken.

## What can wait
- You do not need to know the exact diagnosis before contacting care.
- You do not need to decide now when to return to work, school, exercise, or travel.
- You can postpone non-urgent errands, housework, and catching up until you are clearly improving again.
- You do not need to search every possible cause right now; focus on symptoms, hydration, reducing spread, and getting same-day advice.

## Important reassurance
A fever that returns after improvement can feel alarming, but getting checked is a reasonable next step. You are not overreacting by asking for help, especially when the pattern has changed.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours only. Later decisions, testing, treatment, or follow-up may need a clinician who can assess your symptoms, risks, and exam findings.

## Important note
This guide is general information and is not medical diagnosis, treatment, or professional advice. If you feel very unwell, symptoms are rapidly worsening, or you are worried for any reason, seek urgent medical care.
