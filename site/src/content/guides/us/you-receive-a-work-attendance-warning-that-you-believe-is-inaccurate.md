# What to do if… you receive a work attendance warning that you believe is inaccurate (USA)

## Short answer
Respond in writing and ask for the exact dates, the policy, and the records used. Then send a short factual correction with existing proof, so your side is on file before the issue escalates.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a write-up that says you agree if you do not; ask whether you can write “received only” or “I disagree” before signing.
- Do not edit, recreate, or backfill records to make the problem look fixed; use existing records and explain any missing punch or schedule issue.
- Do not ignore a meeting notice, deadline, or request for information, even if the warning feels clearly wrong.
- Do not vent about it on workplace chat or social media; keep messages factual, professional, and saved.
- Do not quit impulsively right after the warning.
- Do not share detailed medical information unless it is needed for a leave or accommodation process.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the details in writing.** Ask HR or your manager for:
   - the specific dates and times they say were late, absent, or unapproved
   - the attendance rule or policy section they are applying
   - whether this is coaching, a warning, or formal discipline
   - the records they relied on, such as time clock punches, scheduling records, attendance points, leave coding, manager edits, or exception reports
   - whether you may submit a written correction or rebuttal to be kept with the record

2. **If there is a meeting, slow it down enough to be fair.**
   - Ask for the agenda and the attendance records before the meeting.
   - If you are in a union workplace, the meeting is investigatory, and you reasonably believe discipline could result, ask for your union representative before answering questions.
   - If you are not in a union, ask whether policy allows a support person and request a brief reschedule if you need time to gather records.

3. **Build a small proof pack for the disputed dates.** Use records that already exist, such as:
   - timecard screenshots, punch history, or scheduling app screenshots
   - emails, messages, calendar entries, meeting attendance, work submissions, dispatch logs, or job records showing you worked
   - screenshots of approved time off, shift swaps, schedule changes, or leave requests
   - badge or access logs, if you already have access to them
   - a brief medical or appointment confirmation only if the absence may involve leave or accommodation, and only if needed

4. **Send a short factual correction.** Keep it focused and calm:
   - “I believe the attendance warning is based on incorrect data for [dates].”
   - list each disputed date and what is wrong
   - attach or identify the records that support your correction
   - ask HR or your manager to correct the attendance record, points, discipline status, and any related payroll issue
   - ask for written confirmation of the corrected status

5. **If health, disability, pregnancy, or caregiving may be involved, start the right process early.**
   - If the absence relates to your health or a family member who may be covered by FMLA, ask HR for the FMLA process and required forms if you may be eligible.
   - If a medical condition affects attendance and you need flexibility or leave, say plainly that you are asking for a reasonable accommodation, such as attendance flexibility or leave, and ask for the employer’s interactive process.
   - Keep medical details limited to what HR needs to process the request.

6. **If pay was affected, handle that as a separate urgent issue.**
   - Compare the disputed dates with your paystub, hours worked, timecard, and schedule.
   - Ask payroll or HR in writing to correct any missing hours, wrong deductions, or unpaid time worked.
   - Save the paystub, time record, and your correction request.

7. **Use any internal review or appeal step.**
   - Ask where to send a rebuttal or appeal and whether there is a deadline.
   - Keep the appeal focused on dates, records, policy, and the correction you want.
   - Save copies of what you submit and any response.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to file a government complaint or contact an attorney.
- You do not need to write a long explanation of your whole job history.
- You do not need to confront coworkers or find out who entered the record wrong.
- You do not need to disclose a diagnosis or detailed medical history before using the proper leave or accommodation process.
- You do not need to solve every future attendance concern before correcting the disputed record.

## Important reassurance
Attendance disputes can come from ordinary record problems, such as missed punches, schedule changes, leave coding, or manager edits. A calm written correction with specific dates and records can make the issue easier to fix, and it helps keep the file from being one-sided.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to stabilize the situation, correct the record, and preserve options. Later decisions may need help from a union representative, HR, a state labor agency, the U.S. Department of Labor, the EEOC, or an employment attorney, depending on the facts.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. Employment rules, leave rights, wage rules, union rights, and employer policies vary by state, employer size, workplace, and individual situation.
