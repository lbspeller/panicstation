# What to do if… you notice unexplained micro-deposits in your account that you did not request (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank's fraud department immediately to report "unauthorized micro-entries" and request a block on all pending or future ACH debits from those specific originators.

## Do not do these things
- Don't verify the deposit amounts on any website, app, or phone system that you did not personally initiate.
- Don't click links in texts or emails that claim to be from a "verification service" regarding these deposits.
- Don't send money back to anyone who contacts you claiming they "accidentally" deposited funds into your account.
- Don't share the exact cent amounts with anyone except your bank's verified customer service.
- Don't wait for a larger withdrawal to occur before contacting your financial institution.

## What to do now
1. **Record the exact details of the suspicious transactions.** Write down the date, the exact amount in cents, and the "Company Entry Description" or "Originator Name" exactly as it appears in your transaction history.
   - Look for specific 2026 standardized terms like "PURCHASE," "PAYROLL," or "ACCTVERIFY" which may be misused by scammers.
2. **Review your account for matching debits or "offsetting" entries.** Check if the same amount was withdrawn shortly after being deposited, as this confirms the account-linking process is active.
3. **Call your bank using a verified number from your physical card or official app.** Ask specifically for the **Fraud Department** or the **ACH Operations Team**.
4. **State that you are the victim of a "False Pretense" or "Account Takeover" attempt involving unauthorized micro-entries.** Using these specific terms helps the bank agent trigger the correct internal security protocols for 2026 compliance.
5. **Request that the bank place an "ACH Block" or "ACH Filter" on the specific Originator IDs involved.** This prevents the third party from using the successful verification to withdraw larger sums of money.
6. **Ask the bank to review all recently linked external accounts or "Transfer Profiles."** Ensure no new P2P services (such as Zelle, Venmo, or CashApp) or external bank accounts have been added to your profile in the last 72 hours.
7. **Update your digital security credentials from a separate, trusted device.** Change your online banking password and verify that your multi-factor authentication (MFA) is set to a secure method, such as an authenticator app, rather than just SMS.
8. **File a report with the Federal Trade Commission at IdentityTheft.gov.** Use the "I want to report another type of identity theft" option and specify that your bank account details have been compromised.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the person who initiated the deposits; the bank's back-office team will handle the trace via the ACH Network.
- You do not need to close your entire bank account immediately unless the fraud team confirms your full routing and account numbers are broadly exposed.
- Deciding on long-term credit monitoring services can wait until after your immediate account access is secured.

## Important reassurance
Spotting micro-deposits is a successful detection of a "test" phase. Because the scammers are still trying to verify the account, you often have a window of time to block the link before any significant funds are withdrawn. Taking these steps now is the standard procedure for preventing further access.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for unauthorized account-linking attempts. It does not cover the legal recovery process if funds have already been successfully stolen, which requires a formal Regulation E dispute.

Ensure you report the error within 60 days of the statement showing the transaction to preserve your full liability protections.
