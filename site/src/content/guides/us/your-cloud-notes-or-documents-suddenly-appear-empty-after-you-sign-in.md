# What to do if… your cloud notes or documents suddenly appear empty after you sign in (USA)

## Short answer
Pause changes. Confirm you are in the right account and workspace, then check Trash, Recently Deleted, activity, and any restore or version-history tools before you reinstall apps or create new files.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t empty Trash, Recycle Bin, or Recently Deleted.
- Don’t run “reset” or “cleanup” actions to force a refresh.
- Don’t keep editing, uploading, or creating test files in the empty account.
- Don’t uninstall the app, wipe a device, or remove local synced folders yet.
- Don’t trust unexpected support messages, calls, or pop-ups; use the provider’s official website or app.
- Don’t assume it is permanent. An empty view can be the wrong account, wrong workspace, permissions, a filter, sync delay, or recoverable deletion.

## What to do now
1. **Pause changes on the affected device.**
   - Stop editing, uploading, moving, or deleting files.
   - If one device is showing an empty account, turn off internet access on that affected device while you check from a trusted device.
   - Keep one trusted device online so you can check the account safely.

2. **Write down what changed.**
   - Screenshot the empty view, the account email or username, and any error message.
   - Note when you first noticed it, which devices are affected, and any recent change such as a password reset, new phone, new computer, shared-folder change, or app update.

3. **Confirm the exact account and space.**
   - Check the full email address, not just the display name.
   - Check personal versus work or school accounts.
   - Check whether you are in a shared drive, team space, workspace, another browser profile, another Apple Account or Apple ID, another Microsoft account, or another Google account.

4. **Check whether the files are hidden by view or permissions.**
   - Clear filters and search for a distinctive filename or phrase from one missing note or document.
   - Check Recents, Activity, Shared with me, Shared, Team, Workspace, and offline-only views.
   - For shared files, check whether the owner removed your access or moved the folder.

5. **Check recovery areas before doing anything destructive.**
   - Look in Trash, Recycle Bin, Recently Deleted, Deleted files, or the provider’s recovery area.
   - Restore only items you recognize.
   - Do not empty these areas while you are still checking.

6. **Look for mass changes or account takeover signs.**
   - Review account activity, recent file activity, connected apps, and signed-in devices.
   - Watch for many files being renamed, moved, deleted, encrypted, or changed in a short time.
   - If anything looks unfamiliar, do not approve new sign-in prompts or recovery messages you did not request.

7. **Use restore, rewind, or version history if available.**
   - If the service offers a whole-account restore, folder rewind, or file version history, use that official tool rather than manually moving many files back.
   - Before confirming, check what time period and folders the restore will affect, because restore tools can undo later changes too.
   - If this is a work or school account, contact your admin or IT support now; they may have recovery tools that you cannot access yourself.

8. **If you suspect someone got into the account, secure access first.**
   - Use the provider’s official account recovery process.
   - Change the password to a unique password.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication.
   - Sign out other sessions, remove unknown devices, and revoke unknown connected apps.
   - Check recovery email addresses, recovery phone numbers, and forwarding rules.

9. **Check the affected device when it is safe to do so.**
   - Use a trusted device for account recovery if the affected device may be unsafe.
   - Update security software and run a scan, especially if this started after a download, attachment, browser extension, or support link.
   - Do not restore files onto a device you still believe may be infected.

10. **Use federal reporting portals if a scam or identity theft may be involved.**
   - If someone used your personal information, or you think they might, use IdentityTheft.gov for a recovery plan.
   - If you are reporting a scam or fraud without clear identity theft, use ReportFraud.ftc.gov and keep the confirmation details.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to change cloud services today.
- You do not need to rebuild folders, rename files, or start fresh while recovery options are still open.
- You do not need to notify every collaborator until you know whether this is a wrong account, permission change, deletion, or account takeover.
- You do not need to buy new software or pay someone from a pop-up or unexpected message.

## Important reassurance
A sudden empty cloud account view can happen because of account mix-ups, shared-folder changes, filters, sync problems, or deleted-file areas that still allow recovery. Moving slowly now helps protect whatever recoverable copies or versions may still exist.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent further loss and regain control. Later decisions may need help from the cloud provider, your workplace or school IT team, or a qualified technology professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Follow the official instructions for your specific provider and account type.
