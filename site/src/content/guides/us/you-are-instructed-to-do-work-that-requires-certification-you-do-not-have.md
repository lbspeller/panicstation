# What to do if… you are instructed to do work that requires certification you do not have (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign, submit, or certify any record you know to be false. Request the instruction in writing, document the request independently, and escalate the issue through an internal compliance hotline or a relevant federal regulatory agency.

## Do not do these things
- Do not "just follow orders" with the intent to fix it later—submitting a false record can create personal legal liability or grounds for "for cause" termination.
- Do not delete or alter the original, accurate data or the communications containing the request to change it.
- Do not attempt to "clean" or retroactively edit a record you have already submitted; instead, report the error through official channels.
- Do not export or download large volumes of proprietary company data to personal devices, as this often violates security policies and can be used to justify your termination.
- Do not engage in a verbal-only confrontation; avoid making threats of "calling the feds" in the heat of the moment.

## What to do now
1. **State your refusal clearly and neutrally without becoming emotional.** **Inform the person making the request that you are uncomfortable submitting inaccurate data and that you need to verify the source figures first.**
   - Do not say "you are lying"; say "this figure does not match my primary records."
2. **Request the instruction in a documented format.** **Ask the requester to send the specific changes they want made via email or a tracked messaging system, including the business justification for the edit.**
   - If they refuse to put it in writing, send a follow-up email to them stating: "Per our conversation, I understand you are requesting that I change [X] to [Y]. I am unable to do this as it does not reflect the current data."
3. **Create a contemporaneous personal log outside of company systems.** **Record the date, time, location, individuals involved, and the specific details of what you were asked to change.**
   - Include any mentions of "consequences" or "performance impacts" if you were to refuse.
   - List any witnesses who were present during the request.
4. **Identify the correct reporting channel for your industry.** **Determine if your company has a "Speak Up" line, Ethics Officer, or Internal Audit department that is separate from your immediate chain of command.**
   - For public companies, check for SEC-compliant whistleblower portals.
   - For healthcare, locate the Compliance Officer or the HHS-OIG (Office of Inspector General) reporting site.
5. **Report the pressure to the U.S. Department of Labor (DOL) if it involves hours or pay.** **Contact the Wage and Hour Division (WHD) at 1-866-487-9243 if you are being told to work off-the-clock or falsify timecards.**
   - The DOL protects your right to accurately record and report all hours worked.
6. **File an OSHA whistleblower complaint immediately if retaliation occurs.** **Submit a complaint at whistleblowers.gov within 30 days if you are fired, demoted, or threatened after refusing to falsify safety, environmental, or corporate records.**
   - Note that for many statutes, including the OSH Act (Section 11(c)), the filing window is strictly **30 calendar days** from the date you were notified of the retaliation.
7. **Contact the NLRB if the issue involves a group of employees.** **If you and your coworkers are being pressured together to "hit numbers" through falsification, this may be considered "protected concerted activity" under the National Labor Relations Act.**
   - You do not need to be in a union to have these protections.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resign your position immediately unless you are in physical danger or the environment is intolerable.
- You do not need to have a "perfect" or complete file of evidence before making an initial internal report.
- You do not need to determine exactly which federal statute was violated; reporting the factual inaccuracies is sufficient for the initial stage.
- You do not need to go to the media or post on social media; doing so can often complicate legal protections.

## Important reassurance
Being asked to lie about your work or performance is a high-stress event that triggers a natural "flight or fight" response. Refusing to participate in fraud is a standard professional boundary, and federal laws are specifically designed to protect employees who choose accuracy over falsification. You are not "being difficult" by insisting on truthful records.

## Scope note
This guide covers initial stabilization and reporting for employees in the USA. It is focused on the immediate moments after a request to falsify information and does not cover long-term litigation or specific industry-regulated "qui tam" lawsuits.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not legal advice. Whistleblower laws vary significantly by state and industry. If you have already been fired or are facing severe retaliation, you should consult an employment attorney immediately to ensure you meet all statutory filing deadlines, some of which are as short as 30 days.
