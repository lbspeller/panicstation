# What to do if… your travel booking has the wrong gender marker and you are worried about document checks (USA)

## Short answer
Update the airline passenger data so it matches the ID or passport you will use for this trip, and save written confirmation. If you are close to departure, go to a staffed airline desk rather than trying repeated self-service check-in attempts.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave the issue for the TSA checkpoint if you still have time to contact the airline first.
- Do not cancel and rebook in a rush if the airline or booking agent can correct the passenger data.
- Do not assume the visible title, such as Mr, Ms, or Mx, is the same as the Secure Flight or travel-document field.
- Do not guess in a required field if your exact passport or ID marker is not offered by the booking system; ask the airline how they want it entered.
- Do not share extra personal or medical documents to explain yourself unless you choose to.
- Do not try to travel with an expired, invalid, or wrong-person ID.

## What to do now
1. **Pick the document you will actually present.** For a domestic U.S. flight, use the ID you will bring to the airport. For an international flight, use the passport or other accepted travel document you will present for that trip.
2. **Open the booking and find the passenger data fields.** Look under “Manage trip,” “Passenger information,” “Secure Flight,” “travel document,” or “passport details.”
3. **Check the exact fields that matter.** Compare the booking against your travel document for name, date of birth, sex or gender marker, passport number if used, passport expiry date if used, and redress number if you have one.
4. **Correct the field if the system lets you.** Save the change and take a screenshot of the updated passenger details if the page shows them.
5. **If the system does not let you correct it, contact the airline or booking agent.** Use simple wording: “Please correct the Secure Flight passenger data, and the passport or travel-document details if this is an international trip, so they match the document I will present.”
6. **If your valid document has a marker the online form does not offer, do not guess silently.** Ask the airline or booking agent how to enter the required passenger and travel-document data for that booking, and ask for written confirmation of what they changed.
7. **Keep proof easy to reach.** Save the record locator, flight numbers, a screenshot or email showing the correction, and the ID or passport you used as the reference.
8. **If departure is soon, use a staffed airline desk.** If online check-in or a kiosk loops, errors, or still shows old data, go straight to an airline agent and ask them to verify the Secure Flight and travel-document data in the reservation.
9. **Give yourself extra time.** A mismatch or recent correction may still lead to manual review, so arrive earlier than usual and keep your explanation brief: “The booking data was corrected to match this ID; here is the confirmation.”
10. **Use DHS TRIP only for repeated or unresolved screening problems.** It is not a quick fix for one wrong booking field, but it is the official redress route if you are repeatedly delayed, misidentified, or unable to print boarding passes because of screening issues.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make a complaint, change loyalty profiles, or post publicly.
- You do not need to explain your personal history to resolve a booking data issue.
- You do not need to make long-term document decisions before this trip unless the document you planned to use is expired, invalid, or not accepted for the route.
- You do not need to solve every future booking now; focus on getting this reservation aligned with the document you will present.

## Important reassurance
A wrong gender marker in a booking is usually handled as a passenger-data mismatch. Correcting the record, keeping confirmation, and using staffed help points can make the situation more manageable even if someone needs to verify the booking manually.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to reduce day-of-travel disruption and avoid preventable document-check problems. Later decisions about identity documents, discrimination complaints, redress, or country-specific entry rules may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Airline systems, TSA processes, passport rules, and destination entry rules can change; check the airline and official government sources for your exact route.
