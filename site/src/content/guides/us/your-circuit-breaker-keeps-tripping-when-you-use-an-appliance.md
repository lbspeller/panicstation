# What to do if… your circuit breaker keeps tripping when you use an appliance (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using the appliance, unplug it, and do not keep resetting the breaker. If the breaker trips again with everything unplugged, leave it OFF and contact a licensed electrician.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep flipping the breaker back on repeatedly.
- Don’t install a larger breaker or fuse to stop the tripping.
- Don’t force, tape, wedge, or hold a breaker switch in the ON position.
- Don’t keep using the same appliance “just once more.”
- Don’t remove the panel cover or deadfront.
- Don’t touch anything inside the electrical panel beyond the breaker handles.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger signs first.** If you notice smoke, fire, sparks, a burning or rubber smell, buzzing or crackling, scorch marks, or heat at an outlet, plug, cord, power strip, or appliance, stop using that area and keep people away. If there is smoke or fire, leave the area and call 911.
2. **Unplug the appliance that triggered the trip.** Turn it off, unplug it, and leave it unplugged. If it is hardwired, such as a dishwasher or disposal, turn it off only at a nearby wall switch or clearly marked disconnect you can reach safely.
3. **Unplug other items on the same affected circuit or area.** Include power strips, extension cords, chargers, lamps, and nearby small appliances.
4. **Reset the breaker once, only after things are unplugged.** Move the breaker fully to OFF, then back to ON.
   - If it stays on, plug items back in one at a time. If the breaker trips when a specific appliance is plugged in or turned on, stop using that appliance and keep it unplugged.
   - If it trips again with everything unplugged, assume a wiring, outlet, breaker, GFCI, or AFCI problem and leave the breaker OFF.
5. **Check for a GFCI reset only where it is obvious and safe.** Kitchens, bathrooms, garages, basements, laundry areas, and outdoor outlets may have GFCI protection. Press RESET once on the GFCI outlet if you can do so safely. If it will not reset or immediately trips again, stop using that outlet or circuit.
6. **Arrange help without opening anything electrical.**
   - If you rent, tell your landlord or property manager: “The breaker trips when using a normal appliance,” and add whether it trips with everything unplugged.
   - If you own, call a licensed electrician if the breaker will not stay on, trips with nothing plugged in, trips with multiple normal appliances, or there are any heat, smell, spark, scorch, or buzzing signs.
7. **Write down quick details.** Note the breaker label or number, room, outlet, appliance, whether it trips instantly or after a few minutes, whether the area was wet or damp, and whether a GFCI or AFCI button was involved.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide now whether the cause is overload, short circuit, ground fault, arc fault, appliance defect, or a bad breaker.
- You don’t need to take apart outlets, cords, plugs, appliances, or the breaker panel.
- You don’t need to buy a replacement breaker or rewire anything before a qualified person has identified the cause.
- You don’t need to keep testing once the same circuit or appliance has tripped again.

## Important reassurance
A tripping breaker is doing a protective job. Unplugging the appliance, stopping repeated resets, and leaving the breaker off when it will not hold are calm, safe first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps for immediate safety and basic isolation. Later decisions may need help from a licensed electrician, appliance repair professional, landlord, property manager, or local authority.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, electrical, or other professional advice. If there is smoke, fire, active sparking, or a threat to life or property, leave the area and call 911.
