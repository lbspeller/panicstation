# What to do if… your visa or entry permission start date is later than your arrival date (USA)

## Short answer
Do not travel or try to enter early until you have checked which document controls your arrival window. If you are already in the United States, check your I-94 now and get official help quickly if the class of admission or admit-until information is wrong.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the visa in your passport is the same thing as permission to enter or stay in a specific status.
- Do not attempt entry before the allowed window for your category, especially if you are a new F or M student or a J exchange visitor.
- Do not start studying, working, onboarding for work, or program activity until your status and admission record match what you need.
- Do not rely only on what someone said at check-in or at the airport; check the written admission record.
- Do not ignore a wrong I-94 class, admit-until date, name, passport number, or date of birth.

## What to do now
1. **Work out which document has the later start date.**  
   Check your visa foil, Form I-20 for F or M students, Form DS-2019 for J exchange visitors, approval notice if you have one, and your flight arrival date.

2. **If you have not travelled yet, pause travel plans until the dates match the allowed window.**  
   New F and M students are generally not allowed to enter more than 30 days before the program start date. J exchange visitors are also generally not allowed to enter more than 30 days before the program start date on the DS-2019.

3. **Contact the official person for your category before changing anything else.**  
   For F or M students, contact your school’s designated school official or international student office. For J exchange visitors, contact your sponsor’s responsible officer or alternate responsible officer. Ask what date you may enter and whether your document needs to be corrected or reissued.

4. **If an airline is questioning your documents, do not argue from memory.**  
   Have your passport, visa, I-20 or DS-2019, and written school or sponsor guidance ready. If the date is genuinely too early, rebooking is usually safer than trying to force boarding.

5. **If you already entered the United States, retrieve and save your I-94 immediately.**  
   Check the class of admission, admit-until date or D/S notation, your name, date of birth, passport number, and most recent entry date.

6. **If the I-94 or admission record is wrong, use CBP’s official correction route.**  
   Start with the official I-94 website and the CBP deferred inspection site instructions for the port or location handling corrections. Keep the request factual and attach only relevant documents, such as your passport, visa, I-20 or DS-2019, I-94, and travel details.

7. **Until the issue is corrected or clearly explained, act as if the status-dependent activity is not safe to begin.**  
   Do not begin work, program activity, or study that depends on a specific status until your school, sponsor, CBP, or qualified immigration advisor confirms the record is usable.

## What can wait
- You do not need to solve your whole immigration plan today.
- You do not need to decide housing, shipping, job resignation, or other irreversible commitments while the entry record is unclear.
- You do not need to argue about fault; focus on dates, documents, and the official correction route.
- You do not need to contact every agency at once if your school, sponsor, CBP, or qualified advisor has given you a clear next step.

## Important reassurance
Visa dates, program dates, and admission records are easy to confuse. The safest immediate path is to slow down, check the exact document and I-94 record, and use the official contact for your category before doing anything status-dependent.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about changing status, correcting records, re-entering, work, study, or immigration history may need qualified immigration help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, travel, or professional advice. U.S. immigration outcomes depend on your exact category, documents, entry history, and record.
