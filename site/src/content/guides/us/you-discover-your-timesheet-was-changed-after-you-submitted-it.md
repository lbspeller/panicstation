# What to do if… you discover your timesheet was changed after you submitted it (USA)

## Short answer
Preserve proof of the original and changed hours, then dispute the change in writing and ask payroll or your supervisor how the correct hours will be paid.

## Do not do these things
- Do not start with accusations in a hallway conversation; keep it factual and in writing.
- Do not edit the entries back yourself if that could make the record look more confusing.
- Do not sign or approve a timecard statement that says the changed hours are correct if you do not agree.
- Do not rely only on a verbal “we’ll fix it” without a written follow-up.
- Do not make an irreversible job decision in the first panic moment.

## What to do now
1. **Save what you can see before anything changes again.**
   - Screenshot or download the changed timecard, including dates, start and end times, total hours, and any “edited by,” “edited at,” “history,” or “audit” view.
   - Save the original submission confirmation, approval notice, email, app notification, or exported timecard if you have it.
   - Save your schedule, posted shift, assignment record, or message showing when you were expected to work.

2. **Write your own hour record for the affected days.**
   For each changed day, write the start time, end time, meal break, location if relevant, and what work you were doing. Save supporting items you already have, such as work messages, dispatch tickets, job logs, calendar entries, or shift-change texts.

3. **Send a short written dispute as soon as you can.**
   Send it to payroll and your supervisor, or to HR if that is your workplace process. Include:
   - the pay period and affected dates
   - the entries that changed
   - the hours you believe are correct
   - any screenshots or records you are attaching
   - a clear request: “Please review the edit history and confirm how the timecard will be corrected or how the missing pay will be paid.”

4. **Ask for the reason for the edit and any available edit history.**
   Keep the wording neutral: “Can you confirm who made the change, when it was made, and why?” You may not get every system detail, but asking in writing creates a clearer record.

5. **Check whether the change affects pay.**
   If you have already been paid, compare the paystub hours with your own record. If payday has not happened yet, ask payroll what hours will be used and whether the correction can be made before payroll closes or in the next correction run.

6. **Escalate inside the workplace if the answer is unclear or delayed.**
   If you are unionized, contact your union steward or representative. If you are not unionized, forward your written timeline and screenshots to HR or a higher manager and ask for a written response.

7. **Keep the federal recordkeeping point simple.**
   Under the federal Fair Labor Standards Act, covered employers generally must keep accurate records of hours worked and wages paid for covered nonexempt workers. Federal rules generally require payroll records to be kept for at least three years, and records used to compute wages, such as time cards and work schedules, for two years.

8. **Use an outside wage route if pay is shorted or the pattern continues.**
   You can contact the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division to ask questions or file a complaint. Federal law generally prohibits retaliation for protected wage-and-hour activity, such as asking about pay or hours, filing a complaint, or cooperating with an investigation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to sue, resign, or make a public complaint.
- You do not need to prove intent right now; focus on preserving records and getting the hours corrected or paid.
- You can wait to do a detailed overtime or state-law calculation until after you know the employer’s reason and correction plan.

## Important reassurance
A changed timesheet can feel destabilizing, especially when it affects pay. Screenshots, a calm written dispute, and a clear request for correction often resolve mistakes and create a clearer record if the issue needs to be escalated.

## Scope note
These are first steps to protect your record and pay. Later decisions may depend on your job type, exempt or nonexempt status, state wage rules, union contract, and what the employer says happened.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, employment, or professional advice. Wage-and-hour rights can vary by state and by job classification. If the employer will not correct the record or pay the hours you worked, consider contacting the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division, your state labor agency, a union representative, or a qualified employment lawyer.
