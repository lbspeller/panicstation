# What to do if… a joint account holder makes a major change to account settings without telling you (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your financial institution immediately via their verified fraud department number to secure online access and report any unrecognized changes as potential unauthorized activity.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use links or phone numbers from a text or email alert to contact the bank; these may be "spoofed" messages.
- Don’t share your multi-factor authentication (MFA) codes or temporary passcodes with anyone, including bank employees or the other account holder.
- Don’t wait to see if the change "reverts" on its own; settings changes often precede unauthorized fund transfers.
- Don’t attempt to "lock out" the other holder by repeatedly entering the wrong password, as this may trigger a permanent freeze that you cannot easily lift.
- Don’t assume a joint holder has the right to change *your* personal contact information or login credentials; most banks treat this as a security breach.

## What to do now
1. **Locate the bank's official contact number on the back of your debit card or a physical statement.** Avoid using search engine "sponsored" results for bank support numbers.
2. **Call the bank and state clearly: "I am a joint account holder and I am reporting unauthorized changes to my account security and contact settings."**
   - Use the phrase "unauthorized access" if you have been locked out of your own login.
   - Request a "security freeze" or "temporary restriction" on electronic transfers if you see pending transactions you did not authorize.
3. **Review the specific changes with the representative.** Check for:
   - Updated email addresses, phone numbers, or physical mailing addresses.
   - New "External Transfer" or "ACH" profiles and added payees.
   - Changes to "Alert" settings (e.g., turning off low-balance or large-transaction notifications).
   - New devices registered for mobile wallets or "Push" notifications.
4. **Invoke your Regulation E rights if money has moved.** If you see unauthorized electronic transfers:
   - **Tell the bank: "I am reporting an unauthorized electronic fund transfer error under Regulation E."**
   - The bank must permit you to report this orally, but they may require written follow-up. 
   - If they require written notice, **record the specific mailing address or secure portal link** they provide during the call. You generally have 10 business days to provide this written confirmation.
5. **Secure your secondary access points.**
   - **Update your personal email password and enable MFA immediately.** If a joint holder has access to your email, they can often bypass bank security.
   - Check your email "Forwarding" and "Rules" settings to ensure bank alerts are not being sent to a different address.
6. **Report the loss or theft of any "access device" within 2 business days.**
   - If a physical card or your mobile phone (with the banking app) is in the hands of someone who changed your settings without permission, reporting it within 2 business days of discovery caps your liability for unauthorized transfers at $50.
7. **Document the incident for your records.**
   - Note the date and time you discovered the change.
   - Save the representative's name and the "Case Number" or "Interaction ID" for the call.
   - Keep a copy of any alerts or screenshots showing the altered settings.

## What can wait
- Closing the account entirely, which may require both owners' signatures or a legal order depending on the bank's contract.
- Determining the "motive" or intent of the other account holder.
- Filing a police report, unless the bank specifically requests one to process a fraud claim.
- Changing your direct deposit settings, unless you have confirmed that funds are actively being diverted.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of panic or violation when financial access changes without notice. Banks are legally required to have "Error Resolution" procedures in place specifically for these moments. Taking immediate action to secure your login is a standard security step and does not automatically imply a legal accusation against the other holder.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for account security and federal "Regulation E" protections. It does not cover legal disputes over ownership of funds or divorce-related financial proceedings, which require legal counsel.

## Important note
Joint accounts are governed by the specific deposit account agreement signed at the time the account was opened. While federal law protects against unauthorized transfers, it often permits either owner to withdraw funds or change certain settings. If the other holder's actions are related to a legal dispute or coercion, additional state-specific protections (such as coerced debt laws) may apply.
