# What to do if… your bank notifies you it will take money from your account to cover another debt (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately to determine if this is an internal "Right of Offset" (for a debt you owe that bank) or an external "Garnishment" (a court order from a third party), then request a partial release of funds for essential living expenses.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore the notice; banks often move funds within 24 to 72 hours of notification or may have already frozen them.
- Don't use contact information from a text or email; call the verified number on the back of your debit/credit card.
- Don't impulsively transfer remaining funds to other people's accounts, as this can be flagged as fraudulent or result in further account freezes.
- Don't assume federal benefits (like Social Security) are automatically protected from an internal bank offset; while they have high protection against external garnishment, banks can often offset them for their own debts.
- Don't agree to a high-pressure repayment plan over the phone without seeing the terms in writing.

## What to do now
1. **Determine if the "taking" is an internal offset or an external garnishment.**
   - **Ask the bank agent: "Is this an internal Right of Offset for a debt I owe this bank, or is this a legal garnishment/levy from a court or government agency?"**
   - **Request the specific case number (if a garnishment) or the specific account agreement clause (if an offset) that justifies the action.**
2. **Verify if the debt is a consumer credit card balance.**
   - **If the bank is taking money for a consumer credit card debt, ask: "Do you have a signed, consensual security interest in these funds?"**
   - **Under federal law (Regulation Z), banks are generally prohibited from offsetting deposit accounts for consumer credit card debt unless you specifically granted a security interest in writing.**
3. **Notify the bank if the account contains directly deposited federal benefits.**
   - **If it is an external garnishment, the bank MUST follow the "2-month lookback" rule (31 CFR Part 212) and leave a protected amount of Social Security, VA, or SSI benefits in the account.**
   - **Ask: "Have you performed the mandatory account review for federal benefits and established my protected amount?"**
4. **Request a "Hardship Release" for immediate essentials.**
   - **State clearly: "This action will leave me unable to pay for [rent / food / medicine]. I am requesting a temporary release of funds for basic survival."**
   - **If the representative says no, ask for the "Executive Office" or "Customer Advocacy Team" rather than just a supervisor.**
5. **Protect your next incoming deposit.**
   - **Immediately contact your employer or the Social Security Administration to redirect your next direct deposit to a different financial institution where you do not owe any debt.**
   - **Opening a "clean" account at a new bank ensures your next paycheck is not automatically seized upon arrival.**
6. **Gather specific state-level exemption forms.**
   - **If it is a garnishment, search for "[Your State] bank account exemption form" to find legal papers you can file with the court to claim money as exempt (e.g., "wildcard" exemptions or wage protections).**
7. **Contact a consumer advocate or legal aid.**
   - **If the bank cannot or will not explain the legal basis for the seizure, file a complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) immediately.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to pay off the entire debt today to regain access to some funds.
- You do not need to hire an expensive attorney in the first hour; many legal aid offices or bar associations offer free guidance for bank seizures.
- You do not need to close the account entirely right now; focus on stopping the immediate drain of funds first.

## Important reassurance
Seeing money vanish from an account is a high-stress event, but it is a procedural action that can often be paused or partially reversed through specific legal and bank-internal requests. Identifying the "why" (offset vs. garnishment) is the fastest way to regain control.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hours after a notification. It does not cover long-term bankruptcy strategies or court litigation procedures.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal or financial advice. Banking laws vary significantly by state. If your account contains funds for children, business operations, or trust assets, seek qualified legal counsel immediately.
