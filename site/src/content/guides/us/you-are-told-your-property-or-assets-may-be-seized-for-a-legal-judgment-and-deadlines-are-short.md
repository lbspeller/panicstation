# What to do if… you are told your property or assets may be seized for a legal judgment and deadlines are short (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the contact as unverified until you check it directly with the court using contact details you find independently. If the case is real, use the deadline printed on the official notice and ask the court or local legal help what response is available.

## Do not do these things
- Do not make an unverified payment solely because someone is pressuring you to act immediately.
- Do not give anyone your Social Security number, bank login, gift-card details, wire-transfer details, or cryptocurrency to stop a claimed seizure.
- Do not ignore official-looking papers because you feel overwhelmed.
- Do not sign a payment agreement, release, or other document under pressure before you understand it.
- Do not move, hide, sell, or damage property in response to the notice before getting local legal advice.
- Do not assume that a threat of arrest for an ordinary civil debt is genuine. Verify the case independently.

## What to do now
1. **Keep the papers and messages together.**
   - Save the envelope, notice, email, text, voicemail, and any names or phone numbers used.
   - Do not use a link or phone number from a threatening message until you have checked it independently.

2. **Find the court through an official route.**
   - Use an official court website or an official court directory.
   - Call the clerk’s office using the number listed there.
   - Ask whether the case number is real and what case or enforcement information the court can confirm.

3. **Write down the document name and deadline.**
   - Ask what the notice is called in that court and whether it concerns property, a bank account, wages, or another type of enforcement.
   - Write down any response deadline, hearing date, or other action date printed on the official paperwork.
   - Ask whether the court has forms, a self-help service, or local legal-aid information.

4. **Identify what may be affected.**
   - Make a short list of any property, bank account, wages, or benefits mentioned in the notice.
   - Gather copies of the notice, recent bank statements, pay statements, benefit letters, and any proof that the debt was paid or may belong to someone else.
   - Keep the originals and make copies unless the court or a local legal adviser says a specific original must be submitted.

5. **Ask about any local exemption or objection process.**
   - State law and court procedures may protect some property, income, or funds.
   - Ask the court, self-help service, or local legal help what process applies, where it must be submitted, and what deadline applies.
   - Do not assume that a form or deadline from another state applies to your case.

6. **Get local civil legal help promptly.**
   - Tell the legal-aid organisation or lawyer that this concerns possible post-judgment enforcement and a short deadline.
   - Give them the court name, case number, document name, and deadline.

7. **If the judgment may be wrong, say so clearly.**
   - Tell local legal help if you were not served, the debt was paid, the amount appears wrong, or the case may involve mistaken identity.
   - Ask whether there is a way to request a pause while the issue is reviewed.

8. **If someone comes to your home, keep a safe distance and verify safely.**
   - Do not argue or obstruct anyone.
   - Ask to see identification and the paperwork.
   - Verify through the court or local agency using contact details you find independently.
   - If you feel in immediate danger, call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to negotiate a settlement, refinance, or sell property.
- You do not need to prepare a perfect legal argument before checking the case and the deadline.
- You do not need to explain your full financial history to an unverified caller or visitor.
- You do not need to make a payment decision while someone is pressuring you.

## Important reassurance
A notice about possible seizure can feel overwhelming. You can take this one step at a time: verify the case, write down the deadline, identify what may be affected, and get local help with the correct response.

## Scope note
These are immediate first steps only. Enforcement procedures, exemptions, forms, and deadlines depend on the state, court, debt type, and document, so later decisions may need specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Check the case directly with the court and use local legal help for advice about your situation.
