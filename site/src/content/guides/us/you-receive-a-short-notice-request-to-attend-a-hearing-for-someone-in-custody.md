# What to do if… you receive a short-notice request to attend a hearing for someone in custody (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the hearing through the court or the person’s attorney using contact details you find independently. Then ask whether you are ordered to attend, or whether attendance is optional.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t trust a caller, text, or email that pressures you to pay money, click a link, or give sensitive information to “confirm” a hearing.
- Don’t use contact details from a suspicious message until you have checked them against the court’s official website or a known attorney contact.
- Don’t post the person’s case details, booking number, hearing link, or courtroom details in group chats or on social media.
- Don’t go to a courthouse without checking the exact courthouse, room, time zone, and whether the hearing is in person, by phone, or by video.
- Don’t bring weapons, large bags, recording equipment, or other items that the courthouse may not allow.
- Don’t contact victims, witnesses, protected people, or anyone covered by a no-contact order to “help” or “coordinate”.
- Don’t agree on the spot to provide money, housing, transportation, or supervision unless the person’s attorney or the court has explained exactly what that means.

## What to do now
1. Find an official way to verify the hearing.
   - Search for the named court’s official website yourself, or use an official court directory.
   - Call the clerk’s office or court office and ask whether there is a hearing for that person, the date and time, the courtroom or remote access route, and whether the public may attend.
   - If you know the person’s attorney or public defender, contact that office using a number or email you already know or can independently verify.

2. Work out what kind of request you received.
   - Look for the court name, case number, courthouse address, date, time, and words such as summons, subpoena, order, notice, arraignment, detention, bail, bond, or initial appearance.
   - If the message only says “come to court now” or “join this link immediately” without clear court details, treat it as unverified until the court or attorney confirms it.

3. Ask whether you are required to attend.
   - Ask the clerk’s office or attorney: “Am I ordered, subpoenaed, or summoned to appear, or am I only allowed to attend?”
   - If you have a summons, subpoena, or court order and the notice is very short, ask the issuing office or the clerk what the correct next step is for that court.
   - If you cannot attend, tell the attorney or issuing office promptly rather than ignoring it.

4. If it is a remote hearing, get access instructions only from the court or attorney.
   - Confirm the exact link, phone number, meeting ID, passcode, time zone, and any required display name.
   - Do not use a link from an unverified text or email.
   - Join early enough to handle waiting rooms, audio problems, or a wrong link.

5. If you are going in person, reduce the chance of being turned away.
   - Bring government photo ID if you have it.
   - Bring the notice or message you received, the case number if known, your phone, a charger, and any document the attorney specifically requested.
   - Check the court’s security rules before leaving, because courts set local rules on phones, bags, electronics, and prohibited items.
   - Arrive early enough for security screening and to find the correct courtroom.

6. If you are being asked to help with release, bail, bond, housing, transport, or supervision, slow it down.
   - Ask the attorney to explain the exact request before you agree.
   - Ask whether you would be making a promise to the court, signing anything, paying money, receiving phone calls from pretrial services, or taking on any supervision duties.
   - It is acceptable to say you need the terms repeated or written down before deciding.

7. If you suspect a scam, stop the interaction and verify independently.
   - Scam signs include threats of arrest unless you pay immediately, demands for gift cards, wire transfers, payment apps, cryptocurrency, or requests for Social Security numbers or bank details.
   - End the call or message thread, find the court’s official number yourself, and ask the clerk’s office whether the contact was real.
   - If money or sensitive information was requested, report the contact through an official fraud-reporting route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now what the person should argue, what outcome is best, or how to fix the whole case.
- You do not need to gather character letters, records, proof documents, or bail money unless the attorney or court specifically says they are needed for this hearing.
- You do not need to call every courthouse at once. Start by verifying the court, hearing time, and whether your attendance is required.
- You do not need to answer pressure from friends or family about promises you have not understood yet.

## Important reassurance
A sudden court request can feel urgent and confusing, especially when someone is in custody. Taking a few minutes to verify the court, the hearing details, and your role is usually the safest way to avoid scams, wrong locations, missed remote access, or accidental promises.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for handling the immediate request. Questions about legal strategy, bail, bond, detention, release conditions, subpoenas, or what to say in court may need help from a licensed attorney in the relevant state, county, or federal court.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Court procedures vary widely by federal, state, county, court, and case type, including whether hearings are open, how remote appearances work, what courthouse security allows, and what obligations a subpoena or summons creates.
