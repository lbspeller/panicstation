# What to do if… your car battery is dead while you are parked and you need to leave soon (USA)

## Short answer
Make the location safe, then either use a jump pack, get a careful jump start, or call roadside assistance while arranging a backup ride. Do not keep cranking the engine or try a jump if the battery looks damaged, smells like sulfur, is leaking, or feels very hot.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep trying to start it over and over; it can drain the remaining power and may stress the starter.
- Don’t jump start a battery that is cracked, leaking, bulging, very hot, frozen-looking, or giving off a strong sulfur or “rotten egg” smell.
- Don’t let jumper cable clamps touch each other once any clamp is connected.
- Don’t connect the final black clamp to the dead battery if there is a safe unpainted metal ground point available away from the battery.
- Don’t try to jump an electric or hybrid vehicle unless the owner’s manual or jump-pack instructions clearly say how to do it.
- Don’t stand in traffic lanes, between vehicles, or in a place where other drivers may not see you.

## What to do now
1. **Make the parked spot safer first.** Put the vehicle in Park, set the parking brake, and turn on hazard lights if other drivers might not expect a stopped vehicle. If you are near fast traffic or feel exposed, stay out of traffic and call for roadside help rather than setting up cables there.

2. **Check for obvious battery signs.** A dead or weak battery often shows rapid clicking, very dim dash lights, weak interior lights, or no crank. If there is no power at all, look only for an obviously loose battery cable; do not touch corrosion or damaged parts while stressed.

3. **Pick the fastest safe way to leave.**
   - If you have a portable jump starter, use it exactly as its instructions say.
   - If you have jumper cables and another vehicle, do one careful jump attempt.
   - If you have no equipment, no safe helper, or any doubt about an EV or hybrid, call roadside assistance through your insurer, automaker service, motor club, or a nearby repair provider.
   - If you truly need to be somewhere soon, arrange a backup ride at the same time instead of waiting to see whether the car can be fixed quickly.

4. **If using jumper cables, follow your owner’s manual first.** If the manual and cable instructions match this common sequence, use it slowly:
   1. Park the donor vehicle close enough for the cables to reach, with the vehicles not touching.
   2. Turn both vehicles off, set both parking brakes, and turn off lights, fans, chargers, and audio.
   3. Connect one red clamp to the dead battery’s positive terminal.
   4. Connect the other red clamp to the donor battery’s positive terminal.
   5. Connect one black clamp to the donor battery’s negative terminal.
   6. Connect the final black clamp to a solid, unpainted metal part of the disabled vehicle, away from the battery and moving parts.
   7. Start the donor vehicle. After a few minutes, try starting the disabled vehicle.
   8. If it starts, remove the clamps in reverse order, starting with the black clamp on the disabled vehicle’s metal ground.

5. **If it starts, avoid getting stranded again immediately.** Keep the vehicle running long enough to reach your destination, a safer parking place, or a repair shop. Avoid shutting it off for short stops until you are somewhere you can get help again.

6. **If it does not start after a careful attempt, stop.** Check that the clamps are secure and on the correct points only if it is safe to do so. If it still will not start, switch to roadside assistance and your backup ride rather than repeated attempts.

7. **Use roadside caution if you are near traffic.** Move Over laws exist across the USA, but the exact coverage varies by state. Treat any roadside or lot edge as risky: keep hazard lights on when appropriate, stay visible, and keep your body away from moving traffic.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the cause right now, such as an old battery, alternator fault, lights left on, or a drain.
- You do not need to buy a battery before you have safely left, unless roadside help tests it and you choose that option.
- You do not need to decide on repairs while stressed; the immediate goal is safe mobility and not getting stuck again in the next hour.

## Important reassurance
A dead battery is a common sudden “I can’t leave” problem. It can feel urgent, but slowing down for a safe setup and arranging a backup ride usually gives you a workable path within a short time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about replacement, charging-system checks, towing, or repair may need a mechanic, roadside technician, dealer, or vehicle-specific guidance.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, professional, or vehicle-specific advice. Always follow your owner’s manual and the instructions on any jump starter, jumper cables, or roadside technician’s equipment.
