# What to do if… you discover your identity documents were used to apply for credit (USA)

## Short answer
Place a credit freeze with all three bureaus immediately to stop new accounts, then create an official Identity Theft Report at IdentityTheft.gov to trigger federal legal protections for blocking fraudulent debt.

## Do not do these things
- Don't pay any portion of a bill or collection notice for an account you did not open, as this can be interpreted as an admission of the debt.
- Don't provide your Social Security number or full date of birth to anyone calling you to "verify" fraud; hang up and call the lender's official fraud department directly.
- Don't ignore the situation if the application was denied; a denied application still indicates that a third party has your sensitive identity documents.
- Don't attempt to "test" your credit by applying for a small loan or store card to see if it goes through.
- Don't throw away any physical mail, envelopes, or "welcome" packets related to the fraudulent accounts.

## What to do now
1. **Place a security freeze on your credit reports with Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.**
   - You must contact each of the three bureaus individually to place a freeze.
   - This prevents lenders from accessing your credit report, which stops most new accounts from being opened.
   - Use the official websites or automated phone lines: Equifax (800-685-1111), Experian (888-397-3742), and TransUnion (888-909-8872).

2. **Create an Identity Theft Report at IdentityTheft.gov.**
   - Complete the online form to receive an official FTC Identity Theft Report.
   - This document is a federal certification that provides you with significant legal rights, including the right to block fraudulent information from your credit report within 4 business days.
   - Save a digital copy and print several physical copies.

3. **Request a "Block" of fraudulent information from the credit bureaus.**
   - Send your Identity Theft Report and proof of identity (copy of driver's license, utility bill) to the credit bureaus.
   - Specifically request that they "block" any accounts or inquiries resulting from the identity theft.
   - By law, bureaus must block this information within 4 business days of receiving your report and proof of identity.

4. **Contact the fraud department of the lender(s) where applications were made.**
   - Tell them: "I am a victim of identity theft and I did not authorize this application or account."
   - Provide them with your FTC Identity Theft Report number.
   - Ask them to close the account, stop the application, and send you a letter confirming the account is closed and that you are not held liable for the debt.

5. **Place a one-year or seven-year fraud alert if you cannot manage a full freeze.**
   - If you choose a fraud alert, you only need to contact one bureau; they are legally required to notify the other two.
   - An initial alert lasts one year; an "extended fraud alert" (available if you have an Identity Theft Report) lasts seven years.
   - This requires lenders to take extra steps to verify your identity before issuing credit.

6. **Review your full credit reports for other hidden accounts.**
   - Go to AnnualCreditReport.com to get your free weekly reports from all three bureaus.
   - Look for addresses, phone numbers, or employers you do not recognize.
   - Look for "soft" or "hard" inquiries from lenders you have never contacted.

7. **Respond to debt collectors in writing only.**
   - If a collector contacts you about a fraudulent account, tell them once that the debt is the result of identity theft.
   - Within 30 days of receiving a written notice, send a certified letter disputing the debt and include a copy of your Identity Theft Report.
   - Once notified of the identity theft, the collector must stop contacting you and notify the lender.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a local police report immediately unless the lender specifically requires it or you know the person who used your documents.
- You do not need to pay for identity theft insurance or "protection" services today; the free tools (freezes / alerts / FTC reports) are the primary defense.
- You do not need to change your Social Security number; this is an extreme measure that is rarely granted and creates significant long-term complications.

## Important reassurance
Identity theft is a common occurrence, and federal law (the Fair Credit Reporting Act) provides clear, powerful mechanisms for you to clear your name. Once you have the Identity Theft Report and the freezes in place, the immediate danger of new accounts is largely neutralized. The process of removing the entries is procedural and follows a set timeline.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate first-step actions to stop active credit fraud and trigger legal blocking protections. It does not cover tax identity theft, medical fraud, or long-term legal litigation against creditors.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. The effectiveness of these steps depends on your specific situation and the speed with which you act. If you are facing complex legal issues or harassment from collectors, consider contacting a consumer rights attorney.
