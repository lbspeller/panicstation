# What to do if… a loved one dies and their workplace asks you to collect their personal belongings (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the workplace to secure the personal belongings and not dispose of them while you identify one family contact. Ask HR what process and documents they require before collection or shipping.

## Do not do these things
- Do not make a rushed decision to discard, ship, or divide personal belongings.
- Do not have several relatives arrange separate pickups before one contact person has been agreed.
- Do not ask staff to unlock personal devices or access personal accounts.
- Do not send sensitive documents until you have confirmed the recipient and the employer’s secure method for receiving them.
- Do not assume that being next of kin automatically meets the employer’s release requirements.

## What to do now
1. **Send a brief written hold request:**  
   “Please secure the personal belongings and do not dispose of them. Please confirm whether company property will be handled separately and tell me who I should contact about the next step.”
2. **Ask for one workplace contact:**  
   Ask HR or the relevant manager who will coordinate the belongings, whether the items can be held securely, and whether there is a deadline for choosing collection or shipping.
3. **Ask for a written inventory:**  
   Ask for a list of the personal belongings from the desk, locker, or work area. Ask whether photos are available, especially for sentimental items, and where the items will be stored.
4. **Ask the workplace to separate its property:**  
   Ask HR to keep company equipment, badges, keys, tools, and security-sensitive material separate from personal belongings.
5. **Choose one family contact for the immediate conversation:**  
   That person can ask what identification or documents the employer requires before it releases personal belongings. Do not claim estate authority that has not been confirmed.
6. **Make any handover easy to check:**  
   For collection, ask for an appointment and whether photo ID is needed. Ask whether the inventory can be checked at handover and whether the workplace can provide a simple record of what was released. For shipping, ask what will be sent, how it will be recorded, and what delivery method is proposed.
7. **Check estate documentation only if it is needed:**  
   If HR requires formal authority, ask what it will accept. A person named as executor in a will may need letters testamentary issued by a probate court. If no executor is named or available to act, a court may appoint an administrator. Procedures vary by state, so check the relevant state court’s probate information.
8. **Keep pay and benefits separate from the belongings decision:**  
   If final pay or benefits come up, ask HR what paperwork and state-specific process apply. You do not need to resolve those questions during the belongings conversation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide what should happen to every item today.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements before asking the workplace to hold the personal belongings securely.
- Probate, final pay, benefits, and other notifications can be handled as separate tasks after the immediate belongings decision is settled for now.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to ask for time before making decisions about a loved one’s belongings. A secure hold, one contact person, and a written inventory can reduce confusion while you are grieving.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate workplace-belongings decision. Later decisions about estate authority, wages, benefits, or family disagreements may need state-specific information or specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employer policies and state procedures vary. Ask the workplace what it requires, and check the relevant state court’s probate information if formal estate documentation is needed.
