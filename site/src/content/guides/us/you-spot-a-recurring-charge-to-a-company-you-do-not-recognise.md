# What to do if… you spot a recurring charge to a company you do not recognise (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank or card issuer immediately to report the charge as unauthorized and request a "stop payment" or block on future transactions from that merchant. You must follow up in writing to protect your full legal rights under federal law.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore small amounts; unauthorized recurring charges often start small to test if an account is active.
- Don't assume a new card number will stop the payments; many banks use "account updater" services that provide your new card details to recurring merchants automatically.
- Don't click links in text messages or emails that claim to "help you cancel" the mystery charge.
- Don't wait for the next billing cycle to see if the charge repeats before taking action.
- Don't try to contact the merchant first if you suspect fraud or cannot verify who they are.

## What to do now
1. **Contact your financial institution immediately.**
   - **Call the number on the back of your card or your official bank statement.**
   - **Tell the representative you are reporting an unauthorized recurring transaction.**
   - **Request a "stop payment" order or a merchant block to prevent future debits.**

2. **Verify the transaction type and your dispute rights.**
   - **Ask the bank if the charge is a credit card transaction (Regulation Z), a debit card transaction (Regulation E), or an ACH transfer.**
   - **Request the specific "billing inquiries" or "dispute" mailing address for written notices.**

3. **Protect your credit card rights in writing (Regulation Z).**
   - **Send a written "billing error notice" to the issuer's dedicated billing inquiries address within 60 days of the first statement that showed the error.**
      - Include your name and account number.
      - State the dollar amount of the error.
      - Describe the error and why you believe it is unauthorized.
   - **Send this via certified mail with a return receipt to prove they received it.**

4. **Protect your bank account/debit rights (Regulation E).**
   - **Provide notice of the error to the bank within 60 days of the statement transmittal.**
   - **Confirm your oral report in writing within 10 business days if the bank requires it.**
      - Doing this is usually necessary to receive "provisional credit" (temporary refund) while the bank investigates, which they must generally complete within 45 to 90 days.

5. **Stop future automatic withdrawals (ACH).**
   - **Tell the bank you have "revoked authorization" for this specific merchant to debit your account.**
   - **Give a "stop payment order" at least 3 business days before the next scheduled transfer.**
   - **Ask if the bank requires a written follow-up for the stop payment; if so, send it within 14 days of your call.**

6. **Check for "ghost" subscriptions or descriptor confusion.**
   - **Search the exact name on the statement in a search engine.**
      - Merchants often use parent company names or abbreviations (e.g., "SYW*MEMBERSHIP" for Sears/Kmart).
   - **Review your email for "free trial" confirmations that may have expired.**

7. **Secure your online banking access.**
   - **Change your banking password and enable multi-factor authentication (MFA).**
   - **Review your contact information in your bank profile to ensure no unauthorized changes were made.**

8. **File an identity theft report if more than one charge is suspicious.**
   - **Visit IdentityTheft.gov to create a report if you find multiple unknown accounts or merchants.**
   - **Place a "fraud alert" with one of the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion).**

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the person or entity behind the charge before reporting it.
- You do not need to close your entire bank account today unless the bank advises it due to widespread compromise.
- You do not need to file a local police report unless your bank specifically requires it for the investigation.

## Important reassurance
Spotting an unknown charge is stressful, but federal regulations (Reg E and Reg Z) provide strong protections for consumers who act quickly. Most banks have established procedures for these situations, and providing written notice ensures your rights are legally preserved.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for identifying and stopping unauthorized recurring charges. It does not cover long-term credit repair or legal action against merchants for breach of contract.

## Important note
This information is based on U.S. federal regulations (Regulation E and Regulation Z) as of 2026. Individual bank policies and state laws may provide additional protections. Always follow the specific instructions provided by your financial institution's fraud department.
