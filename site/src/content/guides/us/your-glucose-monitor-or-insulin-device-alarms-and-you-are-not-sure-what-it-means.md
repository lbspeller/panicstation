# What to do if… your glucose monitor or insulin device alarms and you are not sure what it means (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the situation as a potential medical emergency by confirming your blood glucose level with a fingerstick check. Prioritize treating suspected low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) or addressing interrupted insulin delivery immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore repeated alarms or silence them to return to sleep without verifying your glucose level.
- Do not take a correction dose of insulin or extra carbohydrates based solely on a reading that does not match how you feel.
- Do not drive, operate machinery, or engage in physical exercise until you have confirmed your glucose is in a safe range.
- Do not provide food or liquid to anyone who is seizing, unconscious, or unable to swallow safely.
- Do not attempt complex device troubleshooting or "deep" settings repairs while in a potential crisis state.

## What to do now
1. **Assess your physical symptoms immediately.** If you feel shaky, sweaty, confused, weak, or have blurred vision, treat for low blood sugar immediately.
2. **Perform a manual fingerstick blood glucose check.** Use a standard blood glucose meter to verify the accuracy of the Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM) alarm.
3. **Follow the 15-15 rule if glucose is below 70 mg/dL or you feel low.**
   - Consume 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrates (e.g., 4 oz juice, glucose tablets, or gel).
   - Wait 15 minutes and recheck your glucose.
   - Repeat until the level is above 70 mg/dL.
4. **Call 911 immediately if someone is unresponsive, having a seizure, or cannot swallow.**
   - Administer emergency glucagon if available and you are trained to use it.
   - **Turn the person on their side** (recovery position) to prevent choking if they vomit.
   - If help does not arrive and they remain unresponsive, a second dose of glucagon may be given after 15 minutes.
5. **Identify the alarm type and isolate the hazard.**
   - **Low / Urgent Low:** Follow the 15-15 rule immediately.
   - **High / Hyperglycemia:** Check for ketones if glucose is over 240 mg/dL; seek emergency care if it stays over 300 mg/dL.
   - **Occlusion / No Delivery / Pump Stopped:** Assume insulin delivery has ceased; check for kinked tubing or a dislodged site.
   - **Sensor Error / Signal Loss / Calibration Required:** Treat the CGM reading as unreliable until the error clears or is resolved.
6. **Switch to a backup insulin plan if a pump failure is suspected.**
   - Use insulin pens or syringes for your basal and bolus needs as directed by your previous clinical instructions.
   - Contact your doctor's on-call line if you are unsure of backup dosages.
7. **Seek emergency evaluation at a hospital immediately if signs of Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) appear.**
   - Symptoms include vomiting, severe abdominal pain, rapid/deep breathing, fruity-smelling breath, or confusion.
   - Seek care if you have moderate-to-high ketones or cannot keep fluids down.
8. **Clear technical barriers to future alerts.** Check that phone volume is up, "Do Not Disturb" is off, Bluetooth is active, and the device has sufficient battery.

## What can wait
- Interpreting historical trend graphs or data patterns.
- Re-pairing Bluetooth sensors or performing software updates.
- Reporting the device malfunction to the manufacturer or the FDA MedWatch program.
- Changing settings like target ranges or alert thresholds.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a surge of adrenaline or confusion when a medical device alarms, particularly during the night. Focus only on the immediate safety check: verify your glucose level with a fingerstick and ensure insulin is still being delivered. You do not need to fix the device hardware to stay safe in this moment.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Specific medical targets, ketone action plans, and insulin-to-carb ratios must be provided by your healthcare team.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not replace professional medical advice. If symptoms are severe, worsening, or if you are unable to confirm your glucose levels or restore insulin delivery, seek immediate professional medical assistance.
