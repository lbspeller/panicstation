# What to do if… a key online account says your identity details were changed and you cannot tell what was altered (USA)

## Short answer
Assume an unauthorized account takeover and secure your primary email account immediately to prevent further access. Use the service's official recovery flow to sign out of all other sessions and reset all recovery phone numbers and email addresses to those you currently control.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links or call numbers provided in the alert message; navigate to the official website by typing the URL directly into your browser.
- Do not make multiple rapid password reset attempts if the first one fails, as this may trigger an automated 24-hour lockout.
- Do not delete the original alert notification, as it contains timestamps and specific "identity detail" wording required for official support tickets.
- Do not assume the account is safe if you still have access; attackers often leave your access active while they change backend recovery details.
- Do not ignore other accounts that share the same password or use the compromised account for "Sign in with..." authentication.

## What to do now
1. **Secure your master email account first.**  
   **Change your email password to a unique, 16+ character passphrase and enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) using an authenticator app.** 
   - Check your email settings for "Forwarding" or "Rules/Filters" that might be sending your mail to an attacker.
   - Review "Recent Activity" and select "Sign out of all other web sessions" to disconnect any unauthorized users.

2. **Access the affected account via a known-safe device.**  
   **Log in to the account using a device you have used successfully in the past, such as your personal phone or computer.** 
   - If you cannot log in, use the "Forgot Password" or "Hacked Account" link on the official login page.
   - For Microsoft accounts, look for the "Sign-in Helper" tool; for Apple, check if a "Recovery Contact" was previously established.

3. **Revoke all active sessions and third-party access.**  
   **Navigate to the security or privacy tab and select the option to "Sign out of all devices" or "Remove all trusted devices."**
   - Manually remove any third-party apps or "Linked Accounts" (such as Facebook or Google logins) that you do not recognize.

4. **Reset all recovery methods to your own details.**  
   **Update the recovery email address and recovery phone number even if they appear correct, as an attacker may have added a secondary "alias" you cannot see.**
   - If the service provides "Backup Codes" or "Recovery Keys," generate a new set immediately; this will invalidate any codes the attacker may have downloaded.

5. **Review all identity-related fields for subtle changes.**  
   **Systematically check your profile for changes in the spelling of your name, your physical address, your date of birth, and any "Security Questions."**
   - Look for new shipping addresses in the "Orders" or "Payment" section.
   - Note any "Pending" changes; some services (like Microsoft) have a 30-day waiting period before a security info change becomes permanent.

6. **Contact the provider’s formal security or fraud department.**  
   **Report the incident using the service's specific "Compromised Account" form and provide the exact time you received the alert.**
   - Ask support to "Roll back" any profile changes made within the last 24–48 hours.
   - If the account involves financial data, ask them to place a temporary "Security Freeze" on the account.

7. **Initiate identity theft protections if sensitive data was exposed.**  
   **Visit IdentityTheft.gov or call 1-877-438-4338 to create an official FTC Identity Theft Report.**
   - Contact one of the three credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion) to place a free one-year "Fraud Alert" on your credit file.
   - If your Social Security Number was involved, contact the IRS at 1-800-908-4490 to request an Identity Protection (IP) PIN.

8. **File a report with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3).**  
   **Submit a complaint at ic3.gov if the account was used for financial fraud, extortion, or the theft of significant personal data.**
   - Save the complaint number provided after submission for your records.

## What can wait
- Identifying the specific method of the breach (e.g., malware vs. phishing) does not need to happen in the first hour.
- Closing the account permanently is not required immediately; stabilization is the priority.
- Notifying your entire contact list can wait until you have confirmed whether the attacker sent outgoing messages from your account.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a notification that identity details have been altered is a standard security protocol designed to give you a window to react. These alerts are often triggered early enough that damage can be mitigated by following automated recovery steps. Urgency and concern are common responses to these notifications.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a digital account compromise. It does not replace the specialized recovery protocols of individual banks, government agencies, or healthcare providers.

## Important note
This information is for practical guidance and does not constitute legal or professional cybersecurity advice. Account recovery processes and interface labels are subject to change by service providers; always follow the instructions on the provider's official verified support pages.
