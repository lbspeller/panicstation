# What to do if… a co-parent has died and you need to manage an immediate child handover (USA)

## Short answer
Pause any informal change to the next handover while you check the latest signed court order and make a short written plan with the surviving parent or another person who can show current authority. If authority cannot be confirmed safely before an imminent handover, seek urgent local legal guidance; call 911 if anyone is in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not rely on an old schedule, a will, an informal promise, or a family relationship alone as proof of current authority.
- Do not send the child with an unfamiliar adult or third party unless the arrangement has been confirmed through the appropriate current authority.
- Do not conceal the child, improvise a surprise exchange, or turn up unannounced at someone’s home.
- Do not try to settle long-term custody or guardianship decisions through hurried messages.
- Do not ask the child to choose sides, explain adult arrangements, or carry messages.
- Do not call 911 solely to settle a custody disagreement. Use 911 for immediate danger.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the child’s immediate safety and the next handover time.**  
   Write down where the child is now, which adult is with them, when the next pickup or exchange is due, and whether any urgent school, childcare, medication, or transport arrangements need attention. Call 911 if anyone is in immediate danger.

2. **Find the latest signed court order and check for any later order.**  
   Locate the case number, the court name, and copies of the most recent signed documents. Look for the parts covering custody, parenting time, pickup arrangements, school or childcare exchanges, and third-party collection. Do not treat an unclear clause as permission to make a new arrangement.

3. **Identify who can confirm the immediate plan.**  
   Contact the surviving parent or another person who can show current legal authority. Keep the message brief and factual: acknowledge the death, confirm the child’s present safety, identify the next scheduled handover, and ask for a written temporary plan and any relevant court document you do not have.

4. **Make a temporary logistics plan for the next day or two.**  
   Record the child’s current safe location, the next confirmed pickup or handover, school or childcare arrangements, transport, and one contact person. Keep this plan temporary. Longer-term decisions can wait.

5. **Contact the school or childcare provider before the next pickup.**  
   Tell them there has been a family death affecting pickup arrangements. Share only the information needed for pickup safety. Ask what pickup permissions are currently recorded, what documentation they require for any change, and what procedure they use if an unfamiliar adult arrives. Do not ask the school or childcare provider to decide a custody dispute.

6. **Use local help promptly if authority is unclear or disputed.**  
   Contact the relevant state or local court clerk, a family-court self-help service if available, and local legal help. Ask whether your state has an urgent-order or temporary-guardianship route and what documents are needed. Court names and procedures vary by state.

7. **Use safety routes only when they fit the situation.**  
   Call 911 for immediate danger. If you suspect child abuse or neglect, use the appropriate state reporting route or a child-welfare referral service. For a non-emergency exchange conflict, ask local law enforcement what assistance may be available; local responses vary, and police may not resolve a custody dispute.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide the long-term custody or parenting-time arrangement today.
- You do not need to resolve estate, insurance, benefits, or probate questions before the next handover.
- You do not need to answer every family member or settle every disagreement immediately.
- You do not need to make permanent school, childcare, or transport changes while the immediate plan is still being checked.

## Important reassurance
You only need to stabilize the next handover, confirm who has current authority, and identify the next appropriate contact. A short written plan can reduce confusion while state-specific advice is obtained.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later custody, parenting-time, or guardianship decisions may need state-specific court guidance or help from a family-law professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Court procedures and authority rules vary by state. Use 911 for immediate danger and seek local legal guidance when the next handover cannot be confirmed safely.
