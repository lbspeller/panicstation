# What to do if… you feel a tingling sensation when touching a tap or metal appliance (USA)

## Short answer
Move away, stop using the tap or appliance, and keep other people away. If you can safely do it without touching anything wet or metal, turn off power to that area at the breaker panel and arrange a licensed electrician or your landlord to check it before it is used again.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep testing the tingle with your fingers.
- Do not touch the tap, faucet, appliance, plug, outlet, or nearby metal if you are wet, barefoot, or standing on a damp floor.
- Do not keep resetting a tripped breaker or GFCI and trying again.
- Do not open outlets, panels, covers, appliances, or junction boxes to investigate.
- Do not use an adapter, extension cord, or different outlet as a workaround.
- Do not assume it is only static if it happens repeatedly in the same place.

## What to do now
1. **Break contact and create space.** Step back. Keep children, pets, and other people away from the tap, faucet, appliance, outlet, wet floor, and nearby metalwork.

2. **If someone cannot let go or seems injured, call 911 now.** Do not touch a person who may still be in contact with electricity. If you can do it safely, turn off the power source; if you cannot, wait for emergency help.

3. **Stop using that water point or appliance.** Do not use that sink, faucet, shower, washer, dryer, dishwasher, refrigerator, range, or other involved item until power is off and the fault has been checked.

4. **Prefer turning power off at the breaker rather than touching the appliance.** If it seems to be one plugged-in appliance, unplug it only if you are dry, away from water, and can touch only the plug without touching metal on the appliance. If there is any doubt, leave it alone.

5. **Turn off power if you can reach the breaker panel safely.** Turn off the clearly labelled breaker for that area. If you cannot identify it and there is an ongoing shock risk, turn off the main breaker only if you can do so safely. If the panel area is wet, damaged, sparking, or unsafe to reach, keep away and call 911 or your electric utility.

6. **Leave a tripped GFCI or breaker off.** Do not reset it to see what happens. A trip may be the device reacting to a real fault.

7. **Record details without touching the area again.** Note where it happened, what you touched, whether the floor or your hands were wet, what appliance was running, and whether any GFCI or breaker tripped. A photo of the panel or GFCI from a safe distance may help.

8. **Contact the right repair help.** If you rent, tell your landlord or property manager that you felt a shock or tingling from a tap, faucet, or metal appliance and have stopped using it. Ask for a licensed electrician to check the wiring, grounding, bonding, GFCI protection, panel, and any involved appliance.

9. **Get emergency help for danger signs.** Call 911 if there is arcing, sparking, smoke, a burning smell, damaged service equipment, a downed outside line, or anyone has severe burns, chest pain, fainting, palpitations, trouble breathing, confusion, seizures, weakness, or loss of consciousness. Stay at least 20 feet away from any downed or damaged outside line.

10. **Call the electric utility if the problem may be outside your home.** Use the number on your bill or the utility’s official website if you suspect damaged service equipment, an outside line problem, or repeated shocks affecting more than one area.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to turn appliances on one by one to rule things out.
- You do not need to decide on repairs, upgrades, replacements, or responsibility today.
- You do not need to argue about fault before the immediate shock risk is controlled.

## Important reassurance
A tingling feeling can be easy to dismiss, but stopping use and switching off power is a reasonable safety step. You are not overreacting by treating a repeated tingle from metal or plumbing as a possible electrical fault.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate risk and prevent repeat exposure. Later decisions about repair, code compliance, rental responsibilities, insurance, or appliance replacement may need help from a licensed electrician, your landlord, your utility, or another appropriate specialist.
