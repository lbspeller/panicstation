# What to do if… you are pressured to work off the clock or without pay (USA)

## Short answer
Record all time you work and avoid agreeing that any work will go unrecorded. Under the federal rule, covered non-exempt employees are generally entitled to at least time-and-a-half their regular rate for hours worked over 40 in a workweek.

## Do not do these things
- Do not clock out and keep working without recording the time.
- Do not agree to leave worked time off a timesheet or timecard.
- Do not sign a statement saying worked time was voluntary, unworked, or should not be recorded while you feel pressured.
- Do not rely only on a verbal promise that payroll will be corrected later.
- Do not quit impulsively if losing income or health coverage would create an immediate crisis.

## What to do now
1. **Ask how to record the time.**  
   If you are told to clock out but keep working, respond calmly, preferably in writing: “Please tell me how to record all time worked, including any time after clocking out.” This can create a clear record of what you were asked to do.

2. **Start a personal hours log today.**  
   Record the date, start and stop times, breaks, tasks completed, who gave the instruction, and any message saying not to record time. Keep relevant schedules, texts, chat messages, timecard records, and pay stubs. Do not take confidential business information unrelated to your hours, instructions, or pay.

3. **Check your recorded hours and pay.**  
   Compare your log with your timecard and pay stub. Note any missing time, edited entries, or overtime hours that were not paid.

4. **Ask for a written correction.**  
   Send a short factual message to payroll, HR, or the person who handles time records: “I worked __ hours during the workweek ending __. Please correct my time record and confirm how these hours will be paid.” Keep a copy.

5. **Check your classification without relying only on the word salary.**  
   Being salaried does not automatically mean you are exempt from overtime. Ask in writing: “Am I classified as exempt or non-exempt, and what is the basis for that classification?” Keep the reply. Exemption rules can depend on job duties, pay basis, and other requirements.

6. **Contact an official agency if the pressure continues or pay is not corrected.**  
   You can contact the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division to ask questions or file a complaint by calling 1-866-487-9243. A state labor office may also have relevant rules or a complaint route.

7. **Document possible retaliation separately.**  
   Keep records of threats, schedule cuts, write-ups, dismissal, or messages telling you to stay quiet after you ask about hours or pay. Retaliation protections may apply when a worker asks about pay or hours, asserts rights, files a complaint, or cooperates with an investigation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to hire a lawyer or file a lawsuit.
- You do not need to settle your full legal classification before recording your hours and requesting a correction.
- You do not need to prove anyone’s intent right now. Focus on the hours worked, instructions given, records changed, and pay received.

## Important reassurance
Pressure to work unpaid time can feel intimidating, especially when it is described as teamwork or normal practice. Keeping a factual record and asking for worked time to be recorded are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce harm and preserve your options. Later decisions may depend on your duties, pay structure, workplace records, and state law, and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide provides general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Rules and available routes can vary. Consider contacting an appropriate government agency or a qualified professional before taking steps that could jeopardize your income.
