# What to do if… you are told a relative died while in custody and you do not know who to contact to confirm details (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the notice as unconfirmed until you verify it through the facility’s official switchboard and the local Medical Examiner or Coroner’s office. Do not provide personal information or money to the original caller; find the agency's public number independently to initiate a formal inquiry.

## Do not do these things
- Don't send money, gift cards, or cryptocurrency to anyone claiming there are "processing fees," "transportation costs," or "release fees" for a body or belongings.
- Don't use a "call back" number provided by the person who notified you; always find the official agency number via a .gov website.
- Don't provide your Social Security number or bank details over the phone to "verify" your identity as next of kin.
- Don't travel to a facility or mortuary until you have confirmed the location of the person and the specific official who is authorized to meet with you.
- Don't sign any documents or agree to recorded interviews regarding the circumstances of the death until the identity and death have been formally confirmed by the Coroner or Medical Examiner.

## What to do now
1. **Document the initial message immediately.**  
   **Write down the exact time of the call, the name and rank of the caller, the agency they claimed to represent, and any reference numbers provided.**
   - Include the specific facility name (e.g., "North County Detention Center") and the reported time of death.

2. **Verify the facility’s official contact information.**  
   **Use a search engine to find the main switchboard number for the jail, prison, or detention center mentioned.**
   - Ensure the website ends in .gov or is the official site of a County Sheriff.
   - Do not trust the caller ID, as numbers can be spoofed to look like government agencies.

3. **Call the facility main line and ask for the Watch Commander or Shift Supervisor.**  
   **State clearly that you have received an unconfirmed report of a death and need to speak with the officer in charge of notifications.**
   - Provide the relative's full legal name and Date of Birth (DOB).
   - Ask for the "Booking Number" or "Inmate ID" to ensure they are looking at the correct file.
   - Request the name and direct extension of the official designated as the family liaison.

4. **Search for "In-custody Death" on the agency's official website.**  
   **Many states (such as California) require agencies to post basic details of deaths in custody on their public website within 10 days.**
   - Look for "Transparency," "Public Information," or "Reporting" sections on the Sheriff or Department of Corrections site.

5. **Contact the County Medical Examiner or Coroner where the facility is located.**  
   **Ask the Coroner's office if they have accepted jurisdiction over a person matching your relative’s name and DOB.**
   - The facility is legally required to transfer deceased persons to the Coroner/Medical Examiner for an autopsy or cause-of-death determination.
   - Ask for the "Coroner Case Number." This is the most reliable way to confirm a death has actually occurred.

6. **Use specific federal or immigration channels if relevant.**  
   **For Federal Prisons (BOP), ask for the Warden's Office or the Chaplain.**
   - For Immigration (ICE), contact the local Field Office Director or the ICE Integrity Coordination Center.
   - If the relative is a non-citizen, contact their home country’s Embassy or Consulate; they are often the first to receive official notification under international treaty.

7. **Provide your contact information once the agency is verified.**  
   **Once you are certain you are speaking to the correct official at a verified government number, provide your name, relationship, and phone number.**
   - Ask: "What is the specific next step for formal identification or notification in this jurisdiction?"

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a funeral home or director in the first few hours.
- You do not need to provide a statement about the relative's medical history or background.
- You do not need to decide on legal representation or media contact right now.
- You do not need to worry about the "cause of death" immediately; official autopsies can take weeks or months to finalize.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a death notification while someone is in custody is a high-stress event that often involves confusing or conflicting information. It is normal to feel suspicious or overwhelmed. Taking the time to verify the caller's identity through official channels is a necessary safety step, not a delay in your responsibilities.

## Scope note
This guide is for the immediate first steps of confirming a death and establishing a reliable point of contact. It does not cover the investigation of the death, legal claims, or the long-term probate process.

## Important note
Notification procedures vary by state law and facility type (local, state, or federal). In 2026, many jurisdictions have moved toward automated transparency portals for death reporting, but direct verbal confirmation from a Coroner or Medical Examiner remains the gold standard for verification.
