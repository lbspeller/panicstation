# What to do if… you smell burning from your vehicle after a short drive and you are now safely stopped (USA)

## Short answer
Assume a fire risk is present: immediately turn off the engine, get everyone out of the vehicle, and move at least 100 feet away (uphill and upwind if possible) before calling 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not restart the vehicle or attempt to move it further if you see smoke or smell strong burning.
- Do not open the hood or trunk if you suspect a fire; the sudden influx of oxygen can cause a flare-up or explosion.
- Do not go back inside the vehicle to retrieve phones, purses, or personal items once you have exited.
- Do not stand in traffic lanes or between the vehicle and oncoming traffic while waiting for help.
- Do not attempt to fight a fire with water or improvised liquids, which can spread oil-based or electrical fires.
- Do not let anyone smoke or use a lighter near a vehicle that may be leaking fluids.
- Do not assume a lack of visible flames means the vehicle is safe to re-enter.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the vehicle and shut off the power immediately.** Turn the ignition to the "off" position to cut fuel flow and electrical power, then remove the keys if possible.
2. **Evacuate all occupants and pets without delay.** Ensure everyone exits the vehicle and check the rear seats quickly to confirm no one is left behind.
3. **Move at least 100 feet away from the vehicle and the roadway.** Maintain a distance of at least 100 feet (about 35-40 paces) and stay behind a guardrail or well off the shoulder to avoid being struck by other motorists.
4. **Call 911 to report a potential vehicle fire.** Provide your exact location, including the highway name, mile marker, or nearest intersection, and specify if there is visible smoke or fire.
5. **Observe the vehicle for signs of escalation from your safe distance.** Look for flickering flames under the chassis, thick black or white smoke, or the sound of popping and hissing.
6. **Alert oncoming traffic only if you can do so from a safe, off-road position.** Do not attempt to place flares or triangles if the vehicle is actively smoking or if you must enter a traffic lane to do so.
7. **Wait for emergency services or professional recovery.** Do not attempt to drive the vehicle again, even if the smell appears to fade, until it has been cleared by a professional.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific mechanical cause of the smell (e.g., stuck brake, leaking oil, or electrical short) right now.
- You do not need to contact your insurance company or a mechanic while you are still in a potentially hazardous roadside position.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of a tow or the inconvenience of the breakdown until your physical safety is confirmed.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sudden surge of adrenaline or confusion when a vehicle smells of burning. Most burning smells are caused by minor mechanical issues, but treating the situation as a potential fire is the only way to ensure the safety of you and your passengers. Taking the time to step away and call for help is a responsible and standard safety protocol.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a vehicle that is already stopped. It does not cover technical repairs, long-term maintenance, or instructions for vehicle fires involving hazardous materials or commercial tankers.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only and does not replace the judgment of first responders or certified mechanics. Vehicle fires can escalate in seconds and fuel tanks or pressurized components may fail without warning. Always prioritize distance and professional intervention over property preservation.
