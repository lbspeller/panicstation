# What to do if… you receive a civil claim notice and the deadline to respond is only days away (USA)

## Short answer
Identify the court and case number immediately, determine your exact deadline based on the date you were served, and file a written Answer or responsive motion with the court clerk before that date to prevent a default judgment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the papers under the assumption they are fake or illegitimate.
- Do not assume that calling the plaintiff’s lawyer or the debt collector stops the legal clock.
- Do not file an informal letter or email unless the specific court's rules explicitly allow it as a formal response.
- Do not admit to the claims or sign settlement papers in a rush without fully understanding the legal consequences.
- Do not attempt to file your response without the required "Proof of Service" or "Certificate of Service" form attached.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the court, case number, and the official date of service from your records.** 
   - Look at the "Summons" or "Notice of Claim" (the first page) to find the specific court name (e.g., County Civil Court, District Court).
   - Locate the Case Number or Index Number.
   - Note the exact date and time the papers were physically handed to you or found, as this starts the response timer.

2. **Calculate your exact deadline based on the rules of that specific court.** 
   - **Federal Court:** Usually 21 days after service. If you signed a "Waiver of Service," it is 60 days from the date the request was sent.
   - **State Court:** Deadlines vary by state (often 20 or 30 days). Check the text of the Summons itself for the specific number of days allowed.
   - **Calculation:** If the final day falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or legal holiday, the deadline is typically extended to the next business day.

3. **Contact the court clerk's office to ask about filing procedures and available forms.** 
   - Ask: "How do I file a response as a self-represented (pro se) defendant?"
   - Ask: "Does this court have a standard 'Answer' form for civil cases?"
   - Ask: "Can I file electronically, or must I deliver the papers in person or by mail?"

4. **Draft a formal written Answer that addresses each numbered paragraph in the Complaint.** 
   - State whether you "Admit," "Deny," or "Lack sufficient information to admit or deny" for every allegation.
   - Include any "Affirmative Defenses" (e.g., the debt is too old, you already paid, or you are the wrong person).
   - Sign and date the document clearly.

5. **Prepare and sign a Certificate of Service to prove you notified the other side.** 
   - This document states that you have sent a copy of your Answer to the plaintiff’s attorney (or the plaintiff if they have no lawyer).
   - List the method of delivery (e.g., first-class mail, hand delivery).

6. **File the original Answer and Certificate of Service with the court clerk before the deadline.** 
   - If filing in person, bring at least two extra copies and ask the clerk to "file-stamp" them for your records.
   - If e-filing, save a copy of the digital confirmation page immediately.

7. **Request a written extension from the plaintiff’s attorney if you cannot meet the deadline.** 
   - If they agree, get the agreement in writing (a "Stipulation to Extend Time") and file it with the court.
   - If they refuse, you must file a "Motion for Extension of Time" with the court before the deadline expires.

## What can wait
- You do not need to have all your evidence or witnesses ready to file the initial Answer.
- You do not need to decide on a long-term trial strategy in the next 48 hours.
- You do not need to pay the full amount claimed immediately just to stop the process.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a lawsuit is a formal process, but it is not an immediate loss. Filing a basic Answer on time is a stabilizing action that preserves your right to be heard and prevents the plaintiff from winning automatically by default.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for civil lawsuits only. It does not apply to criminal charges, evictions (which often have much shorter 3-5 day windows), or family court matters.

## Important note
Legal deadlines are strict and missing them can result in a loss of property or garnished wages. This guide provides general procedural steps; always prioritize the specific instructions found on your court summons and consult local legal aid if you are unsure of your rights.
