# What to do if… someone is repeatedly trying to get residents to open the main entrance (USA)

## Short answer
Do not buzz them in. Move to a secure place, tell building staff or management, and call 911 if anyone is being threatened, entry is being forced, or a crime seems to be happening now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t let them in because they sound convincing, say they live there, claim they are locked out, or say they are making a delivery.
- Don’t go to the lobby or entrance to confront them.
- Don’t try to physically block, follow, detain, or photograph them at close range.
- Don’t hold a secured door open for someone you do not recognize.
- Don’t share door codes, fob access, buzzer instructions, or information about which units are occupied.
- Don’t rely on a phone number, badge, or explanation provided by the person at the door without independent verification.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause first.** If you are in the lobby, hallway, garage, mailroom, or doorway area, move back into your apartment or another controlled-access area and lock the door before making calls or messages.
2. **Do not grant entry through the intercom or buzzer.** If you respond at all, keep it brief: “I can’t let you in. Please contact the resident you are visiting or building management.”
3. **Tell the building’s on-site contact immediately.**
   - If there is a front desk, concierge, doorman, patrol, or security desk, call them now.
   - If there is no on-site contact, message or call the property manager, leasing office, landlord, superintendent, or HOA contact and say that someone is repeatedly trying to get residents to open the main entrance.
4. **Call for outside help if there is immediate risk.**
   - Call 911 if the person is trying to force entry, threatening anyone, following residents inside, damaging the door, entering units or restricted areas, or you think a crime is happening now.
   - If it is suspicious but not an emergency, look up your local police department’s non-emergency number on the city or police department website and report the repeated attempts.
5. **Share only safe, basic details.** From a secure place, note the time, entrance, what they said, whether they followed anyone in, and a general description. Do not move closer to collect details.
6. **Ask management to check building records and footage.** Ask them to review any camera footage, door access logs, visitor logs, or delivery records if the building uses them.
7. **If they claim to be staff, maintenance, a utility worker, or a delivery worker, have management verify it.** Verification should use building records, a known company account contact, a bill, or the company’s official website, not a number or link provided by the person at the door.
8. **Warn nearby residents without creating a crowd.** If your building has an official resident app, desk line, or group notice system, use a calm message such as: “Someone is repeatedly trying to get residents to buzz them into the main entrance. Please don’t buzz in anyone you are not personally expecting. Management has been notified.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether the person is dangerous, confused, locked out, or lying.
- You do not need to prove intent before reporting the access concern.
- You do not need to organize neighbors, review footage yourself, change door hardware, or start a wider building security plan right now.
- Longer-term reminders about tailgating, door propping, access codes, and visitor procedures can wait until the immediate situation is stable.

## Important reassurance
It can feel awkward to refuse entry, especially when someone sounds urgent or familiar. In a secure building, only letting in people you personally know or are expecting is a normal safety boundary, not an accusation.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate moment when someone is repeatedly trying to get residents to open a secured main entrance. Later decisions about police reports, building access rules, lease issues, staffing, cameras, or security changes may need help from building management, local authorities, or another appropriate professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, security, emergency management, or other professional advice. If you feel in danger or think a crime is happening now, call 911.
