# What to do if… a relative is in hospice and you are unsure what to do when death seems near (USA)

## Short answer
Call the hospice agency’s 24/7 on-call number immediately to report that death seems imminent or has occurred. They will provide instructions for comfort care and dispatch a clinician to assist with legal pronouncement and next steps.

## Do not do these things
- Do not call 911 for a natural, expected death while under hospice care.
- Do not start CPR or life-saving measures if a DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) or POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) is in place.
- Do not attempt to force food, water, or oral medications if the person is unresponsive or having trouble swallowing.
- Do not move the body or remove medical equipment (such as oxygen or catheters) until hospice staff advises you to do so.
- Do not rush to notify a funeral home until the hospice nurse has officially confirmed the death.

## What to do now
1. **Identify your current setting and contact the appropriate staff.**
   - **If in a facility (hospice unit, nursing home, or hospital):** Use the call bell or go to the nursing station to notify staff immediately.
   - **If at home:** Locate the hospice folder or magnet and call the **24/7 on-call nurse** number.
2. **State your situation clearly to the triage nurse.**
   - Use this phrasing: *"My relative is a hospice patient and I believe they are in their final hours (or have died)."*
   - Provide the patient's full legal name and your current address.
3. **Check for and describe sensory signs of imminent death to the nurse.**
   - **Breathing:** Note long pauses between breaths (apnea) or loud, rattling sounds.
   - **Skin:** Note if hands, feet, or knees look blotchy, purple, or feel very cold to the touch.
   - **Responsiveness:** Note if they can no longer be woken up or are showing sudden restlessness/agitation.
4. **Follow the nurse's instructions for comfort medications.**
   - **Only administer "as needed" (PRN) medications** for pain, anxiety, or secretions if specifically directed by the nurse during this call.
   - Record the time and dose of any medication given.
5. **Locate and display all end-of-life paperwork.**
   - Place the **DNR and/or POLST** form in plain view (such as on the bedside table or refrigerator) for the arriving clinician.
   - Have the funeral home name and contact information ready, if a choice has already been made.
6. **Remain at the bedside and wait for the hospice clinician.**
   - If death occurs before they arrive, you do not need to do anything to the body.
   - You may speak to your relative; hearing is often the last sense to remain functional.
7. **Ensure safety if the situation changes.**
   - **Call 911 only if** there is a fire, a violent accident, or a medical emergency entirely unrelated to the terminal illness (such as a caregiver injury).

## What can wait
- You do not need to call the funeral home yourself; hospice will often assist with this timing.
- You do not need to start the medication disposal process yet; hospice staff must oversee the disposal of controlled substances.
- You do not need to notify extended family or friends in the first few minutes.
- You do not need to worry about legal certificates or estate matters right now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed or uncertain when the signs of death become visible. The hospice on-call system is designed specifically to handle these moments. You are not expected to be a medical expert; the nurse is there to guide every physical step.

## Scope note
This guide provides stabilization for the immediate hours surrounding an expected hospice death. It does not cover long-term bereavement, estate law, or funeral planning.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace the specific care plan provided by your hospice team. Protocols for the pronouncement of death vary by state and agency policy. If you believe there is an immediate safety hazard, contact emergency services.
