# What to do if… your work email is being deleted and you need to save important records (USA)

## Short answer
Get the cutoff date in writing and ask HR, IT, or your manager for a company-approved way to preserve or transfer the specific records you still need before your access ends. Keep the request narrow and do not send work material to a personal account unless your workplace expressly approves it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not forward large amounts of work email or attachments to a personal email account or personal cloud storage.
- Do not copy client, confidential, regulated, or proprietary material to a personal device for safekeeping.
- Do not delete messages, folders, chats, or files while the handover is being arranged.
- Do not assume you will be able to log in after your access ends.
- Do not use screenshots as a workaround without approval. Screenshots may still breach workplace policy or confidentiality obligations.
- Do not try to create a complete personal archive of the mailbox.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the cutoff details in writing.** Ask HR, IT, or your manager:
   - the date and time your access ends,
   - whether the mailbox and identified records can be retained under normal policy or any applicable hold process while an approved transfer is arranged,
   - who will be responsible for the records after your access ends,
   - who your contact will be after the cutoff.

2. **Make a short list of the records you still need.** Keep it specific. Include dates, subject lines, senders, folders, or system locations where possible. Useful examples may include:
   - your offer letter, job description, or signed acknowledgements,
   - performance reviews, promotion confirmations, compensation notices, or disciplinary notices,
   - benefits information, commission documents, or pay-related records,
   - specific project or client threads that need to be handed over,
   - documents needed for a current workplace issue.

3. **Ask for an approved destination and method.** Depending on workplace policy, this may be:
   - a restricted team-owned folder,
   - a shared mailbox,
   - an IT-managed archive,
   - an HR-provided copy of records HR controls,
   - another company-owned location approved for the material.

4. **Create a company-owned handover note.** List where important records are stored without copying sensitive content into the note. Include:
   - relevant folders and shared drives,
   - key email subject lines and dates,
   - systems used, such as chat, ticketing, CRM, or an HR portal,
   - the manager, project lead, or records owner for each item.

5. **Ask HR about records held outside email.** Rules on access to personnel records vary by state. Ask HR for the written process and use the official state labor office directory if you need to identify the relevant state office.

6. **Check any separate work portals before access ends.** Download only records you are allowed to keep, using the approved route. For example, pay, tax, benefits, or HR documents may be available through a separate employee portal rather than the mailbox.

7. **If you are a federal employee or recently left federal employment, use the federal records route.**
   - If you are still employed, contact your agency HR office for access to your records.
   - If you have left federal employment and fewer than 120 days have passed, contact your last employing federal office.
   - After records have been retired to the National Personnel Records Center, follow the National Archives instructions for requesting your Official Personnel Folder or SF-50.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to file a complaint, seek legal advice, or escalate outside the workplace.
- You do not need a complete archive of every message.
- You do not need to settle every disagreement about the offboarding process right now.
- You do not need to move records until the workplace confirms the approved method and destination.

## Important reassurance
You can handle the immediate problem with a short written request and a focused list of records. Taking a few careful steps now can reduce mistakes and make the handover easier to manage.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce avoidable loss of access and prevent confidentiality or policy mistakes. Later decisions may depend on your role, workplace rules, state law, and the reason you need the records. Specialist help may be useful later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Workplace policies, confidentiality obligations, records systems, and state laws vary. Keep your request narrow, specific, and in writing, and use an approved transfer route.
