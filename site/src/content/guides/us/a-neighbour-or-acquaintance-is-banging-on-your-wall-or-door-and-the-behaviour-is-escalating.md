# What to do if… a neighbour or acquaintance is banging on your wall or door and the behaviour is escalating (USA)

## Short answer
Get behind a locked door and call **911** immediately if you feel your safety is at risk or property is being damaged. Do not open the door to engage with the individual while they are in an escalated state.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door or step into a hallway to "talk it out" while they are agitated.
- Do not yell back, make threats, or return the banging as this frequently escalates the conflict.
- Do not mention or display weapons through the door or window.
- Do not follow or approach the person if they begin to walk away.
- Do not attempt to record them from outside your home or in any position that puts you within their reach.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a secure interior room and lock the door.**  
   Put as many physical barriers as possible between yourself and the person striking the door or wall. Keep your phone, keys, and a pair of shoes with you in case you need to exit through a secondary route.
2. **Call 911 if you believe they are trying to enter or if you hear threats of violence.**  
   State your exact address, including apartment or unit number, and tell the dispatcher:
   - "Someone is outside my door banging and shouting."
   - Specific descriptions of what you hear (e.g., "I hear them hitting the door with an object" or "They are shouting specific threats").
   - Whether you are alone and where you are located within the home.
3. **Contact the police non-emergency line for disturbances that do not involve immediate threats.**  
   In many US cities, you can dial **311** to reach municipal services or search for your local precinct's 10-digit non-emergency number. Request an incident number for your records even if no arrest is made.
4. **Give a single verbal instruction through the locked door and then cease contact.**  
   If you feel you must speak, say only "I am not opening the door. Please leave." Do not respond to any follow-up questions, insults, or demands they make.
5. **Barricade the door if you see the frame or locks beginning to fail.**  
   Use heavy furniture or your own weight only if you cannot safely retreat to another room. If the person successfully breaches the door, exit through a secondary door or window if possible rather than engaging.
6. **Capture evidence from a safe position behind the barrier.**  
   Use your phone to record audio or video of the banging and shouting from inside your home. Note the exact times the behavior started and stopped, and list any specific words or threats used.
7. **Notify building management or on-site security after the immediate threat passes.**  
   If you live in a managed complex or HOA, report the incident in writing. Building common areas (hallways, lobbies) are often covered by separate security protocols or surveillance that management can access for evidence.
8. **Seek guidance on a Civil Harassment Restraining Order if the behavior is repetitive.**  
   Contact your local county courthouse clerk or "self-help" center. Unlike Domestic Violence Protection Orders, a Civil Harassment order is typically designed for neighbors or acquaintances and can legally require the person to stop the banging and stay a specific distance from your door.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to move or break your lease right now.
- You do not need to have a perfect recording or "proof" to call for emergency assistance.
- You do not need to file a formal court petition tonight; the immediate goal is physical separation.

## Important reassurance
Panic, a racing heart, and shaking are normal physiological responses to a home-boundary disturbance. Taking steps to lock doors and call for help is a standard safety procedure, not an overreaction, when someone is striking your home.

## Scope note
This guide focuses on immediate physical stabilization and initial reporting. Long-term resolution may involve housing court, mediation, or civil litigation.

## Important note
This guide provides general safety information and does not constitute legal advice. Jurisdiction-specific laws regarding "stand your ground" or self-defense vary significantly by state; consult a local attorney for legal guidance.
