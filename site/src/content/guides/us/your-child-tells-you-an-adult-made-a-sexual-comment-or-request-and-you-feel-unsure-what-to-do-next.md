# What to do if… your child tells you an adult made a sexual comment or request and you feel unsure what to do next (USA)

## Short answer
Ensure your child is physically safe and separated from the adult, and do not attempt to investigate the incident yourself. Contact a professional reporting line or child protection agency immediately to receive guidance on the next safe steps.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the adult or notify them that a disclosure has been made.
- Do not delete any messages, apps, or social media accounts where the interaction took place.
- Do not repeatedly question your child or press them for specific details or "proof."
- Do not promise that the information will be kept a secret from other adults or authorities.
- Do not ask your child to show you or search for more inappropriate content or messages.
- Do not forward, re-share, or post about the messages or photos to anyone other than law enforcement.

## What to do now
1. **Ensure your child is in a safe location away from the adult immediately.** This includes stopping all visits, rides, or unsupervised contact with the individual. If the adult is currently in your home, move your child to a separate, supervised area or leave the premises if necessary.
2. **Validate your child's disclosure with simple, supportive statements.** Use neutral phrases such as: "Thank you for telling me. I believe you. You are not in trouble." Avoid showing extreme shock or anger in front of the child, as this may cause them to stop sharing.
3. **Contact a child protection professional for guidance today.**
   - If there is an immediate physical threat or the child is in danger right now, call **911**.
   - For online-related concerns (requests for photos, sexual messages), report to the **NCMEC CyberTipline** at **1-800-843-5678** or **report.cybertip.org**.
   - For general concerns or if you are unsure where to report, call the **Childhelp National Child Abuse Hotline** at **1-800-422-4453** to speak with a counselor.
4. **Document the disclosure exactly as it happened without adding your own interpretation.** Write down the date, time, and your child’s specific words as soon as you are in private. Keep this note in a secure place; do not share it on digital platforms or cloud storage.
5. **Preserve existing digital evidence by taking screenshots if it is safe to do so.** Do not go "digging" for more information or opening new files. Keep the device exactly as it is to ensure forensic integrity for law enforcement.
6. **Maintain a normal routine for your child over the next several hours.** Provide basic physical needs like regular meals and a quiet environment. Having a trusted, safe adult nearby can help stabilize the situation while you wait for professional guidance.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to press charges or pursue a legal case.
- You do not need to notify the adult's employer, family, or friends today.
- You do not need to fully understand the legal definitions of the behavior before seeking help.
- You do not need to find a long-term therapist or counselor in the first few hours.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed, confused, or fearful of overreacting. Taking immediate steps to ensure safety and contacting trained professionals is the most protective action a parent can take. You are not required to have all the answers or "prove" anything before asking for help.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Child protection laws and mandatory reporting requirements vary by state; professionals at the provided hotlines can give you jurisdiction-specific instructions.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or clinical advice. In some U.S. states, all adults are legally required to report suspected child abuse. If you suspect immediate danger, contact local law enforcement or 911.
