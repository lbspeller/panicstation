# What to do if… your personal contact details have been shared internally at work without your consent (USA)

## Short answer
Ask HR, your manager, or the relevant privacy or security contact to stop further sharing, remove the contact details from places where they are not needed, and confirm the containment plan in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send an all-staff message about it; that can spread the details further.
- Do not repost, forward, or quote the shared contact details to prove what happened.
- Do not delete emails, chat messages, screenshots, call logs, or texts connected to the sharing.
- Do not add more personal information while you are upset; keep messages short, factual, and limited to the fix you need.
- If you feel unsafe, do not try to manage it quietly by yourself; ask for specific workplace safety steps.

## What to do now
1. **Save a clear record of what happened.** Keep the email, chat post, directory page, spreadsheet, screenshot, or export where your details appeared. Note the date, time, what was shared, where it appeared, and who could likely see it.
2. **Write one containment request.** Send it to HR, your manager, and IT, security, privacy, or compliance if your employer has those contacts. Ask them to remove the information from unnecessary directories, shared drives, chats, emails, documents, or lists.
3. **Ask for access limits going forward.** Request that your personal phone number, home address, or personal email be visible only to people with a work need, such as HR or payroll. Ask whether a work phone number, work email, or other work contact route can be used instead.
4. **Ask for written confirmation.** Ask what was shared, where it appeared, who had access, when it was removed, and what will be changed to prevent repeat sharing.
5. **If unwanted contact has started, report the pattern.** Save call logs, texts, voicemails, emails, or chat messages. Tell HR or your manager that the shared details have led to unwanted contact and ask them to direct staff not to use your personal contact details.
6. **If you feel physically unsafe, ask for immediate protective adjustments.** Depending on your workplace, this may include hiding your details from directories, telling reception or security not to release information about you, using work contact details only, changing visible schedules, or arranging safer arrival and departure support.
7. **If sensitive identifiers were also exposed, use identity-theft resources.** If the sharing included a Social Security number, bank details, copies of IDs, account credentials, date of birth with other identifying details, or information that could be used to open accounts, use official identity-theft guidance and consider a fraud alert or credit freeze.
8. **If the sharing is tied to harassment, discrimination, or retaliation, keep the facts separate and clear.** Write down dates, people involved, what was said or done, and any link to unwanted contact, protected characteristics, prior complaints, or safety concerns. Report those facts through HR, EEO, compliance, or the complaint route your employer provides.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to quit, sue, contact the media, or escalate outside the workplace.
- You do not need to prove whether the sharing was malicious or accidental before asking for removal and access limits.
- You do not need to research every state privacy law right now; start with stopping further spread and getting a written account.
- If the issue involves discrimination, harassment, or retaliation connected to protected activity, outside options such as the EEOC or a state or local fair employment agency can wait until you have saved records and seen how your employer responds, unless a deadline is already close.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel exposed when personal contact details circulate at work. Asking for removal, restricted access, written confirmation, and practical safety steps is a measured response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from an employment lawyer, union representative, workplace advocate, identity-theft resource, or other specialist depending on what was shared and what happens next.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, security, therapeutic, or professional advice. Laws and workplace policies vary by state and situation. If you are in immediate danger, prioritize safety and contact emergency services.
