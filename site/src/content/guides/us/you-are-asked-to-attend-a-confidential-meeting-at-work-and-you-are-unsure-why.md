# What to do if… you are asked to attend a confidential meeting at work and you are unsure why (USA)

## Short answer
Ask for the purpose of the meeting, the attendees, and any proposed documents in writing. Attend to listen and ask questions, but do not sign an agreement, resign, or make a final decision during the meeting.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume that the invitation means you have already been dismissed or accused of something.
- Do not sign a severance agreement, separation agreement, release of claims, confidentiality clause, or non-disparagement clause during the meeting.
- Do not quit or agree to resign just to end an uncomfortable conversation.
- Do not sign merely because you are given a same-day deadline. Ask for the deadline and proposed terms in writing.
- Do not record the meeting unless you have first checked whether recording is allowed under applicable state law and workplace rules. You can ask for permission to record or make a written note afterwards.
- Do not post about the meeting publicly or use workplace systems to vent while you are still finding out what is being proposed.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the purpose and format in writing before the meeting.** Send a brief message such as: “Please confirm the purpose of the meeting, who will attend, and whether any documents will be presented for review or signature.”

2. **Check whether the meeting will include questions about your conduct or performance.** If you are union-represented and reasonably believe that questioning could lead to discipline, request a union representative before answering questions. You usually need to make the request yourself. If you are not relying on union representation, you can briefly ask whether another support person is permitted, but do not assume that the request must be accepted.

3. **Set a calm boundary at the start.** Say: “I am here to understand what is being proposed. I will review any documents outside the meeting and respond later.”

4. **Ask for clarity in plain words.** Ask whether this is a severance, separation, or settlement proposal; whether a release of claims is being requested; what terms are proposed; and what written deadline applies.

5. **Pause if questioning begins unexpectedly.** If you are union-represented and the questions may relate to discipline, say: “Before I answer questions that may relate to discipline, I am requesting my union representative.” A meeting that starts for another purpose can sometimes become an investigatory interview as it progresses.

6. **Ask for a complete copy of any proposed agreement.** Request the written deadline, the contact person for questions, and confirmation of any next meeting or response date. The Fair Labor Standards Act does not require severance pay; an agreement, benefit plan, or other applicable rule may still affect your situation.

7. **Check the wording before signing.** If you are 40 or older and a severance or termination-program agreement asks you to waive claims under the Age Discrimination in Employment Act, federal rules generally provide at least 21 days to consider an individual waiver, at least 45 days for certain group or program waivers, and 7 days to revoke after signing. Different timing rules may apply if an agreement settles an already-filed agency charge or lawsuit. Broad confidentiality or non-disparagement terms may also raise federal labor-law concerns in some circumstances, depending on your role, your employer, and the exact wording.

8. **Make a dated note after the meeting.** Record who attended, what was proposed, what questions were asked, what you said, and every deadline mentioned. Keep the invite and the documents you were given. Do not remove or forward employer information in a way that violates workplace rules or confidentiality obligations.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide during the meeting whether to accept an agreement, resign, or negotiate.
- You do not need to choose a long-term response before you understand what is being proposed.
- You do not need to respond to every issue immediately. Note any written deadline promptly and decide what review or advice you need after the meeting.

## Important reassurance
An unexpected confidential meeting can feel unsettling. Your immediate task is smaller than solving the whole situation: get clarity, listen, collect the written terms, and avoid an irreversible decision in the room.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce pressure and buy time. Later decisions may depend on whether the meeting involves a severance proposal, an investigatory interview, a workplace complaint, or another issue, and specialist help may be useful.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employment rights can depend on federal law, state law, a union agreement, workplace policies, and the wording of any proposed agreement. Consider qualified legal advice before signing a release of claims or an agreement with confidentiality, non-disparagement, arbitration, or broad waiver terms.
