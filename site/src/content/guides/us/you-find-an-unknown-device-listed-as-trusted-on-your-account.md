# What to do if… you find an unknown device listed as trusted on your account (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as possible account takeover. From a device you control, go directly to your Apple, Google, or Microsoft account settings, change the password if the account uses one, secure MFA, and sign out or remove the unknown device.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click “security alert” links in emails or texts to fix this; go directly to the account website or app settings.
- Don’t assume a “trusted” or familiar-looking device name is safe.
- Don’t remove the device and leave your password, recovery email, recovery phone, passkeys, or MFA methods unchecked.
- Don’t use a shared, work-managed, or public computer for recovery if you can use a personal device instead.
- Don’t wipe your phone or laptop first if it is your only way to approve sign-ins; you could lock yourself out.

## What to do now
1. **Use the safest login situation you can.**  
   Use your own phone or computer. If you think that device may be compromised, use another device you control and open a fresh browser window.

2. **Go directly to the account settings.**  
   Type the official account address yourself or use the official Apple, Google, or Microsoft settings app. Do not use links from alerts, texts, or emails.

3. **Secure the sign-in method first.**  
   Change the password to a long, unique one if the account has a password. Turn on or re-check multi-factor authentication. Before deleting any MFA method, passkey, security key, phone number, or recovery email, make sure at least one method you control still works.

4. **Remove or sign out the unknown device or session.**
   - **Apple:** review the Apple Account device list. If you do not recognize a listed device, remove it from the account.
   - **Google:** open Google Account Security, review “Your devices,” and sign out of any device or session you are not sure is yours.
   - **Microsoft:** use “sign out everywhere” if you think someone may have unauthorized access. Microsoft says this can take up to 24 hours. If the Microsoft devices page shows a device you do not own or use, remove it there too.

5. **Check recovery and trusted methods.**  
   Confirm that recovery emails, phone numbers, backup codes, passkeys, security keys, authenticator apps, and remembered browsers are yours. Remove anything you did not add.

6. **Check for ways the attacker could keep access.**  
   In the affected account, especially if it includes email, check forwarding addresses, mailbox rules, filters, connected apps, app passwords, and third-party access. Remove anything unfamiliar.

7. **Check for immediate money or identity impact.**  
   Review recent sign-ins, security events, purchases, subscriptions, cloud files, and saved payment activity tied to the account. If a card or bank account may have been used, contact the bank or card issuer using the number on your card, statement, or official app.

8. **Keep a small record.**  
   Screenshot the unknown device or session and any recent security-event details. Note the date, time, device name, location shown, and what you changed.

9. **Report only if it fits the situation.**  
   If there was cyber-enabled fraud, a scam, or money loss, you can report it to the FBI’s IC3. If you need consumer recovery steps for a hacked account or possible identity theft, use the FTC guidance. If someone is in immediate danger or a local crime is actively in progress, call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to reset every password you have right now. Start with the affected Apple, Google, or Microsoft account and any email account that can reset it.
- You do not need to tell everyone immediately unless messages were sent from your account.
- You do not need to factory reset devices unless there are signs of malware or an account provider, bank, or trusted technical support source tells you it is needed.
- You do not need to work out exactly how this happened before locking the account down.

## Important reassurance
This is a serious warning sign, but it is usually something you can act on step by step. An unknown device can happen after password reuse, phishing, a compromised session, or signing in on a device you forgot about; securing access first is the right priority.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent immediate harm. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if the account is tied to work, school, banking, family sharing, cloud backups, or identity documents.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or other professional advice. Account settings and labels change, so use the provider’s current official instructions if your screen looks different.
