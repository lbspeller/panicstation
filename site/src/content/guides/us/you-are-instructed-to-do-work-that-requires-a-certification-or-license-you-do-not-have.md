# What to do if… you are instructed to do work that requires a certification or license you do not have (USA)

## Short answer
Pause the part of the task that may require the credential at a safe point. State that you do not hold the certification or license, and ask for reassignment or for an appropriately credentialed person to take over.

## Do not do these things
- Do not start, continue, or sign off the part of the task that may require the credential while the requirement is unresolved.
- Do not claim that you hold a certification, license, or authorization that you do not have.
- Do not use someone else’s login, stamp, license number, or credentials.
- Do not let your name be used to suggest that you performed, supervised, or approved work that you did not.
- Do not alter records or delete messages about the instruction.
- Do not turn the conversation into an argument. Keep it factual and brief.

## What to do now
1. **Pause at a safe point.** Step back from the task or equipment before starting anything you cannot safely undo.
2. **State the limitation clearly.** Say: “I do not hold the certification or license required for this work, so I need an appropriately credentialed person to take over or a different assignment.”
3. **Ask for a qualified alternative.** Request reassignment or ask for a person who holds the required credential to perform the relevant part. Do not assume that supervision makes it permitted. Ask your workplace compliance contact to confirm the allowed arrangement before continuing.
4. **Create a short written record.** Send a message or email stating what you were asked to do, the credential you do not hold, and the alternative you requested.
5. **Check the task requirement.** Ask for the written procedure, training record, authorization list, or rule that applies to the task. If the requirement cannot be confirmed quickly, keep the part that may require the credential paused.
6. **Escalate internally if pressure continues.** Contact the appropriate supervisor, safety contact, compliance contact, HR contact, or union representative. Repeat the same brief facts.
7. **Treat immediate danger separately.** Move to a safer pause and call 911 if anyone is in immediate danger. A missing credential alone may not be an OSHA safety complaint. For a serious workplace hazard, OSHA’s protected refusal rules are narrow; use the official right-to-refuse guidance or contact OSHA promptly.
8. **Use the appropriate complaint route if needed.** OSHA provides separate routes for safety-and-health complaints and whistleblower complaints. Coverage and routing may depend on your state and type of employer. If you believe you faced retaliation for raising a safety concern, file promptly. A safety-and-health retaliation complaint under Section 11(c) of the OSH Act generally must be filed within 30 days of the retaliatory action; other whistleblower laws may have different deadlines.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to quit, hire a lawyer, or make a formal complaint.
- You do not need to settle every licensing question in the moment.
- You do not need to confront coworkers or make a public accusation.
- You can focus first on pausing the relevant work, requesting a qualified alternative, and keeping a short written record.

## Important reassurance
Pausing is a practical first step. You do not need to solve the whole situation while you are under pressure, and asking for an appropriately credentialed person or a different assignment is a clear way to buy time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Requirements vary by task, industry, and state. Later decisions may need role-specific guidance from a workplace compliance contact, union representative, relevant licensing or certification body, or employment adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If anyone is in immediate danger, move to safety if you can and call 911.
