# What to do if… a sump pump alarm sounds and water is rising in your basement (USA)

## Short answer
Stay out of standing water and keep everyone away from the basement. If you can reach the main power switch from a dry location, switch off the power; if you cannot, call a qualified electrician and an emergency plumber. If there is smoke, sparking, or a gas smell, leave the home and call 911 from outside.

## Do not do these things
- Do not step into standing water if it may be contacting outlets, cords, appliances, or the breaker-panel area.
- Do not enter standing water to reach the service panel or main power switch.
- Do not touch plugs, outlets, cords, switches, appliances, or sump-pump equipment if anything is wet or you are unsure.
- Do not keep resetting a breaker that trips again.
- Do not reach into the sump pit.
- Do not use a corded wet/dry vacuum, fan, dehumidifier, or other plug-in equipment until a qualified electrician has said the electrical system is safe.
- Do not let children or pets into the affected area.
- Do not run a generator, pressure washer, or other gasoline-powered engine inside a home, basement, or garage, or within 20 feet of a door, window, or vent.
- Do not assume the water is clean. Avoid contact where possible and wash with soap and clean water after contact.

## What to do now
1. **Make a safer pause.** Keep people and pets upstairs. Stay away from the basement entry if the water is rising quickly, you cannot see what the water has reached, or water is near electrical equipment.

2. **Switch off power only from a dry location.**
   - If you can reach the main power switch at the service panel without stepping into water and the panel area is dry, switch off the power.
   - If standing water blocks safe access, do not enter it. Call a qualified electrician.
   - Do not switch the power back on until a qualified electrician has checked the system if water reached electrical circuits or equipment.

3. **Leave immediately if there may be a fire or gas leak.**
   - If you smell gas, see sparking, or see smoke, leave the home without using lights, switches, or anything else that could create a spark.
   - From outside, call 911 for immediate danger. For a suspected gas leak, also contact the gas company.

4. **Check only what you can see from a dry location.**
   - Check whether there is a wider power outage.
   - If you have a battery-backup or secondary-pump alarm panel in a dry area, read its status display.
   - Look for an obvious kink or blockage in a visible discharge hose only if you can do so without entering water or touching wet equipment.
   - Do not touch the plug, outlet, cord, float switch, or pump if there is any moisture or uncertainty.

5. **Call for situation-specific help early.**
   - Call an emergency plumber and say: “The sump pump alarm is sounding and the basement water is rising. There may be an electrical hazard.”
   - If you rent, notify your landlord or property manager immediately.
   - If water has already spread through finished areas, contact a water-damage restoration company after the area is electrically safe.

6. **Move essentials only if the route is dry and safe.**
   - Move documents, electronics, and small valuables to a higher floor only if you can reach them without entering water, approaching wet electrical equipment, or delaying calls for help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact pump fault while water is rising.
- You do not need to reach into the sump pit or test wet equipment.
- You do not need to start demolition, deep cleaning, or mold treatment until the area is electrically safe and the water level is controlled.
- You do not need to decide on pump upgrades, backup systems, or generator purchases now.
- You do not need to complete insurance paperwork before dealing with immediate safety and the rising water.

## Important reassurance
Stopping at a dry, safe point is useful action. You do not need to enter the basement or solve the pump fault yourself while water is rising.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment the alarm sounds and water is rising. Later decisions about drying, cleanup, mold, repairs, and insurance may need help from qualified specialists.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Floodwater and electricity can be dangerous together. If you are unsure whether the basement is safe to enter, stay out until a qualified electrician has confirmed that the electrical system is safe.
