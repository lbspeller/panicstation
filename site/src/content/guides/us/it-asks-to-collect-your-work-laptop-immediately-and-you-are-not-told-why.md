# What to do if… IT asks to collect your work laptop immediately and you are not told why (USA)

## Short answer
If the laptop is company-issued or IT is carrying out an authorized collection of work equipment, hand it over calmly without changing anything on the device. Ask for a written, itemized receipt and ask HR in writing to confirm your work status and next point of contact.

## Do not do these things
- Do not refuse an authorized collection of company-issued equipment or get into a confrontation.
- Do not wipe, reset, delete, move, copy, or “clean up” files, messages, histories, or accounts.
- Do not email work files or screenshots to a personal account.
- Do not try to regain access after you have been told not to use a device, account, or system.
- Do not send long messages guessing why this is happening.
- Do not share confidential material or agree immediately to a discussion restriction that you do not understand. Ask for any instruction in writing.
- Do not sign a document you do not understand. Ask whether your signature only confirms receipt, request a copy, and ask for time to review where possible.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the collection instruction.** Ask IT who authorized the pickup, whether HR is the point of contact, and whether the instruction can be confirmed by email or ticket update.
2. **Get an itemized handover receipt.** Note the date, time, collector’s name, location, laptop serial number or asset tag, and each item taken, such as a charger, dock, badge, or phone. Ask for written confirmation of what was collected and when.
3. **Document condition without exposing information.** Ask permission before taking any photos. Do not photograph the screen, documents, or confidential material.
4. **Do not use the laptop after the collection request unless IT instructs and observes a specific action.** Ask how any personal items, such as a detached USB drive or paper notes, should be returned to you. Tell HR or IT in writing if personal accounts may still be signed in.
5. **Ask HR for a short written status update.** Ask whether you are expected to work today, whether you are being placed on leave or suspension, whether it is paid or unpaid, who your single point of contact is, and whether any limits apply to your access or communications.
6. **Ask for representation before an investigatory interview where applicable.** If you are union-represented and reasonably believe questioning could lead to discipline, ask for your union representative before answering. Under current NLRB guidance, you must make the request; the employer does not have to prompt you. If you are not union-represented, you may still ask for a pause, a support person, written questions, or permission to take notes.
7. **Start a private factual timeline away from work systems.** Record what was said, who said it, when it happened, what was collected, any access changes you noticed, and who was present.
8. **Ask one practical pay question if termination seems possible.** Ask when final wages would be paid and how you would receive benefits or separation information. Federal law does not require an immediate final paycheck, and state rules vary. If the regular payday for your last pay period passes without payment, contact the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division or your state labor office.
9. **Request a controlled route for urgent personal needs.** Ask HR how to obtain personal items, pay stubs, or other personal records without attempting to regain system access.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, threaten legal action, or write a long defense.
- You do not need to guess why the laptop is being collected.
- You do not need to negotiate severance or sign a release while you are overwhelmed. Ask for a copy and review time where possible.

## Important reassurance
The collection alone does not tell you why this is happening or establish your employment status. A calm handover, a clear receipt, and a short written request for your status are enough for the immediate moment.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours only. Later decisions may depend on employer policy, a union agreement if applicable, and state law, and may need specialist help.
