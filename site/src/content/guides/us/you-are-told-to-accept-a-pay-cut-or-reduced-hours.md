# What to do if… you are told to accept a pay cut or reduced hours (USA)

## Short answer
Request a written commitment stating the exact new pay date and amount, and ask for an immediate manual check or off-cycle payment to cover essential expenses.

## Do not do these things
- Do not accept "we are working on it" or "soon" without a specific date and payment method in writing.
- Do not quit your job or stop attending shifts abruptly, as this may complicate your legal standing regarding wage recovery.
- Do not assume federal rules are the only ones that matter; state payday frequency laws are often stricter and provide more immediate penalties.
- Do not sign any document that waives your right to future liquidated damages or penalties in exchange for the late payment.
- Do not ignore your bank account; monitoring for "pending" status can help confirm if a delay is at the bank level or the employer level.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the exact amount and the original scheduled payday.** Locate your latest pay stub or offer letter to verify the expected net pay and the specific date the funds were due according to your employment agreement.
2. **Request a written statement from HR or management regarding the failure.** Ask for an email or formal letter that includes the reason for the delay, the specific date the funds will be available, and the method of payment (e.g., direct deposit or paper check).
3. **Ask for an immediate manual check or off-cycle deposit for essential costs.** Explain if the delay prevents you from paying for housing, food, or medication, and request that the employer issue a "live" check today rather than waiting for the next automated cycle.
4. **Identify your state’s specific payday timing requirements.** Locate your state Department of Labor (DOL) website to determine the maximum allowable time an employer can hold wages after a pay period ends; many states require payment within 7 to 14 days.
5. **Document all expected financial penalties caused by the delay.** Note any upcoming late fees for rent, utilities, or credit cards, and save screenshots of overdraft notices to present to your employer for potential voluntary reimbursement.
6. **Verify your bank account details and direct deposit status.** Check with your financial institution to see if a "reversal" or "failed transfer" message is visible, which can help determine if the error was a transmission failure or a lack of initiated funds.
7. **Notify your landlord or major creditors if a payment will be missed.** Use the written statement from your employer as evidence of the delay when requesting a brief grace period or fee waiver from your service providers.
8. **Check if your state allows for "liquidated damages" or waiting time penalties.** Some jurisdictions, such as California or Washington, may entitle you to additional daily pay for every day the wages remain unpaid after the legal deadline.
9. **Maintain a chronological log of all communications and promises.** Keep copies of every email, text, or memo regarding the payroll failure, along with a record of when the payment finally arrives.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a formal wage claim or lawsuit in the first 24 hours while the employer is attempting to rectify the error.
- You do not need to determine if the payroll vendor or the employer is at fault; your legal relationship is with the employer, and they remain responsible regardless of third-party outages.
- You do not need to decide on long-term career changes today; focus on securing the immediate funds owed for the current pay period.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant stress or panic when expected funds do not arrive, especially if you have immediate financial obligations. Asking for clear dates and a manual check is a standard professional response to a payroll failure and does not reflect poorly on your work performance.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a missing or delayed paycheck due to administrative or system failure. It does not cover long-term wage theft, employer bankruptcy, or independent contractor contract disputes.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Wage and hour laws vary significantly by state and employment classification (exempt vs. non-exempt). If you are not paid within the legal timeframe mandated by your state, or if your employer is unresponsive, contact your state's Department of Labor or a qualified employment attorney.
