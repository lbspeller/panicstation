# What to do if… police contact you and ask for your side of the story (USA)

## Short answer
Do not give a rushed explanation or agree to a voluntary interview on the spot. Say clearly that you are not answering questions and want a lawyer. If the contact is by phone, verify the caller independently before doing anything else.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to talk your way out of it, explain, or “clear it up” in real time.
- Do not guess, speculate, lie, or provide false information or false documents.
- Do not volunteer names, timelines, locations, or messages.
- Do not consent to a search just to show that you have nothing to hide.
- Do not voluntarily provide passwords, unlock a device, or agree to device access before getting legal advice.
- Do not physically interfere if officers proceed with a search or arrest.
- Do not agree to a voluntary “quick chat” or interview before getting legal advice.
- Do not pay money, provide financial information, or follow payment instructions to “resolve” a report.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the interview.** Do not discuss the allegation on the spot. Say:
   - “I am not answering questions. I want a lawyer.”
2. **If the contact is by phone, verify the caller independently.** Ask for the caller’s name, agency or department, badge number if applicable, and case number. Do not discuss the allegation. End the call, find the agency’s main number on its official website, and call that number yourself.
3. **If the caller threatens arrest, demands payment, or asks for personal or financial information, end the call.** Treat it as a suspected impersonation attempt and verify the claim through an independently found official number.
4. **If officers are physically present, ask whether you are free to leave or end the conversation.** Say:
   - “Am I free to leave or end this conversation?”
   If the answer is yes, end the encounter or leave calmly if it is safe and practical. If the answer is no, ask:
   - “Am I being detained?”
5. **Handle identification requests carefully.** Ask whether you are required to provide identification. Rules vary by state and situation. If you are driving and stopped by police, provide the required driving documents. Do not give false information or false documents.
6. **Use the same clear script if substantive questioning continues.** Say:
   - “I am not answering questions. I want a lawyer.”
   Do not rely on silence alone. Do not wait for a Miranda warning before declining substantive questions.
7. **If officers ask to search you, your property, or a device, state your position calmly.** Say:
   - “I do not consent to a search.”
   Keep your hands where officers can see them when safe, avoid sudden movements, and do not physically resist if officers proceed.
8. **Write down the contact as soon as it is safe.** Note the date and time, the caller or officer’s name, agency, case number, contact details, what was requested, and any stated deadline.
9. **Contact a criminal defense lawyer who can advise on the relevant jurisdiction.** If you are arrested and cannot afford a lawyer, ask the court how to request appointed counsel. Availability and process depend on the case and jurisdiction.
10. **Protect records and avoid creating confusion.** Until you have legal advice:
   - Do not delete or alter messages, files, or account records.
   - Do not contact the reporting person or possible witnesses to discuss the allegation.
   - Do not post about the situation or discuss it in messages, email, or social media.

## What can wait
- You do not need to give your full story during the first contact.
- You do not need to prepare a detailed timeline immediately.
- You do not need to agree to a voluntary interview before getting legal advice.
- Do not ignore a warrant, subpoena, summons, court paper, or stated deadline. Get prompt legal advice about any formal document.

## Important reassurance
The urge to explain everything immediately is common. Pausing, declining substantive questions, and asking for a lawyer are practical protective steps. You can take the situation seriously without making rushed decisions.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the first safe pause after sudden police contact. Later decisions may depend on the allegation, whether the contact is voluntary, whether you are detained or arrested, and the law in your state. A qualified lawyer may need to advise you.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If there is an active emergency or immediate danger, call 911.
