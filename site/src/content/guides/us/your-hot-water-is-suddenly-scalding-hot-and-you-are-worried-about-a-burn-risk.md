# What to do if… your hot water is suddenly scalding hot and you are worried about a burn risk (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using hot taps, showers, and baths for now. Keep children and vulnerable adults away from hot-water fixtures, and reduce or shut off the water heater only if you can do that safely without opening panels or touching wiring or gas parts.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using the shower or bath and “just turn it colder”; temperature swings can be sudden.
- Don’t rely on a quick hand-test alone to decide it is safe, especially for babies, children, older adults, or anyone with reduced sensation.
- Don’t repeatedly adjust controls back and forth to see if the problem fixes itself.
- Don’t open access panels, touch wiring, relight a pilot, or work on gas parts unless you are qualified.
- Don’t ignore a gas smell, a carbon monoxide alarm, or symptoms such as headache, dizziness, nausea, weakness, confusion, chest pain, fainting, or unusual sleepiness.
- Don’t put ice, butter, or greasy creams on a burn.

## What to do now
1. **Make immediate use safe.**  
   Tell everyone in the home: “Hot water is unsafe right now.” Keep children and vulnerable adults away from hot taps, showers, and baths. Put a note on affected faucets if that helps.

2. **Stop using heated water.**  
   Use cold water only. Do not shower, bathe, wash a child, or fill a tub from the hot tap until the system has been checked or the hot-water temperature is clearly back under control.

3. **Reduce the heat source only if you can do it safely.**  
   If the temperature control is visible and you can use it without opening panels, touching wiring, or handling gas parts, turn the water heater down toward about 120°F. If you know how to shut the heater off safely, you can do that instead. If you are not sure, leave it alone and move to getting help.

4. **If there is a gas smell, a carbon monoxide alarm, or possible carbon monoxide symptoms, switch priorities.**  
   Get everyone into fresh air immediately. Do not use flames, light switches, appliances, or phones inside the home. From outside, call 911 or the gas utility emergency line, and do not go back in until responders or the utility say it is safe.

5. **Get repair help lined up.**  
   If you rent, report it immediately to the landlord or property manager as an urgent scald-safety issue. If you own, contact a licensed plumber or other qualified water-heater professional. Say: “The hot water suddenly became much hotter than normal, so we stopped using it because of scald risk.”

6. **If anyone has been scalded, start burn first aid.**  
   Cool the burn under clean, cool running water for about 20 minutes if available, and remove nearby jewelry or clothing unless it is stuck to the skin. Seek medical care promptly for a child burn, blistering, a large burn, a burn on the face, hands, feet, joints, neck, or genitals, visible deeper tissue, or any burn in someone very young, older, pregnant, or medically fragile. Call 911 for a severe burn, collapse, breathing trouble, confusion, or any immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the problem is the thermostat, water-heater control, mixing valve, anti-scald valve, or plumbing layout right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to replace the water heater or upgrade the system.
- You do not need repeated temperature tests to prove the problem.
- You do not need to argue about fault or cost before making the hot water unavailable and asking for professional help.

## Important reassurance
Stopping hot-water use is a reasonable first move when the temperature suddenly becomes unsafe. You are not overreacting; you are preventing a fast injury while buying time for the system to be checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce scald risk and avoid making the problem worse. A qualified professional can decide what needs checking, which may include the heater controls, thermostat, mixing valve, or anti-scald devices.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not professional repair, medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. For suspected gas, carbon monoxide, or serious burns, prioritize emergency services and urgent medical care.
