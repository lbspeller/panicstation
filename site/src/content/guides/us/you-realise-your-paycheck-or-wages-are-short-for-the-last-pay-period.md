# What to do if… you realise your paycheck or wages are short for the last pay period (USA)

## Short answer
Check the pay stub against your own hours, rate, overtime, and deductions, then report the shortfall to payroll, HR, or your manager in writing. Ask them to confirm the corrected amount and when and how it will be paid.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume it will fix itself next payday without written confirmation of what they found and what they will do.
- Do not sign a waiver, pay adjustment, or new pay-rate document you do not understand while you are upset or rushed.
- Do not turn it into a public fight before you have documented the pay period, the amount, and who you contacted.
- Do not accept cash back, informal IOUs, or anything that avoids a proper payroll correction and pay stub record.
- Do not quit, stop showing up, or refuse work as your first move unless you are unsafe or have advice specific to your situation.

## What to do now
1. **Check only the last pay period first.**  
   Compare your pay stub with your own records for the pay period dates, hours worked, hourly rate or salary amount, overtime, tips, commissions, bonuses, and deductions. Write down the exact amount you think is missing.

2. **Save proof that is yours to keep.**  
   Save your pay stub, timecard, schedule, clock-in and clock-out records, shift messages, and written approvals for overtime or extra shifts. Do not take confidential company information that is not about your own pay or time.

3. **Report the underpayment in writing.**  
   Send a short message to payroll, HR, or your manager: “My paycheck for the period ending [date] appears short by [$X]. It looks like [Y hours / overtime / rate / commission / deduction] may be wrong. Please confirm the corrected calculation and when it will be paid.”

4. **Ask for a clear correction plan.**  
   Ask them to confirm:
   - the corrected hours, rate, overtime, or deduction
   - whether they can issue an off-cycle correction or when the correction will be included in payroll
   - whether the pay stub will be corrected so taxes and deductions are recorded properly

5. **Escalate internally if you do not get a clear answer.**  
   Send a brief follow-up to HR, payroll leadership, or the next appropriate manager. Attach your pay stub and your own supporting records, and restate the pay period, the amount you believe is missing, and the correction you are asking them to check.

6. **Contact wage enforcement if it is not being fixed.**  
   You can contact the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division to ask about a wage complaint. The WHD complaint line is 1-866-487-9243. Many states also have their own wage claim or labor office process, so check the route for your state as well.

7. **Document any retaliation concerns.**  
   If your hours are cut, you are threatened, disciplined, or treated worse after raising the pay issue, write down dates, times, names, and what happened, and save messages. Retaliation for asking about pay, asserting wage rights, filing a complaint, or cooperating with an investigation can be prohibited under laws the Wage and Hour Division enforces.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to hire a lawyer or sue.
- You do not need to audit every old paycheck right now; start with the last pay period.
- You do not need to resign or make major career decisions while you are in the first wave of stress.
- You do not need to prove the whole case before asking payroll to check the error.

## Important reassurance
A short paycheck can feel urgent and frightening, especially when bills are due. Many underpayments are payroll or timekeeping errors that can be corrected once you give clear dates, numbers, and records.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilize the situation, document the underpayment, and avoid costly mistakes. If the underpayment repeats, involves overtime or minimum wage, affects several workers, or you face retaliation, later decisions may need specialist help for your state and job type.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, employment, or professional advice. Wage rules, deadlines, coverage, and complaint routes can differ by state, job type, employer, and pay arrangement.
