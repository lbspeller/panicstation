# What to do if… you are asked to meet with police and are unsure if you are a witness or a suspect (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before agreeing to a meeting or answering questions. Ask whether the contact is voluntary, and say that you want to speak with a criminal defense lawyer before any interview or statement.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to a voluntary meeting merely to clear things up before you have legal advice.
- Do not answer substantive questions about the incident, timelines, people, messages, or your whereabouts while you are trying to work out your status.
- Do not lie, guess, or fill gaps in your memory under pressure.
- Do not consent to a search or voluntarily hand over your phone, passwords, or documents solely because you are asked or feel rushed.
- Do not delete, edit, hide, or destroy messages, documents, call logs, photos, or other existing material.
- Do not contact other people involved to compare accounts or coordinate what to say.
- Do not sign a statement or written summary before a lawyer has reviewed it.
- Do not ignore a subpoena, summons, court order, or similar legal paper.
- Do not physically resist or obstruct officers if they detain you, arrest you, or carry out a search.

## What to do now
1. **Limit the first contact to logistics.** Note the caller’s name, agency, phone number, email address, the proposed date and place, and any case or reference number. Do not discuss what happened.

2. **Ask whether the request is voluntary.** Ask:
   - “Is this meeting voluntary?”
   - “Am I required to attend?”
   - “In what capacity are you asking to speak with me?”
   Write down the answers. A label such as “witness” may not settle your legal position.

3. **Decline substantive questions for now.** Say:
   - “I am not answering questions at this time.”
   - “I want to speak with a lawyer before any interview or statement.”
   You do not need to explain or argue.

4. **Contact a criminal defense lawyer promptly.** Send the lawyer the request, your notes, and any document you received. Ask the lawyer to confirm whether the meeting is voluntary and to communicate with the investigator for you.

5. **Preserve existing material.** Keep the original request, voicemails, emails, texts, envelopes, documents, and call logs. Do not delete or alter anything, and do not voluntarily send material to investigators before getting advice.

6. **Treat legal papers separately from an informal request.** If you receive a subpoena, summons, court order, or similar legal paper, do not ignore it. Contact a lawyer promptly and check the document for instructions and deadlines.

7. **If officers are carrying out a search, detention, or arrest, do not physically resist or obstruct them.** If it is safe to do so, say calmly that you do not consent to a search and that you want a lawyer. Note the details later when you are in a safe position to do so.

8. **If you are already at a police setting, ask whether you are free to leave.** If the answer is yes, leave calmly. If the answer is no, say that you want a lawyer and that you are remaining silent. Do not physically resist. You may be required to provide your name in some states.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide during the first contact whether to attend a voluntary interview or give a statement.
- You do not need to explain, justify, or defend yourself while you are arranging legal advice.
- You do not need to decide whether to provide documents, messages, passwords, or consent to a search merely because someone asks.
- Longer-term decisions about cooperation can wait until a lawyer has reviewed the request. Any legal paper with instructions or a deadline needs prompt review.

## Important reassurance
An unexpected request to meet with investigators can feel alarming. Pausing to get legal advice is not an admission of guilt, and it can help prevent rushed decisions that are difficult to undo.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A lawyer may need to advise you on your specific situation, including whether a meeting is voluntary, whether any document requires a response, and how state or federal rules may apply.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Rules and procedures can vary by state, agency, and the type of contact.
