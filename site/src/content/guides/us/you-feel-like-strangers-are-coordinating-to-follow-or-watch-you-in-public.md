# What to do if… you feel like strangers are coordinating to follow or watch you in public (USA)

## Short answer
Move into a staffed, public place and ask staff or security to help you call 911 if needed. If you feel in immediate danger or think a crime may be in progress, call 911 now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront them, argue, or try to “catch them out”.
- Don’t go straight home, to your car, or to a secluded parking area if you think you may be followed.
- Don’t move into an alley, stairwell, empty lot, quiet platform, or isolated hallway to “get away faster”.
- Don’t pull out cash, unlock banking apps, or display valuables if you suspect a distraction or theft setup.
- Don’t let embarrassment stop you from asking for help.

## What to do now
1. **Move to staffed help.**  
   Walk into the nearest staffed public place, such as a store, café, hotel lobby, transit help point, building front desk, or security desk. Stay near staff, a counter, or visible security.

2. **Use a simple script that gets action.**  
   Say: “I feel unsafe. Two people seem to be coordinating around me. Can you call security or 911 and let me wait here?” If you are in a mall, transit hub, campus, or large building, ask for security to meet you where you are.

3. **Call 911 if you feel threatened or think a crime may be happening.**  
   Give your current location first. Say what you can see now, what made you feel unsafe, and whether the people are still nearby. Follow the call-taker’s instructions and do not hang up until they tell you to.

4. **Text 911 only if you cannot safely call.**  
   Text-to-911 is not available everywhere. General rule: call if you can, text if you can’t. If text-to-911 is not supported where you are, you may receive a bounce-back message telling you to contact 911 another way.

5. **Make yourself harder to isolate while you wait.**  
   Stay in a busy, well-lit place. Keep your bag closed and in front of you. Keep your back toward a wall or counter if that helps you feel steadier. If you call someone you trust, state your current location and ask them to stay on the line.

6. **Do not lead anyone to your next private destination.**  
   If you were heading home, to your car, or to a hotel room, pause that plan. Stay with staff or security, or move with help to another staffed location.

7. **Note details only from a safer pause.**  
   Without staring or following them, write down clothing, approximate height and build, distinguishing features, direction of travel, and any vehicle make, model, color, or plate if you can see it safely.

8. **If you are safe but still want it logged, ask about local reporting.**  
   Ask staff or security to help you find the local police non-emergency number or local online reporting option. These routes vary by city and county.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide right now whether you were “right” about their intentions.
- You don’t need to confront them or gather proof.
- You don’t need to change your routines tonight or make big life decisions.
- You don’t need to post about it or warn others online in the moment.

## Important reassurance
Feeling singled out by coordinated behavior can make your body go on high alert. Moving to staffed help and involving security or 911 when needed is a practical way to protect yourself without escalating.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate moment. If this may be repeated targeting, stalking, or harassment, later decisions may need specialist help, local victim support, or legal guidance.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, mental health, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in danger or a crime is in progress, call 911.
