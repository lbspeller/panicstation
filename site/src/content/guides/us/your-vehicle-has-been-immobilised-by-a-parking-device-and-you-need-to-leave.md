# What to do if… your vehicle has been immobilised by a parking device and you need to leave (USA)

## Short answer
Do not try to remove, damage, or drive with the device attached. Start the official release process using the notice on the vehicle, and arrange another way to leave now if you cannot wait.

## Do not do these things
- Do not cut, pry, smash, pull, or drive against the device; this may cause damage, towing, extra fees, or legal trouble.
- Do not pay cash to someone who approaches you unless you can independently verify they are the listed operator or property representative.
- Do not use a website or phone number from a random search result if the vehicle notice, posted signs, or official parking agency gives a different route.
- Do not ignore a tow warning on the notice; booted vehicles may be towed quickly in some places.
- Do not argue with an attendant or enforcement worker. Keep the contact brief, calm, and documented.

## What to do now
1. **Move yourself to a safer spot first if needed.**  
   If you are in traffic, in an unsafe area, or feeling threatened, step away from the vehicle to a nearby public, well-lit place before making calls. If there is immediate danger or a medical emergency, call 911.

2. **Check the notice and the device.**  
   Look for a printed notice, sticker, or screen showing the agency or company name, phone number, website, device ID, plate number, payment route, and any tow warning. Also check nearby parking signs for the same operator, property, or agency name.

3. **If the notice is missing or looks suspicious, verify before paying.**  
   Call 311 or the local parking agency if available. On a campus, call campus police or parking services. In a private lot, call the posted property manager or lot operator. If you cannot verify who placed the device, call the local non-emergency police number and ask how to confirm whether the immobilization is authorized.

4. **Document the situation before release.**  
   Take clear photos of the device, full notice text, posted signs, your license plate, the vehicle’s position, the lot or street location, and any payment screen or receipt. Save call times, names, reference numbers, and confirmation numbers.

5. **Contact the listed release route and say you need to leave.**  
   Ask what is needed for immediate release, whether the device uses a self-release code or staff removal, whether towing is scheduled, and whether you can access the vehicle for urgent items such as medication, keys, mobility aids, or childcare items.

6. **Use only the verified release instructions.**  
   Some systems give a release code after payment or after a payment plan is set up. Others send staff to remove the device. Follow the official instructions exactly, including any device return directions, and keep the receipt.

7. **Before paying, ask one simple dispute question if you can.**  
   Ask: “Does paying for release change my ability to contest the ticket, fee, or boot later?” Write down the answer, but do not let this delay you if you need to leave or avoid a tow.

8. **Make a separate plan to leave without the car.**  
   Book a rideshare or taxi, take transit, call someone for a pickup, or ask a nearby trusted person for help arranging transport. Your immediate goal is to get yourself moving while the vehicle issue is handled through the official route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether the boot was fair, lawful, or worth disputing.
- You do not need to write a complaint while you are stuck at the vehicle.
- You do not need to solve every unpaid ticket, fee, or property dispute in the moment.
- You do not need to argue in person; evidence, receipts, and reference numbers can be used later.

## Important reassurance
A boot or windshield device can make you feel trapped, especially when you need to leave. The fastest way to regain control is to verify who placed it, start the official release route, and arrange transport for yourself separately.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about disputes, refunds, tickets, private-lot rules, or complaints may need help from the relevant parking agency, property manager, campus parking office, consumer-protection office, or a legal professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Booting rules, fees, towing timelines, payment options, and appeal rights vary by state, city, county, campus, property, and operator.
