# What to do if… you discover someone saved intimate photos you sent with an expectation of privacy (USA)

## Short answer
Cease communication immediately and do not send additional material or money. Preserve evidence of the saved content and any threats, then use hashing tools like StopNCII.org or Take It Down to block the images from being shared on major platforms.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send "verification" photos, money, or gift cards to prove your identity or secure deletion.
- Don’t delete the original conversation or the evidence before you have taken secure screenshots.
- Don’t negotiate or make counter-threats, as this often triggers immediate distribution.
- Don’t attempt to meet the person in private to witness them deleting the images.
- Don’t forward the intimate images to others while asking for help; use links or screenshots of the file names instead.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific cues of the violation.**
   - Received a "screenshot" notification in a disappearing-message app.
   - Observed a "saved to media" icon or status in a chat interface.
   - Found the images in a shared cloud folder or on a secondary device.
   - Received a message confirming they have "kept" or "archived" the images.
2. **Move to a safer physical and digital space.** **If you are being actively intimidated or feel physically unsafe, leave your current location and call 911.**
3. **Preserve evidence of the saved images and any accompanying messages.** **Take screenshots of the chat history, the person's profile/username, and any timestamps or specific threats made.**
   - Save these to a secure, password-protected folder or a hidden album.
   - Ensure you capture the platform name and any relevant URLs if the content is already public.
4. **Discontinue all contact with the individual.** **Stop responding to messages and avoid explaining why you are stopping contact.**
   - Mute or archive the conversation rather than deleting it, so you retain the logs for potential reporting.
5. **Use hashing tools to prevent the images from being uploaded elsewhere.** **If you are 18 or older, use StopNCII.org to create a digital "fingerprint" of the images that participating platforms use to block uploads.**
   - If the images depict you when you were under 18, use the NCMEC "Take It Down" tool.
6. **Utilize platform-specific reporting for nonconsensual intimate imagery (NCII).** **Report the person’s account and the specific content through the app’s internal safety reporting tools.**
   - Under the federal TAKE IT DOWN Act (2025), major social media and user-generated content platforms are required to remove verified NCII within 48 hours of a valid request.
7. **Report sextortion or blackmail to federal authorities.** **If the person is demanding money or more images in exchange for not sharing the content, file a report with the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3).**
   - If the situation involves a minor, report immediately to the NCMEC CyberTipline at 1-800-843-5678.
8. **Contact a specialist helpline for procedural support.** **Call the Cyber Civil Rights Initiative (CCRI) Crisis Helpline at 800-398-2234 for confidential guidance on takedowns and safety planning.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you will file a civil lawsuit or pursue criminal charges.
- You do not need to notify your employer, school, or family members right now.
- You do not need to delete your entire social media presence; privacy settings can be adjusted later.
- You do not need to confront the person to "verify" they have deleted the content.

## Important reassurance
Discovering that your privacy has been compromised is a significant violation that often triggers a "freeze" response or a feeling of powerlessness. These reactions are natural. Taking these technical steps allows you to regain a measure of control over the digital spread of the images without needing to engage with the person who caused the harm.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a privacy breach involving intimate imagery. It focuses on evidence preservation and digital containment. It does not cover state-specific criminal litigation or long-term therapeutic recovery.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding nonconsensual intimate imagery and digital forgeries vary by state. The 48-hour mandatory removal provision under the TAKE IT DOWN Act applies to "covered platforms" as defined by federal law.
