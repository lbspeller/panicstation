# What to do if… you receive a notice that your parcel has been seized or detained (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay, click links, or give personal information from an unexpected parcel message. First verify the notice through an official U.S. government website or independently found agency contact route, then preserve the paperwork and identify any real deadline.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links or call numbers from an unexpected “detained package” text, email, or voicemail.
- Do not pay “customs fees,” “release charges,” or “fines” by gift card, cryptocurrency, wire transfer, payment app, or a link in a message.
- Do not give your Social Security number, bank details, passwords, one-time codes, or a photo of your ID because of a rushed call or message.
- Do not explain the parcel, the sender, or the item in an unplanned phone call if criminal conduct is mentioned or implied.
- Do not ignore a paper Notice of Seizure, forfeiture notice, or official-looking letter until you have checked whether it is genuine and whether it has a deadline.

## What to do now
1. **Separate the notice from the message.** Write down how it arrived: paper mail, email, text, voicemail, tracking update, or a call. Keep the envelope, screenshots, tracking number, sender name, order receipt, item description, and any case or reference number.

2. **Check the agency without using the message’s links or phone number.** Type the agency name into your browser yourself and use only an official `.gov` site. Compare the agency name, case number, parcel details, public notice information, and the process described in your paperwork.

3. **Treat urgent payment or identity demands as unverified.** A real official process should not require gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfer, or a payment link sent by text. Do not send money or personal information until you have verified the route independently.

4. **Find the deadline on the actual notice.** Look for words such as “claim,” “petition,” “notice of seizure,” “intent to forfeit,” “response,” or “deadline.” Put the date in your calendar and keep a copy of the page showing it.

5. **If the notice appears genuine, follow the exact official route listed for that notice.** It may offer a petition, a claim, or another response option. Use the address, form, portal, or instructions only after you have confirmed they match an official `.gov` source, and keep copies plus proof of mailing or submission.

6. **If someone tries to question you, slow the contact down.** You can say: “I am not comfortable answering questions right now. I want to speak with an attorney first.” Then stop answering questions about the parcel, sender, contents, or reason for shipment.

7. **If it looks like a USPS-related text scam, preserve the details before deleting it.** Screenshot the message, do not click the link, and use the official Postal Inspection Service smishing guidance or another official reporting route. If you entered card, bank, password, or identity details, contact your bank or card issuer using the number on your card or app.

8. **If money has already left your account, act through your bank first.** Tell the bank or card issuer it may be fraud and ask about blocking, reversing, or monitoring transactions. Then report the scam through an official federal reporting route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to abandon the parcel, contest the seizure, or explain the full story until you have verified the notice and deadline.
- You do not need to contact the sender or seller before preserving your records.
- You do not need to write a long statement while you are alarmed; start by confirming the official process and keeping the facts organised.

## Important reassurance
A seizure or detention notice can feel frightening, and scam messages are designed to make you rush. Slowing down, checking official sources, and refusing unusual payment or identity demands are protective steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: verify the notice, avoid scams, preserve records, and avoid missed deadlines or rushed statements. Later decisions may need help from a qualified U.S. attorney, especially if the parcel may involve controlled substances, weapons, counterfeit goods, import violations, or an interview request.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, therapeutic, or professional advice. Procedures can vary by agency, notice type, location, and what was seized.
