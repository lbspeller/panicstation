# What to do if… someone uses money, housing, or practical help to pressure you into sex (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause away from the person, then reach confidential sexual violence support. Consent is only valid when it is freely given, and you can access help without being forced to report the situation to the police.

## Do not do these things
- Do not meet the person alone to discuss the situation if you feel unsafe or pressured.
- Do not hand over your phone, identification, or financial documents to secure housing.
- Do not delete text messages, emails, or call logs that contain threats or "offers."
- Do not assume that you must use physical force to resist for the situation to be considered sexual violence.
- Do not attempt to pay back a debt or favor with sexual acts to resolve the conflict.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize reaching a safer pause immediately.** **Move to a public space, go to a trusted neighbor's home, or stay in a locked room while you contact help.**
   - If you are in immediate physical danger, call **911**.
   - If you are currently in their home or vehicle, find a reason to leave (e.g., "I need to go to the store") and do not return alone.
2. **Contact a confidential specialist for safety planning and support.** **Call the National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-HOPE (4673) or use the online chat at RAINN.org.**
   - These services are available 24/7 and do not require you to report the incident to law enforcement.
   - They can help you find local advocates who understand housing-related exploitation.
3. **Preserve communication evidence without direct interaction.** **Screenshot all messages that mention money, housing, "repayment," or sexual expectations.**
   - Save copies to a secure cloud account or email them to a trusted person so they are safe if your phone is lost or taken.
   - If the person calls you, let it go to voicemail to create a recording of their tone and requests.
4. **Access emergency shelter or financial aid if you need to leave tonight.** **Dial 2-1-1 from any phone to connect with local crisis housing and social services.**
   - Inform the operator if you are fleeing a situation involving sexual pressure or domestic violence, as this may prioritize your placement.
   - If 2-1-1 is unavailable in your area, visit **211.org** to find direct contact numbers for your county.
5. **Seek medical care if you have injuries or safety concerns.** **Go to an emergency room or urgent care clinic for a professional assessment.**
   - You can request a Sexual Assault Medical Forensic Exam (SAFE), often called a "rape kit," which preserves physical evidence.
   - If you do not want to involve police yet, ask the hospital about "anonymous" or "Jane Doe" evidence storage.
   - To preserve physical evidence before an exam:
     - Avoid showering, bathing, or brushing your teeth.
     - Keep the clothing you were wearing in a paper (not plastic) bag.
6. **Connect with the National Human Trafficking Hotline if you feel trapped or "indebted."** **Call 1-888-373-7888 or text "HELP" to 233733.**
   - This is appropriate if someone is holding your documents, threatening to "report" you to authorities, or using a "debt" to force you to stay.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a formal police report today.
- You do not need to figure out long-term housing or employment in the next few hours.
- You do not need to explain your situation to friends or family members unless you feel it is safe to do so.
- You do not need to understand the legal definitions of "quid pro quo" harassment right now; a housing advocate can explain your Fair Housing Act rights later.

## Important reassurance
When someone controls access to your basic needs—like a roof over your head or money for food—they are using power to remove your choice. It is normal to feel frozen, confused, or like you have no other option. These reactions are survival strategies, and what is happening is not your fault.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and harm reduction only. Long-term safety planning, legal options regarding housing discrimination, and medical follow-ups should be handled with the support of professional advocates or clinicians.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and does not constitute legal or medical advice. If you are in a life-threatening situation, call 911. Confidential hotlines are the safest way to get personalized advice without immediate legal consequences.
