# What to do if… IT asks to collect your laptop immediately and you are not told why (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all direct engagement, preserve evidence immediately, and report the threat through your employer's internal safety or HR channels. Call 911 if the threat implies immediate physical harm or if you believe the person knows your physical location.

## Do not do these things
- Don't respond, argue, or attempt to de-escalate the situation through the same online platform.
- Don't delete any messages, comments, or posts until you have verified they are saved as evidence.
- Don't provide personal explanations or details that reveal your location, routine, or family information.
- Don't attempt to "investigate" the person or look up their private information.
- Don't agree to meet the person in a physical location to resolve the dispute.

## What to do now
1. **Assess the threat for immediate physical danger.**  
   If the message contains your home address, current location, or specific threats of physical violence, **call 911 immediately**. Notify local law enforcement if the person has a history of showing up at your workplace or has stated they are on their way.

2. **Preserve a complete digital record of the interaction.**  
   Take screenshots that include **the full username, the timestamp, the platform URL, and the specific text/images of the threat**. 
   - For emails, save the "full headers" if possible to preserve technical routing data. 
   - Create a simple log of the date and time you received each message.

3. **Secure your digital accounts and hardware.**  
   **Change passwords** for your work and personal accounts if you suspect the person is attempting to gain access. Enable **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** on all platforms to prevent unauthorized logins. Review your privacy settings to ensure your workplace and location are not visible to the public.

4. **Report the threat to your employer’s designated safety officer or HR.**  
   File a formal report through your company’s **workplace violence reporting channel**. Request that the employer **log the threat** and preserve internal records, such as customer account notes or call recordings, which may serve as corroborating evidence.

5. **Request specific workplace safety adjustments.**  
   Ask your employer for immediate actions to isolate the hazard:
   - Reassign the client or block their access to your specific work queue.
   - Route all future communication through a generic team inbox or a supervisor.
   - Remove your full name or direct contact details from public-facing directories or websites.
   - If you work in a physical office, brief security or front-desk staff on the threat.

6. **Report the conduct to the platform and the National Coordination Center (NCC).**  
   Use the platform’s "Report Harassment/Threat" tool to request content removal. If the threat involves cyber-enabled extortion or coordinated harassment, report it to the **FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3)**, which coordinates with the NCC operational cell for rapid response to cyber-threats.

7. **Verify your rights under updated state-specific safety laws.**  
   If you are in a state like California or New York, your employer may be required by law (e.g., SB 553 or the Retail Worker Safety Act) to maintain a **written workplace violence prevention plan** and provide specific reporting mechanisms or panic buttons. Ask your HR representative for a copy of this plan.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to file a civil lawsuit or seek a permanent restraining order.
- Responding to secondary comments or "trolls" who join the thread.
- Making a public statement on behalf of yourself or your company.
- Deciding on long-term career changes or resignations while in the initial crisis phase.

## Important reassurance
Receiving threats because of your professional role is a recognized workplace hazard, not a personal failure. It is standard and appropriate to involve law enforcement and your employer when your physical or digital safety is compromised.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Long-term resolution may require legal counsel, specialized cybersecurity services, or formal workplace investigations.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Federal and state laws regarding workplace violence and online harassment are subject to change. If you are in immediate danger, contact local emergency services. Report all work-related threats to your employer to ensure compliance with OSHA's General Duty Clause.
