# What to do if… someone you just met is pushing for your address or ride home and will not drop it (USA)

## Short answer
Stop justifying your refusal and move immediately to a staffed area or among other people. Arrange your own transportation through a verified app or taxi service and do not go directly home if you suspect you are being followed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide a fake address that is still in your neighborhood; it may encourage them to follow you.
- Do not accept a ride "just to be polite" or to end the awkwardness.
- Do not go outside or to a secluded area to "explain" your boundaries.
- Do not engage in an argument or attempt to "teach" them about consent in the moment.
- Do not let the person see your phone screen while you are booking transportation.
- Do not go straight to your front door if you believe you are being shadowed by a vehicle or on foot.

## What to do now
1. **Move immediately to a staffed location or a visible security post.** Walk toward a bar, host stand, front desk, or security guard. Stand in a well-lit area where cameras are likely present.
2. **Deliver one final, non-negotiable statement.** **Say "I am not sharing my address or accepting a ride. I am leaving now."** Do not offer excuses like "my phone is dead" or "I have a roommate," as these invite "solutions" from the persistent person.
3. **Alert staff or management to the specific behavior.** **Tell a staff member: "This person is pressuring me for my address and will not stop; I need to wait here for my ride and may need help getting to it safely."** Ask them to keep the person occupied or away from you while you wait.
4. **Arrange for a controlled exit.** 
   - Book a rideshare or ask staff to call a taxi. 
   - **Verify the vehicle and driver identity** (license plate, make, model) through the app before approaching the car. 
   - If you are worried about being followed, **set the destination to a 24-hour public lobby** (like a hospital or large hotel) rather than your home.
5. **Secure your vehicle if you are driving yourself.** **Ask a staff member or security guard to walk you to your car.** 
   - Have your keys in hand before walking out. 
   - **Lock your doors the second you are inside.** 
   - If you see the person following you in your rearview mirror, do not drive home; drive to the nearest police station or a busy, well-lit gas station and call 911.
6. **Establish an active "safety witness."** **Call a trusted friend or family member and keep them on the line until you are behind a locked door.** Tell them exactly where you are and who you are with. Use "Share My Location" features on your smartphone if available.
7. **Identify immediate escalation triggers.** **Call 911 immediately if the person follows you out of the building, attempts to block your car, or tries to enter your transportation.** Do not wait for physical contact to seek emergency help.
8. **Document the encounter once you are in a locked, safe room.** **Write down the person's physical description, any names or social media handles they used, and their vehicle details if seen.** This information is vital if you need to file a harassment or stalking report later.

## What can wait
- Deciding if the person "meant well" or was just "socially awkward."
- Formally reporting the incident to the police (unless you are in immediate danger).
- Blocking them on apps or social media (wait until you are physically safe to avoid triggering a reaction while you are still near them).
- Analyzing your own behavior or worrying if you were "too mean."

## Important reassurance
Your physical safety is more important than a stranger's feelings or your own desire to be seen as polite. Persistent boundary-pushing is a recognized red flag for future aggression. Feeling a "cold chill," "gut feeling," or physical urge to leave is a valid biological response to a threat.

## Scope note
This guide is for immediate stabilization and safe extraction from a persistent or threatening social situation. It does not cover long-term stalking protection or legal restraining orders, which may require consultation with law enforcement or victim advocacy services.

## Important note
This guide provides general safety information and is not a substitute for professional security or legal advice. If a crime has been committed or you feel your life is in danger, call 911 immediately. For non-emergency reporting, contact your local police precinct's administrative line.
