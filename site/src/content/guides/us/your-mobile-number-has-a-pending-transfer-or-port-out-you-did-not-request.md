# What to do if… your mobile number has a pending transfer or port-out you did not request (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your wireless carrier immediately using an official app, website, bill, or store channel. Say that there is an unauthorized port-out or number transfer pending and you need it stopped, your account locked, and a case number.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use links, callback numbers, or chat links from the alert unless you can confirm they are inside your carrier’s official app or website.
- Don’t share verification codes, transfer PINs, account PINs, passwords, or security answers with anyone who contacts you first.
- Don’t assume it is only a glitch while a transfer is pending.
- Don’t keep relying on SMS codes for email, banking, or payment accounts until your carrier confirms the number is secure.
- Don’t wipe your phone or delete alerts before saving a screenshot and a basic timeline.

## What to do now
1. **Contact your carrier’s fraud, porting, or account security support through an official channel.**  
   Say: **“There is an unauthorized port-out or number transfer pending on my mobile number. Stop it now, lock the account, and give me a case number.”**

2. **Ask the carrier to confirm and secure the exact number.**  
   Ask them to:
   - cancel or stop the pending port-out or transfer;
   - confirm whether the number has already moved;
   - add any available number lock, port freeze, number transfer protection, or equivalent protection;
   - reset your account PIN and any transfer PIN;
   - remove unfamiliar authorized users, email addresses, devices, or account changes;
   - give you written or account-note confirmation of what happened, if available.

3. **If phone support is not working, go to a corporate carrier store with photo ID.**  
   Tell the store staff this is an unauthorized port-out or SIM-swap risk and that you need urgent account recovery, not a normal upgrade or number transfer.

4. **If you have lost service, use another safe way to communicate.**  
   Use Wi-Fi calling if it still works, a trusted person’s phone, a landline, secure email, or in-person help so you can keep contacting your carrier and financial institutions.

5. **Secure your primary email account next.**  
   Change the password, sign out of other sessions, check recovery phone numbers and recovery email addresses, and turn on a non-SMS second factor such as an authenticator app, passkey, or security key.

6. **Contact banks, payment apps, and any account that uses SMS codes.**  
   Say: **“My mobile number may be involved in an unauthorized port-out or SIM swap. SMS codes may not be safe.”** Ask them to check for password resets, new devices, new payees, wire or ACH changes, card-not-present activity, and extra verification options.

7. **Create a quick incident record.**  
   Save screenshots of the transfer alert, write down the date and time, note any “No Service” period, and record carrier case numbers, store visits, and names or IDs of representatives if provided.

8. **Report suspected fraud or identity theft after the urgent account steps are underway.**  
   Use IdentityTheft.gov if personal information, bank details, credit accounts, or online accounts may be affected. Use the FCC complaint center for a phone-service or number-porting complaint.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to work out who did it or how they got your information right now.
- You don’t need to change every password at once; start with email, banking, payment, cloud, and carrier accounts.
- You don’t need to decide today whether to take legal action.
- You don’t need to decide about credit monitoring immediately. If there are signs of broader identity theft, fraud alerts or credit freezes can be handled after the carrier, email, and bank access are stable.

## Important reassurance
This is a recognised phone-account takeover pattern. Treating it as urgent is reasonable; the immediate goal is to stop the transfer, protect accounts that rely on SMS, and make a clear record of what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate damage from a pending mobile number transfer, port-out, or SIM-swap risk. Later decisions, especially if money was taken or accounts were opened, may need help from your carrier, bank, credit reporting companies, regulators, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, law-enforcement, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Carrier tools, names, and recovery steps vary, so ask the carrier to confirm exactly what protections were applied and what you should expect next.
