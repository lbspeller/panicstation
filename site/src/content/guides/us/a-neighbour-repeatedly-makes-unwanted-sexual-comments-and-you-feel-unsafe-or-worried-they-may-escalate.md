# What to do if… a neighbour repeatedly makes unwanted sexual comments and you feel unsafe or worried they may escalate (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause and avoid being alone with the neighbor where reasonably possible. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. You can contact someone you trust or a confidential support service before deciding whether to take any further step.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t meet the neighbor privately to clear the air or hear them out.
- Don’t feel you have to argue, explain, joke back, or act friendly.
- Don’t wait for physical contact before seeking support.
- Don’t pressure yourself to decide about reporting, moving, or court options right now.
- Don’t post identifying details publicly if doing so could increase your risk.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.**  
   Leave the shared area if you can. Go back inside, move to a place where other people are present, or contact someone you trust. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.

2. **Reduce opportunities for private contact.**  
   Where reasonably possible, avoid being alone with the neighbor in hallways, elevators, laundry rooms, parking areas, or shared entrances. It is OK to turn around, wait, or leave.

3. **Only if it feels safe, use one brief boundary and disengage.**  
   You can say, “Stop making sexual comments to me.” You do not need to explain or debate. Skip this step if it could increase your risk.

4. **Ask one trusted person for practical support.**  
   Tell them what has been happening. Ask for a specific check-in, accompaniment through a shared area, or a phone call while you enter or leave.

5. **If it feels safe, save existing messages and make a brief note of what happened.**

6. **Ask for confidential support.**  
   VictimConnect can provide information and individualized referrals for people affected by crime. RAINN publishes safety-planning information for harassment or stalking concerns and offers confidential hotline support for sexual assault or abuse.

7. **Choose whether to create a building record.**  
   If you want to, send a short written message to your landlord, property manager, or HOA. Ask them to record the concern and tell you what practical building-safety steps may be available, such as checking locks, lighting, or entry arrangements.

8. **Choose whether to contact local law enforcement.**  
   If you want to ask about non-emergency options, use your local police department’s official website to find the appropriate contact route. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to move, seek a court order, or make a police report.
- You do not need to confront the neighbor repeatedly.
- You do not need to decide exactly what label applies to the behavior.
- You do not need to gather extensive evidence.
- You do not need to tell the whole building or neighborhood.

## Important reassurance
You do not have to wait for the behavior to become physical before taking your discomfort seriously. It is understandable to freeze, second-guess yourself, or try to smooth things over. You can take one small safety step at a time.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next hours or days. Later decisions may depend on your circumstances, local rules, and the support available in your area. A specialist support service can help you consider options without pressure.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
