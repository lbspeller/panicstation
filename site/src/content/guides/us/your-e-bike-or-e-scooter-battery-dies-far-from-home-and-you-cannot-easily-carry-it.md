# What to do if… your e-bike or e-scooter battery dies far from home and you cannot easily carry it (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer spot away from traffic, power the device fully off, and switch to a no-lift plan: pickup, taxi, rideshare, or transit only if your local agency allows that device.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t try to “revive” it with random chargers, hacked adapters, universal chargers, or unknown outlets.
- Don’t keep moving or transporting it if the battery is hot, swollen, leaking, smoking, making odd noises, or has a burning or chemical odor.
- Don’t ride in a risky way to save walking, such as going wrong-way, weaving through traffic, or using sidewalks where riding is prohibited.
- Don’t roll to a bus stop or station assuming the device will be allowed; transit rules vary by agency, vehicle type, size, weight, and folding ability.
- Don’t leave it blocking curb ramps, doors, crosswalks, aisles, emergency equipment, or narrow sidewalks.
- Don’t lock it to transit property where the transit agency prohibits locking devices there.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause point.** Move out of the roadway to a wide sidewalk, well-lit area, open business, lobby, gas station, or other staffed place before making transport decisions.

2. **Power it fully off and check for battery danger signs.** Turn the device off. Look, listen, and smell without putting your face close to the battery.
   - If you notice heat, swelling, smoke, leaking, odd noises, a burning odor, or a chemical odor, stop handling it if you can, move people away, and call 911 from a safe distance if there may be a fire risk.
   - Do not put a device with warning signs into a car, elevator, bus, train, or other enclosed space.

3. **Decide whether it is a rental or yours.**
   - **Rental:** use the rental app or support number to report that the battery died and you cannot move it easily. If the app allows you to end the ride, do that only after parking it upright and out of the way of curb ramps, doors, crosswalks, driveways, and narrow sidewalks. Take a photo and save the location.
   - **Personal device:** choose pickup, taxi, rideshare, or permitted transit. Do not spend time opening panels or troubleshooting wiring on the street.

4. **Choose the quickest no-lift plan.**
   - **Friend or family pickup:** send your live location and one simple landmark, such as “front doors of the supermarket” or “gas station at 5th and Pine.”
   - **Taxi or rideshare:** request a larger vehicle if possible, message the driver that you have a powered-off e-bike or e-scooter, fold it if it is designed to fold, and be ready to cancel if the driver will not take it.
   - **Transit:** check your local agency’s current rules before going to the stop or station. Some agencies allow only certain devices, only folded devices, or no devices on some vehicles.

5. **If transit is allowed, keep it boring and non-blocking.** For example, MTA New York says personal electric vehicles must not be charged or operated in MTA facilities or vehicles, must remain powered off during transport, and must not block doors, aisles, seats, emergency equipment, or accessible spaces.
   - Fold or compact it if it is designed to fold.
   - Walk it; do not ride it in stations, terminals, platforms, buses, or trains.
   - Keep it close to you and ready to move if asked.
   - If staff say it is not allowed, switch to pickup, taxi, or rideshare instead of arguing.

6. **If you must step away briefly to arrange help, lock and document it.**
   - Lock the frame to something immovable only where locking is allowed.
   - Take a photo of the lockup and nearby landmark.
   - If the battery is removable, cool, normal-looking, and designed to be removed, consider taking it with you. Do not remove or carry the battery if it has any warning signs.

7. **If it is only out of charge and there are no danger signs, handle it gently.**
   - Keep it powered off.
   - Avoid dropping it, crushing the battery area, or forcing it into a space where it does not fit.
   - Once you are somewhere stable, use only the manufacturer-provided or manufacturer-recommended charger.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to diagnose the battery, open panels, test wiring, or attempt street repairs now.
- You do **not** need to decide today whether the battery is ruined or the device needs replacing.
- You do **not** need to resolve warranty, recall, resale, insurance, or reimbursement questions until you and the device are in a stable place.

## Important reassurance
This is a stressful moment because the device is heavy, your route plan has collapsed, and local rules may be unclear. A simple sequence of safer pause, powered off, and pickup or permitted transport is enough for now.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and get out of the immediate problem. Later decisions about repair, battery inspection, recalls, warranty, or local transport rules may need help from the manufacturer, rental company, transit agency, repair shop, or fire-safety authority.

## Important note
This is general information, not mechanical, legal, fire-safety, transport, medical, or professional advice for your specific device, battery, city, or transit system. If you see signs of battery failure, prioritize distance and emergency assistance.
