# What to do if… you receive a debt collection letter for a debt you do not recognise (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay, acknowledge, or negotiate the debt until you have received written verification. Send a written dispute and debt validation request within 30 days of receiving the notice to legally force the collector to pause activity and prove the debt is yours.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide or confirm your Social Security number, bank account details, or full date of birth over the phone.
- Do not make a "good faith" partial payment, as this can restart the statute of limitations on an old or expired debt.
- Do not ignore the letter; silence is legally interpreted as an admission that the debt is valid after 30 days.
- Do not rely on verbal promises from the collector; if it is not in writing, it is not legally binding.
- Do not use the contact links or phone numbers provided in the letter if you suspect a scam.

## What to do now
1. **Preserve the original correspondence and envelope.**  
   **Keep every page of the letter and the envelope, as the postmark date proves when the 30-day dispute window actually started.**
2. **Review the notice for required "Validation Information."**  
   **Under federal Regulation F, the collector must provide an itemized breakdown of the debt, including interest, fees, and payments since the "itemization date."**
   - Check for the name of the original creditor and the current owner of the debt.
   - Look for a "tear-off" dispute form, which many modern notices must now include.
3. **Send a written Debt Validation Letter via Certified Mail.**  
   **Mail a letter stating "I dispute the validity of this debt and request verification" to the collector’s address within 30 days of receipt.**
   - Request the name and address of the original creditor if it is not clear.
   - Use Certified Mail with a Return Receipt Requested to prove the collector received your dispute.
   - Once they receive this, they must stop all collection efforts until they mail you verification.
4. **Pull your credit reports from the official federal source.**  
   **Visit AnnualCreditReport.com to check all three major bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) for this specific account or other unfamiliar entries.**
   - Save a PDF copy of your reports immediately to document how the debt was appearing.
5. **Freeze your credit files if you suspect identity theft.**  
   **Contact each of the three bureaus individually to place a "security freeze," which prevents anyone from opening new accounts in your name.**
   - If you believe the debt resulted from fraud, create an official report at IdentityTheft.gov.
6. **Dispute inaccuracies with the credit bureaus directly.**  
   **If the unrecognized debt is already on your report, file a formal dispute with the Credit Reporting Agency (CRA) first before escalating to federal complaints.**
   - Include a copy of your debt validation request and the identity theft report if applicable.
7. **Invoke your right to limit or stop communications.**  
   **Under the FDCPA, you can send a written "Cease and Desist" letter to stop all phone calls and letters.**
   - Note that collectors may still contact you one last time to confirm they are stopping or to serve legal papers.
   - Remember that a collector is presumed to violate the law if they call you more than 7 times in 7 days about a specific debt.
8. **File a complaint if the collector remains abusive or non-compliant.**  
   **Submit a report to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) if the collector fails to provide verification but continues to demand payment.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to hire an attorney immediately unless you have been served with a court summons or lawsuit.
- You do not need to "prove" the debt isn't yours in the first letter; the burden of proof is on the collector to verify it.
- You do not need to contact the original creditor yet; start with the agency that sent the letter.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an unfamiliar collection notice is a common occurrence due to clerical errors, "zombie" debts that have expired, or identity mix-ups. Taking the single step of sending a written dispute letter protects your legal rights and prevents the collector from assuming the debt is valid by default.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for unrecognized debt notices under federal law (FDCPA / Regulation F). It does not cover legal defense for active lawsuits or state-specific statute of limitations calculations.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and is not legal advice. Debt collection laws and consumer rights may vary by state. If you are served with legal papers or threatened with garnishment, consult a qualified consumer protection attorney or local legal aid office immediately.
