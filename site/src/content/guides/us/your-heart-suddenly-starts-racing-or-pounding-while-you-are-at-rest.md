# What to do if… your heart suddenly starts racing or pounding while you are at rest (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately sit or lie down to prevent a fall and call **911** if the racing heartbeat is accompanied by chest pain, severe shortness of breath, or a feeling that you are about to faint.

## Do not do these things
- Do not **drive yourself** to a hospital if you are feeling dizzy, lightheaded, or physically weak.
- Do not **walk or pace around** to "test" if the sensation goes away with movement.
- Do not take **extra doses** of your current medications or use someone else's heart or blood pressure medication.
- Do not use **stimulants** right now, including caffeine, nicotine, energy drinks, or decongestants containing pseudoephedrine.
- Do not **perform vagal maneuvers** (such as bearing down or coughing) unless a clinician has previously diagnosed your specific arrhythmia and taught you exactly how to do them safely.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all physical activity and get into a safe position.** Sit in a chair with back support or lie flat on the floor or a bed.
   - If you feel very lightheaded, lie flat and elevate your legs above the level of your heart.
2. **Assess for emergency "red flag" symptoms and call 911 if any are present:**
   - Pain, pressure, or a squeezing sensation in the chest, neck, jaw, or arms.
   - Sudden difficulty breathing or being unable to speak in full sentences.
   - Actual fainting (loss of consciousness) or severe "near-fainting" sensations.
   - Profuse, cold sweating or sudden, extreme weakness.
3. **Check your pulse if you can do so without standing up.** Use two fingers on your wrist or neck to feel the rhythm.
   - Note if the beat feels "regular" (like a metronome) or "irregular" (jumping around or skipping).
   - Count the number of beats for 30 seconds and multiply by two to get your resting heart rate.
4. **Capture data from wearable devices if available.** If you are wearing a smartwatch or fitness tracker, save the current heart rate reading or trigger an ECG/EKG recording if the device supports it.
5. **Loosen restrictive items to improve comfort.** Open tight collars, belts, or restrictive waistbands to ensure your breathing is not constricted.
6. **Determine the appropriate level of follow-up care if symptoms are stable but continuing.**
   - **Seek emergency care** if the racing persists for more than a few minutes or if you have a history of heart disease, even if you feel otherwise "okay."
   - **Contact an urgent care or on-call nurse line** if the racing has stopped but was a new or unusually intense experience for you.
   - **Schedule a prompt primary care appointment** if you experience frequent "skipped beats" or brief flutters that do not cause dizziness or pain.

## What can wait
- You do not need to figure out the specific trigger (such as stress vs. caffeine) while the episode is occurring.
- You do not need to decide on long-term lifestyle changes or new prescriptions today.
- You do not need to keep checking your pulse every minute once you have recorded the initial rate and rhythm.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of panic or "impending doom" when your heart rhythm changes suddenly. This physiological reaction often happens because the body releases adrenaline during a rapid heartbeat episode. Taking a seat and focusing on your immediate safety is the most effective way to manage the first few minutes of this sensation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a sudden change in heart rhythm at rest. It is not a substitute for clinical diagnostics such as a 12-lead ECG, blood tests, or professional monitoring which are required to identify the cause of palpitations.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute medical advice or a diagnosis. If you are concerned about your heart health or believe you are experiencing a cardiac event, contact emergency services at **911** immediately.
