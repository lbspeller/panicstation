# What to do if… your child tells you someone online is asking them to keep a secret that feels sexual (USA)

## Short answer

Treat this as a possible child sexual exploitation concern, not a private online problem your child has to manage alone. Stay calm, thank your child for telling you, and help them stop contact without blame.

## Do not do these things

- Do not shame your child or demand they explain everything perfectly.
- Do not message the other person yourself.
- Do not ask your child to keep talking to the person to get proof.
- Do not delete messages, accounts, usernames, or app details right away if you may want support or to report it.
- Do not download, copy, forward, or ask anyone to send sexual images or videos of a minor.
- Do not send sexual content to friends or family for advice.
- Do not promise that nothing will happen next if there may be immediate danger.
- Do not rush into punishments about phone use before the child is settled and safer.

## What to do now

1. **Start with safety and belief.** Say something simple like: “Thank you for telling me. You are not in trouble. We’re going to deal with this together.”

2. **Stop the contact while you are with them.** Help your child stop replying. Keep the device nearby with an adult, and turn off location sharing, live-map features, or public contact settings on the app and device.

3. **Capture only the details needed for help.** Note the app, username, profile link, handle, messages, requests, threats, gifts, attempts to move to another platform, and any mention of meeting in person. Keep this brief and calm.

4. **Preserve a limited record if it is safe to do so.** Take screenshots of the profile, handle, platform, and key non-explicit messages where possible, and store them privately. Do not ask your child to keep engaging with the person, and do not download, forward, or redistribute sexual images or videos.

5. **Check for immediate danger.** Ask whether the person knows your child’s real name, school, phone number, address, location, or has asked to meet in person, send sexual images, or keep things hidden from parents. If there is immediate danger, call **911**.

6. **Block and report inside the app when the basic details are saved.** Use the app’s report tool for sexual contact with a minor, grooming, threats, or requests for images. If blocking would make the child feel safer right now, block first and write down anything you can still see.

7. **Use a U.S. reporting route for online child sexual exploitation.** You can make a report to **NCMEC’s CyberTipline**, the national centralized reporting system for suspected online child exploitation. You do not need a perfect timeline before asking for help.

8. **If sexual images may already exist, use a removal tool built for minors.** **Take It Down** may help remove or limit online sharing of nude, partially nude, or sexually explicit images or videos taken before age 18. Do not download or ask someone to send explicit images just to use the tool.

9. **Get support around the child, not just around the device.** If your child is overwhelmed, panicky, ashamed, or scared of consequences, stay with them, reduce online noise for the rest of the day, and consider contacting NCMEC’s 24-hour call center at **1-800-THE-LOST (1-800-843-5678)**, your child’s pediatrician, or a school counselor for support.

10. **Keep the next few hours low-pressure.** Avoid turning this into a long interview tonight. Keep meals, sleep, and supervision simple, and save bigger decisions for when the child is calmer and you have advice.

## What can wait

You do not need to decide right now whether to change every app rule, whether the other person was “really serious,” or what the long-term response should be. You also do not need to get the whole timeline in one sitting.

## Important reassurance

Secrecy, flattery, sexual attention, gifts, threats, and pressure can be tactics used against children online. A child may comply, freeze, minimize it, or tell the story in pieces; that does not make it their fault.

## Scope note

This is first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, police, or other professional advice. In the USA, suspected online child sexual exploitation can be reported through NCMEC’s CyberTipline, and immediate danger should go to 911. If details are incomplete, it is still reasonable to act cautiously and get support.
