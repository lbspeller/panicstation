# What to do if… police arrive at your home asking to speak with you and you are unsure why (USA)

## Short answer
Keep the door closed and locked, communicate through the door to ask if they have a judicial warrant, and state clearly that you do not consent to entry or questioning.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door even slightly, as this can be interpreted as granting consent to enter.
- Do not invite officers inside "to talk" or step aside to allow them to cross the threshold.
- Do not consent to a search of your person, home, or belongings to "appear cooperative" or "clear things up."
- Do not provide explanations, guesses, or statements about why you think they are there.
- Do not sign any documents or forms (including "consent to search" forms) without a lawyer present.
- Do not physically resist or block entry if officers force their way in; state your objection verbally and stay safe.
- Do not run, hide, or make sudden movements that could be perceived as a threat.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause by keeping the door closed.** Speak through the door or a closed window to maintain the physical barrier of your home.
2. **Verify the identity and agency of the officers.** Ask for their names, badge numbers, and the department they represent.
   - If you are unsure if they are real officers, tell them through the door: "**I am calling 911 to verify your identity before I speak with you.**"
3. **Ask clearly if they have a warrant.** Say: "**Do you have a warrant signed by a judge?**"
   - If they say yes, ask them to slide it under the door or hold it up to a window/peephole so you can read it.
   - Check that the warrant contains your correct address, a judge's signature, and the specific areas or items they are authorized to search.
4. **Identify administrative vs. judicial warrants.** If the officers are from immigration (ICE), they may present a Form I-200 or I-205.
   - **These are administrative warrants and do not authorize entry into a private home without your consent.**
   - Only a judicial warrant signed by a judge or magistrate grants the authority to enter a home without consent.
5. **State your refusal clearly if there is no judicial warrant.** Say: "**I do not consent to you entering my home. I am going to remain silent.**"
6. **Identify the "emergency aid" exception.** Be aware that under recent 2026 guidance, police may enter without a warrant if they have an objectively reasonable basis to believe someone inside is in immediate danger (e.g., a welfare check for a medical emergency).
   - If they state they are there for a welfare check, ask them to explain the emergency through the door.
7. **Maintain silence if they enter.** If officers enter with a warrant or under an exception, do not interfere. 
   - State: "**I do not consent to this search. I am exercising my right to remain silent and I want a lawyer.**"
8. **Ask if you are being detained.** If they ask you to step outside, ask: "**Am I free to stay inside?**" or "**Am I free to go?**" 
   - If the answer is yes, stay inside and close the door.
9. **Document the encounter from a safe distance.** Use a phone to record if possible, but do not hold the phone near the officers or interfere with their movements.
   - Note the time, number of officers, vehicles present, and any specific statements made.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to give a statement or "tell your side" right now.
- You do not need to verify the underlying reason for the visit immediately.
- You do not need to contact your employer or neighbors while the interaction is active.
- You do not need to review your own security footage or records until the officers have left.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel intimidated, confused, or physically shaky when law enforcement arrives unexpectedly. Exercising your constitutional rights to remain silent and refuse warrantless entry is a standard protective measure and does not imply guilt.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for an unexpected police encounter at a private residence in the USA. It does not cover long-term legal strategy or specific state-level "stop and identify" variations for public spaces.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not legal advice. Laws regarding police entry and warrants are subject to judicial interpretation and vary by jurisdiction. If you are detained, arrested, or your home is searched, you should contact a qualified attorney immediately.
