# What to do if… you are asked to attend a court hearing remotely for someone in custody tomorrow (USA)

## Short answer
Do not use a link or passcode from a message until you verify the hearing through the court’s official website or clerk’s office. Then ask the clerk for the correct observer or public remote-access instructions, if remote observation is allowed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click a court link, enter a passcode, or download an app from a text, social message, or forwarded email until you confirm the court and hearing through an official court source.
- Do not pay money or give card, bank, Social Security, or other sensitive information through an unsolicited message or call; verify any claimed fee directly with the clerk using the court’s official website.
- Do not record, stream, screenshot, photograph, or rebroadcast a hearing unless the court clearly says you may.
- Do not share a remote link, dial-in code, or meeting ID with other people unless the court says it is allowed.
- Do not contact the judge directly or send informal statements to the court.
- Do not assume every hearing is open; some juvenile, family, sealed, protected, or criminal matters may be closed or limited.
- Do not use a party, witness, attorney, or defendant appearance link unless the court specifically tells you to use it.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the details you already have.**
   - Person’s full legal name.
   - Date of birth, if they gave it to you.
   - Jail or facility name, county, and state.
   - Court name, county, state, and hearing time.
   - Case number, docket number, booking number, courtroom, judge, or division, if known.
   - Attorney name or public defender office, if known.

2. **Find the court through an official source, not through the message link.**
   - Search for the court’s official website, or use an official court directory.
   - Check that the site belongs to the court system, county, state, or federal court.
   - Use the phone number or contact page on that official site, not a number from the message.

3. **Ask the clerk to confirm the hearing.**
   - Say: “I was told that [name] has a hearing tomorrow. Can you confirm the hearing time, courtroom or division, and whether public or observer remote access is available?”
   - Give only the details needed to identify the case.
   - If the clerk cannot discuss the case, ask what public calendar or docket page you should check.

4. **Ask for observer access, not party access.**
   - Say: “I am a supporter or family member and would like to observe remotely if the court permits it.”
   - Ask whether observers must register, call in, use Zoom, Webex, Teams, or attend in person instead.
   - Ask whether the hearing is open to the public and whether there are any rules about camera, microphone, name display, recording, or sharing the link.

5. **If it is federal court, be especially cautious about remote access.**
   - Federal remote public access varies by case type and judge.
   - Federal criminal proceedings generally do not have remote public access.
   - Some federal civil or bankruptcy non-trial proceedings may have public audio access if the court permits it.
   - Use the federal court’s official website or clerk’s office for that district or court of appeals.

6. **Prepare only the basic tech tonight.**
   - Use a trusted phone or computer.
   - Charge it, test sound, and keep the official dial-in or meeting details somewhere you can find quickly.
   - Use a quiet place and headphones if you have them.
   - Join with your real name if the court instructions require it, and be ready to identify yourself if the court asks.

7. **Join early and stay quiet unless the court addresses you.**
   - Join 10 to 15 minutes early if possible.
   - Keep your microphone muted unless the court tells you otherwise.
   - Keep your camera off unless the court requires it.
   - Do not speak to the person in custody during the hearing unless the court clearly permits it.
   - If you disconnect, use the same official access details to rejoin; if that fails, call the clerk.

8. **Use one fallback if remote access is not available.**
   - Ask the clerk whether the hearing is open for in-person observation.
   - If your concern is immediate safety, health, or welfare in custody, contact the facility using its published main number and ask for the correct welfare, medical, or urgent concern process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide what the person should plead, argue, or say.
- You do not need to prepare a statement for the judge tonight.
- You do not need to contact multiple agencies at once.
- You do not need to post case details online.
- You do not need to solve the whole legal situation before the hearing; first confirm the court, time, and permitted way to observe.

## Important reassurance
A next-day court date can feel sudden and frightening, especially when someone is in custody. The safest useful step is narrow: verify the hearing through the court, get the official access instructions, and avoid anything that could be a scam or a courtroom rule violation.

## Scope note
These are first steps for verifying a next-day court date and trying to attend remotely as an observer or supporter if the court permits it. Later decisions about the case, representation, pleas, release, evidence, or strategy may need help from a qualified legal professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice, professional advice, or a substitute for instructions from the court or a lawyer. Remote access rules vary by state, county, court type, judge, case category, and whether the hearing is open to the public.
