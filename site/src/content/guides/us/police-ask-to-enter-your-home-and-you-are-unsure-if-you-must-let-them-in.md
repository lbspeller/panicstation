# What to do if… police ask to enter your home and you are unsure if you must let them in (USA)

## Short answer
Keep the door closed and speak through it. Ask whether the officers have a warrant and ask to see it through a window or under the door. If they do not show a warrant, do not invite them in; say that you do not consent to entry or a search.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t open the door wide or step aside if you do not want to invite officers inside.
- Don’t physically resist, block, or touch officers if they say they are entering.
- Don’t lie or give false information.
- Don’t get drawn into a long doorstep discussion.
- Don’t make sudden movements or start moving around the home while speaking with officers.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Keep the door closed. Speak through the door and take one slow breath before answering.
2. **Confirm who is there.** Ask for the officers’ names, badge numbers, agency, and the reason they are asking to come inside.
3. **Ask: “Do you have a warrant?”** If they say yes, ask them to hold it up to a window or slide a copy under the door so you can read it without opening the door.
4. **Check the basic details.** Look for the address, the type of warrant, the signature of a judge or judicial officer, and what the warrant covers. A search warrant typically identifies the place to be searched and the scope of the search. An arrest warrant identifies a person.
5. **If they do not show a warrant, state your position clearly.** Say:
   - “I do not consent to you coming inside.”
   - “I do not consent to a search.”
   - “I am going to remain silent.”
6. **If officers say they are entering anyway, do not physically interfere.** They may say they have a warrant, an emergency, or another legal basis to enter without your consent. Say once, “I do not consent,” keep your hands visible, and follow safety directions.
7. **If safe, make notes.** Write down the time, agency, names, badge numbers, the reason officers gave, where they went, and what they took. Ask for a copy of any warrant and a receipt or list for any property taken.
8. **If officers ask detailed questions, decline to answer.** Say, “I do not want to answer questions. I am going to remain silent.” Do not lie. You can contact a lawyer before deciding whether to answer questions.
9. **If you are detained or arrested, ask for a lawyer and stop discussing the incident.** Say:
   - “I want a lawyer.”
   - “I am going to remain silent.”

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide whether the officers are legally correct while they are at the door.
- You don’t need to give a detailed statement right now.
- You don’t need to argue about the warrant on the doorstep.
- Complaints, paperwork, and legal challenges can wait until the situation is stable and you have legal advice.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to feel pressured when officers are at your home. Keeping the door closed, asking to see any warrant, and repeating one clear non-consent sentence can reduce confusion without escalating the situation.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the moment officers ask to come inside. Later decisions may depend on the warrant type, the exact facts, your state, and whether issues such as an emergency, probation, parole, or other legal authority are involved. Specialist legal help may be needed.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Rules can vary by state and situation. Prioritize safety, do not physically resist, and seek legal advice as soon as you can.
