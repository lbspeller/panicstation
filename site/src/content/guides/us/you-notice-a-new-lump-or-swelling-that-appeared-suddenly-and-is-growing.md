# What to do if… you notice a new lump or swelling that appeared suddenly and is growing (USA)

## Short answer
Seek immediate medical evaluation—**call 911** for any facial/throat swelling or breathing trouble, and **go to the Emergency Room** for sudden scrotal pain or rapidly spreading redness. For stable but growing lumps, secure a same-day appointment with a primary care provider or urgent care.

## Do not do these things
- Do not squeeze, press, or attempt to drain the lump with any tools or needles.
- Do not apply heat, ice, or "drawing salves" unless specifically instructed by a clinician.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics or other people's medications to treat the swelling.
- Do not wait for the lump to disappear on its own if it is growing or painful.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you are feeling dizzy, faint, or short of breath.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate emergency triggers:**
   - **Call 911** if you experience swelling of the lips, tongue, or throat, or if you have gasping, wheezing, repetitive coughing, or severe abdominal pain.
   - **Go to the Emergency Room** if you have sudden, severe pain in the scrotum or testicles, as this may be testicular torsion requiring surgery within hours.
   - **Go to the Emergency Room** if redness on the skin is spreading visibly over minutes or hours, or if you have a high fever, chills, and confusion.
2. **Determine the appropriate level of same-day care:**
   - **Urgent Care** is appropriate for lumps that are painful, red, or warm, or if you suspect an infected cyst, abscess, or insect sting reaction.
   - **Primary Care (Same-day)** is the best choice for new lumps in the breast, neck, or groin that are firm or growing but not causing acute distress.
3. **Capture observable details for the clinician:**
   - **Note the exact time** you first noticed the growth and how much it has changed in size since then.
   - **Identify the consistency** of the lump, noting if it feels soft and squishy or hard and fixed to the tissue underneath.
   - **Check for associated symptoms** such as night sweats, unexplained weight loss, or difficulty swallowing.
4. **Document skin changes safely:**
   - **Photograph the area** using a common object (like a coin) next to it for scale.
   - **Draw a line around the edge** of any redness with a pen to track if the infection is spreading while you are in transit to care.
5. **Stabilize the area during transit:**
   - **Elevate the affected limb** or area if possible to reduce pressure and fluid buildup.
   - **Keep the area clean** and avoid wearing tight clothing that causes friction over the swelling.
   - **Leave any drainage alone**; if the lump is leaking fluid, cover it loosely with a clean, dry bandage.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause or diagnosis yourself.
- You do not need to decide on long-term treatment or surgery options today.
- You do not need to compare your lump to images found in search results.
- You do not need to apologize for seeking care for a "minor" growth; sudden changes require professional verification.

## Important reassurance
Finding a new growth is often startling, but most sudden swellings are related to common issues like cysts, infections, or localized inflammatory reactions. Taking the step to get it evaluated now is the most effective way to manage the situation and ensure that any necessary treatment starts immediately.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and routing for acute lumps and swellings only; it does not cover long-term monitoring or diagnostic criteria for chronic conditions.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not replace professional medical judgment. Emergency symptoms like breathing difficulty or sudden, severe pain always require immediate professional intervention via 911 or the nearest emergency department.
