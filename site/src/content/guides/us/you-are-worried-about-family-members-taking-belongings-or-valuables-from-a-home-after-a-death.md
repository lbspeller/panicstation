# What to do if… you are worried about family members taking belongings or valuables from a home after a death (USA)
## Short answer
Pause informal sorting and removals. Check ordinary security without escalating the dispute, and create a neutral record of visible items, rooms, keys, and access.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t divide belongings, make promises, or let people choose items while authority and the process are unclear.
- Don’t assume that everything inside the home belongs to the estate.
- Don’t remove valuables for safekeeping unless there is an immediate risk of loss and you can document the minimum action clearly.
- Don’t discard, donate, sell, or post items online to clear the home.
- Don’t make accusations or argue inside the home.
- Don’t interfere with anyone lawfully living in the home or remove their belongings.
- Don’t change locks, block access, or move items unilaterally unless there is an immediate safety risk or clear authority to do so.
- Don’t treat a person named as executor in a will as automatically authorised to administer the estate.

## What to do now
1. **Pause removals.** Use one neutral line: “Please leave everything in place while we document what is here and confirm who has authority to act.”

2. **Check whether anyone already has proof of authority.**
   - Look for court-issued or other locally recognised proof that someone is authorised to act for the estate.
   - If there is a will, note the person named as executor, but remember that a named executor may still need proof of authority before administering the estate.
   - If nobody has confirmed authority yet, keep actions limited and cautious.

3. **Check ordinary security without escalating the dispute.**
   - Check that doors and windows are locked.
   - List known keys, garage remotes, and access codes, and note who has them.
   - Collect keys only by agreement or with clear authority.
   - If the home will be vacant, contact the property insurer and ask about vacancy and security requirements. If relevant, also contact the landlord or managing agent.
   - If changing locks or limiting access is being considered and access is disputed, get local legal guidance first unless there is an immediate safety risk.

4. **Create a baseline record before sorting starts.**
   - Take dated photos or video of rooms, access points, and visible items that may be valuable.
   - Start a simple log with the item or area, where it is, the date, and who was present.
   - Record what is visible without making claims about who owns each item.
   - Leave closed drawers, cabinets, boxes, and containers alone unless a person with confirmed authority decides otherwise or there is an immediate risk of loss.

5. **If a visible item is at immediate risk of loss, use the smallest practical safeguard.**
   - Record the item and where it was found.
   - Photograph the item and any container used.
   - When safe and feasible, have another person present and note their name.
   - Tell any person with confirmed authority where the item is being kept and share the record promptly.
   - If nobody has confirmed authority, keep a clear record, do not conceal the item, and get local legal guidance promptly.

6. **Keep an entry log.**
   - Record the date, time, reason for entry, and names of people entering the home.
   - When safe and practical, avoid one-person access while the dispute is active.

7. **Use one written channel.**
   - Send factual updates in a single group text or email thread.
   - State what has been paused, what has been documented, and what remains in place.

8. **Find the correct local probate information.**
   - Use the USAGov courts page to find the relevant state or territory court website and local probate information.
   - Ask the court clerk or self-help service, if available, where to find appointment information, filing instructions, and local forms.
   - Ask a local probate lawyer or legal-aid provider whether help is available for disputed access, lock changes, or urgent safeguarding.

9. **Use emergency and crime-reporting routes appropriately.**
   - If there is immediate danger or a crime in progress, call 911.
   - If you suspect theft or property crime but there is no immediate danger, contact the local law-enforcement agency through its non-emergency route and ask how to make a report.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide who receives belongings today.
- You do **not** need to value every item or clear the home right now.
- You do **not** need to resolve every family disagreement before pausing removals and recording the current position.
- You do **not** need to make major access changes until authority and local advice are clearer, unless there is an immediate safety risk.

## Important reassurance
A pause is reasonable. A neutral record and controlled access can reduce uncertainty and give everyone time to confirm authority and get local guidance.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Probate procedures and the powers of a personal representative vary by state and local court, and later decisions may need help from a local probate lawyer or legal-aid provider.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Prioritise immediate safety and local professional guidance before taking unilateral action with disputed property.
