# What to do if… an ex or acquaintance starts showing up near places you regularly go (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a populated public space immediately, notify a trusted person of your location, and begin keeping a log of every encounter. Call 911 if you believe you are being followed or are in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don't confront the person or try to ask them why they are there.
- Don't agree to meet them in private to "settle things" or "talk it out."
- Don't try to lead them to your home or a friend's house to see if they follow.
- Don't delete any digital evidence like texts, calls, or location-tagged posts.
- Don't post your current location or future plans on social media.
- Don't assume the behavior is "just a coincidence" if it causes you to feel unsafe.
- Don't ignore your intuition if a situation feels wrong or dangerous.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe, populated environment.** If you see the person, enter a store, restaurant, or government building with staff and other people present.
2. **Contact a trusted person and share your live location.** Tell them exactly where you are and what is happening.
   - Ask them to stay on the phone with you until you reach a secure destination.
   - Set up a specific time for a "reached safety" check-in call.
3. **Notify the management or security at the location.** Inform staff at your workplace, gym, or apartment building that you are being followed or monitored.
   - Provide a physical description of the person and their vehicle if known.
   - Request that they do not confirm your presence or schedule to anyone.
   - Ask for an escort to your vehicle or public transit.
4. **Create a detailed incident log immediately.** Document the date, time, specific location, and a description of what the person was doing.
   - Include any witnesses who may have seen the encounter.
   - Save screenshots of any digital contact or social media mentions related to the incident.
5. **Call the local police non-emergency line to file a report.** Even if no crime has been committed, establishing a paper trail is necessary for future legal protections.
   - State clearly that this is part of a pattern of unwanted behavior.
   - Obtain a case or incident number for your records.
6. **Inquire about Civil Protection Orders at your local courthouse.** These are civil court orders that require a person to stay away from you and your frequent locations.
   - Ask the clerk for "Pro Se" or "Self-Help" forms if you do not have an attorney.
   - Note that "No-Contact Orders" are typically issued during criminal proceedings, while "Protective Orders" or "Restraining Orders" are initiated by the victim in civil court.
7. **Change your daily routine immediately.** Vary the times you leave for work, take different routes, and avoid using the same entrances or exits for at least one week.
8. **Audit your digital privacy and location settings.** Disable GPS location sharing on all social media apps and check for any unrecognized devices linked to your accounts.
9. **Reach out to national support services for safety planning.** 
   - **VictimConnect Resource Center:** Call or text 1-855-484-2846 for confidential referrals and rights information.
   - **National Domestic Violence Hotline:** Call 1-800-799-7233 or text "START" to 88788 if the person is a current or former partner.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on permanent relocation or job changes right now.
- You do not need to hire a private investigator or forensic digital specialist today.
- You do not need to have "perfect" or "physical" proof of a threat to seek a police report.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel hyper-vigilant or anxious when your routine is disrupted by unwanted appearances. Taking these steps is a proactive way to manage your safety and does not mean you are overreacting.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for responding to unwanted monitoring or "chance" encounters. Ongoing stalking or harassment often requires long-term safety planning with a professional advocate.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Protective order names and processes vary significantly by state and county. Always call 911 in an emergency.
