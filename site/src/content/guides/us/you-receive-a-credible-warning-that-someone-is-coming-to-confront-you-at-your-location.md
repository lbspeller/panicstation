# What to do if… you receive a credible warning that someone is coming to confront you at your location (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a safer position, reduce access to you, and involve other people such as staff, security, a neighbor, or emergency services. If you think harm is possible or the person may arrive soon, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not step outside, open the door, or go to a parking lot to see what happens.
- Do not get pulled into arguing or negotiating by text or call while you are trying to stay safe.
- Do not watch from windows, doorways, or outside areas where you can be seen.
- Do not post your location, movements, or real-time updates.
- Do not stay isolated if you can safely involve staff, security, a neighbor, or another trusted person.
- Do not ask someone else to approach the person or check the area for you.

## What to do now
1. **Move to the safest available place.**
   - If you can leave safely before the person arrives, go to a staffed public place, front desk, lobby, security desk, trusted neighbor, or trusted nearby person.
   - If leaving could increase the risk, stay inside, move away from doors and ground-floor windows, and choose a more interior spot if possible.

2. **Make quick access harder without putting yourself in view.**
   - Lock doors and accessible windows if you can do that safely.
   - Close blinds or curtains if you can do that without standing exposed at a window.
   - If you are in a managed building, do not go into hallways, stairwells, elevators, or parking areas to check whether the person is there.

3. **Get other people involved on-site.**
   - At work, a venue, school, hotel, or clinic, tell reception, a supervisor, security, or another responsible staff member that someone may come to confront you.
   - Ask them not to let the person in, not to connect them to you, and not to reveal whether you are there.
   - At home, call a trusted person and keep them on the phone. Ask someone nearby to come only if they can do so without approaching the threat or entering an unsafe area.

4. **Call 911 if there is immediate danger, a threat of violence, forced entry, stalking at the door, or you believe the person is about to arrive and you feel unsafe.**
   - Start with your exact location, including apartment, room, floor, entrance, or business name.
   - Say what you were told, who may be coming, any threats made, and any description, vehicle, weapon concern, or history of violence you know.
   - Follow the call-taker’s instructions and do not hang up unless told to.

5. **If you cannot safely talk, text 911 where text-to-911 is available.**
   - Call if you safely can; text if you cannot.
   - Put your exact location first, then a short description of what is happening.
   - Avoid emojis, abbreviations, slang, photos, and group texts.

6. **If it is serious but not immediate, use a local non-emergency route.**
   - Search for your city, county, or local police department’s non-emergency number, or your local emergency communications center’s non-emergency number.
   - Use that route only when there is no immediate danger and no urgent need for police, fire, or medical help.

7. **Preserve the warning without engaging.**
   - Screenshot or save the warning message, caller details, voicemail, or social media post.
   - Write down the time, who warned you, what they said, and why you believe the person may come.
   - Do not argue back. If you must reply, keep it brief and do not share your location, movements, or plans.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now about protective orders, formal statements, complaints, moving, or longer-term security changes.
- You do not need to tell the full story to multiple people; share only what is needed to keep access to you controlled.
- You do not need to confront the person, clear the air, prove anything, or explain yourself today.

## Important reassurance
A credible warning can make your body react fast, including shaking, tunnel vision, nausea, or feeling frozen. The safer first step is to increase distance, reduce access to you, and bring in support rather than trying to manage the confrontation alone.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes to hours only. Later decisions, such as reporting options, workplace procedures, housing safety, or legal protection, may need specialist help once you are safe.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. If you think violence is possible or imminent, call 911 and prioritize getting to a safer place.
