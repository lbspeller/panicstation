# What to do if… you are asked to return medical equipment or medications after a death (USA)

## Short answer
Pause any unconfirmed handover. Verify an equipment pickup through the hospice or supplier’s official contact details, keep medications secure in their labeled containers, and ask the hospice about medication disposal or use an FDA-recommended take-back option.

## Do not do these things
- Do not give medications to someone who arrived only to collect equipment. If hospice was involved, confirm its medication-disposal instructions separately.
- Do not release equipment to an unverified person or rely only on a callback number provided by the caller.
- Do not share leftover prescriptions with anyone else.
- Do not mail medicines in an ordinary package.
- Do not flush medicines unless a take-back option is not readily available and the medicine is on the FDA flush list or its labeling tells you to flush it.
- Do not put loose needles or other sharps into household trash or recycling.
- Do not discard equipment labels, medication packaging, or paperwork before you have checked what needs to happen next.

## What to do now
1. **Separate the request into equipment and medications.** Ask exactly which equipment is being requested, which organization is arranging the pickup, and whether the caller is also asking about medications.

2. **Verify the equipment pickup through a trusted contact route.** If hospice was involved, call the hospice agency’s main number from existing paperwork or its official website. Otherwise, use a supplier label or existing paperwork to contact the durable medical equipment supplier. Do not rely only on a phone number sent by the caller.

3. **Record the equipment before it leaves.** Note the items and any serial or asset numbers. Take photos of labels. Ask which accessories should be collected. Leave connected equipment and tubing in place unless the hospice or supplier tells you what to move.

4. **Secure the medications.** Keep medicines in their original labeled containers where possible. Put the containers together in a secure place, ideally locked, and keep them out of reach of children and pets.

5. **Ask the hospice about leftover medications.** If hospice was involved, ask what it wants you to do with the medications. Do not assume that medication collection or transport is part of an equipment pickup.

6. **Use a take-back option when you are responsible for disposal.** A year-round drug drop-off location or a prepaid drug mail-back envelope is usually the preferred route. A pharmacy may be able to tell you which options are available nearby. Before using a drop-off location, scratch out personal information on pill-bottle labels and medicine packaging. For mail-back, follow the envelope instructions.

7. **Follow FDA guidance if take-back is not readily available.** Check whether a medicine is on the FDA flush list. For a medicine that is not on that list, FDA household-trash guidance generally involves removing it from its container, mixing it with an undesirable substance such as used coffee grounds, dirt, or cat litter, sealing the mixture, and scratching out personal information on the empty packaging. Do not crush tablets or capsules. Check product instructions or ask a pharmacist if you are unsure, especially for inhalers, patches, sprays, or lozenges.

8. **Arrange a documented equipment pickup.** Ask for a pickup window and the company name. Request a receipt or written confirmation listing the items collected. Ask before including anything that may have been personally purchased.

9. **Handle sharps separately.** Keep used needles and other sharps in a sharps disposal container. Follow local health department or trash-service guidance for disposal of the closed container.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to sort every medicine immediately. Secure the containers first.
- You do **not** need to decide immediately whether every equipment item was rented, supplied, or purchased. Verify the pickup and document the items first.
- You do **not** need to accept pressure to complete an unconfirmed handover immediately.

## Important reassurance
Requests can arrive quickly after a death, when it is hard to process details. Taking time to verify the pickup, secure medications, and keep a simple record is a reasonable response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later questions about equipment ownership, charges, insurance, estate matters, or a disputed pickup may need help from the supplier, hospice, insurer, or an appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Medication disposal options, local sharps rules, and supplier procedures can vary. When anything is unclear, pause and confirm through an official contact route.
