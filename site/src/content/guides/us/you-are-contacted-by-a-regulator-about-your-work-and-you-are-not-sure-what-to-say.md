# What to do if… you are contacted by a regulator about your work and you are not sure what to say (USA)

## Short answer
Exit the premises or disconnect from work systems immediately if directed, then secure a written confirmation of your leave status, final pay date, and benefits end date.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to bypass security or IT lockouts to "finish a project" or "say goodbye" to coworkers.
- Do not sign any "mutual separation agreement" or "release of claims" on the spot, even if told it is required to receive your notice pay.
- Do not download, forward, or print company-proprietary data, client lists, or trade secrets to "protect your work."
- Do not factory-reset company-issued phones or laptops; this can be characterized as destruction of evidence.
- Do not post details of the leave or lockout on LinkedIn or social media while still technically employed.
- Do not ignore explicit instructions to remain "available" during work hours if your leave is categorized as "administrative" or "garden leave."

## What to do now
1. **Comply with immediate physical or digital exit instructions without argument.** 
   - If in the office, gather personal belongings only (keys, phone, coat) and follow the escorted exit if one is provided.
   - If remote, log out of all company portals and stop using company devices if your access has been revoked.
2. **Request a formal status letter from HR via your personal email.** 
   - **Ask for the specific classification of the leave** (e.g., "Administrative Leave," "Garden Leave," or "Investigative Leave").
   - **Ask for confirmation that your original resignation date is still being honored.**
   - **Request a single point of contact for all future communications.**
3. **Inquire about the "rules of availability" while on paid leave.** 
   - **Ask if you are expected to be reachable during business hours** or if you are prohibited from contacting clients and colleagues.
   - **Confirm how you will receive official notices** if you no longer have access to internal systems.
4. **Secure your final pay and benefits timeline in writing.** 
   - **Ask for the specific date your final paycheck will be issued**, noting that deadlines are governed by state law (e.g., California requires immediate payment upon termination).
   - **Confirm the exact date your health insurance and other benefits expire.**
   - **Verify your mailing address on file** to ensure COBRA election notices (for employers with 20+ staff) arrive at the correct location.
5. **Begin a private personal timeline of events.** 
   - **Record the exact time of the lockout or notification.**
   - **Document the names of any witnesses or HR representatives involved.**
   - **Note the specific wording used by management** regarding the reason for the leave.
6. **Preserve personal employment records you already possess.** 
   - **Save copies of your offer letter, employment contract, and pay stubs** to a personal folder.
   - **Keep a copy of your original resignation email and any timestamped receipt.**
7. **Coordinate the return of company property formally.** 
   - **Request a written inventory list** of all items the company expects back (laptop, badge, keys, credit cards).
   - **Take photos of the equipment condition and serial numbers** before returning them via a trackable shipping method or in-person drop-off.
8. **Check your state’s "final pay" requirements.** 
   - **Consult your state Department of Labor website** to see if your employer must pay you "immediately" or by the "next regular payday" given the specific circumstances of your leave.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain your "absence" to colleagues or clients; the company is responsible for transition messaging.
- You do not need to provide a formal "rebuttal" to an investigation unless a specific allegation is made in writing.
- You do not need to decide today whether to file a claim for retaliation or discrimination.
- You do not need to hire an attorney in the next few hours, though you may wish to consult one before signing any final separation documents.

## Important reassurance
Being placed on paid leave (often called "Garden Leave" or "Notice Leave") is a common administrative practice used by U.S. employers to protect sensitive data or transition clients. It is not an automatic indicator of misconduct or a permanent mark on your professional record. Sudden lockouts are often a standard IT protocol rather than a personal judgment.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate hours after being placed on leave. It does not cover long-term legal strategy, unemployment eligibility appeals, or specific state-by-state litigation for wrongful termination.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and is not legal advice. Employment laws, final pay requirements, and COBRA/mini-COBRA rules vary significantly by state. If you are asked to sign a waiver of rights, are denied your final paycheck beyond state deadlines, or suspect the leave is a retaliatory act for a protected activity (e.g., whistleblowing), consult a qualified employment attorney in your jurisdiction.
