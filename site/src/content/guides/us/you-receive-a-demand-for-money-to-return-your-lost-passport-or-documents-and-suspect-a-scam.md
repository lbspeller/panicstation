# What to do if… you receive a demand for money to return your lost passport or documents and suspect a scam (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay, click links, or share more information. If a valid U.S. passport is missing, report it lost or stolen through the U.S. State Department, then use official channels to replace it safely.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money, gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfers, or app payments to “get your passport back”.
- Do not send a new photo or scan of your passport, visa, boarding pass, signature, Social Security number, or bank details to the person contacting you.
- Do not click links they send, install “tracking” apps, or enter details on a site they provide.
- Do not meet them alone or in a private place to exchange cash for documents.
- Do not trust a claim that they are from a U.S. agency, embassy, airport, hotel, taxi company, or courier unless you verify it through contact details you found yourself.

## What to do now
1. **Treat the demand as unsafe unless official channels confirm it.** A payment demand, urgency, secrecy, gift cards, cryptocurrency, or a link they want you to use is a scam warning sign, even if they know a real detail about you.
2. **Preserve evidence, then stop negotiating.** Screenshot messages, usernames, phone numbers, email addresses, links, payment instructions, wallet addresses, and any threats. Do not argue about the price or send proof that the documents are yours.
3. **If a valid U.S. passport is missing, report it lost or stolen.** Use the U.S. State Department process. Once a valid passport is reported lost or stolen, it is canceled and cannot be used for international travel, even if you later find it.
4. **If you are outside the U.S. and need to travel, contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate.** Use official U.S. government pages or your own saved embassy details, not contact details sent by the person demanding money. Ask about replacement or emergency passport steps.
5. **If it is safe and practical, file a local police report.** Ask for a report or incident number. A police report is not always mandatory, but it may help document the loss or theft.
6. **Verify any “we found it” claim yourself.** If the person says an airport, hotel, taxi, rideshare, bar, police station, or courier has your documents, contact that organisation using its official website, app, booking confirmation, or a number you already trust.
7. **If you paid or shared financial details, contact the payment provider now.** Use the number on the back of your card, your banking app, or the official payment-service app. Say you were targeted by a scam involving a passport or document “return” demand and ask whether the transaction can be stopped or reversed.
8. **If you shared identity details, write down exactly what was shared.** Include passport photos, driver’s licence photos, Social Security number, address, date of birth, account numbers, or login details. Use official identity-theft guidance for the next steps.
9. **If the contact happened online, report it to IC3.** Use the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center complaint form and include the screenshots and payment details you saved.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to confront the person or “prove” the passport is yours.
- You do not need to arrange a meetup, pay a small “test” amount, or keep messaging to see what they say next.
- You do not need to change every travel plan before you have started the official passport replacement process and know your realistic options.
- You do not need to post publicly about the missing passport while you are still securing the document and accounts.

## Important reassurance
This situation feels urgent because a passport controls travel, and scammers use that pressure. Using official replacement routes and refusing private payment demands is the safest way to regain control.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Replacing other documents, dealing with visas, changing travel plans, or identity-protection follow-up may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, security, immigration, or other professional advice. If you are being threatened in person, move toward a safer public place and call local emergency services.
