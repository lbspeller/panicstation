# What to do if… you are asked to speak at a service but you feel unable to do it (USA)

## Short answer
Notify the **funeral director** or the **officiant** immediately to activate a backup plan where your words are read by someone else or replaced by a different tribute.

## Do not do these things
- Don't wait until the service begins to see if the feeling "goes away" as this increases the risk of a mid-service freeze.
- Don't use alcohol or unprescribed sedatives to calm your nerves before the service.
- Don't feel obligated to explain the "reason" you cannot speak; simply stating you are unable is sufficient.
- Don't rely on a digital device (phone/tablet) as your only copy; screen timeouts and battery issues can cause panic.
- Don't force yourself to "power through" if you are experiencing physical symptoms like lightheadedness or chest tightness.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the primary coordinator for the service flow.**  
   - For services at a funeral home or secular venue, this is the **funeral director**.  
   - For services in a church, synagogue, or mosque, this is the **clergy or officiant**.  
2. **Send a clear, immediate notification that you cannot speak.**  
   **Use direct language:** "I have realized I am not able to speak at the service today. We need to use a backup plan for my part of the program."
3. **Select one of the three primary US backup options.**  
   - **The Officiant Read:** Ask the clergy or celebrant to read your prepared text as part of their address.  
   - **A Designated Surrogate:** Ask a friend or family member who is less emotionally close to the situation to read your words for you.  
   - **Silent Tribute:** Ask to have your written tribute printed in the **memorial folder** or **order of service** instead of being read aloud.  
4. **Prepare a "fail-safe" physical copy of your words.**  
   **Print your text in a large, 14-point or 16-point font with double spacing.**  
   - Write "BACKUP COPY" at the top.  
   - Include a clear instruction: "If I cannot continue, please finish reading from here."  
   - Hand this physical copy to the officiant or your backup reader at least 30 minutes before the service starts.  
5. **Request a physical "safety net" if you still intend to try.**  
   **Ask the funeral director to place a chair near the lectern or have a staff member stand nearby.**  
   - Knowing someone is ready to step in or that you can sit down immediately reduces the psychological pressure.  
6. **Limit your speaking time to under 60 seconds.**  
   **Reduce your text to three essential points:** a brief thank you to guests, one short memory, and a final goodbye.  
   - Keeping the duration minimal reduces the window for a panic response.  
7. **Opt for a non-vocal role if the "gap" needs filling.**  
   **Offer to perform a task that does not require speaking,** such as:  
   - Handing out memorial folders at the entrance.  
   - Placing a memento or flower on the casket or at the front.  
   - Lighting a memorial candle.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now how you will "make it up" to the family; your presence is the priority.
- You do not need to finalize a long-form written tribute; a few sincere sentences are enough for the day of the service.
- You do not need to worry about the "flow" of the service; funeral professionals are trained to adjust the program on the fly.

## Important reassurance
It is common and expected for grief to make public speaking physically or emotionally impossible. Funeral directors and clergy deal with last-minute changes to speakers frequently. Choosing a backup reader is seen by most guests as a respectful way to ensure the tribute is delivered clearly.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate pressure of an upcoming service. It focuses on practical coordination with US funeral professionals and does not address long-term grief counseling or public speaking training.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not professional funeral planning or legal advice. Technical capabilities for video/audio playback and the ability to change printed materials vary by venue and timing. Always verify technical requirements with the specific funeral home or house of worship involved.
