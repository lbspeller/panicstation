# What to do if… a hospice provider cannot start support without paperwork being completed today (USA)

## Short answer
Identify if the blocker is the **Election Statement** or the **Physician Certification**, then request that the hospice obtain a **verbal certification** from the doctor to initiate care immediately. Escalate to the hospice admissions supervisor or a physician member of the Interdisciplinary Group (IDG) to authorize a same-day nurse assessment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any document that you have not read or that contains inaccuracies regarding the patient's chosen attending physician.
- Do not assume a missing physical signature requires a 24-hour delay; Medicare permits verbal certifications for the start of care.
- Do not surrender original legal documents such as a Power of Attorney (POA) or Living Will; provide photocopies or digital images only.
- Do not prioritize billing or insurance coordination over immediate symptom management.
- Do not wait for hospice paperwork if the person is in active respiratory distress or experiencing uncontrollable pain.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific document preventing the start of care.**  
   **Ask the hospice intake coordinator to name the exact form and the required signer (e.g., patient, POA, or physician).** 
   - Most delays involve the **Hospice Election Statement** (signed by the patient or representative) or the **Certification of Terminal Illness** (signed by a doctor).
2. **Request a verbal certification from the attending or hospice physician.**  
   **If the written certification is the delay, ask the hospice to call the physician for a verbal certification.** 
   - Federal regulations (42 CFR 418.22) allow care to begin based on a verbal statement that the patient is terminally ill, provided written documentation follows within two calendar days.
3. **Confirm the identity of the authorized representative.**  
   **If the patient cannot sign, verify who is legally permitted to sign as the "authorized representative" under state law.** 
   - Ask the hospice if they accept digital signatures (e-signatures) or scanned copies of a signed form to expedite the process.
4. **Escalate to a hospice Interdisciplinary Group (IDG) physician.**  
   **Request to speak with the on-call Medical Director or an IDG physician member.** 
   - Under 2026 rules, any physician member of the hospice IDG can recommend and authorize the admission to prevent clinical delays.
5. **Contact the referring doctor's office directly.**  
   **Call the physician who made the hospice referral and ask them to provide the verbal certification or fax the signed orders immediately.** 
   - State clearly: "The hospice cannot start care until they receive your certification of terminal illness; please provide a verbal or faxed confirmation now."
6. **Request an immediate clinical assessment for uncontrolled symptoms.**  
   **If the person is in pain or distress, state: "We have a clinical crisis that cannot wait for administrative processing."** 
   - Ask for a nurse to be dispatched for a "start of care" assessment while the final paperwork is being routed.
7. **Transition to emergency services if safety cannot be maintained.**  
   **Call 911 or go to the nearest Emergency Department if the hospice cannot provide a clinical response and symptoms are life-threatening.** 
   - Inform emergency responders that a hospice referral is in progress but care has not yet been initiated.

## What can wait
- Receiving the **Election Statement Addendum** (the list of non-covered items) does not need to happen before care starts.
- Decisions regarding the long-term schedule of hospice aide visits or chaplain services.
- Finalizing the delivery of non-essential medical supplies (e.g., secondary mobility aids).
- Resolving minor discrepancies in insurance secondary-payer sequences.

## Important reassurance
Administrative delays in hospice are frequent and often result from strict Medicare billing requirements rather than a lack of clinical need. Identifying the specific "blocker" allows you to address the technicality without assuming the delay is a permanent denial of care. Feelings of frustration or urgency are a normal response to these procedural barriers.

## Scope note
This guide addresses the immediate transition into hospice care when administrative hurdles arise. It does not cover long-term hospice management, the appeals process for benefit denials, or legal disputes over guardianship.

## Important note
This information is for stabilization purposes and does not constitute legal or medical advice. Procedures may vary by state law and specific insurance payer requirements (Medicare vs. private Advantage plans). Always prioritize the physical safety and comfort of the patient over document completion.
