# What to do if… you lose access to your work email and systems without warning (USA)

## Short answer
Assume it could be an IT or security hold, an admin mistake, or a sudden employment action. Do not try to work around the lockout; contact your manager and IT or security through a known non-work channel, and ask for written confirmation of your status and next steps.

## Do not do these things
- Do not bypass access controls by using personal email, personal cloud storage, a coworker’s login, or asking someone to send work files to you.
- Do not keep guessing passwords or repeatedly triggering password resets.
- Do not click password-reset links from messages you did not request, and do not sign in through unfamiliar pages.
- Do not delete files, wipe a work laptop or phone, or try to “clean up” anything.
- Do not post publicly, mass-message coworkers, or argue in group chats about what you think happened.
- Do not quit, admit fault, or sign anything on the spot because you feel cornered.

## What to do now
1. **Write down what happened while it is fresh.** Note the time access stopped, what you cannot reach, such as email, VPN, HR portal, calendar, chat, or shared drives, and any exact error messages. Take a photo or screenshot if that is allowed and does not expose confidential data.

2. **Contact your manager or supervisor from a known non-work channel.** Use a calm message such as: “I just lost access to my work email and systems. Can you confirm whether this is an IT or security issue, and who I should speak to next?”

3. **Contact IT, helpdesk, or security through an official route you already know.** Say: “My account is inaccessible. Please confirm whether it is locked for security and tell me the official recovery or verification steps.” Use a phone number, portal, or contact route you already trust, not a link from a surprise message.

4. **Treat it as a possible security incident until IT says otherwise.** If you clicked a suspicious link, approved an unexpected login prompt, shared a code, or entered your password on an unusual page, tell IT or security directly. If you reused the same or very similar password on personal accounts, change those personal passwords from a device you trust, but do not move or copy work data.

5. **If you are told you are suspended or under investigation, keep the paper trail clean.** Ask for written confirmation of your status, the HR or employee-relations contact, and what you are allowed to do while locked out, including whether you may contact coworkers, attend meetings, or access any employee portals.

6. **If you are told you are terminated, keep the conversation practical.** Ask HR for written confirmation of your employment status, how and when you will receive final pay or owed wages, how benefits or portals will be handled, and how to return company property or collect personal belongings.

7. **If the lockout happened soon after you raised workplace concerns, document before escalating.** Keep a private timeline of dates, messages, meetings, and the lockout. If the concern involved pay, hours, working conditions, discrimination, harassment, safety, or coworkers acting together, avoid accusations in the moment and get advice before making formal claims.

8. **If fraud, phishing, or identity theft may be involved, take the immediate protective steps.** If money moved or financial details were exposed, contact your bank or card issuer now. If personal information such as your Social Security number was exposed, use an official identity-theft reporting route. If the issue appears cyber-enabled, consider reporting it through federal fraud or cybercrime reporting channels.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to threaten legal action, resign, sign severance paperwork, or argue the whole situation by message.
- You do not need to solve every paycheck, benefits, unemployment, or equipment issue in the first hour.
- You do not need to contact every external agency immediately unless there is clear fraud, identity theft, discrimination, retaliation, or another urgent reason.
- You do not need to explain yourself to coworkers before you know whether this is IT, security, HR, or employment-related.

## Important reassurance
A sudden lockout can feel personal and frightening, but it can happen for routine IT reasons as well as employment reasons. The safest first move is to avoid workarounds, record the basic facts, and get clear written instructions from official channels.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to help you stabilize the first hours and avoid irreversible mistakes. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if you are suspended, terminated, asked to sign documents, accused of misconduct, or concerned about discrimination, retaliation, unpaid wages, fraud, or identity theft.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, employment, or professional advice. Employment rules and final-pay rules vary by state and by workplace policy, and the right next step depends on your exact situation.
