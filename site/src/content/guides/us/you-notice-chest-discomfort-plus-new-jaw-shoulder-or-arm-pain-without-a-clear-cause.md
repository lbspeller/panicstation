# What to do if… you notice chest discomfort plus new jaw, shoulder, or arm pain without a clear cause (USA)

## Short answer
Call **911 immediately** — unexplained chest discomfort combined with pain in the jaw, shoulder, or arm is a potential cardiac emergency and requires professional assessment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not **drive yourself** to the hospital or ask someone else to drive you unless 911 dispatchers explicitly instruct you to do so.
- Do not **wait to see if it passes** or try to "rule out" the cause by exercising, walking around, or lying flat.
- Do not take **aspirin** unless you are specifically told to by the 911 operator or your clinician.
- Do not take **anyone else's** prescription medication, including nitroglycerin.
- Do not take **new painkillers** (like ibuprofen or naproxen) as these will not treat a cardiac event and may mask symptoms.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all physical activity and sit down immediately.** **Sit on the floor with your back supported by a wall or sturdy furniture to prevent injury if you become dizzy or faint.**
2. **Call 911 and state clearly: "I have chest discomfort with pain in my [jaw / shoulder / arm]."** 
   - Keep the phone next to you and put it on speaker.
3. **Follow the dispatcher's instructions exactly without deviation.** 
   - Ask the dispatcher: "Should I take an aspirin?"
4. **If the dispatcher advises you to take aspirin, and you have no allergy or history of bleeding issues, take the dose provided.**
   - **The standard emergency dose is one regular-strength (325 mg) aspirin or four low-dose (81 mg) aspirins.**
   - **Chew the aspirin rather than swallowing it whole to speed up absorption.**
   - **Do not give aspirin to anyone under the age of 18.**
   - **Do not take aspirin if you have a known bleeding disorder or active stomach ulcers.**
5. **If you have been personally prescribed nitroglycerin for chest pain, use it as directed by your clinician.** **Inform the 911 dispatcher that you have taken it and provide the exact time and dosage.**
6. **Prepare the environment for emergency responders.** **Unlock the front door, turn on outside lights, and if someone else is present, have them wait outside to flag down the ambulance.**
7. **Gather essential medical information if you can do so without overexertion.** 
   - A list of current medications (especially blood thinners).
   - A list of allergies.
   - A brief history of heart conditions, recent surgeries, or major bleeding.
8. **Stay on the line with the 911 operator until they tell you to hang up.** **Immediately report any new or worsening symptoms, such as increased pain, difficulty breathing, or feeling like you may pass out.**
9. **If the person becomes unresponsive and stops breathing normally, follow dispatcher guidance for CPR.** **If an Automated External Defibrillator (AED) is available nearby, have someone retrieve it immediately.**

## What can wait
- Determining the exact cause of the discomfort.
- Contacting your primary care physician or local clinic.
- Packing personal items or a hospital bag.
- Notifying your employer or arranging long-term childcare.
- Cleaning up the house or finishing tasks.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel hesitant or worry that symptoms are "not serious enough" to justify an emergency call. Feeling uncertain, anxious, or fearful is a normal reaction to sudden physical discomfort. Emergency services are equipped to handle cases where it turns out not to be a heart attack; prioritize the safety of a professional evaluation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization instructions only. It does not replace professional medical diagnosis or the treatment provided by emergency medical services (EMS).

## Important note
Chest discomfort paired with radiating pain is a high-priority medical symptom. This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute medical advice. If you or someone else is experiencing these symptoms, call 911 now.
