# What to do if… your blood sugar reading is low and keeps dropping despite eating (USA)

## Short answer
Consume **15 grams of fast-acting glucose immediately** and re-check your levels in **15 minutes**. If your blood sugar remains below 70 mg/dL or continues to drop after two treatment cycles, or if you become too confused to swallow safely, call **911** immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive or operate machinery until your blood sugar is stable and within your target range.
- Do not go to sleep while your blood sugar is actively dropping or below 70 mg/dL.
- Do not use high-fat or high-protein foods (like chocolate or peanut butter) for initial treatment; they absorb too slowly.
- Do not drink alcohol, which can further inhibit the liver's ability to release glucose.
- Do not attempt to swallow food or liquid if you are feeling severely drowsy or are having trouble swallowing.
- Do not delay calling for emergency help if symptoms worsen or the "15-15 rule" fails twice.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all physical activity and find a safe place to sit or lie down.** If you are alone, move to a position on the floor to prevent injury from a fall and unlock your front door.
2. **Confirm your reading with a fingerstick if you are using a continuous glucose monitor (CGM).** CGMs may have a lag time of several minutes; a fingerstick provides the most current blood glucose value for immediate treatment decisions.
3. **Consume 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate immediately.** Pure glucose is the preferred treatment.
   - 3 to 4 glucose tablets
   - 1 tube of glucose gel
   - 4 ounces (1/2 cup) of fruit juice or regular (non-diet) soda
   - 1 tablespoon of honey, corn syrup, or table sugar
4. **Wait 15 minutes and re-test your blood sugar.** If the reading is still below 70 mg/dL or your CGM still shows a downward arrow, consume another 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate.
5. **Call 911 if your blood sugar does not rise above 70 mg/dL after two treatments.** Emergency medical services are required if you cannot keep glucose down, if you lose consciousness, or if you are unable to administer treatment yourself.
6. **Administer emergency glucagon if the person is unable to swallow or is unconscious.** If you are a caregiver:
   - Follow the instructions in the glucagon kit (nasal spray or injection).
   - **Call 911 immediately** after administration.
   - Place the person in the recovery position (on their side) to prevent choking if they vomit.
   - If the person does not respond after 15 minutes, a second dose of glucagon may be given if available while waiting for paramedics.
7. **Eat a long-acting carbohydrate snack once your blood sugar is above 70 mg/dL.** If your next scheduled meal is more than an hour away, have a snack containing both starch and protein (such as crackers with cheese) to stabilize your levels.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause of the low (missed meal, exercise, or medication error) until you are physically stable.
- You do not need to adjust your long-term insulin doses or medication schedule during the acute low.
- You do not need to document the event in detail until your glucose has normalized and the immediate risk has passed.

## Important reassurance
Sudden drops in blood sugar trigger a physical stress response that commonly causes feelings of intense anxiety, shakiness, and urgency. These sensations are a result of your body's survival mechanisms and will typically subside once your glucose levels are restored.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute hypoglycemic event. It does not replace a long-term diabetes management plan or medical consultation for recurrent low blood sugar.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only and is not a substitute for professional medical advice or diagnosis. If your blood sugar remains low despite treatment, or if you experience severe symptoms like confusion or seizures, contact emergency services immediately.
