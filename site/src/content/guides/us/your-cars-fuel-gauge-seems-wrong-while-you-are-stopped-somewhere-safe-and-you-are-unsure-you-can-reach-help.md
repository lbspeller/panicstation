# What to do if… your car’s fuel gauge seems wrong while you are stopped somewhere safe and you are unsure you can reach help (USA)

## Short answer
Assume the fuel level is lower than the gauge indicates and do not drive to test the accuracy. Use roadside assistance for fuel delivery or a tow unless a refueling station is visible and accessible via low-speed roads.

## Do not do these things
- Don't drive further to "test" if the gauge will eventually move or reset.
- Don't walk along the shoulder of a high-speed freeway or expressway to find a gas station.
- Don't use a mobile phone or any electronic device within 50 feet of the vehicle if you smell gasoline or see a leak.
- Don't attempt to siphon fuel from another vehicle or perform roadside repairs on the fuel system.
- Don't stand directly behind or in front of the vehicle while waiting for help on a shoulder.

## What to do now
1. **Stabilize your current position and signal for visibility.** **Turn on your hazard lights if you are stopped on a shoulder or in a location where you may be a hazard to other drivers.**
   - Engage the emergency brake and turn the wheels away from the travel lane.
   - Keep your phone connected to a charger to maintain communication.
   - Stay inside the vehicle with seatbelts fastened if you are on a high-speed shoulder, unless there is a safer area behind a guardrail or embankment.

2. **Verify your approximate fuel status using known data points.** **Review your trip odometer or last known refueling time to estimate your actual range.**
   - Compare your miles driven since the last fill-up against your vehicle’s typical fuel economy.
   - Treat any "miles to empty" or "range" estimate as an optimistic maximum rather than a guarantee.

3. **Observe the area around the vehicle for external signs of a leak.** **Look for liquid pooling under the rear of the car or near the fuel tank area without exiting the vehicle into traffic.**
   - Notice if there is a pungent, unmistakable smell of raw gasoline.
   - If a leak is visible or the smell is strong, **do not attempt to start the engine** and move at least 50 feet away from the vehicle.

4. **Contact a roadside assistance provider for fuel delivery or recovery.** **Call 800-AAA-HELP (800-222-4357) or use your insurance provider’s mobile app to request emergency fuel.**
   - Specify whether you need gasoline or diesel.
   - Note that most basic roadside plans (like AAA Classic) require you to pay the market price for the fuel delivered, while higher tiers may include 1–2 gallons for free.
   - If you are on a state-owned primary roadway, you may be able to dial #77 to reach highway patrol or emergency response technicians.

5. **Wait for assistance in the safest possible configuration.** **Remain in the vehicle with the doors locked if you are in a safe parking lot or rest area.**
   - If you must wait on a highway shoulder, move to the passenger side of the vehicle or exit through the passenger door to stand behind a barrier.
   - Keep a window slightly cracked for fresh air if you must run the engine for climate control, and ensure the exhaust pipe is not obstructed.

## What can wait
- Determining if the issue is a faulty fuel sender, a software glitch, or a dashboard cluster failure.
- Seeking a permanent mechanical repair or sensor replacement.
- Verifying the exact accuracy of the gauge through further driving.

## Important reassurance
It is common for fuel gauges to fail due to sensor "dead spots" or float arm issues, and it is normal to feel uncertain when a primary safety metric becomes unreliable. Choosing to stay stationary and call for professional fuel delivery is a standard safety procedure that prevents the much higher risk of a total engine stall in an unmanaged traffic lane.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for users who are currently stopped and uncertain about their fuel levels. It does not cover long-term mechanical diagnostics or repair procedures for fuel systems.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional mechanical or legal advice. In any situation where your location is immediately dangerous—such as being stalled in a live travel lane or experiencing extreme weather—contact 911 immediately. Roadside assistance response times vary and should not be relied upon in life-threatening emergencies.
