# What to do if… you are asked to document your work processes and you suspect your job is being outsourced (USA)

## Short answer
Ask for the scope and priority in writing, then keep a dated, non-confidential record of what was requested and what you delivered. Use company systems and do not copy company files, personal data, passwords, or confidential information into personal accounts or devices.

## Do not do these things
- Do not accuse leadership of outsourcing or send speculative messages while you are still gathering facts.
- Do not delete, alter, or withhold files, tickets, emails, code, documentation, or work history.
- Do not move company files or data to personal email, personal cloud storage, or personal devices.
- Do not send an emotional refusal. Ask for a written scope and priority first.
- Do not share confidential business information or personal data while checking in with coworkers. Discussions about working conditions are separate.
- Do not sign a severance agreement, release, or new confidentiality agreement on the spot. Ask for time to review it and seek qualified advice.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for a written scope.**  
   If the request was verbal, send a brief recap asking which processes are needed, how detailed the notes should be, where they should be stored, who will use them, and the deadline.

2. **Ask for priorities and handling instructions.**  
   Ask which workflows should be documented first. Ask how sensitive information must be handled. Do not place live passwords, access tokens, personal data, or confidential material in ordinary process notes. Ask for an approved secure location or a redacted procedure where needed.

3. **Keep a dated, non-confidential log.**  
   Record the date of each request, the scope, the deadline, and the file names or company-system locations you delivered. Do not copy the contents of company documents into a personal record.

4. **Deliver neutral process notes through company systems.**  
   Use the approved wiki, shared drive, ticketing system, or other company tool. Keep the notes factual and ask whether the format and level of detail meet the request.

5. **Ask one calm question about the context.**  
   For example: “Is this for cross-training and business continuity, or is there an organizational change planned that affects this role or team?”

6. **Use support that fits your situation.**  
   If you are in a union, contact your union representative with the facts. If you need state-specific advice, consider a qualified employment adviser or attorney. Official National Labor Relations Board pages describe protected concerted activity for covered employees, but coverage and protection depend on the facts.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether the request proves that outsourcing is coming.
- You do not need to resign, confront leadership, or make a public announcement.
- You do not need to determine whether federal or state layoff-notice rules apply while no layoff has been announced. If a layoff is announced, check the official U.S. Department of Labor WARN information and any relevant state guidance.
- You do not need to negotiate severance or release terms unless you receive something in writing.

## Important reassurance
A request for detailed process notes can be routine, including cross-training, continuity planning, or audit preparation. It can also happen before organizational change. Asking for a clear scope, using company systems, and keeping a factual record are useful first steps either way.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and prevent irreversible mistakes. If a layoff, outsourcing plan, severance agreement, or dispute becomes explicit, later decisions may need state-specific specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employment rights and obligations vary by state, work status, contract terms, and the facts of the situation.
