# What to do if… you develop new weakness in both legs or worsening trouble walking over a few hours (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a medical emergency. Call 911 immediately and report new or rapidly worsening weakness in both legs or trouble walking that has developed over hours.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to drive yourself to the hospital or "walk it off."
- Do not use stairs, attempt to shower, or carry heavy objects while your balance is unreliable.
- Do not ignore changes in your ability to urinate or any new numbness in your groin or buttocks.
- Do not wait for an appointment or visit an urgent care clinic, as they may lack the imaging equipment required for neurological emergencies.
- Do not eat or drink if you are also experiencing slurred speech or difficulty swallowing.

## What to do now
1. **Sit or lie down in a stable position immediately to prevent a fall.** 
   - Choose a location where you are unlikely to slip or roll.
   - Keep your phone within reach.
2. **Call 911 and state that you have "new, rapidly worsening weakness in both legs."** 
   - Specify if the weakness is moving upward from your feet.
   - If you are alone, unlock your front door only if you can do so without risking a fall.
3. **Check for and report these specific sensory cues to the dispatcher:** 
   - Loss of sensation in the "saddle area" (groin, genitals, or around the anus).
   - Reduced sensation when wiping with toilet paper.
   - Inability to feel if your bladder is full or inability to start urinating.
   - Sudden "pins and needles" or numbness spreading from the feet toward the hips.
4. **Identify the exact time you first noticed the change or when it significantly worsened.** 
   - Write this time down or save it in a phone note for the paramedics.
   - Note if you have had a recent cough, cold, or stomach upset in the last few weeks.
5. **Note any "FAST" symptoms that began at the same time as the walking trouble.** 
   - Check for face drooping, arm weakness (on one or both sides), or difficulty speaking.
   - Report any sudden, severe headache or vision changes.
6. **Gather your ID and a list of current medications if they are within easy reach.** 
   - Prioritize noting if you take any blood thinners (anticoagulants).
   - Do not move around the house to find these items; prioritize staying seated.

## What can wait
- You do not need to figure out the cause (such as a "slipped disc" or "trapped nerve") before seeking help.
- You do not need to "test" your strength by standing up or walking around repeatedly.
- You do not need to pack a bag or prepare for a long stay; medical assessment is the only priority.
- You do not need to wait for a family member to arrive before calling 911.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel hesitant about calling emergency services for symptoms that may seem "internal" or hard to describe. Rapidly worsening weakness in both legs is a recognized medical emergency. Paramedics and ER staff are trained to prioritize these symptoms because early intervention is critical for protecting nerve function.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and emergency navigation only. A hospital evaluation will likely involve a neurological exam and urgent imaging (such as an MRI or CT scan) to determine the source of the weakness.

## Important note
This information is for emergency guidance only and is not a substitute for professional medical diagnosis. Sudden or progressive bilateral leg weakness, especially when combined with bladder changes or "saddle" numbness, requires immediate emergency evaluation.
