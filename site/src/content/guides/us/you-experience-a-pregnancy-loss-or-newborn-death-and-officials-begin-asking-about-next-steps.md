# What to do if… you experience a pregnancy loss or newborn death and officials begin asking about next steps (USA)

## Short answer
Request the presence of a hospital social worker, bereavement coordinator, or patient advocate to act as your primary liaison and pause all non-urgent paperwork until they arrive. Request that all options, legal requirements, and deadlines be provided to you in writing before you authorize any procedures or releases.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign any authorization forms—including autopsy consent, release of remains, or medical testing—until you have had a designated "quiet period" to review them.
- Don’t feel obligated to provide a detailed narrative or timeline of events while in immediate shock; "I cannot answer that right now" is a valid response.
- Don’t assume you must choose a funeral home or disposition method within the first few hours; most hospitals can provide a 24-to-48-hour window for these decisions.
- Don’t allow staff to remove or discard personal items, blankets, or mementos without your explicit verbal or written instruction.
- Don’t agree to a full autopsy if you have religious or personal objections without first asking about "limited" or "partial" examination options.

## What to do now
1. **Request a primary support liaison and a private location.** **Ask for a social worker, bereavement coordinator, or patient advocate to come to your room immediately.**
   - Request that all hospital staff (nurses, physicians, and administrative officials) direct their questions through this single point of contact to reduce repetitive questioning.
2. **Clarify the identity and authority of everyone asking questions.** **Ask for the name, title, and specific department of any official seeking information or signatures.**
   - Determine if the person is from hospital administration, the medical examiner’s office, or a state vital records department.
3. **Verify the involvement of the Medical Examiner or Coroner.** **Ask: "Is the Medical Examiner/Coroner legally required to investigate this death, or is this a hospital-level inquiry?"**
   - If it is a Medical Examiner (ME) case, ask which specific steps are mandated by state law and which are optional.
   - If it is not an ME case, confirm that any hospital-requested autopsy is strictly optional and requires your written consent.
4. **Inquire about "cooling" options to extend your time.** **Ask if the facility has a "Cuddle Cot" or a cooling bassinet available.**
   - These tools allow you to keep your baby with you in your room for a longer period while you process decisions and wait for family to arrive.
5. **Request a written list of mandatory versus optional actions.** **Say: "I need a written list of every form that requires a signature and the specific deadline for each one."**
   - This prevents verbal information from being lost or misunderstood during shock.
6. **Establish your rights regarding "memory-making."** **State your wishes clearly regarding seeing, holding, bathing, or photographing your baby.**
   - You have the right to request mementos such as hand/footprints, a lock of hair, or hospital identification bands; ask the bereavement coordinator to facilitate this.
7. **Address the autopsy request with specific clarifiers.** **If an autopsy is proposed, ask the following questions before signing:**
   - Is there a fee for this procedure (hospital autopsies are typically free to the family)?
   - Can the examination be limited to a specific organ or area to respect our wishes?
   - Will the procedure delay our ability to hold a viewing or funeral?
8. **Clarify the documentation process for your specific situation.** **Ask about the different types of certificates based on the gestational age and state law.**
   - For a live birth followed by death, ask how the hospital coordinates the birth and death certificates.
   - For a stillbirth (typically 20+ weeks), ask about the "Report of Fetal Death" and if your state offers an optional "Certificate of Birth Resulting in Stillbirth" for your records.
9. **Authorize a "Decision Shield" support person.** **Designate a trusted friend or family member to handle logistics, such as calling funeral homes to compare basic services or arranging transportation.**

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to have a funeral home selected before the baby is moved to the hospital morgue or a cooling bassinet.
- You do **not** need to decide on specific burial or cremation details (casket, urn, service type) in the first minutes or hours.
- You do **not** need to notify your employer, insurance company, or extended family immediately; focusing on the immediate pause is the priority.
- You do **not** have to decide right now whether you want to keep mementos; you can ask the hospital to store them securely for you to collect later.

## Important reassurance
The feeling of being overwhelmed, unable to process words, or "frozen" is a standard physiological response to acute loss. Hospital staff are trained to wait; you are not "taking too long" by asking for a pause or for information to be repeated in writing.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for interacting with hospital and local officials immediately following a loss. It does not cover long-term legal claims, insurance navigation, or specific state-by-state funeral laws.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal or medical advice. Laws regarding fetal death reporting and the disposition of remains vary significantly by state. Always confirm mandatory requirements with your hospital's legal department or a patient advocate.
