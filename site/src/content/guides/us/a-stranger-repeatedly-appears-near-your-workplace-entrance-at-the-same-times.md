# What to do if… a stranger repeatedly appears near your workplace entrance at the same times (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause first, such as inside the building, near staff, or in a staffed nearby business. Report the repeated pattern to a manager, security, reception, or building management now, and call 911 if you are in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the person alone.
- Do not go to your car, bus stop, rideshare pickup, or isolated area alone if you feel unsafe.
- Do not post your schedule, workplace entrance, parking location, or live location publicly.
- Do not follow the person, corner them, or move closer to get photos.
- Do not wait until you have perfect proof before asking for help.
- Do not describe the person as suspicious because of appearance alone; report only what you saw them do, where they were, and when it happened.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** Go inside, stay near reception or security, or enter a staffed nearby business. Avoid standing alone outside the entrance while you decide what to do.
2. **Tell the workplace contact who can act today.** Say: “The same stranger has repeatedly appeared near this entrance at the same times. I do not feel safe entering or leaving alone.” Ask who is coordinating the response right now.
3. **Ask for support for your next arrival or exit.** Ask for a security escort, a coworker to walk with you, someone to meet you at the entrance, or permission to use a busier door, lobby, parking area, or pickup point.
4. **Ask the workplace or building to log and preserve what they can.** Ask them to record the dates, times, location, witnesses, and any available camera footage or access-control information. Ask promptly because footage may not be available for long.
5. **Write a brief factual log for yourself.** Record the date, time, exact place, what the person did, how long they stayed, whether they approached or followed you, any safely visible vehicle details, and the names of anyone who saw it.
6. **Use visibility for the next few entries and exits.** Arrive or leave with another person when possible, stay in well-lit and populated areas, and use a staffed lobby or front entrance for rideshare, taxi, or pickup.
7. **Call 911 now if there is immediate danger.** Call if the person approaches you in a threatening way, blocks you, follows you, tries to get into your car or building with you, threatens you, or you believe an emergency is happening.
8. **For a non-emergency repeated pattern, contact local police.** Use the local police non-emergency number or local online reporting if available. Share the factual pattern and ask for an incident or report number.
9. **Ask about property-based options without assuming they apply.** If the entrance, lobby, lot, or garage is private property, ask building management, security, or local police what options may apply for trespass warnings, removal, or extra patrols in your area.
10. **Tell one trusted person outside work.** Share when you expect to leave, how you are getting home, and when you will check in.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this is stalking, loitering, harassment, casing, or a misunderstanding.
- You do not need to identify the person or gather evidence beyond safe notes and workplace reporting.
- You do not need to quit, move, change your whole routine, or make a major life decision in the first hour.
- You do not need to handle this alone before involving workplace security, building staff, or local police.

## Important reassurance
Repeated appearances at the same place and time can be enough reason to ask for support. Reporting the pattern early is a practical safety step, not an accusation you have to prove on your own.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, safe documentation, and getting the right people involved. If the pattern continues, later decisions may need help from workplace security, local law enforcement, a victim advocate, or a qualified professional in your area.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, security, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
