# What to do if… you are asked to pay for training or equipment upfront before your first day at a new job (USA)

## Short answer
Establish the purpose of the meeting immediately by asking if it is an investigatory interview that could lead to discipline. If you are union-represented, formally invoke your Weingarten rights to have a representative present before answering any questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign, "agree to separate," or sign any document terminating your employment under immediate pressure.
- Do not record the meeting secretly, as this is a criminal offense in all-party consent states (e.g., CA, FL, IL, PA) and often a fireable violation of company policy.
- Do not speculate, guess, or offer "hypothetical" explanations for your behavior or the behavior of others.
- Do not volunteer information about your personal life, personal devices, or activities outside of work unless specifically asked.
- Do not refuse to attend the meeting; in most non-union settings, refusing to participate in an investigation is considered insubordination and is grounds for termination.

## What to do now
1. **Take a few seconds to gather a pen and paper for note-taking before entering.** Sending a brief, timestamped email or text to yourself or a trusted contact stating who called the meeting, the time, and the location creates an immediate external record.
2. **Ask for the specific purpose and agenda of the meeting before the questioning begins.** Use precise phrasing:
   - "Is this an investigatory interview that could result in disciplinary action?"
   - "What is the specific topic or incident we are discussing today?"
3. **If you are union-represented, invoke your right to representation if you believe discipline is possible.** State clearly: "If this discussion could in any way lead to my being disciplined or terminated, or affect my personal working conditions, I respectfully request that my union representative, officer, or steward be present at this meeting."
   - Once requested, the employer must either:
     - Grant the request and delay the meeting until a representative arrives.
     - Deny the request and end the meeting immediately.
     - Give you the choice to proceed without representation or end the meeting.
4. **If you are not union-represented, still request a witness or coworker to be present.** While current NLRB rules for private-sector non-union employees do not mandate this, making the request establishes that you are seeking "mutual aid and protection," which may offer some legal shielding later.
5. **Listen for "investigatory" cues that indicate the meeting is not a routine check-in.** These include:
   - The presence of a second manager or an HR staffer solely there to take notes.
   - Questions focused on "why" a past event happened rather than "how" to do a future task.
   - Mention of specific dates, times, or company policies.
6. **Provide factual, minimalist answers and state "I don't recall" if you are unsure.** If the meeting involves complex data or emails you haven't seen in months, ask for copies of the documents before providing a response.
7. **Request to take any disciplinary or "write-up" documents home to review before signing.** If the employer insists on a signature for "receipt" only, write "received for review only" next to your signature and add the date and time.
8. **Document the encounter immediately after leaving the room.** Record the names of everyone present, the specific questions asked, your responses, and the "vibe" or tone of the room while the details are fresh.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to hire an attorney or file an EEOC/NLRB charge.
- Writing a "rebuttal" or a long emotional defense of your actions.
- Analyzing the "true" motivation of your manager or coworkers.
- Worrying about your long-term career path or future at the company.

## Important reassurance
An unexplained summons to HR is a high-stress event, and feeling a "fight or flight" response is a normal physiological reaction to ambiguity and perceived threat. You have the right to take a breath, ask clarifying questions, and insist on a formal, documented process rather than a rushed conversation.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for the immediate summons and initial meeting. It does not cover legal strategy for wrongful termination, long-term labor disputes, or specific state-level "just cause" protections.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and is not legal advice. Employment law in the USA is highly variable; your rights may differ based on whether you are in the public or private sector, your state’s "at-will" status, and the specific terms of your employment contract or collective bargaining agreement. If you believe your rights have been violated, consult with a qualified employment attorney or your local NLRB or EEOC field office.
