# What to do if… lights flicker in multiple rooms and you are unsure if it’s an electrical fault (USA)

## Short answer
Treat multi-room flickering as a potential fire or shock hazard. Reduce the electrical load immediately by turning off high-draw appliances and contact your utility provider or a licensed electrician to rule out a dangerous "open neutral" or panel failure.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore flickering that affects multiple rooms or the whole house, as this often indicates a problem with the main service entrance or utility line.
- Don’t keep high-wattage appliances running (space heaters, dryers, ovens, air conditioners) to see if the flickering stops.
- Don’t touch the electrical panel if you are wet, standing in water, or if the panel itself is buzzing or sparking.
- Don’t remove the "dead front" (the inner cover) of the electrical panel; only operate the external switches.
- Don’t approach any downed power lines outside; stay at least 35 feet away at all times.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate fire or shock cues.**
   **If you see smoke, sparks, or scorch marks, or if you smell burning plastic/ozone or hear loud buzzing from the panel, take these steps:**
   - **Evacuate the immediate area** of the hazard.
   - **Switch OFF the Main Breaker** only if the panel is dry and shows no signs of active arcing or fire.
   - **Call 911** if there is active smoke or fire.

2. **Isolate the house from heavy electrical loads.**
   **Unplug or switch off all high-power devices to prevent further stress on a potentially loose connection.**
   - Items to turn off: window AC units, space heaters, hair dryers, microwaves, and electric clothes dryers.
   - Leave only a few low-wattage LED or incandescent lights on to monitor the status of the power.

3. **Check for a "tingle" or "shock" sensation.**
   **If you feel a slight vibration or tingle when touching a metal appliance, stop using it immediately.**
   - This indicates a dangerous grounding or "open neutral" condition where electricity is seeking an alternative path through the appliance casing.

4. **Verify if the issue is neighborhood-wide.**
   **Look outside at streetlights or neighbors' windows to see if they are also experiencing flickering.**
   - If multiple homes are affected, **call your electric utility’s emergency line** to report a "power quality issue" or potential neutral failure.
   - If it is only your home, the fault is likely in your meter socket, main panel, or service mast.

5. **Test a tripped breaker exactly once.**
   **Open the panel door and look for a breaker handle that is stuck in the middle "tripped" position.**
   - **Switch it fully OFF, then back to ON.**
   - **If it trips again immediately, leave it OFF.** Repeatedly resetting a breaker can cause a fire if there is a short circuit.

6. **Contact a professional for a "neutral" check.**
   **Call a licensed electrician and mention that lights are flickering in multiple rooms.**
   - Specifically ask them to check for a "loose or open neutral" at the main panel or meter, as this can cause voltage to surge in some rooms while dropping in others, damaging electronics.

7. **Maintain 35 feet of distance from exterior lines.**
   **If you see a downed wire or a leaning utility pole, do not approach it.**
   - Electricity can travel through the ground (step potential); stay back at least 35 feet and call 911 and the utility company immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to replace individual light bulbs or light switches yet.
- You do not need to investigate the wiring inside your walls or outlets yourself.
- You do not need to decide between a utility problem or a home fault immediately; once the load is reduced, the primary danger is lowered while you wait for a professional.

## Important reassurance
Flickering across multiple rooms is often a sign of a loose connection at a central point, such as the utility transformer or your main panel. While this requires professional attention, reducing the electrical load and isolating the power is a highly effective way to prevent escalation. Professionals deal with these issues daily and can typically identify the source quickly.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for residential electrical fluctuations only. It does not cover industrial high-voltage systems or DIY repair procedures. All repairs must be performed by a licensed electrician or utility worker.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and is not a substitute for professional electrical inspection. Electrical faults can cause fires or fatal shocks. If you are unsure of the safety of your system, turn off the main breaker and seek professional help.
