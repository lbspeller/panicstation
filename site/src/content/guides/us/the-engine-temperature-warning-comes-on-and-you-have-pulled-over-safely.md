# What to do if… the engine temperature warning comes on and you have pulled over safely (USA)

## Short answer
Shut off the engine immediately and wait at least 30 minutes before touching the hood or cooling system to avoid life-altering steam burns.

## Do not do these things
- Do not remove the radiator cap or coolant reservoir cap while the engine is hot, steaming, or hissing.
- Do not pour cold water directly over a hot engine block or into a hot radiator.
- Do not stand between your vehicle and oncoming traffic while waiting.
- Do not attempt to "limp" the car to a destination if the warning light remains red or the gauge stays in the high zone.
- Do not use ice or ointments if you are burned; use only cool running water.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the vehicle and passengers.** Turn on your hazard lights. Ensure all passengers exit through the non-traffic side doors and move well behind a guardrail or up an embankment.
2. **Turn off the engine but keep the ignition in the "on" or "accessory" position.** This allows electric cooling fans to continue running without the engine generating more heat.
3. **Turn the cabin heater and fan to the maximum settings.** This acts as a secondary radiator by drawing heat away from the engine and into the cabin (keep windows open for your comfort).
4. **Wait a minimum of 30 minutes before opening the hood.** Do not be misled by a dropping dashboard gauge; the metal components and pressurized fluids stay at scalding temperatures much longer than the sensor implies.
5. **Inspect the coolant reservoir level without opening it.** Look at the translucent side of the plastic expansion tank to see if the fluid is between the MIN and MAX lines.
   - If the reservoir is empty or you see a large puddle under the car, do not attempt to drive.
6. **Treat any steam burns immediately if they occur.** Run the affected skin under cool (not ice-cold) water for a full 20 minutes.
   - Call **911** if the burn is larger than the palm of your hand or involves the face or joints.
7. **Contact roadside assistance or a towing service.** Even if the car restarts and the light goes out, an overheating event often indicates a mechanical failure (such as a stuck thermostat or failing water pump) that will recur within minutes of driving.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the exact mechanical cause of the overheating right now.
- You do not need to decide which repair shop to use until the vehicle is safely off the roadway.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of the tow while you are in a high-traffic or high-heat environment.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of urgency when you see steam or warning lights, but the most important safety step is the one you have already taken: stopping the car. Most engine damage occurs in the seconds *before* you stop; once the engine is off, the primary risk is personal injury from steam, which you can avoid by simply waiting.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate roadside stabilization and harm prevention for an overheating vehicle. It does not provide mechanical repair instructions or long-term maintenance advice.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only and does not replace professional mechanical or medical advice. If you are stopped in a dangerous location or see active flames (fire), evacuate the vehicle immediately and call 911. Vehicle cooling systems are under high pressure; improper handling can result in permanent injury or death.
