# What to do if… you suspect someone is repeatedly approaching your family at a park or playground (USA)

## Short answer
Move your family toward a busier, visible, or staffed place and leave the area if you can. If you feel in immediate danger, the person is blocking you, threatening you, or trying to separate a child from you, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay to be polite if the interaction feels wrong.
- Do not let children wander while you assess what is happening.
- Do not confront the person alone, argue, or try to physically block them.
- Do not share identifying details such as children’s names, school, where you live, or your usual routine.
- Do not post your location in real time.
- Do not follow the person, chase them, or try to identify them yourself.
- Do not wait until you can prove intent before moving away or asking for help.

## What to do now
1. **Create space immediately.** Bring children close, collect only what you need, and move toward a busier or more visible area such as an entrance, a staffed building, a ranger station, a concession area, a library, a store, or a group of other families. If you can leave the park safely, leave.
2. **Keep children close and accounted for.** Hold hands, carry a small child if needed, or use a calm direct instruction such as: “Stand right next to me now.” Keep yourself between the person and your children while you move.
3. **Use one clear boundary if the person keeps approaching.** Say, in a firm voice: “Stop approaching us. Do not speak to my children.” Do not debate, explain, or stay to see whether they agree.
4. **Ask nearby people for immediate support.** Say to another adult, staff member, ranger, security guard, or parent: “I do not feel safe. Can you stay with us while we leave or while I call for help?” Ask staff or security to walk with you to a safer place if available.
5. **Call 911 when the situation needs urgent help.** Call 911 if you feel in immediate danger, the person is escalating, blocking your path, making threats, trying to lure or separate a child, following you as you leave, or you believe a crime is happening. Be ready to give your location, what is happening, and a description of the person.
6. **Use a non-emergency route only once you are safe.** If you are away from the person but the behavior was repeated and concerning, contact your local police non-emergency number or local law enforcement contact route to ask how to report it. If you are in a National Park Service site and it is not an emergency, you can also submit a tip to the National Park Service.
7. **Record details without slowing your exit.** As soon as you safely can, write down the time, exact location, what happened, what was said, a physical description, direction of travel, and any vehicle description or plate you clearly saw. If filming or taking a photo would delay leaving, distract you from your children, or escalate the situation, skip it and write the details afterward.
8. **If you cannot safely leave, go more public.** Move toward people, lights, staff, or an open business rather than a secluded path, restroom area, or empty parking area. Keep your children beside you and stay with others until help arrives or you can leave safely.
9. **After you are away, make a short incident log.** Write what happened, where and when it happened, who saw it, what boundary you gave, and what the person did next. If there is a pattern, this can help local law enforcement understand the repeated conduct.

## What can wait
- Figuring out whether the behavior legally counts as stalking, harassment, or another offense.
- Trying to identify the person yourself.
- Posting about it online or warning others publicly.
- Deciding whether any legal options are needed later.
- Explaining the whole situation to everyone right now.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to prove the person’s intent before protecting your family. Repeated unwanted approaches, especially around children, are enough reason to leave, ask for help, and report what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes and hours: create distance, keep children close, involve witnesses or staff, and contact emergency or local help when needed. Later decisions may need support from local law enforcement, victim services, park staff, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, law-enforcement, security, or other professional advice. If you believe you or your children are in immediate danger, call 911.
