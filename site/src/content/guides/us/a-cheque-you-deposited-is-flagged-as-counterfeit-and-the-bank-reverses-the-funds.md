# What to do if… a cheque you deposited is flagged as counterfeit and the bank reverses the funds (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank promptly using a trusted route and ask about the reversal, your current balance, any restrictions, and any fees. Do not send money to the person who gave you the check or to anyone they name.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money, gift cards, cryptocurrency, cash, or a refund because someone tells you to use the check funds.
- Do not assume the check was valid because money appeared in your account or was available to withdraw.
- Do not use a phone number or link from a message about the check to contact your bank.
- Do not share your online banking password, one-time code, or remote access to your device.
- Do not close the account before asking your bank how the reversal and any pending payments will be handled.
- Do not take out a rushed loan or send money from another account because someone is pressuring you.

## What to do now
1. **Contact your bank through a trusted route.**  
   Use the number on your bank card, the bank’s trusted app, or its official website. Ask why the deposit was reversed, what your current balance is, whether the account has restrictions, and whether any fees have been charged.

2. **Ask for records you can keep.**  
   Ask for written confirmation of the reversal, the reason given, the deposit details, and any check image or notice the bank can provide. Save any case number.

3. **Review payments due soon.**  
   Check for scheduled payments and recent debits that may be affected by the reversal. Ask the bank what options are available to reduce avoidable returned payments or fees and whether any fees can be reviewed.

4. **Pause any exchange connected with the check.**  
   Do not provide goods, services, or a refund based on the reversed deposit. If you recently shipped something, contact the carrier and ask whether a hold, redirect, or stop is still available.

5. **Keep the check and related messages.**  
   Save the original check, envelope, emails, texts, screenshots, payment instructions, usernames, phone numbers, dates, and amounts. Do not continue arguing with the sender.

6. **Use the reporting routes that fit what happened.**  
   Report the scam to the FTC through ReportFraud.ftc.gov. If the check or related material came through the mail, use the U.S. Postal Inspection Service reporting page. If the scam happened online, you can also file an IC3 report.

7. **Take identity-protection steps if you shared personal information.**  
   If you shared your Social Security number or other sensitive identity details, review the steps at IdentityTheft.gov. If you are concerned that someone may try to open credit in your name, review the free credit-freeze instructions and contact each of the three major credit reporting agencies.

8. **Document an unresolved bank issue.**  
   Keep a simple record of the dates, names, case numbers, notices, and answers you receive from the bank. If the issue remains unresolved after you contact the bank, you can consider submitting a complaint to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau.

## What can wait
- Replying to or arguing with the person who gave you the check.
- Deciding whether to take legal action.
- Rebuilding your entire banking setup today.
- Making major borrowing or credit decisions before you understand the balance, restrictions, and pending payments.
- Completing every report at once. Contact the bank first, then use the reporting routes that fit what happened.

## Important reassurance
Fake checks can look convincing, and money may appear available before a bank identifies the problem. A reversal is a reason to slow down, stop further payments, and confirm the account position with your bank.

## Scope note
These are immediate first steps only. Later decisions about fees, account restrictions, identity recovery, shipment recovery, or legal options may need help from the bank or an appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Bank processes and the details of each situation can vary. Ask your bank what applies to your account before making irreversible decisions.
