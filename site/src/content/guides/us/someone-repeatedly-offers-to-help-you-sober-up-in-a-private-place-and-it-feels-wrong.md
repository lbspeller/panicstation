# What to do if… someone repeatedly offers to help you sober up in a private place and it feels wrong (USA)
## Short answer
Treat the feeling as enough reason to step away. Move to a staffed, visible area and do not go somewhere private with that person.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to a private room, car, alley, or quiet place with them.
- Avoid taking drinks, food, or other substances from them.
- Do not hand them your phone, keys, or bag.
- Do not feel you have to negotiate or explain. A simple “No” or “I am staying here” is enough.
- Do not leave alone with them, even if they say they are trying to help.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** Go toward a staffed, well-lit, busy area such as a bar counter, host stand, security desk, or hotel lobby. Put space between you and the person.
2. **Tell staff or security clearly.** Say: “I do not feel safe with that person. Please help me stay away from them.” Ask to remain in a staffed, visible area while support is arranged.
3. **Contact someone you trust.** Call or text: “I feel unsafe. I am at [place or address]. Please stay on the phone with me and help me get home.” Share your location if that feels safe.
4. **Use a safer exit.** Ask staff, security, or a trusted person to help you leave. Do not leave alone with the person who offered to help you sober up.
5. **Call 911 for immediate danger or emergency symptoms.** Ask staff to call if that is easier. If you have trouble breathing, feel drunk after little or no alcohol, become suddenly dizzy, vomit, feel confused, or have memory loss, get urgent medical help and tell clinicians you are concerned you may have been drugged or assaulted.
6. **Get specialist support without deciding what to call the situation.** RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline is available 24/7. Call 800-656-HOPE (4673), text HOPE to 64673, or use RAINN’s online chat.
7. If you think you may want medical or forensic options later, try not to shower or change clothes before getting advice, only if that feels safe and manageable.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide what their intentions were.
- You do not need to decide whether the situation was serious enough to seek support.
- You do not need to confront them, collect proof, or post publicly.
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report. Calling 911 for immediate help is about getting help now.
- You do not need a perfect explanation. “I do not feel safe” is enough.

## Important reassurance
Feeling uneasy is enough reason to leave. Freezing, going quiet, trying to please someone, or second-guessing yourself can happen when you feel unsafe. None of that makes this your fault.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you reach a safer pause and get support. Later decisions about medical follow-up, reporting, or other options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or seriously unwell, call 911 or seek urgent medical care.
