# What to do if… you learn your wages or bank account may be subject to garnishment (USA)

## Short answer
Confirm whether the notice is a legitimate court or government order, identify the deadline for a response (often 10–30 days), and file a claim of exemption or request a hearing to protect income needed for basic living expenses.

## Do not do these things
- Avoid paying private companies that call or text claiming they can stop a garnishment immediately without providing a verifiable court case number.
- Avoid ignoring a **Notice of Garnishment** or **Levy**; legal deadlines for challenging these orders are often very short.
- Avoid withdrawing all funds to hide them from a levy, as this rarely prevents a legal freeze and may complicate your standing in court.
- Avoid assuming your bank or employer can stop the process on their own; they are legally required to comply with valid orders until a court or agency tells them otherwise.
- Avoid signing any settlement agreements with debt collectors under immediate pressure until you have verified the debt and understood your exemptions.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the type of debt and the enforcing agency.**  
   **Check the paperwork to see if the creditor is a private entity (like a credit card company), the IRS, a state tax agency, or a child support enforcement office.** Private creditors must usually have a court judgment first, but government agencies can often garnish wages or levy accounts without a separate court order.

2. **Verify the order directly with the court or agency.**  
   **Call the clerk of the court listed on the notice or the government agency using a phone number from an official .gov website.** Confirm that a case exists, verify the judgment or order date, and ask for the specific forms needed to claim an exemption or request a hearing.

3. **Locate the specific "Claim of Exemption" or "Request for Hearing" form.**  
   **Most jurisdictions provide a specific form to list income that cannot be taken, such as Social Security, SSI, Veterans benefits, or a portion of wages needed for survival.** Note the filing deadline, which is often calculated from the date the notice was mailed or served.

4. **Calculate your protected wage threshold.**  
   **Under the federal Consumer Credit Protection Act (CCPA), the maximum weekly garnishment for most consumer debts is 25% of disposable earnings OR the amount by which weekly earnings exceed $217.50 (30 times the federal minimum wage), whichever is less.** State laws may provide even lower limits; use the more protective rule.

5. **Contact your bank's "Legal Orders" department if funds are frozen.**  
   **Ask if they have applied the "2-month Lookback Rule" (31 CFR Part 212) to protect federal benefits.** Banks must automatically protect up to two months' worth of directly deposited federal benefits—including Social Security, SSI, VA benefits, and Federal Railroad Retirement—from being frozen by judgment creditors.

6. **Notify your employer's payroll department to get details on the withholding.**  
   **Ask for a copy of the order they received, the total amount to be withheld per pay period, and the date the withholding is scheduled to start.** Confirm that they are aware of federal protections against firing an employee for a single garnishment.

7. **Submit the exemption paperwork to the court and the creditor.**  
   **File the completed forms with the court clerk and serve copies to the creditor's attorney as instructed on the notice.** If basic needs like rent, utilities, or medicine are at risk, state this clearly in any "hardship" section of the forms.

8. **Prepare for a hearing if one is scheduled.**  
   **Gather recent pay stubs, bank statements showing exempt deposits, and a basic list of monthly essential expenses (rent, food, utilities).** You do not need to prove the debt is invalid at this hearing; the focus is usually on whether the law allows the creditor to take the specific funds they are targeting.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to file for bankruptcy is a significant decision that does not need to be made in the first 24 hours.
- Negotiating a long-term payment plan with the creditor can wait until you have secured your immediate access to essential funds through exemptions.
- Updating your long-term budget or debt management strategy can wait until the current freeze or withholding is stabilized.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a garnishment notice is a high-stress event, but it is a procedural legal step that includes built-in protections. Filing the correct paperwork promptly often results in a reduction or total stop of the garnishment if the funds are legally exempt or required for your basic survival.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and immediate procedural responses for wage or bank garnishment in the USA. It does not cover long-term debt negotiation, bankruptcy filing procedures, or specific state-by-state legal defenses.

## Important note
Garnishment laws vary significantly by state and by the type of debt (e.g., student loans vs. taxes vs. consumer debt). Federal protections for "one debt" do not prevent an employer from discharging an employee if there are multiple separate garnishment orders. Contact a local Legal Aid office or a court self-help center for jurisdiction-specific forms and advice.
