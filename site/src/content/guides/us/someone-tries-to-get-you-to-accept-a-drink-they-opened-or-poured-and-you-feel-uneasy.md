# What to do if… someone tries to get you to accept a drink they opened or poured and you feel uneasy (USA)

## Short answer
Do not drink the beverage. Create physical distance from the person immediately, move toward staff or a crowd, and arrange a safe exit that you control entirely.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t drink any of it to be polite, avoid "making a scene," or de-escalate the person.
- Don’t leave the drink unattended if you plan to keep it for evidence later; otherwise, leave it behind.
- Don’t let the person walk you to your car, wait for your ride with you, or "take you home to sleep it off."
- Don’t wait for "proof" or certain symptoms before you decide to move to a safer area.
- Don’t argue or negotiate; you do not owe anyone an explanation for not drinking.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the interaction immediately by setting the drink down and walking away.** You can say "No thanks" or simply turn toward a different area of the room. **Do not carry the drink with you.**
2. **Move to a high-visibility area near staff, security, or a group of people.** Locate the bar, a host stand, or a security guard and position yourself where you are clearly visible to others.
3. **Inform a staff member or security officer that you feel unsafe and need assistance.** **Ask them to watch the person who offered the drink while you arrange to leave.**
   - Request that they walk you to your vehicle or wait with you at the entrance for your rideshare.
4. **Arrange your own transportation through a service or person you trust.** **Order a rideshare or taxi using your own phone, or call a friend to meet you specifically at the door.** Ensure you stay in a staff-monitored area until the vehicle is at the curb.
5. **Monitor yourself for sudden or unusual physical symptoms.** **If you feel extremely dizzy, confused, nauseated, or more intoxicated than your intake justifies, tell a staff member: "I feel unwell and I think I need medical help."**
6. **Call 911 if you lose coordination, feel like you might faint, or believe you are in immediate danger.** **Request an ambulance specifically if you are having trouble staying awake or breathing.**
7. **Contact a specialized support service for confidential guidance if you are unsure of your next steps.** **You can call the National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN) at 800-656-HOPE to speak with a trained staff member who can explain your options for medical care or reporting.**
8. **Seek medical attention at an emergency department if you believe you have ingested a substance or had non-consensual contact.** **Tell the triage nurse: "I am concerned I was drugged," and ask if a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) or a medical forensic exam is available.** You can receive medical care and evidence collection without being required to file a police report immediately.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you want to file a formal police report.
- You do not need to determine exactly what substance (if any) was in the drink.
- You do not need to explain your departure to the person or the rest of your social group.
- You do not need to "finish the night" or stay until the event ends.

## Important reassurance
Trusting your intuition is a proactive safety measure. If a situation feels "off," your body is signaling a need for distance, and responding to that signal is the correct priority. Social pressure and the "freeze" response are common in these moments; moving toward staff or friends can help break that cycle.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate safety and harm reduction. It does not cover long-term legal strategies or comprehensive medical recovery plans.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not a substitute for professional medical or legal advice. If you are in immediate danger or experiencing a medical emergency, call 911. Support and medical care are available regardless of whether you are certain of what happened.
