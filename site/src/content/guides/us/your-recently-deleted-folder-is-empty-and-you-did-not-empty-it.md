# What to do if… your recently deleted folder is empty and you did not empty it (USA)

## Short answer
Pause syncing if you can do so without locking yourself out. Then check official recovery routes for the service, and secure the account after you have captured what you can see.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using the affected device heavily for new photos, downloads, installs, or large file changes while you are trying to recover data.
- Don’t factory reset, wipe, or reinstall the device as your first move.
- Don’t pay anyone who contacts you first claiming they can urgently recover your files.
- Don’t install remote-control software for a stranger or unofficial recovery service.
- Don’t remove sessions, revoke access, or sign out devices before taking quick screenshots if you still have safe access.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the sync spiral if it is safe to do that.**  
   If you have another trusted device or can still access the provider’s website, turn on Airplane Mode or temporarily disable Wi-Fi and cellular data on the affected device. Do not disconnect the only device that can receive your sign-in codes unless you have another way back into the account.

2. **Capture what you can see.**  
   Take screenshots of the empty “Recently Deleted,” Trash, or Recycle Bin area; recent account activity; security alerts; shared folders or shared albums; and the list of signed-in devices. Write down the date, time, service, device, and what you first noticed.

3. **Check the official recovery layers for that service.**  
   Look for the provider’s Trash, Recycle Bin, Recently Deleted area, file version history, photo archive, shared folder activity, and any account or storage restore feature the provider offers. Also check local backups such as an external drive, Time Machine, Windows File History, or another backup app you already use.

4. **Check whether the files are somewhere else before assuming they are gone.**  
   Search the provider’s website, not only the app. Check archive folders, shared folders, other signed-in accounts, local device storage, and any device that may not have synced yet.

5. **Review account access.**  
   In the account security page, check recent sign-ins, security events, recovery email and phone, trusted devices, connected apps, and shared access. If anything is unfamiliar, treat the account as possibly compromised.

6. **Secure the account in an order that reduces lockout risk.**  
   Confirm that the recovery email and phone are still yours. Change the password to a new, unique one. Turn on two-factor authentication or two-step verification. Then sign out unknown devices and remove suspicious third-party app access.

7. **Ask official support about any remaining recovery option.**  
   Contact the provider through its official help page or app. Say: “My Recently Deleted, Trash, or Recycle Bin was emptied without my action. Are any file, account, backup, version history, or administrative recovery options available?” Give the timestamps and screenshots you captured.

8. **Use official reporting routes if this involved fraud, extortion, or identity misuse.**  
   If someone is demanding money, using the account for scams, or misusing personal information, use official U.S. reporting and recovery resources such as the FTC, IdentityTheft.gov, or the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now that recovery is impossible.
- You do not need to migrate to a new service today.
- You do not need to message everyone you know unless there is clear evidence your account is sending spam, scams, or harmful messages.
- You do not need to rebuild your whole backup system before checking official recovery options and securing the account.

## Important reassurance
An emptied “recently deleted” folder can happen through syncing, shared access, automatic cleanup, account compromise, or a mistaken action on another device. Taking calm steps in order gives you the best chance to limit further loss and find any remaining recovery path.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, such as deeper malware checks, device rebuilding, formal reports, or long-term backup changes, may need specialist help after the account is secure and recovery options have been checked.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Recovery options depend on the provider, account settings, device state, backups, and whether items were permanently removed.
