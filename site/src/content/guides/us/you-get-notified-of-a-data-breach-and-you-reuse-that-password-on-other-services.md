# What to do if… you get notified of a data breach and you reuse that password on other services (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the password as compromised across all services: change it immediately starting with your email account, then enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on all high-priority accounts.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, scan QR codes, or call phone numbers provided in the breach notification until you have verified the notice through a separate, trusted channel.
- Don’t use the "forgot password" link within the same email or text message that notified you of the breach.
- Don’t share one-time passcodes (OTP), 2FA codes, or Apple/Google/Microsoft verification codes with anyone, even if they claim to be from the breached company’s security team.
- Don’t update the password to a similar version (e.g., changing "Spring2025" to "Spring2026"); hackers use automated tools to guess these patterns.
- Don’t delay securing your email account while focusing on less sensitive services like social media or shopping apps.
- Don’t store your new passwords in unencrypted formats such as plain text files, phone notes, or screenshots.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the breach notice using an independent source.**  
   **Go directly to the service's official website by typing the URL into your browser or using their official mobile app.** 
   - Check the internal "messages" or "security" section of your account for a matching alert. 
   - If the notice was a physical letter, verify the contact details against the company’s official "Contact Us" page before calling.

2. **Secure your primary email account immediately.**  
   **Because your email allows for password resets on other sites, it is the most critical point of failure.** 
   - Change your email password to a unique passphrase (a string of four or more random words).
   - Enable the strongest available multi-factor authentication (MFA), such as an authenticator app, a physical security key, or a passkey.
   - Review "Forwarding" and "Filters" in your email settings to ensure an attacker hasn't set up rules to divert your mail.

3. **Change the password on the breached account and terminate all active sessions.**  
   **Create a new, unique password for the specific account mentioned in the notice.** 
   - Look for a "Sign out of all devices" or "Manage sessions" option in the security settings and activate it.
   - This ensures that if an attacker is currently logged in using a stolen session token, they are forced out.

4. **Identify and update every other account where you reused that same password.**  
   **Prioritize your accounts in the following order to prevent a "domino effect" takeover:** 
   - Financial institutions (Banks, Credit Unions, PayPal, Investment accounts).
   - Major identity providers (Apple ID, Google Account, Microsoft/Outlook).
   - Mobile carrier accounts (to prevent "SIM swapping" attacks).
   - Work or employer-provided accounts.
   - Any service holding saved payment methods or sensitive personal data.

5. **Enable MFA/2FA on every account that supports it.**  
   **Move beyond just a password by requiring a second form of verification.** 
   - Use "phishing-resistant" methods like passkeys or FIDO2 hardware keys where possible.
   - For other accounts, use an authenticator app rather than SMS (text) codes to avoid interception.
   - Save your "backup codes" or "recovery keys" in a secure, physical location.

6. **Place a credit freeze or fraud alert if personal data was included.**  
   **If the breach notice mentions Social Security numbers or financial details, protect your credit file.** 
   - **Credit Freeze:** Contact all three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion) separately to lock your credit. This is the strongest protection.
   - **Fraud Alert:** Contact any one of the three bureaus; they are legally required to notify the other two.

7. **Monitor for signs of secondary phishing or "follow-up" scams.**  
   **Attackers often send fake "help" or "account recovery" messages shortly after a real breach is announced.** 
   - Be skeptical of any urgent request for your info, even if the sender seems to know about the breach.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to delete the breached account immediately unless you no longer use the service.
- You don’t need to replace your physical credit or debit cards today unless the breach notice specifically confirms your full card numbers were stolen.
- You don’t need to buy a paid "identity protection" subscription immediately; federal law provides free credit freezes and fraud alerts.

## Important reassurance
Experiencing a data breach notification is a routine occurrence in modern digital life and does not mean you have been personally targeted. Most account takeovers following a breach are performed by automated "credential stuffing" bots. By changing reused passwords and enabling MFA, you effectively break the automation and secure your identity against the majority of these threats.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step guidance only for stabilizing your digital security after a breach. If you find evidence that your identity has already been misused, such as unauthorized charges or new accounts in your name, you must follow the specific recovery steps at IdentityTheft.gov.

## Important note
This information is for general harm reduction and does not constitute legal or cybersecurity professional advice. Data breach laws vary by state, and specific technical requirements for your devices may differ. If you suspect significant financial loss or a complex identity theft incident, contact your financial institutions and local law enforcement immediately.
