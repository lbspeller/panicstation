# What to do if… your device suddenly asks for administrator permission for normal settings changes (USA)
## Short answer
Do not approve the request yet. Pause, note exactly what app or process is asking, and disconnect from the internet if the prompt is unexpected, repeated, appears during browsing, or involves an unknown app.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t type an administrator password into a prompt if you can’t clearly identify the app requesting it.
- Don’t click “Allow” or “Yes” if the prompt appeared after an unexpected file, link, download, pop-up, or browser warning.
- Don’t call numbers shown in pop-ups or install “support” software that a warning recommends.
- Don’t give remote access to anyone who contacted you because of the prompt.
- Don’t turn off security prompts, antivirus, firewall settings, or system protections just to make the prompt go away.
- Don’t use the device for banking, shopping, email, or password-manager logins while you still suspect compromise.

## What to do now
1. **Freeze the moment and record what you see.**  
   Take a screenshot or phone photo of the prompt, including the app name, publisher, file path, and any warning text. Write down what you were changing and what happened immediately before it, such as an install, update, email attachment, download, browser pop-up, or USB drive.

2. **If the prompt was unexpected, isolate the device.**  
   Turn off Wi-Fi and unplug Ethernet. If you cannot stop repeated prompts, browser lock screens, or remote-control activity any other way, shut the device down and get help before using it again.

3. **Check whether the request matches something you started.**
   - **Windows:** If the User Account Control prompt has “Show more details” or similar wording, open it and check the program name, verified publisher, and file location. Be cautious with unknown publishers, misspelled company names, temporary-folder locations, or apps you did not open.
   - **Mac:** Only enter an administrator name and password if you recognize the app and you intentionally started an action that usually needs admin rights, such as installing software or changing security, privacy, startup, or system settings.

4. **If it is a work or school device, stop and use official IT.**  
   Do not try to bypass the prompt or install a workaround. Contact your helpdesk through a known-good channel, such as the company portal, internal directory, device sticker, or a number you already had saved, and share the screenshot.

5. **If it is your personal device, close the suspicious source before approving anything.**
   - Close the browser tab, app, installer, file, or settings window that triggered the prompt.
   - If the prompt disappears and does not return, reopen only the setting or app you intentionally meant to use.
   - If the prompt comes back immediately or repeatedly, treat it as suspicious and do not approve it.
   - Remove recent apps, installers, browser extensions, “system cleaners,” or surprise “security scanners” you do not recognize.
   - Use only built-in update tools or official app stores to update your operating system, browser, and security software.
   - Run a full scan with security software you already trust, and follow its recommended actions.

6. **Pause sensitive logins on that device until it looks clean.**  
   Do not log into banking, shopping, email, work systems, or password managers on the affected device while you still suspect compromise. Use a separate trusted device if you need to secure accounts.

7. **If you already entered an administrator password or clicked Allow, contain it.**
   - Disconnect the affected device from the internet again.
   - If the password was also used for any online account, change those online account passwords from a separate trusted device.
   - Change the affected device’s local administrator password after you have stopped the suspicious activity or received trusted help.
   - Turn on multi-factor authentication for important accounts where available.
   - Run updates and a full security scan before using the device for sensitive activity.

8. **If you lost money, shared personal information, or allowed remote access, report and preserve evidence.**  
   Save screenshots, phone numbers, payment details, emails, download names, remote-access tool names, and any account alerts. If anyone is in immediate danger, call 911. For cyber-enabled fraud or scams, file a report with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide right now exactly how it happened.
- You do not have to wipe or reinstall the device immediately unless scans, official IT, or trusted support indicate that is needed.
- You do not have to decide every reporting or repair step in the moment; first preserve evidence, stop new access, and protect important accounts.
- You do not have to approve the prompt just because you were trying to make a normal settings change.

## Important reassurance
Administrator prompts can be normal during legitimate installs and system settings changes. They are also a point where scams and malware try to get deeper access. Pausing before you approve is a reasonable safety step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce harm and buy time. If prompts continue, scans find threats, money or data may be at risk, or you cannot confirm what is requesting access, later decisions may need official IT, trusted device support, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not cybersecurity, legal, financial, repair, or other professional advice. If you are unsure, stopping and getting trusted help is safer than approving a prompt that could allow hard-to-reverse device changes.
