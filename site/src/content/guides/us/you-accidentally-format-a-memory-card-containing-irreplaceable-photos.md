# What to do if… you accidentally format a memory card containing irreplaceable photos (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using the memory card immediately and remove it from the camera, phone, drone, or computer. The main risk now is writing new data over the photos you are trying to recover.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t take new photos or videos on that card.
- Don’t format it again, even if a device asks you to.
- Don’t click “repair,” “fix,” or “scan and fix” prompts before you have copied or recovered what you can.
- Don’t save recovered files back onto the same memory card.
- Don’t run lots of different tools in a panic.
- Don’t give remote access to your computer to someone who contacted you unexpectedly or is pressuring you.
- Don’t trust anyone who guarantees recovery before examining the card.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all writes to the card.**  
   Take the card out and put it somewhere separate. If it helps, put it in an envelope and write “DO NOT USE” on it.

2. **Use a different card for anything you still need to do today.**  
   Do not put the formatted card back into the camera, phone, drone, dashcam, or card reader for normal use.

3. **If the card has a lock switch, lock it before connecting it.**  
   A full-size SD card or SD adapter may have a LOCK slider. Sliding it to LOCK may help prevent changes, but do not rely on it as the only protection.

4. **Decide whether to try professional recovery first.**  
   If the photos are truly irreplaceable, or the card is disconnecting, not mounting, making errors, or getting unusually hot, consider stopping here and contacting a reputable data recovery service before trying DIY recovery.

5. **If you try DIY recovery, keep it read-only and save elsewhere.**  
   Use a card reader and reputable photo recovery software to scan the card. Save anything found to your computer’s internal drive or to a separate external drive, not to the memory card.

6. **Check for copies that may already exist.**  
   Look in your phone, tablet, computer photo app, camera import folders, external drives, cloud photo services, and trash or recently deleted areas. If you use iCloud Photos, check Recently Deleted on your device or on iCloud.

7. **Watch for recovery scams.**  
   Be cautious if someone asks for remote access, demands upfront payment under pressure, claims a guaranteed result, or contacts you unexpectedly. If you paid a scammer, contact your bank or card issuer and report it to the FTC.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide whether to reuse the card later.
- You don’t need to organize, rename, edit, or share recovered files right now.
- You don’t need to decide who is at fault right now.
- You don’t need to buy replacement gear until recovery attempts are finished or the card has been assessed.

## Important reassurance
Formatting a card feels awful, especially when the photos matter. It does not always mean the photos are gone forever; if the card has not been used since, recovery may still be possible.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce harm and preserve recovery chances. Later decisions, such as paid recovery, forensic handling, insurance, or dispute steps, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not professional, forensic, legal, financial, or technical advice. Recovery success depends on the card, the type of format, card condition, and any new data written afterward.
