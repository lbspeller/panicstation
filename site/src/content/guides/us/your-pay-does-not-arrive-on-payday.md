# What to do if… your pay does not arrive on payday (USA)

## Short answer
Secure physical or digital proof of all hours worked and commissions earned immediately, then initiate a formal paper trail through your state’s labor department or a federal wage claim before company systems and contacts disappear entirely.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a sudden closure exempts the employer from final pay laws; the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) still requires payment by the next regular payday at minimum.
- Do not sign any "release of claims," severance agreement, or document stating you have been "paid in full" until you have the actual funds in your possession.
- Do not return company property (laptops, keys, badges) without obtaining a dated, signed receipt or taking a photo of the return.
- Do not leave behind personal items or original records; take everything personal with you and keep copies of all pay-related documents.
- Do not wait for "the dust to settle" before acting; in a total shutdown, the first people to file claims are often the only ones who recover funds.
- Do not share your Social Security number or bank details with "new" or unverified HR consultants claiming to help with the transition.

## What to do now
1. **Document the specific "signs of closure" and your last hours worked immediately.**  
   Write down exactly what you observed (e.g., chains on the door, IT systems locked, a verbal announcement) and list your hours for the current pay period, including any unpaid overtime or commissions.

2. **Secure all available pay records before you lose digital access.**  
   Download or screenshot your most recent pay stubs, W-2s, timecards, and any written policies regarding PTO (Paid Time Off) or vacation payouts.
   - Save copies of your employment contract or offer letter.
   - Keep any emails or texts where managers promised specific pay dates.

3. **Send a formal, written demand for your final wages to the highest-level contact you have.**  
   State clearly that you are requesting your final paycheck, including all earned wages and accrued benefits, and provide a reliable mailing address for the check. Use a method that provides proof of delivery, such as "Certified Mail, Return Receipt Requested" or a tracked email.

4. **Verify if the company has filed for bankruptcy and locate the case number.**  
   Check the U.S. Bankruptcy Court "PACER" system or watch for a "Notice of Commencement of Case." If a case exists, your wage claim becomes a "priority unsecured claim" (up to $15,150 for 2026 cases) which gives you a higher standing than many other creditors.

5. **File a Proof of Claim (Official Form 410) if a bankruptcy is active.**  
   **Complete the form precisely and attach your pay stubs or time records as evidence.** Ensure you file before the "Bar Date" (the court-ordered deadline), as missing this date usually forfeits your right to payment from the bankruptcy estate.

6. **Submit a wage claim with your State Department of Labor or Labor Commissioner.**  
   Most states provide an administrative process to recover unpaid wages, which is often faster and cheaper than hiring a private attorney. Note that if the employer is in bankruptcy, an "automatic stay" might pause state-level proceedings, but filing the state claim still creates an official government record of the debt.

7. **Contact the U.S. Department of Labor (DOL) Wage and Hour Division.**  
   Call 1-866-4US-WAGE (1-866-487-9243) if you suspect the closure involves a violation of federal minimum wage or overtime laws. They can investigate whether the employer failed to pay for all "hours worked" leading up to the shutdown.

8. **Apply for unemployment benefits immediately through your state agency.**  
   Do not wait for a final paycheck or a formal "separation notice" to apply. State clearly that the business closed suddenly; this is a qualifying event for benefits in all U.S. jurisdictions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to join a class-action lawsuit in the first 48 hours.
- You do not need to have a perfect, down-to-the-penny calculation of your claim before filing your initial state or federal complaint.
- You do not need to resolve health insurance or 401(k) rollovers today; focus on the immediate cash-flow issue of your final wages first.
- You do not need to respond to unofficial "venting" groups on social media that may contain inaccurate legal rumors.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of betrayal and high anxiety when a workplace shuts down without warning. Employers often go silent not out of malice, but because they are legally restricted or physically unable to manage the collapse. Using established government and court channels is the most effective way to protect your rights while the business is being liquidated or reorganized.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and evidence preservation for unpaid wages following a sudden US business closure only. It does not cover long-term litigation, individual tax advice, or the recovery of specialized executive bonuses.

## Important note
Wage and hour laws vary significantly by state, especially regarding the payout of unused vacation or PTO. If the employer has entered bankruptcy, federal bankruptcy law generally takes precedence over state labor laws regarding the timing and priority of payments. Consult official state labor department websites or the U.S. Department of Labor for 2026-specific thresholds and filing procedures.
