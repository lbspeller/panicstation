# What to do if… you notice signs your phone camera or mic may have been accessed and you fear intimate content was captured (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the compromised device immediately, switch to a known-safe device for sensitive communications, and contact a confidential specialist helpline (800-656-HOPE or 844-878-CCRI) for safety planning and removal assistance.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the suspected person or "test" the device by taking more photos or talking about the situation near the phone.
- Do not pay any money, provide more images, or respond to threats from someone claiming to have captured content.
- Do not factory reset the phone yet if you intend to report the crime later, as this may destroy forensic evidence.
- Do not use the compromised device to change passwords or access bank accounts.
- Do not broadcast the situation on social media or send mass messages from the affected device.

## What to do now
1. **Move the phone to a different room or cover the lenses and microphone.** Place the device in a drawer or cover cameras with opaque tape to prevent further visual or audio capture while you plan your next steps.
2. **Switch to a trusted device for all sensitive actions.** Use a different phone or a secure computer that you are certain has not been accessed by others to make calls, change passwords, and seek help.
3. **Contact confidential specialist support for safety and image removal.** 
   - Call the **National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN)** at **800-656-HOPE (4673)** for 24/7 confidential crisis support and safety planning.
   - Call the **Cyber Civil Rights Initiative (CCRI) Image Abuse Helpline** at **844-878-CCRI (2274)** for technical advice on image takedowns and legal referrals.
4. **Use proactive tools to prevent the spread of content.**
   - For adults: Use **StopNCII.org** to generate "digital fingerprints" of your images/videos; this allows participating platforms to detect and block them from being uploaded.
   - For content involving anyone under 18: Use the **NCMEC Take It Down** service (takeitdown.ncmec.org).
5. **Exercise your rights under the Take It Down Act (2025).** Under federal law, major online platforms are required to remove nonconsensual intimate images within 48 hours of a valid report; use the platform's official reporting tools to trigger this legal requirement.
6. **Secure your primary accounts from your safe device.** 
   - Change your **email password** first, then enable **multi-factor authentication (MFA)** using an authenticator app rather than SMS if possible.
   - Change passwords for cloud storage (iCloud, Google Photos), social media, and any messaging apps.
   - Select "Log out of all other sessions" in your account security settings.
7. **Report the incident through official channels (optional).** 
   - File a report with the **FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3)** at **www.ic3.gov**.
   - If you are in immediate physical danger, call **911**.
   - For non-emergency reporting of a past incident, call your local police department's non-emergency line or dial **311** if available in your city.
8. **Preserve evidence safely.** If you have received threats or messages, take screenshots from a safe device or use a digital camera to photograph the screen of the compromised phone. Save these to a secure, encrypted cloud folder or a physical USB drive kept in a safe place.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify exactly how the phone was accessed or find the specific "spyware" yourself.
- You do not need to decide right now whether to pursue a full criminal case or civil lawsuit.
- You do not need to explain the situation to friends, family, or employers until you have stabilized your digital security.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of violation, panic, or "digital claustrophobia" when you suspect your privacy has been breached. These are normal reactions to a high-stress situation. You are taking the necessary steps to regain control and protect yourself.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm reduction for suspected unauthorized device access and image abuse. Technical forensic analysis and long-term legal strategies require specialized professional assistance.

## Important note
This information is for guidance only and does not constitute legal advice. Federal and state laws regarding nonconsensual intimate imagery (NCII) are subject to change. If the content involves a minor, contact the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children (NCMEC) at 800-843-5678.
