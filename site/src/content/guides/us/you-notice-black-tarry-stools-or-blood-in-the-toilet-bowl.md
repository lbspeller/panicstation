# What to do if… you notice black, tarry stools or blood in the toilet bowl (USA)

## Short answer
Seek medical evaluation immediately; black, tarry stools or blood in the toilet can indicate gastrointestinal bleeding. If you feel faint, have severe pain, or see heavy bleeding, call **911** or go to an Emergency Room now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore black, sticky, or tarry stools even if you feel physically well.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you feel dizzy, lightheaded, short of breath, or confused.
- Do not take aspirin, ibuprofen, or naproxen (NSAIDs) for any associated pain, as these can worsen internal bleeding.
- Do not stop taking prescribed anticoagulants or antiplatelet medications without professional medical instruction.
- Do not use laxatives, enemas, or "colon cleanses" to clear the bowel.
- Do not attempt to self-diagnose based on diet or recent medication use alone.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate emergency triggers for a 911 call or ER visit.**
   - **Check for signs of shock or severe blood loss.**
   - **Call 911 immediately if you experience:**
     - Heavy or continuous rectal bleeding or large clots.
     - Vomiting blood or vomit that looks like dark coffee grounds.
     - Feeling faint, severely dizzy when standing, or losing consciousness.
     - Cold, clammy, or very pale skin.
     - Rapid heart rate or severe abdominal or chest pain.
2. **Seek same-day emergency evaluation for melena (black, tarry stools).**
   - **Go to an Emergency Room today if your stool is black, sticky, and has an unusually foul odor.**
   - **This appearance often signifies bleeding in the upper gastrointestinal tract (stomach or esophagus) and requires urgent clinical stabilization.**
3. **Contact a primary care clinician for minor bright red spotting if you are stable.**
   - **If you see small amounts of bright red blood only on the toilet paper or as a light streak on the stool, call your doctor for a same-day appointment.**
   - **Use an Urgent Care center if your primary doctor is unavailable and you remain otherwise asymptomatic.**
4. **Prioritize care if you take high-risk medications or have liver disease.**
   - **Notify medical staff immediately if you take blood thinners (anticoagulants/antiplatelets) such as warfarin, apixaban (Eliquis), or clopidogrel (Plavix).**
   - **Users of these medications, or those with known cirrhosis or liver disease, are at higher risk for rapid escalation and should go to the ER.**
5. **Note the specific timing and appearance of the symptoms for the triage nurse.**
   - **Prepare to describe:**
     - The color (bright red, maroon, or black/tarry).
     - The frequency of bowel movements since the bleeding started.
     - Any accompanying symptoms like nausea, weakness, or cramping.
     - Recent use of iron supplements, Pepto-Bismol (bismuth), or anti-inflammatory drugs.
6. **Move to a seated or lying position while waiting for transport or care.**
   - **Keep your activity to a minimum to prevent falls if you become lightheaded.**
   - **If your condition worsens—such as feeling more confused or weak—escalate to 911 immediately.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the underlying cause (such as ulcers, polyps, or hemorrhoids) right now.
- You do not need to decide on long-term dietary changes or "gut health" protocols today.
- You do not need to schedule a colonoscopy or endoscopy yourself; these will be arranged by the evaluating clinician.
- You can wait to discuss long-term prevention until you are medically stabilized.

## Important reassurance
Gastrointestinal bleeding is a common clinical emergency with established protocols for rapid assessment and stabilization. Seeking immediate help is a standard safety precaution and does not always indicate a chronic or life-threatening condition.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and triage only. Definitive diagnosis and treatment require a physical exam, lab work, and potentially specialist evaluation by a gastroenterologist.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and does not constitute a medical diagnosis. If you have heavy bleeding, feel faint, or have severe pain, call 911 or seek emergency care immediately.
