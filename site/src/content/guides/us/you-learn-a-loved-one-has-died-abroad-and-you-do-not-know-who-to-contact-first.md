# What to do if… you learn a loved one has died abroad and you do not know who to contact first (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the U.S. Department of State’s Bureau of Consular Affairs (Overseas Citizen Services) or the nearest U.S. embassy in the country of death to verify the report and establish an official line of communication.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wire money to any individual, hotel, or "agent" claiming they need immediate funds for body release or medical bills.
- Do not provide the deceased's Social Security number or financial passwords to anyone other than verified U.S. consular officials.
- Do not sign any contracts with foreign funeral homes or repatriation services until you have spoken with the U.S. embassy.
- Do not assume a social media post or private message is accurate; wait for official confirmation from local authorities or the State Department.
- Do not feel pressured to authorize immediate cremation or burial until you understand the local legal requirements and your options.

## What to do now
1. **Locate the specific U.S. embassy or consulate for the region where the death occurred.**  
   - Use the official usembassy.gov website to find the correct jurisdiction.
   - Look for the **American Citizen Services (ACS)** section or the **U.S. Citizens Services Navigator** tool on their homepage.

2. **Call the U.S. Department of State emergency line if you cannot reach the embassy directly.**  
   - **From the U.S. and Canada:** Call **1-888-407-4747**.
   - **From overseas:** Call **+1-202-501-4444**.
   - Tell the operator: "I am reporting the death of a U.S. citizen abroad and need to speak with Overseas Citizen Services."

3. **Gather the deceased’s full legal information for the official report.**  
   - Be ready to provide:
     - Full legal name and date of birth.
     - U.S. passport number (if known).
     - Last known location and the source of the death notification.
     - Your relationship to the deceased (Next of Kin).

4. **Request assistance with the "Consular Report of Death of a U.S. Citizen Abroad" (CRDA).**  
   - Ask the consular officer what local documents (such as a foreign death certificate) are required to generate the **CRDA (Form DS-2060)**.
   - Note that the CRDA is the primary document used in the U.S. for insurance, Social Security, and estate matters.

5. **Establish your legal standing as the "Next of Kin" (NOK).**  
   - Ask the officer if you need to execute an **Affidavit of Surviving Spouse or Next of Kin** to authorize the disposition of remains.
   - Clarify if there are "Privacy Act" restrictions that prevent the embassy from sharing details with anyone other than the legal NOK.

6. **Request the embassy’s list of local funeral homes and repatriation specialists.**  
   - Consular staff cannot pay for services, but they provide vetted lists of providers experienced in international transfers.
   - Ask: "What are the local timeframes for burial or cremation before authorities intervene?"

7. **Verify any third-party contact via a formal "Call-Back" protocol.**  
   - If a stranger calls you claiming to be a "consular agent" or "police officer," hang up.
   - Call the official embassy number back yourself to confirm the person’s identity and role.

## What can wait
- Notifying the Social Security Administration or the VA (the CRDA process often handles part of this notification).
- Canceling credit cards, utilities, or cell phone plans.
- Arranging a local U.S. memorial service or funeral.
- Deciding on the final distribution of personal effects or "estate" items.

## Important reassurance
Receiving news of a death abroad is a profound shock that causes significant cognitive fog. You do not have to navigate foreign legal systems alone; the U.S. Department of State is specifically tasked with acting as a bridge between you and foreign authorities. Taking ten minutes to breathe and verify the official channel will prevent significant financial and administrative errors later.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and notification. It does not provide legal advice regarding foreign probate, inheritance, or the investigation of criminal circumstances abroad.

## Important note
U.S. embassies and consulates do not have funds to pay for the return of remains or local burial. All costs associated with the death and repatriation are the responsibility of the family or the estate. Procedures and costs vary significantly by country.
