# What to do if… a freelance platform suspends your account while you have active work in progress (USA)

## Short answer
Mute the chat immediately to stop the escalation, preserve the message history as evidence, and report the situation to your employer’s designated HR or management channel.

## Do not do these things
- Do not engage or attempt to "win" the argument, as this often generates evidence that can be used against you.
- Do not delete or edit messages; company systems may retain original logs, and altering them can look like tampering.
- Do not screenshot the chat and send it to coworkers or post it on personal social media.
- Do not agree to private phone calls or meetings with the aggressors while you are in an escalated state.
- Do not make threats regarding legal action or employment status within the chat itself.
- Do not use the hostile chat to vent about company management or reveal confidential information.

## What to do now
1. **Initiate a safety pause by muting all notifications for the specific chat.** Step away from the device for several minutes to ensure you do not respond impulsively while the tone is spiraling.
2. **Capture and preserve evidence before messages can be deleted or scrolled away.** Screenshot the following items clearly:
   - The participant list showing who is in the group.
   - The notification showing when and by whom you were added.
   - Specific hostile messages with visible timestamps and sender names.
   - Any content involving slurs, sexual remarks, threats, or references to protected traits (race, sex, religion, disability, etc.).
3. **Send exactly one neutral exit message if you feel a response is required to explain your silence.** Do not engage with the content; use a single boundary statement:
   - "I am not comfortable with the tone of this conversation. I am stepping away from the chat and will address work-related matters through formal channels."
4. **Identify your internal reporting path according to company policy.** Check your employee handbook or intranet for the specific harassment reporting procedure, which usually involves:
   - A direct supervisor (if they are not part of the chat).
   - Human Resources or People Operations.
   - A Union Representative or Steward, if you are in a collective bargaining unit.
5. **Submit a formal, factual report of the incident to the chosen contact.** Keep the communication brief and focus on the behavior rather than your emotional interpretation:
   - State that you were added to a chat where the tone has become hostile and escalating.
   - Attach 2–3 of the most clear, objective examples of harassment.
   - State that you are requesting a prompt resolution to ensure a professional work environment.
6. **Request specific protective measures to resolve the immediate conflict.** You may ask for:
   - Removal from the group chat by an administrator.
   - A directive for the participants to cease using the chat for non-work purposes.
   - Clarification on the company's policy regarding electronic communications and harassment.
7. **Document any behavior involving protected characteristics for potential external reporting.** If the harassment is based on race, sex, age (40+), disability, or other federal/state protected traits, keep a separate personal log of the dates, times, and exact language used, as this is required for EEOC or state agency complaints.
8. **Prioritize physical safety if the chat contains credible threats of violence.** If you feel physically unsafe at your location or if messages suggest a physical confrontation:
   - Move to a secure or public area.
   - Contact building security or local law enforcement by calling **911**.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to file a formal charge with the EEOC or a state civil rights agency.
- Collecting and annotating every single message in the history; focus on the high-impact examples first.
- Responding to private "side-bar" messages from other participants.
- Deciding on long-term employment changes like quitting or transferring.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel overwhelmed when workplace hostility becomes public in a group setting. Taking a pause and refusing to engage is a standard professional safety tactic. Stepping back allows the record of the other participants' behavior to stand on its own without your involvement.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only to prevent escalation and preserve your rights. Ongoing issues may require legal advice, union representation, or formal grievance procedures.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and is not legal advice. Workplace protections vary significantly based on your specific state, whether you are a public or private employee, and whether you are classified as an independent contractor. If you believe your physical safety is at risk, contact emergency services immediately.
