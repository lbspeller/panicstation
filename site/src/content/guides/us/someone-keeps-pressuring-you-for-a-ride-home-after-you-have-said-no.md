# What to do if… someone keeps pressuring you for a ride home after you have said no (USA)

## Short answer
Do not accept the ride. Treat the repeated pressure as enough reason to use a safer, more public way home today and involve another person now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t get into their car to avoid awkwardness or because you feel pressured.
- Don’t share your address, where you park, your route, or your usual schedule.
- Don’t let them move you into a quiet place to “talk,” such as a parking lot corner, stairwell, empty hallway, or isolated room.
- Don’t handle it alone if you feel uneasy; involve someone safe early.
- Don’t post your real-time location or commute routine online.
- Don’t wait for proof before asking for help getting home safely.

## What to do now
1. **Move toward people and a staffed place.**  
   Stay inside, near a front desk, security desk, reception area, busy shop, transit staff, or a group of people. Avoid walking to a car, stop, station, or parking area alone if you feel unsafe.

2. **Use a short boundary once, then end the interaction.**  
   Say: “No. Do not ask me again.” Do not explain, negotiate, or give details about your plans. Move toward other people after saying it.

3. **Get home using a safer option you control.**  
   Leave with a trusted person, ask security or front desk staff for an escort, ask someone you trust to meet you, use public transit from a busy stop, or use a taxi or ride app you booked yourself.

4. **Set a check-in for this trip home.**  
   Text or call one trusted person before you leave. Tell them where you are leaving from, when you expect to arrive, and that you will message when you are inside.

5. **If you think they may follow you, go toward help rather than trying to lose them alone.**  
   Go to a staffed, public place or a known safe location. If you are in immediate danger or they are threatening you, blocking you, chasing you, or trying to force you into a vehicle, call **911**.

6. **If this is connected to work, school, campus, housing, or an event, ask for practical safety help.**  
   Tell a supervisor, HR, building security, campus safety, residence staff, event staff, or your school’s Title IX office: “Someone is repeatedly pressuring me for rides after I said no. I need help leaving safely.” Ask what safety options are available, such as an escort, a buddy system, a changed exit point, or help communicating that the contact must stop.

7. **Keep one simple private note if you can do it safely.**  
   Write down the date, time, place, what happened, and any witnesses. Save messages without replying if you do not want to. Do not put yourself at risk to collect proof.

8. **Contact confidential support if you want help thinking it through.**  
   RAINN offers confidential support through the National Sexual Assault Hotline. You can call **800-656-HOPE (4673)**, use online chat, or text **HOPE** to **64673**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to file a police report, school complaint, HR complaint, or formal report.
- You do not need to confront them beyond a clear “no” and ending the interaction.
- You do not need to prove their intent before taking safety steps.
- You do not need to explain your plans, your route, or why you do not want the ride.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to take repeated pressure seriously after you have already said no. You are allowed to protect your route home, involve other people, and ask for help without apologising or proving that something worse has happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on getting through the immediate situation safely. Later decisions about reporting, workplace or school processes, housing, transport, or legal options may need help from a local advocate, trusted professional, or specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, safety-planning, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call **911**.
