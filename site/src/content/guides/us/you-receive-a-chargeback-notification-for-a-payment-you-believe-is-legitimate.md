# What to do if… you receive a chargeback notification for a payment you believe is legitimate (USA)

## Short answer
Open your processor, platform, or acquirer dashboard through your normal login route, confirm the notice is real, and note the dispute reason, stage, and deadline. Then either accept it or submit a focused evidence response through the official dispute workflow.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t miss the response deadline; you may lose the chance to contest the dispute.
- Don’t click login links from an unexpected email; use your usual bookmark, app, or typed web address.
- Don’t handle a formal chargeback only by arguing with the customer; it usually has to be handled through the processor, platform, or acquirer workflow shown in the notice.
- Don’t upload a large document dump; irrelevant, duplicated, or unreadable evidence can make the response harder to review.
- Don’t threaten, harass, or publicly accuse the customer.
- Don’t refund and also contest unless your processor says that is appropriate for the exact stage you are in.
- Don’t submit before checking that the response is final; some platforms allow only one evidence submission.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the notice and capture the key details.**
   - Log in through your normal route and open the dispute in the dashboard.
   - Write down the reason code or category, deadline, amount, fees, transaction ID, and current stage.
   - Save a copy of the notice and any dashboard page showing the deadline.

2. **Decide whether this is worth contesting before collecting everything.**
   - Consider accepting if the customer’s claim appears correct, the order was not fulfilled, or you do not have evidence that directly answers the stated reason.
   - Consider contesting if you can answer the specific reason with clear order, delivery, access, refund, or communication records.
   - If the case is only an inquiry or early warning, follow the platform’s instructions for that stage instead of treating it as a finished chargeback.

3. **Build a short evidence packet that matches the dispute reason.**
   - For an unauthorized or fraud claim: include account history, order confirmation, prior successful transactions, digital access or usage records, shipping address consistency, and device or IP information already available in your order or fraud tools.
   - For an item not received claim: include carrier tracking, delivery confirmation, shipment date, delivery address, and delivery-related customer messages.
   - For a not as described claim: include the listing, description, photos, service terms, proof of what was delivered, and customer messages about the issue.
   - For a refund expected claim: include the refund policy shown at checkout, cancellation terms, customer acceptance of those terms, any refund record, and a timeline of the customer’s request.

4. **Write a brief rebuttal summary.**
   - State that you are contesting the dispute.
   - Use 3–6 bullets that connect each attachment to what it proves.
   - Keep the timeline simple: purchase, fulfillment or access, customer contact, and dispute notice.

5. **Submit through the official portal before the deadline.**
   - Check file limits, page limits, and evidence categories in your processor’s form.
   - Do not rely on links inside the evidence packet if the form asks for uploaded documents.
   - Save the submission confirmation and a copy of exactly what you uploaded.

6. **Do a quick account-safety check.**
   - Look for other disputes tied to the same email, address, device, product, or account.
   - Review recent staff logins, admin changes, payout changes, and bank-detail changes in your payment dashboard.
   - If the notification came by email, treat it as a possible phishing hook until the dashboard confirms it.

7. **Use official reporting routes only if there is more than an ordinary dispute.**
   - If you suspect cyber-enabled crime, you can report it to the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center.
   - If you suspect a broader fraud or scam pattern, you can report it to the Federal Trade Commission.
   - Reporting does not replace your processor’s chargeback response.

8. **If your provider shows a pre-chargeback or early warning alert, respond inside that case.**
   - Some providers, including PayPal, offer alerts before a chargeback fully lands.
   - Follow the platform’s instructions immediately because early-warning windows can be short.
   - If the order was already fulfilled, provide the evidence the alert asks for rather than issuing a duplicate refund without checking the case stage.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to sue, use collections, or make major business policy changes.
- You do not need to write a long argument with the customer before dealing with the processor’s deadline.
- You do not need perfect evidence; you need relevant evidence that directly answers the dispute reason.
- You do not need to redesign your fraud prevention process while the response clock is running.

## Important reassurance
A chargeback notice can feel personal, but it is usually part of a structured card dispute process. The safest first response is to slow down, verify the case in the official dashboard, and answer the exact dispute reason with clean evidence.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and avoid irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about repeated chargebacks, legal action, account reserves, or business risk may need help from your merchant services provider, payment platform, accountant, attorney, or another qualified adviser.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Chargeback rules, deadlines, fees, and acceptable evidence vary by card network, issuing bank, processor, acquirer, platform, contract, and dispute category. Follow the instructions shown in your specific case.
