# What to do if… you are asked for a certified translation of a travel document and you do not have one (USA)

## Short answer
Request the exact certification wording and deadline from the requester, then obtain a professional English translation that includes a signed "Certificate of Accuracy" confirming the translator's competence and the translation's completeness.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use machine translation tools or self-translate for official vital records like birth or marriage certificates.
- Do not assume that a notarized document is automatically a "certified translation" or vice versa.
- Do not assume an apostille replaces the need for a translation.
- Do not hand over original documents to a translation service unless they specifically require physical inspection.
- Do not sign a translator's certification yourself if you are the applicant, as this often triggers a rejection for bias.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific certification requirements for your current situation.** 
   - Ask the requester if they require a specific "Certification of Accuracy" statement.
   - Confirm if the translator must be a member of the American Translators Association (ATA) or another professional body.
   - Ask if a digital PDF with an electronic signature is acceptable or if they require a physical hard copy with a wet signature.

2. **Verify if the document must follow USCIS regulation 8 CFR 103.2(b)(3) standards.**
   - USCIS requires a full English translation for any foreign language document.
   - The translator must provide a signed statement certifying that the translation is "complete and accurate."
   - The translator must certify that they are "competent to translate from the foreign language into English."

3. **Capture high-resolution scans of every page of the document immediately.**
   - Ensure all stamps, seals, handwritten marginalia, and the reverse side of the paper are visible.
   - Save the files as a single multi-page PDF to facilitate rapid "same-day" or "express" professional services.

4. **Request a conditional acceptance or a filing extension from the authority.**
   - Ask: "Can I submit the original foreign document now and provide the certified translation within [number] hours/days?"
   - If at an airport, ask if the airline can accept a digital copy of a previous translation while you wait for a formal certification.

5. **Order a professional certified translation from a provider that offers 24-hour turnaround.**
   - Provide the translator with the specific wording required by the requesting agency.
   - Ensure the final packet includes the English translation, a copy of the original document, and the signed certification statement.
   - If the requester explicitly demands notarization, ensure the translator signs the certification in the presence of a Notary Public.

6. **Clarify whether you also need an apostille for international use.**
   - If the document is for use outside the U.S., check if the destination country requires an apostille from the Secretary of State (for state documents) or the U.S. Department of State (for federal documents).
   - Complete the translation *after* the apostille is attached if the apostille itself must also be translated.

## What can wait
- You do not need to book an appointment with a Notary Public until the requester confirms that a standard translator's certification is insufficient.
- You do not need to rebook travel until you have received a definitive "denied boarding" or "rejected" status from the official in charge.
- You do not need to obtain an ATA-certified translator unless the specific agency (e.g., some specific state courts) explicitly mandates that specific credential.

## Important reassurance
Translation requirements are a standard procedural hurdle and do not necessarily mean your document is invalid or that your travel is cancelled. Professional translation services in the U.S. are equipped to handle urgent requests and can often produce the necessary certification within a few hours once they have a clear scan of the document.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for document translation requests in the USA. It does not cover specific legal strategies for immigration or complex litigation. Requirements for specific foreign embassies or niche state agencies may vary.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Requirements for "certified translations" vary significantly between federal agencies (like USCIS), state agencies (like the DMV), and private entities (like airlines). Always follow the written instructions provided by the requesting authority.
