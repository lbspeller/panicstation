# What to do if… you are asked for a certified translation of a travel document and you do not have one (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the requester to confirm exactly which document needs translating, what they mean by “certified,” the accepted format, and the deadline. Get the requirement in writing where possible, then obtain a translation that matches it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume that a machine translation or an informal translation will be accepted.
- Do not assume that “certified” means “notarized.”
- Do not pay for an apostille or authentication certificate unless the receiving authority says it is required.
- Do not send away or leave your only original document with a private service unless you understand how it will be returned.
- Do not cancel or rebook travel before asking whether the requester accepts a digital copy, a later submission, or another route.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the exact requirement.**
   - Ask which document or pages need translating.
   - Ask whether the translation must be complete or whether selected pages are enough.
   - Ask whether a scan or PDF is accepted.
   - Ask whether the translator must include a signed statement, contact details, notarization, or another specific form of certification.
   - Ask for the deadline and the accepted way to submit it.

2. **Make a clear scan pack.**
   - Scan or photograph every required page clearly.
   - Include the reverse side if it has stamps, seals, or writing.
   - Keep a backup copy.

3. **Match the translation to the process.**
   - For a document submitted to USCIS that contains a foreign language, provide a full English translation. The translator must certify that the translation is complete and accurate and that they are competent to translate from the foreign language into English.
   - For immigrant visa civil documents, follow the instructions for your case. Documents not written in English or in the official language of the country where you are applying generally need a certified translation with a signed statement that the translation is accurate and the translator is competent to translate.

4. **Order only what the requester requires.**
   - Send the translator the written requirement and a clear copy of the document.
   - Ask the translator to include the required signed statement and any requested contact details.
   - Request notarization only if the receiving authority specifically requires it.
   - For an immigrant visa upload, include the original-language document and its certified translation in one file. For another process, follow the requester’s written submission instructions.

5. **Ask for the accepted next step if the deadline is immediate.**
   - At a check-in desk, office, or appointment, ask whether a digital copy, later submission, supervisor review, or another documented next step is available.
   - Do not assume that an extension or conditional acceptance will be offered.

6. **Keep translation separate from apostille or authentication.**
   - Translation changes the language of a document.
   - An apostille or authentication certificate verifies signatures, stamps, or seals on some U.S. official documents used outside the United States.
   - Ask the receiving authority whether either process is required before paying for it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to cancel or rebook travel until you know the accepted next step.
- You do not need notarization unless the receiving authority specifically requires it.
- You do not need an apostille or authentication certificate unless the receiving authority says it is required for your document.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to solve every possible document issue at once. Getting the exact written requirement first can prevent unnecessary work, cost, and rushed decisions.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Requirements vary by requester, application type, and destination. Later decisions may need help from the relevant authority or a qualified specialist.
