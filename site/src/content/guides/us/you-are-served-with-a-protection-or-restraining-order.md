# What to do if… you are served with a protection or restraining order (USA)

## Short answer
Check whether the packet includes an issued court order, not only a petition or notice. Follow any current restrictions exactly and avoid contact with the petitioner while you confirm what is in effect.

## Do not do these things
- Do not contact the petitioner directly or ask anyone else to pass a message unless the current order expressly permits a specific method.
- Do not go to any place the current order says you must avoid, even briefly.
- Do not reply impulsively to messages, delete messages, or alter records.
- Do not post about the case online or ask mutual contacts to argue your side.
- Do not ignore the packet, a stated deadline, or a hearing date because you feel overwhelmed.
- Do not pressure potential witnesses or try to coordinate their account of events.
- Do not access accounts, devices, or information unless you clearly have permission to do so.

## What to do now
1. **Create a calm separation.** If you are near the petitioner or a place that may be restricted, leave calmly if it is safe to do so. Silence notifications so you do not reply impulsively. Keep existing messages and records.

2. **Check what you actually received.** Look for:
   - a petition or application,
   - a notice of hearing,
   - an issued temporary or final order,
   - the issue date and any service information,
   - any page stating that restrictions are currently in effect.

   If the packet is incomplete, contact the court using its official website or an independently verified court number. Ask how to obtain a complete copy and whether an order is currently in effect.

3. **Write down the immediate terms.** Note:
   - the hearing date, time, location, and any remote-access instructions,
   - the expiration date, if listed,
   - contact restrictions, including third-party contact,
   - distance limits and restricted places,
   - child-exchange instructions, if included,
   - firearms, ammunition, weapons, or surrender instructions,
   - any filing or service deadline.

4. **Follow the issued order exactly.** Do not assume that a filed petition creates the same restrictions as an issued order. If wording is unclear, pause any action that could violate the order. Ask the court about procedure and ask a local lawyer or court self-help service about the meaning of the terms.

5. **Confirm the local response process.** Ask the court clerk:
   - whether a written response is required,
   - which form to use and when it is due,
   - how to file it,
   - whether another party must be served,
   - how to submit documents for the hearing,
   - how to request more time if needed.

6. **Look for local legal help today.** Contact a local lawyer, legal-aid organization, bar referral service, or courthouse self-help center. Protection-order procedures vary by state and court.

7. **Avoid accidental contact during shared logistics.**
   - If children, school pickup, work, or property are involved, use only a method expressly allowed by the current order.
   - If you need belongings from a shared home, do not arrive unannounced. Ask a lawyer, the court, or local law enforcement whether a permitted retrieval process or civil-standby option is available.

8. **Read any firearms or weapons section now.** Some protection orders can trigger state or federal firearms or ammunition restrictions. Follow any stated surrender or transfer process exactly. Do not improvise or ask someone else to hold items unless the paperwork or qualified local legal advice confirms that the method is allowed.

9. **Make a small hearing folder.** Keep the petition, notice, issued order, proof of service, and court instructions together. Add a short factual timeline and copies of records you already lawfully possess.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a long defense statement tonight.
- You do not need to convince friends, relatives, coworkers, or mutual contacts of your side.
- You can wait to make longer-term strategy decisions until you have read the packet and sought local help, but do not miss any stated deadline or hearing date.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shocked, angry, or frightened can make it harder to read court papers carefully. You can reduce the immediate risk by creating distance, avoiding contact, identifying any issued order, and taking one procedural step at a time.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the initial shock period only. Later decisions may need help from a local lawyer or court self-help service because protection-order rules and procedures vary by state and court.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal or professional advice. A local lawyer can advise you about your specific papers, deadlines, restrictions, and options.
