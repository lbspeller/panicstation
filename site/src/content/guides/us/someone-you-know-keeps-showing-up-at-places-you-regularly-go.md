# What to do if… someone you know keeps showing up at places you regularly go (USA)

## Short answer
If you see them now, go to a staffed public place or another safer location. Call 911 if you are in immediate danger; once you have a safer pause, tell someone you trust, record what happened, and contact a victim advocate for support.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront them alone, follow them, set a trap, or agree to meet to talk things out.
- Don’t post real-time location details, check-ins, travel plans, or routine updates.
- Don’t delete messages, voicemails, emails, notes, or gifts.
- Don’t make account or device changes on a device you think may be monitored before saving evidence safely or getting technology-safety advice.
- Don’t use a device or browser you think may be monitored to look up support if you have a safer option.
- Don’t assume you need an explicit threat before asking for help. Repeated unwanted appearances can still be worth documenting and discussing with an advocate.
- Don’t make major decisions such as moving or quitting a job while you are in the first wave of panic.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safer pause.** If you have just seen them, move into a staffed public place, a locked workplace area, or another location where other people are present. Call 911 if you are in immediate danger.
2. **Tell one person now.** Share where you are and what happened. Ask for a check-in, a ride, or company while you leave the area.
3. **Alert useful locations where safe for you.** Consider telling workplace security, a front desk, campus security, a school office, gym staff, or a building manager. Ask them not to share your schedule, to contact you if the person appears, and to help you reach your car or transport safely.
4. **Start a simple incident log.** Record the date, time, place, what happened, any witnesses, safely saved messages or photos, and any police report numbers. Keep the log somewhere the other person cannot access.
5. **Consider reporting the pattern.** If it is not an emergency, contact your local police department’s non-emergency route, where available, or ask a local victim advocate how reporting works where you live. Explain that the appearances are repeated and are causing safety concerns.
6. **Ask about local protection options.** Court-order names, eligibility rules, and filing routes vary. A local victim advocate or court self-help service can explain what may apply, including whether a civil protective order or a criminal no-contact order is relevant.
7. **Reduce routine exposure for now, if practical.** Ask for an escort, use staffed entrances, avoid leaving alone, and choose well-lit parking or pickup points.
8. **Use a safer device for account checks if possible.** Review location sharing, shared accounts, and app permissions from a device the other person cannot access. If you suspect monitoring, get technology-safety guidance before making changes that could alert them.
9. **Use specialist support.** VictimConnect offers confidential referrals by phone or text at 855-484-2846. If this involves an ex-partner or dating relationship, the National Domestic Violence Hotline is available at 800-799-SAFE (7233).

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to relocate, change jobs, or confront the person.
- You do not need perfect proof before asking for help.
- Longer-term legal, housing, work, or account-security decisions can wait until you have support and a safer pause.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to second-guess yourself when each incident could appear explainable on its own. You do not need to settle the person’s intent right now. Your immediate task is to create distance, keep a record, and build support.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a local victim advocate, a lawyer, law enforcement, workplace security, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Call 911 if you are in immediate danger.
