# What to do if… your approved time off is cancelled with very short notice (USA)

## Short answer
Get the cancellation and the expectation to work in writing, then check your employer’s PTO policy, contract, or handbook. Reply calmly in writing with your constraint, two workable alternatives, and a request for a clear written decision about the dates and your PTO balance.

## Do not do these things
- Do not no-call/no-show; that can create disciplinary risk even if the cancellation feels unfair.
- Do not rage-text, threaten lawsuits, or quit on the spot; pause and keep messages factual.
- Do not rely on a verbal promise such as “we’ll make it up to you”; ask for dates, pay, PTO balance, or any exception in writing.
- Do not give detailed medical information to a manager; if the time off may be health-related or family-care leave, ask HR for the proper leave process.
- Do not assume there is one nationwide PTO rule; vacation and PTO are often controlled by employer policy, contracts, union agreements, and state or local law.

## What to do now
1. **Save the proof before replying.** Save the original approval, the cancellation message, schedule changes, PTO balance screenshot, travel or childcare commitments, and any handbook page that mentions cancelling approved time off.

2. **Write a short timeline.** Note who approved the time off, the dates approved, who cancelled it, when you were told, what work you are now expected to do, and whether you are already traveling or locked into non-refundable plans.

3. **Confirm exactly what the employer is requiring.** Send a short message such as: “I saw the cancellation notice for my approved time off on [dates]. Please confirm whether I am being required to work those dates and what will happen to the PTO hours already approved.”

4. **Check whether this is ordinary PTO or protected leave.** If the time off is for vacation, it is usually treated as an employer benefit. If it is for your own serious health condition, care for a qualifying family member, military-family reasons, pregnancy-related needs, paid sick leave, or another protected reason, ask HR for the correct leave process and keep medical or family details out of manager messages.

5. **Pull the workplace rule.** Check the employee handbook, PTO policy, offer letter, union contract, scheduling policy, or HR portal. Look for cancellation rules, blackout periods, emergency staffing language, approval wording, payout or forfeiture rules, and who can approve an exception.

6. **Offer two realistic alternatives.** Name options that could actually work, such as partial coverage for specific hours, remote coverage if you can do it, a shift swap with a named coworker, moving the PTO to specific replacement dates, or using unpaid time if your workplace allows it.

7. **If money is already committed, ask for an exception in writing.** Do not assume trip, childcare, or appointment costs will be reimbursed. State the non-refundable commitment briefly and ask whether the policy, contract, or HR exception process allows reimbursement, restored PTO, replacement dates, or another written accommodation.

8. **If you cannot work those dates, say so plainly.** Use one calm sentence: “I cannot work [dates] because [brief constraint], but I can [nearest workable alternative]. Please confirm the decision in writing.” Avoid arguing point by point in a fast-moving message thread.

9. **Keep the record in one place.** Use email, HR ticketing, or another written channel where possible. Keep copies outside any system you could lose access to, while following workplace confidentiality rules.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign.
- You do not need to research every state law before getting the cancellation, work requirement, and PTO balance decision in writing.
- You do not need to make a formal complaint immediately unless there is a protected-leave, discrimination, retaliation, wage, safety, or union issue that needs urgent help.
- Longer-term decisions, such as escalating to HR leadership, contacting a state labor office, filing a complaint, or looking for another job, can wait until the immediate dates and written record are clearer.

## Important reassurance
A last-minute reversal can feel chaotic, especially when money, care arrangements, travel, or family plans are already involved. Keeping the next message short, written, and option-focused can reduce panic and help you get either the time off restored or a clear decision you can act on.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the cancellation involves protected leave, discrimination, retaliation, union rights, wage or benefit disputes, or state-specific PTO rules, later decisions may need specialist help for your state and circumstances.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. PTO and leave rules can vary by employer policy, contract, union agreement, and state or local law.
