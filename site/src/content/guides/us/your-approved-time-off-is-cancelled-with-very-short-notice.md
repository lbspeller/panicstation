# What to do if… your approved time off is cancelled with very short notice (USA)

## Short answer
Do not agree or sign immediately. Request the proposed changes in writing with a specific effective date and compare them against your original offer letter, employment agreement, or union contract before responding.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any document during the initial meeting if you feel pressured or do not fully understand the legal rights you may be waiving.
- Do not assume a change is "informational" or "automatic" — treat any modification to pay, hours, or duties as a formal amendment to your working conditions.
- Do not quit your job in the heat of the moment, as this can forfeit your eligibility for unemployment benefits or severance in many states.
- Do not agree to a "Stay or Pay" or training repayment clause without checking 2026 state-specific bans (such as those in California or New York).
- Do not allow a change to be applied retroactively to work you have already performed; this is generally illegal wage theft.
- Do not rely on verbal promises that the change is "temporary" — only the written terms carry weight.

## What to do now
1. **Request the full change in writing.** Ask for a document that explicitly details:
   - The exact nature of the change (pay rate, commission structure, hours, location, or duties).
   - The specific effective date for the change.
   - Whether this change supersedes or amends specific sections of your existing signed agreements.
2. **Review your state's mandatory notice period for pay cuts.** 
   - Several states (including CA, NY, NV, and MO) require employers to provide written notice between 24 hours and 30 days before a pay reduction takes effect.
   - Confirm if your employer has met the specific notice threshold for your jurisdiction before the new rate is applied.
3. **Gather all governing employment documents.** Keep copies of:
   - Your original offer letter and any subsequent written amendments.
   - Signed employment contracts or executive agreements.
   - The current employee handbook and any "acknowledgment of receipt" forms.
   - Bonus or commission plan documents that define when a payment is "earned."
4. **Contact your union representative immediately if you are represented.**
   - Changes to "mandatory subjects of bargaining" (wages, hours, and working conditions) generally require the employer to bargain with the union first.
   - Under 2026 NLRB standards, employers must show a "clear and unmistakable waiver" in the contract to make unilateral changes; ask your rep if the change violates this standard.
5. **Verify the local and state minimum wage for 2026.**
   - Ensure the new pay rate does not fall below the 2026 minimum wage for your specific city or state, which may be significantly higher than the federal rate.
   - For exempt (salaried) employees, confirm the new salary still meets the increased 2026 "white collar" salary thresholds required to maintain overtime-exempt status.
6. **Send a formal "under review" response.** 
   - State in writing (email or letter) that you have received the proposal, are currently reviewing it, and will provide a response or request a meeting to discuss it by a specific date. 
   - Do not state that you "accept" or "agree" in this initial message.
7. **Document the "earning moment" for commissions or bonuses.**
   - If your commission structure is changing, identify whether you have already "earned" specific deals under the old plan. 
   - Employers generally cannot retroactively change the rate for commissions where the work is complete and the conditions for payment were met.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to stay or resign in the first 24 hours.
- You do not need to hire an attorney immediately, provided you have not signed away your rights or accepted the new terms.
- You do not need to provide a counter-offer or negotiate until you have verified your baseline legal protections under state law.
- You do not need to file a formal wage claim or NLRB charge today; your priority is establishing a written record of the employer's proposed change.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel startled or defensive when the terms of your livelihood change without your input. Taking a pause to verify the legality of the change is a standard professional practice and does not signify a lack of loyalty or "troublemaking."

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for employment term changes in the United States. It does not cover specific industry-wide regulations (such as healthcare or government contracting) or complex international "secondment" agreements.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Employment law in the U.S. is highly dependent on state-specific statutes and "at-will" presumptions that may allow for significant employer discretion. If you are being pressured to sign a document that involves waiving your right to sue, non-compete clauses, or significant back-pay, consult a qualified employment attorney or a local legal aid clinic immediately.
