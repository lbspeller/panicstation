# What to do if… an unexpected subscription renewal has overdrawn your bank account (USA)

## Short answer
Cancel the renewal at the subscription service and contact your bank, credit union, or card issuer promptly. Ask them to identify the payment type, explain how to stop future attempts, and review any overdraft or non-sufficient funds (NSF) fees.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume turning off auto-renew will remove a charge that has already posted.
- Don’t assume an overdraft opt-in setting prevents fees for every payment type. Recurring electronic payments may be treated differently from ATM withdrawals and one-time debit card purchases.
- Don’t close the bank account in a rush. That can disrupt wages, rent, and essential bills.
- Don’t pay the subscription company a second time until you know whether the first charge will be refunded.
- Don’t take expensive credit in a rush.
- Don’t ignore other payments that are already queued against the account.

## What to do now
1. **Save the visible details.** While the account page is open, save or write down the renewal amount, merchant name, date, current balance, and any overdraft or NSF fee. Check which essential payments are pending next.

2. **Turn off the renewal at the subscription service.**
   - Use the account, app, or website to turn off auto-renew or cancel the subscription.
   - Save the confirmation email, reference number, or screenshot.
   - Check whether you cancelled only the automatic payment or also ended the subscription. Stopping an automatic payment does not necessarily remove any amount you still owe under an agreement.

3. **Ask the subscription company for a refund.**
   - Say that the renewal was unexpected and caused an overdraft.
   - Ask whether the posted charge can be refunded.
   - Keep a dated record of the request and any reply.

4. **Contact your bank, credit union, or card issuer.**
   - Ask them to identify whether the renewal was a preauthorized electronic fund transfer from your bank account, another bank-account debit, a debit card charge, or a credit card charge.
   - Ask what can be done to stop future attempts from that subscription service.
   - Ask whether any other payments are pending and whether additional fees could still be charged.

5. **For a preauthorized electronic fund transfer from your bank account, ask about stopping future payments.**
   - Tell the company that you revoke permission for future automatic withdrawals and keep a written record.
   - Tell your bank or credit union that you revoked permission and ask about its stop-payment process.
   - An oral or written stop-payment notice made at least three business days before a scheduled preauthorized electronic fund transfer can stop that transfer. If the bank or credit union requires written confirmation after an oral request, send it within 14 days.

6. **Ask for fee relief.**
   - Say: “This unexpected subscription renewal overdrew my account. Can you review and reverse any overdraft or NSF fee?”
   - Ask whether there is anything you can do today to reduce the risk of more fees from queued payments.

7. **Protect the most urgent essentials.**
   - Check whether rent, utilities, childcare, medication, or transport payments are due before the account is stable.
   - If there is not enough money to cover an urgent payment, contact that biller and ask whether the payment date can be moved or whether a short extension is available.

8. **Raise a dispute promptly if the charge was not authorised or another charge appears after cancellation or revocation.**
   - For a bank-account transfer, tell the bank or credit union what happened and ask which electronic-transfer error process applies.
   - For a debit card or credit card charge, tell the issuer what happened and ask which dispute process applies. Ask whether written follow-up is needed and where to send it.
   - Provide the cancellation confirmation, revocation record, and merchant messages you saved.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to use the service again.
- Changing banks or moving every automatic payment today.
- Reworking your full budget.
- Making an outside complaint before you have contacted the company and your bank, credit union, or card issuer, unless you need to escalate an unresolved problem later.

## Important reassurance
An unexpected renewal can create several urgent-looking problems at once. You do not need to solve everything today. Start with the posted charge, future renewal attempts, fees, and the next essential payments.

## Scope note
These are first steps to limit further disruption and buy time. Later decisions may need help from an appropriate financial or consumer-support specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes can vary by payment type, financial institution, account terms, and the facts of the charge. Ask your bank, credit union, or card issuer to identify the payment type and explain the process that applies.
