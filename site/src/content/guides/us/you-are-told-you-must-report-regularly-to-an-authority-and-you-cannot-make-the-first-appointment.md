# What to do if… you are told you must report regularly to an authority and you cannot make the first appointment (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the specific supervising office **immediately** via their official phone number or email to request a reschedule, then document your attempt with a screenshot or call log. If this is an ICE check-in, you can often report any time on the scheduled day without a penalty.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait until after the appointment time has passed to make your first contact.
- Do not assume that leaving one voicemail fulfills your reporting obligation.
- Do not share personal details about your underlying case; keep the communication strictly about the scheduling conflict.
- Do not ignore the 72-hour reporting rule if you were just released from federal prison.
- Do not skip reporting because you lack a specific document; report what you have.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific agency and your identifier numbers.** Locate your paperwork (such as a Judgement in a Criminal Case, Form I-385, or a Release Order) to find:
   - The agency name (U.S. Probation, Pretrial Services, or ICE/ERO).
   - Your Subject ID, Case Number, or A-Number.
   - The specific address of the office where you were told to appear.
2. **Call the office's main line or the assigned officer immediately.** If you cannot reach a specific person, ask to speak with the **Duty Officer** or a supervisor.
   - For ICE appointments, if the local office does not answer, call the national help line at 1-833-383-1465.
3. **State your name, case number, and the specific logistical barrier.** Use a neutral statement:
   - "I am [Name], Case [Number]. I have a first reporting appointment on [Date] at [Time] but I cannot attend due to [Transportation/Illness]. I am requesting a rescheduled time."
4. **Attempt to reschedule online if the agency provides a portal.** 
   - For ICE check-ins, use the official scheduler at checkin.ice.gov to move the date.
   - Save the confirmation page or take a photo of the screen once the change is submitted.
5. **Report later the same day if possible for immigration check-ins.** If you miss the specific time for an ICE/ERO appointment, arriving later the same day (during business hours) is generally recorded as being in compliance.
6. **Create a physical record of your communication attempts.** Write down the date, time, phone number called, and the name of every person you spoke with.
   - Send a follow-up email to the office's official address immediately after any phone call to confirm what was discussed.
7. **Verify the 72-hour rule for federal supervision.** If you have just been released from custody, you are typically required by law to report to the probation office within 72 hours; if you cannot physically arrive, you must obtain a specific alternative instruction from a probation officer within that same window.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide medical records or proof of your excuse during the very first phone call.
- You do not need to speak with your lawyer before making the initial call to report the scheduling conflict.
- You do not need to worry about long-term changes to your reporting schedule right now.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of panic when a mandatory appointment conflicts with an emergency or a logistical failure. Courts and agencies generally distinguish between a "willful failure to report" and a "communicated scheduling conflict." Providing immediate notice is the primary way to demonstrate you are not evading supervision.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate scheduling conflicts with reporting authorities. It does not cover legal defense for existing violations or motions to modify the permanent conditions of your release.

## Important note
Reporting requirements are legally binding conditions of release. Failure to comply can result in a bench warrant, arrest, or detention. This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. If you are under court supervision, you should notify your attorney of any missed or rescheduled appointments as soon as possible.
