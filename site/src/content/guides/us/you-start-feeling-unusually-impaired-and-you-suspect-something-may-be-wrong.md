# What to do if… you start feeling unusually impaired and you suspect something may be wrong (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safe, public, or well-ventilated area immediately and notify a trusted person or staff member. Call 911 if you are struggling to stay awake, having trouble breathing, or experiencing severe confusion.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive any vehicle or operate machinery.
- Do not leave a public venue alone or with someone you do not know well.
- Do not consume any more food, drinks, or substances to try to "neutralize" the feeling.
- Do not try to induce vomiting.
- Do not re-enter a building if you suspect carbon monoxide or a gas leak.
- Do not wait to see if symptoms pass if you are alone; seek a witness or helper immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer environment immediately.**  
   **Get to a staffed area such as a security desk, bar, or reception if you are in public, or get into fresh air if you are indoors.** If you feel physically unstable, sit or lie down once you have reached a person who can assist you.
2. **Stop all intake and secure your belongings.**  
   **Immediately stop eating or drinking whatever you were consuming and keep your phone and ID physically on your person.** If you suspect a drink was spiked, do not leave it unattended; if possible, alert staff so they can secure the container for potential testing later.
3. **Call 911 if you experience emergency symptoms.**  
   **Contact emergency services immediately if you have chest pain, seizures, difficulty breathing, or if you cannot keep your eyes open.** Tell the operator: "I am experiencing sudden, unexplained impairment and I need medical assistance."
4. **Place yourself in the recovery position if you feel you may lose consciousness.**  
   **Lie on your side with your top leg bent and your mouth angled toward the ground to keep your airway clear.** This prevents choking if you become unresponsive or vomit.
5. **Contact Poison Control for guidance on stable symptoms.**  
   **Call the Poison Help hotline at 1-800-222-1222 for free, confidential triage if you are conscious and stable.** They can determine if your symptoms require an emergency room visit or if you can be monitored at home.
6. **Identify yourself to venue staff or a trusted friend.**  
   **Tell a staff member "I feel unwell and I think my drink was spiked" or "I am confused and need help."** Do not let yourself be led away to a private area by anyone other than verified emergency medical personnel or a person you came with and trust fully.
7. **Evacuate if environment-related poisoning is suspected.**  
   **Move all people and pets to fresh air immediately if multiple people feel sick or if symptoms like headache and nausea improve when you go outside.** Do not go back inside for any reason until the fire department or utility company confirms the area is safe.
8. **Record basic facts if you are able.**  
   **Type a quick note in your phone or tell a helper what you last ate or drank and what medications you have taken today.** This information is critical for paramedics if you become unable to communicate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the exact cause of the impairment before seeking help.
- You do not need to decide whether to file a formal police report right now.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of an ambulance or ER visit during the initial crisis.
- You do not need to explain or justify why you feel impaired to bystanders.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel frightened or confused when your coordination or thinking changes suddenly. These physical reactions—such as a racing heart or mental fog—are common responses to impairment and do not mean you are overreacting. Taking a safe pause to get help is the standard procedure for these symptoms.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for sudden impairment. It does not cover long-term recovery, forensic evidence collection, or legal proceedings.

## Important note
This information is for situational stabilization only and is not a substitute for professional medical diagnosis or legal advice. If you suspect you have been the victim of a crime or a toxic exposure, professional medical evaluation is the only way to confirm what has happened to your body.
