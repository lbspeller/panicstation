# What to do if… you have severe cramps and weakness after vomiting or diarrhea and cannot keep fluids down (USA)

## Short answer
Severe cramps and weakness after vomiting or diarrhea may be signs of dehydration, and severe dehydration can be life-threatening. Arrange prompt medical assessment now if you cannot keep enough liquids down or are barely urinating; call 911 now for loss of consciousness, confusion, a seizure, or a marked change in alertness.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delay getting help if you are faint, confused, unusually drowsy, getting worse, barely urinating, or unable to keep liquids down.
- Do not drink a large amount all at once if vomiting is continuing.
- Do not use alcohol or caffeinated drinks to rehydrate.
- Do not rely only on soda or other sugary drinks when you are struggling to rehydrate.
- Do not take salt tablets.
- Do not start an over-the-counter antidiarrheal medicine if you have bloody stools, black stools, or a fever. Ask a clinician or pharmacist first.
- Do not give an over-the-counter antidiarrheal medicine to a child unless a clinician or pharmacist says it is appropriate.
- Do not drive yourself for care if you feel faint, confused, unusually drowsy, or very weak.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 now for emergency warning signs.**
   - loss of consciousness
   - confusion, a seizure, or a marked change in alertness
   - collapse or inability to stay awake

2. **Arrange prompt medical assessment now if you cannot rehydrate.**
   - Contact a clinician for prompt advice and assessment if you cannot keep enough liquids down, are urinating much less than usual, have very dark urine, feel dizzy when standing, or are getting weaker.
   - Use the ER if the symptoms are severe or worsening, you have no urine, or prompt assessment is not available.
   - Seek urgent medical assessment now for severe abdominal pain, black or tarry stools, or bloody stools. Use the ER if the symptoms are severe or worsening or a prompt clinician route is not available.
   - Severe dehydration may need hospital treatment, including IV fluids.

3. **Try small, frequent sips while arranging care.**
   - Use an over-the-counter oral rehydration solution, such as Pedialyte or a store-brand oral rehydration solution.
   - If vomiting continues, take small sips frequently rather than drinking a large amount at once.
   - Try sucking on ice chips if small sips are difficult.
   - Increase the amount gradually only if the small sips stay down.

4. **Watch for worsening dehydration signs.**
   - much less urination than usual or no urination
   - very dark urine
   - dizziness, light-headedness, or fainting when standing
   - increasing weakness, confusion, or unusual drowsiness
   - rapid heartbeat or rapid breathing

5. **Tell the clinician about higher-risk circumstances.**
   - Say if the person is pregnant, an older adult, a baby or child, or has diabetes, kidney disease, a weakened immune system, or another significant health condition.
   - Mention any medicines already taken and whether liquids are staying down.

6. **Let food wait until liquids are staying down.**
   - Do not force a meal while vomiting is continuing.
   - Resume small amounts of food gradually when you can tolerate them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to force a normal meal while liquids are difficult to keep down.
- You do not need to decide about work, school, or travel now.
- You do not need to try several over-the-counter medicines while you are weak or dehydrated.

## Important reassurance
Getting assessed promptly is a reasonable protective step. You do not need to work out whether this is a stomach bug, food poisoning, or another cause before asking for help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A clinician may need to assess the cause, check for complications, and decide whether further treatment is needed.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice or a diagnosis. Seek prompt medical care if you cannot keep enough liquids down or have signs of dehydration.
