# What to do if… your employer warns you that they may not be able to pay your wages (USA)

## Short answer
Save your pay records outside company systems and ask for written details about the affected payday. If wages are late or short, act promptly: contact your state labor office and ask the U.S. Department of Labor’s Wage and Hour Division whether a federal law it enforces may also apply.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign, sign an agreement, or make a rushed decision before saving your records and checking your options.
- Do not rely only on messages, files, or pay portals that you can access only through your work account.
- Do not agree in a rush to gift cards, crypto, an IOU, a loan document, a release, or another substitute for expected wages.
- Do not assume a paper check has solved the problem. Keep the check, pay stub, deposit receipt, and any bank notice if payment fails.
- Do not stop keeping your own record of hours worked, pay dates, and amounts owed.
- Do not share sensitive personal information through an unfamiliar contact route. Confirm that you are communicating with the company’s usual payroll or HR contact.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for written details.** Request:
   - the pay date that may be affected
   - whether the concern is a delay, a missed payroll, or a partial payment
   - which workers may be affected
   - the payroll or HR contact to use if a deposit or check fails
2. **Save your records outside company systems.** Keep:
   - your offer letter, employment agreement, or other record of your pay rate and usual pay schedule
   - recent pay stubs
   - your own record of hours worked, including schedules or timekeeping exports if relevant
   - records of commissions, bonuses, overtime, or other amounts you believe may be owed
   - written messages about the payroll warning
3. **Write down the next expected payday.** Note your usual payment method and the amount you normally expect. If the payment is missing, short, reversed, or returned, save the bank notice, account screenshot, check, and pay stub.
4. **Ask for details before agreeing to any pay change or delay.** Ask for the proposed amount, effective date, affected pay period, expected payment date, deductions, and any remaining balance in writing. Get advice before signing anything you do not understand.
5. **If you are in a union, contact your union representative.** Ask what internal route to use if the expected payment is late or short.
6. **Locate your state labor office before payday.** Use the state labor-office directory in the resources below and find the current wage-claim or wage-payment guidance for your state.
7. **If the expected pay is late or short, act promptly.**
   - tell payroll or HR in writing what is missing
   - ask when and how the missing amount will be paid
   - keep copies of the reply and your payment evidence
   - contact your state labor office for state-specific guidance
   - ask the U.S. Department of Labor’s Wage and Hour Division whether a federal complaint route may apply to your situation
8. **If the company may close or cut off access, save essential contact details.** Save a payroll or HR contact method that you can reach without your work account, if one is available. Confirm the address where tax forms should be sent and download your pay records now.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, sue, or change jobs.
- You do not need to negotiate a long-term arrangement while you are still gathering the basic facts.
- You do not need to sign a document immediately because someone says it is urgent.
- You do not need to work out every possible benefit or tax question now. First secure your records and prepare for the next expected payday.

## Important reassurance
A payroll warning is serious, but you do not need to solve every possible outcome today. Saving records, getting the details in writing, and identifying the correct state contact are useful steps even if the company pays on time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on your state, your employment arrangement, the type of pay involved, and what happens on the expected payday. Specialist help may be useful if wages are actually late or short.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or other professional advice. Wage-payment rules and complaint routes vary by state and situation. Use the official resources below and ask the relevant agency or a qualified adviser about your circumstances.
