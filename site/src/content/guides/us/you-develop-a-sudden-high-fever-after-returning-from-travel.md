# What to do if… you develop a sudden high fever after returning from travel (USA)

## Short answer
Get urgent medical advice today and clearly say: “I have a high fever after travel.” If you have severe symptoms such as trouble breathing, chest pain, confusion, fainting, seizure, severe weakness, uncontrolled bleeding, or a purple rash or spots that do not fade when pressed, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait several days to “see if it passes” when a sudden high fever starts after international travel.
- Do not leave out travel details because it was “just a resort,” “just a cruise,” or “only a stopover.”
- Do not take leftover antibiotics, antimalarials, or someone else’s prescription.
- Do not take aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen, or other anti-inflammatory painkillers if dengue is possible or you have not been assessed yet, unless a clinician has told you to.
- Do not drive yourself for care if you feel faint, confused, severely weak, short of breath, or rapidly worse.
- Do not delay emergency care in order to research the most likely illness.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency signs.**  
   Call 911 now if you have trouble breathing, chest pain, new confusion, seizure, fainting or collapse, blue, gray, or pale lips or skin, severe dehydration, uncontrolled bleeding, a very severe headache with stiff neck or light sensitivity, or a purple rash or spots that do not fade when pressed.

2. **Arrange urgent same-day medical evaluation.**  
   If you are stable, call your primary care office, a travel medicine clinic, urgent care, or the nearest emergency department and say: “I have a high fever after travel.” If symptoms are significant, rapidly worsening, or you cannot get same-day help, go to an emergency department.

3. **Say your travel history early, before routine fever assumptions are made.**  
   Tell the clinician the countries and regions you visited, stopovers, cruise ports, travel dates, return date, and the day the fever started.

4. **If you were in a malaria-risk area, treat this as time-sensitive.**  
   Say clearly: “I was in a malaria-risk area and need urgent malaria testing.” Malaria can still happen even if you took preventive medicine.

5. **Write down a short exposure list before you are seen.**  
   Include mosquito bites, freshwater swimming, animal bites or scratches, tick bites, caves, farms, sick contacts, new foods, sexual exposure, medical or dental care abroad, vaccines, travel medicines, missed doses, and your highest measured temperature.

6. **Reduce possible spread while getting care.**  
   Stay away from others as much as you can, wash hands often, do not share cups or utensils, and wear a mask around other people or while travelling to care.

7. **Use safer fever support while you are arranging care.**  
   Sip fluids often. Use acetaminophen for fever or pain only if you can take it safely and you are not exceeding the label dose or a clinician’s instructions. Avoid alcohol and heavy exertion.

8. **Mention dengue warning signs if you were in a dengue area or had mosquito exposure.**  
   Seek urgent care if you have severe belly pain, repeated vomiting, bleeding from the nose or gums, blood in vomit or stool, extreme tiredness, restlessness, or symptoms that suddenly get worse after the fever begins to settle.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the diagnosis yourself now.
- You do not need to decide whether this was caused by the flight, food, insects, water, or another person.
- You do not need to handle travel insurance, refunds, work, school, or complaints before getting medical advice.
- You do not need to gather perfect records; a rough travel timeline is enough to start.

## Important reassurance
Fever after travel can feel frightening, and getting checked quickly is the sensible step. Your travel timeline gives clinicians important clues and helps them decide what to test for first.

## Scope note
These are immediate first steps only. Testing, isolation, treatment, and follow-up may need a clinician, emergency department, travel medicine specialist, infectious disease specialist, or public health advice based on your symptoms and travel history.

## Important note
This is general information, not a diagnosis, medical advice, or professional advice. If symptoms escalate quickly, you feel unsafe at home, or you have any emergency signs, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency department.
