# What to do if… someone repeatedly pressures you to remove clothing for photos and calls it harmless fun (USA)
## Short answer
Reach the safest pause you can. You do not need to remove clothing, pose, send a photo, explain yourself, or keep engaging with someone who is repeatedly pressuring you.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to a photo because they keep insisting, joking, bargaining, or acting offended.
- Do not keep trying to prove that your no is reasonable.
- Do not send a less revealing photo to calm them down.
- Do not meet them alone to sort it out if they have already ignored your boundaries.
- Do not blame yourself for freezing, going quiet, laughing nervously, or trying not to upset them.

## What to do now
1. Move toward a safer pause. Leave the room, end the call, move near other people, or contact someone you trust. If replying feels unsafe, you do not need to reply.

2. Do not remove clothing, pose, or send a photo. If it feels safe and useful, a brief message such as “Stop asking” is enough. You do not owe a longer explanation.

3. Add one immediate layer of protection. Avoid being alone with the person, ask someone to stay with you, or mute or block the contact if doing so feels safe.

4. Tell one trusted person what has been happening and ask for one specific action, such as staying with you, helping you leave, or sitting with you while you contact support.

5. If it is safe and you may want a record later, keep existing messages or screenshots somewhere the other person cannot access.

6. If this is connected to a school, college, or university, you can ask the school support route or a Title IX coordinator, where applicable, what options may reduce contact. Before sharing details, you can ask what they may need to pass on and whether a confidential support route is available.

7. If this is connected to work, you can check your employer’s harassment policy or ask HR or a supervisor which route handles concerns and what may reduce contact.

8. RAINN offers confidential support by phone, text, and chat. You can ask about privacy before sharing identifying details.

9. If an image was taken, threatened, or shared without your agreement, use the specialist image-support links below when you are ready. If you are under 18, or the image was taken when you were under 18, reach a safe adult or specialist support service as soon as possible.

10. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide right now whether to confront the person, make a police report, file a formal complaint, or explain every detail in the best order. You also do not need to settle whether this counts enough before seeking support or putting distance in place.

## Important reassurance
Repeated pressure to remove clothing or pose for photos is not made harmless by joking, flirting, or calling it fun. Freezing, going quiet, laughing nervously, or doubting yourself can happen under pressure. None of those reactions make the pressure acceptable.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about ongoing contact, school or workplace options, reporting, or specialist support may need help from an appropriate service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice.
