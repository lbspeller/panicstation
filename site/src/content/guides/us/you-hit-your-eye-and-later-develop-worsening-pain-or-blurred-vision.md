# What to do if… you hit your eye and later develop worsening pain or blurred vision (USA)

## Short answer
Seek immediate medical evaluation at an emergency room or with an ophthalmologist today. Worsening pain or vision changes after an impact can indicate serious internal damage, such as retinal detachment or internal bleeding, which requires professional treatment to prevent permanent vision loss.

## Do not do these things
- Do not rub, press, or apply any pressure to the eye or eyelid.
- Do not take aspirin or ibuprofen (Advil / Motrin), as these thin the blood and can increase internal bleeding in the eye.
- Do not wait until tomorrow to see if symptoms improve if your vision is currently blurry or pain is increasing.
- Do not try to remove contact lenses, as the manipulation can cause further trauma to a potentially damaged cornea.
- Do not apply any ointments, "redness-relief" drops, or numbing drops to the eye.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if your vision is impaired, double, or if you feel nauseated.

## What to do now
1. **Go to an Emergency Room immediately if you have high-risk symptoms.** **Go to an ER now** if you notice any of the following:
   - Sudden decrease in vision or total vision loss.
   - Severe, persistent, or "boring" pain that is getting worse.
   - Double vision or seeing "rainbow halos" around lights.
   - Nausea or vomiting accompanying the eye pain.
   - A pupil that is a different size or shape than the other eye.
   - Blood visible in the clear part of the eye (hyphema).
   - The eye seems to bulge or does not move as well as the other.
2. **Call 911 if you cannot safely transport yourself to care.** **Call emergency services** if you are alone, unable to see well enough to navigate, or feeling faint.
3. **Protect the eye with a rigid shield during transit.** **Place a rigid cover over the eye** to prevent accidental bumping or rubbing.
   - Use a clean paper cup with the bottom cut out or a dedicated eye shield.
   - Tape the shield to the bones of the forehead and cheek so it does not touch the eyelid or globe.
4. **Apply a cold compress only if there is no suspected puncture.** **Gently rest a cold pack on the surrounding bone** if the injury was a blunt hit (like a ball or fist) and there is no visible cut or fluid leaking.
   - Never apply pressure with the compress.
   - If you suspect the eye is cut or punctured, do not use a compress and do not wash the eye with water.
5. **Keep your activity level to a minimum.** **Sit or lie down with your head elevated** until you reach medical care to help manage internal eye pressure.
6. **Note the specific details of the injury for the clinician.** **Be ready to describe exactly what happened**, including the speed of the object, the exact time symptoms started to worsen, and if you are seeing new flashes of light or "floaters."

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the injury (such as a scratch versus a detachment) before seeking care.
- You do not need to find your specific vision prescription or previous eye records before leaving for the ER.
- You do not need to decide on long-term follow-up care or specialists until the acute injury is stabilized.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for eye symptoms to appear or worsen several hours after an impact as inflammation develops or internal pressure changes. Feeling anxious about your vision is a standard response to this type of injury, and seeking professional help is the most effective way to address that concern.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage for acute eye trauma. It does not replace a physical examination by a medical professional.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not medical advice. Eye injuries are time-sensitive; delaying professional evaluation can lead to permanent vision impairment.
