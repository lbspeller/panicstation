# What to do if… an officer asks you to provide your name and address and you are unsure what to say (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the officer: **"Am I legally required to provide my name and address right now?"** If the answer is yes, provide your true information; if the answer is no, you may choose to remain silent or ask if you are free to leave.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide a false name, false address, or fake identification documents.
- Do not physically resist or reach into your pockets or bags without notifying the officer of your intent.
- Do not engage in a debate regarding constitutional law or state statutes during the encounter.
- Do not volunteer information about where you are going, where you are coming from, or your immigration status.
- Do not assume that refusing to answer will automatically end the encounter; it may lead to a temporary detention while the officer confirms your identity.

## What to do now
1. **Clarify your status immediately.** Say:
   "I want to be cooperative. **Am I free to leave or am I being detained?**"
2. **Determine the legal requirement for identification.** If the officer says you are being detained, ask:
   "**Am I legally required to provide my name, address, or date of birth under state law?**"
3. **Comply with state-specific "Stop and Identify" mandates.**
   - If you are in a state with "Stop and Identify" laws and the officer has reasonable suspicion of a crime, you must generally provide your **true full name**.
   - In some states (e.g., Indiana, Ohio), you may also be required to provide your **address** and **date of birth**.
4. **State your intent to remain silent after identifying.** If you provide your name as required, follow it with:
   "**I am providing my name as required, but I am invoking my right to remain silent for all other questions.**"
5. **Handle traffic stops as a separate legal category.**
   - If you are the driver of a vehicle, you are generally required to provide your **driver's license, registration, and proof of insurance** upon request.
   - Passengers in a vehicle are generally not required to identify themselves unless there is independent reasonable suspicion regarding that specific passenger.
6. **Provide information verbally if you lack physical ID.** You are generally not required to carry a physical ID card while walking in public; if required to identify, stating your **true name and address** verbally is typically sufficient to satisfy the law.
7. **Observe the officer's response to your refusal.** If you are not in a "Stop and Identify" state and choose not to identify, say:
   "**I choose to remain silent. Am I free to go?**"
   - If the officer orders you to stay, do not leave.
   - If the officer repeats the demand for ID, you must decide whether to continue refusing (which may lead to arrest for "obstruction" in some jurisdictions, even if later overturned) or comply to end the encounter.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a formal complaint or a civil rights lawsuit while the encounter is active.
- You do not need to verify the officer's "reasonable suspicion" on the spot; that is determined later by a court.
- You do not need to justify your presence in a public area or explain your activities.

## Important reassurance
Hesitation or a stress response is a common reaction when being questioned by someone in a position of authority. Using a simple, repeatable question like "Am I free to leave?" can help you orient yourself and prevent you from accidentally volunteering information that is not legally required.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for pedestrian encounters and initial traffic stop identification. It does not cover procedures for arrest, home searches, or federal border/airport checkpoints where different identification rules apply.

## Important note
Identification laws vary significantly between states and are subject to change. This guide is for general information and does not constitute legal advice; the most cautious approach is to provide accurate identifying information if an officer states it is a legal requirement.
