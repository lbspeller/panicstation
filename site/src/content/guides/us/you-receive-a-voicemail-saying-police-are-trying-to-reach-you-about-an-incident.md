# What to do if… you receive a voicemail saying police are trying to reach you about an “incident” (USA)

## Short answer
Do not call back the number provided in the voicemail or use the "redial" function. Save the message and verify the contact independently by looking up your local agency's official non-emergency number on a ".gov" or ".org" website.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t call back the number left in the voicemail or trust the caller ID display.
- Don’t provide sensitive data such as your Social Security number, birth date, or bank details to an inbound caller.
- Don’t pay any "fine" or "bond" over the phone using gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfers, or digital payment apps.
- Don’t agree to a phone interview or answer questions about an "incident" while you are startled or overwhelmed.
- Don’t delete the recording or the call history, as these are necessary for reporting impersonation.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safe pause and do not engage immediately.** Sit down and allow the immediate rush of adrenaline to subside before taking any digital action.
2. **Preserve the evidence on your device.** Save the voicemail to your phone's storage or a cloud drive and take screenshots of the incoming call log, including the time and date.
3. **Locate an official verification channel.**
   - Call **911** only if you believe there is a person outside your home or you are in immediate physical danger.
   - Dial **311** (if available in your city) to request the non-emergency contact number for your local precinct.
   - Search for the agency’s official website (look for a .gov domain) to find their verified 10-digit non-emergency line.
4. **Initiate an independent verification call.** Call the official number you found and explain that you received a message; ask the dispatcher to verify if an officer or detective by that name is assigned to your case or has attempted to reach you.
5. **Request specific identifying information if the contact is real.** If the agency confirms the call was legitimate, ask for:
   - the officer's full name and badge number
   - the specific department or unit (e.g., "Robbery-Homicide" or "Traffic")
   - an official case or incident number
   - the best way for your legal counsel to contact them
6. **Exercise your right to remain silent if questioned.** If the contact is real and they want to discuss an incident, you may choose to state: "I am not comfortable answering questions without an attorney present."
7. **Report suspected impersonation to federal authorities.**
   - Visit **ReportFraud.ftc.gov** to log the scam attempt.
   - Visit **www.ic3.gov** by typing the address directly into your browser to report the spoofing attempt; avoid clicking "sponsored" search results for this site.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide whether to cooperate with an investigation in the next few minutes.
- You do **not** need to explain your whereabouts or "clear your name" during the initial verification phase.
- You do **not** need to confirm your identity or address until you have verified the caller is a legitimate official.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a message about a police "incident" is designed to create a sense of urgency and fear. Scammers rely on this high-stress reaction to bypass your logic. Taking the time to verify independently is a standard and responsible safety procedure that legitimate law enforcement officers understand.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and verification procedures only. It does not provide legal strategy. If the contact is verified as legitimate law enforcement, you should consult with a qualified attorney to understand your rights and obligations in your specific jurisdiction.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Law enforcement protocols vary by state and municipality. If you are unsure of the legitimacy of any official contact, always prioritize verification through a secondary, trusted source before providing information or appearing in person.
