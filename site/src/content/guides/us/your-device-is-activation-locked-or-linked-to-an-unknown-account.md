# What to do if… your device is activation locked or linked to an unknown account (USA)

## Short answer
Pause setup and do not enter passwords, one-time codes, ID images, or payment details into any link or message you were sent. Treat it as either a real ownership or management lock, or a possible account-takeover attempt, and use only official account pages typed or opened from your own bookmarks.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay for “unlock” services, “activation bypass,” or “FRP bypass” tools; many are scams and some can install malware.
- Don’t keep resetting the device or repeatedly guessing passwords; it may make recovery harder or erase useful evidence.
- Don’t click sign-in links from texts or emails claiming the device was “found,” “locked,” “verified,” or “ready to unlock.”
- Don’t share receipts, ID photos, one-time codes, recovery codes, or account recovery answers with anyone who contacted you first.
- Don’t accept “just reset it again” as the solution for a second-hand device that still asks for a previous owner’s account.

## What to do now
1. **Document the exact message before changing anything.**  
   Take photos or screenshots showing the wording, account hint, serial number, IMEI, order number, seller listing, and any messages from the seller or sender.

2. **Check whether the screen looks like an ownership lock or organization management.**  
   Look for wording such as **“iPhone Locked to Owner,” “Activation Lock,” “previously synced Google account,” “managed by your organization,” “work or school,” “Company Portal,” “MDM,”** or **“this device belongs to...”** If it looks managed and you do not recognise the organization, stop setup and contact the seller, employer, school, or platform that supplied it.

3. **If you bought it used or refurbished, contact the seller or platform in writing.**  
   Ask the seller to remove the device from their account using the official Apple, Google, Samsung, Microsoft, or platform route. If they cannot or will not do that, request a return or refund through the marketplace, payment method, or retailer.

4. **If the message came through a text, email, marketplace chat, or pop-up link, treat it as suspicious.**  
   Do not reply. Do not use the link. Open the official Apple, Google, Microsoft, Samsung, retailer, carrier, or marketplace site yourself from a trusted browser or app.

5. **Check your own account security from a trusted device.**  
   Go directly to the official account page for any account you own that might be involved. Review signed-in devices and recent activity. Remove sessions or devices you do not recognise. Change the password and confirm two-factor authentication if you entered credentials anywhere suspicious or think the account may be compromised.

6. **If it is an iPhone or iPad showing Activation Lock, use Apple’s official route only.**  
   If it is your device, follow Apple’s Activation Lock removal steps or start Apple’s support request; proof of purchase may be needed. If it belongs to a previous owner, ask that owner or seller to remove it from their Apple Account. If they will not, pursue a return or refund.

7. **If it is Android after a reset, treat it as Device Protection or Factory Reset Protection.**  
   Google’s Device Protection may require the screen lock or a Google Account previously on the device. For a second-hand device, ask the previous owner or seller to remove the account properly. For a Galaxy device, Samsung may ask for proof of purchase through an authorized service route. For other Android devices, use the device maker’s official support.

8. **If it is a Windows or Microsoft account device, check whether it is managed or already registered.**  
   A personal Microsoft account can show devices linked to that account, but a device owned or managed by a company or organization should not be registered to your personal account. Contact the seller, workplace, school, or Microsoft support route rather than trying to force setup.

9. **Report scam messages or fraud through official channels.**  
   For spam texts, forward the text to **7726 (SPAM)** if your carrier supports it, or use your messaging app’s junk or spam report option. Report scams or fraud to **ReportFraud.ftc.gov**. If you lost money, shared sensitive information, or the issue appears cyber-enabled, you can also report it to the FBI’s **IC3**.

10. **If you suspect the device was stolen or there is immediate danger, stop using it.**  
   Keep the device, packaging, receipts, listing, and messages together. Use the platform or payment method for a refund or dispute. If there is immediate danger, call **911**; otherwise contact local non-emergency law enforcement if you need to report suspected theft.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to wipe the device again.
- You do not need to solve the lock today by installing tools or paying an unlock service.
- You do not need to negotiate live by phone or chat; written messages through the seller platform are usually safer.
- You do not need to decide whether this is a scam, theft issue, seller mistake, or account problem before you have saved evidence and checked your own accounts.

## Important reassurance
This is a common situation with second-hand, refurbished, reset, work, and school devices. Slowing down helps protect your accounts, preserves refund evidence, and avoids making the device harder to resolve.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Account recovery, refund disputes, manufacturer support, workplace or school device management, and suspected theft may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, technical repair, or professional advice. If you believe a crime is in progress or someone is in immediate danger, call 911.
