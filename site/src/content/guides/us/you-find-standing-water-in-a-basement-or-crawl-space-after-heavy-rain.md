# What to do if… you find standing water in a basement or crawl space after heavy rain (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the area as an active electrocution and health hazard. Do not enter the water until you have confirmed the power is off from a dry, remote location and you are wearing protective gear.

## Do not do these things
- Do not enter standing water if it has reached outlets, baseboard heaters, appliances, or your electrical service panel.
- Do not touch any electrical switches, circuit breakers, or plugged-in devices with wet hands or while standing on a damp floor.
- Do not use a portable generator inside any room, garage, or within 20 feet of windows and doors.
- Do not pump out the water all at once if the ground outside is still saturated, as this can cause foundation walls to buckle or collapse.
- Do not allow children or pets to come into contact with the water, which may contain sewage or chemical runoff.
- Do not discard damaged items until you have photographed them for insurance or your landlord.

## What to do now
1. **Evacuate the immediate area and restrict access.** Close doors to the basement or crawl space and ensure all household members and pets stay away from the entrance.
2. **Identify life-safety triggers from a distance.** If you smell gas (rotten eggs), hear hissing or electrical buzzing, or see structural sagging in the floor above, leave the house immediately.
   - Call **911** and your gas utility once you are at a safe distance.
3. **De-energize the area only if it is safe to do so.**
   - If your main electrical panel is located in a dry, safe area away from the flooding, turn off the main breaker.
   - If you must step into water or onto a damp floor to reach the panel, **do not touch it**. Call your electric utility or an electrician to perform a remote or safe disconnect.
4. **Document the water levels and damage.** Take clear photographs or video from the stairs or a dry doorway before moving anything.
   - Capture the height of the water against walls and appliances.
   - Document any visible entry points, such as foundation cracks or window wells.
5. **Contact your insurance provider or landlord immediately.** Report the "Notice of Loss" to start the claim process.
   - If you have National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) coverage, ask about the "Proof of Loss" requirements, which typically must be filed within 60 days.
   - Request guidance on "mitigation" steps that are approved under your policy.
6. **Follow a staged water removal protocol if you are DIY pumping.** Wait until the water outside has receded below the ground surface.
   - Pump out only about one-third of the water volume per day (or 1 foot of depth) to equalize pressure.
   - Monitor for new wall cracks or bulging while pumping; if they appear, stop immediately.
7. **Wear high-level protection for any necessary contact.** Assume the water is contaminated with bacteria or chemicals.
   - Use rubber boots, waterproof gloves, and eye protection to prevent splashes.
   - Wash your hands and any exposed skin with soap and clean water immediately after leaving the area.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact source of the leak (e.g., hydrostatic pressure versus gutter failure) while the water is still standing.
- You do not need to begin scrubbing or disinfecting surfaces until the area is fully drained and an adjuster or landlord has viewed the damage.
- You do not need to decide on permanent waterproofing solutions or sump pump upgrades today.

## Important reassurance
Finding water in a lower level after a storm is a common residential event. Most structures can be successfully dried and restored if the process is handled methodically. Focus on maintaining electrical safety and capturing documentation first; the cleanup and repair phases will follow once the situation is stabilized.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization only. Specialized drying equipment, mold remediation, and electrical inspections are required before the space can be safely occupied or used.

## Important note
This information is for general safety and does not replace professional electrical, structural, or environmental health advice. If water reached any gas-fired appliances or electrical systems, they must be inspected by a licensed technician before being returned to service.
