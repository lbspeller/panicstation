# What to do if… you are on a work trip and a colleague pressures you to meet alone in a hotel room (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a public, staffed area like the hotel lobby immediately and notify your company’s travel security or management to arrange a safe separation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to their room or allow them into yours to explain your refusal.
- Do not engage in long negotiations or justifications for why you are saying no.
- Do not let yourself remain isolated in a hallway, elevator, or empty meeting room.
- Do not delete any recent messages, call logs, or digital evidence of the pressure.
- Do not assume you must comply to protect your professional reputation or project.
- Do not feel required to make a legal decision or file a formal report in the next hour.

## What to do now
1. **Change your immediate environment to a public space.** Move to the hotel lobby, a staffed restaurant, or the front desk area where other people are present.
   - If you are already in your room and they are outside, keep the door deadbolted and latched.
   - Use the room phone to call the front desk rather than using your personal cell if you feel monitored.
2. **Deliver a final, clear refusal and stop responding.** Use short, unambiguous language that does not leave room for "negotiation" or "smoothing things over."
   - "No. I am not meeting in a room."
   - "I am in the lobby and will not be coming up."
   - "Do not contact me again tonight."
3. **Engage hotel staff to create a physical buffer.** Go to the front desk and state that you are being harassed or pressured by another guest and need assistance staying separate.
   - Request a room change to a different floor without informing the colleague.
   - Ask the hotel to block external and room-to-room calls to your new extension.
   - Request a security escort if you need to move between the lobby and your room.
4. **Contact your workplace through official emergency channels.** Reach out to your manager, HR, or your company’s 24/7 travel security hotline if one exists.
   - State clearly: **"A colleague is pressuring me into an isolated situation in a hotel room, and I feel unsafe."**
   - Ask for an immediate change in travel arrangements, such as a different hotel or an early flight home.
5. **Establish a "public only" rule for the remainder of the trip.** If you must continue the work trip, do not attend any meetings that are not in public view or group settings.
   - Use the "buddy system" with other trusted colleagues for all transitions between locations.
   - Decline rides in private cars or Ubers with the individual.
6. **Capture a brief record of the interaction while details are fresh.** Note the time, location, specific words used, and any witnesses who saw the colleague approaching you.
   - Take screenshots of texts or call logs immediately.
   - Email these details to yourself or a trusted friend to create a timestamped record.
7. **Contact confidential support if you are unsure of your next steps.** You can speak with a specialist who is not affiliated with your company to discuss your safety and options.
   - Call the **RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline** at **800-656-4673**.
   - Text **"HOPE" to 64673** for discreet text-based support.
8. **Call 911 if you believe you are in immediate physical danger.** Use emergency services if the person is attempting to force entry into your room or following you aggressively through the hotel.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to file a formal EEOC complaint or lawsuit tonight.
- You do not need to figure out how this will affect your long-term career path right now.
- You do not need to confront the colleague or explain the consequences of their behavior to them.
- You do not need to have a "perfect" record of every past interaction to get help today.

## Important reassurance
Feeling startled, confused, or even guilty for "causing a scene" is a common reaction to workplace harassment. These feelings do not mean you have done anything wrong. Your primary responsibility in this moment is your own physical and psychological safety, not the comfort of your colleague or the convenience of the work trip.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions for an active crisis. It does not cover long-term legal strategies, HR investigation procedures, or medical diagnostic steps.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Workplace protections under Title VII apply to work-related travel, but specific company policies and state laws may vary. In an immediate emergency, always contact local law enforcement or hotel security.
