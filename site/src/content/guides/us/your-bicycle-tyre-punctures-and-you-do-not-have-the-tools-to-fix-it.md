# What to do if… your bicycle tyre punctures and you do not have the tools to fix it (USA)

## Short answer
Get out of traffic first. Treat this as a mobility problem, not a repair problem: walk the bike to a safer public place, then arrange a ride, a bike shop, or local transit.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep riding on a fully flat tire; it can damage the wheel and make the bike harder to control.
- Don’t stop on a narrow shoulder, bridge, curve, ramp, or anywhere drivers cannot see you early.
- Don’t try to repair the bike in the roadway or in a traffic lane.
- Don’t accept a ride or help that makes you feel uneasy; move toward a public, well-lit place and keep your phone accessible.
- Don’t leave the bike unsecured unless there is no safer option.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause point immediately.** Get off the bike and walk it to the nearest safer place you can reach: a sidewalk where present, a parking lot, a driveway or entrance, a gas station, a transit stop, or a well-lit business.
2. **Make yourself visible.** Turn on any bike lights you have. If you are near moving traffic, stand away from the lane and avoid being trapped between the bike and the road.
3. **Do only a quick visual check.** Look for an obvious flat, torn tire, broken valve, or object in the tire. Do not handle glass, wire, or sharp debris unless you can do it safely with a covered hand and away from traffic. Without a pump, tube, patch kit, or tire levers, do not spend energy trying to complete a repair.
4. **Choose the lowest-risk way to move yourself and the bike.**
   - **Call a pickup:** ask a friend or family member, or use a taxi or ride-hail vehicle that can take a bicycle. Tell the driver you have a bike before they arrive.
   - **Walk to a bike shop:** search for the nearest open shop and call first to ask whether they can help with a flat tire.
   - **Use local transit:** many transit agencies allow bicycles in some form, such as bus racks or rail rules, but policies vary. Check the local transit agency’s bike policy before committing to a stop or station.
5. **If you are near a freeway or high-speed road, keep the plan simple.** Move only if there is a safe legal path to an exit area, business frontage, rest area, or other safer place. If you are injured, stranded where traffic is an immediate danger, or cannot reach safety, call **911**.
6. **For official road or travel information, try 511 where available.** 511 traveler information is handled by state and local services, so it may vary by location. If 511 does not connect or is not useful where you are, search your state DOT traveler information page.
7. **Protect essentials while you wait.** Keep your phone, wallet, keys, lights, and bags with you. Wait in a visible public place when possible.
8. **If leaving the bike is the last resort, reduce the loss risk.** Lock it to a solid object where parking is allowed, remove easy-to-steal accessories, take a photo of the exact location, and note a nearby address or landmark.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to patch the tube or replace it right now.
- You do not need to buy tools or parts while you are stressed, cold, wet, late, or standing near traffic.
- You do not need the perfect route home; you only need the next safer stop and a way to move from there.

## Important reassurance
A flat tire without tools is frustrating, but it is common and manageable. The right first move is to stop riding, get away from traffic, and use a simple plan to get yourself and the bike to help or home.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repair, replacement parts, transport rules, theft reporting, or a damaged wheel may need help from a bike shop, transit agency, insurer, or local authority.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, mechanical, transport, safety, or other professional advice. Traffic conditions, walking space, roadway access, bike parking rules, and transit bike policies vary by city, state, and agency.
