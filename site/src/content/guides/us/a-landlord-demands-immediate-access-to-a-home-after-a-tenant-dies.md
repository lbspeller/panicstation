# What to do if… a landlord demands immediate access to a home after a tenant dies (USA)

## Short answer
If there is no apparent emergency requiring immediate assistance, do not make a rushed decision at the door. Ask the landlord to put the purpose and timing of the requested entry in writing, then promptly check the lease, local rules, and who has authority to act for the deceased tenant’s estate.

## Do not do these things
- Do not argue at the door or agree to entry only because you feel pressured.
- Do not claim to represent the estate only because you are a relative.
- Do not hand over keys, sign a surrender, or authorize disposal of belongings until authority and local procedures have been checked.
- Do not sign a lease-termination agreement or other document on the spot while you are upset or unsure.
- Do not assume that a threat to change the locks or remove belongings makes that action lawful.
- Do not put yourself in danger to physically block entry. If there is an apparent emergency or confrontation, move to a safer place and call 911 if immediate assistance is needed.

## What to do now
1. **Move the conversation to a safer pause.**
   If the landlord is at the door and there is no apparent emergency, say that you need the request in writing before making a decision. If anyone is threatening you or immediate police, fire-department, or ambulance assistance is needed, call 911.

2. **Ask for a written request.**
   Ask the landlord to state:
   - why entry is requested,
   - whether they believe there is an emergency,
   - who would enter,
   - what they intend to do,
   - the proposed date and time,
   - and any lease term or local rule they believe applies.

3. **Do not assume you can consent or refuse on behalf of the estate.**
   A family relationship alone may not establish authority. A court-appointed personal representative may have documents called letters testamentary or letters of administration, but names, alternatives, and required proof vary by state. A co-tenant or another person may also have separate rights. Ask local legal aid what applies before making commitments.

4. **Check the lease and local tenant-rights route promptly.**
   Look for any clause about landlord entry, emergencies, notice, the tenant’s death, abandonment, or belongings left in the home. Use the official tenant-rights resource below to find the relevant state agency or local route.

5. **Make a simple record before anything changes.**
   Save messages and voicemails. Note the date, time, names, and stated reason for the request. If you are already lawfully inside the home and can do so safely, take quick photos of entry points and the general condition of the home and belongings.

6. **If an entry visit is appropriate, propose a documented arrangement.**
   Put the agreed purpose, date, and time in writing. Where feasible, ask to attend or have an authorized representative present. Do not authorize removal or disposal of belongings unless the relevant authority and local procedure have been checked.

7. **Get local help if a lockout, forced entry, or removal of belongings is threatened.**
   For an immediate emergency requiring police, fire-department, or ambulance assistance, call 911. For a non-emergency dispute, use the official tenant-rights or legal-aid resources below and describe the issue as a possible unauthorized entry, lockout, or removal of belongings after a tenant’s death.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide at the door how the lease ends, what rent may be owed, or how any deposit will be handled.
- You do not need to agree at the door to a full clean-out, disposal of belongings, or open-ended access.
- You do not need to settle every question about probate documents immediately.
- Access arrangements, deadlines, and next steps can be checked after the written request, lease, local rules, and authority documents are reviewed.

## Important reassurance
Grief and pressure at the door can make this feel urgent even when some decisions can pause. Asking for the request in writing and checking authority before making commitments is a reasonable way to protect the home, the belongings, and everyone involved.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Landlord-entry rules, lease terms, probate procedures, and the rights of any co-tenant vary by state and locality, so later decisions may need local legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. If there is an immediate safety emergency, prioritize personal safety and call 911.
