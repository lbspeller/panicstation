# What to do if… your computer will not start and you need to recover files stored on it (USA)

## Short answer
Do not reset, erase, reinstall, or factory-reset anything yet. Your safest first goal is to make a file-first copy where possible, or stop and use reputable data-recovery support if the drive may be failing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not choose “Reset this PC,” “Erase,” “Factory reset,” “Erase all content and settings,” or similar options while the files are still the priority.
- Do not reinstall the operating system unless you are sure the option preserves existing files and you already have a separate copy of the most important data.
- Do not install recovery tools onto the same drive you are trying to rescue.
- Do not keep forcing restarts for hours, especially after a drop, spill, power surge, burning smell, clicking, grinding, or repeated startup failure.
- Do not open the sealed casing of a hard drive or try freezing, tapping, shaking, heating, or other risky tricks.
- Do not hand the device to a repair shop, warranty service, or manufacturer without asking in writing whether they may wipe, erase, replace, or return the storage.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and record what you see.**  
   Note whether there are lights, fans, beeps, a logo, a black screen, an error message, clicking, grinding, liquid damage, or a burning smell. Photograph any error screen. Write down what happened just before the problem, such as a drop, spill, update, power outage, or power surge.

2. **Do only low-risk checks.**  
   Unplug USB devices, docks, external drives, printers, and memory cards. Try a known-good charger or power outlet if one is easily available. If the computer still will not start, stop trying to “fix” it and move to file-first steps.

3. **Check for copies before touching the drive.**  
   From your phone or another computer, check OneDrive, iCloud Drive, Google Drive, Dropbox, email attachments, messaging apps, external drives, work or school cloud storage, and any automatic photo or document sync. Save any found copies somewhere safe.

4. **If it is a Mac and it powers on, try Apple’s file-transfer route first.**  
   If it is an Intel Mac, Target Disk Mode may let another Mac see it like an external drive. If it is an Apple silicon Mac, macOS Recovery may let you use Share Disk from another Mac. Use the correct cable, copy the most important folders first, and stop if the disk appears and disappears, copies extremely slowly, or makes unusual noises.

5. **If it is a Windows PC with a removable internal drive, consider a careful file-first connection.**  
   If the computer is a desktop, or a laptop with an easily removable drive, you may be able to remove the storage drive and connect it to another computer using the correct USB-to-SATA adapter or NVMe enclosure. Do this only if you can identify the drive type and remove it without force. If the drive appears, copy the most important files first: documents, work files, photos, tax files, passwords export files, and project folders.

6. **Do not force open a difficult laptop or Mac.**  
   Many newer laptops have storage that is soldered, glued, encrypted, or difficult to access safely. If the device does not clearly have a removable drive, skip DIY removal and contact a reputable repair or data-recovery provider.

7. **Plan for encryption without panic.**  
   A Windows drive may ask for a BitLocker recovery key. Check your Microsoft account, a printed or saved copy, a USB key file, or your employer or school IT support for a managed device. A Mac may need the login password, Apple Account recovery setup, a FileVault recovery key, or an administrator-managed recovery key. Do not reset the device just because you cannot find the key quickly.

8. **Stop and power off if the drive may be physically failing.**  
   Turn the computer off if you hear clicking or grinding, smell burning, see liquid damage, the drive is not detected, the drive appears and disappears, or the files are irreplaceable. Contact a reputable data-recovery provider and say that you need file recovery, not a wiped repair.

9. **If warranty or repair is involved, protect the files first.**  
   Before sending the device anywhere, ask the seller, manufacturer, or repair provider in writing what happens to customer data and storage. Ask whether they may wipe, erase, replace, or return the drive. If you need the files, recover or copy them before repair whenever possible.

10. **If it is a work or school device, contact IT before removing anything.**  
   Managed devices often have encryption, device policies, asset rules, and recovery keys held by the organisation. IT may also already have backups or approved recovery steps.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to repair or replace the computer.
- You do not need to organise, rename, or clean up files before copying them.
- You do not need to run system repair, reset, reinstall, or recovery tools until the file-first options have been considered.
- You do not need to choose a long-term backup plan right now.

## Important reassurance
A computer that will not start is not the same as files being gone. Many startup failures still leave the storage readable, and pausing before resets or repeated restarts can improve your chances.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on avoiding avoidable data loss and getting to a safer pause. Hardware repair, warranty choices, professional data recovery, and future backup planning may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional technical, legal, financial, or data-recovery advice. If the data is valuable, sensitive, work-related, or irreplaceable, stopping early and using reputable professional support may reduce the risk of making recovery harder.
