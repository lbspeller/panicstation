# What to do if… your device suddenly asks for a disk-encryption recovery key after a restart (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as an active account takeover: immediately use the email provider’s official account recovery page to regain access, sign out of all sessions, and secure your 2-factor authentication settings.

## Do not do these things
- Don't use password-reset links from unexpected emails or texts (go directly to the provider's official website).
- Don't repeatedly guess passwords, as this may trigger a long security lockout.
- Don't reuse your old password or minor variations of it.
- Don't ignore "forwarding" or "filters" settings once you get back in; attackers use these to stay hidden.
- Don't use the compromised account to warn others while the attacker still has access.

## What to do now
1. **Switch to a different, trusted device if you suspect your computer or phone has malware.** If your device is behaving strangely, shows unknown apps, or has new browser extensions, use a separate, clean device to perform the recovery steps.
   - Run a full security scan on the original device before using it for sensitive logins again.
2. **Start the account recovery process through the provider's official "Forgot Password" or "Account Recovery" portal.** Navigate directly to the login page of your email provider (e.g., Google, Outlook, Yahoo) and select the recovery option.
   - If your recovery email or phone number has been changed, look for a "Try another way" or "I don't have these" link to access alternative verification methods.
3. **Sign out of all active sessions and devices once you regain access.** Look for "Security" or "Recent Activity" settings and select "Sign out of all devices" to force the attacker's connection to drop.
4. **Change your password to a long, unique phrase and enable 2-factor authentication (2FA).** Use a password you have never used on any other site and store it in a reputable password manager.
   - Choose an authenticator app or a physical security key over SMS-based codes if the provider allows it.
   - Save your "backup codes" in a physical location or a secure offline file.
5. **Review and clean up your mailbox settings to remove hidden access points.** Attackers often set up rules to forward your mail or hide incoming alerts.
   - Check "Forwarding and POP/IMAP" to ensure your mail isn't being sent to an external address.
   - Check "Rules" or "Filters" for instructions that automatically delete or move emails.
   - Verify "Delegated Access" or "Mailbox Sharing" settings.
6. **Secure other accounts that use this email for logins or password resets.** Prioritize financial accounts, payment apps, and primary social media profiles.
   - Change passwords for these accounts and ensure they each have their own unique 2FA method.
7. **Contact your mobile carrier to verify your account security and prevent SIM-swapping.** Call your provider or log in to their site to ensure no unauthorized changes have been made to your lines.
   - Specifically ask to enable "Port-Out Protection" or set a unique "Transfer PIN" to prevent your phone number from being moved to another device.
8. **Inform your primary contacts through a different communication channel.** Use text, phone calls, or a separate secure email to tell family, employers, or clients that your account was compromised.
   - Advise them to ignore any suspicious requests for money, gift cards, or sensitive data sent from your address.
9. **Report the incident to the appropriate federal authorities if fraud or financial loss occurred.** This helps law enforcement track trends and may assist in recovering stolen funds.
   - Visit **IdentityTheft.gov** and click "Get Started" to create a recovery plan.
   - File a report at **IC3.gov** (the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center) for cyber-enabled fraud, noting that while they may not respond individually, the data is used for investigations.
10. **Escalate to your IT or Security department if the compromised account is for work or school.** Organizations have specific protocols for "Business Email Compromise" (BEC) and may need to reset shared credentials or audit internal systems.

## What can wait
- You don't need to determine exactly how the password was stolen right now.
- You don't need to delete your entire email history or close the account immediately unless advised by security professionals.
- You don't need to file a local police report unless you have experienced a direct financial loss or identity theft that requires a formal report for your bank.

## Important reassurance
Account takeovers are common and often result from automated credential-stuffing attacks rather than a personal targeted hack. Regaining access, rotating your passwords, and securing your recovery information are the most effective ways to stop the attack and prevent it from recurring.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for personal or business email account takeovers. It does not cover deep-system forensic analysis or long-term identity monitoring.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal, financial, or technical advice. Account recovery processes are controlled by the service providers; if you cannot satisfy their automated verification requirements, you may need to contact their official support channels directly. Always navigate to official domains by typing them into your browser rather than clicking links in alerts.
