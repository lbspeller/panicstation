# What to do if… an electronic monitoring device malfunctions and you are told it could be treated as a violation (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately notify your supervising officer (Probation or Pretrial Services) and the monitoring provider to log the technical failure, then remain at your approved location and document your efforts to comply.

## Do not do these things
- Don't touch, pull, cut, or attempt to "re-seat" the strap or the device housing.
- Don't use third-party USB cables, non-issued chargers, or "fast-chargers" not provided by the agency.
- Don't leave your approved residence or "zone" to find a better signal or to visit the monitoring office without prior express authorization.
- Don't ignore a low battery or "no signal" light assuming the agency already knows it is a glitch.
- Don't reset your home Wi-Fi router, change phone permissions, or delete monitoring apps unless explicitly instructed by support staff.
- Don't attempt to clean the area under the strap with chemicals or tools that might trigger a tamper sensor.

## What to do now
1. **Remain at your approved location immediately to prove you are not absconding.** Unless you are in a "free movement" window, stay within range of your base unit or at your residence so that your presence can be verified if an officer arrives.
2. **Call your assigned supervising officer or the after-hours duty line listed on your paperwork.** **This is your primary legal protection.** Tell them: "I am at my approved location and my device is showing a [malfunction type] error; I am reporting this immediately to remain compliant."
3. **Contact the monitoring company’s technical support line next.** Ask for a specific "Ticket Number" or "Reference Number" for the technical issue and write down the name of the representative.
4. **Follow only the specific troubleshooting steps provided by the official support agent.** If they ask you to plug the device into a different outlet or restart a base unit, do so, but record the exact time you performed these actions.
5. **Create a written log of the event including every call made and the device’s behavior.** Note the exact time the malfunction started, what lights or sounds the device produced, and the battery percentage if visible.
6. **Take clear photographs of the device and any base equipment while they are in the error state.** Ensure the photo shows the strap is intact and the device is properly attached to your body without you touching or pulling on the equipment.
7. **Screenshot your outgoing call log and any app error messages on your phone.** Keep these as evidence that you attempted to report the issue the moment it was discovered.
8. **Request a clear instruction on whether you should remain stationary or report to the office.** If the officer tells you to "stay put," ask them to confirm they have logged that instruction in the monitoring system.
9. **Prepare to show your charger and equipment to an officer if they conduct a "field check."** Keep all equipment visible and do not hide the device under heavy clothing or blankets while waiting for a resolution.

## What can wait
- You do not need to contact your attorney before reporting the malfunction; reporting to your officer is the priority to stop a violation clock.
- You do not need to explain why the malfunction happened (e.g., "I think the battery is old"); stick to the observable facts of the error.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of the device or potential fees right now; focus entirely on maintaining your "compliant" status.

## Important reassurance
Technical errors with GPS and RF equipment are common and well-documented in the US justice system. If you report the issue promptly and remain at your approved location, you are demonstrating "good faith compliance," which is the strongest defense against a technical violation charge.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for technical malfunctions only. It does not provide legal strategies for "Order to Show Cause" hearings or revocation proceedings.

## Important note
Procedures for electronic monitoring (EM) vary significantly between Federal, State, and County jurisdictions. Your specific "Conditions of Release" or "Probation Orders" are the final authority; if they contradict these steps, follow your court-ordered instructions exactly.
