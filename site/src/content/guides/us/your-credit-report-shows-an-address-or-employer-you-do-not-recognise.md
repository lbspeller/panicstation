# What to do if… your credit report shows an address or employer you do not recognise (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the error by pulling your full reports from all three bureaus via the official federal channel, then place a credit freeze or fraud alert and submit a formal dispute for the inaccurate personal information.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the entry even if your credit score has not changed.
- Do not click on sponsored search engine results or "ad" links when looking for credit bureaus.
- Do not call phone numbers or use contact details listed directly within the suspicious report entry.
- Do not provide your Social Security number to any "credit repair" site that is not the official bureau portal.
- Do not apply for new credit to "test" if your file is working while the investigation is active.

## What to do now
1. **Save a permanent record of the incorrect information immediately.**  
   **Download or print the specific page of the credit report showing the unrecognized address or employer.**
   - Note the date the report was accessed.
   - Identify which specific bureau (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion) is reporting the data.

2. **Access your full reports from all three nationwide bureaus for free.**  
   **Go to AnnualCreditReport.com or call 1-877-322-8228 to request your files from Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.**
   - In 2026, you are entitled to free weekly online reports from all three bureaus.
   - Compare the "Personal Information" section across all three reports to see if the error is isolated or widespread.

3. **Screen your file for secondary signs of identity theft.**  
   **Review every page of the reports for accounts, hard inquiries, or public records you do not recognize.**
   - Look for variations of your name or different Social Security numbers associated with your profile.
   - Check for "soft inquiries" from companies you have no relationship with.

4. **Activate a security block on your credit files.**  
   **Choose between a one-year fraud alert or a permanent credit freeze to prevent unauthorized new accounts.**
   - **Fraud Alert:** Contact one bureau (e.g., Experian) to place an alert; they must legally notify the other two. This lasts one year and is renewable.
   - **Credit Freeze:** You must contact Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion individually to freeze your files. This is free under federal law and stays in place until you lift it.

5. **Submit a formal dispute to each bureau reporting the wrong information.**  
   **Use the bureau's official online dispute portal or send a letter via certified mail to contest the inaccurate address or employer.**
   - Provide a clear statement that you have never lived at the address or worked for the employer listed.
   - Under 2026 FCRA rules, bureaus must conduct a 10-day preliminary investigation for high-risk errors and generally resolve disputes within 30 days.

6. **Notify the "furnisher" of the incorrect data if they are identified.**  
   **Send a written dispute to the company that provided the wrong information to the bureau, such as a former landlord or a background screening firm.**
   - Use a trackable mailing method.
   - Request that they stop reporting the inaccurate data to all credit agencies.

7. **File an official report if you find evidence of fraud.**  
   **Visit IdentityTheft.gov to create an FTC Identity Theft Report if you find unauthorized accounts or applications.**
   - This document provides legal protections that force bureaus to block fraudulent information within four business days of receipt.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a local police report unless a specific creditor requires it for a fraud claim.
- You do not need to hire a professional credit repair service to handle these disputes.
- You do not need to close your existing, legitimate credit accounts while the personal information is being corrected.

## Important reassurance
Seeing an unfamiliar address or employer is a common occurrence that can result from "mixed files"—where two people with similar names have their data merged—or outdated records from a previous tenant at your current residence. While it is a primary indicator of identity theft, it is often a clerical error that can be corrected through the standard dispute process.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for correcting personal information on a credit report. It does not cover the long-term recovery process for clearing fraudulent debts or legal action against creditors.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. If you see active unauthorized withdrawals from your bank account, contact your financial institution’s fraud department immediately.
