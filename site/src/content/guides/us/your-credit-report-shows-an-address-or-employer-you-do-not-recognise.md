# What to do if… your credit report shows an address or employer you do not recognise (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the unfamiliar address or employer as something to verify today: get your credit reports from all three nationwide credit bureaus, check for accounts or inquiries you do not recognize, and place a fraud alert or credit freeze if anything looks suspicious. A credit freeze is the stronger default block against new credit being opened in your name.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore it just because your credit score looks unchanged.
- Don’t call numbers or click links shown beside suspicious entries; use verified contact routes.
- Don’t pay, acknowledge, or “verify” any account you do not recognize.
- Don’t apply for new credit just to test whether your file is safe.
- Don’t send your Social Security number, ID documents, or proof of address unless you are using a legitimate bureau, creditor, government, or bank route.

## What to do now
1. **Save what you saw.**  
   Download or print the page showing the unfamiliar address or employer. Note the date, the credit bureau, and whether the entry appears under personal information, an account, an inquiry, or a collection.

2. **Get your full reports from all three nationwide credit bureaus through the official site.**  
   Use AnnualCreditReport.com to check Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. Compare the personal-information sections and look for the same unfamiliar address, employer, phone number, name variation, account, or inquiry across reports.

3. **Check whether it is only personal information or possible identity theft.**  
   Look for unfamiliar accounts, hard inquiries you did not authorize, collections you do not recognize, address changes, phone numbers you do not use, or name variations that do not fit you. If any of these appear, treat it as possible identity theft while you verify it.

4. **Put protection on your credit file.**
   - **Fraud alert:** Contact one of the three nationwide credit bureaus. For an initial fraud alert, that bureau must tell the other two. An initial fraud alert lasts one year and can be renewed.
   - **Credit freeze:** Contact Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion separately. A freeze is free, lasts until you lift it, and generally stops new credit from being opened while it is in place.

5. **Dispute the unfamiliar address or employer with each bureau where it appears.**  
   State exactly what is wrong and ask for it to be corrected or removed. Include copies, not originals, of documents that support your real address or identity. Save confirmation numbers, screenshots, letters, and copies of anything you submit.

6. **Dispute with the company that supplied the data, if you can identify it.**  
   If a creditor, collector, landlord, lender, or other furnisher appears tied to the wrong address or employer, send a written dispute to that company too. If mailing it, certified mail with return receipt gives you proof it was received.

7. **If you suspect identity theft, file an FTC identity theft report.**  
   Use IdentityTheft.gov to make a report and get a recovery plan. Keep the report because bureaus, creditors, or collectors may ask for documentation.

8. **Check the accounts that could be affected right now.**  
   Sign in through your bank and card apps or verified websites. Look for address changes, replacement cards, new payees, new login devices, unfamiliar transactions, or alerts you did not set up. Change the password on your main email account and turn on two-factor authentication if it is not already on.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to hire help or pay for credit repair.
- You do not need to close every financial account unless you see active misuse on that account.
- You do not need to decide today whether to contact local police unless a bank, creditor, or recovery process specifically requires a police report.
- You do not need to prove the whole cause immediately; first block new credit, save records, and dispute what is inaccurate.

## Important reassurance
An unfamiliar address or employer can come from outdated application data, a reporting mistake, or a mixed credit file. It is not automatic proof of identity theft, but checking all three reports and adding protection is a calm, practical response.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and reduce the chance of further harm. If you find fraudulent accounts, debt collection, denied credit, or repeated reporting problems, later steps may need help from the specific company, IdentityTheft.gov, the CFPB, or a qualified consumer-law professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. If money is being taken right now, contact your bank or card issuer immediately using verified contact details.
