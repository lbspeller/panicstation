# What to do if… you faint briefly and wake up feeling confused (USA)

## Short answer
Lie down and do not stand up quickly. Ask someone to stay with you and call 911 if you do not wake within about 1 minute, do not become alert quickly, have worsening confusion, chest pain, trouble breathing, an irregular or pounding heartbeat, seizure-like activity, stroke-like symptoms, heavy bleeding, or a serious head injury.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stand up quickly to test whether you are fine.
- Do not drive yourself anywhere afterward.
- Do not take extra medicine, stop prescribed medicine, or mix alcohol or drugs with the situation unless a clinician tells you to.
- Do not eat or drink while you are still confused, very drowsy, vomiting, or not fully alert.
- Do not stay alone if you are still confused, injured, fainted more than once, or are unsure what happened.

## What to do now
1. **Get safe and stay down.** Lie flat if you can. If there is no obvious injury and you can do it safely, raise your legs. Loosen tight clothing around your neck or waist and avoid getting up too fast.

2. **Ask someone to do a quick check.** Have them check whether you are breathing normally, whether you hit your head, whether there is heavy bleeding, and whether you can speak clearly and move both sides of your body normally.

3. **Call 911 now if any emergency warning sign is present.** This includes not regaining consciousness within about 1 minute, not becoming alert quickly, worsening confusion, unusual behavior, trouble staying awake, chest pain or pressure, trouble breathing, a pounding or irregular heartbeat, heavy bleeding, serious injury, seizure-like shaking, loss of bowel or bladder control, trouble speaking, facial droop, one-sided weakness or numbness, new vision trouble, severe unsteadiness, or a sudden severe headache.

4. **Treat head injury as higher risk.** If you hit your head and have worsening confusion, a headache that gets worse, repeated vomiting, slurred speech, weakness, numbness, poor coordination, seizure-like activity, one pupil larger than the other, or trouble staying awake, call 911 or go to an Emergency Department.

5. **Get medical advice today if you are not calling 911.** If this was your first faint, the faint was unexplained, you woke up confused, you fainted during exercise, you fainted while lying down, you fainted more than once, or you have diabetes or possible low blood sugar, contact a primary care clinician, urgent care, or an Emergency Department for same-day guidance.

6. **Avoid another fall while you wait.** Stay seated or lying down, keep your phone near you, and ask someone to stay with you or check in. If you are alone and worried you may need emergency help, consider unlocking your door only if it feels safe.

7. **Write down the basics for the clinician.** Note the time, what you were doing, whether you had warning symptoms, whether you hit your head, how long you seemed out, how confused you were afterward, and any witness details such as shaking, pale skin, trouble waking, or unusual behavior.

8. **If you have diabetes or suspect low blood sugar, follow your plan.** Check your glucose if you can do so safely. If you cannot safely eat or drink, cannot check your glucose, or confusion continues, get urgent medical help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether this was fainting, seizure, panic, low blood sugar, concussion, or something else.
- Driving, exercise, alcohol, swimming, heights, and risky tasks can wait until you are clearly back to baseline and have had medical advice.
- Long-term prevention plans can wait until a clinician has assessed what happened.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaken or having a short memory gap can happen after a sudden faint, and it does not mean you did something wrong. Because confusion can also be a warning sign, the safest next step is to stay down, avoid another fall, and check whether emergency help or same-day medical advice is needed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about tests, medicines, driving, work, sport, or preventing another episode may need help from a clinician.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical diagnosis, treatment, or professional advice. If symptoms are new, worsening, unclear, linked to a head injury, or you are not quickly back to normal, use 911 or Emergency Department care.
