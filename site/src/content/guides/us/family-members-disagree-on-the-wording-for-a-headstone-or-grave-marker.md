# What to do if… family members disagree on the wording for a headstone or grave marker (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the monument company and cemetery in writing to pause any further work. Ask the cemetery to state in writing what approval route and documentation it requires before anyone signs a final proof.

## Do not do these things
- Do not approve wording just to end the disagreement.
- Do not assume that the person paying, the plot owner, the closest relative, or any one family member automatically controls the wording.
- Do not assume that one signature or every relative’s agreement is required; ask the cemetery what it requires.
- Do not let several people send separate versions marked as final.
- Do not rely on being able to change or add wording later; ask before treating a later change as an option.

## What to do now
1. **Send a written pause request.**  
   Write to the monument company and cemetery: “Please pause any further work on this marker order. Please confirm whether any paperwork submission, engraving, fabrication, or installation has started, and what can still be paused. Please do not continue until the cemetery confirms the approval route in writing.”

2. **Ask the cemetery for its approval route.**  
   Ask: “Who may submit and approve this marker and inscription under your rules and records? What signature, form, or documentation do you require? If more than one person is listed in your records, do you require one approval or multiple approvals?”

3. **Ask for the current order status.**  
   Ask the monument company to confirm what has already happened, whether any wording cutoff applies, and whether changing or pausing the order could create a charge.

4. **Keep one working proof.**  
   Put the proposed inscription in one document. Check each name, date, spelling, punctuation mark, symbol, and line break. Ask the cemetery what record it wants used if records conflict. Ask relatives to send any concern as an exact proposed correction.

5. **Use one communication channel.**  
   Ask the monument company and cemetery to accept instructions only through the approval route they confirm. Keep the current proof and written confirmations together.

6. **Check the VA route if a government-furnished marker is involved.**  
   For a VA national cemetery or another Veterans cemetery, ask the cemetery staff how inscription details must be provided. For a government-furnished VA marker in a private cemetery, check the current VA application instructions and ask the private cemetery what it requires before installation. Do not rely on being able to add an inscription later without checking first.

7. **Pause if the records or authority remain unclear.**  
   Ask the cemetery not to accept a final proof until it has explained what documentation it needs. Avoid trying to settle the wider family dispute while the approval route is still unclear.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose final wording today.
- You do not need to decide on a fallback inscription until the approval route is clear.
- You do not need to settle broader family disagreements or probate issues now.
- You do not need to decide about mediation or legal advice unless the records remain disputed or the order is continuing despite the written pause request.

## Important reassurance
Disagreement about a headstone or marker can happen during grief. A written pause is a reasonable way to prevent a rushed decision while the cemetery confirms the process.

## Scope note
These are first steps to pause the order, reduce confusion, and clarify the approval route. Later decisions may need help from the cemetery, the monument provider, a mediator, or an attorney if the records or authority remain disputed.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Cemetery rules, contracts, records, and state laws can vary. Ask the cemetery for its written requirements before approving a final proof.
