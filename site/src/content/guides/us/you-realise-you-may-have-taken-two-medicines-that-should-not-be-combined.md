# What to do if… you realise you may have taken two medicines that should not be combined (USA)

## Short answer
Call **Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222** immediately for triage and guidance. If the person has collapsed, is having a seizure, or is struggling to breathe, call **911**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take more of any medication to try to "neutralize" or counteract the effects of the first ones.
- Do not attempt to induce vomiting or use home remedies like charcoal or "cleanses" unless a specialist tells you to.
- Do not drive yourself anywhere; if you need to go to a hospital, have someone else drive or call an ambulance.
- Do not wait for severe symptoms to appear before calling for help.
- Do not discard the packaging, bottles, or labels, as these contain the exact dosage and ingredient information required.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate emergency signs.** **Call 911 right away if you or the person involved is unconscious, having a seizure, experiencing severe chest pain, or having difficulty breathing.**
2. **Contact Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222.** **Speak to a specialist who can assess the specific medications you took and determine if you need to go to the emergency room.**
   - Do not rely solely on online interaction checkers, as they may not account for your specific health history, age, or weight.
3. **Gather all medication containers and a clock.** **Keep the physical bottles or blister packs in front of you so you can read the generic and brand names, strengths, and exact formulations (such as "extended release") to the specialist.**
   - Identify exactly how much of each was taken and at what time.
   - Note down any symptoms currently present, even minor ones like a headache or slight nausea.
4. **Identify the person’s age and weight.** **Provide these details to Poison Control, as toxicity thresholds vary significantly based on body mass and developmental stage, especially for infants and seniors.**
5. **Pause all further medications until instructed.** **Do not take the next scheduled dose of any medication until the Poison Control specialist or your doctor confirms it is safe to do so.**
6. **Bring all evidence if you are directed to a hospital.** **Take all medication containers, even empty ones, and a written timeline of the events with you to the emergency department or urgent care.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to change your long-term medication routine or stop a prescription permanently.
- You do not need to perform exhaustive research into the pharmacology of the drugs; the specialists have this data ready.
- You do not need to notify your pharmacy or primary doctor in this exact minute, provided you have reached Poison Control first.

## Important reassurance
Accidental medication mix-ups are a common reason for Poison Control calls. These services are staffed by nurses, pharmacists, and doctors who provide confidential, non-judgmental guidance. Reaching out early is a proactive safety step that often prevents a hospital visit.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a suspected medication interaction. It is not a substitute for professional clinical advice or long-term medication management.

## Important note
This information is for general stability purposes only. In the United States, 1-800-222-1222 connects you to a network of certified poison centers. Always prioritize 911 for life-threatening emergencies.
