# What to do if… your windows are fogging up and indoor dampness and mould are appearing (USA)

## Short answer
Bring moisture down now: ventilate when it is safe and useful, dry the wettest surfaces, run exhaust fans, a dehumidifier, or both, and check for an active leak. If you rent, send a written maintenance notice and keep photos.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t paint, caulk, or cover moldy areas while they are still damp.
- Don’t dry-brush, scrape, sand, or aggressively disturb moldy areas.
- Don’t vacuum visible mold with a regular household vacuum.
- Don’t mix cleaners, especially bleach with ammonia or other cleaners.
- Don’t touch water, switches, outlets, light fixtures, or electrical panels if water is nearby.
- Don’t keep drying laundry indoors in the same damp space unless you can ventilate it and run a dehumidifier.

## What to do now
1. **Start with the wettest room.**  
   Go to the room with the heaviest fogging, wet sills, damp walls, or musty smell. Close the door to slow moisture spreading. Open a window a little only if it is safe and the outdoor air is not very humid. Run the bathroom fan, kitchen exhaust, a dehumidifier, or both.

2. **Remove liquid water you can see.**  
   Wipe condensation from windows, frames, sills, wet trim, and nearby walls. Move wet towels, damp clothes, and soaked cloths out of the room. Dry the surface again if water quickly returns.

3. **Stop the likely moisture source for the next few hours.**  
   After showers, leave the bathroom fan running and keep the door partly open only after the steam has been vented. While cooking, use the range hood and cover pots. Pause indoor clothes drying if you can; if you cannot, keep it in one ventilated room with the door shut and a dehumidifier running.

4. **Do a fast leak check.**  
   Look under sinks, behind toilets, around tubs and showers, near the dishwasher and washing machine, around HVAC condensate lines, and below any ceiling stain. If you find active leaking and it is safe, place a container under the drip, put towels around spreading water, and shut off the nearest water valve or the main water shutoff.

5. **Treat electrical risk as a stop point.**  
   If water is near outlets, light fixtures, appliances, the electrical panel, or standing water on the floor, keep people and pets away from that area. Do not touch wet switches or plugged-in items. If there is sparking, smoke, fire, shock risk, or immediate danger, call 911.

6. **Clean only small mold on hard surfaces if you can do it without stirring it up.**  
   Ventilate the area. Wear gloves; consider eye protection and an N95 respirator. Scrub hard surfaces with detergent or soap and water, remove what you can see, then dry completely. Do not try to clean moldy carpet, ceiling tile, insulation, or other porous material as a quick wipe-down.

7. **Use bleach only with caution.**  
   Bleach is not needed for every small mold cleanup. If you choose to use it, follow the product label, ventilate, keep people away from fumes, and never mix it with ammonia, vinegar, or any other cleaner.

8. **Stop and get experienced help if the problem is bigger than a small patch.**  
   If the moldy area is more than about 10 square feet, water damage is widespread, the HVAC system may be contaminated, sewage or contaminated water is involved, or you suspect mold inside walls or under flooring, pause cleanup and seek help from someone experienced with mold or water-damage remediation.

9. **If you rent, document and report it in writing.**  
   Take photos or video of fogged windows, wet patches, mold spots, musty areas, and suspected leak points. Send a clear maintenance request by the method your lease or property manager uses, such as email, tenant portal, or text, and keep copies.

10. **Take breathing or allergic symptoms seriously.**  
   Move the person away from the damp or moldy area. If someone has trouble breathing, worsening wheeze, chest tightness, severe allergic symptoms, or symptoms that feel sudden or dangerous, seek medical help promptly or call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether the home needs full remediation.
- You do not need to buy specialty chemicals before drying, ventilation, and leak checks.
- You do not need to clean every room at once; focus on wet surfaces, the strongest moisture source, and visible early growth.
- You do not need to argue about cause right now; photos, written notice, and stopping further wetting are enough for the first step.

## Important reassurance
Fogging windows, damp air, and early mold often mean moisture is entering or staying indoors faster than it can dry. Acting quickly on drying, ventilation, and leak checks can keep a small problem from becoming harder to fix.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilize the situation and reduce further damage. If dampness keeps returning, later decisions may need a landlord, building maintenance, HVAC technician, plumber, mold remediation professional, doctor, or local housing specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. For electrical danger, fire, major water intrusion, sewage contamination, structural danger, or severe breathing symptoms, use urgent professional help or emergency services.
