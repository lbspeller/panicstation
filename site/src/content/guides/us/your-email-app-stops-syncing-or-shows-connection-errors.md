# What to do if… your email app stops syncing or shows connection errors (USA)

## Short answer
Check whether webmail works and whether your provider has an outage before changing settings. Then refresh the connection and sign-in safely, one reversible step at a time.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep entering your password repeatedly if the app keeps failing; it may lock the account or trigger extra security checks.
- Don’t uninstall the mail app, wipe the device, or factory-reset anything as a first step; it can create new problems and usually is not the first safe step.
- Don’t click unexpected “account verification” links in emails, texts, pop-ups, or ads; phishing often imitates email connection fixes.
- Don’t change several server, password, VPN, and security settings at once; make one change, test, then move on.
- Don’t assume the account has been compromised just because syncing fails; outages, expired sign-ins, network filtering, and background sync settings can cause this too.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether the provider is down.**
   - For iCloud Mail, check Apple System Status.
   - For Gmail or Google Workspace, check the Google Workspace Status Dashboard.
   - For Microsoft 365, check Microsoft’s public service status page.
   - For Outlook.com, check Microsoft Support or try Outlook on the web.
   - If it is a work or school account, your organization may also need to check its admin service health page.
   - If there is an outage, stop changing settings and use webmail if it still works.

2. **Find out whether the problem is the app, the device, or the account.**
   - Try the same mailbox in a browser using webmail.
   - Try the same mailbox on another device if one is available.
   - If webmail works, focus on the app or device: network, VPN, expired sign-in, background sync, Fetch, or Push.
   - If webmail also fails, focus on account access: password, account lock, provider outage, or workplace security rules.

3. **Do a low-risk connection reset.**
   - Turn Airplane Mode on, wait a few seconds, then turn it off.
   - Switch between Wi-Fi and cellular data if both are available.
   - Temporarily turn off a VPN, private DNS, or security filtering app only if you can do that safely.
   - Restart the phone, tablet, or computer, then reopen the mail app.

4. **Check the device settings that commonly stop mail from updating.**
   - Make sure automatic date and time are on.
   - On iPhone or iPad, check that the Mail account is enabled and review Fetch or Push settings.
   - On Android, check that Gmail or the mail app is allowed to sync, and that battery saver or data saver is not blocking background activity.
   - In Gmail on Android, try a manual refresh by pulling down in the inbox.

5. **Refresh sign-in safely if the app keeps asking for your password.**
   - Sign in only through the app’s normal account screen or the provider’s official website or app.
   - If you use multi-factor authentication, complete the prompt directly through the provider’s normal sign-in flow.
   - If this is a work or school account, a new security rule may require device approval, a managed app, or help from your IT desk.
   - If you clicked a suspicious link or typed your password into a page you now distrust, stop troubleshooting and change the password through the provider’s official website or app.

6. **Use a built-in refresh, re-sync, or reset option if the app has one.**
   - Look for options such as refresh, sync now, reset account, re-sync, or clear sync state.
   - Use the built-in option once, then test before trying another fix.
   - Avoid clearing app data unless you understand what local drafts, downloaded messages, or settings may be removed from that device.

7. **If it still fails, remove and re-add only the affected account.**
   - Before removing it, check webmail to confirm your messages are still available online.
   - Note any manual IMAP, SMTP, or Exchange server settings if you entered them yourself.
   - Remove only the affected account from the mail app or device.
   - Restart the device, then add the account back using the official sign-in flow.

8. **Pause if a managed work or school account asks for new control over your device.**
   - Stop and ask your IT or help desk before approving a device management profile, device-admin permission, security app, or unexpected certificate.
   - Do not approve a management prompt that arrived from an unexpected link, email, or pop-up.
   - Use your organization’s known help route, not a link in the error message.

9. **If phishing or account takeover is possible, switch to containment.**
   - Change the email password through the provider’s official website or app.
   - Review recent sign-ins or security activity if the provider offers that option.
   - Use the provider’s built-in report phishing or report spam feature if available.
   - In the U.S., report scams to the FTC and internet crime complaints to the FBI’s IC3.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to switch email apps, change email providers, or move all your mail.
- You do not need to reorganize folders, delete messages, or clean up your inbox to fix a connection error.
- You do not need to troubleshoot manual server settings unless you use IMAP, SMTP, or Exchange settings you entered yourself and the safer steps have failed.
- You do not need to respond to non-urgent messages until you have a stable way to read and send mail.

## Important reassurance
Email sync failures can be caused by outages, expired sign-in tokens, VPN or network filtering, background sync restrictions, or provider security checks. Slow, reversible steps reduce the chance of locking yourself out or being pushed into a fake fix.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, such as account recovery, workplace device management, suspected compromise, fraud reporting, or replacing an email app, may need help from the provider, your organization’s IT team, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional IT, legal, cybersecurity, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Use official provider and reporting channels, especially if you suspect fraud, identity theft, or account takeover.
