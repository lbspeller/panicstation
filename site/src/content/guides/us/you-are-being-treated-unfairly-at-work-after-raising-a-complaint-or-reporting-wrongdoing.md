# What to do if… you are being treated unfairly at work after raising a complaint or reporting wrongdoing (USA)

## Short answer
Pause, make a dated record, and preserve evidence without taking restricted files. Some retaliation deadlines are short, so identify the right complaint channel early.

## Do not do these things
- Do not quit on impulse or send an emotional resignation message; pause first and protect your options.
- Do not sign severance, waivers, releases, final warnings, or performance agreements on the spot; ask for time to review.
- Do not delete emails, texts, messages, calendar invites, or account records.
- Do not take confidential customer data, trade secrets, medical records, personnel files, or restricted files.
- Do not record calls or meetings unless you have checked your state’s consent law and workplace policy.
- Do not vent publicly or on social media; keep communications factual and limited.
- Do not assume an internal grievance, union process, or informal discussion pauses an agency filing deadline.

## What to do now
1. **Make a clean timeline today.** Write down what you reported, when, to whom, and what changed afterward. Include discipline, demotion, reduced pay or hours, schedule changes, threats, exclusion, bad evaluations, sudden performance plans, or termination warnings.
2. **Preserve evidence safely.** Save copies of your own emails, letters, meeting notices, pay records, schedules, and messages that show dates and times, as long as you can do this without breaking law or policy. If something is only on a work platform you cannot copy, write down where it is, including the date, sender, subject line, channel, and what it shows.
3. **Create a paper trail from now on.** Ask for allegations, performance concerns, policy references, and process steps in writing. After meetings, send a short factual recap such as: “To confirm, we discussed X, you said Y, and the next step is Z by this date.”
4. **Use the employer’s channel if it is safe and does not delay an urgent deadline.** Use HR, ethics, compliance, your union representative, or the channel your employer identifies. Keep it short: what you reported, what changed afterward, why you believe it is connected, and what immediate pause or review you are asking for.
5. **Match the outside channel to the kind of retaliation.**
   - **Retaliation connected to discrimination, harassment, accommodation, or other EEOC-enforced rights:** you generally must file an EEOC charge within 180 days. This is extended to 300 days in many places where a state or local agency enforces a similar law, but some claims, including age or equal pay claims, can have different rules.
   - **Federal employees or federal job applicants:** you generally must contact an agency EEO counselor within 45 days of the discriminatory or retaliatory event.
   - **Workplace safety, injury reporting, or other laws enforced by OSHA whistleblower programs:** deadlines vary by statute and may be as short as 30 days. Many OSHA whistleblower deadlines run from when the retaliatory action occurs and is communicated.
   - **Union activity or protected concerted activity:** NLRB unfair labor practice charges generally need prompt action and are usually subject to a six-month timing limit.
6. **If discipline, suspension, or termination is happening now, get the rules and dates in writing.** Ask for the policy being used, the specific allegations, the evidence being relied on, and the deadline to respond or appeal. If you need time to gather records or seek advice, ask in writing for a short extension.
7. **Keep doing the job as safely and normally as you can.** Follow lawful instructions, meet deadlines you can meet, and avoid giving the employer a new reason to discipline you. If an instruction seems unsafe, illegal, or impossible, ask for it in writing and seek advice quickly.
8. **Get quick, reputable advice.** Bring your timeline, the exact adverse actions, and the dates to an employment attorney, legal aid organization, worker center, union representative, or the relevant agency. If stress is affecting your safety or basic functioning, contact a clinician, trusted person, or EAP for immediate support.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to sue, settle, resign, or stay long term.
- You do not need a perfect case file before asking for advice or using a filing channel.
- You can postpone public complaints, broad job decisions, and confrontation until you have protected evidence and urgent deadlines.
- You do not need to convince the person you suspect is retaliating; keep it procedural and documented for now.

## Important reassurance
Retaliation can feel disorienting because it may be presented as performance management, restructuring, or business need. A simple timeline, preserved records, and deadline check can make the situation clearer and reduce the risk of rushed decisions.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about filing, negotiating, responding to discipline, resigning, or bringing a claim may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. The right agency and deadline can depend on what you reported, who your employer is, where you work, and what adverse action happened.
