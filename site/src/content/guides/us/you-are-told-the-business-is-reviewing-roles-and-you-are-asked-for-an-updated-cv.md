# What to do if… you are told the business is reviewing roles and you are asked for an updated CV (USA)

## Short answer
Do not agree, resign, or sign any documents in the first meeting; instead, request the full relocation requirement and any associated financial terms in writing for a formal review.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign or stop showing up to your current location immediately, as this may be classified as a "voluntary quit" and disqualify you from unemployment benefits.
- Do not sign a relocation agreement, "stay-or-pay" contract, or acceptance form without verifying if such clawbacks are legal in your state as of 2026.
- Do not accept any relocation funds or reimbursements until you have a written copy of the repayment terms.
- Do not make irreversible personal commitments, such as selling a home or breaking a lease, until you have a confirmed start date and final work address in writing.
- Do not assume that a disability automatically entitles you to permanent remote work to avoid a commute, as legal standards for "commute accommodations" are strictly limited.

## What to do now
1. **Request the relocation directive and all specific details in writing.** **Ensure the document includes the exact new work address, the required start date, whether the change is permanent or temporary, and the consequences of declining the move.**
2. **Retrieve and secure copies of your existing employment documents.** **Locate your original offer letter, current employment contract, employee handbook, and any existing remote work or telework agreements.**
   - Do not download or remove proprietary company data or client lists.
   - Focus exclusively on personnel policies and your own signed agreements.
3. **Check your state's 2026 laws regarding relocation repayment clauses.** **If you work in California (AB 692) or New York (Trapped at Work Act), many relocation repayment or "stay-or-pay" provisions executed after January 1, 2026, are now restricted or void.**
4. **Determine if the move triggers federal or state WARN Act notice requirements.** **Ask HR in writing if a Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification (WARN) notice has been filed, particularly in states like California where relocation of operations is a specific legal trigger.**
5. **Send a neutral "holding" response to establish a pause.** **Use a clear statement such as: "I have received the relocation notice and am currently reviewing the details and my ability to comply; I will provide a formal response by [Date]."**
6. **If you have a disability that makes the new location or commute impossible, initiate the interactive process immediately.** **Submit a written request for a reasonable accommodation, focusing on how the new location creates a barrier to performing your essential job functions rather than just a difficult commute.**
   - Be aware that 2026 EEOC guidance clarifies that employers generally do not have to accommodate the commute itself unless they provide such assistance to non-disabled employees.
7. **Contact your union representative if you are in a union-protected position.** **Your collective bargaining agreement (CBA) likely contains specific rules regarding mandatory transfers, seniority rights, and the employer's duty to bargain over the "effects" of a relocation.**
8. **Inquire about alternative sites or phased transitions in writing.** **Ask if there are closer worksite options, hybrid schedules, or a "transition period" that would allow you to remain at your current site for a set duration.**
9. **Maintain a contemporaneous log of all communications regarding the move.** **Note the dates, times, and names of individuals involved in every meeting, and follow up every verbal discussion with a summary email to confirm your understanding.**
10. **Avoid using "resignation" or "quit" language if you are unable to move.** **If the employer terminates you for declining the move, ensure your records reflect "termination due to worksite relocation" or "lack of work at current site" to protect your eligibility for unemployment benefits.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to move your family or household in the next 24 hours.
- You do not need to negotiate the specifics of a relocation package until you have confirmed the move is mandatory and legal under your contract.
- You do not need to file an unemployment claim yet; this only happens after a separation occurs.
- You do not need to finalize a new commute route until the office location and your start date are confirmed in a final, signed document.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel pressured or panicked when your worksite changes unexpectedly. You have the right to a reasonable window of time to review how this change affects your contract and your legal rights before you commit to a decision.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for employees facing a forced relocation in the USA. It focuses on documentation and harm prevention. It does not cover long-term career planning, real estate advice, or specific legal litigation.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment laws, WARN Act triggers, and the legality of repayment clauses vary significantly by state and are subject to 2026 legislative updates. If you are a union member, consult your union representative first. If you face termination or a high-value repayment demand, consult a qualified employment attorney in your specific state.
