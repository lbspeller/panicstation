# What to do if… you have a fever after starting an immune-suppressing medication (USA)

## Short answer
Call the clinician who prescribed the medication, or the on-call service, now and tell them your temperature, symptoms, medication name, and when you started it. Call 911 for severe trouble breathing, severe chest pain or pressure, fainting or collapse, sudden confusion, difficulty waking, or symptoms that are rapidly worsening.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for the fever to settle before contacting the prescribing team.
- Do not use acetaminophen, ibuprofen, or another fever-reducing medicine as a substitute for contacting a clinician. Ask what is safe for you to take.
- Do not change, stop, skip, or take an extra dose of the immune-suppressing medication unless the prescribing team tells you what to do.
- Do not rely on an online symptom checker as your main decision tool.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel faint, confused, severely weak, or unsafe to drive.

## What to do now
1. **Take your temperature and write down the reading and time.** Note any chills, shaking, cough, sore throat, breathing difficulty, burning with urination, vomiting, diarrhea, rash, or new pain. You can recheck your temperature while arranging care, but do not delay the call.
2. **Call the prescribing team or its on-call service now.** Say: “I started an immune-suppressing medication on [date]. My temperature is [temperature]. My symptoms are [symptoms]. What should I do about urgent assessment and my next dose?”
3. **Follow any treatment-specific plan you were given.** If you are receiving chemotherapy and your temperature is `100.4°F (38°C)` or higher, CDC guidance says to call your doctor immediately because fever during chemotherapy is a medical emergency. For any other immune-suppressing treatment, ask the prescribing team what to do next and whether you need urgent in-person assessment.
4. **Call 911 for emergency symptoms.** Call for severe trouble breathing, severe chest pain or pressure, fainting or collapse, sudden confusion, difficulty waking, or symptoms that are rapidly worsening.
5. **Have your key information ready.** Bring or photograph your medication list. Include the immune-suppressing medication name and dose, the start date, the last dose, other medicines, allergies, temperature readings, and any recent lab results you already have.
6. **Tell intake staff about the immune-suppressing medication.** If you go to a clinic or emergency department, state the medication name, when you started it, your temperature, and your symptoms as soon as you check in.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause of the fever before calling.
- You do not need to decide whether the medication is right for you long-term.
- You can leave non-urgent work, school, and household tasks until you have spoken with the prescribing team or arranged urgent assessment.

## Important reassurance
Calling now is a sensible step, not an overreaction. Fever after starting an immune-suppressing medication deserves prompt attention and may be an early sign of infection.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The right next step depends on the medication, your symptoms, your underlying condition, your recent bloodwork, and the instructions from your specialist team.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice or a diagnosis. A clinician needs to assess your specific situation.
