# What to do if… the only entrance to a station or terminal closes suddenly and you need an alternative route inside (USA)

## Short answer
Step out of the crowd, locate uniformed staff or official digital signage for alternate routing, and use the nearest designated public entrance—do not attempt to force doors or bypass security.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t push into a crowd at the closed doors or attempt to force automated shutters.
- Don’t follow others through staff-only areas, restricted security corridors, or service elevators.
- Don’t use emergency exits or alarmed doors unless there is an immediate threat to life or staff specifically instruct you to.
- Don’t bypass security screening or hop turnstiles to find a "shortcut"—this triggers a high-level security response.
- Don’t touch or investigate unusual items near the closure, such as abandoned bags or leaking fluids.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause of 10–30 seconds.** Move at least 20 feet away from the closed doorway and any gathering crowd to avoid being caught in a surge. Stand where you have a clear line of sight to digital message boards or uniformed personnel.
2. **Identify the correct authority for immediate directions.** 
   - Look for **Transit Station Agents** or **Customer Service** in high-visibility vests.
   - Look for **Security Officers** or **Law Enforcement** (TSA, Amtrak Police, or local PD).
   - Check **Digital Information Displays** (PIDS) for "Alternate Entrance" scrolling text.
3. **Ask a single, precise question for rerouting.** Say: “Where is the nearest open public entrance for **[your airline/train/bus]**, and is the accessible route open?” 
4. **Follow specific 2026 airport terminal protocols.** 
   - If the closure is screening-related, wait for **TSA officer** instructions regarding the new 2026 self-screening lanes or automated ID verification.
   - Be prepared to present a **REAL ID-compliant document** if rerouted through a different security checkpoint to avoid the $45 non-compliance fee.
5. **Locate the primary alternate access point for rail or bus facilities.** 
   - Check the opposite corner of the building or a connected concourse from a nearby commercial property.
   - For Amtrak stations, look for "Station Advisory" signage indicating temporary entrance relocations due to 2026 infrastructure projects.
6. **Request immediate accessibility assistance if needed.** Clearly state to staff if you require a **step-free route**, elevator, or assistance with a mobility device. Ask specifically for the location of the **ADA-compliant entrance** if the main entrance is shuttered.
7. **Monitor sensory cues for safety triggers.** If you smell a strong chemical odor, see smoke, or hear electrical buzzing/arcing, move further away from the building and toward an open-air area immediately.
8. **Check official service alerts on your device once in a stable position.** 
   - Access the **transit agency app** or website (e.g., MTA, CTA, Amtrak, or Airport Authority).
   - Look for "Service Disruptions" or "Station Advisories" to confirm if the entire facility is closed or just one entry point.
9. **Secure your travel documents and identification.** Ensure your boarding pass, ticket, and ID are easily accessible but secure in a pocket or bag to avoid losing them in a crowd.
10. **Signal for emergency help if the situation escalates.** If there is a medical emergency, fire, or violence, call **911** and provide your specific location relative to the closed entrance.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide/do now: You don't need to resolve ticket refunds or missed connection compensation while standing at the closed door.
- You do not need to decide/do now: You don't need to determine if the closure is "fair" or argue with security about why the doors are locked.
- You do not need to decide/do now: You don't need to file a formal ADA complaint or customer service report until you are in a safe, quiet location.

## Important reassurance
Entrance closures are common for managing crowds, maintaining security, or addressing technical issues. Feeling frustrated or disoriented is a normal reaction to a sudden change in plans. Taking a moment to step back and find an official alternate route is the most effective way to stay on schedule.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only for the immediate minutes following a sudden entrance closure. Later decisions regarding travel insurance, legal rights, or long-term rerouting should be handled after reaching a stable environment.

## Important note
This guide provides general information for navigating sudden station or terminal access changes in the USA. Procedures vary significantly between airports, subway systems, and rail hubs. Always prioritize the instructions of on-site law enforcement, TSA, and facility staff over general guidelines.
