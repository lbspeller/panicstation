# What to do if… you suddenly have no running water in your home (USA)

## Short answer
First work out whether this is only your home or a wider outage, then contact the right person: your water utility, landlord, property manager, plumber, or well service. Do not assume tap water is safe after a no-water or low-pressure event if local officials issue, mention, or are checking a drinking-water advisory.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t drink, cook with, brush teeth with, or make ice from tap water if your utility or health department has issued a boil water, do-not-drink, or do-not-use advisory.
- Don’t boil water if officials say “do not drink” or “do not use”; use bottled water or follow the exact local notice.
- Don’t force a stuck main shutoff valve if it will not turn easily.
- Don’t step into standing water near outlets, cords, appliances, or a breaker panel.
- Don’t use water from radiators, boilers, heating systems, toilet tanks, or chemical containers for drinking or cooking.
- Don’t assume the utility already knows; report a no-water or low-pressure outage if the utility asks residents to report problems.
- Don’t wait to tell your landlord, property manager, or emergency maintenance contact if you rent or live in a managed building.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether any water is coming in.**
   - Try the cold tap closest to where water enters the home, often a kitchen, laundry, basement, or outdoor tap.
   - Try one other cold tap.
   - If only one fixture is affected, the problem may be local to that fixture rather than the whole home.

2. **Find out whether neighbors are affected.**
   - Ask a neighbor, building desk, property manager, or building chat whether they also have no water or very low pressure.
   - If several homes are affected, treat it as a likely utility or building-wide interruption.

3. **Check for a simple shutoff only if it is safe and familiar.**
   - If you know where your main water shutoff is, look to see whether it has been turned off.
   - If you are in an apartment or condo, ask building management before touching shared valves.
   - If the valve is stuck, damaged, leaking, or unfamiliar, leave it alone and call maintenance or a plumber.

4. **Look for damage that needs faster help.**
   - If you see leaking, flooding, wet ceilings, or water coming from walls or floors, contact emergency maintenance or a plumber.
   - If water is near electricity, keep away from that area and contact emergency help or your utility if there is immediate danger.
   - If you can safely stop an active leak with a known shutoff valve, do that first.

5. **Contact the right responder.**
   - If neighbors are affected, check your water utility’s website, alert page, phone line, or text-alert system, if available, and report no water or low pressure.
   - If only your unit or rented home is affected, contact your landlord, property manager, or emergency maintenance contact and ask what they want you to do next.
   - If you own the home and neighbors have water, contact a licensed plumber.
   - If you use a private well, contact a licensed well or pump service and avoid DIY electrical work on the pump or pressure system.

6. **Treat drinking water cautiously until local guidance is clear.**
   - Follow instructions from your local water utility and local or state health department.
   - If there is a boil water advisory, use bottled water or bring clear tap water to a rolling boil for 1 minute, or 3 minutes above 6,500 feet, then let it cool.
   - If officials say “do not drink” or “do not use,” boiling may not make the water safe; use bottled water and follow the notice.

7. **Cover basic needs while you wait.**
   - For drinking, cooking, brushing teeth, making ice, and pets, use bottled water or water you know is safe.
   - If there is no running water for handwashing, use hand sanitizer when hands are not visibly dirty.
   - Many toilets can be flushed by carefully pouring non-drinking water into the bowl; stop if the toilet backs up or starts to overflow.
   - Write down when the water stopped, who you contacted, and any utility or landlord messages. Take photos if there is visible damage.

8. **Use stored water calmly.**
   - Prioritize drinking, essential food preparation, medicines, infant feeding, pets, and basic hygiene.
   - As a planning benchmark, CDC recommends storing at least 1 gallon of water per person per day for 3 days, with more for pets, people who are sick, pregnant people, and hot weather.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify every possible plumbing cause right now.
- You do not need to decide on major repairs unless there is active leaking, flooding, or damage.
- You do not need to flush every faucet or appliance until the utility or health department says what to do.
- You do not need to solve long-term water storage during the first few minutes; focus on safe drinking water, toilets, damage prevention, and the correct contact route.

## Important reassurance
Sudden loss of water is often a temporary utility issue, building shutoff, valve issue, pump problem, or repair interruption. The useful first move is not to panic-troubleshoot everything; it is to check whether the outage is wider than your home, avoid unsafe water use, and get the right responder involved.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Longer outages, repeated shutoffs, private well problems, contamination notices, water damage, rent disputes, or repair costs may need help from a plumber, well professional, property manager, local health department, utility, insurer, tenant adviser, or other specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not professional plumbing, medical, legal, engineering, or emergency-management advice. Follow instructions from your local water utility, landlord or property manager where relevant, and local or state health department.
