# What to do if… you are asked for a police report abroad for a stolen document and you do not know how to obtain one (USA)

## Short answer
Locate the nearest police station or "tourist police" and request an incident reference number and a stamped confirmation of the report. If a full report is unavailable, obtain written verification from a hotel, airline, or security office and contact the nearest U.S. Embassy for guidance on replacement.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay unofficial intermediaries or "fixers" to procure a police report.
- Do not sign any document in a foreign language if you do not have a reliable translation of the text.
- Do not delay reporting a stolen U.S. passport to the State Department while waiting for a police report.
- Do not leave your original remaining identification documents with anyone other than official police or consular officers.
- Do not assume that a verbal report or "noting it down" by an officer is sufficient without a reference number or stamp.

## What to do now
1. **Document the details of the theft immediately.** Record exactly what was stolen, the approximate time, the specific location, and any identifying features of the incident.
2. **Locate the nearest police station or specialized "Tourist Police" unit.** 
   - Use a search engine for "[City] Tourist Police" as many major tourist hubs have dedicated offices for foreign visitors.
   - Ask for directions at an official transit information desk or your hotel reception.
3. **Request a crime reference number and a stamped summary of the report.** 
   - Ask for a "denuncia" (in Spanish-speaking countries) or "incident report."
   - Ensure the document includes the date, the station's official stamp, and a reference number.
4. **Use a translation app or a trusted local to bridge language barriers.** 
   - Request assistance from hotel staff or a tour representative to act as a witness or translator.
   - Do not hand over your phone to officers; hold it yourself while using translation software.
5. **Document the attempt if the police refuse to issue a formal report.** 
   - Note the station address, the date/time of your visit, and the badge number or name of the officer who assisted you.
   - Request a "complaint number" even if a full printed report is not available.
6. **Obtain secondary written proof from third-party officials.** 
   - Request a written statement from hotel security, airline staff, or venue management if the theft occurred on their premises.
   - Keep copies of all communication regarding the stolen items.
7. **Report your passport as lost or stolen to the U.S. Department of State.** 
   - **Use the online Form DS-64 process** to invalidate the document immediately.
   - **Be aware** that once reported, the passport is permanently cancelled and cannot be used even if recovered.
8. **Contact the nearest U.S. Embassy or Consulate to schedule an emergency appointment.** 
   - Check the embassy website for "U.S. Citizen Services" and "Lost or Stolen Passports."
   - Inform them if you have urgent travel within the next 24 to 48 hours.
9. **Verify local exit requirements for travelers without entry stamps.** 
   - Ask the consular officer if the host country requires a special exit visa or police report to leave the country if your entry stamp was in the stolen passport.

## What can wait
- Pursuing a "final" or "official" investigation report can wait; the immediate reference number is the priority for travel.
- Filing detailed insurance claims for non-essential items can wait until you are in a safe, stable location.
- Replacing non-travel documents like library cards or local memberships.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed by foreign bureaucracy, especially when dealing with a language barrier. Most U.S. Embassies deal with hundreds of lost or stolen documents monthly and are equipped to help you navigate the process even if the local police are uncooperative. A reference number or a brief stamped slip is often all that is needed to initiate the replacement process.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for obtaining documentation of a theft while abroad. It does not cover specific legal procedures for every country or long-term identity restoration.

## Important note
This guide provides general information for U.S. citizens and is not legal advice. Local police procedures vary significantly by jurisdiction. If you feel unsafe entering a local police station, prioritize your physical safety and contact the U.S. Embassy or Consulate first for alternative instructions.
