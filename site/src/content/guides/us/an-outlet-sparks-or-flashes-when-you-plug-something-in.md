# What to do if… an outlet sparks or flashes when you plug something in (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using the outlet immediately and move away from the area. If you see smoke or fire, evacuate the building and call **911** from a safe distance.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to plug anything else into the outlet to test it.
- Do not touch the outlet, the wall plate, or the plug if there are visible scorch marks.
- Do not try to unplug the device if the outlet is actively sparking, smoking, or buzzing.
- Do not use water or liquid extinguishers on an electrical fire or sparking outlet.
- Do not repeatedly reset a circuit breaker or Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter (GFCI) if it trips.
- Do not remove the outlet cover plate or attempt to inspect the internal wiring.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance from the hazard.** Immediately step back from the outlet and ensure children and pets are kept away from the room.
2. **Assess for immediate fire signs.** Check for visible smoke, a "fishy" or "burning plastic" smell, or glowing orange/red light inside the receptacle.
   - If smoke or fire is present, **evacuate immediately and call 911**.
   - Do not attempt to fight an electrical fire yourself.
3. **Turn off the power at the main breaker panel.** If it is safe to do so, locate your electrical service panel and switch the specific circuit breaker to the "OFF" position.
   - If the breakers are not labeled, switch the **Main Breaker** to the "OFF" position to cut power to the entire property.
4. **Identify and isolate the appliance.** Once the power is confirmed off at the breaker, you may safely unplug the device that caused the spark.
   - Look for damage on the plug prongs, such as pitting, melting, or blackening.
   - Do not use this appliance in any other outlet until it has been inspected.
5. **Check the outlet for physical damage.** Inspect the faceplate from a distance for yellowing, browning, or melting of the plastic.
   - If the outlet feels hot to the touch even with the power off, do not re-enter the room and call the fire department non-emergency line or 911 if heat increases.
6. **Contact a licensed electrician.** Report the incident, noting the color of the spark (e.g., blue, white, or yellow) and whether any safety devices like a breaker or GFCI tripped.
7. **Notify property management if you rent.** Provide a written notice that you have disabled the circuit due to a fire hazard and request an urgent inspection by a qualified professional.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the fault is in the wiring or the appliance right now.
- You do not need to replace the outlet yourself.
- You do not need to worry about the loss of power to other rooms if you had to shut off the main breaker; safety takes priority over convenience.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel startled or anxious when electricity behaves unexpectedly. Sparking is often a sign that a safety threshold has been reached or a connection has loosened. By stopping use and cutting power, you have already significantly reduced the immediate risk of a home fire.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for residential electrical sparking. It does not provide instructions for repair, diagnostic testing, or industrial electrical systems. All electrical faults require inspection by a licensed professional.

## Important note
Electrical incidents carry a high risk of shock or fire. If you cannot access your breaker panel safely, or if the sparking continues after the breaker is turned off, treat the situation as an active fire emergency.
