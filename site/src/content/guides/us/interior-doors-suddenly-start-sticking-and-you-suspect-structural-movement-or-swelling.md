# What to do if… interior doors suddenly start sticking and you suspect structural movement or swelling (USA)

## Short answer
Stop forcing the doors and check for imminent hazards like sagging ceilings, large new cracks, or utility leaks. If you hear loud cracking sounds or see visible structural failure, evacuate and call **911**; otherwise, document the changes and notify your insurance or landlord before making any alterations.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t plane, sand, or trim the door or frame (altering the door can mask the underlying cause and may void insurance coverage or complicate professional inspections).
- Don’t keep slamming or forcing a sticking door (this can cause further damage to the framing or hinges and may strip screws).
- Don’t ignore it if several doors and windows are affected simultaneously or if the change is accompanied by new wall cracks or sloped floors.
- Don’t enter or remain under areas with visible distress, such as sagging drywall, bowed walls, or falling debris.
- Don’t attempt to "jack up" or level floors yourself (structural stabilization requires professional engineering and specialized equipment).

## What to do now
1. **Ensure you have a clear way out of every room.** If a sticking door is the only exit from a room, leave that door open or ensure it can be opened without excessive force.
2. **Check for immediate utility hazards near the movement.** Look and listen for signs of secondary damage:
   - Smell for gas (rotten egg odor) or listen for hissing sounds.
   - Watch for flickering lights, sparking outlets, or a burning smell, which can indicate shifted wiring.
   - If you suspect a gas leak or electrical fire risk, evacuate immediately and call **911**.
3. **Perform a visual scan for signs of active structural failure.** Look for the following red flags:
   - Large diagonal cracks (wider than 1/4 inch) branching away from the corners of door or window frames.
   - Ceilings that appear to be bowing downward or floors that have developed a sudden, noticeable slope.
   - New gaps between the walls and the ceiling or between the walls and the floor.
4. **Identify potential moisture sources if swelling is suspected.** If the movement followed a storm or plumbing event, check for:
   - Fresh water stains on walls or ceilings.
   - Active leaks from pipes, appliances, or the roof.
   - If a leak is found, shut off the water at the main valve if it is safe to do so.
5. **Document the current state of the doors and cracks.** **Use a smartphone to take photos and videos of the rub points, frame gaps, and any new cracks.**
   - Include a ruler or a common object (like a coin) in photos of cracks for scale.
   - Note the date and time you first noticed the sticking.
6. **Notify the appropriate party to initiate a professional assessment.**
   - **If you own the home:** contact your homeowners insurance company to report the damage and ask for their required protocol before starting repairs.
   - **If you rent:** notify your landlord or property manager in writing immediately, using the term "urgent structural or water-related concern."
7. **Maintain a safe distance from affected areas until they are inspected.** **Avoid placing heavy furniture or high-traffic activities in rooms where doors are severely binding or floors feel unstable.**

## What can wait
- You don’t need to determine the exact cause (foundation vs. humidity) right now; that is the role of a structural engineer or inspector.
- You don’t need to hire a contractor for repairs until the underlying movement has been professionally diagnosed.
- You don’t need to worry about cosmetic drywall repairs today; safety and stabilization are the priorities.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel anxious when a home's structure appears to be changing. Sticking doors are often the first sign of movement, but they do not always mean a collapse is imminent. Taking a moment to assess the situation and document the evidence is the most effective way to protect your safety and your property.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and hazard-identification guidance only. It is not a substitute for a professional structural engineering report or a building safety inspection.

## Important note
If you believe your home is in immediate danger of collapse or you hear structural members snapping, evacuate all occupants immediately and contact emergency services. Structural assessments must be performed by licensed professionals.
