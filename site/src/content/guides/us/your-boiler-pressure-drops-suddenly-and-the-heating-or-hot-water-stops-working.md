# What to do if… your boiler pressure drops suddenly and the heating or hot water stops working (USA)

## Short answer
If you suspect a gas leak or carbon monoxide, evacuate immediately and call 911. Otherwise, check the pressure gauge; if it is below 12 psi, you may need to add water via the manual feed valve or automatic filler to restore the system's minimum operating pressure.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay inside to investigate if you smell gas / "rotten eggs" or if a carbon monoxide alarm sounds.
- Do not operate light switches, phones, or flashlights in an area where you suspect a gas leak.
- Do not add water to a hot, dry boiler, especially steam systems, as this can cause the heat exchanger to crack or explode.
- Do not attempt to bypass safety switches or low-water cutoffs.
- Do not increase pressure beyond 25 psi; the pressure relief valve will typically discharge at 30 psi.
- Do not keep refilling the system if the pressure drops again immediately; this indicates a significant leak.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate environmental hazards.**
   - If a **carbon monoxide (CO) alarm** sounds or you feel dizzy / nauseated: **leave the building immediately** and call 911 from outside.
   - If you **smell gas** (rotten eggs): **evacuate all occupants**, do not use any electronics or switches, and call your gas utility’s emergency line or 911 from a safe distance.
2. **Identify your system type.**
   - Locate the **manufacturer’s data plate** or the expansion tank.
   - **Hydronic (Hot Water) Boilers:** Usually have a small, rounded expansion tank (often gray or blue) and a gauge reading in PSI and Fahrenheit.
   - **Steam Boilers:** Usually have a large vertical glass tube (sight glass) showing the water level and a gauge reading in lower PSI (0 to 15).
3. **Turn off the boiler power and allow it to cool.**
   - Locate the **emergency shut-off switch** (usually a red switch plate) or turn the thermostat to "Off."
   - **Wait at least 30–60 minutes** for the unit to cool before adding water to prevent thermal shock to the metal.
4. **Read the pressure gauge.**
   - For most **residential hydronic systems**, the cold pressure should be between **12 psi and 15 psi**.
   - If the needle is **below 10 psi**, the low-water cutoff may have tripped to prevent the burner from firing.
5. **Locate the water feed valve and add water slowly.**
   - Find the **pipe connecting your domestic water supply** to the boiler.
   - Look for a **lever-handled valve** or a **pressure-reducing valve** with a "fast fill" lever on top.
   - **Open the valve slowly** while watching the gauge; stop when the needle reaches **12–15 psi** (or the specific cold-fill pressure marked on your system).
   - Ensure the valve is **completely closed** once the target pressure is reached.
6. **Restart the system and monitor for leaks.**
   - Turn the **power switch back on** and set the thermostat to call for heat.
   - **Watch the pressure gauge** as the water heats up; it is normal for pressure to rise toward 20–25 psi, but it must not reach 30 psi.
   - **Check for water** at the baseboards, radiators, and the "discharge pipe" (the pipe pointing toward the floor from the boiler).
7. **Notify necessary parties if the issue persists.**
   - If you are a **tenant**, call your landlord or property manager immediately if the pressure drops again or if heat is not restored.
   - If you are a **homeowner**, contact a licensed HVAC technician if the boiler "locks out" (shuts down) again or if you see water pooling.

## What can wait
- You do not need to bleed every radiator in the house immediately unless they remain cold after the pressure is restored.
- You do not need to replace the expansion tank or relief valve today unless a professional identifies them as failed.
- You do not need to drain or flush the system unless a technician recommends it for maintenance.

## Important reassurance
Low pressure is one of the most common reasons a boiler stops working and is often a simple safety shutdown intended to protect the equipment. Restoring the water level frequently resolves the "no heat" issue without permanent damage to the system.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for residential hydronic and steam boilers in the USA. It does not replace the manufacturer's manual or professional diagnostic service for recurring leaks or mechanical failures.

## Important note
Working with pressurized vessels and combustible fuels involves inherent risk. If you cannot locate the filling valves or are uncomfortable interacting with the boiler, keep the power off and wait for a qualified professional.
