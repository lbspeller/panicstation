# What to do if… you are blamed for a data protection incident and asked for an immediate response (USA)

## Short answer
Do not rush into admissions. Ask for the allegation and questions in writing, request a short fact-check window, preserve relevant work records where they are, and use the organization’s security or privacy incident channel.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess, speculate, or fill gaps to sound cooperative.
- Do not say “I caused the breach” or “I am responsible” just to calm the situation down.
- Do not delete, edit, rename, backdate, or clean up emails, files, chats, tickets, logs, or device records.
- Do not move work data to personal email, personal cloud storage, a personal phone, or a USB drive.
- Do not search through systems, logs, files, or personal data unless that access is part of your normal role or you are specifically instructed to do it by the right internal team.
- Do not sign a statement, interview summary, or timeline that you think is incomplete, inaccurate, or unclear.
- Do not argue with rumors or try to prove everything at once.

## What to do now

1. Create a calm written record of the request. If the request was verbal, send a brief same-day message saying what you were asked, who asked, and the response deadline you were given.

2. Ask for the exact allegation and questions in writing. Ask what incident is being discussed, what time period, what system or data is involved, what they believe your role was, and what specific questions they want answered.

3. Ask for a short, defined fact-check window. A safe first reply is: “I want to respond accurately. Please send the exact questions and deadline. I can provide an initial factual response after checking my relevant emails, tickets, notes, and work records.”

4. Preserve relevant work records where they are. Do not manually delete or alter anything connected to the incident. If logs, tickets, chats, or emails may be overwritten, ask IT, security, privacy, or the incident-response team to preserve them through the organization’s process.

5. Report or route the issue through the correct internal incident channel. Use the security team, privacy office, incident hotline, ticket category, or other process your organization uses. Keep it factual: what you learned, when you learned it, what systems or accounts are being discussed, and what you have done to preserve records.

6. Prepare a narrow first response, not a full story. Include what you personally know, what you have not verified yet, what records you will check, when you can respond further, and that you have used the internal incident channel.

7. If you are union-represented and an investigatory interview may lead to discipline, consider clearly asking for your representative before answering detailed questions. If the employer refuses, calmly ask whether they are ending the interview or asking you to continue without representation, then contact your union as soon as you can.

8. Get quick confidential advice before giving a detailed statement if the accusation could affect your job, license, immigration position, security clearance, or professional reputation. This may be your union, an employment attorney, or another qualified adviser.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to quit, accept discipline, take the fall, or write a complete timeline from memory. You do not need to determine legal notice duties yourself in your first reply; focus on accurate facts, preserving records, and routing the issue to the right internal team. You also do not need to fix systems, revoke access, contact customers, or report externally unless the proper internal team tells you to do that as part of your role.

## Important reassurance
Being asked for an immediate response does not prove you did anything wrong. In the first hours, the safest position is usually to slow the exchange down enough to avoid guessing, preserve the record, and answer only what you can accurately verify.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the first hours or day. Later decisions about formal statements, discipline, regulator contact, customer notices, insurance, or litigation may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Workplace rules and employee rights vary by state, contract, role, policy, and union status. If anyone asks you to destroy records, misstate dates, hide information, or use personal accounts for work data, stop and get independent advice immediately.
