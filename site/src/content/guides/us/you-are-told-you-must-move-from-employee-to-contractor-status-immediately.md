# What to do if… you are told you must move from employee to contractor status immediately (USA)

## Short answer
Request the specific allegations and the identity of the reporting body in writing, then contact your professional liability insurer or a specialized defense attorney before providing any verbal or written statements.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign or "voluntarily" surrender your license or clinical privileges on the spot to avoid a report.
- Do not provide a written narrative or "explanation" to your employer before seeing the evidence against you.
- Do not sign any document titled "agreed facts," "settlement," or "voluntary withdrawal" without legal review.
- Do not contact the licensing board impulsively to "get ahead of it" before you have confirmed your self-reporting window.
- Do not delete emails, logs, or workplace messages related to the incident.
- Do not discuss the threat with colleagues or on social media platforms.

## What to do now
1. **Request the specific allegations and reporting details in writing immediately.** **Ask for the exact policy violated, the specific board or agency being notified, and a copy of the information the employer intends to send.**
2. **Contact your professional liability insurance provider or defense organization.** **Most professional policies include "license defense" or "administrative hearing" coverage that provides a specialized attorney to handle board-related threats.**
   - Do this before your next meeting with HR or management.
   - Ask for a "reservation of rights" or confirmation of coverage for an impending board report.
3. **Invoke your right to representation if you are in a union-represented position.** **State clearly: "If this discussion could in any way lead to my being disciplined or terminated, or affect my personal continued employment, I request that my union representative, officer, or steward be present."**
   - If you are not in a union, check if your state provides "concerted activity" protections that allow a witness to attend investigatory meetings.
4. **Verify your specific board’s self-reporting deadlines and triggers.** **Search the official website of your state board (e.g., Nursing, Medicine, Pharmacy, Teaching) for "Mandatory Self-Reporting" rules.**
   - Some boards require you to report an "adverse action" or "investigation" within 15 to 30 days.
   - Note that an employer's threat to report is not always the same as a board-mandated trigger, but a professional attorney must confirm this.
5. **Acknowledge the NPDB reporting rules if you are a healthcare practitioner.** **Be aware that federal law requires hospitals and health entities to report the surrender of clinical privileges while under investigation, even if you were not formally told an investigation had started.**
   - Resigning "to keep your record clean" often results in an automatic, permanent report to the National Practitioner Data Bank.
6. **Secure your own copies of relevant, legally accessible evidence.** **Create a factual timeline of events, including dates, times, and names of individuals involved, and keep this document on a personal device.**
   - Do not download proprietary company data or patient records, as this may constitute a separate, reportable security violation.
7. **Request a copy of the employer's internal reporting and investigation policy.** **Ask specifically for the "Mandatory Reporting" or "Peer Review" policy to see if the employer is following its own established procedures.**
8. **Provide only neutral, procedural responses to employer requests for meetings.** **Use a standard template: "I am willing to cooperate with the investigation and will be prepared to respond once I have reviewed the written allegations with my representative."**

## What can wait
- Drafting a detailed rebuttal or "my side of the story" document.
- Deciding whether to file a counter-complaint or lawsuit against the employer.
- Contacting former employers or references about the situation.
- Informing your current or future secondary employers about the threat.

## Important reassurance
A threat to report is a standard component of many high-stakes workplace investigations and does not guarantee that a board will take action or that you will lose your license. Licensing boards are separate legal entities from your employer and follow their own independent due process, which often takes months or years to resolve.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate period following a threat of a professional board report. It does not cover the long-term defense of a board complaint, nor does it address criminal allegations or specific state-level employment litigation.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding professional licensure, peer review immunity, and the National Practitioner Data Bank (NPDB) are complex and vary by state and profession. If your situation involves patient harm, controlled substances, or alleged fraud, the risk of immediate summary suspension is higher and requires urgent consultation with a licensed attorney.
