# What to do if… you smell a strong chemical or solvent odour indoors and cannot identify the source (USA)

## Short answer
Get everyone and any pets into fresh air immediately. If the odor is strong, spreading, possibly flammable, or anyone feels sick, call 911 from outside or away from the smell.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay inside trying to track the source while breathing it in.
- Do not flip electrical switches, use doorbells, use garage door openers, plug or unplug cords, or turn appliances on or off if natural gas, propane, gasoline, fuel, or solvent vapors are possible.
- Do not light candles, smoke, use matches, or use any open flame.
- Do not use a phone inside the affected area if flammable vapors are possible; call after you are outside or away from the smell.
- Do not mix cleaners, spray air freshener, or add more chemicals to cover the odor.
- Do not re-enter if you feel lightheaded, nauseated, short of breath, confused, faint, or have chest tightness, wheezing, eye burning, or throat burning.

## What to do now
1. **Move to fresh air first.** Get everyone outside or to an open-air area. Take pets if you can do so without delaying.
2. **Do not delay to ventilate.** If gas, fuel, or solvent vapors are possible, leave doors and windows as they are rather than stopping to open them.
3. **Call 911 from outside or away from the smell** if the odor is strong, spreading, in a hallway or shared building area, possibly flammable, or causing symptoms. Say you suspect chemical fumes indoors and cannot identify the source.
4. **Call Poison Control once you are in fresh air:** 1-800-222-1222. Tell them what you smelled, how long you were inside, who was exposed, and what symptoms anyone has.
5. **If anyone has serious symptoms, call 911 now.** Serious symptoms include trouble breathing, chest pain or tightness, fainting, confusion, seizures, repeated vomiting, chemical burns, eye injury, or symptoms that are getting worse.
6. **If natural gas, propane, gasoline, or fuel vapors are possible, stay out and keep others away.** From a safe place, call 911 if it feels urgent, strong, or spreading. If you already have your gas utility’s emergency number, you can also call it from outside.
7. **If chemical liquid, mist, or residue may be on you, remove contaminated outer clothing if you safely can.** Put clothing in a plastic bag, then another plastic bag if available. Rinse exposed skin with lukewarm water and mild soap. Do not put the bags in regular trash until Poison Control or responders tell you what to do.
8. **If your eyes burn or your vision is not normal, rinse with clean running water for 10 to 15 minutes.** Do not use eyedrops unless Poison Control or a clinician tells you to.
9. **If you are in an apartment or shared building, do not re-enter affected halls or utility areas.** From a safe place, tell 911 or building management that the odor may affect more than one unit. Warn nearby people only if you can do it without going back into the fumes or using buzzers, switches, or doorbells where flammable vapors may be present.
10. **Write down simple facts from outside:** when it started, where it seemed strongest, whether anyone feels unwell, and any recent painting, renovation, fuel storage, pest control, cleaning, or garage activity.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact chemical right now.
- You do not need to clean, deodorize, test, or neutralize the odor right now.
- You do not need to decide today who is responsible, such as a landlord, neighbor, contractor, or repair company.
- You do not need to collect samples or inspect vents, pipes, appliances, storage areas, or the garage before responders or Poison Control advise you.

## Important reassurance
Leaving first is a reasonable protective step, even if the source later turns out to be temporary or lower risk. Strong unfamiliar odors can cause panic and real symptoms, and you do not have to solve the cause before getting help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repairs, building management, landlord notices, a local health department, cleanup, or testing may need specialist help after the immediate risk is controlled.

## Important note
This is general first-step safety information, not medical, legal, environmental, or professional advice. If you think there is immediate danger, call 911. For exposure guidance, call Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222.
