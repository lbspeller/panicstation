# What to do if… a person you care about is transferred to another detention facility with little notice (USA)

## Short answer
Confirm which custody system currently holds the person and use an official locator or official facility contact route before traveling, sending money, or mailing anything. Once the destination is confirmed, check the receiving facility’s rules.

## Do not do these things
- Do not travel to the reported destination until you have confirmed the current facility and checked its visiting rules.
- Do not send money, mail, property, or time-sensitive documents to an address that has not been confirmed through an official source.
- Do not treat a rumor, social-media post, or secondhand message as final confirmation.
- Do not post the person’s date of birth, inmate number, detainee number, or A-number publicly.
- Do not rely on repeated calls alone. Keep a brief call log and use official locator and facility contact routes.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the details you already have.**  
   Note the person’s full legal name, date of birth, inmate or detainee number if known, A-number if relevant, last known facility, who told you about the transfer, the reported destination, whether they may still be in transit, and any reference number.

2. **Identify the likely custody system.**  
   Start with the last known facility. Ask which agency currently has custody and which official locator or contact route you should use. The person may be in a county jail, state prison, federal custody, immigration detention, or another detention setting.

3. **Use the official locator or official facility route that matches the custody system.**
   - **Federal custody:** use the Federal Bureau of Prisons inmate locator.
   - **State prison custody:** check the official state corrections website.
   - **County jail custody:** check the county’s official jail or sheriff website if it provides a lookup, or call the jail using the number on its official website.
   - **Immigration detention:** use the ICE Online Detainee Locator System. You can search using the person’s A-number, or their name, country of birth, and birth date.

4. **If the person does not appear in the expected locator, do not guess.**  
   An official locator may not answer the question straight away. Re-check later and use an official contact route. For immigration detention, the official USA.gov guidance says to contact an ICE Enforcement and Removal Operations field office if the person cannot be found in the online system.

5. **Once the destination is confirmed, check the receiving facility’s rules before acting.**  
   Ask:
   - whether the person has arrived, if that information can be shared;
   - whether phone, mail, or other contact services are available yet;
   - the correct mailing-address format;
   - the approved deposit method;
   - the current visiting rules and whether advance approval is required.

6. **If you have a specific urgent welfare concern, say so clearly.**  
   Call the facility’s official main number and ask how to pass on an urgent welfare concern to the appropriate staff. Keep the message factual and brief. If you believe there is an immediate threat to life and cannot reach the facility, contact local emergency services.

7. **If a lawyer or caseworker is already involved, tell them promptly.**  
   Share the confirmed facts and ask whether the transfer changes any contact or paperwork arrangements.

8. **Check for optional custody-status notifications if they would help.**  
   If a custody-status notification service is available to you, consider using it. Some state and county systems use VINE or another service. Availability, eligibility, and alert types vary. Check the official state or county route before registering.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out why the transfer happened right now.
- You do not need to send money, property, mail, or paperwork until the receiving facility confirms the correct process.
- You do not need to travel for a visit until the location and visiting rules are confirmed.
- Broader complaints and legal strategy can wait unless there is a specific urgent welfare concern.

## Important reassurance
A sudden transfer can be unsettling, especially when the information is incomplete. It can take more than one official check to confirm the current location. The useful first step is to confirm the custody system and current location through an official route.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours or day after you hear about a transfer. Later decisions about visits, property, complaints, or legal options may need case-specific help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Procedures differ across county jails, state prisons, federal custody, immigration detention, and individual facilities. Treat unconfirmed information as uncertain and prioritize verified location details and urgent welfare contact.
