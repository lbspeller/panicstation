# What to do if… you receive an unexpected message claiming your phone number or email is linked to a case (USA)

## Short answer
Do not respond through the message, click its links, or use the contact details it provides. Verify through an official agency or court contact you find yourself, and if anyone tries to question you about possible wrongdoing, say: “I’m not answering questions. I want a lawyer.”

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, open attachments, scan QR codes, or install any “secure reply” app from the notice.
- Don’t trust caller ID, badge numbers, case numbers, seals, or logos that appear only in the message.
- Don’t share your SSN, date of birth, account logins, security answers, one-time passcodes, or photos of identity documents.
- Don’t pay by gift card, cryptocurrency, wire transfer, payment app, or bank transfer to “clear,” “close,” or “pause” a case.
- Don’t explain your side by phone, text, or email because someone says you will be arrested if you do not respond now.
- Don’t delete the message yet; keep it for verification and reporting.

## What to do now
1. **Save the notice without opening anything inside it.** Screenshot the full message, sender address or number, time and date, any case reference, the exact threat or deadline, and the visible link text.
2. **Verify through a route you choose yourself.**
   - If it claims to be from local or state law enforcement, use the agency’s official website or published non-emergency number and ask whether the contact is genuine.
   - If it claims to be from a federal agency, use that agency’s official website and ask for the relevant field office, inspector general, or public contact route.
   - If it claims to be from a court, use the court’s official website or the court Clerk’s Office contact details. Federal court scam warnings say courts do not use phone calls or emails to demand personal or financial information or threaten people who do not comply.
3. **Keep the verification short.** Say that you received a message claiming your phone number or email is linked to a case, give only the case reference shown in the message if needed, and ask whether there is a legitimate contact route.
4. **If someone starts questioning you, stop the conversation.** Say: “I’m not answering questions. I want a lawyer.” Do not argue, explain, or try to fix it on the call.
5. **Report likely impersonation or phishing.**
   - Report scam or government-imposter contact to the FTC.
   - Report internet-enabled fraud, phishing, smishing, or spoofing to the FBI’s IC3.
   - If a court name is being impersonated, tell the relevant court Clerk’s Office through official contact details.
6. **Secure the email or phone account mentioned in the notice.** Change the password for the email account if it may be involved, turn on multi-factor authentication, and check for unfamiliar forwarding rules, recovery emails, recovery phone numbers, or recent sign-ins.
7. **If you already paid or shared sensitive information, contain the damage.**
   - Contact your bank, card issuer, payment app, or wire-transfer company immediately and ask whether the payment can be stopped or disputed.
   - If you shared your SSN, identity documents, or date of birth, use IdentityTheft.gov and consider a credit freeze.
   - If you shared a one-time passcode or suspect a SIM swap, contact your mobile carrier and secure the affected account right away.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a full statement now.
- You do not need to prove your innocence through the message.
- You do not need to pay today to prevent arrest.
- You do not need to give remote access to your phone, computer, email, or bank account.
- You do not need to keep replying while you verify whether the notice is real.

## Important reassurance
A message like this can feel urgent because it is built around fear, deadlines, and official-sounding language. Slowing down and verifying through an independent official route protects you whether the notice is fake, mistaken, or connected to a real matter.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: preserve the message, avoid irreversible mistakes, verify the contact, and stop unsafe engagement. If it turns into a real legal issue, decisions after that may need help from a qualified attorney in your state.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. If you believe there is an immediate emergency or someone is in danger now, call 911.
