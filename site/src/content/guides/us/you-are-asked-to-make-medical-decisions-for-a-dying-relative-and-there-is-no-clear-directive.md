# What to do if… you are asked to make medical decisions for a dying relative and there is no clear directive (USA)

## Short answer
Ask the clinical team to identify the legal surrogate according to state law and request a family meeting with the attending physician to establish a care plan based on your relative's known values.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel forced to give an immediate "yes" or "no" to complex treatments—request a pause to speak with the doctor.
- Do not assume your relationship (e.g., oldest child) automatically makes you the legal surrogate—laws vary by state.
- Do not engage in arguments at the patient’s bedside—ask for a private room or a facilitated meeting.
- Do not confuse "DNR" (Do Not Resuscitate) with a "no care" order—comfort care and symptom management continue regardless of code status.
- Do not make verbal promises to other family members regarding specific medical outcomes before the team explains the prognosis.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the current medical urgency and the patient’s capacity to speak for themselves.**  
   - Ask the nurse or doctor: "Is my relative capable of making this specific decision right now?"
   - Ask: "How much time do we have before a decision must be made to prevent suffering or unwanted intervention?"
2. **Request an immediate search of the medical record for any existing portable medical orders.**  
   - Specifically ask for a POLST (Provider Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment), MOLST, or POST form.
   - Ask if a Durable Power of Attorney for Health Care (DPOAHC) or "Health Care Proxy" was scanned during a previous admission.
3. **Ask the hospital’s social worker or patient advocate to confirm the state’s default surrogate hierarchy.**  
   - Most states have a legal list (usually starting with a spouse, then adult children, then parents).
   - If multiple people are at the same level (e.g., three siblings), ask how the hospital handles a "majority" or "consensus" requirement.
4. **Call for a formal family meeting with the attending physician and the palliative care team.**  
   - Request that the meeting includes a bedside nurse and, if desired, a hospital chaplain or spiritual care provider for emotional support.
   - Use a speakerphone or video call for family members who cannot be physically present.
5. **Provide the clinical team with "substituted judgment" based on the patient's past statements.**  
   - Tell the team about the relative's previously expressed views on ventilators, feeding tubes, and "quality of life."
   - Focus on what the patient would choose for themselves if they could wake up for ten minutes to see their current condition.
6. **Ask the doctor to explain the "benefits and burdens" of each proposed intervention.**  
   - Use the phrase: "What is the most likely best-case outcome and the most likely worst-case outcome if we proceed?"
   - Ask: "What does 'comfort-focused care' look like in this specific unit?"
7. **Request an Ethics Consultation if there is a conflict between family members or the medical team.**  
   - An ethics consultant can help mediate disputes and clarify the ethical obligations of the surrogate.
   - Note that an ethics consultation is typically an advisory service, not a legal ruling.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family conflicts or "fix" relationships today.
- You do not need to make funeral or memorial arrangements while active medical decisions are pending.
- You do not need to research the legalities of guardianship or court-ordered interventions immediately; these are last-resort measures the hospital will initiate if necessary.
- You do not need to answer every phone call or update every relative personally; designate one spokesperson to handle outgoing information.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed, guilty, or uncertain when making these choices. You are not "killing" your relative; you are acting as their voice to ensure the medical care they receive matches the life they lived. The clinical team is there to support you through the process, and you are allowed to ask for help multiple times.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for urgent medical decision-making in a hospital setting. It does not replace legal advice or specific state statutes regarding probate or guardianship.

## Important note
Surrogate laws and "default" hierarchies differ significantly between states. Always ask the hospital’s patient representative or legal department to clarify who is authorized to sign consents in your specific jurisdiction and ensure this is documented in the electronic health record.
