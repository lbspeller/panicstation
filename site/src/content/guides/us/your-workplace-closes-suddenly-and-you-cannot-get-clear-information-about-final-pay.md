# What to do if… your workplace closes suddenly and you cannot get clear information about final pay (USA)

## Short answer
Do not resign or sign new contracts immediately. Request all proposed changes in writing and secure copies of your current personnel file, pay records, and benefit summaries before your system access changes.

## Do not do these things
- Do not submit a resignation to "beat" the sale (this may disqualify you from unemployment or severance).
- Do not sign a new offer letter, non-compete, or arbitration agreement under immediate pressure.
- Do not assume your current contract or handbook automatically transfers to the buyer.
- Do not remove proprietary company data or customer lists to personal devices.
- Do not rely on oral promises from managers who may not have authority under the new ownership.

## What to do now
1. **Request a written disclosure of the transaction type and the impact on your role.** **The legal obligations of the buyer often depend on whether the deal is a stock purchase or an asset sale.**
   - Ask for the "effective date" or "closing date" of the transition.
   - Request a specific list of changes to your title, compensation, and work location.
2. **Download and save your entire employment history from the current HR portal today.** **You may lose access to these systems the moment the sale closes.**
   - Save your original offer letter and any signed restrictive covenants (non-competes).
   - Export your last 12 months of pay stubs and commission statements.
   - Save your most recent performance review and your current PTO / sick leave balance.
3. **Verify if a federal WARN or state "mini-WARN" notice has been triggered.** **As of 2026, some states like California require notices to include specific workforce support and public assistance information (SB 617).**
   - Ask HR: "Is this transaction classified as a plant closing or mass layoff under WARN?"
   - Note that in asset sales, the seller is generally responsible for notice before closing, while the buyer is responsible after closing.
4. **Insist on a review period for any new paperwork or "onboarding" documents.** **Tell management you need a reasonable window to review the terms before signing.**
   - Look for new "restrictive covenants" (non-competes or non-solicits), as 2026 laws in several states now limit these for laid-off or low-wage workers.
   - Check for arbitration clauses that might waive your right to a jury trial for future disputes.
5. **Contact your union representative immediately if you are in a collective bargaining unit.** **The "Successor Bar" doctrine may protect your union's status for 6 to 12 months after the acquisition.**
   - Ask if the buyer has agreed to assume the existing Collective Bargaining Agreement (CBA).
   - Do not sign individual "new terms" that conflict with your union contract.
6. **Clarify the exact date your current health insurance coverage ends.** **You generally have 60 days from the loss of coverage to elect COBRA continuation or enroll in a Marketplace plan via a Special Enrollment Period (SEP).**
   - Ask if the buyer is offering a "successor plan" that waives waiting periods for pre-existing conditions.
   - Confirm if your deductible progress (year-to-date) will transfer to the new plan.
7. **Document all communications regarding "stay bonuses" or "retention agreements."** **If you are promised a bonus to stay through the sale, ensure the triggers and payment dates are in a signed document.**
   - Keep a log of dates, names, and specific statements made during "all-hands" meetings.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you like the new owners yet.
- You do not need to update your LinkedIn or resume in the first hour.
- You do not need to confront the buyer about long-term career paths during the initial announcement.
- You do not need to liquidate your 401(k) or retirement accounts; these can usually be rolled over later.

## Important reassurance
Hearing that your employer is being sold is naturally destabilizing and may trigger a high-stress response. It is a procedural event, not necessarily a personal one. By focusing on gathering documents and refusing to sign anything under duress, you maintain your leverage and protect your legal options while the transition settles.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate stabilization of your employment status during a business sale announcement. It does not provide legal strategy for specific contract disputes, severance negotiations, or complex tax implications of stock options.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Employment laws, including 2026 updates to the NLRB Joint Employer Standard and state-level WARN requirements, vary significantly by jurisdiction. If you are presented with a severance agreement or a waiver of rights, consult with an employment attorney in your state.
