# What to do if… you notice water seeping through a basement wall even before it starts pooling (USA)

## Short answer
Avoid contact with the wet area and any nearby electrical components; prioritize keeping the space clear and dry until the source is identified.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t enter the area if water is near outlets, extension cords, or appliances.
- Don’t touch the electrical panel or breakers if you must stand on a damp or wet floor to reach them.
- Don’t use electrical equipment (such as shop-vacs or fans) in the wet area unless the circuit has been verified as safe by a professional.
- Don’t ignore the moisture; mold can begin to colonize damp surfaces within 24 to 48 hours.
- Don’t apply "waterproof" paint or sealant over an active leak or seeping crack.

## What to do now
1. **Clear the area and restrict access.** **Keep children and pets away from the damp wall and any moisture on the floor.**
   - Ensure the path to the area is clear of trip hazards.
   - Assume any damp surface is a slip risk.
2. **De-energize the area if it is safe to do so.** **Only turn off power to the basement if the electrical panel is in a completely dry location.**
   - If you must walk through water or stand on a damp floor to reach the panel, do not touch it; call your electric utility or an electrician.
3. **Conduct a brief visual check for internal leaks.** **Look for nearby pipes, water heaters, or HVAC condensate lines that may be the actual source.**
   - If the seepage is clearly coming from a plumbing fixture, turn off the main water valve if it is in a dry, safe location.
4. **Isolate and protect belongings.** **Move cardboard boxes, textiles, electronics, and furniture at least several feet away from the seeping wall.**
   - Elevate items off the floor using plastic bins or pallets if possible.
   - Use towels or absorbent snakes to contain moisture and prevent it from reaching floor drains or other rooms.
5. **Document the seepage point.** **Take clear photographs and video of where the water is entering the wall.**
   - Note if the water is coming through a specific crack, a tie rod hole, or generally "weeping" through the masonry.
   - Record the time and recent weather conditions (e.g., heavy rain or snowmelt).
6. **Start a 48-hour drying clock.** **Aim to have the surface dry to the touch within two days to minimize mold growth.**
   - Use a dehumidifier in a safe, dry part of the basement to lower overall humidity.
   - Do not point fans directly at the seep if you suspect mold is already present.
7. **Contact assistance based on the source.** **Call a basement waterproofing specialist if the water is coming through the foundation, or a plumber if it is a pipe leak.**
   - Check your insurance policy for "Water Backup and Sump Discharge" coverage; standard homeowners insurance often excludes groundwater seepage.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide on a permanent foundation repair or drainage system today.
- You do **not** need to tear down finished walls or drywall immediately unless they are sagging or structurally compromised.
- You do **not** need to call an emergency disaster restoration company for minor seepage that is not yet pooling.

## Important reassurance
Noticing seepage before it turns into standing water gives you time to protect your property and prevent electrical hazards. Most seepage is a result of hydrostatic pressure or poor exterior drainage and can be managed once the immediate moisture is contained.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Seepage often indicates a need for exterior gutter maintenance, soil grading, or foundation repair, which require professional assessment once the area is dry.

## Important note
This information is for harm-reduction and does not constitute professional engineering, plumbing, or electrical advice. If you see electrical sparking, smell gas, or notice significant new cracks or bulging in the wall, evacuate the area and call for professional emergency help immediately.
