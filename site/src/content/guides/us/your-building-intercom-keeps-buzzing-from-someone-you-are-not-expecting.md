# What to do if… your building intercom keeps buzzing from someone you are not expecting (USA)

## Short answer
Do not buzz anyone in just to stop the noise. Keep your own door locked, verify only without opening your door, and call 911 if you believe someone is trying to get in unlawfully or you feel in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not press “unlock” to make the buzzing stop.
- Do not go to the lobby, vestibule, hallway, or outer door alone to confront or check who it is.
- Do not share personal details over the intercom, including your name, whether you are alone, or your schedule.
- Do not hold the outer door for someone you do not recognize or cannot verify.
- Do not assume “delivery” or “maintenance” is genuine without checking through your building’s normal process.
- Do not post real-time details publicly.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Stay inside your unit. Lock your door. If you can safely see the entry from a peephole, window, intercom camera, or door camera, look without opening your door.
2. **Use the intercom only to verify.** If you answer, ask, “Who are you here to see?” and “What unit number?” If the answer is vague, inconsistent, or pressuring, end the call and do not buzz them in.
3. **Redirect them away from you.** Tell them to contact the resident they are visiting, the front desk, building security, or property management.
4. **Loop in building support.** If your building has a front desk, doorman, concierge, security desk, or maintenance emergency line, call it and say an unknown person is repeatedly buzzing your unit.
5. **Warn one trusted nearby person if it is safe.** If it seems like multiple units are being buzzed, message a trusted neighbor or building group: “Unknown person repeatedly buzzing; please do not buzz in anyone you cannot verify.”
6. **Save only what is already available.** Write down the time, what was said, and the unit or name they claimed. If your intercom camera or door camera already recorded it, save the clip; do not go looking for the person.
7. **Escalate based on immediacy.** Call 911 if you believe there is a threat, attempted forced entry, someone trying doors, threats over the intercom, or a crime in progress. If it is suspicious but not an emergency, look up your local police department’s non-emergency number or online reporting option.
8. **Ask management for specific checks.** Ask them to check whether the outer door is closing and locking reliably, whether the intercom directory is sending visitors to the wrong unit, whether access logs or building cameras show misuse, and whether residents can be reminded not to buzz in unknown visitors.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now about moving, changing locks, upgrading the system, or taking legal steps.
- You do not need to confront anyone to prove your concern is valid.
- You can write a clearer timeline for property management or police later, when the immediate buzzing has stopped and you feel steadier.

## Important reassurance
Repeated buzzing can be confusion, a wrong unit, a delivery mistake, or someone trying multiple units to get access. Not buzzing in an unknown person is a normal safety choice, and it can help protect other residents too.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the next minutes and hours. If the buzzing becomes repeated harassment or your building’s entry system is routinely being misused, later decisions may need help from property management, local police non-emergency services, a tenant organization, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe or believe a crime is in progress, trust your judgment and contact emergency services.
