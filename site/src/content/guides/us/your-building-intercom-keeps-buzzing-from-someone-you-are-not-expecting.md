# What to do if… your building intercom keeps buzzing from someone you are not expecting (USA)

## Short answer
Do not unlock the door to stop the noise. Keep your unit door locked, verify the visitor's identity through the intercom or peephole only, and call 911 if you believe someone is attempting unauthorized entry or you feel in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not press the "unlock" or "buzz-in" button simply to silence the system.
- Do not go down to the lobby, vestibule, or hallway to confront the person.
- Do not allow entry to anyone claiming to be a "locked-out neighbor" or "Airbnb guest" without verification from management.
- Do not provide your name, unit number, or occupancy status (e.g., "I'm home alone") over the intercom.
- Do not assume a visitor is a delivery driver or utility worker just because they are wearing a uniform or mention a neighbor's name.
- Do not hold the outer door open for "tailgaters" (people following behind you) if you exit or enter during the incident.

## What to do now
1. **Maintain your safe pause inside your locked unit.** Do not open your apartment door to look into the hallway. If you have a peephole or a linked smart-camera, check it without making your presence known.
2. **Listen to the type of buzz to distinguish between a person and a fault.**  
   - If the buzz is a steady, unceasing electrical drone or hum, it is likely a mechanical/wiring fault or a stuck button.  
   - If the buzz is intermittent or rhythmic, a person is actively pressing the button.
3. **Use the intercom for verification only if you feel safe doing so.** If you answer, ask: "Who are you here to see?" and "What unit number?"
   - If the response is vague (e.g., "I have a package") or the caller asks to be let in "just to leave it in the lobby," **end the call** and **do not grant access**.
   - If the caller claims to be a resident who forgot their key, tell them to call the property manager or a locksmith.
4. **Contact building security or the front desk immediately.** If your building has staff, notify them that someone is attempting unauthorized entry or "buzzer bombing" (pressing multiple units' buttons).
5. **Call 311 or use a non-emergency police line for persistent harassment.** If the buzzing is frequent but there is no immediate threat of violence or forced entry, report it as a quality-of-life or harassment issue.
6. **Call 911 if you observe an active threat.**  
   - Call if you see someone attempting to force the outer door or your unit door.  
   - Call if the person is making verbal threats or acting erratically on camera.
7. **Notify a trusted neighbor if the buzzing is building-wide.** Send a brief text to alert them so they do not accidentally buzz the stranger in out of frustration or politeness.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify exactly which neighbor the person might actually be visiting.
- You do not need to investigate the lobby or stairwell yourself.
- You can wait until the immediate situation has passed to write a formal complaint to the property management company.
- You do not need to decide on permanent security upgrades or moving out during the event.

## Important reassurance
Intercom harassment or "buzzer bombing" is often a tactic used by couriers to gain fast entry or by scammers looking for unlocked mailrooms. Choosing not to buzz in an unverified person is a standard safety protocol, not an act of rudeness. Your priority is maintaining the security of the building's perimeter.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active intercom disturbance. It does not cover long-term legal disputes with neighbors or technical repair of intercom hardware.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. If you believe a crime is occurring, contact law enforcement.
