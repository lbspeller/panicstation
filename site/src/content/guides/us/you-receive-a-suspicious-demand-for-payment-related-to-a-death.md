# What to do if… you receive a suspicious demand for payment related to a death (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay, click, or share information from the demand. Verify it using contact details you find independently, then report it through official scam-reporting channels if it appears false, coercive, or impersonating someone.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay an unverified demand to “unlock” or “release” a body, documents, benefits, estate money, insurance money, or inheritance.
- Do not click links, scan QR codes, open attachments, or follow payment instructions in the message.
- Do not call the number in the message or keep texting to prove anything.
- Do not share your Social Security number, driver’s license, bank details, card details, login codes, or copies of ID with an unverified sender.
- Do not pay by wire transfer, cryptocurrency, gift card, payment app, or cash because someone says it is urgent or confidential.
- Do not let anyone isolate you with phrases like “do not tell the family” or “this must stay private.”

## What to do now
1. **Pause and treat the demand as unverified.** Put the phone down if you can. The safest first move is to slow the timeline down before money or information leaves your control.

2. **Preserve the message without engaging.** Screenshot texts, save emails and voicemails, and write down the date, time, sender details, amount requested, and exactly what they said the fee was for. Do not open links or attachments unless your bank, law enforcement, or an official reporting form specifically asks for the information.

3. **Check whether the sender is real using a separate route.**
   - If it claims to be from a funeral home, call the funeral home using paperwork you already have or a main number you find independently. Ask for an itemized bill or general price list before paying anything.
   - If it mentions release of the body, a morgue fee, a coroner fee, or a medical examiner fee, contact the county medical examiner, coroner, hospital bereavement office, or funeral director through contact details you locate yourself.
   - If it claims to involve an estate, inheritance, insurance payout, death benefit, or funds being held, contact the executor, probate attorney, insurer, employer benefits office, or financial institution through a verified main number. A demand to pay first to receive money is a strong scam warning sign.

4. **If you already paid, contact the payment provider immediately.**
   - Call your bank or card issuer using the number on the back of your card, the official app, or a statement, and ask for the fraud department.
   - If it was a wire transfer, contact the bank or wire transfer company and ask whether the transfer can be stopped or reversed.
   - If it was a card payment, ask about disputing the charge and replacing the card.
   - If it was a payment app, cryptocurrency transfer, gift card, or cash, report it to the company used and ask whether any recovery step is available.

5. **If you shared identity details, start identity-recovery steps.** Use IdentityTheft.gov for guided steps if you shared your Social Security number, driver’s license, account details, login information, or copies of ID. Consider a fraud alert or credit freeze, especially if Social Security, driver’s license, or financial details were shared.

6. **Report the suspicious demand.**
   - Report scams and fraud to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.
   - If the contact was by email, text, website, payment app, social media, or another internet-enabled route, file a complaint with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center at IC3.gov.
   - Type official addresses yourself or use trusted bookmarks, because scammers sometimes create look-alike reporting pages.

7. **If you are being threatened or pressured in real time, get help.** Call 911 for immediate danger. If there is no immediate danger, use your local police non-emergency line or ask a trusted person to sit with you while you verify the demand.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the scammer or send a perfect reply right now.
- You do not need to post publicly or confront the sender today.
- You do not need to make probate, estate, funeral, insurance, or family decisions in this moment.
- You do not need to handle every call yourself; you can ask one trusted person to help with verification and note-taking.

## Important reassurance
Scammers often use grief, urgency, and official-sounding words because those can make anyone feel rushed and unsure. Slowing down, refusing to pay through the message, and checking through independent contact details are protective steps, not overreactions.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce immediate risk, preserve useful information, and buy time. Later decisions about funeral bills, estate administration, identity theft, bank disputes, or police reports may need help from the relevant provider, agency, attorney, or financial institution.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, law enforcement, or professional advice. Practices can vary by state, county, funeral provider, hospital, bank, and payment service. If you are unsure, do not pay the demand until you have verified it through an independent official or business contact route.
