# What to do if… your email account password has been changed and you did not do it (USA)

## Short answer
Use your email provider’s official account-recovery page or app immediately. Once you get back in, sign out of other sessions, change the password, secure two-factor authentication, and remove any forwarding, filters, or connected apps you did not set.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click password-reset links from unexpected messages; go directly to the provider’s official website or app.
- Do not keep guessing passwords repeatedly; some providers may slow or lock recovery attempts.
- Do not reuse an old password or a close variation.
- Do not send warnings from the compromised email account until you know the attacker is signed out.
- Do not pay anyone who claims they can recover the account or money through the FBI, IC3, FTC, or another government agency.
- Do not assume changing the password alone is enough; forwarding rules, filters, recovery details, and connected apps can keep the attacker connected.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer device if needed.** If the device you usually use has strange pop-ups, unknown browser extensions, unfamiliar apps, or other signs of malware, use a different trusted device for account recovery. Update the original device and run a reputable security scan before signing back in on it.
2. **Start recovery only from the provider’s official route.** Type the provider’s address yourself, use its official app, or use a saved bookmark you already trust. Follow the account-recovery steps even if the recovery email or phone number was changed.
3. **Change the password as soon as you regain access.** Use a long, unique password that you have not used on any other account. Store it in a password manager or another safe place you can reach later.
4. **Sign out everywhere and remove unknown access.** Use the account security settings to sign out of other sessions or devices. Remove unfamiliar devices, connected apps, third-party mail clients, delegated access, or mailbox sharing.
5. **Secure two-factor authentication and recovery details.** Turn on two-factor authentication if available, preferably with an authenticator app or security key when the provider offers it. Check that the recovery email and phone number are yours, and save backup codes somewhere safe.
6. **Check mailbox settings for attacker persistence.** Remove anything you did not create, including forwarding addresses, rules, filters, “send as” addresses, signatures, auto-replies, and blocked-address changes.
7. **Protect accounts that use this email for password resets.** Start with banking, credit cards, payment apps, tax accounts, cloud storage, and primary social accounts. Change reused passwords, enable two-factor authentication, and look for recent password-reset or payment-change activity.
8. **Check your mobile carrier account.** If your phone number is used for account recovery or text-message codes, sign in to your carrier account or contact the carrier using a known official route. Ask about an account PIN, number-transfer lock, or port-out protection if available.
9. **Warn the people most likely to be harmed, using another channel.** Contact key work, finance, family, or invoice contacts by phone, text, or another email account. Say that unexpected payment requests, password-reset requests, gift-card requests, or “new bank details” from your email should be ignored until you confirm.
10. **Report fraud or identity theft through official sites.** If personal information was stolen or misused, use IdentityTheft.gov. If money was taken, a scam was attempted, or the compromise involved business email, invoices, payroll, or cyber-enabled fraud, you can report it to IC3 by typing ic3.gov directly into your browser.
11. **Escalate work or school accounts immediately.** Contact your IT or security team using a known phone number, helpdesk portal, or in-person route. They may need to reset sessions, block forwarding, preserve logs, and protect connected systems.

## What can wait
- You do not need to fully diagnose how the account was taken over right now.
- You do not need to delete your email history or close the account immediately.
- You do not need to warn every contact at once; start with people who might act on urgent requests involving money, passwords, documents, or approvals.
- You do not need to decide on every legal, credit, or device-hardening step before regaining access and blocking the attacker.
- You can save a fuller timeline, screenshots, and cleanup notes for later unless money is actively moving, there is immediate danger, or your work or school tells you to preserve information now.

## Important reassurance
Email account takeovers happen to careful people, often through reused passwords, phishing, malware, or stolen credentials from unrelated breaches. The immediate goal is to regain control, remove the attacker’s access paths, and protect the accounts that depend on that email.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to stabilize the situation and reduce immediate harm. Later decisions about identity theft recovery, credit protection, device cleanup, workplace reporting, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, IT security, or other professional advice. Follow instructions from your email provider, bank, employer, school, law enforcement, or a qualified professional when they apply to your situation.
