# What to do if… you hear someone outside calling your name when you are home alone (USA)

## Short answer
Stay inside, keep a locked barrier between you and the person, and do not confirm your name or that you are alone. If they are trying to enter, threatening you, refusing to leave, or you feel in immediate danger, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door or step outside to see what they want.
- Do not call back confirming your name, location, or that you are home alone.
- Do not stand in a lit window, open blinds, or move where you can be seen from outside.
- Do not let a stranger pressure you into opening the door for an emergency you cannot verify.
- Do not chase them, threaten them, or try to handle it yourself.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer place inside.** Step away from exterior doors and ground-floor windows. Lock exterior doors and any windows that could be reached from outside.
2. **Check only from a safe barrier.** Use a peephole, doorbell camera, security camera, or already-safe view if you have one. Keep the door fully closed and locked.
3. **Do not engage unless you need to.** Silence is acceptable. If you must speak to avoid opening the door, keep it brief through the closed door, such as: “I can’t come to the door.”
4. **Call 911 if there is immediate danger.** Call if they try the handle, circle the home, make threats, refuse to leave, damage property, appear to be forcing entry, or you believe a crime may be happening.
5. **Give the dispatcher simple facts.** Say your address, that someone outside is calling your name, whether they are at a door, window, hallway, or yard, and what they are doing now. Stay on the line until the call-taker says to hang up.
6. **Use text-to-911 only if speaking could put you at more risk.** A voice call is usually the best way to reach 911. If you text, put your exact location first; if text-to-911 is not available in your area, you should receive a bounce-back message and will need another way to reach help.
7. **Bring in help without exposing yourself.** Call or text a trusted neighbor, nearby friend, building security, front desk, or property manager. Ask them to stay on the phone, check from their own safe position, or meet responders if they can do so safely.
8. **Save details without making yourself visible.** Note the time, what was said, where the voice came from, what the person did, and any description you can get from inside. Save doorbell or security footage if you already have it.
9. **If the person leaves and there is no immediate danger, use local non-emergency options.** Look up your local police department’s 10-digit non-emergency number. In some places, 311 can connect you with local non-emergency city services, but it is not available everywhere.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether it is a prank, scam, mistaken identity, burglary distraction, or something else.
- You do not need to confront the person, post about it, or work out how they knew your name while it is happening.
- You can handle later follow-up, such as telling property management, asking neighbors about cameras, checking privacy settings, or improving locks and lighting, after you are safe.

## Important reassurance
It makes sense to feel alarmed when someone outside uses your name unexpectedly. Staying inside, not confirming information, and calling for help if the situation feels unsafe are reasonable protective steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate moment only. If this happens again, escalates, or involves someone you know, later decisions may need support from local law enforcement, building management, a victim-support organisation, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, professional security, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in danger or someone is attempting to enter your home, call 911 and focus on staying behind a locked barrier until help arrives.
