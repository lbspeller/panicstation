# What to do if… your heating is stuck on and your home is overheating (USA)

## Short answer
If you smell gas, hear hissing, or a carbon monoxide alarm is sounding, get everyone to fresh air first and call 911 from a safe place. Otherwise, turn the thermostat to OFF; if heat keeps coming, shut off the heating equipment at its service switch or clearly labelled circuit breaker, then cool people and pets.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep troubleshooting while the home keeps heating; stop the heat source or leave for a cooler safe place.
- Do not open furnace, boiler, or air-handler panels or work inside the equipment if you are not trained.
- Do not ignore a carbon monoxide alarm, gas smell, hissing sound, or symptoms such as headache, dizziness, nausea, confusion, fainting, or unusual sleepiness.
- Do not use switches, thermostats, phones, garage doors, matches, lighters, appliances, or vehicles inside the home if you suspect a gas leak.
- Do not guess at breakers if nothing is labelled and you are uncertain; use a nearby service switch if present, leave if there is danger, or call for help.

## What to do now
1. Check for immediate danger first:
   - If a carbon monoxide alarm is sounding, move everyone and pets to fresh air immediately, account for everyone, call 911, and do not go back inside until emergency responders say it is safe.
   - If you smell gas or hear hissing, leave immediately on foot. Do not touch switches, thermostats, phones, appliances, garage doors, or anything that could spark. Call 911 from a safe location, then call your gas utility emergency number if you know it.
   - If anyone is confused, fainting, having a seizure, unconscious, or getting rapidly worse in the heat, call 911 and start cooling them while waiting.
2. If there is no gas smell, hissing, or carbon monoxide alarm, do the quick thermostat checks:
   - Set the system mode to OFF, not heat or auto.
   - If the fan is set to ON, switch it to AUTO.
   - If the thermostat is blank or erratic and batteries are easy to reach, replace them once. If heat is still being produced, move to the shutoff step.
3. Shut off the heating equipment if heat is still coming from vents, radiators, or baseboards:
   - Use the furnace, boiler, or air-handler service switch if you can identify it. It is often a light-switch-style switch near the equipment.
   - Use the circuit breaker only if it is clearly labelled for the furnace, boiler, air handler, or HVAC and you are confident it is the correct one.
   - Once it is off, do not keep power-cycling it.
4. Move people and pets to the coolest available safe place:
   - Use the lowest level, shaded side, or a cooler room.
   - Close sunny blinds or curtains.
   - Use fans if they are safe to use and there is no suspected gas leak.
   - Open windows only if outdoor air is cooler and there is no gas smell, hissing, or carbon monoxide alarm.
5. Cool anyone who may be heat-unwell:
   - Move them to a cool place.
   - Loosen extra clothing.
   - Use cool wet cloths, misting, fanning, or a cool bath or shower if safe.
   - Call 911 if they are confused, fainting, having a seizure, unconscious, or symptoms are severe or worsening.
6. Arrange urgent help for the heating system:
   - If you rent, call the landlord, property manager, or emergency maintenance line and say: “The heater is stuck on, the indoor temperature is rising, and I shut off the HVAC at the service switch or breaker.”
   - If you own, call an HVAC technician for urgent service and tell them what you tried, whether the equipment is gas or electric, and whether you used the service switch or breaker.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the problem is the thermostat, relay, control board, limit switch, valve, or wiring right now.
- You do not need to decide on repair versus replacement until the home is stable and people are safe.
- You do not need to keep testing settings once the heat is off.
- You do not need to clean filters, remove panels, or inspect parts during the overheating moment.

## Important reassurance
A stuck-on heating system is alarming, but the first goal is simple: get away from gas or carbon monoxide danger, stop the heat if it is safe to do so, and cool people and pets. Taking those steps buys time for emergency responders, maintenance, or an HVAC technician to handle the cause.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the immediate overheating situation. Repairs, electrical work, gas concerns, carbon monoxide concerns, and later housing decisions may need qualified emergency responders, utility staff, HVAC professionals, medical care, or local tenant-support help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, electrical, gas-safety, HVAC, or other professional advice. If there is immediate danger, a carbon monoxide alarm, suspected gas leak, severe heat illness symptoms, fire, smoke, or uncertainty about safety, call 911.
