# What to do if… someone offers you a job, audition, or “photoshoot” and then introduces nudity or sexual expectations (USA)

## Short answer
Leave the location immediately and move to a safe, public space before attempting to process the situation. Contact the National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-HOPE for confidential support and safety planning.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t stay to “negotiate” or explain your boundaries if you feel unsafe or pressured.
- Don’t comply with unexpected nudity or sexual demands to "save the opportunity" or prove your professionalism.
- Don’t agree to a second private meeting, even if they apologize or promise a "clean" shoot.
- Don’t pay any "portfolio," "insurance," or "booking" fees that are suddenly requested after sexual demands are made.
- Don’t delete any communication (texts, emails, DMs) as these are vital evidence of coercion or scams.
- Don’t feel obligated to be "polite" when your safety or consent is being bypassed.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize an immediate physical exit from the space.**  
   Leave without finishing the session or explaining yourself. If you are in a building, move to the lobby, a busy street, or a nearby business where other people are present. 

2. **Call for immediate emergency help if you are being followed or prevented from leaving.**  
   Dial **911** if the person attempts to block your exit, touches you without consent, or threatens you physically.

3. **Contact a confidential specialist to document the event and plan next steps.**  
   Call **RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline** at **800-656-4673** or text **HOPE** to **64673**. They provide 24/7 support and can help you identify if a crime has occurred and how to access local medical or legal resources.

4. **Verify your rights if the "job" involves a minor or a professional set.**  
   In many states, including New York under the Fashion Workers Act, you have the right to a chaperone or agent on set. If you are under 18, a parent or guardian must provide written consent and usually be present; any attempt to bypass this is a major red flag.

5. **Capture digital evidence of the bait-and-switch or demands.**  
   Take screenshots of the original job posting, all messages where nudity was introduced, and any profile pages of the individual. Save these to a secure folder or send them to a trusted friend for safekeeping.

6. **Treat threats to release images as "Sextortion" and report it formally.**  
   If the individual threatens to leak photos unless you provide money or more images, do not pay or comply. Report the incident to the **FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3)** at **ic3.gov** or call **1-800-CALL-FBI**.

7. **Contact the National Human Trafficking Hotline if you feel trapped by debt or threats.**  
   If you are being told you "owe" money for the shoot or that your documents/housing are at risk, call **1-888-373-7888** or text **BEFREE** to **233733**. 

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether you want to file a formal police report.
- You do not need to confront the person or "warn" the industry immediately.
- You do not need to figure out if this will "ruin your career" while you are still in shock.
- You do not need to label the event as "assault" or "harassment" to be worthy of help.

## Important reassurance
Coercion often relies on the "sunk cost" of your time and your professional ambitions. It is a normal stress response to feel confused, frozen, or like you should "just get through it." You are not at fault for a professional environment being turned into a predatory one.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction for individuals facing professional sexual coercion. It does not replace legal advice or long-term therapeutic support.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance and is not a substitute for legal counsel or professional medical care. If you have been physically harmed, seek immediate medical attention at an emergency department.
