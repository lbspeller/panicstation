# What to do if… you find a locked safe or strongbox and you suspect vital documents are inside (USA)

## Short answer
Do not attempt to force the safe open. Secure the unit and wait for the legally appointed executor or personal representative to manage a documented opening, typically performed by a certified locksmith to preserve the integrity of the contents.

## Do not do these things
- Don't use power tools, pry bars, or torches to open the unit as heat and vibration can permanently destroy paper documents or trigger internal relockers.
- Don't move the safe from the premises without notifying all legal heirs or the estate attorney, as this may be interpreted as a "removal of assets" or concealment.
- Don't attempt to "guess" a digital code more than three times; many modern safes enter a "penalty lockout" mode that disables the keypad for 30 to 60 minutes.
- Don't share the existence of the safe or its suspected contents on social media or with unauthorized family members.
- Don't assume that power of attorney grants you the right to open the safe after the owner has died; power of attorney expires immediately upon death.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate and secure the container.** Move the safe to a secure, climate-controlled area of the home where it is not visible from windows or accessible to casual visitors.
2. **Document the external condition immediately.** Use a smartphone to record high-resolution images or video of:
   - The safe’s exact location when discovered.
   - The brand name, model number, and serial number (usually on the back or near the hinges).
   - Any visible damage or signs of prior tampering.
   - Any keys, key tags, or handwritten codes found in the immediate vicinity.
3. **Verify the legal authority to open the unit.** 
   - If a Will exists: Only the named Executor has the authority to handle the safe.
   - If no Will exists: A family member must petition the local probate court for "Letters of Administration."
   - Small Estates: In some states, a "Small Estate Affidavit" may allow a successor to access property if the total estate value is below a specific state-mandated threshold.
4. **Conduct a limited search for the access method.** Spend no more than 15 minutes checking the deceased’s "obvious" locations:
   - Desk drawers or file cabinets labeled "Important Papers."
   - The back of user manuals or within original purchase receipts for the safe.
   - Digital password managers or "Notes" apps on the deceased’s phone.
5. **Contact a certified safe technician if a code or key is not found.** Verify the locksmith's credentials through the Associated Locksmiths of America (ALOA) and ensure they specialize in "Safe and Vault" services.
   - Ask for "non-destructive entry" (manipulation or dialing) before agreeing to a "drill and repair" procedure.
   - Check if your state (one of 15 as of 2026) requires the locksmith to hold a specific state-issued license.
6. **Perform a witnessed and inventoried opening.**
   - Ensure at least two disinterested parties or the estate attorney are present.
   - Record the opening on video, ensuring the first view of the interior is captured clearly.
   - Create a written inventory of every item removed, signed and dated by all witnesses present.
7. **Follow bank-specific protocols for safe-deposit boxes.** If the unit is a bank safe-deposit box rather than a home safe, do not attempt to bring a locksmith to the bank.
   - Provide the bank with a certified copy of the death certificate and your court-issued Letters Testamentary or Letters of Administration.
   - Note that many states allow a "Will Search" only, where a bank officer supervises the opening solely to see if a Will or burial plot deed is inside.

## What can wait
- You do not need to distribute the contents or determine their value today.
- You do not need to decide which family member "gets" the safe itself.
- You do not need to report the contents to the IRS or state tax authorities until the formal estate inventory is filed.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of panic when vital documents like a Will or life insurance policy are locked away. However, the legal system provides specific "safe-search" provisions to ensure these documents are retrieved without compromising the estate's legality. Taking a slow, documented approach protects you from potential litigation or accusations of theft.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Access rules for safe-deposit boxes and home safes are governed by specific state probate codes; later steps will require coordination with a probate court or estate attorney.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding "fiduciary access" vary significantly by state. If the safe appears to have been tampered with or if family members are in active conflict, leave the unit sealed and contact an estate attorney immediately.
