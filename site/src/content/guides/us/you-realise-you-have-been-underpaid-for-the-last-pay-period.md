# What to do if… you realise you have been underpaid for the last pay period (USA)

## Short answer
Determine immediately if the issue is a missing deposit (delivery error) or a specific deduction/withholding on your pay stub, then submit a written request to payroll for an itemized breakdown and a correction timeline.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the system will automatically correct the error in the next pay cycle.
- Do not ignore new line items for garnishments or levies, as these often indicate a legal requirement.
- Do not share copies of your pay stub on social media or with coworkers; it contains sensitive tax and bank identifiers.
- Do not resign or stop working before you have received a written explanation of the discrepancy.
- Do not rely on verbal promises from a manager; you need an email or paper trail from HR or Payroll.

## What to do now
1. **Identify if you have a payment delivery problem or a calculation problem.** **If your pay stub shows the correct amount but your bank account is empty, the issue is likely a failed direct deposit or routing error.**
   - If the pay stub itself shows $0.00 or an unexpectedly low "Net Pay," the issue is rooted in gross pay (missing hours) or deductions (withholding).
2. **Review the "Gross Pay" and "Hours" sections of your statement before looking at deductions.** **Compare the hours listed on the stub against your personal time logs or schedule to ensure no shifts were missed or "unapproved" by a manager.**
   - Check if your "Rate of Pay" has been changed without notice.
   - Verify the pay period dates to ensure this statement covers the days you expected.
3. **Verify every deduction line item, specifically looking for 2026 tax changes.** **Look for entries related to the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), which may have altered how your overtime or tips are taxed and itemized.**
   - Check for new "Garnishment," "Levy," or "Student Loan" entries; federal student loan garnishments resumed in early 2026 for those in default.
   - Ensure your insurance or 401(k) contributions have not increased due to an open enrollment period or system reset.
4. **Send a formal "Payroll Discrepancy Inquiry" in writing to HR or Payroll immediately.** **Include your employee ID, the pay period in question, and a clear request for a line-by-line explanation of why your pay reached zero or the reduced amount.**
   - Ask for an "Off-Cycle Payment" if the error was employer-based (e.g., missed hours or routing mistake) and you cannot wait until the next payday.
   - Request a "Corrected Wage Statement" if the current stub contains factual errors.
5. **Check for state-specific protections regarding wage deductions.** **In many states, employers cannot deduct for business losses, broken equipment, or cash drawer shortages if it drops your pay below the federal or state minimum wage.**
   - If you are in a state like California or New York, you have specific rights to receive an itemized statement within a set timeframe.
6. **Secure your evidence outside of company systems.** **Download and save copies of the suspicious pay stub, your timecard, and your recent communications with HR to a personal device or email.**
   - Maintain a log of every contact, including the time, date, and name of the person you spoke with.
7. **Escalate to a labor agency if the employer does not provide a correction or explanation within 48 hours.** **Contact your State Department of Labor for timing issues (unpaid wages) or the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division (WHD) for minimum wage or overtime violations.**
   - Note that if a court-ordered garnishment is the cause, you must contact the issuing agency (e.g., IRS or Department of Education) rather than your employer to dispute the amount.

## What can wait
- You do not need to hire an attorney before you have received the first written response from payroll.
- You do not need to update your tax forms (W-4) until you understand if the low pay was an error or a correct adjustment based on current laws.
- You do not need to prove "intent" or "theft" yet; focus entirely on the math and the missing funds.

## Important reassurance
Sudden changes to a paycheck are common during payroll system transitions, tax law updates (like the 2026 OBBBA), or the start of a new benefits year. While the missing funds are an immediate stressor, most payroll errors are documented within the system and are reversible once a formal inquiry is opened.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying the source of a pay discrepancy and initiating a formal correction request. It does not cover long-term legal action or collective bargaining procedures.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Wage payment laws vary significantly by state; if your pay is zero due to an error and you are unable to pay for housing or food, notify your local labor commissioner or a legal aid society for emergency assistance.
