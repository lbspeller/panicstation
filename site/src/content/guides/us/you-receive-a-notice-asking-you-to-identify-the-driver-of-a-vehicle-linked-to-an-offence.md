# What to do if… you receive a notice asking you to identify the driver of a vehicle linked to an offence (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as time-sensitive: check that the notice is real, write down the deadline, and respond only through the official court, agency, toll authority, or portal named on the notice. Do not guess who was driving.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore it because you were not driving; some systems begin with the registered owner unless the owner responds correctly.
- Do not pay first if the notice says payment may close your chance to contest, dispute, or deny responsibility.
- Do not name someone unless you can truthfully do so.
- Do not sign a declaration, affidavit, or statement that you cannot stand behind.
- Do not use phone numbers, QR codes, or payment links from a suspicious text or letter until you have checked the official court or agency website.
- Do not assume rules from another city, county, state, or toll program apply to your notice.

## What to do now
1. **Identify exactly who issued the notice.**  
   Look for the court, city, county, police agency, toll authority, administrative hearing office, or federal ticket office. The right response depends on the issuer.

2. **Verify the notice through an official route.**  
   Use the issuing court or agency’s official website to find a citation lookup page, case lookup page, toll invoice page, or main contact number. Check that the citation, notice, invoice, or case number exists before you pay or submit personal information.

3. **Write down every deadline and what the deadline means.**  
   Check whether the notice says the response must be mailed, filed, uploaded, received, paid, or set for a hearing by a certain date. Aim to respond early and keep proof.

4. **Find the response option that matches your situation.**  
   The notice may use words such as affidavit of non-liability, declaration of non-responsibility, dispute, hearing request, contest, not guilty plea, transfer of responsibility, or statement of facts. Use the form or portal the notice tells you to use.

5. **If you were not driving, respond with only what you truthfully know.**  
   If the notice asks who was driving, give only information you can truthfully provide. If you do not know, say that plainly if the form allows it, and do not invent a name to make the notice go away.

6. **If the vehicle was sold, stolen, rented, leased, repossessed, or out of your control, gather proof now.**  
   Useful proof may include a bill of sale, title transfer record, police report number, rental or lease paperwork, insurance claim, toll account record, repair record, or state motor vehicle agency record. Send copies unless the notice specifically requires originals.

7. **If the plate or vehicle details look wrong, compare them carefully.**  
   Check the plate number, state, vehicle make, model, color, date, time, and location against the notice. If the plate may have been stolen, cloned, or misread, contact the issuing agency through an official route and ask what proof they want.

8. **Keep a clean paper trail.**  
   Save the notice, photos, envelopes, screenshots, forms, upload confirmations, certified mail receipts, fax confirmations, emails, and names of people you spoke with. Put the deadline and confirmation number in one place.

9. **Confirm your response was received.**  
   After submitting, check the official portal or contact the clerk, agency, or toll authority using verified contact details. This matters most when the deadline is close.

10. **Get local legal help early if the consequences look serious.**  
   Consider speaking with a licensed attorney in the relevant jurisdiction if the notice mentions a court appearance, criminal charge, warrant, license suspension, registration hold, collections, false statement warning, identity misuse, or plate misuse.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a long argument about the underlying allegation right now; first protect the deadline, the correct form, and proof of submission.
- You do not need to decide every later hearing, settlement, payment, or appeal option today.
- You do not need a perfect story. A short, factual, documented response is safer than a rushed emotional explanation.
- You do not need to confront the possible driver before you understand what the notice actually asks you to do.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an official-looking notice can feel like you are already in trouble. In many cases, the first safe move is procedural: verify the notice, use the official response route, meet the deadline, and keep proof.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to avoid missed deadlines, wrong forms, accidental admissions, or lost proof. Later decisions may need help from a local traffic attorney, court clerk, toll authority, state motor vehicle agency, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, professional, or other specialist advice. U.S. traffic, toll, photo enforcement, and court procedures vary by state and by local agency, so follow the instructions on your notice and the official site for the issuing court or agency.
