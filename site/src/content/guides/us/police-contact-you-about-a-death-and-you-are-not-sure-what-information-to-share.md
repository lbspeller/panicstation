# What to do if… police contact you about a death and you are not sure what information to share (USA)

## Short answer
State clearly that you are invoking your right to remain silent and that you want a lawyer before answering any questions or participating in an interview. Ask if you are free to leave or end the contact; if you are not being detained, physically remove yourself from the interaction.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide any false information, even if you are trying to be helpful / lying to law enforcement is a separate criminal offense.
- Do not speculate or guess about times, locations, motives, or the medical cause of death.
- Do not assume that remaining silent is the same as invoking your right to remain silent / you must verbalize the invocation.
- Do not consent to a search of your home, vehicle, person, or digital devices without a warrant.
- Do not try to "explain the situation" or "clear your name" without legal counsel present.
- Do not sign any documents or adopt any written summaries of your conversation.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm if you are being detained.** Ask: "Am I free to go?" or "Am I free to end this conversation?"
   - If the officer says yes, state that you are not answering questions at this time and leave or close the door.
   - If the officer says no, you are being detained or are under arrest.
2. **State your invocation clearly.** Say: "I am invoking my Fifth Amendment right to remain silent and I want a lawyer."
   - Do not use ambiguous phrases like "I think I might need a lawyer."
   - Once you have said this, stop talking immediately.
3. **Gather the officer's professional details.** Request their name, badge number, department, agency-issued phone number, and the case or incident number.
   - **Write these details down immediately.**
4. **Clarify your status if the officer continues to talk.** Ask: "Am I being questioned as a witness or as a suspect?"
   - Note that police are permitted to be deceptive about your status during an investigation.
5. **Establish boundaries if the police are at your door.** Communicate through the door or step outside and close it behind you.
   - If they ask to enter, say: "I do not consent to a search of my home."
   - If they present a warrant, read the "scope" to see exactly which rooms or items are listed.
6. **Request to schedule any witness interview for a later time.** If you choose to act as a witness, say: "I want to be accurate, but I am in shock. I will schedule an interview through my lawyer for a later date."
7. **Document the encounter once you are alone.** Write down every question asked and exactly what you said before the invocation.
   - **Include the time, location, and names of any other people present.**
8. **Secure your digital devices.** If police ask for your phone or computer, say: "I do not consent to a search of my devices."
   - Do not provide passwords or biometric unlocks (fingerprint / face ID) unless a warrant specifically orders it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the deceased immediately if you are not in a state to do so.
- You do not need to decide whether to provide a formal statement today.
- You do not need to explain why you are requesting a lawyer.
- You do not need to provide access to social media accounts or private messages.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant pressure, guilt, or confusion when contacted by police following a death. Requesting a lawyer and refusing to answer questions immediately are standard procedural steps used to ensure accuracy and protect your rights; these actions are not evidence of guilt. Police investigators are trained to handle silence and the presence of counsel as part of their routine process.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for initial police contact regarding a death investigation in the United States. It does not cover trial testimony, grand jury subpoenas, or long-term legal defense strategies.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding police interactions and the invocation of rights vary by state and local jurisdiction. If you are being investigated for a crime, you should consult a licensed criminal defense attorney immediately.
