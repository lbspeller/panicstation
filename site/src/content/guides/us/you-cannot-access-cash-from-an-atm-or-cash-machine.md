# What to do if… you cannot access cash from an ATM or cash machine (USA)

## Short answer
Stop retrying the same ATM. Use one safer alternative: ask for cash back with a debit purchase, visit a staffed branch, or try one other ATM in a visible, well-lit location. Contact your bank or credit union if your card is being declined or the problem looks wider than one machine.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep retrying the same ATM. Repeated attempts may run into account-specific limits or make later transactions harder to check.
- Do not use an ATM if the card reader, keypad, or faceplate looks loose, crooked, damaged, or altered.
- Do not stay at an ATM that is secluded, poorly lit, or feels unsafe.
- Do not let anyone watch you enter your PIN. Shield the keypad with your hand.
- Do not share your PIN, account details, or login details with anyone offering help.
- Do not post account or personal information publicly while asking about an outage.
- Do not take a credit-card cash advance before checking the fee and interest terms.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether the problem is one ATM or your account.**
   - Check your bank or credit union app for alerts if you can access it safely.
   - Use the customer-service number on the back of your card or on your statement if the app is unavailable.
   - If you already need to buy something, see whether your debit card works for that ordinary purchase.

2. **Ask for cash back with a debit or prepaid purchase.**
   - Ask the store whether cash back is available before you approve the purchase.
   - Ask what amounts are available and whether there is a fee.
   - Cash back is generally offered with a PIN-authenticated debit or prepaid card transaction.

3. **Ask for a teller withdrawal at a staffed branch.**
   - Visit your bank or credit union branch if one is open and reachable.
   - Ask what identification the branch requires.
   - Bring your card and photo ID if they are available.
   - Ask whether a teller withdrawal or another bank-specific access method is available.

4. **Try one other ATM only if it is a sensible option.**
   - Choose an ATM in a visible, well-lit location.
   - Check the card reader, keypad, and faceplate for loose, extra, damaged, or altered parts.
   - Read any fee notice shown at the ATM before you continue.
   - Check your account terms if using another ATM may create a separate fee from your bank or credit union.
   - Leave if the machine looks altered or the location feels unsafe.

5. **Report an ATM error immediately if your account was charged but you received no cash, or the amount was wrong.**
   - Keep the receipt if there is one.
   - Note the ATM location, time, and amount.
   - Call your bank or credit union immediately.
   - If the ATM is not owned by your bank or credit union, also contact the ATM owner or operator.

6. **Contact your bank or credit union if the problem looks like an outage or card block.**
   Ask:
   - whether there is a known outage,
   - whether your card has been blocked,
   - whether a teller withdrawal, another ATM network, or another access method is available.

   Then:
   - check any urgent automatic payments that may be due before access is restored,
   - ask the bank to credit fees caused by the outage,
   - watch your account for unfamiliar transactions.

7. **Use a non-cash fallback for urgent essentials when possible.**
   - Ask whether the person or business can accept a card, check, or electronic payment instead of cash.

## What can wait
- You do not need to close your account, cancel your card, or move all your money unless your bank tells you the card or account may be compromised.
- You do not need to withdraw a large amount as soon as an ATM works again.
- You do not need to solve non-urgent payments today.
- You do not need to file a complaint immediately. Keep basic notes and deal with escalation later if needed.

## Important reassurance
An empty or offline ATM does not by itself mean your money is gone. There are ordinary ways to check what is happening and try another safe access route without rushing.

## Scope note
These are first steps for stabilizing the situation and avoiding expensive or risky decisions. If the problem continues, causes fees, or affects payments, later follow-up with your bank or credit union may be needed.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not financial, legal, or other professional advice. Bank, card-network, ATM-owner, and retailer policies vary. If you feel unsafe while trying to access cash, leave the location and use another option.
