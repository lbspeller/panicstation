# What to do if… you hear loud clicking or grinding from one wheel after a tyre change and you stop to check (USA)

## Short answer
Assume the wheel is not securely mounted and cease all standard driving immediately. If you are stopped on a high-speed roadway or narrow shoulder, prioritize moving away from the vehicle and calling for professional recovery.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t continue driving to see if the noise stops or to "break in" the new tires.
- Don’t stand on the side of the vehicle exposed to moving traffic while inspecting the wheel.
- Don’t attempt to tighten lug nuts with a standard lug wrench if the wheel is already tilted or studs are snapped.
- Don’t crawl under the vehicle or place any part of your body in the wheel well while on a roadside shoulder.
- Don’t move the vehicle if you see missing fasteners, shiny metal shavings around the studs, or a wheel that is not flush against the hub.

## What to do now
1. **Ensure the vehicle is as far from the travel lane as possible before inspecting.** **If you are on a narrow shoulder of a highway or in a high-risk area, do not exit the vehicle to inspect the wheel; stay buckled and call 911 or roadside assistance.**
2. **Perform a visual check for catastrophic wheel-end failure without touching the components.** **Look for missing lug nuts, studs that appear snapped or sheared off, or a wheel that is leaning at an angle relative to the others.**
   - Check for "shiny" metal dust or fresh rust-colored powder around the lug nuts, which indicates the wheel has been moving against the hub.
   - Look for elongated or oval-shaped holes where the lug nuts meet the wheel.
3. **Call for a tow or professional roadside service if any visual abnormality is present.** **State clearly to the operator: "Possible wheel detachment risk following tire service; audible grinding and clicking; vehicle is stationary."**
4. **Contact the service center that performed the tire change immediately.** **Inform them that the noise began immediately after their service and that you have grounded the vehicle for safety.**
   - Ask for their specific procedure for "post-service wheel-end noise."
   - Do not agree to drive the vehicle back to the shop if you have seen missing lug nuts or felt a steering pull.
5. **Move to a safe distance from the roadway if you are waiting for help.** **If there is a guardrail, stand behind it; keep all passengers away from the vehicle and the flow of traffic.**
6. **Document the current state of the wheel with photos if it is safe to do so.** **Take clear images of the lug nuts and the seating of the wheel against the hub to provide evidence of the installation state.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the noise is a loose brake shield or a loose lug nut right now.
- You do not need to check the tire pressure or other wheels if the noise is isolated to one corner.
- You do not need to negotiate the cost of the tow or repair while on the roadside.

## Important reassurance
Hearing a new, loud noise and stopping immediately is the correct response to prevent a wheel-off incident. These noises are often early warning signs that allow you to stabilize the situation before a total mechanical failure occurs.

## Scope note
This guidance is for first-step stabilization and harm reduction immediately following the discovery of post-service wheel noise. It does not replace a professional mechanical inspection or provide instructions for DIY repair.

## Important note
This information is for general safety and does not constitute mechanical or legal advice. In any situation where the vehicle is disabled in a travel lane or on a high-speed shoulder, contact emergency services (911) immediately. Individual tire shop warranties and roadside assistance policies vary by provider.
