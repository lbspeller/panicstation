# What to do if… your luggage is too heavy to manage alone after a sudden platform change or diversion (USA)

## Short answer
Stop somewhere safe with your bags, confirm the new track or platform, and ask official station staff for baggage assistance before you try stairs, rushing, or lifting alone. If you are on Amtrak, ask whether Red Cap or other station assistance is available.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t haul a heavy bag up stairs, onto an escalator, or across a platform gap while rushed; you could get hurt.
- Don’t sprint to a new track or platform with luggage.
- Don’t leave bags unattended while you check the new platform.
- Don’t accept help from someone who is pushy, asks to take your bag away from you, or does not seem safe.
- Don’t block doors, aisles, escalator landings, or platform edges while you regroup.

## What to do now
1. **Make a safe pause point immediately.** Move out of the main flow, such as beside a wall, seating area, staffed counter, or information desk, and keep every bag within arm’s reach.
2. **Confirm the change before moving everything.** Check the nearest departure board, station announcement, operator app, or staffed desk so you know the exact train, track, platform, or gate you now need.
3. **Ask official staff for help right away.** Say: *“My luggage is too heavy to move safely. Can someone help me get to track/platform X?”*
4. **If you are on Amtrak, ask whether Red Cap is available at this station.** Say: *“Is Red Cap available right now, and where do I meet them?”* Amtrak says Red Cap service is offered at select or many major stations, can help with baggage to and from trains, and baggage handled by Red Cap is protected by a claim check.
5. **If Red Cap is not available, ask what this station can provide.** Ask specifically about handcarts, elevators, ramps, baggage assistance, or a staffed route to the train. At Amtrak-staffed stations, staff may be able to help with stairs, and courtesy wheelchairs are available at most staffed stations.
6. **Choose the step-free route if one is available.** Before moving, ask: *“Are the elevators or ramps working, and which route avoids stairs?”*
7. **If you are already on the train and a diversion affects your stop, tell onboard staff as soon as practical.** Say: *“I need a moment or help to get off with heavy luggage.”*
8. **If you may miss boarding because moving the luggage is unsafe, tell staff plainly.** Ask where to wait with your bags and whether rebooking or waiting for the next departure is possible.
9. **If you must move a bag a short distance yourself, make it one controlled move.** Keep the bag close, use both hands where possible, avoid stairs if you can, and stop again if it feels unsafe.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to solve the whole trip right now.
- You do **not** need to repack every bag in public unless it is needed to make one safe move.
- You do **not** need to rush onto the next train if doing that risks injury, dropping a bag, or losing track of your luggage.
- You can sort out onward connections, refunds, complaints, or replacement plans after you and your bags are stable.

## Important reassurance
A sudden platform change or diversion can overwhelm anyone, especially when you are alone with heavy luggage. Pausing, keeping your bags with you, and asking official staff for help is a normal and sensible response.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent injury, keep you with your luggage, and get through the immediate station disruption. Later decisions about rebooking, missed connections, accessibility complaints, or alternate transport may need help from the rail operator, station staff, travel insurer, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Assistance, staffing, elevators, handcarts, and baggage services vary by station and operator. If you are injured or feel unwell, tell station staff and use emergency services if needed.
