# What to do if… your commission or bonus is withheld or missing from your paycheck (USA)

## Short answer
Do not quit, sign anything, or argue in public channels while you are shocked. Get the employer’s reason in writing, save the commission or bonus plan, and compare the written promise with what was actually paid.

## Do not do these things
- Do not quit on the spot before saving your plan documents, pay records, and the employer’s written explanation.
- Do not rely only on a verbal “we’ll fix it next check” without an amount, a date, and written confirmation.
- Do not sign a new plan, acknowledgment, release, or waiver under pressure just to get paid.
- Do not accept retroactive rule changes without asking for the exact written rule and the date it took effect.
- Do not vent publicly or in broad workplace channels; keep messages factual and targeted.
- Do not stop showing up for work as a tactic; it can create a separate attendance or discipline issue.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm whether the payment was delayed, reduced, or denied.**  
   Email payroll, HR, or your manager and ask for the amount withheld, the pay period or sales period it covers, and the exact plan, policy, or clause they are relying on.

2. **Save your commission or bonus evidence now.**  
   Download or screenshot your offer letter, commission plan, bonus plan, quota or eligibility rules, rate tables, target documents, emails, portal pages, CRM or sales reports, approval messages, pay statements, and direct deposit records.

3. **Ask for the calculation, not just a promise to fix it.**  
   Ask for an itemized calculation showing the sales, quota, rate, threshold, adjustment, holdback, chargeback, or eligibility reason used to calculate the commission or bonus. Employers covered by federal wage law generally must keep wage-and-hour records, but the exact pay-statement rules can vary by state.

4. **If you are hourly or overtime-eligible, flag the overtime issue.**  
   Ask payroll to confirm whether the commission or nondiscretionary bonus affects your regular rate of pay and whether any overtime recalculation will be made when the payment is corrected.

5. **Send one short written correction request.**  
   State what you expected, why you expected it, what you received, and what documents support your position. Ask whether the correction will be made by off-cycle payment or by the next payroll date, and ask how it will appear on your pay record.

6. **If they call it “discretionary,” ask what they mean.**  
   Ask them to confirm in writing whether no bonus was awarded, or whether a bonus was awarded but reduced, delayed, or withheld. Ask for the reason and the plan language they are relying on.

7. **Use the right complaint route if they will not correct it.**  
   If the issue may involve minimum wage, overtime, retaliation, or federal wage records, contact the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division and ask whether you can file a complaint. If the issue is mainly a commission or bonus promise under a plan or contract, ask your state labor office whether it accepts wage claims for commissions or bonuses.

8. **If the job has ended, check final pay separately.**  
   Federal law does not generally require an immediate final paycheck, but some states may. If the regular payday for your last pay period has passed and the commission or bonus is still missing, contact the Wage and Hour Division or your state labor office.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to sue, go public, or leave your job.
- You do not need a perfect legal theory right now; first get the plan terms, the numbers, and the written reason.
- You can wait to involve an attorney until you know the amount at stake, whether a state wage-claim route may apply, and whether you are being asked to sign something.

## Important reassurance
A withheld commission or bonus can feel destabilizing because it affects money you planned around. A short written record, saved documents, and a clear calculation request give you a stronger position without forcing a major decision today.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: stabilize, document, and find the correct complaint path. Later decisions may need specialist help because commission and bonus rights depend heavily on your state, your written plan, your pay status, and whether the issue is wage-and-hour law or a contract dispute.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, employment, therapeutic, or other professional advice. The FLSA does not require employers to offer commissions, and commission or bonus rights can vary widely by state and by the wording of your plan, offer letter, or agreement. If you are being pressured to sign a waiver, release, or retroactive change, consider getting independent advice before signing.
