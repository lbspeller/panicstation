# What to do if… a gas appliance keeps going out and you’re worried the ventilation or supply is unsafe (USA)

## Short answer
Turn the appliance off immediately and stop all attempts to relight or reset it. If you detect a rotten egg smell, hear hissing, hear a carbon monoxide alarm, or feel unwell, evacuate everyone and call 911 or your gas utility from a safe distance.

## Do not do these things
- Do not continue relighting the pilot or repeatedly resetting the furnace, boiler, or water heater.
- Do not use lighters, candles, matches, or any open flames in the vicinity.
- Do not operate light switches, flashlights, thermostats, or garage door openers if you suspect a leak.
- Do not use a telephone or cell phone while inside the building if a leak is suspected.
- Do not attempt to "air out" the room and continue using the appliance.
- Do not block or tape over combustion air openings or vents.
- Do not attempt to repair gas lines, adjust regulators, or modify venting yourself.
- Do not ignore symptoms like headache, dizziness, nausea, or lethargy, even if they seem mild.

## What to do now
1. **Shut down the appliance immediately using the thermostat or the control knob.** Stop any further attempts to force the unit to start or stay lit.
2. **Scan the area for danger signals including the smell of sulfur, hissing sounds, or visible soot.** 
   - Check for physical symptoms: headache, dizziness, weakness, nausea, vomiting, chest pain, confusion, or sudden lethargy.
   - Look for secondary signs: yellow or flickering flames (instead of blue), soot marks on the appliance, or dying house plants.
3. **Evacuate the building immediately if any danger signal or alarm is present.**
   - Leave the door open as you exit to help dissipate gas or fumes.
   - Take all people and pets with you to a safe location upwind of the building.
4. **Call 911 or your local gas utility’s emergency line from a safe distance once outside.**
   - Do not assume someone else has called.
   - Stay at the safe location until emergency responders or utility technicians confirm the building is safe to re-enter.
5. **Keep the appliance off and arrange for a professional safety inspection if no immediate leak is found.**
   - Contact your gas utility first; many provide free safety checks for "red-tag" situations where an appliance is failing safety thresholds.
   - If directed by the utility, hire a licensed HVAC technician or plumber to verify the flue, venting, and gas pressure.
6. **Notify your landlord or property manager in writing if you are a tenant.**
   - State clearly that the appliance has been shut off due to safety concerns and requires a certified inspection before it is used again.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific mechanical failure, such as a faulty thermocouple or blocked flue, right now.
- You do not need to decide between repair or replacement during the initial safety stabilization.
- You do not need to investigate the gas meter or external pipes yourself.

## Important reassurance
Treating a repeating safety shutdown as an emergency is a correct and protective action. Safety mechanisms in modern gas appliances are designed to fail "off" when conditions are unsafe, and trusting that shutdown prevents fires and poisonings.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for suspected gas or ventilation failures in the USA only. It does not replace the instructions of 911 dispatchers or on-site utility professionals.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and is not a substitute for professional mechanical or medical advice. If you suspect carbon monoxide poisoning, seek emergency medical care immediately.
