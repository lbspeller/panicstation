# What to do if… a funeral provider says a body must be moved today due to capacity limits (USA)

## Short answer
Verify whether the medical examiner or the funeral home currently has legal custody, request all transport plans and costs in writing, and authorize only a "transfer for temporary storage" until you have selected a final provider.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not** sign a full service contract or purchase "packages" solely because you feel rushed by storage constraints.
- **Do not** agree to embalming as a solution to capacity issues unless you specifically want that service; refrigeration is the standard legal alternative for temporary storage.
- **Do not** allow a transfer to occur without a confirmed destination address and a written itemized estimate of the transfer and storage fees.
- **Do not** assume the funeral home can hold the remains as collateral; they cannot legally withhold a body for unpaid debts or to force a service selection.
- **Do not** rely on verbal promises regarding costs or destination; ensure every detail is sent via email or text.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the entity that currently has legal custody of the remains.**  
   **Ask the provider: "Is this case still under the jurisdiction of the medical examiner/coroner, or has the body been released?"**  
   - If the Medical Examiner (ME) has jurisdiction, they control when and where the body moves.  
   - If released, the legal next of kin (or designated agent) has the right to direct where the remains are taken.

2. **Demand a written summary of the proposed move before granting authorization.**  
   **Instruct the funeral home to provide the following details in writing (email or text):**  
   - The specific reason for the "today" deadline.  
   - The name and physical address of the facility where the body will be stored.  
   - A list of the specific fees for transfer and daily storage.  
   - A statement that no irreversible services (like embalming) will be performed.

3. **Verify the provider's General Price List (GPL) digitally.**  
   **Access the provider’s website to locate their mandatory online price disclosures.**  
   - Under 2026 FTC regulations, funeral providers with a website must make their General Price List available online.  
   - Review the "Transfer of Remains" and "Receiving Remains" sections to ensure the quoted price matches their public list.

4. **Execute a controlled transfer to a different provider if you feel pressured.**  
   **Contact an alternative funeral home and ask if they have refrigeration space and what their "transfer of remains" fee is.**  
   - Once confirmed, notify the first provider in writing: "I am authorizing [New Funeral Home] to pick up the remains today. Please have the body ready for release."

5. **Formalize the refusal of embalming.**  
   **State clearly: "I do not authorize embalming at this time. Provide your refrigeration options and costs for temporary storage."**  
   - If they claim state law requires it, ask them to cite the specific statute; most states allow refrigeration as an alternative for a set period (usually 24–72 hours before a decision is mandatory).

6. **Establish a 24-hour "cooling off" period for service decisions.**  
   **Tell the provider: "The move is authorized for storage only. I will not be making decisions on caskets, ceremonies, or disposition until tomorrow."**  
   - This separates the physical logistics (the move) from the financial commitment (the funeral).

## What can wait
- **You do not need to choose a casket or urn today**; focus only on getting the body to a safe, refrigerated location.
- **You do not need to pay the full funeral bill today**; transfer and storage fees are usually the only immediate costs.
- **You do not need to finalize the obituary or service dates** while the body is being moved for capacity reasons.
- **You do not need to resolve family disputes about the final method of disposition** (burial vs. cremation) just to facilitate a storage transfer.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel panicked when a provider uses "must move today" language. This is often a logistical reality for them, but it does not diminish your legal right to choose where your loved one goes. Taking ten minutes to get details in writing is a safe and appropriate way to handle this, even in an "emergency" move.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for remains custody and consumer right protection in the USA. It does not cover long-term funeral planning, international shipping of remains, or legal litigation for funeral home negligence.

## Important note
Funeral laws vary by state. While federal FTC rules provide price transparency, the order of "next of kin" and specific refrigeration timelines are governed by state boards. This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice.
