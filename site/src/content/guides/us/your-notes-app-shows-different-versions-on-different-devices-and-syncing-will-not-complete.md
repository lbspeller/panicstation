# What to do if… your notes app shows different versions on different devices and syncing will not complete (USA)

## Short answer
Remotely lock or erase the device immediately using another computer or phone, then contact your wireless carrier to suspend the SIM card and protect your mobile number.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attempt to track the device to a specific physical location or confront a suspected thief.
- Don’t click links in text messages or emails claiming your phone has been found; these are often phishing scams designed to steal your passcode.
- Don’t assume biometrics are enough if you suspect the thief saw you enter your passcode.
- Don’t wait to report the theft to your bank if you see unauthorized activity.
- Don’t disable "Find My" or remove the device from your account until insurance claims are processed, as this can disable Activation Lock.

## What to do now
1. **Lock or erase the phone remotely from a different device or computer.** 
   - For iPhone: Go to iCloud.com/find or use the Find My app on another Apple device to mark the phone as "Lost" or select "Erase This Device."
   - For Android: Go to google.com/android/find or use the Find My Device app to "Secure device" (lock) or "Erase device."
   - Note: The device must be powered on and connected to Wi-Fi or cellular data for the command to execute; if it is offline, the command will queue until it connects.

2. **Call your wireless carrier to suspend your service and block the SIM card.** 
   - Request that they place a "hold" or "suspend" on your account to prevent the thief from receiving your 2-factor authentication (SMS) codes.
   - Ask the carrier to blacklist the device's IMEI number to prevent it from being used on other networks.
   - Set or update your carrier account PIN / passphrase to prevent unauthorized "port-out" or SIM-swap attacks.

3. **Secure your primary email account using a trusted computer.** 
   - Change your email password immediately, as most other account resets are sent to your inbox.
   - Navigate to the "Security" or "Devices" section of your email settings and select "Sign out of all other sessions."
   - Verify that your recovery phone number and backup email address have not been changed.

4. **Update the passwords for your Apple ID or Google Account.** 
   - Change the password and ensure that the stolen device is no longer listed as a "Trusted Device" for receiving security codes.
   - If you use a password manager that was logged in on the phone, change your master password and de-authorize the stolen device.

5. **Notify your financial institutions and mobile payment providers.** 
   - Call your bank or credit card issuers to report the theft if you used Apple Pay, Google Pay, or have banking apps installed.
   - Ask them to "suspend tokens" for mobile payments associated with the stolen device and review recent transactions.

6. **File a report with your local police department.** 
   - Obtain a case or incident number, which is often required by insurance companies and wireless carriers for fraud investigations.
   - Provide the device's serial number or IMEI if you have it (often found on the original box or your carrier's website).

7. **Report identity theft to the Federal Trade Commission if you see unauthorized account changes.** 
   - Go to IdentityTheft.gov to create a recovery plan if the thief has successfully accessed your personal information or opened new accounts.
   - Consider placing a "fraud alert" or "credit freeze" on your credit reports at Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.

8. **Document the details of the theft for your records.** 
   - Note the exact time, location, and the status of the device (e.g., was it unlocked when taken).
   - List which critical apps were logged in (banking, work email, social media) to help prioritize which passwords to change next.

## What can wait
- Purchasing a replacement device or hardware accessories.
- Restoring your data from a cloud backup to a new phone.
- Changing passwords for low-risk accounts (streaming services, gaming accounts) that do not have payment info saved.
- Deciding whether to change your phone number permanently.

## Important reassurance
Digital theft is highly time-sensitive; once you have remotely locked the device and suspended your SIM card, the window for a thief to access your personal accounts closes significantly. Panic is a common response, but following these steps systematically reduces the risk of long-term identity or financial loss.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for digital security after a physical theft. It does not cover physical recovery of the device, police investigations, or long-term insurance claim disputes.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal, financial, or technical advice. The effectiveness of remote locking depends on device settings and network connectivity. Always prioritize your physical safety over the recovery of hardware. If you suspect your Social Security number or high-value financial assets are compromised, contact the relevant authorities and institutions immediately.
