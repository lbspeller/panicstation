# What to do if… your debit card PIN is changed and you did not request it (USA)

## Short answer
Lock the debit card immediately, then contact your bank or card issuer using your official banking app or a trusted number from the card or bank website. Treat the PIN change as possible account compromise until the bank has blocked access and checked recent activity.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try the new PIN at an ATM or store to test it.
- Do not click links in a PIN-change text or email.
- Do not call a phone number sent in a text, email, or pop-up.
- Do not share one-time codes or approve push prompts you did not start.
- Do not keep using the card while the bank checks it.
- Do not wait for your next statement before reporting it.

## What to do now
1. **Lock or disable the debit card now.**  
   Use your bank’s official app or website if you can. If you cannot lock it yourself, call the bank and ask them to block the debit card, ATM withdrawals, and any card-not-present use while they investigate.

2. **Tell the bank exactly what happened.**  
   Say: “My debit card PIN was changed and I did not request it.” Ask the bank to cancel and replace the card, confirm whether the replacement will have a new card number, and check for recent PIN changes, new device logins, and changes to your phone number, email, mailing address, or security settings.

3. **Ask the bank to check mobile wallets and linked services.**  
   Ask whether the card was added to Apple Pay, Google Pay, Samsung Wallet, a merchant wallet, a payment app, or any other digital wallet. Ask them to remove anything you do not recognize.

4. **Review recent activity and report unauthorized items immediately.**  
   Look for ATM withdrawals, debit purchases, online payments, transfers, or balance changes you did not authorize. Ask the bank to open an unauthorized electronic fund transfer or debit card fraud dispute, even if you do not yet have every detail.

5. **Report fast and ask the bank what deadline applies.**  
   U.S. debit-card protections can depend on how quickly you notify the bank. Regulation E includes timing rules such as notifying within two business days after learning that an access device such as a card, code, or PIN was lost or stolen, and notifying within 60 days after a statement showing an unauthorized transfer was sent. The safest first step is to report now and ask the bank to record the date and time of your notice.

6. **Secure the routes that could have allowed the PIN change.**  
   From official apps or websites only, change your online banking password and the password for the email account tied to your bank. Turn on stronger sign-in protection if available. If you lost cell service, received a SIM-change or port-out notice, or stopped receiving security codes, contact your mobile carrier from an official number and ask about possible SIM swap or port-out fraud.

7. **Save a simple record.**  
   Write down the date and time you noticed the PIN change, when you contacted the bank, who you spoke with, what was blocked, and any dispute or case number. Take screenshots of suspicious alerts or transactions before they disappear, but do not delay reporting in order to collect screenshots.

8. **Consider broader identity-theft steps after the card is blocked.**  
   If you think your personal information is being used beyond this one debit card, consider placing a fraud alert or credit freeze. A credit freeze usually needs to be placed separately with each major credit bureau.

9. **Report online scam contact if it happened.**  
   If someone tricked you into sharing a code, approving a prompt, downloading software, or visiting a fake bank page, you can report the incident to the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center and keep the confirmation with your bank records.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to close the whole bank account; first stop card access and open the dispute.
- You do not need perfect documentation before reporting; a clear statement that you did not request the PIN change is enough to start.
- You do not need to contact every agency at once; once the card is locked and the bank has recorded the dispute, the remaining steps can be handled one at a time.
- You do not need to argue with a suspicious caller or texter; end the contact and use an official route to the bank.

## Important reassurance
A PIN change you did not request is a valid reason to act quickly. You are not expected to know how it happened before you call the bank; your first job is only to stop access, report unauthorized activity, and protect the accounts used for resets.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation. Later decisions, such as written dispute forms, police reports, affidavits, credit monitoring, or account closure, may need help from your bank, a consumer protection agency, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or other professional advice. Bank policies and account facts vary. If you feel pressured during any call or message exchange, end it and contact your bank again using a trusted route.
