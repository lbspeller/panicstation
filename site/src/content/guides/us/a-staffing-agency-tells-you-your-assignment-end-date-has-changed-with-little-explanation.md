# What to do if… a staffing agency tells you your assignment end date has changed with little explanation (USA)

## Short answer
Send a neutral email requesting a written summary of the allegations and clarification on whether the meeting is an investigatory interview that could lead to discipline. If you are union-represented, formally invoke your Weingarten rights before the meeting begins.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess or speculate about the allegations during the meeting; only provide facts within your direct knowledge.
- Do not sign any "meeting summary," "statement," or "admission" on the spot without taking it away for a thorough review.
- Do not delete, modify, or move any company files, emails, or messages, as this may be flagged as "destruction of evidence" or "interference."
- Do not record the meeting secretly, as this may violate state "two-party consent" laws or company policy; ask to take written notes instead.
- Do not confront potential witnesses or coworkers you suspect made a complaint, as this can be classified as retaliation or intimidation.

## What to do now
1. **Send a neutral email to the meeting organizer asking for the agenda and nature of the meeting.** Use clear language to establish a paper trail.
   - Ask for a written summary of the specific conduct or incident being investigated, including the relevant timeframe.
   - Ask for the names and titles of everyone who will be attending the meeting.
   - Request a copy of the company policy or rule that is allegedly involved.
   - Ask if the company will provide relevant documents (such as time cards or emails) for you to review during the meeting.
2. **Determine if you have a legal right to representation based on your employment status.**
   - If you are a union-represented employee (private or public sector), you have Weingarten rights under the NLRA or 5 U.S.C. § 7114.
   - If you are a public safety officer or firefighter, check if your state has a "Procedural Bill of Rights" (such as POBR in CA) which often mandates written notice before interviews.
   - If you are a non-union private sector employee, you generally do not have a statutory right to a representative, but you may still request one under company policy.
3. **Formally invoke your Weingarten rights if you are in a union.**
   - Say: **“If this discussion could in any way lead to my being disciplined or terminated, or affect my personal working conditions, I respectfully request that my union representative, officer, or steward be present at this meeting. Until my representative arrives, I choose not to participate in this discussion.”**
   - If the employer denies the request and insists you answer, repeat the request and note the refusal in your own records; do not walk out unless instructed, but remain silent on the investigation questions.
4. **Prepare a private, "facts-only" timeline of your recent work activities.**
   - Write down dates, times, and names of witnesses for any recent projects or incidents you suspect might be the subject of the meeting.
   - Note any recent positive feedback or performance reviews that might contradict sudden allegations.
5. **Slow the pace of the meeting and ask for definitions before answering questions.**
   - **Ask the interviewer to state the specific allegation on the record at the very start of the meeting.**
   - If the questions are vague, ask for clarification: "Can you tell me the specific date and time of the incident you are referring to?"
   - Take your own detailed notes of every question asked and the names of the people asking them.
6. **Use "safe" phrases to avoid accidental misstatements during questioning.**
   - If you are unsure, say: "I do not recall the specifics of that event right now; I would need to review my records."
   - If you need time, say: "I want to be accurate; I will check my notes/emails and follow up with a written response."
   - Avoid "always" or "never" statements which can be easily used to catch you in a contradiction.
7. **Send a "contemporaneous" recap email immediately after the meeting concludes.**
   - **Summarize what you were told the allegations were and who was present.**
   - List any requests you made that were denied (e.g., "I requested a written summary of the complaint, which was declined").
   - Correct any factual errors the interviewer made during the meeting while the conversation is fresh.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a formal complaint or lawsuit today.
- You do not need to provide a full, detailed rebuttal to the allegations in the first meeting; you can wait until you have seen the evidence.
- You do not need to decide on your long-term employment status or whether you should resign immediately.

## Important reassurance
Feeling startled or anxious when called into a sudden, vague meeting is a natural physiological response. Taking a moment to ask for written details and invoking your rights (if applicable) is not an admission of guilt; it is a standard procedural protection used by employees in all sectors.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the initial meeting. It does not cover long-term legal strategy, arbitration, or litigation. Future steps depend on whether the investigation results in a formal disciplinary charge or a performance improvement plan.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Employment rights in the USA vary significantly based on union status, public vs. private sector employment, and specific state laws. If you are union-represented, your shop steward or union representative is your primary resource for immediate assistance.
