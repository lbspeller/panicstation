# What to do if… a loved one has died in another city and you need to travel urgently (USA)

## Short answer
Confirm the death through an official point of contact, write down the case or reference details, and choose the safest realistic travel option. Let one trusted person handle repeated calls or updates while you get yourself to the right place.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t drive long distance if you are numb, shaking, sleep-deprived, or unable to focus; use a safer option or travel with someone.
- Don’t pay “urgent fees” or give payment details to an unsolicited caller; pause and verify through the hospital, police department, medical examiner, coroner, or funeral provider using a number you looked up yourself.
- Don’t assume you must decide burial, cremation, transportation, or services today; many decisions can wait until you speak to the office handling the death and a funeral provider you choose.
- Don’t post detailed travel plans, names, locations, or payment needs publicly; it can draw scams and unwanted pressure.
- Don’t start closing accounts, signing legal paperwork, or taking property if you are not sure who has authority; ask the official contact what documentation is needed.

## What to do now
1. **Get the official facts and a reference number.**  
   Ask the caller: “Where is my loved one now? Which office is handling this? Is there a case, incident, or report number? Who is the assigned contact, and what is their direct number?” Write down the person’s name, title, office, phone number, address, and the time of the call.

2. **Verify the contact before acting on payment or personal-information requests.**  
   Look up the hospital, police department, medical examiner, or coroner through an official directory or website. Call the main number and ask to be transferred to the person or unit you were given.

3. **Ask exactly where you should go and what you need to bring.**  
   Ask whether you should go to a hospital, medical examiner or coroner office, police department, funeral home, or another location. Ask whether an appointment is needed, whether viewing or identification is required or optional, and what ID or documents you should bring. Bring photo ID and any basic identifying details you already have, but do not text or email a Social Security number unless an official office tells you how to provide it safely.

4. **Make a shock-safe travel plan.**  
   Ask someone to come with you, drive you, or meet you at the airport, train station, or destination. Pack only essentials: ID, phone, charger, payment card, any medication, water, a snack, a warm layer, and the written contact details. If you must drive, plan breaks before you leave and stop if your attention drops.

5. **Choose one person to manage calls and updates.**  
   Give them a short message to repeat: “We have been notified and are traveling. We will share confirmed details when we can.” Ask them to write down names, phone numbers, and requests instead of putting you on every call.

6. **If a medical examiner or coroner is involved, ask about the release process.**  
   Offices vary by state and county, and delays can happen. Usually, the person with legal authority chooses a funeral home or mortuary and signs authorization before release when the office is able to release the person. If you are not sure whether you are the legal next of kin, say that clearly and ask what documentation they need.

7. **Do not let funeral decisions be rushed.**  
   When you contact a funeral provider, ask for the itemized General Price List. Under the FTC Funeral Rule, you can generally choose only the goods and services you want, with some exceptions. A simple script is: “Please send me the General Price List. I cannot decide today, and I will call back.”

8. **Ask about personal property before you travel or leave the building.**  
   Ask where belongings are held, what ID or paperwork is required, who is allowed to collect them, and whether the hospital, police, medical examiner, coroner, or funeral provider controls the release.

9. **Send a minimal work message if you need to leave immediately.**  
   “A close family death occurred out of town. I need emergency time off and I am traveling today. I will update you tomorrow.” Company policies vary, so keep it brief and avoid negotiating details while you are in shock.

## What can wait
- Deciding on services, timing, obituary wording, flowers, clothing, music, or extended family roles.
- Sorting the estate, debts, insurance, taxes, benefits, and account closures.
- Ordering many certified copies of the death certificate before you know what is needed; the state vital records office where the death occurred, or the funeral provider, can usually tell you the process.
- Replying to every message or explaining everything to everyone.
- Making long-term travel, housing, or family decisions beyond getting safely to the next official contact.

## Important reassurance
Feeling blank, unreal, shaky, or oddly calm can happen after sudden death news. You are not required to make every decision now. The next few hours are about safe travel, verified information, and avoiding pressure.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for the urgent travel window. Procedures vary by state, county, office, and whether the death is handled by a hospital physician, medical examiner, coroner, police department, or funeral provider. Later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, employment, or other professional advice. If you are unsure who has authority, or if anyone is pressuring you to sign or pay immediately, pause and verify through the official office and a funeral provider you choose.
