# What to do if… your cane, walker, or other walking aid breaks while you are out (USA)

## Short answer
Stop moving on the broken aid if you can. Get to the nearest safe pause point, sit or brace, and switch to asking for help and arranging a safer ride instead of risking a fall.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep walking on a cracked, bent, loose, or collapsing cane, walker, or rollator.
- Do not rush across traffic, stairs, curbs, escalators, or uneven ground to “just get there.”
- Do not let someone lift or carry you unless emergency responders or trained staff say it is needed.
- Do not force a jammed walker joint, brake, wheel, or lock if it is stuck halfway.
- Do not stand in a doorway, road edge, platform edge, or parking-lot lane to get attention.

## What to do now
1. **Get to the nearest safer pause point.** Use the closest bench, wall, lobby, counter, station entrance, clinic desk, store entrance, or other flat place. If moving feels unsafe, stay where you are and ask someone to bring help to you.

2. **Check whether this is now a medical emergency.** Call 911, or ask someone nearby to call, if you fell and cannot move safely, hit your head and feel confused or faint, have severe pain, have a possible head or neck injury, have chest pain, have trouble breathing, or have bleeding that will not stop.

3. **Move the broken aid only if it is safe to do so.** Put it beside a wall, under a bench, or with nearby staff so it does not trip you or someone else. If carrying it would make you unstable, leave it where staff can identify it.

4. **Ask for one clear piece of help at a time.** Say: “My walking aid broke. I need a chair and a safe place to wait.” Then ask for help calling a pickup, taxi, rideshare, building security, station staff, customer service, or a nearby clinic desk.

5. **Choose the lowest-risk way to continue.**
   - **Trusted pickup:** call or text a friend, family member, neighbor, coworker, or caregiver. Send your exact location, entrance name, nearby landmark, and whether you can stand.
   - **Taxi or rideshare:** request pickup from a sheltered, flat, well-lit spot if possible. Message or tell the driver that you need extra time to get seated and cannot walk far.
   - **Public transit staff:** tell the operator, station agent, or customer service staff that your mobility aid broke and you need a safe place to sit, extra time, and help reaching an accessible exit or pickup point.
   - **Paratransit if you are enrolled:** call your local dispatch number and say your walking aid broke while you are out and you need advice about a ride change or safe pickup.

6. **If you have no safe ride option, try 211.** In many areas, dialing 211 connects you with local community services. Ask specifically about accessible transportation, disability transportation, senior transportation, medical transport, or local ride programs, and ask what is realistic today.

7. **If you are dealing with public transportation, describe what you need in plain words.** You can say: “I need a safe place to sit, extra boarding time, and help reaching a safe pickup point because my walking aid broke.” You do not need to use legal wording to ask for a reasonable change to usual procedures.

8. **Cancel or delay the destination if continuing is unsafe.** Call the appointment, workplace, school, friend, or service you were going to and say: “My mobility aid broke and I am arranging safe transport. I may be late or need to reschedule.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide what replacement cane, walker, rollator, or mobility aid to buy right now.
- You do not need to keep the original plan if getting there would risk a fall.
- Repair paperwork, refunds, complaints, and replacement research can wait until you are somewhere safe.
- You do not need to explain everything to everyone; “my walking aid broke and I need to sit” is enough.

## Important reassurance
A broken walking aid can make you feel exposed, rushed, and stranded. Stopping and asking for practical help is a reasonable safety step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce the chance of injury and help you reach a safer place. Medical assessment, equipment repair, replacement choices, transit complaints, insurance questions, and longer-term mobility planning can come later with appropriate specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you may be injured, feel faint, or cannot move safely, call 911 or ask someone nearby to call for you.
