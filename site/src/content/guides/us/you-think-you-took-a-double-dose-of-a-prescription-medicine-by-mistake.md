# What to do if… you think you took a double dose of a prescription medicine by mistake (USA)

## Short answer
Do not take another dose until you have checked what to do. Call the Poison Help line at 1-800-222-1222 now for specific instructions; call 911 if the person has severe symptoms or cannot be awakened normally.

## Do not do these things
- Do not take another dose "just in case" while you are unsure.
- Do not try to fix it by doubling, halving, skipping, or moving future doses unless Poison Help or a clinician tells you to.
- Do not add over-the-counter medicines that might overlap, such as pain relievers, sleep aids, or cold and flu medicines, until you have checked the ingredients.
- Do not try to vomit, take activated charcoal, or use an "antidote" unless Poison Help or emergency clinicians tell you to.
- Do not drink alcohol, drive, or do hazardous tasks if you feel sleepy, dizzy, confused, shaky, faint, or unwell.
- If your prescription label warns not to stop suddenly, do not make your own changes; get urgent guidance on the next dose.

## What to do now
1. **Check the dose record for no more than a few minutes.** Look at the prescription label, blister pack, pill organizer, reminder app, pharmacy text, or written log. If you still cannot be sure, treat it as a possible double dose.
2. **Put the medicine container in front of you.** Keep the bottle, box, or blister pack with the prescription label visible so you can read the exact name, strength, directions, and prescriber or pharmacy details.
3. **Write down the key facts before you call, if you can.** Note the medicine name, strength, number of tablets or capsules, time taken, other medicines taken today, age, approximate weight, health conditions, and any symptoms.
4. **Call the Poison Help line now.** Call 1-800-222-1222 from anywhere in the United States and many U.S. territories. Say that this may be a double dose of a prescription medicine and read the details from the label and your notes.
5. **Use webPOISONCONTROL only if it fits the situation.** It can give online guidance for many poison exposures, but call Poison Help instead if the person is pregnant, younger than 6 months, older than 79 years, exposed to more than one product or substance, struggling with self-harm, very drowsy, unwell, or developing symptoms.
6. **Call 911 now for emergency symptoms.** Call 911 if the person collapses, has a seizure, has trouble breathing, cannot be awakened normally, has chest pain, has severe confusion, or is rapidly getting worse.
7. **Treat some medicines as urgent even if symptoms are mild.** Contact Poison Help immediately for medicines that can affect breathing, alertness, heart rhythm, blood pressure, blood sugar, blood clotting, or seizure control, including opioids, sedatives, insulin, diabetes pills, blood thinners, heart medicines, and seizure medicines.
8. **Follow the next-dose plan you are given.** Poison Help may tell you to monitor at home, change the timing of the next dose, contact your prescriber or pharmacist, or seek urgent care, depending on the medicine and dose.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to change medicines, switch pharmacies, or rebuild your medication routine right now.
- You do not need to make up for doses today unless Poison Help, your pharmacist, or a clinician tells you to.
- Prevention steps, such as a pill box, alarm, or written dose log, can wait until after you have clear safety instructions.

## Important reassurance
Medication mix-ups happen, especially when routines change, labels look similar, or you are tired or distracted. Calling Poison Help is a normal, practical step and does not mean you have done something wrong.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The safest next move depends on the exact medicine, dose, timing, symptoms, age, weight, and health history, and later decisions may need help from Poison Help, a pharmacist, a prescriber, or urgent care.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, or a substitute for professional care. If symptoms change, you are worried, or you cannot reach Poison Help or your poison center promptly, seek urgent medical care.
