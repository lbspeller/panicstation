# What to do if… someone keeps pressuring you to send sexual voice notes or videos you do not want to record (USA)
## Short answer

Do not send the recording. Leave the live conversation for now. Consider contacting someone you trust if that feels safe.

## Do not do these things

- Do not send a recording to make the requests stop.
- Do not keep debating after you have said no.
- Do not meet the person alone to discuss it if the pressure feels threatening or controlling.
- Do not send sexual content because you are worried they will get angry, leave, expose you, or keep asking.
- Do not assume the pressure is harmless because it is online or involves audio rather than video.

## What to do now

1. **End the live pressure.** Leave the chat, mute notifications, or put your phone down. A brief reply such as “I’m not sending that” is enough if you choose to reply.

2. **Move to a safer pause.** If the person is nearby, threatening you, or making you feel unsafe, move toward a safer place or contact someone who can stay with you. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.

3. **Consider contacting one trusted person.** If it feels safe, say that someone is pressuring you to make sexual recordings you do not want to make. You can ask them to stay with you while you decide what feels safest for the next few hours.

4. **If it may help later, consider saving a copy of the messages before deleting anything.**

5. **Use platform safety tools when it feels safe.** You can mute, restrict, block, or use a platform’s reporting option. If you think the person may be monitoring your device or account activity, consider using a safer device before changing settings or contacting support.

6. **Contact specialist support.** RAINN offers support through its National Sexual Assault Hotline. If the pressure is connected to a dating relationship or other relationship abuse, the National Domestic Violence Hotline can help you think through safer next steps.

7. **If you are under 18, try to contact a safe trusted adult promptly.** This could be a parent, caregiver, school counselor, teacher, or another adult you trust. NCMEC’s CyberTipline is available for reports of suspected online child sexual exploitation.

8. **If a sexual image or video has already been shared, see the current FTC guidance page.** It explains platform-removal requests and routes for further help. You can ask a trusted person or support service to go through it with you.

## What can wait

You do not need to decide right now whether to make a police report, confront the person again, or make a big decision about the relationship. You do not need to reread every message repeatedly to decide whether the pressure was serious enough.

## Important reassurance

Pressure to make sexual recordings you do not want is not your fault. You are allowed to stop replying, leave the conversation, ask for help, and take the situation seriously even if you did not send anything.

## Scope note

These are first steps only. They are intended to help you stabilize the immediate situation and avoid making it harder in the next few hours. Later decisions about reporting, safety, or the relationship may need specialist support.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, crisis counseling, or other professional advice. Rules and platform options can vary by state and platform. You do not have to decide everything now.
