# What to do if… you are asked to coordinate a rapid handover from hospital to hospice and information is missing (USA)

## Short answer
Stop the transfer until you have the printed medication reconciliation, the physical portable code status order (POLST / MOLST or Out-of-Hospital DNR), and a confirmed 24/7 contact number for the receiving hospice. Federal law (42 CFR § 482.43) requires the hospital to provide all necessary medical information at the time of discharge.

## Do not do these things
- Do not allow the patient to leave the hospital without the physical, original portable medical order (POLST / MOLST) if one exists; digital copies may not be honored by EMS or transport.
- Do not accept a verbal medication list or a "summary" that does not include dosages, frequencies, and the "last dose given" time.
- Do not assume the hospital and hospice have shared the same medication list; discrepancies are common during rapid handovers.
- Do not transport the patient until you have verified that the hospice has confirmed the delivery time for "comfort meds" or that the hospital is providing a bridging supply.
- Do not sign the Hospice Election Statement if the "Attending Physician" field is blank or incorrect, as this can delay medication orders.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the hospital discharge lead and hospice liaison by role immediately.**
   - **Ask for the Case Manager or Discharge Planner assigned to the patient.**
   - **Request the "Charge Nurse" if the case manager is unavailable or the situation is after-hours.**
   - **Identify the Hospice Intake Coordinator or Liaison responsible for the transition.**

2. **Secure the "Minimum Safe Handoff Set" in printed form before the patient leaves the unit.**
   - **Request the Medication Reconciliation (Med Rec) which must include current meds, discontinued meds, and known allergies.**
   - **Obtain the physical Out-of-Hospital DNR or POLST/MOLST form.**
     - Note that in-hospital "Code Status" orders are often not valid once the patient leaves the hospital doors.
   - **Get the Discharge Summary or After-Visit Summary (AVS) containing the primary diagnosis and recent clinical course.**

3. **Conduct a "Meds Tonight" verification with both the hospital and hospice staff.**
   - **Verify which medications are being sent physically with the patient.**
   - **Confirm with the hospice which medications will be delivered to the home and the specific ETA for that delivery.**
   - **Ask the hospice nurse: "If the patient has pain or respiratory distress before the delivery arrives, what is the backup plan?"**

4. **Review the Hospice Election Statement and request the Addendum if needed.**
   - **Verify that the hospice agency name and the chosen attending physician are listed correctly.**
   - **Ask if the patient or representative wishes to receive the "Hospice Election Statement Addendum."**
     - This document lists items, services, and drugs the hospice has determined are unrelated to the terminal illness and will not be covered; you have a right to this information under Medicare rules.

5. **Confirm that Durable Medical Equipment (DME) and Transport are active.**
   - **Call the DME provider to confirm the delivery window for the hospital bed, oxygen, or other essential equipment.**
   - **Verify with the hospital transport desk the scheduled pickup time and the "Level of Service" (e.g., whether oxygen is provided during the ride).**

6. **Escalate to the Hospital Nursing Supervisor or Patient Advocate if the handover is unsafe.**
   - **Contact the "House Supervisor" (available 24/7) if paperwork is missing and staff are pushing for discharge.**
   - **State clearly: "Under CMS Conditions of Participation § 482.43, the hospital is required to provide necessary medical information for an effective transition. We are missing [specific document] and cannot safely discharge."**

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve long-term billing or insurance questions today.
- You do not need to organize a full medical history; the current discharge packet is the priority.
- You do not need to notify all family members before the physical transfer is stabilized.
- You do not need to decide on a funeral home or final arrangements during the active handover.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel pressured by hospital "throughput" or bed-clearing goals. However, requesting the basic documentation required for pain control and safety is a standard part of the care process. Taking ten minutes now to secure a medication list can prevent hours of crisis later tonight.

## Scope note
This guide covers the first-step stabilization of a hospital-to-hospice transition. It does not replace the hospice clinical assessment or legal advice regarding advance directives.

## Important note
This is general information and does not constitute medical or legal advice. If a patient is in an immediate life-threatening emergency during transit or at home before hospice arrives, call 911. Once hospice care has started, use the hospice 24/7 emergency number provided in your intake packet.
