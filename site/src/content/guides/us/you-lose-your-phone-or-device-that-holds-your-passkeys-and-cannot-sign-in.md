# What to do if… you lose your phone or device that holds your passkeys and cannot sign in (USA)

## Short answer
Secure the missing device and your main email, Apple, Google, or Microsoft account first, then use the service’s official account recovery route from a trusted device or computer.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t follow login links from texts or emails claiming your phone was found or your account needs urgent verification.
- Don’t wipe the only device that still has you signed in somewhere unless the risk is immediate and you accept that it may remove your last working recovery route.
- Don’t keep retrying the same recovery form after you hit a lockout or rate limit; stop and note the exact message.
- Don’t give anyone a one-time code, backup code, recovery key, or security-info screenshot.
- Don’t create a new account with the same email to get back in; it can complicate recovery and support checks.

## What to do now
1. **Treat the missing passkey device as at risk.**
   - If it might be stolen, lost in public, or out of your control, assume someone else may try to unlock it or use recovery prompts.

2. **Lock the missing device using official device tools.**
   - **Apple:** use Find My to mark the device as lost.
   - **Android:** use Google’s lost-device tools to secure the device. Erase only if you are confident you no longer need that device as a recovery route.
   - **Windows:** use Microsoft Find My Device to locate or lock it if the feature was enabled.

3. **Protect the phone number if the missing passkey device is a phone.**
   - Contact your mobile carrier using its official app, website, or a number from a bill.
   - Ask about suspending the line, replacing the SIM or eSIM, and adding account protections against SIM-swap or account-takeover attempts.

4. **Use any still-signed-in device as your lifeline.**
   - Check a laptop, tablet, desktop, or old phone you normally use.
   - If you are still signed in to the affected service, go to its security settings and look for devices, sessions, passkeys, sign-in methods, or recovery options.
   - Remove the missing device from trusted devices if the service offers that option, revoke unknown sessions, and add a new passkey on a device you control.

5. **Recover synced passkeys only through the supported account route.**
   - If your passkeys were saved with iCloud Keychain or Google Password Manager, they may become available again after you sign in on a replacement device and complete the required verification.
   - If you use an Apple Account recovery key, follow Apple’s recovery-key steps carefully; some recovery options may be different.

6. **Start the service’s official account recovery flow.**
   - Use a trusted computer or device and type the provider’s sign-in address yourself.
   - Choose the service’s option such as “Can’t sign in?”, “Forgot password?”, or “Account recovery.”
   - Write down the email or username you tried, the recovery options shown, and any lockout or error message.

7. **Prioritise accounts that can reset other accounts.**
   - Focus first on your main email, Apple, Google, or Microsoft account, then financial accounts, then other logins.
   - If a financial account may be exposed, contact the bank or card issuer using a known-good number from its official app, website, or card.

8. **Use official U.S. reporting routes if there are signs of misuse.**
   - If personal information is being misused, use IdentityTheft.gov for a recovery plan.
   - If phishing, account compromise, or cyber-enabled money loss is involved, consider reporting it to the FBI’s IC3.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to recover every login today.
- You don’t need to decide now whether to switch phone brands, password managers, or security setups.
- You don’t need to rebuild every account while stressed; the immediate goal is to secure the missing device, protect the main recovery accounts, and regain safe access.

## Important reassurance
This is a known failure point with passwordless logins, and it can feel sudden and frightening. Many services have recovery paths, but scams also target people at this exact moment, so moving slowly through official routes is safer than rushing.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilize access and reduce the chance of irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about stronger recovery methods, extra trusted devices, or specialist help can wait until the immediate lockout is under control.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Recovery options vary by provider, device, account settings, and evidence of compromise.
