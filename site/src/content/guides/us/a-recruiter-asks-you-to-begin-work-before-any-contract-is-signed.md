# What to do if… a recruiter asks you to begin work before any contract is signed (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign or agree to any terms during the meeting; instead, listen, take notes, and request all proposals and documents in writing for external review.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any severance agreement, release of claims, NDA, or waiver of rights during the initial meeting.
- Do not assume "confidential" means the conversation is off-the-record or legally protected for your benefit.
- Do not resign or "voluntarily" quit on the spot to avoid the discomfort of the meeting.
- Do not secretly record the meeting, as this may violate company policy or state wiretapping laws (all-party consent states).
- Do not react with anger or make verbal counter-offers until you have seen the full written proposal.
- Do not let an employer pressure you with a "today only" offer; most severance waivers require a legal review period.

## What to do now
1. **Request the meeting purpose and any documents in writing before attending.** Send a neutral email asking for the specific topic (performance, layoff, or investigation) and if there are documents you should review in advance.
2. **Identify observable cues that indicate a formal exit or settlement meeting.**
   - Presence of an HR representative and a senior manager you do not normally report to.
   - Pre-printed folders or document packets placed on the table.
   - A meeting location outside of your usual workspace or a "last minute" calendar block.
3. **Verify your right to representation if you are in a union.** If you are a union-represented employee and have a reasonable belief the meeting is an "investigatory interview" that could lead to discipline, you must affirmatively request a union representative (Weingarten rights).
   - Note: In 2026, these rights generally do not extend to non-union private-sector employees unless specifically granted by state law or the employer's own policy.
4. **State your boundary at the beginning of the discussion.** Use the following phrase: **"I am here to listen and understand the company's position, but I will not be making decisions or signing any documents today."**
5. **Ask specific clarifying questions if a "settlement" is mentioned.** 
   - "Is this a proposal for a voluntary separation?"
   - "Is this meeting related to a specific workplace complaint or investigation?"
   - "What is the deadline for a response to this proposal?"
6. **Take the documents and exit the room once the proposal is explained.** Do not feel obligated to stay and "chat" once the offer is handed over. 
   - State: **"I have the documents now. I will review these with my own advisors and get back to you by the deadline."**
7. **Check the age-related review periods if you are 40 or older.** Under the Older Workers Benefit Protection Act (OWBPA), if you are waiving age discrimination claims (ADEA), the employer must generally provide:
   - **At least 21 days** to consider the offer for individual terminations.
   - **At least 45 days** to consider the offer if it is part of a group termination or exit incentive program.
   - **A 7-day revocation period** to cancel the agreement after signing.
8. **Check for state-specific protections if you are in California.** Under the "Silenced No More Act" (SB 331), California employees must be given at least five business days to review a separation agreement and must be notified of their right to consult an attorney.
9. **Identify "gag clauses" that may be federally unlawful.** For non-supervisory employees, broad non-disparagement or confidentiality clauses that prevent you from discussing workplace conditions or labor disputes are likely unenforceable under recent NLRB rulings (McLaren Macomb).

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether the offer is "fair" or "good" while in the room.
- You do not need to explain your feelings or your perspective on your performance or the company.
- You do not need to hire a lawyer before the meeting, though you should consider one before signing the final documents.
- You do not need to tell your colleagues about the meeting or the offer immediately.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a surprise invite for a "settlement discussion" is a high-stress event that often triggers a "fight or flight" response. It is normal to feel lightheaded, anxious, or defensive. The most protective action you can take is to remain silent and take the documents home; the "power" in a settlement negotiation often shifts to the employee once they leave the room and gain time to think.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and harm prevention when a confidential meeting is first requested. It does not cover the legal strategy of negotiating a higher severance amount or filing a lawsuit.

## Important note
This guide provides general information about U.S. employment norms and federal protections as of 2026. Employment laws vary significantly by state and are subject to change by the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) or through new legislation. This is not a substitute for legal advice from a qualified employment attorney licensed in your jurisdiction.
