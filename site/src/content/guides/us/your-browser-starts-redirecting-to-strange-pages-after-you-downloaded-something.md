# What to do if… your browser starts redirecting to strange pages after you downloaded something (USA)

## Short answer
Access the provider’s official recovery or appeal portal directly through their known website address and initiate a formal review while immediately backing up any accessible data.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links or call numbers provided in an email or text warning of "final deletion" as these are often phishing attempts.
- Do not attempt the same verification method repeatedly after a failure; this often triggers a 24-hour to 7-day automated lockout.
- Do not send copies of your government ID through email or social media direct messages.
- Do not sign out of any device currently logged into the account, as this may be your only remaining path for data export.
- Do not pay third-party "account recovery experts" found on social media; they cannot override provider security systems.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the warning by navigating directly to the provider's official domain.**  
   Type the address (e.g., accounts.google.com, iforgot.apple.com, or microsoft.com/digitalsafety) into a fresh browser window. Do not use the link in the message you received.
2. **Identify the specific recovery path for your provider.**  
   - **Google:** Sign in to the disabled account. If eligible, select "Start Appeal" to begin the review or "Download Data" to export files before deletion.
   - **Apple:** Use the "Account Recovery" flow at iforgot.apple.com. Be aware of the 2026 two-phase process: a 24-72 hour evaluation phase followed by a multi-day waiting period.
   - **Microsoft:** Use the official "Account Reinstatement" form. If hacked, use the "Digital Safety" appeal portal.
3. **Capture evidence of the current account status.**  
   **Take screenshots of the exact error message, any case or ticket numbers, and the stated reason for the deletion risk.** Save any recent billing receipts or subscription confirmation emails which may be used to prove ownership.
4. **Export critical data immediately if any access remains.**  
   **Prioritize irreplaceable files over bulk storage.**
   - Original photos and personal videos.
   - Password manager exports or vault backups.
   - Recovery codes for other services (2FA backup codes).
   - Legal, financial, or tax documents.
5. **Secure the primary recovery email and phone number.**  
   **Ensure the email account used for recovery has a unique password and multi-factor authentication (MFA) enabled.** Check for unauthorized forwarding rules in that email's settings which could allow a hijacker to intercept recovery codes.
6. **Report the incident if you suspect identity theft or fraud.**  
   **Go to IdentityTheft.gov to create an official report and receive a personalized recovery plan.** This document can be used to prove to the cloud provider that a compromised account was the result of a crime.
7. **Submit a formal appeal using only official channels.**  
   **Provide clear, factual information matching your account history (billing name, previous passwords, or account creation date).** Avoid emotional language or speculation; focus on the timeline of access loss or the specific verification error.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact policy you supposedly violated before filing the initial appeal.
- You do not need to download your entire account history at once; focus on the "Must-Have" files first.
- You do not need to decide on a new cloud provider while the recovery process is active.

## Important reassurance
Warnings of permanent deletion are designed to be high-pressure, but automated systems often provide a window of several days or weeks before data is actually purged. Following the official recovery steps and securing your evidence is the most effective way to prevent permanent loss. Confusion or stress is a standard response to this situation.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and data preservation steps for cloud accounts at risk of deletion. It does not cover long-term storage strategies or legal litigation against providers.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. As of January 1, 2026, California residents have expanded rights under the CCPA to request access to their personal data even if an account is disabled, unless the account was involved in egregious criminal activity. Provider policies and recovery interfaces are subject to frequent automated changes. If you believe you are the victim of a scam, report it to ReportFraud.ftc.gov.
