# What to do if… you are pressured to stay silent after reporting sexual harassment or misconduct at work (USA)
## Short answer
Create a pause: do not sign or agree to anything in the moment, ask for the request and any proposed document in writing, and contact independent support before responding.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a confidentiality, settlement, separation, or severance document while you are being rushed.
- Do not delete messages, documents, or notes connected to the report or the pressure to stay quiet.
- Do not meet alone with the accused person or anyone pressuring you if you feel unsafe or intimidated.
- Do not feel pushed into withdrawing your report, quitting, posting publicly, or making another major decision today.
- Do not rely on verbal assurances about an agreement. Ask for a written copy.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** If someone is pressuring you live, say: “I am not able to agree to anything right now. Please send the request and any document in writing.” Then end the interaction if you can do so safely.
2. **Move the conversation into writing.** Ask for any confidentiality request, proposed agreement, deadline, or next step by email or in a document. You do not need to debate it in the moment.
3. **Contact independent support.** Consider telling a trusted person, contacting a qualified advisor, or using a specialist support service. If support related to sexual violence would help, you can contact RAINN for support.
4. **Ask about an external workplace route promptly.** Pressure or negative treatment connected to a workplace harassment report may raise retaliation concerns. You can contact the EEOC to ask which route applies. Filing time limits may apply, and federal employees use a different complaint process. Information you give the EEOC when you first contact it is confidential and is not revealed to your employer unless you file a charge. If you file a charge, the EEOC provides your name and basic information about the allegations to the employer.
5. **Slow down any agreement.** If you receive a settlement or severance document, ask for time to review it and consider independent legal advice. EEOC guidance states that an agreement cannot prevent you from filing a charge with the EEOC or participating in an EEOC investigation, hearing, or proceeding. Other terms may still need careful review.
6. **Preserve only what is safe and appropriate.** If you choose to keep records, save messages and documents you personally received and a brief factual note of the pressure, without taking unrelated employer data.
7. **Prioritize immediate safety.** If you are being threatened, stalked, or fear violence, move to a safer place and call 911 in an emergency.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to quit, sue, withdraw your report, sign an agreement, or speak publicly.
- You do not need to produce a perfect account of what happened before getting support.
- You do not have to reply immediately to a pressure message unless there is a genuine urgent safety issue.

## Important reassurance
Being pressured to stay quiet can make it hard to think clearly. Creating a pause, asking for written information, and getting independent support are reasonable first steps. You do not have to make a rushed decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on your workplace, your employment status, your state, and what happened. Specialist support or qualified advice may be useful.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
