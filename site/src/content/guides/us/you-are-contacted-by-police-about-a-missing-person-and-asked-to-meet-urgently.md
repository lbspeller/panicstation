# What to do if… you are contacted by police about a missing person and asked to meet urgently (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the contact through the department’s official number before agreeing to meet or answering substantive questions. Share any immediate danger information promptly: call 911 for an emergency where you are, or contact the local law-enforcement agency where an emergency is happening if it is elsewhere. Ask whether the contact is voluntary and consider speaking with a lawyer before an interview.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go to a private or unfamiliar location based only on an unverified call, text, or email.
- Don’t answer detailed questions while you feel rushed or shaken.
- Don’t speculate, guess, or offer theories about what happened to the missing person.
- Don’t send money, gift cards, cryptocurrency, or personal or financial information to resolve the situation.
- Don’t volunteer passwords, unlock devices, or consent to searches while you feel pressured.
- Don’t physically interfere if officers say they are acting under legal authority. State that you do not consent and ask to speak with a lawyer.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the contact.**  
   Ask for the officer’s name, agency, badge or ID number if available, and a case number if available. End the call. Find the department’s official non-emergency number through its official website or local government website, then call that number to confirm the officer and case.

2. **Ask whether the conversation or meeting is voluntary.**  
   Ask: “Is this voluntary? Am I free to end this call or leave?” You can also ask: “Am I being contacted as a witness, a possible suspect, or in another capacity?” The answer may not settle your status, so avoid substantive questions until you are comfortable proceeding.

3. **Use a simple script if you do not want to answer questions before legal advice.**  
   Say: “I want to remain silent. I want to speak with a lawyer before answering questions.” If you are free to end the call or leave, do so calmly.

4. **Ask before giving identification details.**  
   Rules can vary by state and circumstances. Ask: “What information am I required to provide in this situation?” Do not provide false information.

5. **Set safer meeting conditions.**  
   If you decide to attend a voluntary meeting, request a verified police station and a time that feels safe. Ask who will be present and whether the interview will be recorded. Consider speaking with a lawyer before attending and having a lawyer present.

6. **Keep a home visit simple.**  
   You can speak through a closed door or from a place where you feel safe. Ask: “Do you have a warrant?” and “Am I being detained?” If you do not want officers to enter or search, say: “I do not consent to entry or a search.” Do not physically interfere if officers enter or search.

7. **Pass on urgent safety information promptly.**  
   If there is an emergency where you are, call 911. If an emergency is happening elsewhere, contact the local law-enforcement agency where it is taking place. Otherwise, give urgent factual information to the verified department, such as a last confirmed contact, a last known location, or a medical risk you know about. Avoid rumors.

8. **Write down the contact details.**  
   Record the date and time, the name and agency given by the caller, the number shown on your phone, the official number you used to verify the contact, the case number if available, and any proposed meeting details.

9. **Tell one trusted person about an in-person meeting.**  
   If you attend, share the verified station address and planned check-in time with someone you trust.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to give a full narrative, timeline, or message history during an unverified call.
- You don’t need to volunteer access to devices or consent to searches immediately. If officers present a warrant, court order, or other legal process, avoid physical interference and ask to speak with a lawyer.
- You don’t need to attend a voluntary meeting immediately just because the caller says it is urgent.
- You don’t need to contact the missing person’s family or friends unless you choose to do so and feel safe.

## Important reassurance
An urgent police contact can feel alarming. Taking a moment to verify the caller, share any immediate safety information, and avoid rushed decisions is reasonable. You can ask for legal advice before answering substantive questions.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and reduce irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about an interview, search, device access, or legal process may need advice from a lawyer familiar with your state.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Laws and police practices vary by state and situation. If you feel pressured or unsure, focus on verification, immediate safety information, whether the interaction is voluntary, and asking to speak with a lawyer before answering substantive questions.
