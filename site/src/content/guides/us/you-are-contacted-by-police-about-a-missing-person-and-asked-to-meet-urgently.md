# What to do if… you are contacted by police about a missing person and asked to meet urgently (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the officer's identity through official dispatch or a non-emergency number before agreeing to meet. Clarify your status as a witness or suspect, and exercise your right to remain silent or request a lawyer if the questioning suggests you are being investigated.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t meet an officer at a private residence, parking lot, or any location other than a police station or a public, well-lit area.
- Don’t provide a false name, birthdate, or false information, as this is a criminal offense in all jurisdictions.
- Don’t answer detailed questions or speculate about the missing person's whereabouts while you are in a state of shock or panic.
- Don’t give money, cryptocurrency, or digital gift cards to anyone claiming to be law enforcement; police never demand payment to "resolve" cases.
- Don’t unlock your phone, provide passwords, or consent to a search of your vehicle or home without a warrant or legal advice.
- Don’t feel obligated to "prove your innocence" by talking; you have a constitutional right to remain silent.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the caller or visitor is a legitimate law enforcement officer.**  
   Ask for the officer’s full name, badge number, department, and the case number. If on the phone, hang up and call the department’s official non-emergency number found on a .gov website to confirm they are an active employee. If they are at your door, ask them to hold their credentials up to the window or peephole.
   - If you feel unsafe or the person is aggressive, call 911 to verify if an officer has been dispatched to your location.
2. **Determine your legal status in the investigation.**  
   **Ask specifically: "Am I being contacted as a witness, or am I a suspect?"** If they refuse to answer or give a vague response, treat the interaction as high-risk and prioritize your right to legal counsel.
3. **Invoke your right to remain silent if you feel pressured.**  
   **Say clearly: "I want to remain silent and I want to speak with a lawyer."** Once you have invoked these rights, stop answering all substantive questions. If you are not under arrest, ask: "Am I free to leave?" If the answer is yes, end the interaction calmly.
4. **Provide only required identification information.**  
   **Ask the officer: "What information am I legally required to provide right now?"** In many states, you are only required to identify yourself if you are being lawfully detained or arrested based on reasonable suspicion of a crime.
5. **Set the terms for any requested meeting.**  
   **Request that any interview take place at the police station during business hours.** Do not feel pressured to meet immediately. You have the right to schedule the meeting for a time when you can have a lawyer or a trusted advocate present.
6. **Communicate through the door if police are at your home.**  
   **Keep the door closed and ask: "Do you have a warrant?"** If they have a search warrant, ask them to slide it under the door or hold it up to a window so you can read the address and items listed. If they do not have a warrant, you may choose not to let them in.
7. **Document the interaction as soon as possible.**  
   **Write down the time, date, officer names, vehicle numbers, and exactly what was said.** Note whether the officers were in uniform, in a marked car, and if you heard any active police radio traffic.
8. **Notify a trusted contact of your location.**  
   **Tell a friend or family member if you are going to the station or meeting an officer.** Provide the station address and a time you expect to check back in with them.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a written statement or a signed document today.
- You do not need to consent to the extraction of data from your phone or computer.
- You do not need to decide whether to participate in a formal interview without first consulting an attorney.
- You do not need to contact other potential witnesses or the missing person's family until you have a clear understanding of the investigation.

## Important reassurance
It is standard procedure for police to contact everyone close to a missing person. Being contacted does not mean you are in trouble. Taking time to verify credentials and protect your rights is a responsible way to ensure the investigation remains accurate and that you are safe.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for initial police contact only. It does not cover the specifics of state-level criminal defense or long-term missing person search strategies.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding police stops, identification, and "stop and identify" statutes vary significantly by state. If you are detained, arrested, or believe your rights have been violated, seek professional legal counsel immediately.
