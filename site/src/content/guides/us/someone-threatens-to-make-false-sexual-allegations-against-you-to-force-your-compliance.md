# What to do if… someone threatens to make false sexual allegations against you to force your compliance (USA)
## Short answer

Get to a safer pause and do not comply with demands under pressure. You do not need to solve the whole situation immediately.

## Do not do these things

- Do not send money, sexual images, passwords, account access, or a forced apology to make the threat stop.
- Do not meet the person alone or start a private confrontation.
- Do not threaten them back or post about them publicly.
- Do not send long explanations while you are under pressure.

## What to do now

1. Get to a safer pause. If you are in immediate danger or fear immediate harm, call 911.

2. If it is safe, keep the original messages and avoid altering or deleting them.

3. Stop bargaining if you safely can. Do not promise money, images, access, a meeting, or a detailed apology under pressure.

4. Tell one trusted person what is happening. Ask them to stay with you, call you, or remain available while you handle the immediate situation.

5. If the threat happened online, use the platform’s report option if one is available and it is safe to do so. For an internet-enabled or cyber-enabled crime, you may also file an IC3 complaint. IC3 is not an emergency route.

6. If you are not sure where to start, VictimConnect can help you consider support and referral options.

7. If the person is a partner, ex-partner, or family member, the National Domestic Violence Hotline may be relevant. Threats tied to demands may be part of relationship abuse or a wider pattern of control.

8. If the demand includes sexual contact, sexual images, or other sexual coercion, RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline may be a useful confidential support route.

9. If an employer, school, university, licensing body, or police department contacts you, ask for a brief pause where possible. Consider getting state-specific legal advice before giving a detailed response.

## What can wait

You do not need to decide today whether to confront the person, explain everything to everyone, make a non-emergency report, or predict the final outcome. You do not need a perfect plan before asking for support. Focus first on safety and reducing pressure.

## Important reassurance

A threat tied to demands can create intense pressure to act quickly. Freezing, second-guessing yourself, or wanting the problem to disappear are understandable reactions. Taking a safer pause and getting support around you are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note

These are first steps only. Later decisions about reporting, school or workplace procedures, and legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Options can vary by state and by the circumstances.
