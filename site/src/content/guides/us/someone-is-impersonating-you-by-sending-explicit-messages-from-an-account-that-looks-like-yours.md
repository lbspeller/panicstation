# What to do if… someone is impersonating you by sending explicit messages from an account that looks like yours (USA)

## Short answer
Report the copied account through the platform and warn key contacts not to reply or click links. Check your real account directly and secure it if there are signs that anyone else accessed it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply to the impersonator or confront someone you suspect.
- Do not send intimate images, money, gift cards, or personal documents to make the messages stop.
- Do not click links sent by the copied account.
- Do not publicly identify someone while you are overwhelmed.
- Do not delete everything before saving a minimal record, if saving one feels manageable.
- Do not download, forward, or reshare a sexual image involving a minor.
- Do not blame yourself. The copied account and the messages are not your fault.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If you or someone else may be in immediate danger, call **911** or local police. Do not confront the impersonator.
2. **Check your real accounts directly.** Open your email and social accounts from your usual apps or saved addresses, not from links in messages. Check for unfamiliar sessions, devices, recovery details, or changes you did not make.
3. **Secure any account that may have been accessed.** Start with your email. Use the service’s account-security settings to change the password to a new, unique password, sign out unfamiliar sessions where available, and turn on multifactor authentication where available. Do the same for any affected account.
4. **Report the copied account through the platform.** Look for an impersonation or pretending-to-be-someone option. If that option is not available, use the closest profile-report route. Ask trusted contacts who received messages to report the copied account too, if the platform allows it.
5. **Send one short warning through a different channel.** Tell key contacts: “Someone is impersonating me and sending explicit messages. Please do not reply or click links. Please report the copied account. Verify with me through this channel if needed.”
6. **Save a minimal record if you can tolerate it.** Keep the copied profile name, handle, profile URL, date and time, and one or two screenshots of the profile or messages. A trusted contact can save a message they received if you do not want to view it. Do not download or forward a sexual image involving a minor.
7. **Consider an IC3 report for cyber-enabled crime.** If there are threats, blackmail, fraud, or continued impersonation, you can file a report at IC3 even if you are unsure whether the complaint qualifies. Use **911** or local police for immediate danger.
8. **Use IdentityTheft.gov if personal information may be misused.** This is relevant if someone may be using your personal information for accounts, purchases, benefits, or other identity theft beyond the copied profile.
9. **Use child-safety support if a minor is involved.** Tell a trusted adult. You can use the NCMEC CyberTipline for child sexual exploitation concerns. If an explicit image or video was taken before age 18 and is already on your device, Take It Down may help. Do not download, send, or share an image to submit it.
10. **Use adult-image support if it applies.** If you are currently over 18 and an intimate image or video of you was taken when you were 18 or older, StopNCII.org may be an option if you still have the image or video on your device. You do not need to use this route if the problem is limited to messages.
11. **Use one support contact if needed.** At work, this may be a trusted supervisor or HR contact. At school, this may be a trusted support contact. If the situation may involve sex-based harassment connected to your school, a Title IX coordinator may also be relevant.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify who made the copied account today.
- You do not need to read every message or reply to every contact.
- You do not need to decide now whether to post publicly, hire a lawyer, or make every possible report.
- You do not need to prove everything before taking the first small steps: report the copied account, warn key contacts, and secure any account that may have been accessed.

## Important reassurance
A copied account does not by itself prove that your real account was accessed. A few contained steps are enough for now, and you do not have to manage every part of this at once.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need more tailored help from the platform, a bank or service provider, a school or workplace support contact, a specialist organization, or law enforcement.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you or someone else is in immediate danger, call 911. If you feel at risk of self-harm or cannot stay safe, call or text **988**, use the 988 chat service, or call 911 for immediate danger.
