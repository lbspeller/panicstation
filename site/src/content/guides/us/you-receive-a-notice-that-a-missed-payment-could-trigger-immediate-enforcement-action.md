# What to do if… you receive a notice that a missed payment could trigger immediate enforcement action (USA)

## Short answer
Do not issue any payment or provide personal identifying information during the initial contact. Verify the legitimacy of the notice through independent government or court channels before engaging with the sender.

## Do not do these things
- Do not make a payment using gift cards, cryptocurrency, or wire transfers.
- Do not provide your Social Security number or bank account details to an unsolicited caller.
- Do not click links or download attachments in unexpected emails or text messages regarding debt.
- Do not assume a caller is legitimate because they have your name or a case number.
- Do not ignore formal court papers that have been physically served or sent via certified mail.
- Do not follow contact instructions provided within the suspicious notice itself.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific type of threat being made.** 
   - Note if the sender claims to be a law enforcement officer, a court official, a government agency like the IRS, or a private debt collector.
   - Look for "red flag" cues such as demands for immediate payment to avoid arrest, suspension of a driver's license, or a "warrant."
2. **Retrieve the official contact information for the alleged sender independently.**
   - Search for the official .gov website or a verified court directory for the jurisdiction named in the notice.
   - Do not use the phone number or link provided in the message to perform this verification.
3. **Verify the case or account status directly with the official entity.**
   - If the notice claims to be from a court, use the court's online docket search or call the clerk of court to verify the case number.
   - For federal cases, use the Public Access to Court Electronic Records (PACER) system.
   - If the notice claims to be from the IRS, log in to your official account at IRS.gov to check for outstanding balances or notices.
4. **Demand a written Validation Notice if the sender is a debt collector.**
   - Under Regulation F, debt collectors must provide "validation information" in their initial communication or within 5 days of first contact.
   - Check that this notice includes the "Itemization Date" and the current amount of the debt.
   - Notify the collector in writing within 30 days if you dispute the debt to halt collection activity until verification is provided.
5. **Report impersonation scams to the appropriate federal agency.**
   - Report IRS-themed scams to the Treasury Inspector General for Tax Administration (TIGTA) at 800-366-4484 or via their official website.
   - Report general debt collection scams or illegal harassment to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB).
6. **Respond to legitimate court summons within the state-mandated deadline.**
   - Read the "Answer" deadline on the summons carefully; this is often 20 to 30 days from the date of service.
   - Contact a local legal aid office or a licensed attorney if the papers are verified as legitimate court filings.
7. **Contact local law enforcement if an individual arrives at your home.**
   - Keep doors locked and do not allow the person entry.
   - Request to see official credentials through a window or door.
   - Call 911 if the person refuses to leave or behaves in a threatening manner.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide how to pay a debt until its validity is confirmed in writing.
- You do not need to respond to aggressive verbal demands for "immediate" wire transfers.
- You do not need to sign any settlement agreements or payment plans during a first-contact phone call.
- You do not need to provide an immediate explanation for why a payment was missed.

## Important reassurance
Threats of "immediate enforcement" are a standard tactic used by both illegal scammers and aggressive collectors to bypass your judgment. Taking time to verify the facts through independent channels is the safest way to protect your finances and identity. Feeling startled or anxious is a common reaction to these messages and does not mean you have made a mistake.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying scams and understanding initial debt rights. It does not provide legal strategy for defending a lawsuit or financial advice for debt settlement.

## Important note
Procedures for debt collection and court filings vary significantly by state. This information is based on federal standards including the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) and CFPB Regulation F as of 2026. Consult a qualified professional for specific legal or financial matters.
