# What to do if… you sent money to the wrong person or the wrong account details (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank, credit union, card issuer, or payment app immediately and ask whether the payment is still pending and can be stopped, canceled, recalled, reversed, returned, or disputed. Speed matters because completed wires, ACH payments, and person-to-person app payments may become difficult or impossible to undo.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send a second payment to “fix” the first one.
- Don’t pay anyone who contacts you offering to recover the money for a fee.
- Don’t move a payment-app conversation to text, WhatsApp, email, or another app if the official app has a support or dispute route.
- Don’t assume nothing can be done without checking the payment status and reporting it.
- Don’t send strangers screenshots showing full account numbers, routing numbers, card numbers, login details, or security codes.
- Don’t accept a “refund” that requires you to send money back by a different method.

## What to do now
1. Save the key details before screens change:
   - amount
   - date and time
   - recipient name, phone number, email address, account number, or other identifier
   - confirmation number
   - payment method
   - whether it says pending, scheduled, processing, completed, or failed

2. Check whether you can cancel it yourself.
   - Look in the bank, card, or app activity page for pending, scheduled, processing, cancel, stop, or edit.
   - For Zelle, a payment can generally only be canceled if the recipient has not enrolled and the payment is still pending.
   - Do not try repeated test payments.

3. Contact the provider that sent the money.
   - Use the official number on your card, bank statement, banking app, or provider website.
   - Say: “I made an erroneous transfer to the wrong recipient or wrong account details. Is it still pending, and can you stop or cancel it? If not, please start any recall, reversal, return, recovery, or dispute process that applies.”
   - Ask for a case number and write down the date, time, and name or ID of the person you contacted.

4. If it was a wire transfer, ask for an urgent wire recall.
   - Ask your bank to contact the receiving bank.
   - A wire recall is not guaranteed, and Fedwire payments are generally final once processed, but asking quickly is still the right first step.
   - If the wire involved a hacked email, fake invoice, business email compromise, or other online fraud, also report it to IC3 and keep the report number.

5. If it was an ACH payment, ask whether an ACH reversal or return is possible.
   - Say clearly whether the error was the wrong recipient, wrong account, duplicate payment, wrong amount, or wrong date.
   - ACH reversals are limited and time-sensitive; for permitted erroneous entries, the bank or originator may have only a short window, commonly tied to 5 banking days after settlement.
   - Ask what the bank needs from you today.

6. If it was Zelle, Venmo, Cash App, PayPal, or another payment app, use the official in-app help or dispute flow.
   - Report the wrong recipient or wrong details inside the app.
   - Keep all messages in the app if possible.
   - If the recipient is someone you know, send one calm message: “I sent you money by mistake. Please don’t spend it. I’m asking the bank or app to reverse it.”
   - If they ask you to refund or resend money by a different method, stop and continue only through the provider.

7. If it was a debit or credit card payment, contact the card issuer.
   - Ask whether the charge is pending and can be canceled.
   - Ask whether dispute or chargeback rights apply to the specific transaction.
   - If it is a recurring payment or saved payee, ask how to stop future payments.

8. Follow up in writing the same day.
   - Use secure message, support ticket, or email through the official provider route.
   - Repeat the amount, date, recipient details, confirmation number, and what you asked them to do.
   - Save copies of the message and any reply.

9. If there is any chance this was a scam, say that clearly.
   - Tell your bank or provider: “This may involve fraud.”
   - Report fraud to the FTC.
   - Report internet-enabled fraud, hacked email, fake invoices, or business email compromise to IC3.
   - Keep all report and case numbers together.

10. Protect the account only if there are signs it was compromised.
   - If you see unknown logins, new payees, password reset emails, strange messages, or transactions you did not make, change the password from a safe device.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication where available.
   - Ask the bank or app whether they need to restrict the account, card, or payment features.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to sue anyone or use small claims court.
- You do not need to argue with the recipient before the bank or app process is started.
- You do not need to make a full complaint to a regulator before you have contacted the provider, unless the provider is unreachable and the situation is urgent.
- You can consider a CFPB complaint later if it involves a covered financial product or service and the provider does not respond or gives a final answer you need to challenge.

## Important reassurance
Mistaken transfers happen to careful people because contact lists, saved payees, autofill, and routing details are easy to mix up. The useful thing right now is to move quickly, keep records, and avoid any step that could create a second loss.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on the payment type, timing, provider rules, whether the recipient cooperates, and whether fraud or account compromise was involved.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, fraud-recovery, or professional advice. A bank, card issuer, payment provider, regulator, law enforcement agency, or qualified adviser may need to assess the details of your situation.
