# What to do if… you are asked for personal details to register a death and you do not have them (USA)

## Short answer
Tell the filer (usually the funeral home) what you don’t know, do not guess, and ask exactly how they record “unknown” items and how updates/corrections work in your state.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t invent a Social Security number, date of birth, or spelling “just to complete the form.”
- Don’t assume you personally must have every detail before anything can be filed — often the funeral director completes and submits most of it.
- Don’t share sensitive documents (SSN card, tax returns) with unverified helpers.
- Don’t spiral into contacting many agencies at once before you know what the filer actually needs today.
- Don’t order many certified death certificates until you’re confident the key details are as correct as you can make them.

## What to do now
1. **Work out who is actually filing in your case (pick the first that applies and contact them).**  
   - **If a funeral home is involved:** call them first (they usually handle filing).  
   - **If the person died in a hospital/care facility and you don’t have a funeral home yet:** ask for the facility’s bereavement/decendent affairs contact and who files in your area.  
   - **If neither applies:** contact the **county/local vital records office** for the place of death and ask who files and what the minimum information is.

2. **Send a clear message: “I can’t confirm some items and I don’t want to guess.”**  
   Ask:
   - which items can be recorded as **Unknown**,  
   - what they want you to do if you later find the correct detail, and  
   - how amendments/corrections work in your state (so you know the safe path).

3. **Use only “fast, accurate sources” for missing facts (limit yourself to a short search).**  
   In the next hour, check:
   - a labeled personal file box/folder/safe (if you have lawful access)  
   - prior-year tax paperwork or official benefit letters (often include SSN)  
   - hospital/care facility intake or insurance records (often include DOB/SSN on file)  
   - employer HR records (if the person was employed)

4. **If you don’t know the Social Security number, treat it as “unknown” unless you can confirm it.**  
   Funeral-director guidance used for U.S. death certificates says that if the SSN cannot be verified from reliable sources, it should be recorded as **“Unknown” (and not left blank)** in the applicable field. Tell the filer you cannot confirm it and ask them to record it the correct way in their system.

5. **Ask for the funeral home’s “informant worksheet” (or equivalent) by email.**  
   Filling it out calmly lets you mark unknowns clearly and reduces the risk of accidental guessing while on the phone.

6. **If you’re being asked to notify Social Security, first ask whether the funeral home will report the death.**  
   Funeral homes generally report deaths to Social Security. If a funeral home isn’t involved or doesn’t report it, Social Security provides a phone route for families to report the death.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to solve every missing detail today if the filer can proceed with Unknowns or later updates.
- You do **not** need to notify every agency/business immediately to address this specific problem.
- You do **not** need to make big estate or probate decisions right now to handle this specific issue.

## Important reassurance
It’s common for families to be missing pieces of information at the worst moment — especially SSNs, exact birthplaces, or legal-name spellings. The safest move is to avoid guessing and to let the official process record unknowns correctly.

## Scope note
These are first steps to prevent irreversible errors and keep the filing process moving. Later steps (amendments, certified copies, benefit notifications) vary by state and may need more detailed local guidance.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Death certificate filing rules and amendment processes vary by state and sometimes by county. When in doubt, your funeral director (if involved) or the local vital records office for the place of death is the most practical “source of truth” for what can be recorded as unknown and how to correct it later.
