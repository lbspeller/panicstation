# What to do if… you are asked for personal details to register a death and you do not have them (USA)

## Short answer
Inform the person filing the certificate (the funeral director or medical examiner) exactly which details are missing and ask them to record those fields as "Unknown." Do not guess or invent data, as the informant is legally responsible for the accuracy of the personal information section.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t guess or estimate dates, Social Security numbers, or the spelling of legal names to fill gaps.
- Don’t assume the death cannot be registered without a Social Security number.
- Don’t provide sensitive identification documents to individuals or services you have not verified.
- Don’t order multiple certified copies of the death certificate until the information is as accurate as you can make it.
- Don’t delay contacting the funeral home or vital records office because you are missing information.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the official responsible for filing the death certificate in your specific jurisdiction.**  
   - If you have chosen a funeral home, they are the primary point of contact for the "informant" (the person providing personal details).  
   - If the person died in a hospital or facility and no funeral home is yet chosen, contact the facility's decedent affairs / bereavement office.  
   - If the death is being handled by a Medical Examiner or Coroner, contact their office to ask who is responsible for the personal data portion of the record.

2. **Formally notify the filer that certain items must be marked as Unknown.**  
   - Specifically ask: "Which fields in this state can be recorded as 'Unknown' if the information is not obtainable?"  
   - Ask for a copy of the "Informant Worksheet" to be sent to you so you can review the required fields in writing.

3. **Check standard locations for missing vital statistics if you have lawful access to the deceased person's residence.**  
   - Look for the most recent federal or state tax return, as these contain the Social Security number (SSN).  
   - Locate discharge papers from a hospital or care facility, which often list the full legal name and date of birth used for insurance.  
   - Check for a DD-214 form if the person was a veteran, as this contains full name, birth, and service details.

4. **Verify the Social Security number reporting procedure with the funeral home.**  
   - Most funeral homes report the death to the Social Security Administration (SSA) electronically.  
   - If no funeral home is involved, you must report the death yourself by calling the SSA at 1-800-772-1213 (TTY 1-800-325-0778) between 8:00 a.m. and 7:00 p.m. local time.

5. **Request a draft or "proof" copy of the death certificate data before it is finalized.**  
   - Review every character of the name, parents' names, and birthdate for typos.  
   - Confirm that any field you could not verify is explicitly marked as "Unknown" rather than left blank, which can cause system rejections in some states.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find the deceased person's original birth certificate immediately; the information can be reported without the physical document.
- You do not need to decide on a headstone or final memorial details today to move forward with the registration.
- You do not need to provide the exact names of the deceased person’s parents if they are genuinely unknown.
- You do not need to finalize the "Cause of Death" section; this is completed by a medical professional or coroner, not by you.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for informants to be missing details like a Social Security number or the maiden name of the deceased person’s mother. Vital records systems are designed to handle "Unknown" entries. Recording a field as unknown is a standard procedure and is significantly safer than providing a guess that would require a legal amendment or court order to correct later.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate stabilization of the death registration process when information is missing. It does not cover the legal process of amending a certificate once it has been filed, which varies significantly by state.

## Important note
This information is not legal advice. Requirements for "Unknown" entries and the process for later amendments are governed by state law and local vital statistics regulations. For the most accurate guidance, consult the local registrar of vital records in the county where the death occurred.
