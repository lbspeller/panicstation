# What to do if… your partner is controlling your contraception, appointments, or medication (USA)

## Short answer
Use the safest private way available to contact a domestic violence hotline, sexual assault hotline, or clinic without your partner involved. Ask for a private appointment and privacy-protective communication before anything is sent, called, or posted.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront them about contraception, appointments, medication, or “control” if that could make things more dangerous right now.
- Do not argue at the clinic door if that could escalate; instead, look for any private moment to tell staff, “I need to be seen alone.”
- Do not stop essential medication suddenly unless a clinician tells you to; ask a clinician or pharmacist for a safer plan.
- Do not use a phone, patient portal, email account, insurance account, or mailing address they monitor if that could reveal help-seeking.
- Do not assume you have to report to police, leave the relationship today, or explain everything perfectly.

## What to do now
1. **Create the safest private pause you can.** Step outside, take a short errand, use a restroom break, or contact help from a trusted friend’s phone, work phone, library computer, or clinic phone if your own device is monitored.
2. **If you are in immediate danger, call 911.** If calling is not safe, move toward a public place, a neighbour, a front desk, a clinic reception area, or someone nearby who can call for you.
3. **Contact confidential support for relationship abuse.** Call the National Domestic Violence Hotline at 1-800-799-SAFE (7233), text START to 88788, or use its chat if that is safer.
4. **If anything sexual happened without consent, or you are not sure, contact sexual assault support.** Call RAINN at 800-656-HOPE (4673), use its online hotline, or text HOPE to 64673.
5. **Ask a clinic to help you be seen alone.** When calling or checking in, say: “My partner is controlling my contraception, appointments, or medication. I need to be seen alone and I need help keeping communication private.”
6. **Use privacy-protective wording with the clinic or health plan.** Say: “I am requesting confidential communications. Please use only this safe phone number or address, and please do not leave detailed messages.” Under HIPAA, you can ask for an alternative contact method or location; the clinic or plan may ask for the request in writing, a safe contact method, and how payment should be handled.
7. **Tell the clinician the specific interference in one or two sentences.** For example: “They hide my pills,” “They insist on coming into appointments,” “They pressure sex without a condom,” “They check my portal,” or “I am worried my contraception or medication has been tampered with.”
8. **Ask for practical care options that reduce partner interference.** Ask about contraception options, medication storage, refills, pharmacy pickup, appointment notes, portal access, and reminder settings that fit your safety situation.
9. **Make one small safety note for the next 24 hours.** Choose one safe person, one safer place you could go, and one safer way to reach a clinic or hotline. Do not store this note somewhere your partner can access.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to leave, report, file for anything, or confront your partner.
- You do not have to gather evidence or give a full history before asking for help.
- You do not have to solve every insurance, records, or medication logistics issue before contacting support; start by telling the clinic or hotline that privacy is the immediate risk.
- You do not have to choose a contraceptive method today; you can ask what options exist and decide when you have private clinical support.

## Important reassurance
If this feels coercive, that feeling matters. Interference with contraception, appointments, or medication is a serious boundary and safety issue, and you deserve healthcare decisions and as much privacy as can safely be arranged.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, private access to healthcare, and specialist support. Later decisions about healthcare, reporting, legal options, housing, insurance, or the relationship may need help from a clinician, advocate, or other qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If your safety could worsen if your partner learns you sought help, use the safest communication method available and contact a hotline or clinic experienced with coercion and privacy risks.
