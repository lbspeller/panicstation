# What to do if… you suspect someone else has access to your private photos or cloud account (USA)

## Short answer
From a safer pause, secure the account that controls access first: your Apple Account, Google Account, or the email account used for password resets. Change the password, sign out other devices, turn on 2-factor authentication, and get specialist support if intimate images may be shared or used to threaten you.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront or negotiate with the person you suspect right now; it may escalate or prompt quick sharing.
- Don’t delete everything in panic; it may remove useful records and it does not remove copies someone else may already have.
- Don’t click password-reset links from messages; go directly to the service or app yourself.
- Don’t use a device you think may be monitored for security resets if you have another trusted option.
- Don’t pay, send more images, or comply if someone threatens to release images; it may lead to more demands.
- Don’t blame yourself for not noticing sooner.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause first.**  
   If you are in immediate danger, call **911**.

2. **Use a trusted device if possible.**  
   Use a friend’s phone, a library computer, or another device the other person has not had access to. Use a connection the other person does not control if you can.

3. **Secure the account that controls your photo backups first.**  
   Start with your Apple Account, Google Account, or the email account used for password resets. Do these in order:
   - Change the password to a new, unique password.
   - Sign out of all other devices and active sessions where the service allows it.
   - Turn on 2-factor authentication, 2-step verification, or passkeys if offered.
   - Remove unfamiliar devices, recovery details, and third-party apps with access.

4. **Check the quiet access routes that can expose intimate photos.**  
   Look for and turn off or remove anything you do not recognise:
   - Shared albums, shared libraries, and family sharing.
   - Public links to albums, folders, or cloud drives.
   - Auto-backup folders that were shared before and forgotten.
   - Recovery email addresses or phone numbers you do not control.

5. **Use built-in safety checks where they fit your device.**  
   On an iPhone, Apple Safety Check can help review or stop sharing and account access on supported devices. On a Google Account, review the devices with account access and sign out any device you do not recognise.

6. **If you are being threatened or images may be circulating, contact specialist support.**  
   RAINN can offer confidential support and options. If you are 18 or older, StopNCII may help participating platforms detect and reduce re-sharing by creating a digital fingerprint of the image on your device.

7. **If anyone in the images is under 18, use child-safety routes.**  
   Report to NCMEC CyberTipline. If the images or videos were taken when you were under 18, Take It Down from NCMEC may help with removal from participating platforms.

8. **If it is safe, keep minimal proof without saving explicit images.**  
   Keep screenshots or notes of threats, usernames, dates, times, platform names, and account alerts. Do not send explicit images to friends or helpers.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to involve law enforcement.
- You do not need to contact every platform today; securing account access is the first priority.
- You do not need to explain yourself to friends, family, school, or work immediately.
- You do not need to figure out exactly how the access happened before you lock things down.
- You do not need to make long-term decisions about the relationship or the device right now.

## Important reassurance
This situation can make anyone feel panicked, ashamed, or powerless. The immediate task is smaller than the whole problem: stop access where you can, end active sessions, and reach support that understands tech-enabled sexual abuse.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate access and harm. If the situation involves coercion, stalking, domestic control, sextortion, or images involving someone under 18, later decisions may need specialist support, legal advice, platform help, or safety planning.

## Important note
This guide offers general, immediate harm-reduction information and is not legal, medical, financial, forensic, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If changing passwords or settings could put you at risk, prioritize personal safety and seek confidential specialist support.
