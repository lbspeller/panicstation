# What to do if… you are told to report to your probation or supervision office on short notice (USA)

## Short answer
Make contact with your supervising officer or office immediately through a trusted contact path. Confirm the time and location, ask what to do if you cannot arrive as instructed, and keep a brief record of your contact attempts and any response.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the instruction because it feels sudden or confusing.
- Do not rely only on a phone number or link in an unexpected message if you have another trusted contact path.
- Do not assume that a phone call, voicemail, or proposed alternative replaces an in-person reporting instruction unless your supervising officer or office confirms that.
- Do not send money, buy gift cards, or share payment details in response to a message claiming that payment will prevent arrest or another penalty.
- Do not take weapons or other items prohibited by your conditions or by the office’s rules.
- Do not argue at the office or send a long explanation while you are trying to confirm the immediate next step.

## What to do now
1. **Use a trusted contact path.**  
   Call the supervising officer or office using a number from your conditions paperwork, a prior official message, an appointment card, or an official office website. Do not rely only on the contact details in a new message that seems suspicious. For a federal case, the official U.S. Courts Federal Court Finder can help you locate a U.S. Probation and Pretrial Services office.

2. **Confirm the immediate details.**  
   Ask whether you must report in person, the exact time, the address, and who you should ask for when you arrive. Ask for written confirmation if that is available. If it is not, write down the time of the call, the name of the person you spoke with, and what they said.

3. **Say clearly if you cannot arrive as instructed.**  
   Explain the specific barrier, such as transportation, work, childcare, illness, disability access, or an incorrect address. Ask what the office wants you to do now. You may ask whether a later arrival, another reporting time, or another form of contact is accepted, but do not assume an alternative is approved unless the office confirms it.

4. **Keep a simple record if you cannot reach anyone.**  
   Leave a brief voicemail if possible. Use an approved text, email, portal, or other contact method if your officer or office already uses one. Keep your call log, messages, and a note of each attempt. Continue trying to make contact.

5. **Prepare for the office visit without adding delay.**  
   Unless the office tells you otherwise, bring identification and your supervision paperwork if you have them. Check any office instructions you already have. Leave behind weapons and other prohibited items. If you need an accommodation or must bring a medically necessary item, mention that when you contact the office.

6. **Treat payment demands as a red flag.**  
   If the message demands cash, gift cards, a transfer, or payment details, stop engaging with that sender. Contact your supervising office through a trusted route to check whether the reporting instruction itself is genuine.

7. **If you are told there may be a violation issue, stay focused on the next step.**  
   Keep records of what you were told and what you did. Seek case-specific legal help when you can, but do not delay required contact or reporting while waiting for a reply.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a full account of everything that has happened before making contact.
- You do not need to decide today whether the short notice was fair.
- You do not need to gather perfect documentation before asking what the office wants you to do now.
- You do not need to predict the outcome of a missed or delayed report while you are still trying to confirm the immediate next step.

## Important reassurance
A short-notice reporting instruction can feel alarming. Making prompt contact through a trusted route, confirming the details, and keeping a simple record are practical stabilizing steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Probation, parole, supervised release, and other supervision arrangements vary across federal, state, county, and local systems. Your own conditions and the instructions from your supervising office matter most. The federal resources below do not replace instructions from a state or local supervising office. Later decisions may need case-specific legal help.

## Important note
This guide provides general information, not legal advice or professional advice. A missed or delayed report may have consequences depending on your conditions and supervision system. Confirm the immediate instruction with your supervising officer or office as soon as possible.
