# What to do if… you are contacted by police or investigators and fear saying the wrong thing (USA)
## Short answer
Do not answer substantive questions. Say clearly: **“I am invoking my Fifth Amendment right to remain silent. I want a lawyer. I will not answer questions without a lawyer.”**

## Do not do these things
- Don’t try to clear things up in a casual chat, phone call, text thread, or voluntary interview before getting legal advice.
- Don’t guess, estimate, fill in gaps, or lie.
- Don’t rely on the absence of a Miranda warning as a safety net. Miranda warnings are generally linked to custodial interrogation.
- If asked for consent to a search, don’t consent to a search of your phone, car, home, accounts, or belongings. Say that you do not consent, but do not physically resist or interfere.
- Don’t delete, edit, hide, or destroy messages, photos, files, posts, or documents.
- Don’t discuss the facts with other involved people, coordinate accounts, or ask anyone to change or delete material.
- Don’t sign a statement, explanation, waiver, or agreement before a lawyer has advised you.

## What to do now
1. **Use one clear script, then stop discussing the facts.**
   - Say: “I am invoking my Fifth Amendment right to remain silent. I want a lawyer. I will not answer questions without a lawyer.”
   - If asked for consent to a search, say: “I do not consent to any search.”
2. **Ask whether you are free to leave.**
   - Say: “Am I free to leave?”
   - If the answer is yes, end the conversation and leave calmly.
   - If the answer is no, do not argue, resist, or try to leave. Ask: “Am I being detained or arrested?”
3. **If the contact is by phone, message, or voluntary invitation, do not discuss the incident.**
   - Ask for the person’s name, agency, contact details, and any case or reference number they are willing to provide.
   - Say: “A lawyer can contact you about any request for an interview.”
   - End the call, message exchange, or voluntary conversation. Do not keep talking while waiting for the other person to end the contact.
4. **If you are detained or arrested, keep your words to the minimum needed.**
   - Identification requirements vary by state and situation. Provide information required by law for the situation, but do not discuss the incident.
   - Repeat: “I am invoking my Fifth Amendment right to remain silent. I want a lawyer.”
5. **Contact a lawyer with relevant experience.**
   - If the contact is from law enforcement or concerns possible criminal allegations, seek a criminal-defense lawyer.
   - If another agency is involved, seek a lawyer with experience relevant to that type of investigation.
   - If you already have a lawyer, contact them or ask a trusted person to contact them.
   - If you do not have a lawyer, use a state or local bar lawyer-referral service.
   - If you are charged with a crime, cannot afford a lawyer, and may face jail or prison, ask how to request a public defender or other appointed lawyer when you first appear in court.
6. **Preserve a simple record for your lawyer.**
   - Keep voicemails, messages, letters, business cards, notices, and documents.
   - Write down the date, time, location, agency, names, contact details, what was requested, and any stated dates.
   - Show any subpoena, warrant, court document, or notice to a lawyer promptly and ask what requires a response.
7. **Keep the situation off social media and group chats.**
   - Don’t post an explanation or try to set the record straight online.
   - Keep substantive discussion for your lawyer.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide now whether to give an interview, statement, or written explanation.
- You do **not** need to investigate the situation, contact witnesses, or build a detailed timeline right now.
- You do **not** need to negotiate, bargain, or prepare a statement, waiver, or agreement on the spot.
- Preserve existing material, but leave decisions about next steps for a conversation with a lawyer.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressure to explain yourself is understandable. Taking a pause, clearly saying that you will not answer questions without a lawyer, and avoiding an improvised statement are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to stabilize the moment and prevent irreversible mistakes. Later decisions may depend on the facts, your custody status, state law, and the type of investigation, and may need help from a qualified lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Laws and procedures vary by state and situation.
