# What to do if… someone tries to get you to pull over to “sort it out” after a minor traffic incident that feels suspicious (USA)

## Short answer
Do not stop in an isolated or dark location. Drive immediately to the nearest well-lit, populated public area or a police station while calling 911 to report that you are seeking a safe location to exchange information.

## Do not do these things
- Do not follow the other driver to a second location of their choosing, such as a side street or "just around the corner."
- Do not get out of your vehicle to inspect damage if the other driver is acting aggressively or if the environment feels unsafe.
- Do not lower your window more than an inch if the other driver approaches your vehicle.
- Do not accept cash offers to settle the matter without a police report or insurance exchange.
- Do not hand over your physical phone, wallet, or original documents to the other driver.
- Do not drive to your home or place of work if you believe you are being followed.

## What to do now
1. **Signal your intent to stay present without stopping immediately.** Turn on your hazard lights and maintain a slow, legal speed to show the other driver you are not fleeing the scene (avoiding a "hit and run" accusation).
2. **Drive to a safe, well-lit, public location.**
   - A busy gas station.
   - A large grocery store parking lot with active foot traffic.
   - The nearest local police precinct or fire station.
3. **Call 911 while you are in motion.** Tell the dispatcher you were involved in a collision and that you feel unsafe stopping at the current location because of the other driver's behavior or the isolated area.
4. **Provide your specific details to the dispatcher.**
   - Your current location and direction of travel.
   - The make, model, color, and license plate of the other vehicle.
   - A description of the driver and any other occupants.
5. **Stay inside your locked vehicle once you reach a stopping point.** Keep your seatbelt fastened and the engine running in case you need to depart quickly if the situation escalates before help arrives.
6. **Communicate through a closed or cracked window only.** If the other driver approaches, state clearly that you have called the police and will exchange information once an officer is present or when dispatch confirms it is safe.
7. **Document the situation using your phone or dashcam from inside the car.** Take photos of the other vehicle’s plate and the driver if they are visible, but do not point the camera in a way that further agitates them.

## What can wait
- Inspecting the physical damage to your own vehicle.
- Arguing about who caused the collision.
- Calling your insurance company to start a claim.
- Searching for your physical insurance card or registration (wait until you are in a stationary, safe spot).

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a "fight or flight" response when a routine traffic event feels suspicious or predatory. Choosing to prioritize your physical safety by moving to a public area is a recognized safety protocol, provided you are simultaneously communicating with emergency services to document your actions.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for personal safety during a suspicious traffic encounter. It does not cover long-term insurance litigation, state-specific "move over" laws for emergency vehicles, or the technical process of filing a DMV accident report.

## Important note
State laws generally require drivers to stop and exchange information after a crash; however, 911 dispatchers can provide a "safe harbor" by documenting that you are moving to a safer location due to an immediate threat. Always follow the specific directions given by the 911 operator during the call.
