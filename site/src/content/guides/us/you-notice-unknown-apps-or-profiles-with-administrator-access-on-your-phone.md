# What to do if… you notice unknown apps or profiles with administrator access on your phone (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using the phone for sensitive activity, disconnect it from networks, and use a different trusted device to secure your key accounts. Then remove any unknown administrator app or management profile if it is not expected work, school, or family management and you can do so safely.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use the phone for banking, email recovery, password changes, password-manager access, or verification codes until you have contained the issue.
- Don’t open the unknown app, approve prompts, install updates from it, or grant it more permissions.
- Don’t wipe the phone immediately if you can safely note the app or profile name first.
- Don’t disable built-in security warnings just to make the alert go away.
- Don’t remove work, school, or organization management from a device that may have been issued or managed by that organization until you check with them.
- Don’t confront anyone about it while the phone may still be accessible or monitored.

## What to do now

1. **Disconnect the phone from networks.**
   - Turn on **Airplane Mode**.
   - If Wi-Fi or Bluetooth turns back on separately, turn those off too.
   - Stop using the phone for anything private or financial.

2. **Make a quick record, only if safe.**
   - Take a screenshot of the unknown app, profile, or administrator access screen.
   - Write down the app or profile name, icon, and when you first noticed it.
   - Do not spend time investigating who installed it right now.

3. **If it is an iPhone, check for unknown management profiles.**
   - Go to **Settings → General → VPN & Device Management**.
   - If you see a profile or management entry you do not recognise, select it and delete it if the phone allows that.
   - Restart the iPhone after deleting a profile.
   - If removal is blocked, or the phone may be managed by work or school, pause and verify with that organization.

4. **If it is Android, check Device Admin apps.**
   - Go to **Settings → Security** or **Settings → Security & privacy**, then look for **Device admin apps**, **Device administrators**, or a similar name.
   - If you cannot find it, search the Settings app for **device admin**.
   - Turn off administrator access for any app you do not recognise.
   - Then go to **Settings → Apps**, select that app, and uninstall it if the phone allows that.

5. **If Android will not let you remove the app, use Safe Mode.**
   - Restart the phone into **Safe Mode** using the phone maker’s steps.
   - In Safe Mode, third-party downloaded apps are usually not running.
   - Repeat the Device Admin check and uninstall the unknown app.
   - Restart normally after removal.

6. **Run the built-in Android scan.**
   - Open **Google Play Store → profile icon → Play Protect**.
   - Run a scan.
   - Follow any prompt to uninstall, disable, or remove a harmful app.

7. **Check other control areas for anything unknown.**
   - Check **VPN** settings and remove any VPN you did not set up.
   - Check **Accessibility** settings and turn off access for apps you do not recognise.
   - Check special app access areas, such as notification access, and remove access you did not choose.

8. **Use a different trusted device to secure accounts.**
   - Start with your email account, because it can reset other accounts.
   - Then secure your Apple Account or Google Account.
   - Then secure banking, payment, and phone-carrier accounts.
   - Change passwords, turn on multi-factor authentication where available, review recovery email addresses and phone numbers, and sign out of other sessions where the account offers that option.
   - In email, check for forwarding rules, filters, or recovery settings you did not create.

9. **Report only what is useful right now.**
   - If money, identity documents, or personal information may have been stolen, use FTC or IdentityTheft.gov guidance.
   - If there was cyber-enabled fraud, extortion, or financial loss, you can file a complaint with IC3.
   - Use official sites only; avoid ads, lookalike reporting pages, or links sent by strangers.

10. **If you still cannot trust the phone, consider a factory reset.**
   - A factory reset can be a practical fallback when unknown administrator access or management keeps coming back.
   - Save only irreplaceable items if you need to, such as photos or contacts.
   - Be careful with backups: restore only from a backup you are confident was made before the unknown app or profile appeared.
   - If you are not sure, rebuild the phone without restoring apps or device settings at first.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out exactly who did it right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to replace the phone.
- You do not need to install a new security product before removing the unknown access and securing accounts.
- You do not need to confront anyone, post screenshots publicly, or explain the situation to everyone.
- You do not need a complete long-term security plan before taking these first steps.

## Important reassurance
Finding unknown administrator or management access is a valid reason to pause and contain the situation. The first useful move is not to solve the whole mystery; it is to reduce access, protect key accounts, and avoid rushed decisions.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate digital harm and regain basic control. If apps or profiles reappear, removal is blocked, money is missing, or you suspect targeted monitoring or harassment, later decisions may need specialist technical, legal, workplace, or victim-support help.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional security, legal, law-enforcement, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Phone menus vary by model, carrier, and software version. Some administrator apps and management profiles are legitimate on work, school, family-managed, or organization-managed devices.
