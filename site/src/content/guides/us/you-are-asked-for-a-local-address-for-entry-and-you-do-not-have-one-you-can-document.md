# What to do if… you are asked for a local address for entry and you do not have one you can document (USA)

## Short answer
Do not invent an address. Check whether the airline form or officer is asking for your U.S. destination address, then give truthful details that match your actual plan or ask staff how to complete the field for your itinerary.

## Do not do these things
- Do not copy an address from the internet or use an address you cannot explain truthfully.
- Do not enter a placeholder unless airline staff tells you what truthful entry is accepted for your itinerary.
- Do not create a booking only to make your plans appear different from what they are.
- Do not hand your passport to an unofficial helper or pay someone unofficial to alter your details.
- Do not guess when you are unsure what a field means. Ask staff to clarify the exact field.

## What to do now
1. **Ask what address is being requested.**  
   Say: “Is this the U.S. destination-address field, and what should I enter for my itinerary?” An airline check-in field, an ESTA application if you are using ESTA, and a question from a CBP officer may not be asking exactly the same thing.

2. **Use the truthful option that fits your current plan.**  
   - If you are staying at a hotel and the airline destination-address field is blocked because you do not know the street address, give airline staff the hotel name if known, the city of your first-night stay, and the state. Ask staff to enter the accepted format.
   - If you are staying with someone, use the real address only when you are confident it is correct. Ask the person to send the address by text or email if needed.
   - If you have a connecting flight within the United States, ask airline staff whether the field needs your final U.S. destination.
   - If you are arranging accommodation after arrival, tell airline staff that truthfully and ask what accepted entry fits the form.

3. **If you are only transiting through the United States and completing ESTA, use the transit instruction.**  
   In the ESTA address lines under `Address While In The United States`, enter `In Transit` and your final destination. Do not invent a local U.S. address.

4. **Keep any real details easy to show.**  
   Save the hotel confirmation, host message, or onward itinerary on your phone. Use the same truthful details where the same information is requested.

5. **If online check-in will not continue, use the staffed desk.**  
   Ask the airline representative to identify the blocked field and explain which truthful entry is accepted for your itinerary. Ask for a supervisor if the first representative cannot resolve it.

6. **If a CBP officer asks where you will stay, answer truthfully.**  
   Give the information you currently have. Say clearly when part of your plan is not yet arranged. Do not guess or add details you are not sure about.

7. **If you are sent for additional screening, keep the next step simple.**  
   Answer questions truthfully, ask for clarification when needed, and provide documents you actually have. If you need consular assistance, ask whether you may contact your country’s embassy or consulate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to make unrelated trip decisions while you are trying to understand one address field.
- You do not need to fill gaps by guessing.
- You do not need to explain your whole itinerary unless staff asks for more information.
- You can deal with later accommodation decisions after the immediate check-in or entry question is clear, unless staff asks for more information.

## Important reassurance
An address field can be difficult when plans are flexible or a form does not fit the itinerary. Pause, use truthful details, and ask the airline desk or officer to clarify what information is needed.

## Scope note
These are first steps for a U.S. destination-address question during airline check-in, an ESTA application, or U.S. border questioning. The correct entry can depend on the traveler’s itinerary, documents, and the specific field. Later decisions may need help from the airline, CBP, a consular representative, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. It does not guarantee boarding or admission to the United States. Airline staff and U.S. border officials may need to assess the traveler’s specific circumstances.
