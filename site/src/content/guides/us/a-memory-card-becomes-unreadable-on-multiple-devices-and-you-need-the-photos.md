# What to do if… a memory card becomes unreadable on multiple devices and you need the photos (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using the card immediately and reject all prompts to "Format," "Erase," or "Repair" the device. Physical and logical stabilization is the priority to prevent permanent data overwriting.

## Do not do these things
- Don't click **Format**, **Initialize**, or **Erase** when prompted by a computer or camera.
- Don't run repair utilities like **CHKDSK**, Windows **Scan and fix**, or macOS **First Aid** before files are recovered.
- Don't attempt to "test" the card by taking new photos or deleting existing ones.
- Don't save any recovered files back onto the same failing card.
- Don't use physical "hacks" such as putting the card in a freezer or using tape on the pins.
- Don't repeatedly insert the card into different devices once it has failed on two separate systems.

## What to do now
1. **Apply physical write protection to the media immediately.**  
   **Locate the small sliding lock switch on the side of full-sized SD cards and move it to the "Lock" position.**
   - For microSD cards, insert the card into a microSD-to-SD adapter and move the adapter's switch to the "Lock" position.
   - If no physical switch exists, store the card in a hard case and do not insert it into any device until you have recovery software ready.

2. **Check all automated cloud and local synchronization paths.**  
   **Verify if the photos were already imported or backed up before the card became unreadable.**
   - Search Google Photos, iCloud, OneDrive, or Dropbox accounts.
   - Check the "Imported" folder in your computer's photo library.
   - Check the internal storage of the camera or phone used to take the photos.

3. **Perform a single, non-invasive detection check on a computer.**  
   **Connect the card using a known-good external card reader and a different USB port to rule out hardware interface failure.**
   - On **Windows**: Open **Disk Management** (Right-click Start > Disk Management) and check if the disk appears as "Removable" with a "RAW" or "Unallocated" file system.
   - On **Mac**: Open **Disk Utility** (Command + Space > type Disk Utility) and check if the card name appears in the sidebar, even if the partition is greyed out.
   - If the card is not detected at all in these system menus, stop and seek professional recovery.

4. **Create a bit-for-bit disk image if the card is detected.**  
   **Use a data recovery tool designed to create "sector-by-sector" or "DD" images rather than copying files via File Explorer.**
   - This process reads the card once from start to finish and saves it as a single file on your computer's hard drive.
   - Once the image is created, disconnect the physical card and run recovery software against the image file only.

5. **Engage a professional data recovery lab for high-priority images.**  
   **Contact a reputable service provider if the card is physically warm to the touch, has visible cracks, or contains irreplaceable data.**
   - Ensure the lab provides a "no data, no fee" policy and a written estimate.
   - Clearly state: "The card is unreadable on multiple devices / please do not format or initialize."

6. **Preserve consumer rights for defective hardware (USA).**  
   **Collect your receipt and packaging if the card is new or under warranty.**
   - Under the Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA), you have 60 days from the date of the first statement showing the charge to dispute a payment for defective goods.
   - Contact your credit card issuer to initiate a dispute if the retailer or manufacturer refuses a refund for a non-functional card.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a replacement card brand or backup strategy today.
- You do not need to "fix" the card for future use; assume this specific card is permanently compromised.
- You do not need to finalize a refund or warranty claim until you have attempted or completed the photo recovery process.

## Important reassurance
In many cases, the photos are still physically on the card; the "unreadable" status usually means the index or file system is damaged, not the data itself. By refusing to "Format" or "Repair" the card, you are successfully preventing the most common cause of permanent photo loss.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction for digital media failure. It does not cover advanced forensic recovery or hardware repair for snapped or snapped-in-half cards.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not guarantee data recovery. Digital storage media is volatile and can fail completely without warning. If data is critical, professional intervention is the only way to minimize the risk of total loss.
