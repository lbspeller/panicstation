# What to do if… your water meter is moving even though all taps and appliances are off (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as a likely active leak: shut off water at your home’s main shutoff valve, then check whether the meter still moves. That usually tells you whether the leak is likely inside the home or between the meter and the house shutoff.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore a moving leak indicator when all water is off; it usually means water is flowing somewhere.
- Don’t force a stuck shutoff valve, curb stop, meter valve, or meter box lid.
- Don’t dig, probe, or shovel near the meter box, curb, yard line, or service line area.
- Don’t leave water on if you suspect a significant leak and you can safely shut it off.
- Don’t run toilets, faucets, irrigation, hoses, washing machines, dishwashers, or icemakers while you are checking the meter.
- Don’t touch wet electrical equipment, outlets, power strips, or a breaker panel.

## What to do now
1. **Look for immediate danger or damage.** Check for pooling water, wet ceilings or walls, water near electrical items, a damp water heater area, a toilet that keeps refilling, a wet crawl space, or a soggy patch in the yard near the meter or service line.
2. **Shut off water at the main house shutoff.** This is often where the water pipe enters the home, such as a basement, garage, crawl space, utility closet, or outside wall. Turn it fully off if it moves normally.
3. **Do not force the curb stop or meter valve.** If there is a clearly marked customer shutoff near the meter and it moves easily, you may use it. If it is stuck, unmarked, locked, or appears to belong to the utility, leave it alone and call the water utility.
4. **Check that the house shutoff is actually off.** Turn on one indoor faucet for a few seconds. If water keeps flowing strongly, the shutoff may not be working, so you may not be able to use this test reliably.
5. **Re-check the water meter.** Many meters have a small leak indicator, often a triangle, gear, star, snowflake, or small dial that moves with low flow.
   - If the leak indicator or meter hand keeps moving with the house shutoff off and the shutoff appears to work, the leak may be between the meter and the house shutoff, often an underground service line leak.
   - If the leak indicator or meter hand stops, the leak is more likely inside the home, after the house shutoff.
6. **Record what you see.** Take a photo or short video of the meter, including the moving indicator if visible. Note the time, keep all water off, and check again after 30 minutes to a few hours if it is safe and practical.
7. **Check common quiet leak sources inside the home.** Listen for a toilet refilling, look for trickling into the toilet bowl, check under sinks, check around the water heater, check appliance supply lines, confirm irrigation timers are off, and look at hose bibs and outdoor spigots.
8. **Choose a safe water plan for the next few hours.** If you can manage without water, leave the main shutoff off to reduce damage. If you need water briefly for sanitation, medical needs, or safety, turn it on only while someone is watching and turn it back off after.
9. **Contact the right help.**
   - If the meter suggests an inside-home leak, contact a licensed plumber.
   - If the meter suggests a leak between the meter and the house shutoff, call your water utility first and say: “The meter leak indicator is moving with the house shutoff valve closed.”
   - If anyone needs to dig, contact 811 or your state 811 center before digging and wait for utility marking instructions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find the exact leak location right now.
- You do not need to decide tonight about repiping, service line replacement, landscaping repair, or insurance.
- You do not need to settle who is responsible for the repair in the moment; first reduce the flow, document what you saw, and contact the water utility or a plumber.

## Important reassurance
A moving meter can feel alarming because the leak may be hidden, but the meter is useful information. The shutoff-and-check test usually gives enough direction to reduce damage and get the right help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Repairs, permits, utility inspections, billing adjustments, and responsibility for the service line depend on your local utility rules and the location of the leak.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional, legal, plumbing, electrical, insurance, or financial advice. If there is fast-rising water, water near electrical equipment, a bulging ceiling or wall, a sinkhole, or any immediate danger, move away from the area and call 911, your utility emergency line, or an appropriate licensed professional.
