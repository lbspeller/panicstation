# What to do if… your windshield wipers stop working while you are driving in bad weather (USA)

## Short answer
Stay stopped somewhere visible and as far from traffic as you safely can. Do not merge back into traffic or keep driving unless you can keep the windshield clear enough to see.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive on through rain, spray, snow, or slush if the windshield is not staying clear.
- Do not stand beside traffic to inspect the wipers, open the hood, or look for tools.
- Do not force wipers that may be frozen to the glass.
- Do not try to move away from a poor stopping place unless you can see well enough and it is safer than staying put.
- Do not use hazard lights as a reason to keep driving without visibility.
- Do not unlock the doors for a stranger if you feel unsafe; speak through a slightly opened window or call official help.

## What to do now
1. **Make the stopped vehicle safer.**
   - Keep your seatbelt on.
   - Turn on hazard lights while you are stopped.
   - Put the vehicle in park, or set the parking brake if needed.
   - Check your mirrors before making any movement, and do not pull back into traffic if you cannot see clearly.

2. **Decide whether staying inside or moving away is safer.**
   - If you are on a highway shoulder, in heavy spray, in darkness, or close to fast traffic, generally stay in the vehicle with your seatbelt on while you call for help.
   - If the vehicle is in a lane of traffic, at a blind curve, or in another place that feels immediately dangerous, call 911 and follow the dispatcher’s instructions.
   - If responders tell you to leave, or if staying in the vehicle is clearly more dangerous, exit on the side away from traffic and move well away from the roadway.

3. **Do only quick checks that do not put you near traffic.**
   - Try one normal wiper setting, one faster setting, and the washer control once.
   - Turn on the defroster to keep the inside of the windshield clear.
   - If snow or ice is involved and the wipers may be frozen to the glass, do not force them.
   - If the windshield does not stay clear right away, treat the car as disabled and wait for help.

4. **Call for the right help.**
   - Call 911 if you are stopped in a dangerous place, traffic is close, visibility is very poor, you have been hit, or anyone may be hurt.
   - If it is not an emergency, call roadside assistance, your insurer, an auto club, the vehicle manufacturer’s roadside line, or a tow service.
   - Give your location as clearly as you can: road name, direction of travel, nearest exit, mile marker, cross street, landmark, or what your phone map shows.
   - Call 511 or check your state DOT road information only for travel and road-condition information where available; do not use it instead of 911 in danger.

5. **Stay visible and stable while you wait.**
   - Keep your phone charged if you have a charger.
   - Keep doors locked if that helps you feel safer.
   - If you must run the engine for heat in snow, make sure the exhaust pipe is not blocked, crack a window slightly, and run the engine only as needed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the wiper motor, fuse, relay, linkage, or wiring on the roadside.
- You do not need to choose a repair shop or approve repairs while you are still in a risky place.
- You do not need to decide insurance, blame, or cost issues right now.
- You do not need to keep driving just because the destination is close.

## Important reassurance
Pulling over was a sensible first step. A car with failed wipers in bad weather is a visibility problem, not something you have to solve on the shoulder.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate roadside situation. Later decisions about repairs, towing, insurance, or a mechanical inspection may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, emergency-service, mechanical, medical, financial, or professional advice. If you think you are in immediate danger, call 911.
