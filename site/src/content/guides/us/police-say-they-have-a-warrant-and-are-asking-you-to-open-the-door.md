# What to do if… police say they have a warrant and are asking you to open the door (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a safer pause inside, keep the door closed if you can do so safely, and ask to see the warrant through a window or by having a copy slid under the door. If there is immediate danger, call 911; if there is no immediate danger and time allows, call the agency’s publicly listed number yourself and ask what to do next.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stand directly behind the door or block the entryway.
- Do not open the door merely to inspect the warrant if you can read it through a window or from a copy slid under the door.
- Do not consent to a search merely because you feel pressured.
- Do not argue, physically resist, obstruct entry, or make sudden movements if officers enter.
- Do not lie, provide false documents, hide items, or destroy anything.
- Do not assume that a claimed name or badge number proves identity by itself.
- Do not volunteer explanations about the case at the door.

## What to do now
1. Move away from the door’s swing path. Keep children, other people, and pets back from the entry if you can do so safely. Keep your phone with you.
2. Speak through the closed door. Say: “I am trying to verify who you are and read the warrant. Please slide a copy under the door or hold it up to the window.”
3. Ask what agency they are from, their names, their badge or identification numbers, and what type of warrant they say they have. Write the details down if you can do so without delaying or interfering.
4. Read what you can safely see. For a search warrant, check the address and the listed places, people, or items. For an arrest warrant, check the named or described person. Do not try to block entry while you read.
5. If you think the people at the door may not be officers, use an independent contact route. If there is immediate danger, call 911 and describe what is happening. If there is no immediate danger and time allows, call the agency’s publicly listed number yourself, explain that people at your door claim to be officers with a warrant, and follow the instructions you receive.
6. If officers enter, keep your hands visible and avoid sudden movements. Say once: “I do not consent to any search beyond what the warrant authorizes.” Do not physically resist or interfere.
7. Do not answer investigative questions. Say: “I am going to remain silent. I want to speak with a lawyer before answering questions.”
8. If you can do so safely and without interfering, observe where officers go and what they take. Ask for a copy of the warrant and any receipt or inventory for seized items.
9. As soon as it is safe, write down the time, the agency, names or badge numbers, what was searched, what was taken, and who else was present. Contact a lawyer when you can.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain your side of the story at the door.
- You do not need to decide whether to make a complaint or take legal action right now.
- You do not need to work out whether the warrant is legally valid while officers are at the door.
- You do not need to sign a consent form merely because you feel pressured.

## Important reassurance
You do not have to solve the whole situation at the doorway. Asking to see the warrant, using an independent contact route when needed, keeping your answers brief, and avoiding physical resistance can help you get through the immediate moment more safely.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the doorstep moment only. Later decisions about a search, seized property, an arrest, or a possible impersonation attempt may need help from a lawyer or the relevant agency.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or other professional advice. State, local, and federal rules can differ, and the type of warrant and the circumstances can matter. Emergency circumstances may also affect what officers can do. For a federal search warrant, Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 41 generally requires the executing officer to provide or leave a copy of the warrant and a receipt for property taken; state and local procedures can differ.
