# What to do if… you receive a notice that your goods or property are being held pending verification (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay, click links, or send original documents until you have verified the notice through an official website or published agency number you find yourself. Once verified, respond in writing with the case or receipt number and copies of ownership proof.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wire money, buy gift cards, use cryptocurrency, or pay a “verification” or “release” fee from a link or number in the notice before independent verification.
- Do not share one-time passcodes, passwords, full card numbers, or bank login details.
- Do not mail original identity documents, title documents, deeds, receipts, or the only proof you have.
- Do not ignore a verified deadline if the notice mentions seizure, forfeiture, abandonment, disposal, or appeal rights.
- Do not write a long account of what happened if the hold might relate to an investigation; start with identity, ownership, status, and return or release.
- Do not assume federal, state, county, police, shipping, jail, and prison property rules are the same.

## What to do now
1. **Preserve the notice exactly as received.** Save the email, PDF, envelope, tracking page, text message, screenshots, attachments, and caller information. Write down the date you first received it and every case, receipt, tracking, inventory, or seizure number shown.

2. **Verify the notice using a route you find yourself.** Go to the agency’s official website or the official website for the city, county, state, or federal agency named. Use the main phone number or contact portal there and ask: “Can you confirm this case number and that this notice was issued by your office?”

3. **Ask what kind of hold it is.** Ask the verified office to confirm, preferably in writing, whether the goods or property are being held as evidence, safekeeping, lost property, shipping or customs screening, contraband screening, jail or prison property, or seizure with possible forfeiture.

4. **Ask for the inventory or receipt.** If police, a sheriff’s office, a jail, a prison, a shipping inspector, or another agency has the item, ask for the item description, serial numbers if any, date and place taken or received, unit or officer if applicable, and the release conditions.

5. **Make a simple written ownership packet.** After verification, send copies only. Include your name and contact details, the case or receipt number, a one-sentence request for release or return after verification, and copies of relevant proof such as receipts, order confirmations, tracking records, photos of serial numbers, insurance schedules, repair records, registration papers, or title paperwork.

6. **If the notice says forfeiture or intent to forfeit, treat it as urgent.** Use the deadline and filing address in the verified notice. For many federal administrative forfeiture notices, contesting forfeiture in U.S. District Court requires filing a claim with the notifying agency by the notice deadline, and the claim generally must identify the property, state your ownership or other interest, and be made under oath or penalty of perjury.

7. **Do not confuse a claim with a petition.** In federal forfeiture, a claim is usually the route to contest forfeiture in court. A petition for remission or mitigation is different and is usually a request for discretionary return or relief. Petition deadlines and requirements may be different, so check the verified notice and official instructions before choosing.

8. **If this is jail or prison property, use the facility’s property-office process.** Ask what is being verified, what documents they accept, whether the person in custody must sign a release or facility form, whether pickup must be scheduled, and whether the item can be mailed or must be collected. Federal prison rules are not the same as state or county jail rules.

9. **If you cannot get basic written confirmation, escalate calmly.** Ask for the property or evidence unit supervisor, the facility property officer, or the agency’s published complaint or records-request route. Keep each message short and include the case or receipt number.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to sue, make a public complaint, or replace the item.
- You do not need to explain every detail of how you got the goods or property unless a verified process asks for specific ownership proof.
- You do not need to use the contact details inside the notice until you have checked them against an official source.
- You do not need to choose between a forfeiture claim and petition without first checking the verified notice and, if needed, getting quick legal help.

## Important reassurance
A verification hold does not automatically mean you did anything wrong or that the property is permanently lost. Calm written steps now help you avoid scams, preserve proof, and keep track of any real deadline.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: verify the notice, preserve evidence of ownership, ask for written status, and avoid irreversible mistakes. If the notice involves forfeiture, criminal allegations, large-value property, immigration concerns, or a very short deadline, later decisions may need legal help in the relevant state or federal process.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, investigative, or professional advice. Procedures vary across federal, state, county, city, shipping, jail, and prison systems, so use official contact routes and the verified notice for the exact next step.
