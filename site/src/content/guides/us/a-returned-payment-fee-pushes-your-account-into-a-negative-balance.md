# What to do if… a returned payment fee pushes your account into a negative balance (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all automated retries by merchants immediately, bring the balance to $0 or higher to stop daily penalties, and contact the bank to request a reversal of "unanticipated" fees.

## Do not do these things
- **Don’t** keep rerunning the same payment or letting a merchant "re-present" the item until you have verified available funds; multiple retries can trigger multiple fees for one purchase.
- **Don’t** perform small "test" debit transactions to see if the card still works, as each attempt may trigger a new $25–$35 fee if the balance is negative.
- **Don’t** ignore the account for more than 24 hours; many banks charge "sustained" or "continuous" negative balance fees for every day the account stays below $0.
- **Don’t** assume the bank's "available balance" was correct at the time of the swipe; authorization holds from gas stations or hotels often hide the true balance.

## What to do now
1. **Identify if the fee is for an "Overdraft" (item paid) or an "NSF" (item returned).**  
   **Check your transaction history for the specific code.** 
   - An Overdraft fee means the bank paid the merchant and you now owe the bank that money plus the fee. 
   - An NSF (Non-Sufficient Funds) fee means the bank rejected the payment and the merchant likely still expects payment.

2. **Contact the merchant to stop "re-presentment" retries.**  
   **Call the merchant who attempted the withdrawal and ask them to stop automated retries for at least 72 hours.** 
   - Merchants often retry a failed ACH or check 2–3 times automatically. 
   - Each retry is a separate event that can trigger a new fee.

3. **Check for "Authorized Positive, Settled Negative" (APSN) errors.**  
   **Compare the date the transaction was "authorized" with the date it "settled."** 
   - If your balance was positive when you swiped the card (authorized), but the bank charged an overdraft fee because other items settled first, this is considered an "unanticipated" fee. 
   - The CFPB (Consumer Financial Protection Bureau) has designated these as potentially unfair practices.

4. **Deposit funds using the fastest "available" method.**  
   **Use cash at a branch or an internal transfer from a linked account to bring the balance to at least $0.01.** 
   - Ask the teller or check the app specifically for the "Available Balance," not the "Ledger Balance." 
   - Confirm that the deposit will be applied to the negative balance immediately to stop continuous overdraft fees.

5. **Request a fee waiver using specific regulatory language.**  
   **Call the bank and ask for a "courtesy reversal" or a waiver of "unanticipated fees."** 
   - If the fee was caused by an APSN transaction (see step 3), explicitly state: "This was an authorized positive transaction that settled negative due to your processing order." 
   - If this is your first fee in 12 months, many institutions have an automated "one-time" waiver policy.

6. **Verify your Regulation E (Opt-In) status for one-time transactions.**  
   **Ask the representative: "Am I currently opted in for overdraft coverage on ATM and one-time debit transactions?"** 
   - Banks cannot charge overdraft fees for one-time debit card swipes or ATM withdrawals unless you affirmatively opted in. 
   - If they charged a fee for a one-time swipe and you did not opt in, reference "CFPB Circular 2024-05 regarding phantom opt-ins" and demand a refund.

7. **Ask about "continuous" or "daily" negative balance fees.**  
   **Specifically ask: "How many days do I have before a sustained negative balance fee is applied?"** 
   - Some banks charge an additional $5–$10 per day if the account is not positive within 3–5 business days. 
   - Knowing this deadline helps you prioritize which funds to move first.

8. **Document the conversation and outcomes.**  
   **Record the name of the representative, the date, and the specific "Reference Number" for any promised fee reversals.** 
   - Reversals usually take 24–48 hours to appear in the "Available Balance."

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to close the account today; doing so while the balance is negative can result in a report to ChexSystems, making it harder to open accounts elsewhere.
- You do **not** need to send a formal written dispute letter to the CFPB yet; wait to see if the bank honors the verbal waiver request first.
- You do **not** need to change your direct deposit settings immediately; wait until the current balance is stabilized.

## Important reassurance
Seeing a negative balance and stacking fees is a common experience that often stems from the complex way banks sequence transactions behind the scenes. This situation is a technical financial hurdle, not a reflection of personal character. Most banks have established protocols for reversing these fees when asked promptly and clearly.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. It focuses on stopping escalating fees and correcting immediate balance errors. Long-term financial planning or credit repair requires a separate process.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Banking regulations, including Regulation E and CFPB enforcement priorities, are subject to change. Individual bank policies regarding "posting order" and "funds availability" vary significantly. If the bank refuses to reverse a fee you believe was charged in error or without a valid opt-in, you may need to file a formal complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau.
