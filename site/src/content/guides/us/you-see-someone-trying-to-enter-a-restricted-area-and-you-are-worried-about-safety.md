# What to do if… you see someone trying to enter a restricted area and you are worried about safety (USA)

## Short answer
Get yourself to a safer position and report what you are seeing to on-site security, staff, or 911 if there may be immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront, physically stop, block, or chase the person.
- Don’t follow them into the restricted area or try to investigate alone.
- Don’t stand close to record if filming puts you at risk.
- Don’t post images, live updates, or location details online while it is unfolding.
- Don’t wait for certainty if the concern is that someone could be harmed soon.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer position first.**  
   Step back, keep an exit available, and put a barrier such as a wall, desk, column, or closed door between you and the person if you can do that without moving closer.

2. **Alert the nearest responsible person on-site.**  
   Tell security, a front desk, a supervisor, event staff, facility management, or another staff member with access to the site’s emergency process. Say plainly: “Someone is trying to get into a restricted area and I’m worried it could lead to harm.”

3. **Call 911 if it is urgent or escalating.**  
   Call 911 if there is forced entry, violence, threats, a weapon, a fire or medical danger, or you believe someone could be harmed soon. If you are unsure, describe what you are seeing and let the dispatcher decide what response is needed.

4. **Give facts responders can use immediately.**  
   Give the exact location, such as building, entrance, floor, room, gate, or nearby landmark. Describe what the person is doing, such as testing locked doors, tailgating through a secure door, climbing a fence, forcing a door, hiding near an entrance, or entering without a badge.

5. **Give a simple description without getting closer.**  
   Share clothing, approximate height and build, direction of travel, whether they are alone, and any visible vehicle or bag only if you can observe this safely. Mention nearby risks such as crowds, children, patients, machinery, vehicles, or a secured control area.

6. **Use venue-specific emergency routes if they are available.**  
   In places such as airports, stadiums, hospitals, transit hubs, workplaces, schools, or campuses, use posted emergency phones, security desks, panic buttons, badge-access help points, or the site’s security number if you can do so safely. Still call 911 if immediate danger may be present.

7. **After reporting, stop engaging and stay reachable.**  
   Move somewhere safer. Keep your phone available in case a dispatcher, security officer, or responder calls back for clarification. Do not re-enter the area to check what happened.

8. **If it is not happening right now, use a non-emergency route.**  
   Report the concern to the site owner, workplace security, campus security, or the local police non-emergency number listed on the agency’s official website. Federal tip routes usually can wait unless there is immediate danger or 911, local police, or site security tell you to use them.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide whether it was definitely dangerous before reporting.
- You don’t need to identify the person, collect evidence, interview witnesses, or prove intent now.
- You don’t need to write a detailed report immediately; the first priority is getting the key facts to someone who can respond.
- You don’t need to decide whether this is trespassing, terrorism, workplace misconduct, or a misunderstanding right now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel unsure when something might be innocent but could also become serious. Reporting a restricted-access concern is a reasonable precaution, and you are allowed to choose safety over certainty.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the earliest safe pause. After you are safe and the concern has been reported, later decisions may need help from site security, local law enforcement, workplace leadership, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, emergency-management, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you think anyone may be in immediate danger, prioritise safety and call emergency services.
