# What to do if… you cannot access cash because local ATMs are down or cash machines are empty (USA)

## Short answer
Stop attempting withdrawals at the same machine and use a merchant's cash-back service or a bank teller withdrawal as an immediate alternative. Verify your account status via your bank's mobile app to rule out a card block or a wider system outage.

## Do not do these things
- Don't make more than two withdrawal attempts in a row (this frequently triggers automated security blocks or "Speed Bump" fraud holds).
- Don't accept help from bystanders or "staff" standing near the machine.
- Don't use non-bank ATMs in isolated or poorly lit areas (higher risk of skimming devices and physical theft during outages).
- Don't enter your PIN if the keypad feels loose, thick, or looks altered.
- Don't assume a "declined" message means your account is empty; it may indicate a network synchronization error.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm if the issue is the specific machine or your debit card.**
   - Open your bank or credit union’s mobile app to check for "service outage" banners or "card frozen" alerts.
   - Attempt a small, non-cash purchase at a nearby shop to see if the chip or magnetic stripe is functioning.
   - If the card works for purchases, the problem is likely limited to the ATM network or the specific machine's cash supply.

2. **Request cash back at a large retail register or grocery store.**
   - **Select the "Debit" option on the card reader and enter your PIN to access the cash-back menu.**
   - **Confirm the specific fee before approving the transaction, as many large retailers and dollar stores now charge $1.00 to $1.50 per withdrawal.**
   - Ask the cashier for the maximum limit, which is often lower ($20–$100) than standard ATM limits.

3. **Visit a staffed bank or credit union branch for a teller withdrawal.**
   - **Bring a valid government-issued photo ID and your physical debit card to the counter.**
   - **Check the branch door or website first, as many locations in 2026 operate on an "Appointment-Only" model for large cash transactions.**
   - If the branch is out of cash, ask for a cashier's check if you need to pay a specific bill immediately.

4. **Report the failure to your financial institution immediately if the ATM showed a "withdrawal successful" message but did not dispense cash.**
   - **Note the exact time, the ATM's location (address or ID number on the machine), and the specific amount that failed to dispense.**
   - **Call the number on the back of your card to initiate a "Regulation E" error resolution claim.**
   - Request a "provisional credit" if the bank indicates the investigation will take longer than 10 business days.

5. **Utilize peer-to-peer (P2P) transfers for urgent payments if you cannot get physical cash.**
   - **Ask the recipient if they accept mobile payments via bank-integrated apps.**
   - **Be aware that in 2026, many P2P services have "Cool Down" periods (24–48 hours) for transfers to new recipients to prevent fraud.**
   - Avoid "instant" transfer options if the system is currently experiencing a network-wide outage.

6. **Verify state-specific protections regarding declined transaction fees.**
   - **If you are in California, state-chartered banks and credit unions are prohibited from charging "junk fees" for ATM withdrawals that are instantaneously declined due to insufficient funds (SB 1075).**
   - Review your transaction history for any "NSF" (Non-Sufficient Funds) fees applied during the outage and request their removal if a system error occurred.

## What can wait
- You do not need to cancel your debit card if the machine was simply empty.
- You do not need to move all your funds to a different bank today; temporary outages usually resolve within 4–12 hours.
- You do not need to file a formal CFPB complaint immediately; wait for the bank's internal error resolution process to conclude first.

## Important reassurance
ATM outages and empty machines are common during holiday weekends, major storms, or local events. Most modern banking systems have "fail-safe" protocols that prevent money from being permanently lost during a mid-transaction crash. Your funds remain secure even if the physical access point is temporarily unavailable.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for accessing emergency funds during a localized or temporary ATM failure. It does not cover long-term banking disputes, lost/stolen card procedures, or legal action against financial institutions.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute financial or legal advice. Terms, fees, and daily limits are set by your individual bank's "Deposit Account Agreement" and are subject to change. If you feel physically unsafe at an ATM location, leave the area immediately without completing the transaction.
