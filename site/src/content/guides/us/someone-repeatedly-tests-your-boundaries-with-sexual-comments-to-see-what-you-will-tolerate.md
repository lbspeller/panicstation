# What to do if… someone repeatedly “tests” your boundaries with sexual comments to see what you will tolerate (USA)

## Short answer
Create immediate physical or digital distance and contact a trusted specialist service like RAINN to discuss your options in a confidential environment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel required to laugh, participate, or stay "polite" to manage the other person's reaction.
- Do not agree to meet the person alone to resolve the issue if you feel any level of hesitation.
- Do not provide personal information, such as your home address or daily schedule, in an attempt to pacify them.
- Do not delete evidence like text messages or call logs if it is safe to keep them for future reference.
- Do not attempt to confront the person if there is a significant power imbalance or if you are in an isolated location.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause by ending the current interaction immediately.** Step into a public area, hang up the phone, or stop responding to digital messages.
2. **State a single, firm boundary once and then disengage from the conversation.** Use a clear statement without room for debate.
   - "Do not make sexual comments to me."
   - "That is inappropriate; do not speak to me like that."
3. **Change the conditions of their access to you.** Use privacy settings to mute or block them online, or ensure you are never alone with them in physical spaces.
   - Request to move your desk or shift if the situation is at work.
   - Transition communication to group threads or official email channels only.
4. **Create a private record of the incidents for your own use.** Note the date, time, specific words used, and any witnesses present.
   - Take screenshots of digital comments and store them in a secure folder.
   - Keep a log of your specific responses to their comments.
5. **Contact a confidential specialist service for professional support.** These services can help you plan for safety without requiring you to report.
   - Call RAINN at 800-656-HOPE (4673) for 24/7 support.
   - Access the online chat at RAINN.org/hotline.
   - Text "HOPE" to 64673 to connect with a specialist.
6. **Follow your organization's reporting protocol if the situation involves work or school.** Contact Human Resources or a designated Title IX coordinator to notify them of the conduct.
7. **Contact 911 if you are in immediate physical danger.** Seek help from emergency services if the person is following you, threatening you, or preventing you from leaving.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you want to file a formal legal claim or a police report.
- You do not need to have a perfect "label" for the behavior before you take steps to protect yourself.
- You do not need to convince others that the behavior is "serious enough" before seeking support.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel confused or startled when boundaries are tested in small, escalating increments. Stress responses like freezing, "fawning" (acting overly nice to stay safe), or feeling unsure of your own memory are common. Taking steps to increase your distance and safety is a standard and protective response to unwelcome behavior.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. It focuses on reducing immediate exposure and connecting with confidential support services. Long-term decisions regarding legal action or formal complaints should be made with specialist advice.

## Important note
This document is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal or clinical advice. Reporting requirements and workplace laws vary by state. If you are in immediate danger, please contact local emergency services or 911.
