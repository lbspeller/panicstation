# What to do if… you are worried a partner will not respect your request to stop or pause sex (USA)

## Short answer
Move toward the earliest safer pause available. When it is safe to do so, do not proceed with sex if you are worried your request to pause or stop may not be respected; call 911 if you are in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not push past your discomfort because you feel you owe someone sex.
- Do not assume you have to argue, persuade, or confront the other person.
- Do not rely on alcohol or drugs to tolerate the situation.
- Do not rely on being able to manage the other person’s reaction alone.
- Do not assume browsing or messaging is private if the other person may monitor your device.
- Do not pressure yourself to make relationship or reporting decisions right now.

## What to do now
1. **Move toward the earliest safer pause available.** If sex has not started and it is safe to step away, do not proceed. If something is already happening, prioritize reaching a safer place when you can.
2. **Use words only if speaking feels safe.** A brief “stop,” “pause,” or “I need to leave” is enough. If speaking feels unsafe, focus on reaching a safer place when you can. You do not have to explain.
3. **Contact emergency help if you are in immediate danger.** Call 911 when you can do so safely.
4. **Contact support from a safer place or device when possible.** A sexual assault advocate or relationship-abuse advocate can listen and help you consider immediate options. Ask about privacy before sharing identifying details if that matters for your safety.
5. **Make the next contact safer.** Before being alone with the person again, consider whether seeing them at all feels safe. If you choose to meet, keep your phone accessible, arrange your own transportation, and let a trusted person know where you are. If those things do not feel possible, avoid being alone with them.
6. **Consider medical support if unwanted sexual contact happened or you are concerned about your health.** An advocate, hospital, or clinic can explain local medical options. Ask what privacy and reporting rules apply locally before deciding next steps.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide right now whether to report, confront the person, or end the relationship.
- You do not have to explain or prove your feelings before asking for support.
- You do not have to write a detailed account before speaking with an advocate or clinician.
- You do not have to contact the person to restate your boundary.

## Important reassurance
Freezing, going quiet, or complying to reduce risk can happen when someone feels unsafe. None of those responses makes unwanted sexual contact your fault. Wanting to pause or stop is enough.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety and support. Later decisions may benefit from specialist sexual violence or relationship-abuse support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911 when you can do so safely.
