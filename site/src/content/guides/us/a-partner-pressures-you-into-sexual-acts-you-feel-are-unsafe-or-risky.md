# What to do if… a partner pressures you into sexual acts you feel are unsafe or risky (USA)
## Short answer
You do not have to agree. Get to a safer pause first. If anything sexual happened without your free agreement, or there may be pregnancy, STI, or HIV exposure, seek prompt medical care.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to something you consider risky or unsafe just to avoid conflict, sulking, guilt, or threats.
- Do not keep negotiating in the moment if you feel scared or cornered. Focus on getting space first.
- Do not use alcohol or drugs to get through the situation or make yourself agree.
- Do not assume you must make a police report before asking for medical care or advocacy. Ask a provider or advocate about local rules and options.
- If it feels safe and useful, do not delete messages or notes you may later want to keep.

## What to do now
1. **Create a pause.**  
   Use a short sentence if speaking feels safe: “No. I’m not doing that.” Move to a safer place as soon as you can.

2. **Call 911 if you are in immediate danger.**  
   If making the call yourself is not safe, ask someone nearby to call.

3. **Create distance if you can do so safely.**  
   Contact a trusted person and say: “I do not feel safe. Can you call me or stay on the phone while I get somewhere safer?”

4. **Contact confidential specialist support.**  
   RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline is available 24/7 at 800-656-HOPE. You can also use its online chat or text `HOPE` to `64673`. A trained support specialist can provide support and local referrals. Ask about confidentiality limits before sharing identifying details if that matters to you.

5. **Seek medical care if something happened without your free agreement, or if you are unsure.**  
   A medical provider can check for injuries, infections, and pregnancy. Ask the provider or RAINN about local clinic, hospital, and exam options.

6. **Ask urgently about HIV PEP if there may have been HIV exposure within the last 72 hours.**  
   PEP should be assessed as soon as possible. If more than 72 hours have passed, still seek medical care to discuss other appropriate testing and support.

7. **Ask promptly about emergency contraception if there may be pregnancy risk.**  
   Emergency contraceptive pills should be taken as soon as possible within five days. A copper IUD may also be an option within five days. A clinician can help you choose an available option.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide right now what label fits the situation.
- You do not have to decide right now what to do about the relationship.
- You do not have to decide right now whether to report to police. An advocate or provider can explain local rules and options.
- You do not need to confront your partner or write a long message now.

## Important reassurance
Your boundaries matter. Wanting safer sex practices or refusing a specific act is valid. People may freeze, comply, or try to keep things calm under pressure. That does not make the pressure your fault.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: reach a safer pause, stabilize the moment, and keep medical and support options open. Later decisions may need help from a qualified specialist. Confidential support can help if the pressure continues or you are unsure what to do next.

## Important note
This guide provides general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. You can ask about your options before deciding what to do next.
