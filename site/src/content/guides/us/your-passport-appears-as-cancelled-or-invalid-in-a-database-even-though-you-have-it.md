# What to do if… your passport appears as cancelled or invalid in a database even though you have it (USA)

## Short answer
Do not report the passport lost or stolen; instead, obtain the exact system error from the airline or border agent and contact the National Passport Information Center (NPIC) or the nearest U.S. embassy immediately for a status verification.

## Do not do these things
- Do not submit a DS-64 (Lost/Stolen report) if the passport is in your physical possession — this permanently kills the electronic chip and cannot be undone.
- Do not attempt to "test" the passport at multiple different kiosks or airlines once a hard "invalid" or "stolen" flag is confirmed.
- Do not book non-refundable travel until a federal official confirms your record has been cleared or a new document is issued.
- Do not pay third-party "expediting" websites to check your status; only the Department of State can see the internal flag.

## What to do now
1. **Request the specific error code or status description from the official agent.**  
   Ask the airline or immigration officer: *"Is the system showing the document as cancelled, reported lost/stolen, or is there a data mismatch?"* Write down the specific terminology used by the system and the name of the database if mentioned (e.g., APIS or Interpol SLTD).

2. **Verify that the digital record matches your physical document exactly.**  
   Confirm with the agent that the following were entered correctly into their system:
   - The full name, including middle names/initials.
   - The passport number (specifically check if an "O" was entered as a "0").
   - The date of birth and the expiration date.

3. **Call the National Passport Information Center (NPIC) if you are currently in the United States.**  
   Contact them at 1-877-487-2778 (TTY: 1-888-874-7793).
   - Representatives are available Monday–Friday, 8:00 a.m. to 10:00 p.m. ET, and Saturday/Sunday, 10:00 a.m. to 3:00 p.m. ET.
   - For life-or-death emergencies outside these hours, call the State Department Duty Officer at (202) 647-4000.

4. **Contact the nearest U.S. Embassy or Consulate immediately if you are abroad.**  
   Locate the "American Citizen Services" (ACS) section. Tell them you have a "passport in hand" that is flagging as invalid or cancelled. Ask for an emergency appointment or a "status verification" to see if the record can be cleared without a full replacement.

5. **Direct airline supervisors to contact the CBP Regional Carrier Liaison Group (RCLG).**  
   If you are being denied boarding for a U.S.-bound flight, ask the airline supervisor to contact the RCLG. This 24/7 unit provides real-time document validity advice to carriers and can sometimes resolve "mistaken" flags if the traveler is physically present with the document.

6. **Gather your recent passport history for the official representative.**  
   Have ready the dates for:
   - When you last renewed your passport.
   - Any previous reports you made about a lost or stolen passport (even if you later found it).
   - Any recent applications that were denied or suspended.

7. **Schedule an "Urgent Travel" appointment if the record cannot be cleared.**  
   If NPIC confirms the passport is permanently invalidated (even in error), you must apply in person at a Passport Agency.
   - You must have proof of international travel within 14 calendar days.
   - You must bring the invalid passport, a new DS-11 application, and standard passport fees including the $60 expedite fee.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify who made a mistaken report or file a complaint until you have a valid travel document.
- You do not need to contact local police unless you suspect identity theft is the cause of the invalidation.
- You do not need to determine if your airline is at fault for the database error until your immediate travel is resolved.

## Important reassurance
System errors and "false matches" in international databases like Interpol's SLTD do occur, often due to clerical errors during renewal or previous lost-passport reports that were never properly closed. While the document cannot be "fixed" by the airline, federal authorities have established pathways (Emergency Passports and RCLG verification) specifically to handle these administrative conflicts.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate stabilization steps for travelers facing a document rejection at an airport or border. It does not cover long-term legal remedies for identity theft or the processing of routine renewals.

## Important note
The Department of State is the only authority that can definitively verify or change the status of a U.S. passport record. Decisions made by foreign border authorities or private air carriers regarding boarding are ultimately at their discretion under their respective laws and terms of carriage.
