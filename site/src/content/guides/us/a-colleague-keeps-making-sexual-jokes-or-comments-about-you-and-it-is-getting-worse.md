# What to do if… a colleague keeps making sexual jokes or comments about you and it is getting worse (USA)

## Short answer
Create a safer pause: move near other people or away from the colleague, and contact someone at work you trust. You can choose whether to raise it through a supervisor, manager, HR contact, union representative, or another workplace channel; you do not have to confront the colleague first.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay alone with the colleague if you feel unsafe.
- Do not feel you have to laugh, reply, or play along.
- Do not confront the colleague if you think it may make the situation less safe.
- Do not send a long or heated message while you are overwhelmed.
- Do not access accounts, files, or messages you are not authorized to access.
- Do not make a major job decision while you are trying to get through the immediate moment.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Move to a more public area, stay near a supportive coworker, or leave the immediate work area. Contact on-site security, if your workplace has it, or a manager if you need help creating distance. Call 911 if you are in immediate danger.
2. **Choose one support contact.** Ask a trusted coworker to stay with you or accompany you. You can also contact a supervisor, manager, HR contact, union representative, or another workplace channel that feels safer.
3. **Ask what immediate separation options can be arranged.** Examples include not working alone with the colleague, moving your seating or workspace, adjusting a schedule, or naming one manager as your point of contact.
4. **Use a short boundary only if it feels safe.** You can say: “The sexual jokes or comments about me are unwelcome. Stop.” Then disengage. Skip this step if it may increase risk.
5. If safe and useful, keep existing messages and a brief private note of what happened; only keep records you can access legitimately and do not put yourself at risk.
6. **Raise any retaliation concern simply.** If you choose to speak to someone at work, you can say that you are worried about being treated differently after raising the issue.
7. **Use specialist support or official external information when you are ready.** RAINN offers confidential 24/7 hotline support. The official EEOC pages explain sexual harassment, retaliation, external routes, and time limits. EEOC time limits may be short and rules vary, so check the official time-limits page or ask EEOC if you want to explore an external route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to quit, transfer, or take legal action.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to make a formal internal complaint or explore an external route.
- You do not need a perfectly written account before asking for support or a safer work arrangement.
- You do not need to confront the colleague again.
- You do not need to decide the exact label for the behavior right now.

## Important reassurance
You are not overreacting by making space and seeking support. You can take one step at a time and choose the option that feels safest.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help from a union representative, an employment attorney, EEOC, or a support organization such as RAINN.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Call 911 if you are in immediate danger.
