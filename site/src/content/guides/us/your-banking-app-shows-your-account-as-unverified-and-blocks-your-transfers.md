# What to do if… your banking app shows your account as unverified and blocks your transfers (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank using a trusted number, such as the number on the back of your card or the bank’s official website, and ask what exact verification or security step is needed to restore transfers. If a bill is due soon, ask the bank what payment options still work while the app transfer feature is blocked.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click “fix your verification” links from texts, emails, search ads, or social media messages.
- Do not share one-time passcodes, passwords, PINs, or full account details with anyone who contacts you.
- Do not approve login prompts or device prompts you did not start yourself.
- Do not install remote-access apps for someone claiming to be bank support.
- Do not keep resubmitting the same transfer over and over; it may create more security flags.
- Do not move money to a “safe account” because someone is rushing or frightening you.

## What to do now
1. **Call the bank through a trusted route.**  
   Use the number on the back of your debit card, a statement, or the bank’s official website. Ask for digital banking support and, if needed, fraud or security support. Say: “After the app update, my account is marked unverified and transfers are blocked. What exact verification or security step is needed, and what transfer methods are still available?”

2. **Confirm what is blocked and what still works.**  
   Check whether you can still view balances, use your debit card, withdraw cash, receive deposits, use bill pay, deposit checks, or access online banking in a browser. Write down only the payments due in the next few days.

3. **Do low-risk app checks without following outside links.**  
   Confirm the app came from the Apple App Store or Google Play. Force close the app, restart your phone, check that date and time are set automatically, and sign in again. Try the bank’s website in a browser by typing the address yourself or using a saved trusted bookmark.

4. **Ask what verification path the bank wants.**  
   The bank may ask for secure document upload, identity questions, a photo ID check, a phone number review, or an in-branch visit. Ask the bank to describe the step from their side before you upload documents or change settings in the app.

5. **Ask for an urgent payment workaround.**  
   Options vary by bank, but you can ask whether web banking, bill pay, a debit card payment, an ATM cash withdrawal, a branch transfer, a cashier’s check, or a wire is available. Ask about fees before using a wire or cashier’s check.

6. **Protect the account if anything looks unfamiliar.**  
   Look for unexpected login alerts, changed contact details, new payees, unfamiliar devices, or transfers you did not attempt. If you see any of these, tell the bank immediately and ask them to secure the account before re-enabling transfers.

7. **Contact urgent payees before a fee is added.**  
   For rent, utilities, loans, or other time-sensitive payments, tell the payee you have a temporary bank access problem and ask what short grace period or alternate payment method they can accept.

8. **Keep a short record.**  
   Save screenshots of the “unverified” message and blocked transfer message. Write down call dates, names or departments, what you were told, and any case number.

9. **Escalate if the bank does not resolve it.**  
   Ask the bank how to submit a written complaint. If you still cannot get a clear answer, use an official consumer complaint route such as the CFPB. For a credit union, the NCUA complaint route may apply. For a national bank or federal savings association, the OCC HelpWithMyBank route may apply. If you are unsure which regulator applies, USA.gov and Federal Reserve Consumer Help can help point you to the right route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to close your account or switch banks today.
- You do not need to wipe your phone or repeatedly reinstall the app unless your bank or a trusted security professional tells you to.
- You do not need to make long-term payment plans right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to file a regulator complaint before you have contacted the bank and recorded what happened.

## Important reassurance
An “unverified” or blocked-transfer message does not automatically mean your money is gone. It may be a verification, fraud-prevention, or technical restriction, and the safest first step is to contact the bank through a trusted route and confirm what they need from you.

## Scope note
These are first steps to regain transfer access, avoid scam traps, and cover urgent payments. If the restriction continues, later decisions may need help from the bank’s complaint team, a consumer complaint route, or another qualified professional.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Bank processes vary, and a bank may restrict transfer features for security, fraud-prevention, technical, or compliance reasons. If anyone pressures you to share codes or move money for safety, stop and contact your bank through a trusted number.
