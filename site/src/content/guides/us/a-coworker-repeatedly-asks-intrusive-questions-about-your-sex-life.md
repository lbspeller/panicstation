# What to do if… a coworker repeatedly asks intrusive questions about your sex life (USA)

## Short answer
Repeated intrusive sexual questions from a coworker may be sexual harassment. Create some distance, make a brief record of what happened, and use a reporting route listed in your employer’s policy when you can.

## Do not do these things
- Do not share more personal details just to keep the peace.
- Do not feel pressured to confront the person if that feels unsafe.
- Do not keep handling it alone if the questions continue or become more intrusive.
- Do not send retaliatory or insulting messages.
- Do not feel that you have to rely only on an informal promise if the questions continue.
- Do not assume you have to quit immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer buffer.** Reduce time alone with the person where you reasonably can. Stay near other people, avoid private one-to-one settings, or use a neutral exit line such as: “I need to get back to work.”
2. **Use one clear boundary only if it feels safe.** You can say: “Stop asking me about my sex life. I do not want to discuss that at work.” You do not need to debate or explain.
3. **Make a private factual log.** Note the date, time, place, what was said or done, any witnesses, and how you responded. Keep the log somewhere private that you can safely access.
4. **Preserve relevant messages that already exist.** Keep emails, texts, chat messages, or meeting notes where you can do so without forwarding confidential workplace information or breaking workplace rules. Do not try to create more incidents to collect evidence.
5. **Check your employer’s reporting route.** Look for the anti-harassment policy or handbook. Use a listed route such as a manager, HR contact, or reporting channel. If your manager is involved or dismissive, use another listed route where available.
6. **Ask about practical measures.** You can ask whether a seating change, schedule change, different meeting arrangement, different reporting line, or another temporary measure is available while the concern is addressed.
7. **Check promptly whether an external EEOC route may apply.** EEOC coverage and filing time limits can depend on the workplace and circumstances. Federal employees and applicants for federal jobs generally follow a different process. You do not need to work this out alone; use the EEOC resources below or ask EEOC about the applicable route.
8. **Treat immediate danger as urgent.** If there are threats, stalking, coercion, or physical intimidation, move to a safer place and call 911 if you believe you are in immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to file an external charge or take legal action.
- You do not need to write a perfect narrative before raising the concern.
- You do not need to confront the coworker again if that increases risk.
- You do not need to make a major job decision today.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to decide whether the conduct meets a legal definition before taking first steps. Repeated intrusive sexual questions can be unsettling, and it is reasonable to create distance, record what happened, and ask for the questions to stop.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on your workplace response, the facts, and any applicable federal, state, or local rules. Specialist support may help with those later decisions.

## Important note
This guide provides general information for urgent first steps. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 911.
