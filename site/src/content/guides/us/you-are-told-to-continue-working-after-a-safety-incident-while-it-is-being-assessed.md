# What to do if… you are told to continue working after a safety incident while it is being assessed (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a safer position away from the affected task or area. Tell your supervisor what you are concerned about, ask what has changed to make the work safe, and ask for other work while the incident is assessed. If there is an emergency or an imminent life-threatening danger, follow workplace emergency procedures and call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not re-enter the affected area or restart the affected task while you believe doing so could expose you to danger.
- Do not make the conversation about blame in the moment; keep it focused on the hazard, what has changed, and what work can be done safely.
- Do not sign a statement saying the area or task is safe if you do not believe that is accurate.
- Do not treat a general assurance that the incident is being reviewed as an answer to your specific safety concern.
- Do not leave the worksite merely because you filed a complaint, unless leaving is necessary for safety or your employer tells you to leave.
- Do not post incident details on social media while the situation is still unfolding.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Step away from the equipment, task, or area involved. If there is an emergency, follow workplace emergency procedures and call 911 if needed.
2. **State the concern plainly.** Say: “There was a safety incident at [location]. I am concerned that continuing this task could expose me to [hazard].”
3. **Ask what has changed.** Ask what has been done to make the affected work safe and who confirmed that the work can restart.
4. **Ask for other work.** Request a different task or area while the incident is being assessed.
5. **Make a short factual note.** Record the time, location, what happened, the hazard you are concerned about, who told you to continue, what you asked, and the response. Ask for any instruction to restart the affected work in writing where practical.
6. **Use the OSHA dangerous-work refusal guidance only where it fits.** OSHA describes a narrow situation where refusing a specific dangerous task may be protected: where possible, you asked the employer to correct the danger and it was not corrected; you genuinely believe there is imminent danger; a reasonable person would agree there is a real danger of death or serious injury; and there is not enough time to use regular enforcement channels. OSHA advises asking for correction or other work, saying you will not perform the affected work until the hazard is corrected, and remaining at the worksite until your employer tells you to leave, unless staying would expose you to danger.
7. **Report an unresolved hazard promptly.** You can use OSHA’s safety and health complaint route. Check OSHA’s State Plans page to find the appropriate workplace safety agency for your location and type of employment.
8. **Act promptly if retaliation starts.** Record any threats, discipline, schedule changes, pay changes, or other adverse actions. An OSH Act section 11(c) retaliation complaint has a 30-day deadline, and other whistleblower laws can have different deadlines. OSHA whistleblower complaints cannot be anonymous. If OSHA proceeds with an investigation, OSHA will notify your employer and give the employer an opportunity to respond.
9. **Prioritise health after an injury or possible exposure.** Use the workplace emergency or medical process. If a hazardous chemical may be involved, ask for the Safety Data Sheet and share it with medical staff if you seek care.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to resign or take formal legal action.
- You do not need to determine blame while the incident is still being assessed.
- You do not need to write a long report immediately; a short factual note is enough for now.
- You do not need to identify every possible regulation before asking for a safer task or reporting the hazard.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to pause and ask what has changed after a safety incident. You can keep the conversation calm and specific: identify the affected work, name the concern, ask what has been done, and ask for a safer alternative while the situation is assessed.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the period immediately after a workplace safety incident. Later decisions may depend on the hazard, your type of work, your location, your employer’s response, and advice from an appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Use emergency procedures and emergency services for immediate danger.
