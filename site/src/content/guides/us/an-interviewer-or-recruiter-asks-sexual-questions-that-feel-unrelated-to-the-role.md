# What to do if… an interviewer or recruiter asks sexual questions that feel unrelated to the role (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a safe location or end the communication immediately. You are not required to answer personal or sexual questions to be considered for employment in the United States.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide personal sexual, relationship, or medical details to keep the peace or "pass" the interview.
- Do not worry about appearing "unprofessional" for ending an interview where your boundaries are being violated.
- Do not delete any evidence including texts, emails, LinkedIn messages, or call logs.
- Do not post identifying details of the individual or company on social media while you are still in the immediate aftermath.

## What to do now
1. **End the interaction and reach a safe pause immediately.** If you are in a physical office, stand up and leave for a public area or your vehicle; if you are on a video or phone call, hang up without feeling obligated to explain.
2. **Contact a confidential support service if you feel overwhelmed or unsafe.** 
   - Call or text the National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN) at 800-656-HOPE for 24 / 7 confidential support.
3. **Record the details of the interaction while they are fresh.** **Write down the date, time, location, names of everyone present, and the specific questions or comments made.**
   - Include any sensory details like the tone of voice or physical gestures.
   - Save copies of any written communication, including job descriptions and interview invites.
4. **Determine the type of organization involved.** 
   - If a third-party recruiter was involved, contact their agency’s management or HR department to report that their client or employee asked inappropriate sexual questions.
   - If the interviewer was a direct employee of a company, identify their HR department or a senior manager not involved in the interview.
5. **Send a brief, factual notification of the incident.** **Email the relevant HR contact or agency supervisor to state that you are withdrawing or pausing your application due to inappropriate sexual questioning.**
   - Keep the message short and focused on the facts of the questions asked.
   - Request that this incident be logged formally within their system.
6. **Be aware of federal and state filing deadlines if you intend to take legal action.** **Most private sector employees must file a charge with the EEOC within 180 or 300 days, but federal job applicants generally must contact an EEO counselor within 45 days of the incident.**
   - Note that in early 2026, federal guidance regarding harassment shifted; however, Title VII remains the governing law prohibiting sex-based discrimination and harassment.
7. **Secure your personal communication channels.** **Block the individual on social media and personal phone lines if they continue to contact you outside of professional channels.**
   - Ensure you have saved screenshots of any harassing messages before blocking.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide immediately whether you want to file a formal lawsuit or an EEOC charge.
- You do not need to respond to follow-up "apology" or "clarification" messages from the interviewer right now.
- You do not need to figure out how this will affect your long-term career path today.

## Important reassurance
Panic, confusion, or a "freeze" response are standard reactions when professional boundaries are suddenly violated. Leaving the situation or refusing to answer is an appropriate and protective action, regardless of the job opportunity at stake.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only. Later decisions regarding legal action or formal EEOC charges may require consultation with an employment attorney or a state fair employment agency.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and does not constitute legal advice. Deadlines and specific protections vary significantly for federal employees, contractors, and individuals in states with additional labor protections.
