# What to do if… someone you rely on for care makes sexual comments and you fear retaliation (USA)

## Short answer
Get to the safest pause available and avoid handling this alone. If it feels safe, ask a trusted person, agency contact, or facility contact to help ask for the safest available care arrangement without that caregiver where possible.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the caregiver alone if you are worried about retaliation or escalation.
- Do not let anyone pressure you to stay quiet in exchange for care, rides, medication access, keys, or other help.
- Do not give up your phone or agree to be isolated during a visit if you can avoid doing so safely.
- Do not assume you need proof before asking for a different care arrangement.
- Do not make a public post while the caregiver still controls access, transport, keys, or essential assistance if that could increase your immediate risk.

## What to do now
1. **Reach the safest pause available.**  
   If you are in immediate danger, call **911**. If the caregiver is with you now, move to a place where another person is present or end the visit only if you can do so safely.

2. **Ask one trusted person to help with the next contact.**  
   This could be a family member, friend, neighbor, advocate, building staff member, case manager, or another person you choose. Tell them that the caregiver is making sexual comments, you rely on the caregiver for assistance, and you are worried about retaliation.

3. **Contact the care provider if there is one.**  
   Contact the agency supervisor, scheduler, case manager, program coordinator, or facility administrator. You can say: “This caregiver is making sexual comments. I am worried about retaliation and need a safer care arrangement.”  
   Ask what can be changed safely, such as:
   - No further contact with that caregiver where possible
   - A different worker or changed visit arrangement
   - Another person present during visits
   - A plan for keys, transport, medication access, or other assistance the caregiver currently controls
   - One contact person for follow-up

4. **Consider contacting local Adult Protective Services if it may fit your situation.**  
   Adult Protective Services may be an option for an older adult or an adult with a disability who may be experiencing abuse, neglect, self-neglect, or financial exploitation. Local rules and services vary. You can ask what help may be available without having to decide every next step first. The Adult Protective Services resource below lists local contacts. You can also use 211 to look for local services. For an older adult or caregiver, Eldercare Locator can help find community services.

5. **Contact the Long-Term Care Ombudsman Program if you live in a long-term care setting.**  
   If you live in a nursing home, board and care home, or assisted living facility, the Long-Term Care Ombudsman Program can help you raise a concern and ask about safer care arrangements.

6. **Use specialist support without deciding about a report today.**  
   RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline offers support 24 hours a day. You can ask about privacy before sharing identifying details, especially if you are concerned about what may happen after the conversation.

7. **Keep a brief private note only if it feels safe.**  
   You may note the date, what was said, and what assistance you depend on, but skip this if keeping a note could put you at risk.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to contact police, make a formal complaint, or take legal action.
- You do not need a perfect explanation before asking for a safer care arrangement.
- You do not need to gather extensive evidence now.
- You do not need to make long-term care decisions while you are trying to reach a safer pause.

## Important reassurance
Relying on someone for daily assistance can make it harder to speak up when their behavior feels unsafe. You are allowed to ask for support, a safer care arrangement, and time to decide what you want to do next.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the immediate situation and protect access to care. Later decisions may need support from a specialist service, advocate, care provider, or other professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
