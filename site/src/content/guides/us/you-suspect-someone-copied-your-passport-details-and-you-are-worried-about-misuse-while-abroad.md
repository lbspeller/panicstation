# What to do if… you suspect someone copied your passport details and you are worried about misuse while abroad (USA)

## Short answer
Keep the physical passport secure, make a clear record of what was copied, and add credit and account protections so you can respond quickly if misuse appears.

## Do not do these things
- Do not report your passport lost or stolen if it is still safely in your possession; once a valid U.S. passport is reported lost or stolen, it is cancelled and is no longer valid for travel.
- Do not send another passport photo or scan to “confirm” details unless you have verified that the request is legitimate and necessary.
- Do not ignore unexpected verification codes, password resets, bank alerts, new account emails, or debt notices.
- Do not assume a copied passport number is the same as someone having your physical passport.
- Do not confront a person or business if that could make you less safe; keep any contact brief and practical.

## What to do now
1. **Get the physical passport back under your control.** If a hotel, rental desk, tour operator, or other business is holding it, ask for it back and store it securely.
2. **Stop further copying where you safely can.** Ask whether they can record only the legally required details rather than keeping a full scan or photo, but do not argue if local rules or safety make that unwise.
3. **Write down what happened.** Note the date, time, place, business or person involved, what was copied, and whether they kept a photo, scan, paper copy, or digital file. Save receipts, messages, and screenshots together.
4. **Check whether anything already looks wrong.** Look for new account emails, unfamiliar credit checks, bank alerts, mobile-phone account changes, password resets, or debt collection messages.
5. **Consider a credit freeze with all three major credit bureaus.** A credit freeze is free and can make it harder for someone to open new credit in your name. For full coverage, place it separately with Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. If that is too much to do right now, a fraud alert is a lighter free option that you can place through one of the three bureaus.
6. **Secure your most important accounts.** Change your email and banking passwords if they may be weak or reused. Turn on multi-factor authentication and transaction alerts for banking, cards, email, mobile-phone, and travel accounts.
7. **Use IdentityTheft.gov if there is a sign of misuse.** If you see a new account, unfamiliar inquiry, debt notice, account takeover, or other suspicious activity, use the official identity theft reporting tool to create a recovery plan and report.
8. **Only report the passport lost or stolen if the physical passport is missing, stolen, or out of your control.** Use the State Department online form filler, mail Form DS-64, or report it while applying for a replacement. If you are abroad and need a replacement passport, contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to apply for a new passport if the passport is safely in your possession and there is no sign of misuse.
- You do not need to contact many offices at once if nothing suspicious has happened; focus on the physical passport, credit protection, account security, and your written record.
- You do not need to solve whether the copying was legal in that country right now.
- You do not need to confront the person or business again unless there is a safe, practical reason.

## Important reassurance
A copied passport number or scan is concerning, but it is not the same as someone having your physical passport. U.S. passport guidance says a person cannot travel using only a U.S. passport number; the practical first step is to protect the real document and watch for identity misuse.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce risk while you are abroad. If misuse appears, later decisions may need help from your bank, the credit bureaus, the State Department, the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, travel, or professional advice. Procedures and local document-checking rules can vary by country, and urgent passport or safety problems should be handled through official consular support or local emergency help.
