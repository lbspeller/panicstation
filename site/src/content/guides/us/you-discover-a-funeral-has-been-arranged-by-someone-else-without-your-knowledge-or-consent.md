# What to do if… you discover a funeral has been arranged by someone else without your knowledge or consent (USA)

## Short answer
Call the funeral home now and ask it to pause any irreversible step that has not yet happened, especially cremation, burial, release, or transfer of the remains, while it checks what authority is required under the applicable state law. Ask what documents it needs to review and when the next scheduled step is due to happen.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume that being immediate family automatically proves that you control the arrangements.
- Don’t sign a cremation, burial, authorization, payment, or financing document unless you understand what it authorizes.
- Don’t rely only on a phone conversation; ask for written confirmation of what has and has not been paused.
- Don’t threaten staff or make public accusations while the position is still being checked.
- Don’t pay money just to try to take control of the arrangements.

## What to do now
1. **Call the funeral home and ask for an immediate pause.**  
   Say: “There is a dispute about who is authorized to give instructions. Please pause any step that has not yet happened, especially cremation, burial, release, or transfer of the remains, while you check what authority is required under state law.”  
   Ask for written confirmation of the pause, what has already happened, and the date and time of the next scheduled step.

2. **Ask what the funeral home needs to verify authority.**  
   Ask what it can confirm about:
   - who gave instructions
   - whether any cremation, burial, or other authorization has been signed
   - what document or state-law priority it is relying on
   - what documents it wants you or another family member to provide  
   The funeral home may not be able to disclose every document or personal detail to every caller.

3. **Look for documents left by the person who died.**  
   Check estate-planning files, email, safe storage, and paperwork held by a lawyer for:
   - a document appointing someone to control funeral decisions, burial, cremation, or other handling of the remains
   - written funeral or burial wishes
   - a prepaid funeral contract or existing funeral-home paperwork  
   A prepaid plan may show wishes or arrangements without appointing the legally authorized person. Send any relevant document to the funeral home and ask it to review it.

4. **Ask what happens if relatives with the same priority disagree.**  
   Do not assume that every state uses the same rule. Ask the funeral home what the applicable state rule requires and whether it needs further documents, agreement from additional relatives, or legal direction.

5. **Get local legal help urgently if an irreversible step is close.**  
   If the funeral home says that cremation, burial, release, or transfer may happen soon and authority is still disputed, contact a local attorney who handles probate, estate, or funeral-related disputes. Tell them the next scheduled date and time.

6. **Use the state licensing-board route for complaint information if needed.**  
   If the funeral home will not explain what it needs or you need the relevant complaint route, use the state licensing-board directory and contact the appropriate board for the state where the funeral home is located. A board may provide licensing or complaint information, but do not assume that contacting it will automatically pause the arrangements.

7. **Ask for pricing information before agreeing to costs.**  
   Ask for accurate price information by phone. If you visit the funeral home in person, ask for its written General Price List to keep. Ask what additional costs may arise while the dispute is being checked, and keep copies of messages, documents, and invoices.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve inheritance or probate disagreements right now.
- You do not need to decide every service detail today.
- You do not need to make a public announcement or answer every family message now.
- You can focus first on pausing any irreversible step and confirming the next scheduled date and time.

## Important reassurance
Finding out late can feel deeply upsetting. Asking for a pause, written confirmation, and a check of the relevant documents is a reasonable first step when authority is unclear.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A longer dispute may need state-specific legal advice from a local attorney.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. In the USA, who may control funeral arrangements, burial, cremation, or other handling of the remains can depend on state law, the circumstances, and any valid written appointment made by the person who died.
