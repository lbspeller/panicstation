# What to do if… your email messages start auto-archiving or skipping your inbox unexpectedly (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible account-security issue until you have checked the settings. Change your email password, turn on two-factor authentication, sign out other sessions where available, then note and disable any unfamiliar rules, filters, or forwarding settings.

## Do not do these things
- Do not start password resets for other important accounts until you have secured the email account.
- Do not use security links from unexpected emails, messages, or pop-ups. Open your provider’s official site or app directly.
- Do not delete large amounts of mail while stressed. Messages in Archive, Spam, Trash, or Deleted Items may help you understand what changed.
- Do not remove a session, device, or shared-mailbox connection unless it is clearly unfamiliar. Ask your employer or school IT team before changing a managed account.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the email account first.** Open your provider’s official site or app directly. Change the password to a strong, unique password. Turn on two-factor authentication. Sign out other sessions or devices where your provider offers that option. Check that the recovery email address and phone number are still yours.

2. **If this is a work or school account, contact IT or security promptly.** Tell them that messages are being moved or skipped without a settings change. Follow their process before disabling rules or removing connections from a managed mailbox.

3. **Check inbox rules and filters.** Look for unfamiliar settings that archive, move, delete, forward, redirect, or mark messages as read. For Gmail, review filters for actions such as archiving or forwarding. For Outlook or Outlook.com, review rules that move or delete incoming messages. If it feels manageable, take a screenshot or note the setting before disabling it. Deleting it can wait until you are sure it is unwanted.

4. **Check forwarding and redirect settings.** Disable any forwarding address or redirect rule that you did not set. In Gmail, review the forwarding settings as well as filters. In Outlook or Outlook.com, check whether a rule forwards or redirects incoming messages.

5. **Review sessions and devices if your provider shows them.** Sign out other sessions where that option is available. Remove a device or session only when it is clearly unfamiliar. If you are unsure, leave it in place temporarily and ask your provider or IT team.

6. **Check where new mail is landing.** Look in All Mail or All Messages, Archive, Spam or Junk, Trash or Deleted Items, and any inbox tabs shown by your provider, such as Focused or Other. Send yourself a harmless test email from another account and note where it appears.

7. **If the unwanted behavior returns, check the devices you use for email.** Temporarily sign out of one mail app or device, then check whether the issue returns. Update your device security software and run a scan.

8. **Use an optional reporting route only when relevant.** If the incident involved cyber-enabled crime, scams, money loss, or impersonation, you can file a complaint with the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center. Reporting is separate from securing the account.

## What can wait
- Reorganizing folders, labels, or old messages.
- Writing a detailed explanation for contacts.
- Working out exactly how the setting changed.
- Making wider account changes that are not needed to stop the immediate diversion.

## Important reassurance
This can be caused by a rule, filter, forwarding setting, mail-client issue, or account-access issue. Securing the account first and then checking the diversion settings is a sensible, reversible way to reduce the immediate risk.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from your email provider, your employer or school IT team, or a qualified security professional.
