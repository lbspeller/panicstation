# What to do if… your browser shows connection not private warnings on many trusted sites at once (USA)

## Short answer
Stop entering passwords, payment details, or personal information. Switch to a connection you trust, such as your phone’s hotspot, and check your computer’s date, time, and time zone.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t bypass the warning to sign in, pay, or enter personal information.
- Don’t install “certificate fixes,” “security updates,” or browser tools suggested by pop-ups or unfamiliar websites.
- Don’t keep using public Wi-Fi for sensitive tasks if you have cellular or another trusted network available.
- Don’t permanently disable security protections just to make the warning disappear.
- Don’t assume it is safe because the warning appears on many sites.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the sensitive task.** Close login, payment, email, banking, healthcare, shopping checkout, and work tabs for now.
2. **Check the computer’s date, time, and time zone.** Correct anything that is wrong, then try one trusted site again. A wrong clock can make valid certificates look expired or not yet valid.
3. **Switch networks to isolate the problem.**
   - Disconnect from the current Wi-Fi and try mobile hotspot or cellular.
   - If the warnings disappear on hotspot, the problem is likely connected to the original Wi-Fi, router, or network environment.
4. **If you are on hotel, airport, café, school, or guest Wi-Fi, treat it as a possible captive-portal problem.**
   - Disconnect and reconnect to the network.
   - Use only the venue’s stated Wi-Fi name and sign-in instructions.
   - Do not enter passwords or card details and do not bypass certificate warnings just to reach the Wi-Fi login page.
   - If the login page does not appear, use hotspot or ask the venue for help.
5. **Temporarily test common traffic-intercepting tools.**
   - Temporarily turn off VPN, proxy settings, or antivirus features labelled “HTTPS scanning,” “encrypted web scanning,” or similar.
   - Test one trusted site.
   - Turn protections back on unless your IT team or the product vendor tells you otherwise.
6. **If this is a work or school device, or you are on a work or school network, report it instead of changing managed settings.** Tell the helpdesk or security team: “I am seeing certificate or connection-not-private warnings on multiple trusted sites at once.”
7. **If you entered a password after the warnings began, secure the account from a known-good connection or device.**
   - Change that password, starting with email.
   - Sign out of other devices if the service offers that option.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication if available.
   - Check the account recovery email address and phone number.
8. **If you think someone may have used your personal information or taken over an account, use official recovery guidance.** IdentityTheft.gov can help you make a recovery plan, and FTC guidance can help with hacked email or social media accounts.

## What can wait
- You do not need to factory reset your computer right now.
- You do not need to decide whether it is malware before checking the clock and testing another network.
- You do not need to contact every website; many trusted sites failing at once usually points back to the device, browser, security software, or network.
- You do not need to solve public Wi-Fi access right now if hotspot or cellular lets you finish the urgent task safely.

## Important reassurance
This pattern is often caused by fixable things such as a wrong clock, a Wi-Fi sign-in page, VPN or proxy settings, or security software inspecting encrypted traffic. It is still worth treating the warning seriously until you know why it is happening.

## Scope note
These are first steps to contain the immediate risk and narrow down the cause. If the warnings continue across multiple trusted networks after date, time, VPN, proxy, and security-software checks, get hands-on technical help before using the computer for sensitive logins.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional cybersecurity, legal, financial, or technical advice. If you believe credentials, payment details, or identity information were exposed, prioritize securing the affected accounts and contacting the relevant providers.
