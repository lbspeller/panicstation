# What to do if… your credit card payment is marked as returned after you thought it cleared (USA)

## Short answer
Verify your current bank balance and immediately make a replacement payment (at least the minimum) through your card issuer's app or website to stop further fees or delinquency.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep attempting the same payment method without verifying your bank's available balance first.
- Do not wait for a paper notice in the mail if you have already seen the "returned" status online.
- Do not assume the "posted" status on your bank statement means the money has reached the issuer; it can take several days for a return to process.
- Do not ignore your bank account balance; the card issuer may attempt to "re-present" the payment automatically, which could cause additional overdraft fees.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the exact reason for the return by checking your secure message center or alerts.**  
   - Common codes include NSF (Non-Sufficient Funds), "Account Closed," or "Invalid Account Number."
   - If the return was due to a typo in the routing or account number, do not use that saved payment method again.

2. **Make a replacement payment immediately using a confirmed funding source.**  
   - Use a different bank account or a debit card if your issuer accepts one-time debit payments for immediate credit.
   - **Pay at least the minimum amount due** to prevent the account from being marked as late or delinquent.

3. **Check the legal cutoff time for today if the due date has passed.**  
   - Under Regulation Z, the cutoff time for electronic payments cannot be earlier than 5 p.m. in the time zone where the issuer receives payments.
   - If your due date falls on a Sunday or a federal holiday and the issuer does not accept payments that day, a payment made by the next business day is legally considered on time.

4. **Contact the card issuer's customer service department using the number on the back of your card.**  
   - **Ask the representative to waive the returned payment fee** if this is your first occurrence or if the return was due to a technical error.
   - **Request a "goodwill" removal of any late fees** associated with the return if you are making the replacement payment while on the phone.

5. **Verify if the return will trigger a "Penalty APR" on your specific account.**  
   - Ask the representative if the returned payment has changed your interest rate.
   - **Note the date and the name of the representative** you spoke with for your records.

6. **Check your bank account for a "Non-Sufficient Funds" (NSF) or overdraft fee.**  
   - If the return was a bank-side error, ask your bank to provide a letter of explanation so you can forward it to the credit card issuer.

## What can wait
- You do not need to dispute your credit report today; late payments are generally not reported to credit bureaus until they are at least 30 days past the due date.
- You do not need to close the bank account or the credit card account.
- You do not need to file a formal complaint with the CFPB until you have given the issuer a chance to resolve the fee or interest rate issue through their standard support.

## Important reassurance
A single returned payment is a common banking issue and does not result in immediate credit score damage. If you replace the payment within a few days of the due date, you can typically prevent the most severe consequences like penalty interest rates or negative credit reporting.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a returned payment notification. It does not cover long-term debt management, credit repair strategies, or legal disputes regarding banking errors.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance regarding US credit card regulations and banking practices. Individual cardholder agreements and specific bank policies vary; always refer to your issuer’s official terms for definitive rules on fees and reporting.
