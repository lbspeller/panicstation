# What to do if… your employer is reviewing roles and has asked for an updated CV or resume (USA)

## Short answer
Ask in writing what the role review is for, what your updated resume or CV will be used for, and what the next steps are. Do not resign or sign anything immediately; taking time to understand the process may protect your options.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign in panic or offer to leave to look cooperative.
- Do not sign a severance agreement, release, or new contract immediately.
- Do not assume the request proves either a routine update or a job loss.
- Do not exaggerate dates, titles, duties, or achievements on your resume.
- Do not delete work messages or records because you are panicking. Follow normal retention rules, and do not remove or copy confidential, proprietary, or personal information that you are not allowed to keep.
- Do not post accusations publicly while you are still trying to understand the process.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the purpose and next steps in writing.** Ask whether this is a reorganization, role-mapping exercise, internal selection process, reassignment process, or possible position elimination. Ask what the updated resume or CV will be used for and when decisions are expected.
2. **Ask whether providing the resume is optional.** A simple question is: “What happens if I do not provide an updated resume?”
3. **Keep permitted records.** Save the request, your response, and meeting invitations if you are allowed to keep them. After a verbal discussion, send a short follow-up email confirming your understanding. Do not copy restricted business information, other people’s personal data, or material you are not entitled to retain.
4. **Update your resume carefully.** Use accurate dates, titles, duties, and achievements. If your working title differs from the title in your HR record, ask how the business wants that shown.
5. **Ask about notice rules if a larger layoff or site closure is mentioned.** Ask whether any federal WARN or state notice rules may apply and whether any notice has been issued.
6. **Ask for the selection criteria if roles may be reduced or reassigned.** Keep a dated note of the answer. If you are concerned that the criteria may be biased, record the facts calmly and consider independent advice.
7. **Ask for representation if a qualifying union issue arises.** If you are union-represented and a meeting becomes an investigatory interview that you reasonably believe could lead to discipline, ask for a union representative before answering questions.
8. **Gather basic personal records you are allowed to keep.** Keep copies of recent pay stubs, your benefits information, and an HR contact route. If your employment ends, unemployment insurance is generally claimed through the state where you worked. If you worked remotely, across state lines, or in more than one state, ask a state unemployment office where to file.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to accept a new role, severance agreement, or proposed separation.
- You do not need to threaten legal action to ask for basic process information.
- You do not need to build a full job-search plan today.
- You do not need to volunteer personal medical or family details while asking what the role review means.

## Important reassurance
A request for an updated resume or CV does not by itself confirm what the outcome will be. Asking for the purpose, timeline, and next steps in writing is a steady way to slow the situation down and keep your choices open.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and prevent rushed decisions. Later decisions may need specialist advice based on your state, employment documents, union status, and the facts of the process.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employment rights and processes vary by state and situation. Consider independent advice before agreeing to a separation, signing a release, or making a decision under pressure.
