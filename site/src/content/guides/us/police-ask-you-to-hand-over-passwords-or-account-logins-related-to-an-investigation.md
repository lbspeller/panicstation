# What to do if… police ask you to hand over passwords or account logins related to an investigation (USA)

## Short answer
State clearly that you are invoking your right to remain silent and your right to an attorney. Do not physically unlock the device, provide a passcode, or share login credentials unless a judge has issued a specific order to do so after you have consulted with a lawyer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide a verbal passcode or type it into the device "just to clear things up."
- Do not use biometric features (FaceID, TouchID) to unlock the device if requested, as this may be interpreted as consent or a non-testimonial act in some jurisdictions.
- Do not share two-factor authentication (2FA) codes, backup codes, or password manager master keys.
- Do not log into cloud accounts, social media, or email on a police device or your own while in custody.
- Do not argue the law or Fourth Amendment rights with the officer; simply state your invocation.
- Do not delete data, wipe the device remotely, or change passwords after a seizure, as this may lead to "obstruction of justice" or "tampering with evidence" charges.

## What to do now
1. **Determine if you are being detained.** Ask: “Am I free to leave?”
   - If the officer says yes, leave the area immediately and contact a lawyer.
   - If the officer says no or remains silent, you are being detained or are under arrest.
2. **Verbally invoke your constitutional rights.** Speak clearly and do not use ambiguous language.
   - Say: “I am invoking my right to remain silent. I want to speak to a lawyer. I do not consent to a search of my devices or my accounts.”
3. **Stop all physical interaction with the device.** Place the device down or allow it to be seized without providing the unlock code. 
   - Note that physically unlocking the device for an officer often functions as "consent" even if you do not say the word.
4. **Identify the legal authority being used.** If the officer claims to have a warrant, ask to see a physical or digital copy.
   - Check if the warrant specifically names the device or account they are asking to enter.
   - Do not provide the password even if they have a warrant for the *device* itself; a warrant to search a device is not always a court order to *compel* your password.
5. **Handle border or airport requests specifically.** At U.S. ports of entry, Customs and Border Protection (CBP) has broader authority under Directive 3340-049B (Jan 2026).
   - If you are a U.S. citizen, you cannot be denied entry for refusing a password, but your device can be seized for 5–15+ days.
   - If you are a non-citizen (visa/ESTA), refusal to provide a password can result in denial of entry.
6. **Request a "Compelled Decryption" hearing.** If you are served with a court order (not just a search warrant) to provide a password, tell the officer you will comply only after your lawyer has reviewed the order.
7. **Document the interaction details.** As soon as you are alone, write down:
   - Officer names, badge numbers, and agency.
   - The exact time and location of the request.
   - Whether any threats were made regarding the refusal.
   - Whether they attempted to use your finger or face to unlock the device against your will.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain why you have a passcode or why you want privacy.
- You do not need to decide if you will eventually cooperate; that is a decision for after you meet with counsel.
- You do not need to notify the service provider (Google, Apple, Meta) immediately; your lawyer will handle preservation or "Gag Order" checks.
- You do not need to prove your innocence by "showing them one thing" on the device.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant pressure or fear when an authority figure demands access to your personal life. Invoking your rights is a standard legal procedure and is not an admission of guilt. Most digital privacy laws in 2026 are complex and vary by state; pausing to get a lawyer is the most effective way to protect your long-term legal standing.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization during a police encounter regarding digital access. It does not cover the technical process of encryption, the specific "Foregone Conclusion" doctrine used in grand jury subpoenas, or long-term defense strategies for digital evidence.

## Important note
This information is not legal advice. Laws regarding "Compelled Decryption" and the Fifth Amendment are currently subject to a circuit split in U.S. federal courts and vary significantly by state. Border searches are governed by administrative directives that differ from domestic police stops. Always consult a criminal defense attorney immediately if your devices are seized or if you are served with a warrant.
