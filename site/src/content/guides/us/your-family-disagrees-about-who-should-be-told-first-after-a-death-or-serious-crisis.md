# What to do if… your family disagrees about who should be told first after a death or serious crisis (USA)

## Short answer
Pause the argument. Use one temporary coordinator for the next round of calls, agree a short list of people who should not hear the news second-hand, and hold wider messages until those direct calls are made.

## Do not do these things
- Do not post online or send a broad family message before the agreed direct calls are made.
- Do not treat being told first as a final decision about anyone’s importance, status, or legal authority.
- Do not have several people contact the same relative with different versions of the news.
- Do not share details that have not been confirmed.
- Do not use children or teens as messengers.
- Do not force an overwhelmed person to make calls they cannot manage.
- Do not try to settle older family disputes while the news is still being shared.

## What to do now
1. **Create a brief pause.**  
   Say: “Let’s stop for five minutes and make one contact list so nobody hears this second-hand.” If the argument becomes threatening or anyone is in immediate danger, move to a safer place and dial 911.

2. **Write down only what is confirmed.**  
   Use one or two factual sentences. If the situation is a death, only say that the person has died when this has been confirmed by an appropriate source. Do not guess about causes, blame, or details that may change.

3. **Choose one temporary coordinator for the next round of calls.**  
   The coordinator keeps the list, tracks who has been reached, and helps prevent duplicate messages. This is a practical role for the next few hours. It does not decide anyone’s family status or legal authority.

4. **Make a short direct-call list.**  
   Include people who are most likely to be harmed by hearing indirectly, people in the same household, and anyone with an urgent care or support need. Consider whether children should hear the news from an appropriate adult who can stay with them.

5. **Assign one caller to each person.**  
   Parallel calls are fine when they are coordinated. Mark each name as `not called`, `contacted`, or `call back needed`. If you reach voicemail, ask for a call back rather than leaving sensitive details unless there is a clear reason to do otherwise.

6. **Use one short script.**  
   For a confirmed death, use:  
   “I’m sorry to tell you that [Name] died on [date, if confirmed]. We do not have more confirmed details to share yet. We are calling close family directly before sending a wider update. Please hold off posting or forwarding messages for now.”

   For another serious family crisis, replace the first sentence with the brief confirmed fact and keep the rest minimal.

7. **Ask involved services how family updates should work.**  
   If a hospital, hospice, care facility, medical examiner, or coroner is involved, ask which contact route to use, what staff can share, and whether one family point of contact would help reduce duplicate calls.

8. **Send a minimal wider update after the direct calls.**  
   Share the confirmed fact, what is still unknown, and a request not to post or forward details yet. Say that another update will follow when there is confirmed information.

9. **Use one boundary sentence if the disagreement restarts.**  
   Say: “This is only the order for the next calls so people do not hear second-hand. We can discuss the wider family questions later.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide who matters most in the family.
- You do not need to resolve old disputes or define anyone’s long-term role.
- You do not need to decide funeral plans, obituary wording, travel, or a social-media announcement now.
- You do not need to notify workplaces, friends, or wider groups unless there is an urgent practical reason and the family agrees.
- If the situation is a death, the direct family calls can come before many administrative tasks. Check the official links below promptly afterward because some notifications may need early attention. Funeral homes generally tell Social Security when someone dies, but check rather than assume.

## Important reassurance
A temporary call order is not a judgement about who is closest or most important. The aim is simply to reduce the chance that someone receives serious news through a forwarded message, a group chat, or social media.

## Scope note
These are first steps for sharing serious family news during the earliest manageable pause. Later decisions about legal authority, funeral arrangements, estate tasks, safety planning, or longer-term family boundaries may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Rules, roles, and available services vary by state and circumstance. If anyone is in immediate danger, dial 911.
