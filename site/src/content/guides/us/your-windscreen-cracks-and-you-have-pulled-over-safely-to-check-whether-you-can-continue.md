# What to do if… your windscreen cracks and you have pulled over safely to check whether you can continue (USA)

## Short answer
Do not continue driving if the crack is in your direct line of sight, is larger than 3/4 of an inch, intersects another crack, or reaches the edge of the glass. Use roadside assistance or a tow to move the vehicle to a repair facility.

## Do not do these things
- Do not continue driving if the damage is in the "critical viewing area" (the area cleared by your windshield wipers).
- Do not attempt to "test" the crack by pressing on the glass or applying pressure from the inside.
- Do not use high-heat defrost or maximum air conditioning directly on the damaged glass, as rapid temperature shifts cause cracks to spread.
- Do not apply household tape or film if it creates glare or further obscures your view of the road.
- Do not ignore a crack that has reached the edge of the windshield, as this compromises the structural integrity of the roof support.
- Do not attempt a DIY resin repair while parked in an unsafe location or on a highway shoulder.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm a safe position.** Ensure your hazard lights are on and you are parked completely off the roadway. If you are on a narrow shoulder with high-speed traffic, stay inside the vehicle with your seatbelt fastened.
2. **Evaluate the damage against federal safety thresholds.** Use the following criteria to decide if the vehicle is unsafe to drive:
   - **Size:** Any single chip or "bullseye" larger than 3/4 of an inch (roughly the size of a nickel).
   - **Intersection:** Any two or more cracks that meet or cross each other.
   - **Placement:** Any damage within the area extending upward from the steering wheel, excluding a 2-inch border at the top and a 1-inch border on the sides.
3. **Check for "edge-to-edge" cracks.** If a crack originates from or reaches the outer plastic seal/frame of the windshield, treat the vehicle as non-drivable.
4. **Assess environmental visibility factors.** Consider if upcoming conditions will make the damage more dangerous:
   - Bright, low-angle sunlight creating blinding glare through the cracks.
   - Rain or snow that will catch in the crack and distort your view.
   - Night driving where headlights from oncoming traffic will diffract through the glass.
5. **Contact roadside assistance if thresholds are exceeded.** Request a flatbed tow if the crack is severe. Inform the dispatcher if you are in a vulnerable location.
6. **Limit relocation to the nearest safe haven.** If the damage is minor and you choose to move, drive only to the closest well-lit parking lot or service station.
   - Drive at reduced speeds.
   - Avoid potholes, curbs, and speed bumps that cause frame vibration.
7. **Document the damage for insurance.** Take a clear photo of the crack from the driver's seat perspective and a close-up of the impact point once you are in a secure location.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the glass requires a full replacement or a simple resin repair right now.
- You do not need to research specific state statutes; the federal 3/4-inch and "no intersecting cracks" rules are the safest baseline to follow.
- You do not need to choose a repair shop immediately; your insurance provider will likely provide a list of approved vendors once you file a claim.
- You do not need to worry about deductibles immediately; some states (like FL, KY, and SC) may require $0 deductible glass replacement by law.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel a sense of uncertainty when the glass directly in front of you is damaged. Pulling over to check the obstruction was the correct safety-first action. Most windshield damage is a standard insurance event, and choosing not to drive with compromised visibility is a routine and responsible decision.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety criteria for an immediate roadside decision. It does not cover long-term glass quality standards, insurance negotiations, or technical repair procedures.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and does not constitute legal or professional advice. Windshield regulations (49 CFR 393.60) and state-specific laws vary. If you are unsure if your view is obstructed, assume the vehicle is unsafe and seek professional assistance.
