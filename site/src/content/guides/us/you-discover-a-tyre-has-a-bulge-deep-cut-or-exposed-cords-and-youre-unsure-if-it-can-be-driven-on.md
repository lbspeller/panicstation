# What to do if… you discover a tyre has a bulge, deep cut, or exposed cords and you’re unsure if it can be driven on (USA)

## Short answer
Assume the tire is **structurally compromised and unsafe to drive**. Get the vehicle to a safe stop immediately and arrange for a spare tire installation or a tow, as these defects can lead to a sudden blowout without further warning.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t continue driving at highway speeds, even if the tire pressure feels normal.
- Don’t touch exposed cords or steel belts with bare hands, as they can cause deep cuts or metal splinter injuries.
- Don’t attempt to use "fix-a-flat" foam, patches, or plugs on sidewall damage.
- Don’t ignore a bulge just because it is small; it indicates a failure of the internal carcass.
- Don’t jack up the vehicle if you are on an incline, soft shoulder, or in close proximity to live traffic.

## What to do now
1. **Move the vehicle to a safe pause immediately.** **Gently ease off the accelerator, avoid sudden steering inputs, and navigate to the nearest parking lot, gas station, or wide pull-off area.** 
2. **Perform a quick visual inspection from a safe distance.** **Confirm if you see a protruding lump (bulge), a gash that reveals fabric or metal (deep cut), or visible wiry strands (exposed cords).**
   - Treat any of these three symptoms as a terminal tire failure that requires immediate replacement.
3. **Call 911 if you are stranded in a live traffic lane or a dangerous freeway position.** **If you cannot move the vehicle to a shoulder and are at risk of being struck by other motorists, stay in the vehicle with your seatbelt on and request emergency traffic control.**
4. **Install your spare tire only if you have a safe, level area to work.** **Locate your vehicle's jack, lug wrench, and spare tire (often under the trunk floor or the vehicle rear).**
   - Check the pressure of the spare tire before installation if possible.
   - If using a temporary "donut" spare, do not exceed 50 mph or drive further than 50 miles.
5. **Contact roadside assistance if you cannot safely change the tire yourself.** **Call your insurance provider, AAA (1-800-AAA-HELP), or a local towing service and explicitly state that the vehicle has "non-repairable sidewall damage."**
6. **Identify the tire age and specifications for the replacement.** **Look for the DOT Tire Identification Number (TIN) on the sidewall; the last four digits indicate the week and year of manufacture.**
   - This information helps the tire shop ensure they have a matching speed and load-rated tire in stock.
7. **Notify the relevant authorities for fleet or rental vehicles.** **Call the rental company's emergency line or your fleet manager before authorizing repairs, as many have specific approved vendors and require documentation for safety records.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause of the damage (e.g., pothole vs. defect) while on the roadside.
- You do not need to decide whether to replace all four tires or just the damaged one right now.
- You do not need to file a road hazard claim with the city or state until you are in a permanent safe location.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for the vehicle to feel stable despite significant tire damage. Modern tires are engineered to hold air even when the internal structure is failing, but this "normal" feeling is deceptive. Choosing to stop and seek help is the only way to prevent a high-speed blowout.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. A certified tire technician must inspect the wheel rim for cracks and the suspension for alignment issues following a tire-damaging impact.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not replace the safety instructions in your vehicle's owner's manual or professional mechanical advice. Roadside tire changes are inherently dangerous; if you feel unsafe due to traffic or terrain, remain in your vehicle and wait for professional assistance.
