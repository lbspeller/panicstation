# What to do if… your employer pressures you to choose a new role or sign documents immediately (USA)

## Short answer
Do not choose, resign, or sign anything in the meeting. Ask for the role options, deadlines, and consequences in writing, then ask for time to review before responding.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign, “self-select out,” or send a message that could be read as quitting.
- Do not sign a severance agreement, separation agreement, waiver, or release in the meeting.
- Do not accept a role without the essentials in writing: pay, title, exempt or nonexempt status, benefits, location, schedule, reporting line, duties, and start date.
- Do not assume “you must choose today” is a legal deadline; it may be an internal deadline.
- Do not rely on verbal assurances about pay, duties, remote work, severance, or future openings.
- Do not argue through the whole meeting if you are overwhelmed; keep asking for the terms in writing.

## What to do now
1. **Use a simple pause script.**  
   Say: “I’m not able to choose immediately. Please send me the role details, the deadline, and what happens if I do not choose by that deadline. I’ll respond after I’ve reviewed them.”

2. **Ask for the written role details.**  
   Ask HR or your manager to send the job description, title and level, pay rate or salary, exempt or nonexempt classification, expected hours, schedule, work location, remote-work expectations, reporting line, start date, benefits changes, and whether the role is treated as at-will, contract-based, or covered by a collective bargaining agreement.

3. **Ask what happens if you do not choose.**  
   Ask for a direct written answer: is there a default assignment, a selection process, a layoff, or a severance option? Ask whether declining the role will be treated as resignation, termination, layoff, or something else.

4. **Make a dated factual note.**  
   Write down who was present, what roles were offered, what deadline was given, what documents were shown, and any words used about resignation, severance, layoff, or losing employment. Keep the note somewhere you can lawfully access.

5. **If a severance or release appears, slow the process down.**  
   Ask to take it away and review it. If you are 40 or older and the agreement includes a waiver of age-discrimination claims, federal rules generally require at least 21 days to consider the agreement, 45 days in certain group termination or exit-incentive programs, and 7 days to revoke after signing. Do not let a same-day meeting rush you past a review period that may apply.

6. **If layoffs may be involved, ask about WARN notice.**  
   Ask whether the employer believes the federal WARN Act applies and whether any state WARN or “mini-WARN” notice may apply. For covered employers and covered plant closings or mass layoffs, federal WARN generally requires at least 60 calendar days’ advance written notice, with exceptions.

7. **Use representation if you have it.**  
   If you are in a union, contact your union representative before choosing or signing, and forward the written information you receive.

8. **Get advice before an irreversible step.**  
   Consider a brief employment-law consultation before signing any release, accepting a major pay or classification change, or sending any message that could end your employment.

9. **If you suspect discrimination or retaliation, keep the record factual.**  
   Ask for the selection criteria in writing. Keep relevant performance reviews, job postings, restructuring documents, and communications, without taking confidential material you are not allowed to keep.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to accept a lower title, lower pay, relocation, or a changed schedule.
- You do not need to sign anything to “keep the peace.”
- You do not need to negotiate every detail while you are still trying to understand the options.
- You do not need to make a full unemployment, healthcare, or job-search plan right now.
- Your immediate priority is to avoid an accidental resignation, a rushed signature, or a role choice without written terms.

## Important reassurance
Being pushed to choose immediately can make your brain go blank. A calm pause, written details, and a clear deadline can give you enough space to avoid a one-way decision while you are overwhelmed.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Your options may depend on your state, your offer letter or contract, company policies, any collective bargaining agreement, the size and structure of the layoff, and the exact documents you are given.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employment rules vary by state and by situation. Before signing a waiver, release, or agreement that changes or ends your employment, consider getting qualified advice.
