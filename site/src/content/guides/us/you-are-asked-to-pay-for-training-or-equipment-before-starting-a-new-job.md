# What to do if… you are asked to pay for training or equipment before starting a new job (USA)
## Short answer
Pause before sending money. Verify the employer independently and ask for the written terms of the request before paying for training, equipment, or supplies.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money by wire transfer, cryptocurrency, gift card, or payment app because someone says it will secure the job.
- Do not deposit a check from a supposed employer and use the money to buy equipment, buy gift cards, or send money onward.
- Do not share your Social Security number, banking details, or identity documents until you have independently verified the employer and the request.
- Do not log in through a link in the message or install an unfamiliar app to complete the payment.
- Do not let a promised reimbursement rush you into paying before you understand the written terms.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the payment.**  
   Send one calm message saying that you need the written offer and the written policy for any required training, equipment, supplies, or reimbursement before you proceed.

2. **Verify the employer independently.**  
   Find the company’s official website yourself. Use the main phone number or a publicly listed contact method, not the contact details in the message, to confirm the role, the person who contacted you, and the payment request.

3. **Slow down if the contact details look unusual.**  
   A look-alike website address, a free email account, a chat-only interview, or pressure to pay immediately is a reason to verify more carefully. Do not rely on a link, phone number, or email address supplied by the recruiter alone.

4. **Ask for the exact written terms.**  
   Ask what the payment is for, whether it is required, who receives the money, whether you can obtain the item elsewhere, and whether any reimbursement applies. Ask for the pay rate, start date, and written offer as well.

5. **Stop if they sent a check for equipment or supplies.**  
   Do not deposit or use the check while the employer is unverified, and do not send money onward. The FTC describes fake-check job scams involving equipment or supplies. If you already deposited the check, contact your bank immediately and explain what happened.

6. **Keep the federal wage rule in mind if the employer appears genuine.**  
   For workers covered by the Fair Labor Standards Act, required uniforms and other items primarily for the employer’s benefit generally cannot reduce pay below the required federal minimum wage or cut into required overtime pay. State law may provide additional protections.

7. **Ask about required training in writing.**  
   For workers covered by federal wage law, training time generally counts as working time unless the general four-part exception is met: attendance is outside regular working hours, voluntary, not directly related to the job, and involves no productive work. Special situations can apply, so keep the written details and seek state-specific guidance later if needed.

8. **Contain the damage if you already paid or shared information.**  
   Contact the company you used to send the money, such as your bank, card issuer, payment app, wire-transfer company, gift-card issuer, or cryptocurrency platform. Report the suspected fraud and ask whether reversal or other protective steps are possible. Change any exposed passwords. If your Social Security number was shared, or you think your identity may have been misused, use IdentityTheft.gov. Report the scam to the FTC. For a cyber-enabled recruitment scam, you can also file a report with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to accept the job while the payment request is still unclear.
- You do not need to debate the law with the recruiter.
- You do not need to work out every state-specific rule before pausing the payment.
- You can review longer-term employment options after you have checked whether the employer and the request are genuine.

## Important reassurance
Pausing does not mean you have done anything wrong. An upfront request does not prove that the job is fake, but it is reasonable to slow down, verify the employer, and protect your money and personal information.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate financial harm and clarify the request. Later decisions may need state-specific wage-and-hour guidance or other specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Laws and workplace rules vary by state, job type, and the facts of the situation. If you feel pressured, prioritize pausing the payment and verifying the employer independently.
