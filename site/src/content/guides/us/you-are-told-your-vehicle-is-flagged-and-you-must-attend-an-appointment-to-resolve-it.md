# What to do if… you are told your vehicle is flagged and you must attend an appointment to resolve it (USA)

## Short answer
If an officer is stopping you now, pull over when safe, follow instructions, and avoid sudden movements. Otherwise, do not attend, send documents, or move the vehicle based only on an unexpected message. Contact the agency through independently verified public contact details and ask whether the request is genuine, whether the appointment is voluntary, whether the vehicle should be brought, and what documents are needed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money, personal information, or copies of documents to clear a flag until you have independently verified the agency and the request.
- Do not click links or use phone numbers from an unexpected text, email, voicemail, or caller ID display.
- Do not alter, remove, swap, hide, or discard the plate, vehicle documents, or messages you received.
- Do not ignore uncertainty about the vehicle record. If practical, pause use of the vehicle until you have verified instructions about driving or arranging transport.
- Do not argue or physically resist if an officer stops you. Follow lawful safety instructions and keep the encounter simple.

## What to do now
1. **Save the message and verify the agency independently.**
   - Keep the text, email, voicemail, envelope, screenshots, and any reference number.
   - Find the agency’s public contact details through its official website or a government directory. Do not use contact details from the unexpected message.
   - Ask whether the agency sent the message and whether there is a case, incident, or reference number.

2. **Ask what the word “flagged” means in your case.**
   - Ask whether the issue concerns the license plate, vehicle identification number (VIN), registration record, registered owner, or another agency record.
   - Ask whether the appointment is voluntary.
   - Ask whether the agency wants the vehicle brought in, inspected where it is, or left where it is until you receive instructions.

3. **Pause before driving or arranging transport.**
   - If practical, avoid driving until you have verified whether the issue involves a stolen plate, stolen-vehicle record, plate and VIN mismatch, registration problem, or insurance problem.
   - Ask the verified agency or your state motor vehicle office what to do before moving the vehicle.
   - Do not arrange a tow until you know whether the vehicle should stay where it is or be taken to a specific location.

4. **Prepare only the documents that may help confirm the record.**
   - Government photo ID.
   - Registration card.
   - Current proof of insurance.
   - Title, bill of sale, or recent purchase paperwork if relevant.
   - The original message and any reference number.
   - Ask the verified agency what to bring and how to submit any copies securely.

5. **Get legal advice before attending if the situation may involve possible wrongdoing.**
   - Consider speaking with a lawyer before the appointment if the agency mentions a warrant, possible arrest, suspected stolen property, an ownership dispute, or questioning about an offence.
   - If officers question you about possible wrongdoing, you can say that you wish to remain silent and want to speak with a lawyer before answering questions.
   - Follow lawful instructions and do not physically resist.

6. **Treat unverified demands as a possible impersonation scam.**
   - Do not attend an appointment based only on an unverified message.
   - Do not pay a fee or send personal information to clear the issue.
   - Report suspected impersonation scams through the Federal Trade Commission resources below.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to file a complaint, request records, contact a seller, or pursue compensation.
- You do not need to write a detailed explanation before you know what record is involved.
- You do not need to send every document you have. First ask the verified agency what is needed.

## Important reassurance
The word “flagged” does not explain the issue by itself. A record may concern a plate, a VIN, a registration record, or another agency record. Verifying the request and pausing before moving the vehicle are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a lawyer, your state motor vehicle office, your insurer, or the verified agency handling the record.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, insurance, or other professional advice. Procedures vary by state and agency.
