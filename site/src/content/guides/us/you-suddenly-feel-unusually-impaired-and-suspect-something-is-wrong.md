# What to do if… you suddenly feel unusually impaired and suspect something is wrong (USA)

## Short answer
Stop what you are doing, get to a safer place with a trusted person, and call 911 if you are very drowsy, confused, collapsing, having trouble breathing, or getting worse.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive or travel alone if you feel impaired.
- Do not leave with someone you do not fully trust, even if they seem helpful.
- Do not keep drinking alcohol or take more substances to try to counteract it.
- Do not try to make yourself vomit.
- Do not over-drink water to try to flush anything out.
- Do not ignore the possibility of poisoning, carbon monoxide exposure, or a medical emergency just because you are unsure of the cause.
- If carbon monoxide is possible, do not delay getting outside to do quick checks first.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause immediately.**  
   Move to a staffed, public place such as venue security, bar staff, reception, a store counter, or a public front desk. Sit down. If you are with someone you trust, stay physically close to them.
2. **Stop intake and separate from the possible source.**  
   Stop drinking or eating anything you are currently consuming. If you still have the drink, food, medication package, container, or substance, set it aside and do not keep consuming it.
3. **Call 911 now if any of these apply:**  
   - you might pass out or cannot stay awake
   - severe confusion, chest pain, seizure, repeated vomiting, trouble breathing, or symptoms rapidly worsening
   - you feel unsafe with the people around you
   Tell the dispatcher: **“I feel suddenly unusually impaired and I am worried something is wrong. I may have been drugged or poisoned.”**
4. **If it is not clearly life-threatening, contact Poison Help for fast guidance.**  
   Call **1-800-222-1222**. It is available in the United States 24 hours a day. If calling is hard, use the online help tool at poison.org. Tell them what you consumed, any medications or substances taken today, your symptoms, and when it started. If they advise emergency evaluation, follow that advice promptly.
5. **If you are in a venue, involve staff or security.**  
   Ask them to get you to a safe area, help contact a trusted friend, and call EMS if you worsen. Do not let yourself be escorted out alone by someone you do not know.
6. **If the environment could be causing it, treat it as possible carbon monoxide exposure.**  
   If symptoms started indoors, multiple people feel unwell, a carbon monoxide alarm is sounding, fuel-burning equipment is nearby, or you feel better outside:
   - get into fresh air immediately and stay out
   - call 911 or the fire department
   - do not re-enter until responders say it is safe
   - do not use a suspected appliance again until it has been checked by trained personnel
7. **Have a trusted person stay with you and take over logistics.**  
   Ask them to stay with you until you are assessed or safely home, and to arrange safe transport such as a trusted ride, taxi, or rideshare. Keep your phone, keys, cards, and ID together.
8. **Make a quick minimum-facts note while you still can.**  
   In your phone, write where you are, when symptoms began, what you consumed, and all medications or substances taken today. This can help EMS or emergency department staff make faster, safer decisions.

## What can wait
- You do not have to figure out the exact cause right now. Focus on safety and medical assessment.
- You do not need to decide right now whether to make a police report; you can do that later if you choose.
- Messages, missed plans, payments, and replacing cards can wait until you are safe and medically checked.

## Important reassurance
Sudden, unusual impairment is a valid reason to ask for help. When your thinking, alertness, or coordination is affected, the safest move is to slow down, get supervised support, and let trained people help assess what is happening.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate risk and buy time. Later decisions may need medical care, specialist support, or legal advice depending on what happened.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical, legal, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you have severe symptoms, are getting worse, or feel unsafe, call 911. In the U.S., Poison Help at 1-800-222-1222 can provide rapid guidance for suspected poisoning exposures and can help you decide whether emergency care is needed.
