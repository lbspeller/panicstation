# What to do if… your employer says your bonus is being clawed back unexpectedly (USA)

## Short answer
Verify your employment status with HR through a direct, non-portal channel before taking any irreversible action. Request written confirmation of your hire status, start date, and the completion of any pre-employment contingencies.

## Do not do these things
- Do not resign from a current position or commit to financial obligations (leases, moving costs) until you have a confirmed start date and clear status.
- Do not enter sensitive data or banking details into a portal that displays conflicting information; verify the portal’s legitimacy first.
- Do not send aggressive or accusatory messages to the hiring team; maintain a neutral, inquiry-based tone.
- Do not assume a system status is a legal notice of rescission; digital systems often use "not hired" as a placeholder for technical transitions.
- Do not ignore emails from third-party vendors, such as background check companies or drug-testing clinics.

## What to do now
1. **Document the current status (2 minutes).** Take a screenshot or photo of the portal screen showing "not hired," ensuring the date, time, and candidate ID are visible.
2. **Review your signed offer letter for contingencies.** Identify specific conditions you were required to meet, such as background checks, drug screenings, or Form I-9 verification.
   - Note which items you have completed and which are still in progress.
   - Check for "at-will" language or specific rescission clauses.
3. **Contact the HR representative named in your offer letter directly.** Use a verified email address or phone number from the company’s official website rather than links provided within the portal.
   - Ask for a written update on your hire status and start date.
   - Specifically ask if the portal status reflects a technical error or a change in your employment offer.
4. **Request FCRA notices if a background check is the cited cause.** If the employer indicates the status is due to a background check or credit report, you have specific rights under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA).
   - Ask for a "Pre-Adverse Action Notice" and a copy of the report used.
   - Verify that you have been given a "reasonable period" (typically at least 5 business days) to dispute inaccuracies before a final decision is made.
5. **Ensure your Form I-9 Section 1 is ready for completion.** Federal law requires Section 1 to be completed no later than your first day of work for pay.
   - Confirm if the portal error is preventing you from meeting this legal deadline.
   - Ask HR for an alternative method to submit your employment eligibility documents if the system is locked.
6. **Preserve a timeline of all communications.** Record the dates and times of your offer, your acceptance, the portal status change, and all subsequent interactions with the company.
7. **Inquire about "promissory estoppel" if you have suffered financial loss.** If you resigned from a job or relocated based on a firm offer that is now being rescinded, you may have legal grounds for a claim even in at-will states.
   - Consult a local employment attorney to discuss "detrimental reliance."
8. **Note EEOC deadlines if you suspect discrimination.** If you believe the offer was revoked for an unlawful reason (such as race, age, or disability), you generally have 180 or 300 days to file a charge with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC).

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on legal representation until you receive a definitive answer from HR.
- You do not need to explain or defend your qualifications to the company unless they explicitly state the offer is being reconsidered due to a specific finding.
- You do not need to worry about long-term career gaps until the company confirms the offer is officially rescinded.

## Important reassurance
Automated Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) and onboarding portals frequently trigger "not hired" or "closed" statuses when a recruiter merges duplicate profiles, moves a candidate to a payroll system, or adjusts a start date. A status change in a portal is often a technical misalignment rather than a reflection of your actual employment standing.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate stabilization and evidence preservation steps for users facing conflicting digital employment data. It does not constitute legal advice and does not cover the specifics of union contracts or government-sector hiring.

## Important note
Employment law in the USA varies significantly by state. Most employment is "at-will," meaning offers can be rescinded for any lawful reason at any time. However, federal laws such as the FCRA and ADA provide specific procedural protections during the hiring phase. If you have incurred significant financial loss or suspect discrimination, seek professional legal counsel in your specific jurisdiction.
