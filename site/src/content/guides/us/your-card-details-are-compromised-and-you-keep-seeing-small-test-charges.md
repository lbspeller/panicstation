# What to do if… your card details are compromised and you keep seeing small “test” charges (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately lock the card through your banking app and call the official fraud department number on the back of your physical card to initiate a permanent block and replacement.

## Do not do these things
- Don't ignore transactions under $1.00; these "penny tests" or "micro-entries" are used by criminals to verify card validity before larger thefts.
- Don't call numbers provided in a "fraud alert" text or email, as these are frequently phishing attempts designed to capture your login credentials.
- Don't share one-time passcodes (OTP) or PINs with anyone, including people claiming to be bank employees.
- Don't assume a "pending" charge will disappear on its own if you did not authorize it.
- Don't wait for the physical statement to arrive before reporting activity you see in your real-time app or online history.

## What to do now
1. **Freeze or lock the compromised card immediately using your issuer’s mobile app or website.** This prevents any new "live" transactions from processing while you prepare to speak with a representative.
2. **Call the card issuer's fraud department using only the number on the back of your card.** If the card is not present, use the customer service number from your most recent official billing statement or the "Contact Us" section of the official bank website. 
   - Inform them of the specific "test" charges.
   - Request a "total card replacement" with a new account number.
   - Ask for a "merchant block" on the specific descriptors associated with the test charges.
3. **Request the revocation of all digital wallet tokens and "stored card" permissions.** Fraudsters often add compromised cards to Apple Pay, Google Pay, or Samsung Pay; a standard card replacement may not automatically kill these digital "tokens." Explicitly ask the bank to "reset all digital wallet tokens" associated with the old card.
4. **Determine if the charges are Credit (FCBA) or Debit (EFT/Reg E) transactions.** 
   - **For Credit Cards:** You must send a written billing-error notice to the issuer's "dispute address" (often different from the payment address) so it arrives within 60 days of the first statement containing the error.
   - **For Debit Cards:** Your liability for unauthorized transfers is limited to $50 if reported within 2 business days of discovery, or $500 if reported after 2 business days but within 60 days of the statement being sent.
5. **Check for unauthorized ACH Micro-Entries if your checking account is involved.** If you see two very small deposits followed by a withdrawal (Micro-Entries), tell the bank these are "unauthorized ACH entries" and ask them to verify if an external account has been linked to your bank profile.
6. **Change your online banking password and enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).** Do this from a secure device after you have secured the card. Use an authenticator app rather than SMS-based codes if possible.
7. **Review your credit reports for other signs of identity theft.** Visit AnnualCreditReport.com to ensure no new accounts have been opened in your name. If you see unrecognized accounts, visit IdentityTheft.gov to file a formal report and initiate a credit freeze.

## What can wait
- Notifying legitimate merchants of your new card number (wait until the new card arrives and the old one is fully deactivated).
- Filing a local police report (this is usually only necessary if the issuer requires it or if you know the identity of the perpetrator).
- Reviewing old statements from more than 12 months ago unless you suspect a long-term compromise.

## Important reassurance
Seeing "test" charges is a standard precursor to fraud, and modern banking systems are designed to handle this specific event. Identifying the pattern early and locking the card is a highly effective way to prevent the loss of larger sums of money.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for card-specific compromise only. If your Social Security number or full identity has been stolen, you will need to follow the comprehensive recovery steps found at IdentityTheft.gov.

## Important note
Federal protections under the Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA) and the Electronic Fund Transfer Act (EFTA) have strict windows for reporting. Always follow up phone calls with written notification to ensure your legal rights are preserved.
