# What to do if… a childcare provider or school threatens to end your place over an unpaid balance (USA)
## Short answer
Verify the message through a contact route you already trust. Ask for the balance, what may happen, and when it may happen in writing, then ask whether a short extension or payment arrangement is available.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay through a new link, unfamiliar payment app, or changed account details until you have verified the request through a known phone number, saved contact, or portal you have used before.
- Don’t agree to withdraw your child or send an angry reply while you are under pressure.
- Don’t assume the message is about attendance at the school. It may concern childcare, meals, an activity, or another paid service.
- Don’t leave the message unverified if you can safely check it today. The stated deadline may affect care or paid-program access under the provider’s policy.

## What to do now
1. **Save the message.** Take a screenshot of the email, text, or portal notice. Note the time, the amount requested, and who sent it.
2. **Verify the request independently.** Call the childcare provider or school using a number from its official website, prior paperwork, or a saved contact. Open a known portal or app yourself rather than using a new payment link.
3. **Ask for a written breakdown.** Request the itemized balance, the service or fee involved, what may happen, when it may happen, and the policy or contract term they are using.
4. **Ask for a same-day pause.** Ask whether a short extension, payment arrangement, or partial payment would pause the stated consequence. Ask for written confirmation before relying on any arrangement.
5. **If the message came from a school, identify what may stop.** Ask whether it concerns attendance at the school, childcare, meals, transportation, an activity, or another paid service. If the answer is unclear, ask the registrar, principal’s office, or program office to confirm it in writing.
6. **If the balance is for school meals, ask for the unpaid-meal-charge policy.** School food authorities participating in federal child nutrition programs must develop and communicate a policy for handling unpaid meal charges. Ask how to apply for free or reduced-price meals if that may be relevant.
7. **If your family is in temporary or unstable housing, ask for the district’s local homeless education liaison.** Federal McKinney-Vento rules require state and local education agencies to address barriers to enrollment and retention tied to outstanding fees or fines for eligible children and youth experiencing homelessness. Ask the liaison whether these protections apply in your situation.
8. **If you dispute a childcare charge, keep the dispute short and specific.** Ask for an itemized ledger and state which amount you dispute. Before making any payment, ask whether it would pause the stated consequence and how it would be recorded.
9. **Start a parallel help route if you cannot pay today.** Call 211 and ask whether local help with childcare costs is available. Check Child Care Aware of America’s payment-help and state-resource pages. Contact your local Child Care Resource and Referral organization to ask about childcare assistance options and local resources.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide today whether to change schools or childcare providers long-term.
- You do **not** need to write a long explanation. A short request for written details and a pause is enough for today.
- You do **not** need to resolve every disputed fee before asking whether the immediate deadline can be paused.

## Important reassurance
A same-day payment deadline can feel overwhelming. You can take one step at a time: verify the message, get the details in writing, and ask what would pause the immediate consequence.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on the provider’s contract, the school or district policy, the type of fee, and any state-specific rules, and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or other professional advice. Policies and contracts vary. Verify unexpected payment requests independently before sending money.
