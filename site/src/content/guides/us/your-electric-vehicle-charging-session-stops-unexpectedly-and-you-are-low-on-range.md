# What to do if… your electric vehicle charging session stops unexpectedly and you are low on range (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safe place to stop, then call the charging network support number shown on the station or in the app while you prepare to call roadside assistance if you may not safely reach another charger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep driving if you are cutting it close; running out of charge can leave you stopped somewhere unsafe.
- Don’t stop in travel lanes, on ramps, or anywhere other drivers may not see you.
- Don’t accept improvised towing or pushing; EVs can be damaged if moved the wrong way.
- Don’t let your phone die while troubleshooting; keep power for calls, location sharing, and emergency contact.
- Don’t rely on “maybe there’s a charger ahead” without confirming a reachable option.

## What to do now
1. **Make a safe pause your first goal.**
   - If you are at the charger and the site feels safe, stay there while you troubleshoot.
   - If you are on a high-speed road and your remaining range is uncertain, aim for the nearest safe place to stop, such as a parking lot, service area, or well-lit public place.
   - Use a shoulder only if you have no safer option.

2. **Do one quick charger check.**
   - Reseat the connector firmly.
   - Check the station screen and your app for a clear error, such as fault, timeout, payment problem, or session ended.
   - If there is an obvious start or retry option, try once.

3. **Call the charging network support line shown on the station or in the app.**
   - Ask whether the stall is faulted.
   - Ask whether they can restart or reset the session remotely.
   - Ask whether another stall at the same site is working.
   - Ask them to point you to the nearest working station they can see.

4. **Choose one realistic next move.**
   - Use your vehicle navigation, the charging app, or the U.S. Department of Energy station locator to find one charger you can reach with a buffer.
   - If the route is close, includes high-speed roads, or you are unsure the charger is working, treat recovery as the safer plan.
   - Avoid repeatedly changing plans while the battery is dropping.

5. **Call roadside assistance before you reach empty.**
   - Use your vehicle roadside assistance, insurer, AAA, or another roadside provider.
   - Say clearly: “EV with very low battery; need EV-appropriate tow to the nearest charger or dealership.”
   - Ask for a flatbed if appropriate, and tell dispatch to follow your vehicle’s towing instructions if you can access them safely.
   - Share your exact location from your phone or vehicle navigation.

6. **If you are stopped in an unsafe location, treat it as an emergency.**
   - Turn on hazard lights.
   - If you are in a travel lane, on a narrow shoulder, near a blind curve, in heavy traffic, or you feel at risk, call 911 and give your exact location.
   - Stay out of moving traffic and follow dispatcher instructions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now who is at fault.
- You do not need to handle refunds, complaints, or fault reports beyond getting immediate help.
- You do not need to optimize the whole route; your priority is a safe, reachable stop or professional recovery.

## Important reassurance
This can happen for ordinary station, app, payment, timeout, or connector reasons. Calling support early and arranging EV-appropriate recovery if needed is a calm way to protect yourself and the vehicle.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and avoid being stranded in traffic. Later decisions, including refunds, repair checks, charger reports, or insurance questions, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This guide is general information for urgent first steps, not legal, mechanical, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or stopped in an unsafe location, call 911.
