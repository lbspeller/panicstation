# What to do if… you feel unsafe at home because someone is sending threatening messages and says they are coming over (USA)

## Short answer
If you believe they may harm you, force entry, or are on the way and you feel in immediate danger, call 911 now. Get behind a locked door, do not open the door to them, and bring another trusted person onto the phone if you can.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door to “talk it out” or prove you are not afraid.
- Do not go outside to meet them or check whether they are really coming.
- Do not get pulled into rapid back-and-forth texting; it can escalate and distract you from safety.
- Do not reveal where you are inside your home or what steps you are taking.
- Do not ask a friend, neighbor, or relative to confront them for you.
- Do not delete messages, voicemails, or call logs in the moment.
- Do not assume you are wasting 911’s time if you feel in danger.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 if you feel in immediate danger.**  
   Call if they have threatened you, are nearby or on the way, may force entry, have been violent before, are violating a protective or no-contact order, or your instincts say this could escalate fast. Be ready to give your exact address, apartment or room number, the nature of the emergency, and whether the person is outside, nearby, or still messaging.

2. **Get to a safer setup inside your home.**  
   - Lock doors and accessible windows. Use any door chain, deadbolt, or latch you already have.
   - Keep your phone with you and plug it in if you can do that without delaying safety.
   - Keep keys, shoes, coat, medication, and any essential child or pet items close only if they are already nearby.

3. **Gather people and pets away from entry points.**  
   Bring children, pets, or anyone vulnerable with you so you are not split up. Move away from doors, ground-floor windows, garages, and other places where the person could see or reach you.

4. **Move to a room that is easier to secure.**  
   Choose a room with a lock if possible and stay away from the front door. If you have a safer room away from the kitchen, garage, or utility areas, use that while you call or wait for help.

5. **Bring in another person without creating a confrontation.**  
   Call a trusted friend, family member, neighbor, front desk, or building security and say clearly: “Someone is threatening to come over. I feel unsafe. If we disconnect, call 911.” Ask them not to approach or confront the person.

6. **Send one clear boundary message only if it will not increase danger.**  
   If it feels safe, send: “Do not come to my home. If you show up, I will call 911.” Then stop replying. If any message feels risky, skip this.

7. **If they arrive, do not engage at the door.**  
   Do not open the door. Call 911 or stay on the line and say: “Someone I know is outside my home and I am afraid. They said they were coming and have been escalating by messages.” If there is a protective order or no-contact order, tell the dispatcher.

8. **Text 911 only if you cannot safely call and text-to-911 works where you are.**  
   Calling is usually best when you can speak safely, and text-to-911 is not available everywhere. If you text 911, send your exact address first, then what is happening in plain words. If you get a bounce-back message saying text-to-911 is not available, try calling 911 if you can.

9. **Preserve what you already have without investigating.**  
   Save the threatening messages, voicemails, call logs, and the time they said they were coming. Take a few screenshots if you can do that quickly, but do not spend time building a perfect record while you are unsafe.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to file a police report, seek charges, or apply for a restraining or protective order.
- You do not need to write a detailed timeline tonight; a few key screenshots and rough times are enough for now.
- You do not need to negotiate, explain, or justify your “no”.
- You do not need to decide what label fits the situation before getting help.
- You do not need to solve housing, relationship, family, work, or school decisions in this moment.

## Important reassurance
When someone you know escalates, it is common to doubt yourself, freeze, or worry you are making it worse. Feeling unsafe is enough reason to act. Reaching out early, before the person is at your door or inside your building, can help create a safer pause.

## Scope note
This is first-steps-only guidance for the next minutes and hours. Later decisions, including longer-term safety planning, changing locks, protective orders, police reports, housing options, or support services, may need specialist help once you are in a safer and calmer position.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 911. Support services can help you think through safer options without pressure, especially if the person is a partner, ex-partner, family member, acquaintance, or someone whose behavior is part of a pattern of control, threats, harassment, or stalking.
