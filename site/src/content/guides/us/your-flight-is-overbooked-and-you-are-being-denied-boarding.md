# What to do if… your flight is overbooked and you are being denied boarding (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before accepting anything. Ask whether the airline is asking for volunteers or involuntarily denying boarding because the flight is oversold, then leave the counter with a confirmed new itinerary or refund confirmation and written documentation of what happened.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t accept a voucher just because you feel trapped; ask about expiry, blackout dates, transfer limits, and whether you are giving up any cash compensation rights.
- Don’t leave the gate area before you have a confirmed replacement itinerary, a refund confirmation, or clear written next steps from the airline.
- Don’t rely on a standby promise unless you are willing to risk waiting longer.
- Don’t escalate into confrontation; behavior issues can change what the airline says the problem is.
- Don’t assume every removal, delay, aircraft change, cancellation, downgrade, or missed connection is covered by oversales compensation rules.

## What to do now
1. **Keep proof that you were ready to travel.**  
   Keep your boarding pass, booking confirmation, check-in proof, app screenshots, baggage receipt, and a note of when you reached the gate.

2. **Ask the key question clearly.**  
   Say: “Am I being asked to volunteer, or am I being involuntarily denied boarding because this flight is oversold?”

3. **If they are asking for volunteers, make the offer specific before accepting.**  
   Ask:
   - Is the replacement seat confirmed or standby?
   - What exactly am I receiving: cash, check, travel credit, voucher, miles, meals, hotel, or ground transport?
   - What are the expiry dates, blackout dates, transfer limits, and other restrictions?
   - Will accepting this mean I am treated as a volunteer rather than involuntarily bumped?

4. **If they say you are being involuntarily denied boarding, ask for the written statement.**  
   Say: “Please give me the written denied boarding statement with the compensation rules and your boarding priority criteria.”

5. **Check whether the oversales compensation rules likely apply.**  
   You generally need all of these: the flight departs from a U.S. airport, you had a confirmed reservation, you met the airline’s check-in and gate deadlines, the flight is oversold, and the airline cannot get you to your destination or first stopover within one hour of the original planned arrival time.

6. **Watch for common exceptions.**  
   Denied boarding compensation may not apply if the issue is a canceled flight, a smaller aircraft substituted for operational or safety reasons, certain small-aircraft or weight-balance limits, a downgrade with a fare difference refund, failure to meet ticketing or check-in rules, or a passenger safety, security, health, or conduct issue.

7. **Sanity-check the denied boarding compensation tier if you are eligible.**  
   For eligible involuntary oversales bumping:
   - Domestic flights: no compensation for a 0–1 hour arrival delay; 200% of the one-way fare for a 1–2 hour delay, capped at $1,075; 400% for over 2 hours, capped at $2,150.
   - International flights leaving the United States: no compensation for a 0–1 hour arrival delay; 200% of the one-way fare for a 1–4 hour delay, capped at $1,075; 400% for over 4 hours, capped at $2,150.
   - Airlines may offer more than the required minimum.

8. **Ask how and when payment will happen.**  
   If you qualify for denied boarding compensation, ask for cash or an immediately negotiable check at the airport. If substitute transportation leaves before payment can be prepared, ask how they will send payment within 24 hours.

9. **Get the travel plan in writing before you walk away.**  
   Ask for one of these:
   - a confirmed replacement itinerary;
   - a refund confirmation;
   - a written explanation of why neither is being provided yet.

10. **Ask about unused optional fees.**  
   Ask the airline to document paid extras you did not receive, such as a seat assignment, baggage service, or upgrade, so you can request a refund for unused optional services not provided on your alternate transportation.

11. **If staff say you are being removed after boarding, keep the problem separate.**  
   Stay calm, follow crew instructions, ask for the reason in writing when it is safe to do so, and focus on rebooking, refund confirmation, and documentation. You can challenge the explanation later through the airline and, if needed, the U.S. Department of Transportation complaint process.

12. **If the airport desk cannot fix it, preserve the record.**  
   Save names or badge numbers if visible, times, screenshots, receipts, the written statement, the replacement itinerary, and any messages from the airline. File with the airline first, then use the DOT complaint route if the airline does not resolve it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to argue the exact compensation math at the counter if you are overwhelmed; get the written statement, the confirmed plan, and your documents first.
- You do not need to file a DOT complaint while standing at the gate unless documenting the refusal is the only way to preserve the issue.
- You do not need to decide about later reimbursement or settlement offers right now; focus on getting rebooked, refunded, or safely unstuck.

## Important reassurance
Being told you may not have a seat is stressful and public, but it is also a situation with a defined process. Your strongest next move is to slow the conversation down, clarify whether this is voluntary or involuntary, and get the airline’s plan and paperwork in writing.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the airport moment. Later decisions, complaints, refund disputes, or compensation disputes may depend on the exact reason for the denied boarding and the airline’s written records.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. U.S. oversales denied boarding rules generally apply to flights departing a U.S. airport; other journeys, contracts, or facts may have different rules. When in doubt, prioritize written documentation, confirmed rebooking or refund, and keeping all travel records and receipts.
