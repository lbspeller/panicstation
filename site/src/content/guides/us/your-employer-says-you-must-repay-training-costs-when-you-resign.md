# What to do if… your employer says you must repay training costs when you resign (USA)
## Short answer
Pause and ask for the demand, the agreement they rely on, and an itemized cost breakdown in writing. Do not sign a new repayment plan or wage-deduction authorization on the spot.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a new repayment agreement, wage-deduction authorization, or release while you are under pressure.
- Do not agree to a deduction or payment plan just to end the conversation.
- Do not assume the demand is enforceable because someone says it is standard policy.
- Do not delete messages, paystubs, training records, or the written demand.
- Do not ignore a deduction on a paystub. Keep the paystub and object in writing promptly.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the demand in writing.** Request:
   - the agreement or policy they say applies
   - the date they say you accepted it
   - the event they say triggered repayment
   - an itemized list of the training costs
   - the total amount they claim
   - any proposed wage deduction or invoice
2. **Ask whether they plan to deduct money from your wages or send a bill.** If they mention a wage deduction, ask what existing authorization they rely on. Ask your state labor office whether the proposed deduction is permitted and whether any existing authorization is sufficient.
3. **Save the documents before your access changes.** Keep copies of:
   - the written demand and related messages
   - your offer letter, employment agreement, and handbook acknowledgments
   - any training repayment form or wage-deduction authorization
   - your resignation notice and related messages
   - paystubs and time records, especially recent ones
   - training receipts, course descriptions, and messages showing whether the training was required for the role
4. **Put your position in writing.** A short message is enough: state that you are reviewing the demand, request the agreement and itemized costs, and say that you are not signing a new authorization or repayment plan at this time.
5. **If a deduction is proposed or appears on a paystub, keep the records and ask about complaint routes promptly.** Federal wage rules generally require wages to be paid free and clear. A deduction or reimbursement for an item primarily benefiting the employer may raise a federal wage concern if it cuts into required minimum wage or overtime in a workweek. Ask the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division and your state labor office how the rules may apply.
6. **If an invoice or collection letter arrives, do not pay on the spot.** Keep the letter and ask for the amount and basis of the demand in writing. The next step may depend on who sent the letter and your state’s rules.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you will pay.
- You do not need to decide today whether you will negotiate.
- You do not need to argue about whether the training benefited you.
- You do not need to threaten legal action before you have the agreement, the itemized costs, and your pay records.

## Important reassurance
A training-cost demand can make leaving a job feel urgent or impossible. You are allowed to slow the conversation down, collect the documents, and decline to sign a new authorization or repayment plan on the spot.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Whether the employer can recover money, deduct wages, or pursue a claim may depend on the agreement, the facts, and your state’s rules. Later decisions may need state-specific professional advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Keep records and seek prompt guidance from the appropriate government labor agency or a qualified professional if wages are withheld or the amount is significant.
