# What to do if… your bank account is being closed and you need to move your money fast (USA)

## Short answer
First, verify the notice using your usual banking app, the bank’s typed-in website, or the number on the back of your card. Then open a new account and move income and essential payments before moving the full balance.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links or call a number from the notice until you have independently verified it.
- Don’t send money to a “safe account” suggested by a caller, text, email, or pop-up.
- Don’t wire money unless the destination account is yours and the routing and account details are verified through a trusted channel.
- Don’t withdraw a large amount of cash in panic unless the bank confirms there is no safer practical option.
- Don’t stop paying rent, mortgage, utilities, insurance, or minimum loan payments without a backup payment route.
- Don’t make a one-time catch-up payment until you check whether the old autopay may still run.
- Don’t move every dollar without checking pending card transactions, checks, automatic debits, and bank instructions.
- Don’t accept a cashier’s check solution from anyone who contacted you; only get one directly from your bank and keep it in your control.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the notice safely.**  
   Log in using your usual banking app or type the bank’s official website yourself. If you need to call, use the number on the back of your debit card, on a statement you already trust, or on the bank’s verified website.

2. **Ask the bank for the exact closure details.**  
   Ask:
   - What is the exact closure date?
   - Will online access, debit card use, checks, ACH, wires, or branch withdrawals change before that date?
   - How will any remaining balance be returned?
   - Are there holds, pending transactions, negative balances, or documents that could affect access to the money?
   - Can the bank note that you are urgently moving direct deposits and automatic payments?

3. **Open a new bank or credit union account now.**  
   The goal is continuity, not finding the perfect long-term account. You need somewhere safe for income to land and essential payments to leave from.

4. **Move the three most important payment routes first.**  
   1) **Income:** update payroll, benefits, pension, Social Security, tax refunds, or other direct deposits to the new routing and account number.  
   2) **Essential autopay:** move rent or mortgage, utilities, car payment, insurance, childcare, loan minimums, and credit card minimums.  
   3) **Near-term spending money:** move enough accessible money to the new account for food, transport, medicine, housing, and essential bills.

5. **Choose the transfer method based on time and control.**  
   If you have a few days, an ACH transfer may work, but it can take time and may have limits. If the deadline is very close, ask the bank about a wire transfer to your own account or an official cashier’s check issued directly to you. Keep receipts, confirmation numbers, screenshots, and copies of any secure messages.

6. **Make a quick recurring-payment list.**  
   Check the last one or two statements and write down every recurring deposit, autopay, subscription, check, and scheduled transfer. Move essentials first. Nonessential subscriptions can wait.

7. **Download records before access changes.**  
   Save recent statements, tax documents, transaction history, check images if available, notices, and secure messages about the closure. Save them somewhere you can access without the old bank login.

8. **Handle pending items deliberately.**  
   If the bank confirms normal access continues until the closure date, leaving a small buffer may help cover checks, card transactions, or automatic payments that have not cleared. If access may change sooner, ask what funds can be moved now, move accessible money only to an account you control, and keep a written list of anything still pending.

9. **If the notice is fake or you already sent money, contact your bank immediately.**  
   Use a verified phone number or branch. Ask whether the transaction can be stopped, reversed, recalled, or flagged as fraud. Also change passwords and report the scam through an official route when you are safe to do so.

10. **If the closure seems wrong or the bank will not explain how to get your money, escalate in parallel.**  
   Start with the bank or credit union’s formal complaint channel. You can submit a complaint to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau for consumer financial products and services. For national banks and federal savings associations, HelpWithMyBank is the OCC’s consumer complaint route. For credit unions, the NCUA Consumer Assistance Center or a state credit union regulator may be the right route.

## What can wait
- Comparing rewards, rates, branch networks, or long-term account features.
- Writing a full complaint before your income and essential bills are protected.
- Moving every subscription today.
- Deciding whether you will ever use that bank again.
- Closing the old account yourself unless the bank tells you that you need to take a specific step.

## Important reassurance
This is stressful, but the first steps are manageable: verify the notice, create a safe place for incoming money, move essential payments, and keep records. You do not have to solve the whole dispute today.

## Scope note
These are first steps to keep money accessible, reduce missed-payment risk, and avoid scam losses. Later decisions about complaints, account reports, credit union or bank regulators, and longer-term banking choices may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, or professional advice. Banks and credit unions may close accounts for different reasons and may share limited details in some situations. If you are at risk of missing housing payments or essential bills, seek local consumer or legal help promptly.
