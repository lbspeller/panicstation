# What to do if… you receive a message saying you must pay an urgent fee to release a visa or permit and it feels suspicious (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay, do not click links, and do not provide personal data. Verify the claim independently by navigating directly to an official .gov website or contacting the relevant embassy through verified channels you found yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use any link, phone number, or "payment portal" provided in the suspicious message.
- Do not pay using gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfers (Western Union / MoneyGram), or payment apps (Zelle / Venmo / PayPal).
- Do not open attachments, download "forms," or scan QR codes included in the message.
- Do not share sensitive identifiers like your Social Security Number (SSN), passport photo, or one-time passcodes (OTP).
- Do not engage in a conversation with the sender to "verify" if they are real; scammers use high-pressure tactics to prevent rational checking.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all interaction with the message immediately.** **Your priority is to prevent the loss of funds or identity data by stepping away from the "urgent" pressure of the communication.**
2. **Document the communication without clicking any internal links.** **Take screenshots of the sender's address, the timestamp, and the specific wording of the demand, then save these to a secure folder.**
   - If the message contains a URL, copy the text of the link into a text note instead of clicking it.
3. **Verify the status of your application through official .gov portals only.** **Navigate manually to the official website of the agency mentioned (e.g., uscis.gov or travel.state.gov) to check your case status.**
   - If the message claims you owe a "Visa Integrity Fee" (a 2026 requirement), remember this is typically paid at the time of issuance at a consulate or via an official .gov portal, never via an emailed link.
   - For paper-filed USCIS forms, legitimate manual payments are only accepted via Form G-1450 (Credit Card) or Form G-1650 (ACH).
4. **Report the incident to federal authorities.** **Use official reporting channels to help stop the scammer and document the attempt for your own protection.**
   - Report to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.
   - Report cyber-enabled fraud to the FBI at ic3.gov.
   - If the fraud involves a U.S. visa overseas, contact the fraud prevention unit at the specific U.S. Embassy or Consulate that is handling your application.
5. **Secure your accounts if you have already interacted with the message.** **If you paid money or shared a password, you must take immediate steps to mitigate the damage.**
   - **Call your bank or credit card issuer immediately** to report the fraud and request a chargeback or account freeze.
   - If you shared a password, change it now on a separate, secure device and enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).
   - If you paid via gift card, contact the company that issued the card (e.g., Apple, Google, Amazon) immediately to see if the funds can be frozen.

## What can wait
- You do not need to delete the message yet; keep it as evidence until you have filed your reports.
- You do not need to worry about your "visa being cancelled" based solely on this message; official revocations involve formal, verifiable notices.
- You do not need to hire a lawyer immediately to "fix" the issue until you have confirmed a legitimate problem exists through official channels.

## Important reassurance
Scammers design these messages to bypass your logic by creating a sense of extreme urgency and fear of legal consequences. It is normal to feel panicked when your legal status or travel plans are threatened. Taking a few minutes to verify through a separate, official channel is the most effective way to stay in control of the situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying and responding to suspicious payment demands. It does not replace official legal or immigration advice regarding your actual case status or eligibility.

## Important note
U.S. government agencies (USCIS and Department of State) will never ask for payment over the phone, via text, or through unofficial third-party payment services. Always ensure the website you are visiting ends in ".gov" before entering any personal or financial information.
