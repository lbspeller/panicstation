# What to do if… you are told a loved one has died and you need to verify the details (USA)
## Short answer
Pause before telling a wider circle. Independently find and call the published main number for the law-enforcement agency, hospital, medical examiner office, or coroner office involved, then ask what has been confirmed and whether a reference number is available.

## Do not do these things
- Do not post the news on social media or send a group message before you have independently verified it.
- Do not rely on the incoming phone number, caller ID, voicemail, text, or link as your only verification route.
- Do not give an unverified caller a Social Security number, banking information, payment details, or account verification code.
- Do not feel pressured to update everyone immediately.
- Do not drive or travel immediately if you do not feel safe to do so.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and make notes.** Write down what the caller or message said. Ask one trusted person to stay with you while you verify the information, without notifying a wider group yet.
2. **Record the incoming details without treating them as verified.** Ask for the caller’s full name, title, organization, location, callback number, and whether a reference number is available.
3. **Find a published main number yourself.** Use the organization’s official website. Call the law-enforcement agency, hospital, medical examiner office, or coroner office through that number and ask to be connected to the appropriate staff member for the reported situation.
4. **Ask for the minimum confirmation you need before informing others.** Keep the questions plain:
   - “Has the death been confirmed?”
   - “Is my loved one’s identity confirmed?”
   - “Is anything still pending?”
   - “What can you share with me now?”
5. **Use an official channel for any information they request.** Ask what minimum information is needed and how to provide it safely. Do not provide financial information or payment details.
6. **Ask for a contact route and a follow-up time.** Ask who you should contact next, how to reach them through an official number, and when to call again or expect an update if that is known.
7. **Write down what is confirmed and what is still pending.** Include the date, time, names, organizations, published phone numbers, and any reference number.
8. **Once the death and identity are confirmed, choose a manageable way to inform others.** Tell one trusted support person first if that would help. Inform close family privately in the sequence that feels workable, and ask them not to post publicly until the people closest to the loved one have been told.
9. **If you are asked to travel somewhere, ask practical questions first.** Ask where to go, why your attendance is requested, what to bring, and whether a support person can come with you. Do not drive yourself if you do not feel safe to do so.

## What can wait
- You usually do not need to deal with death certificates, benefits, insurance, or accounts in the first hours.
- You usually do not need to make funeral decisions immediately unless an official contact explains an immediate need or you choose to begin.
- You do not need to answer extended family, coworkers, or social media messages immediately.

## Important reassurance
Taking time to verify the information before telling others is a careful and reasonable step. You can keep the next task small: use a published number, ask what is confirmed, and write it down.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on the circumstances and on local procedures, and you may need help from the relevant agency, hospital, medical examiner office, coroner office, funeral provider, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Procedures vary by state, county, organization, and circumstance. If information is inconsistent, pause and verify it again through a published main number.
