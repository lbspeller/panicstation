# What to do if… a restaurant tip adjustment posts days later and pushes your account into overdraft (USA)

## Short answer
Compare the final posted restaurant total with the receipt or tip you approved. If the posted amount is wrong, ask the restaurant and your bank or card issuer to correct it today; whether it is wrong or correct, ask your bank to review any overdraft fee and tell you what may still post while the balance is negative.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the pending amount was the final total. Some restaurant tips may be added after the initial authorization.
- Don’t dispute a total that matches the amount you approved. Ask the bank separately to review the overdraft fee.
- Don’t keep making payments from the overdrawn account until you have checked what is scheduled to post.
- Don’t cancel or replace the card solely because the final total is wrong. Tell your bank promptly if the payment is unrecognized or you suspect fraud.
- Don’t wait for the next statement if fees are appearing now or essential payments are at risk.
- Don’t send your full card number or security code to the restaurant.

## What to do now
1. **Compare the posted amount with what you approved.**
   - Open the final posted restaurant charge in your bank app.
   - Record the merchant name, transaction date, posting date, and final amount.
   - Compare that amount with your receipt, receipt photo, or the tip you selected on a payment screen.
   - Save a screenshot of the posted transaction.

2. **Reduce the risk of more account problems.**
   - If you safely can, add funds to the account. This may reduce the risk of more fees, returned payments, or declined essentials.
   - Check for scheduled payments that may post soon, such as rent, utilities, or automatic bill payments.
   - Ask your bank what may still post and what immediate options are available while the account is negative.

3. **Ask the restaurant to check the submitted total if the amount looks wrong.**
   - Ask the restaurant to confirm the base bill amount and the tip amount it submitted.
   - If the tip was entered incorrectly, ask for a correction or refund for the difference.
   - Ask for written confirmation of any correction or refund.

4. **Contact the bank or card issuer for the payment method today.**
   - If the final posted amount is wrong, say: “The final posted restaurant amount does not match what I approved. I want to dispute the incorrect amount.”
   - Whether the total is wrong or correct, say to your bank: “This posting pushed my account into overdraft. Please review any related overdraft fee.”
   - Ask for a case number and ask how to upload your receipt, screenshot, and any restaurant confirmation.

5. **If you paid with a debit card, ask for an error-resolution case.**
   - Tell the bank that you are reporting an incorrect electronic fund transfer amount.
   - Report the issue promptly. Regulation E generally requires notice no later than 60 days after the bank sends the statement first showing the alleged error.
   - If you report by phone, ask: “Do you require written confirmation within 10 business days? If so, where should I send it?”
   - Ask when you should expect an update. Investigation timeframes can vary, including for point-of-sale debit-card transactions.

6. **If you paid with a prepaid card, contact the issuer promptly.**
   - Ask the issuer to open an error-resolution case for an incorrect posted amount.
   - Ask which reporting timeframe applies to your prepaid account.
   - Ask whether registration or identity verification affects the protections available for your card.
   - Follow the issuer’s instructions for sending your receipt, screenshot, and a short description of the error.

7. **If you paid with a credit card, use the credit-card billing-dispute route.**
   - Call the card issuer promptly.
   - To protect billing-dispute rights, send a written billing-error notice within 60 calendar days after the charge appeared on your statement.
   - Ask the issuer for the correct address and what supporting documents to include.

8. **Ask about overdraft coverage if a one-time debit-card transaction caused a fee.**
   - Ask whether you opted in to overdraft coverage for one-time debit-card transactions and ATM withdrawals.
   - Ask whether you want to revoke that coverage for future one-time debit-card and ATM transactions.
   - Checks and recurring electronic payments may be handled differently, so ask what applies to your account.

9. **Keep a simple record.**
   - Save the receipt, screenshots, restaurant messages, bank messages, case number, and the date of each call.
   - If the initial bank or card-issuer contact does not resolve the issue, a Consumer Financial Protection Bureau complaint is a later escalation option.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide today whether to switch banks or accounts.
- You don’t need perfect paperwork before contacting the bank. Start with the posted transaction, your receipt if available, and a short timeline.
- You don’t need to file several complaints at once. Open the bank or card-issuer case first unless the institution is inaccessible or the situation is urgent.
- You don’t need to make a long-term decision about account settings before the immediate risk is stable.

## Important reassurance
A later posted restaurant total can be a tip adjustment rather than a second charge. It is reasonable to compare the amount with your receipt, ask for a correction if it is not what you approved, and ask the bank to review any overdraft fee. A fee reversal is not automatic, but asking for a review is appropriate.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from your bank’s disputes team, your card issuer, or a consumer assistance resource.

## Important note
This guide provides general information, not legal, financial, or other professional advice. The right next step depends on the payment type, what you approved, your account terms, and your bank or card issuer’s process.
