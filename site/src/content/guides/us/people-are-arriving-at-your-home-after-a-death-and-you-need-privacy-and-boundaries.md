# What to do if… people are arriving at your home after a death and you need privacy and boundaries (USA)

## Short answer
If anyone needs immediate police, fire, or ambulance help, call 911 first. Otherwise, you can usually choose not to let visitors in right now. Ask one trusted person to handle the door and messages, then use one clear boundary: “We’re not having visitors today. Please leave a message.”

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door just because you feel guilty, shocked, or pressured.
- Do not try to host people, answer every question, or manage other people’s emotions.
- Do not get pulled into arguments or family meetings in your home today.
- Do not hand over keys, identification, wallets, medications, or paperwork in the rush of the moment.
- Do not make major decisions about money, belongings, arrangements, or disputes while visitors are pressing for answers.
- Do not let anyone pressure you to drink alcohol or use substances if you feel unsteady.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether emergency help is needed.** If anyone needs immediate police, fire, or ambulance help, call **911** before doing anything else.
2. **Create a quieter pause.** Lock the door if that helps you feel safer. Silence non-urgent phone notifications, but keep one trusted contact audible.
3. **Choose one gatekeeper.** Call or text one person you trust: “I need help handling the door and messages for the next few hours. Can you come over, or stay on the phone with me?” If they come over, ask them to be the point of contact.
4. **Use one boundary sentence.** Through the door, by text, or by phone, say: “Thank you for coming. We’re not able to have visitors today. Please leave a message and we’ll reach out.” Repeat it once if needed, then stop responding.
5. **Stop more visitors from arriving.** Ask your gatekeeper to send one group message: “Please don’t come to the house. We’re taking private time today. We’ll share updates when we can.”
6. **Put up a simple door note if the knocking continues.** Write: “No visitors today. Please do not knock. Leave a message.”
7. **Keep essentials with you.** Put your keys, wallet, phone, medications, and important papers in one bag or one secure place. If anyone is already inside, say: “Please don’t clean, sort, or remove anything.”
8. **Accept only limited help that feels useful.** For example: “Please leave groceries at the door,” “Please call the relatives and tell them not to come over,” or “Please sit with me quietly for a few minutes.”
9. **Escalate if someone will not leave or you feel unsafe.** If you need immediate help from police, fire services, or an ambulance, call **911**. For a non-emergency concern, look up your local law enforcement agency’s official website and use its listed non-emergency contact route.
10. **Use crisis support if the distress feels too heavy to hold alone.** You can call or text **988**, or use the 988 Lifeline chat service, for emotional support. If you are in immediate danger or cannot keep yourself safe, call **911**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to answer every call, reply to every message, or update everyone today.
- You do not need to decide arrangements, belongings, or family disagreements while people are at your door.
- You do not need to host visitors or take care of other people’s feelings.
- Decisions and non-urgent paperwork can wait until the immediate pressure has eased. Keep any urgent instructions already given by emergency responders or local authorities.

## Important reassurance
Wanting privacy and control of your home after a death is understandable. A clear boundary is not unkind. It can give you the breathing room to get through the next few hours.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce pressure, protect your space, and get immediate support. Later decisions and practical tasks may need help from appropriate specialists.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Contact emergency services if you need immediate help.
