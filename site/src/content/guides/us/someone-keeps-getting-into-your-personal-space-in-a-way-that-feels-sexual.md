# What to do if… someone keeps getting into your personal space in a way that feels sexual (USA)
## Short answer
Move toward other people, staff, or a safer visible area. You do not need to decide what to call the behaviour or prove intent before creating distance and asking for help.

## Do not do these things
- Do not move somewhere quieter with them when you have a safer option.
- Do not feel that you have to confront them, explain yourself, or work out their intent in the moment.
- Do not stay close to film or photograph them if moving away is safer.
- Do not blame yourself if you freeze, go quiet, or do not react immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Create distance.** Step away, change seats, move to another part of the space, or move closer to other people or staff.
2. **Use a brief boundary phrase only if it feels safe.** You can say: “Back up,” “Do not touch me,” or “Give me space.” You can skip this step.
3. **Ask someone nearby for immediate help.** Say: “Can I stand with you? Someone keeps getting into my space.” Ask staff or security to help you move away from the person.
4. **If you are on transit or in a venue, use the safest available option.** Move to another area, alert available staff, or use an available help point if it is safe to do so.
5. **If this is happening at work or school, move to a safer area and ask for support.** A supervisor, HR contact, school staff member, campus staff member, or Title IX contact may be able to help create distance or arrange immediate support.
6. **If you need immediate emergency help, call 911.** If you are safe and want confidential support, call the National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-HOPE (4673), use its online chat, or text HOPE to 64673.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide now whether to make a police report or formal complaint.
- You do not have to work out whether the contact was intentional before moving away.
- You do not have to confront the person again.
- Writing down what happened can wait until you are safe and only if you later find it useful.
- Workplace, school, or other reporting options can be considered later with support.

## Important reassurance
Feeling unsure, frozen, angry, or shaken does not mean you did anything wrong. Moving away and asking for support is reasonable even when you are not certain what the other person intended.

## Scope note
These are first steps for reaching a safer pause. Later decisions about support or reporting may be easier with help from a specialist service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you need immediate emergency help, call 911.
