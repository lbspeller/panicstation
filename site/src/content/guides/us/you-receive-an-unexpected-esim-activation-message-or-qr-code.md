# What to do if… you receive an unexpected eSIM activation message or QR code (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible SIM-swap or port-out attempt. Do not scan the QR code or tap links in the message, and contact your wireless carrier through a trusted route to block any unauthorized eSIM, SIM change, or number transfer.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t scan the QR code or continue with any “Add eSIM” prompt from the message.
- Don’t click “cancel,” “verify,” or “secure your account” links in the message.
- Don’t install any app suggested by the message.
- Don’t reply to the text or email.
- Don’t share verification codes, one-time passcodes, account PINs, or passwords with anyone who contacted you.
- Don’t call a phone number shown in the message; use your carrier’s official app, your bill, or the carrier’s official website.
- Don’t reset or wipe your phone as the first step; it may slow you down and usually does not stop a phone-number takeover.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the activation path.**  
   Leave the QR code alone. Do not scan it, do not approve a new eSIM, and do not follow any links in the message.

2. **Contact your carrier through a trusted route.**  
   Use the carrier’s official app, a number on your bill, or the carrier’s official website. Say: “I received an eSIM activation message or QR code I did not request. Please check for an unauthorized SIM change, eSIM issue, or port-out request and secure my account.”

3. **Ask the carrier for specific protections.**  
   Ask whether they can:
   - Block or cancel any pending eSIM activation, SIM change, or number transfer.
   - Add or reset an account PIN or passcode.
   - Add a number lock, transfer lock, or port-out lock if available.
   - Require extra verification before any future SIM or eSIM change.
   - Tell you whether any eSIM was issued, whether your line was moved, and whether any port-out request is pending.
   - Give you a case number and note the account as a suspected unauthorized SIM or eSIM change.

4. **Check whether service has already been affected.**  
   If your phone suddenly shows “No Service,” calls or texts stop working, or login codes are not arriving, tell the carrier this may be a SIM swap in progress and ask them to restore your line only after securing the account.

5. **Secure the accounts that could reset everything else.**  
   From a device you still control, change the password on your primary email account first. Then secure your Apple ID or Google account, banking apps, payment apps, and any account that recently sent a login or password-reset alert.

6. **Move important logins away from SMS where you can.**  
   For email, banking, payment, and cloud accounts, switch from text-message codes to an authenticator app, passkey, or security key where the service offers it.

7. **Contact banks or payment providers if there are warning signs.**  
   If you see unfamiliar login alerts, password resets you did not start, missing funds, new payees, new cards, or suspicious transactions, contact the fraud number in the official app, on your card, or on your statement. Ask them to add extra verification and review recent activity.

8. **Report it if there was fraud, account takeover, or money loss.**  
   Use the FTC’s fraud reporting site for scams or attempted scams. Use IdentityTheft.gov if personal information was misused. Use IC3 if you believe this was cyber-enabled fraud, account takeover, or financial loss.

9. **Write down a short timeline.**  
   Save screenshots of the message, the time it arrived, any service loss, account alerts, carrier and bank contacts, case numbers, and what each organization said it did.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to change carriers.
- You do not need to decide now whether to change your phone number.
- You do not need to publicly post about it or confront the sender.
- You do not need to do a full device wipe unless a trusted support channel identifies a separate device compromise.
- You do not need to solve every account at once; start with your carrier, then email, then financial accounts.

## Important reassurance
This kind of message is meant to rush you into scanning, tapping, or “verifying.” Pausing, avoiding the QR code, securing the carrier account, and protecting email and financial accounts can reduce the chance of further harm.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilize the situation. If accounts were accessed, money was taken, or the carrier confirms an unauthorized change, later decisions may need help from the carrier’s fraud team, your bank, identity-theft recovery resources, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or other professional advice. Carrier protections and feature names vary by provider and plan.
