# What to do if… someone threatens to leak private sexual messages or claims to have access to your accounts (USA)

## Short answer
Pause contact. Do not send money, verification codes, passwords, or more sexual content under pressure. Secure the email account used for password resets and your key accounts through their official apps or websites.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay or keep negotiating under pressure; paying may not stop further demands.
- Don’t send more intimate content, passwords, or verification codes.
- Don’t click links, install apps, or use recovery pages supplied by the person making the threat.
- Don’t post publicly or confront the person while your accounts may still be at risk.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Silence notifications from the person and stop replying while you take the next steps. If you are in immediate physical danger, call 911.
2. **Secure your email account first if it is used for password resets.** Open the official app or website independently. Change the password to a strong, unique password, sign out of other devices, turn on two-factor authentication, and check that the recovery email address and phone number are yours. If you are locked out, use the provider’s official recovery process.
3. **Secure the other accounts most likely to matter.** Change any reused passwords, sign out of other sessions, and turn on two-factor authentication where available. Prioritize social, messaging, and cloud accounts mentioned in the threat.
4. **Check for signs of access using official account settings.** Look for unfamiliar login alerts, notifications about password changes you did not make, recovery details you do not recognize, email forwarding rules you did not create, or messages sent from your account.
5. **Keep evidence minimal.** If it feels safe, save one or two screenshots showing the threat, the profile or handle, and any payment demand; do not investigate further or download sexual content.
6. **Use the platform’s safety tools.** Block the account or number. You may also report the account through the platform and request removal if content is posted.
7. **Use an image harm-reduction service only if images or videos are involved.**
   - If an intimate image or video shows you when you were 18 or older and you already have access to it, StopNCII.org may help participating platforms look for matching copies that violate their policies. It does not remove content from the whole internet.
   - If an image or video was taken before you were 18, Take It Down may help limit sharing on participating public or unencrypted services. Do not download, send, or share content solely to submit it.
8. **Choose any reporting support that feels manageable.**
   - You may file a report with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center for a cyber-enabled threat, even if you are unsure whether the complaint qualifies.
   - If the content involves a minor, you can use NCMEC’s CyberTipline for specialist support and reporting.
9. **If you sent money, contact the relevant company promptly.** Contact your bank, card issuer, payment app, transfer service, gift-card issuer, or cryptocurrency company and ask whether the transaction can be reversed.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to prove whether the person truly has access before securing your accounts.
- You don’t need to collect perfect evidence or investigate the person.
- You don’t need to close every account at once after securing your email and the accounts most likely to matter.
- You don’t need to decide now whether to post publicly, confront the person, or tell a wider group of people.

## Important reassurance
This kind of threat is meant to create shame, fear, and urgency. You are not to blame for someone choosing to threaten or pressure you. Taking one small step at a time can help you regain some control.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, intended to reduce immediate harm and buy time. Later decisions may need support from the platform, a cybersecurity specialist, law enforcement, or a victim-support service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, cybersecurity, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate physical danger, call 911.
