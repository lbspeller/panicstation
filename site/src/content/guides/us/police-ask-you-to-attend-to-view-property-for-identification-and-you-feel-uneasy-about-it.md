# What to do if… police ask you to attend to view property for identification and you feel uneasy about it (USA)

## Short answer
Independently verify the request by calling the department’s official non-emergency or dispatch number found on a verified government website. Do not meet anyone or provide personal data until you have confirmed the officer’s identity and the existence of a valid case number.

## Do not do these things
- Do not follow instructions from a caller or texter who pressures you to act immediately or stay on the line.
- Do not rely on caller ID names, "official" photos of badges sent via text, or social media profiles as proof of identity.
- Do not provide sensitive personal information such as Social Security Numbers, banking details, or passwords to a caller.
- Do not pay any "recovery fees," "storage fees," or "processing costs" via wire transfer, cryptocurrency, payment apps (Zelle / Venmo), or gift cards.
- Do not agree to meet a "plainclothes" officer in a secluded area, a private home, or a parking lot if you feel unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Request specific identifying details and end the call.** Ask for the **officer’s full name**, **badge or serial number**, **department name**, and the **case or incident number** associated with the property.
   - Inform the caller that you will call the department back through their main switchboard to confirm the details.
2. **Locate the department's official contact information independently.** Search for the **city or county government website** to find the main non-emergency number or the Property and Evidence Unit.
   - Avoid using any "callback" numbers provided by the original caller or found in a text message.
3. **Call the verified number to authenticate the request.** Ask for the **Records Division** or the **Property Room** and provide the case number and officer’s name for verification.
   - Confirm whether there is an active request for a citizen to identify or retrieve property.
4. **Schedule the meeting in a secure, public police facility.** If the request is legitimate, **arrange the viewing during business hours** inside the main police station lobby or at the designated public service window of the Property and Evidence Unit.
5. **Verify the identity of any person claiming to be an officer in person.** Request to see **both a metal badge and a department-issued photo ID card** if you meet an officer.
   - Legitimate officers will allow you to note their information and verify it with a supervisor or dispatch.
6. **Involve a witness or support person.** Take a **trusted friend or family member** with you to the station and ensure they stay with you during the interaction.
7. **Document the interaction for your records.** Maintain a **written log of the officer's name**, badge number, case number, and the date and time of the contact.
   - Save copies of any emails or screenshots of texts received regarding the property.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a formal statement or participate in an interview immediately upon identifying the property.
- You do not need to justify your decision to verify the officer’s credentials; legitimate law enforcement expects and encourages verification.
- You do not need to accept or take possession of the property immediately if you are unsure of its origin or legal status.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard safety practice to verify the identity of someone claiming to be a government official. Legitimate law enforcement agencies have established protocols for property recovery and will not be offended by a request for verification. Scams involving police impersonation are frequent, and your hesitation is a normal, protective response.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for verifying police contact regarding property. It does not cover legal defense, the specifics of criminal evidence law, or the long-term recovery of seized assets which may require an attorney.

## Important note
This information is for safety and harm-prevention purposes and does not constitute legal advice. If you believe a person is currently impersonating an officer in your presence or you are in immediate physical danger, call **911**.
