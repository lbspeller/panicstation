# What to do if… your computer displays a ransom note and you have not paid or contacted anyone yet (USA)

## Short answer
Disconnect the affected computer from the internet and any network first. Do not pay, click anything in the note, or contact the attacker.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay, bargain, or message the attacker “to see if it’s real.”
- Don’t click links, QR codes, chat buttons, “support” buttons, or attachments in the ransom note.
- Don’t plug in backup drives, USB storage, phones, or other computers to “save what you can.”
- Don’t log into email, banking, cloud storage, work systems, or your password manager from the affected computer.
- Don’t install random “decryptor,” “cleanup,” or “recovery” tools from ads, pop-ups, emails, or people who contact you first.
- Don’t wipe, reinstall, or restore from backup yet, especially if this is a work, school, or managed device.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the affected computer.**
   - Turn off Wi-Fi.
   - Unplug the Ethernet cable if there is one.
   - Turn off Bluetooth if it is on.
   - Disconnect from any dock, office network, shared drive, or network storage.
2. **Do not attach anything else to it.**
   - Leave backup drives, USB sticks, phones, cameras, and external hard drives unplugged.
   - If a backup drive is already connected, do not open files on it or move it to another computer yet.
3. **Record what you can see without interacting with the note.**
   - Take a photo of the ransom note with your phone.
   - Capture the victim ID, contact method, payment demand, cryptocurrency address if visible, file extensions, and the date and time.
   - Write down what changed, such as files not opening, renamed files, a locked screen, or a shared drive being affected.
4. **If this is a work, school, or managed device, stop self-remediation and report internally.**
   - Contact IT, security, or the helpdesk from a different device or phone.
   - Say: “Ransom note on screen; device isolated; no payment; no contact with attacker.”
   - Follow their instructions before powering off, wiping, restoring, or transporting the device.
5. **From a known-clean device, protect key accounts.**
   - Change passwords for email, banking, Apple, Google, Microsoft, cloud storage, remote-access tools, and any account you used on the affected computer.
   - Turn on multi-factor authentication where available.
   - Treat unexpected password reset prompts, login alerts, or new device notices as warning signs.
6. **Report through an official channel.**
   - Individuals and organizations can report ransomware to the FBI’s IC3.
   - CISA says victims can report to the FBI, CISA, or the U.S. Secret Service, and usually only need to report the incident once.
   - Use a clean device and include the details you recorded, if you can do so safely.
7. **If money or identity theft risk is possible, slow down financial exposure.**
   - Call your bank using the number on your card, bank app, or bank website.
   - Do not use any phone number, link, QR code, or chat option shown in the ransom note.
   - Watch for unauthorized transfers, new payees, password reset emails, and unfamiliar login alerts.
8. **Keep the affected computer isolated until there is a trusted recovery plan.**
   - If you must move it, keep it disconnected from networks and other devices.
   - For a personal computer, use a reputable local technician, your device manufacturer, or a trusted security professional rather than a pop-up service.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you will ever pay.
- You do not need to try decryption immediately.
- You do not need to prove whether the attacker really has your data before you contain the device.
- You do not need to rebuild, wipe, restore from backup, or investigate everything right now.
- You can leave deeper recovery, backup checks, data-theft review, and insurance or legal questions until the device is isolated and the first report or internal notification is started.

## Important reassurance
Ransom notes are designed to make you rush. Because you have not paid or contacted anyone yet, you still have room to slow the situation down, protect other devices and accounts, and get help without giving the attacker more information.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for containment and harm prevention. Full recovery may need IT support, backup restoration, malware removal, data-theft review, incident response help, or specialist advice.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, forensic, cybersecurity, or professional advice. If the computer belongs to an employer, school, client, or organization, or contains other people’s data, follow the relevant incident process and get appropriate specialist help.
