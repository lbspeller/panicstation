# What to do if… police ask to search your car and you want to know your options (USA)

## Short answer
If an officer asks to search your car and you do not want to agree, calmly say: **“I do not consent to a search.”** Do not physically interfere if the officer proceeds anyway.

## Do not do these things
- Do not block a door, grab an item, argue, run, or physically resist.
- Do not make sudden movements toward the glove box, center console, under a seat, or your phone.
- Do not agree to a search just because you feel pressured, if you do not want to agree.
- Do not give a long explanation or try to debate the law at the roadside.
- Do not lie or present false documents.

## What to do now
1. **Stabilize the stop.** Pull over safely. Keep your hands visible. If you need to move, say what you are about to do before reaching.
2. **Handle driving documents carefully.** If asked for your license, registration, proof of insurance, or other driving documents required by applicable law, say where they are and wait for the officer’s response before reaching.
3. **Ask one status question when it is safe to do so.** Say: **“Am I free to go?”** If the answer is yes, leave calmly. If the answer is no, remain where you are unless the officer tells you otherwise.
4. **If asked for permission to search, answer clearly.** Say: **“I do not consent to a search.”** Repeat it once if needed. Do not argue.
5. **If the officer searches anyway, do not physically interfere.** You can calmly say: **“I am not resisting. I do not consent to the search.”**
6. **If you are a passenger, ask about your own status.** Say: **“Am I free to leave?”** If the answer is yes, confirm that you may step away, then leave calmly without sudden movement.
7. **Note details when it is safe.** Write down the location, time, agency, officer name or badge number if provided, patrol-car number if visible, what was said, what areas were searched, and whether anything was taken.
8. **Do not make recording your priority.** If you decide to record, prioritize safety. Do not interfere with the stop or make a sudden reach for your phone. Recording and audio rules can depend on the place and circumstances.
9. **Ask about paperwork if property is taken or the car is towed.** When it is safe, ask how to obtain any available property, towing, inventory, or case paperwork.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove your rights at the roadside.
- You do not need to decide now whether to file a complaint or contact a lawyer.
- You do not need to decide on the roadside whether to answer optional questions. You can calmly say that you would like to remain silent, while providing documents and identifying information required by applicable law.
- You do not need to organize every detail immediately. Brief notes are enough until you are somewhere safe.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaky or confused during a police stop is understandable. A clear statement that you do not consent can help make your position clear. Staying calm does not mean you are agreeing to a search.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Vehicle-search rules are fact-specific, can vary by state, and may differ in border or checkpoint situations. Later decisions may need advice from a qualified lawyer, especially if property was taken, the car was towed, or an arrest occurred.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal or professional advice. Officers may be able to search a vehicle without consent in some circumstances. Do not physically resist a search.
