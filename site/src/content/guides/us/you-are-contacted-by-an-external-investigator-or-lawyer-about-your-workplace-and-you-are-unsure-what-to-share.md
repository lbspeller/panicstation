# What to do if… you are contacted by an external investigator or lawyer about your workplace and you are unsure what to share (USA)

## Short answer
Decline to sign the document immediately and request an electronic copy for formal review. Verify your state's specific 2026 enforceability thresholds and check if the agreement is supported by new "consideration" (benefits) before responding.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any document during an exit interview or under a "same-day" deadline.
- Do not acknowledge receipt in a way that implies "acceptance" (e.g., "I agree to these terms" via email).
- Do not disclose your new employer’s identity, salary, or client list while under pressure.
- Do not assume a document is valid just because it was presented by HR or a lawyer.
- Do not delete correspondence or notes regarding threats to withhold your final paycheck.
- Do not use company equipment or accounts to research non-compete laws.

## What to do now
1. **Decline immediate signing and request the full document in a portable format.** **Inform the representative that you are not prepared to sign new legal terms today and require time to review the complete agreement.**
   - Request that they email the final version to your personal email address.

2. **Determine if this is a new agreement or a modification of an existing one.** **Ask HR or your manager in writing to clarify if this document replaces, amends, or adds to any previous agreements you signed during your tenure.**
   - Request a copy of your original offer letter and any previously signed restrictive covenants for comparison.

3. **Identify the "consideration" being offered in exchange for your signature.** **Review the document to see if the company is offering a specific benefit—such as severance pay or a bonus—that you are not already entitled to receive.**
   - In many states, a non-compete signed after employment has ended is unenforceable unless the employer provides new, valuable consideration.

4. **Verify the non-compete laws in the state where you work.** **Determine if your state has a total ban (California, Minnesota, Oklahoma, North Dakota, Wyoming) or an income-based threshold (such as Washington, Colorado, or Maryland).**
   - Note that California law (as of 2024/2025) voids non-competes even if they were signed in another state where they might be legal.

5. **Isolate your final paycheck from the negotiation.** **If the company implies your final wages are contingent on signing, state in writing that federal and state laws require the payment of earned wages regardless of post-employment agreements.**
   - If wages are withheld, contact the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division at 1-866-487-9243 or your state's labor department.

6. **Establish a secure log of all exit communications.** **Save copies of any emails, texts, or meeting notes where the employer mentions your next job, deadlines, or conditions for your exit.**
   - Keep these records on personal devices only.

7. **Consult an employment attorney licensed in your specific state.** **Present the document to a professional to determine if the geographic scope, duration, and industry restrictions meet the "reasonableness" test required in non-ban states.**
   - Use your state bar association’s referral service if you do not have an attorney.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to rescind your acceptance of a new job offer today.
- You do not need to respond to "reminders" or "follow-up" messages from HR for at least 48 to 72 hours while you seek advice.
- You do not need to justify why you are refusing to sign or provide details about your future plans.
- You do not need to resolve whether the non-compete is "fair"; you only need to determine if it is "enforceable."

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel startled or intimidated when presented with legal restrictions during your final hours at a company. Taking a pause to review a contract is a standard business practice and does not imply you are being uncooperative. Many post-resignation non-competes are unenforceable or "nuisance" documents designed to discourage mobility, and you have the right to professional counsel before agreeing to them.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Non-compete enforceability is highly technical and depends on state-specific statutes, recent 2026 court rulings, and the specific wording of the agreement.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and is not legal advice. The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) shifted in 2025/2026 to case-by-case enforcement rather than a nationwide ban; therefore, your rights depend almost entirely on state law. If your final pay, severance, or ability to start a new role is at risk, you must contact a qualified employment lawyer in your jurisdiction immediately.
