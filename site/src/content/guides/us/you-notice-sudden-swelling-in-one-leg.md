# What to do if… you notice sudden swelling in one leg (USA)

## Short answer
If sudden one-sided leg swelling comes with new shortness of breath, chest pain, coughing up blood, fainting, or feeling severely unwell, call 911 now. If those symptoms are not present, arrange same-day medical care to check for causes that can include a blood clot.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sleep on it when the swelling is sudden and mainly in one leg.
- Do not repeatedly press, rub, or stretch the swollen area to see if it goes away.
- Do not take someone else’s anticoagulant, blood thinner, or leftover prescription medicine.
- Do not start a long drive or flight, or sit immobile for hours, while waiting to decide what to do.
- Do not ignore new breathing symptoms, chest pain, coughing up blood, fainting, or severe lightheadedness.

## What to do now
1. Check for emergency symptoms. Call 911 if you have any of these:
   - new or unexplained shortness of breath
   - chest pain or chest discomfort, especially if it is worse when breathing deeply or coughing
   - coughing up blood
   - fainting, near-fainting, severe lightheadedness, confusion, or feeling severely unwell

2. If you do not have emergency symptoms, get same-day medical advice or evaluation.
   - Call your primary care clinician, nurse line, or local urgent care and say: "I have sudden swelling in one leg. Can you evaluate a possible blood clot today?"
   - If they cannot assess you today, cannot advise you quickly, or cannot arrange same-day testing if needed, go to an emergency department.
   - Do not drive yourself if you feel faint, short of breath, confused, or unsafe to drive.

3. If you are pregnant or recently postpartum, call your OB, midwife, or labor and delivery unit for same-day direction. If you cannot reach them quickly, use an emergency department, or call 911 if emergency symptoms are present.

4. Write down the details before you leave or while someone else helps you arrange care:
   - when the swelling started
   - whether the swelling is in the calf, ankle, thigh, foot, or whole leg
   - whether there is pain, tenderness, warmth, redness, discoloration, tightness, or a visible size difference
   - recent long travel, bed rest, surgery, injury, illness, pregnancy, postpartum period, hormones, cancer treatment, previous clots, or family history of clots
   - your medicines, allergies, and any bleeding problems

5. While arranging care or transport:
   - Remove tight socks, tight shoes, or tight clothing around the swollen area.
   - Keep the leg comfortable and avoid vigorous activity.
   - If you have to wait briefly, avoid staying completely still for hours; change position gently if comfortable.
   - Bring ID, insurance information if you have it, and a medication list.

## What can wait
- You do not need to figure out whether it is definitely a clot before getting help.
- You do not need to compare photos online or try home tests.
- You do not need to decide treatment now; the immediate task is getting assessed.
- You do not need to contact many places at once. Choose the fastest safe route to same-day medical care, or call 911 if emergency symptoms appear.

## Important reassurance
Sudden swelling in one leg does not always mean a blood clot, but it is a symptom that should be taken seriously. Getting checked promptly is a sensible safety step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Diagnosis, testing, and treatment decisions may need a clinician or emergency medical team.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, or a substitute for professional care. If symptoms escalate, especially breathing symptoms, chest pain, coughing up blood, fainting, or severe illness, call 911.
