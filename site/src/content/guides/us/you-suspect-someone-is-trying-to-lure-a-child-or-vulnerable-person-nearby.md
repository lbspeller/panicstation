# What to do if… you suspect someone is trying to lure a child or vulnerable person nearby (USA)

## Short answer
Stay at a safe distance and call 911 if there may be immediate danger or you are unsure. Keep watching from safety and be ready to describe where you are, who is involved, and what you observed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the person or try to catch them.
- Do not shout accusations or create a crowd.
- Do not follow closely, chase, block a vehicle, or physically intervene if that would put you in danger.
- Do not wait until you are certain before reporting a possible urgent risk.
- Do not post images, video, names, faces, license plates, or identifying details online.

## What to do now
1. Move to a safer vantage point with visibility and an easy exit. If you are in a store, transit hub, school area, park, library, or venue, move toward staff, security, a staffed counter, or other adults rather than moving closer alone.

2. Call 911 if the person may leave with the child or vulnerable person soon, if there is pulling, blocking, pressure, distress, confusion, or a vehicle involved, or if you are unsure how urgent it is. Say plainly: “I am at a safe distance. I am concerned someone may be trying to lure a child or vulnerable person.”

3. If it is clearly not immediate danger, contact the local police non-emergency number for the place where it happened. If anything changes while you are deciding or reporting, call 911.

4. Report observations, not conclusions. Give the exact location, entrance, nearby landmark, direction of travel, and what you saw or heard, such as offering a ride, gifts, money, food, a phone, secrecy, help finding something, or urging the person away from a caregiver or group.

5. Note identifying details without getting closer. Include clothing, height, build, hair, distinguishing features, bags, and any vehicle color, make, model, license plate, parking spot, and direction it could leave. Note the time.

6. Take a photo or video only if you can do it from where you already are, it does not make you more visible, it does not require following, and it does not escalate the situation. A clear description is enough.

7. Quietly involve on-site staff or security if they are present. Say: “I have contacted police because I am worried someone may be trying to lure a child or vulnerable person. Please keep eyes on it and avoid confrontation.”

8. If the person leaves before officers arrive, do not pursue. Stay safe and update 911 or local police with the direction of travel, vehicle details, and whether the child or vulnerable person left with them.

9. After immediate danger is addressed, use the right reporting route for ongoing risk. For a missing or exploited child, you can also contact the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children at 1-800-THE-LOST, 1-800-843-5678. For an older adult or adult who may be vulnerable to abuse, neglect, or exploitation, local Adult Protective Services routes vary by state; use your state or local APS contact, the NAPSA state directory, or the Eldercare Locator for routing.

## What can wait
- Proving intent or deciding whether a crime definitely happened.
- Building a perfect timeline.
- Calling several organizations while the situation is still live.
- Posting community alerts online.
- Deciding what should happen to the suspected person.

## Important reassurance
You do not have to be certain to report a possible urgent risk. Your job is to stay safe, pass on what you observed, and let responders assess it.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first few minutes only. Later decisions, reports, or support may need help from law enforcement, child-protection staff, Adult Protective Services, or another specialist service.

## Important note
This guide is general information for quick, harm-reducing first actions. It is not legal, medical, therapeutic, child-protection, investigative, or other professional advice. If you believe anyone is in immediate danger, call 911.
