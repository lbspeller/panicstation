# What to do if… you hear birds in a chimney or wall cavity and think they may be trapped (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using the fireplace or any appliance venting into that flue immediately and contact a licensed wildlife rehabilitator to determine if the birds are nesting or physically trapped.

## Do not do these things
- Do not light a fire or run any appliance that vents into the chimney or flue.
- Do not use smoke, chemicals, water, or loud noises to attempt to drive birds out.
- Do not use chimney brushes, rods, or power tools to investigate the noise.
- Do not cut into drywall or dismantle chimney masonry without professional guidance.
- Do not block the chimney top or exterior vents while sounds are present.
- Do not handle any bird with bare hands or attempt to remove an active nest.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the hazard by disabling all combustion appliances connected to the affected flue.** Stop using the fireplace, wood stove, or gas heater immediately, as heat and carbon monoxide can kill birds or push them further into the structure.
2. **Listen for 60 seconds to determine the specific location and nature of the sound.** Note if you hear repetitive high-pitched chirping (likely a nest), scratching (likely a trapped bird), or heavy fluttering (likely a larger bird like an owl or duck).
3. **If a bird has already entered the living space through the fireplace, darken the room and provide a single exit.** Close all interior doors to contain the bird, turn off all lights, draw curtains on all windows except one, and open that window or an exterior door to the outside.
4. **Identify the presence of an active nest by listening for rhythmic "peeping" sounds.** If you hear persistent begging calls from chicks, treat the area as a federally protected nest under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act and do not attempt to move it.
5. **Contact a licensed wildlife rehabilitator using a state-specific directory.** 
   - Search for "licensed wildlife rehabilitator [Your State]" to find professionals permitted to handle migratory birds.
   - Contact a CSIA-certified chimney professional if the bird is in the flue and you suspect a blockage.
6. **Check the fireplace damper if you hear fluttering at the very bottom of the chimney.** If you have a fireplace insert or doors, look through the glass with a flashlight to see if a bird is resting on the damper or has fallen into the firebox.
7. **Observe the exterior of the house from the ground for 10 minutes.** Look for adult birds entering or exiting the chimney top, soffit gaps, or wall vents to help the rehabilitator identify the species and entry point.
8. **Wear heavy gloves and safety glasses if a rehabilitator instructs you to move a grounded, soot-covered bird.** Place the bird in a cardboard box with air holes and a soft cloth, then transport it immediately to a licensed facility.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact bird species to begin stabilization.
- You do not need to purchase or install a chimney cap until the birds have fledged or been removed.
- You do not need to clean the chimney or remove old nesting material until a professional confirms the nest is inactive.
- You do not need to decide on permanent repairs for wall cavities or vents immediately.

## Important reassurance
Birds frequently use chimneys and wall voids as substitute nesting sites or accidentally fall into flues. In many cases, such as with Chimney Swifts, the birds are intentionally there and will leave on their own within a few weeks. Taking a moment to pause and seek professional guidance prevents legal violations and avoids injuring the wildlife.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and harm reduction for homeowners. It is not a substitute for professional wildlife removal or certified chimney inspection.

## Important note
Disturbing or removing active nests of migratory birds is a federal offense in the USA. Always verify the legal status of the species with a licensed rehabilitator or state wildlife agency before taking any physical action.
