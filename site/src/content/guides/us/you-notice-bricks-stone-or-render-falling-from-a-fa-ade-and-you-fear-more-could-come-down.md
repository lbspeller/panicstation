# What to do if… you notice bricks, stone, or render falling from a façade and you fear more could come down (USA)

## Short answer
Move immediately away from the building and keep all others out of the potential fall zone. Call 911 if debris is actively falling onto a public sidewalk or street, or if there is an imminent danger of collapse.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stand directly under the facade to look up or investigate the source of the falling material.
- Do not enter the "drop zone" to move debris, clean the sidewalk, or check the damage.
- Do not attempt to pull at loose pieces or perform temporary repairs, as this can trigger a larger secondary fall.
- Do not use the building entrance directly below the affected area if any other exit is available.
- Do not assume the danger has passed if the falling stops, as masonry often sheds intermittently.
- Do not delay reporting the hazard to wait for a landlord or property manager to respond first.

## What to do now
1. **Clear the immediate exclusion zone.** Move at least 20 feet away from the building line, or across the street if possible.
   - Debris falling from height can bounce or shatter, significantly increasing the hazard area beyond the direct vertical drop.
2. **Warn pedestrians and building occupants.** Alert people nearby to stay back and avoid the sidewalk directly adjacent to the affected wall.
   - If there is a doorman or security guard for the building, notify them immediately to cordoning off the area.
3. **Call 911 for active or high-risk hazards.**
   - Use 911 if pieces are currently falling, the sidewalk is busy, someone has been struck, or you see a large section of the wall bulging or leaning outward.
4. **Report non-active hazards to 311 or local building enforcement.**
   - If the material fell previously but is not currently shedding, call your city’s non-emergency line (311) or the Department of Buildings / Code Enforcement.
   - Request an "emergency facade inspection" for a hazardous exterior wall.
5. **Provide specific sensory details to the dispatcher.** Note the exact address, which side of the building (e.g., North side, above the main entrance), and the material involved.
   - Describe if you see "visible cracks," "bulging masonry," or "exposed underlying structure."
6. **Isolate the interior area if you are inside.** Stay away from windows and balconies directly beneath or adjacent to the shedding facade.
   - Close blinds or curtains to provide a minimal barrier against potential glass breakage if a large object falls.
7. **Notify the property owner or management after the area is secured.** Inform the landlord, Homeowners Association (HOA), or facility manager so they can initiate a professional engineering assessment.
8. **Note the time and conditions from a safe distance.** Observe if there was a specific trigger, such as high winds, heavy rain, or nearby construction vibration, to help inspectors identify the cause.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the structural cause of the failure or the extent of the repairs required.
- You do not need to clean up the fallen stone or brick until an inspector or contractor declares the area safe.
- You do not need to negotiate lease terms or liability while the area is still hazardous.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of urgency or alarm when seeing a building "shed" its exterior. Small pieces of masonry can indicate significant underlying pressure or moisture damage. Taking immediate steps to create distance and alert authorities is the most effective way to prevent injury.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and reporting for falling exterior materials. It is not a substitute for a professional structural or facade engineering inspection (such as FISP/Local Law 11 in NYC or similar high-rise safety programs).

## Important note
Building facades are complex systems. Only licensed professionals should assess the stability of masonry or stucco. If you are a tenant, your local municipality's building department has the authority to issue "unsafe" orders and mandate protective sidewalk sheds.
