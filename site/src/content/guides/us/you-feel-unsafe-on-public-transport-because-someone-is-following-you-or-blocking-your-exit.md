# What to do if… you feel unsafe on public transport because someone is following you or blocking your exit (USA)

## Short answer
Create distance without confronting them, and move to where other riders, the operator, or transit staff can see you. If you feel in immediate danger or a crime is in progress, call 911 and use the transit system’s emergency intercom, help point, call box, or staff channel if it is safe and available.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t argue, threaten, shame, or try to prove what they are doing.
- Don’t move into an emptier car, stairwell, platform end, parking area, or isolated stop to avoid embarrassment.
- Don’t force your way through someone who is deliberately blocking you unless you need to escape immediate physical danger.
- Don’t follow them after you exit.
- Don’t share your name, address, workplace, planned stop, or where you are going.
- Don’t pull an emergency brake, force doors, enter tracks, or leave a vehicle between stops unless transit staff or emergency responders direct you, or there is an immediate life-threatening danger.
- Don’t stay on board just because it is your planned route or stop.

## What to do now
1. **Move toward visibility.**  
   Stand up or shift seats without explaining. Move toward a group of riders, the bus operator, a staffed booth, a platform help point, or a brighter busier area. Keep your phone, bag, or a seat back between you and the person if that helps you create space.

2. **Change the pattern without confronting them.**  
   Let other riders pass between you and them. Switch sides of the aisle, move nearer the door before the next stop, or stand where you can step out with other people. If you are on a train, move to another car only where the system allows it and it can be done safely; otherwise, wait for the next stop and switch cars there.

3. **Use the next busy stop as a reset.**  
   Get off at the next staffed, well-lit, or busier stop if that feels safer than staying on board. Stay near other people, a booth, a shop, a help point, or a visible employee while you decide your next move. You do not have to continue to your planned stop.

4. **If they block your exit, use another exit or leave with others.**  
   Step sideways, choose a different door, or let a group leave first and move with them. If you believe you are being physically prevented from leaving, threatened, touched, or followed off the vehicle, treat it as an emergency and call 911 as soon as you safely can.

5. **Get help through the closest safe channel.**  
   On a bus, move near the operator and say clearly, “I need help. That person is blocking me,” or “Please call police.” On a train or at a station, use an emergency intercom, help point, call box, staffed booth, or transit police contact option if available. If your immediate safety is threatened, call 911.

6. **Make yourself visibly connected.**  
   Call or message someone and say your route, vehicle, stop, and what is happening. You can say out loud, “I’m getting off at the next busy stop and staying near staff.” Share your live location if that is easy and safe.

7. **When you are in a safer place, record only the useful basics.**  
   Note the time, route or line, direction of travel, stop or station, vehicle or car number, and a brief description of what happened. Report it through the local transit agency’s safety, police, customer service, app, or online reporting option when you are ready.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a police report or formal complaint.
- You do not need to prove the person’s intent before moving away or asking for help.
- You do not need to keep riding to avoid inconvenience.
- You do not need to post about it, confront them later, or investigate who they are.
- You can decide later whether to change routes, ask someone to meet you, or seek more support.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to act when someone repeatedly closes distance, shadows your movements, or blocks the way out. You are allowed to move, exit early, ask for help, and trust a safety signal before anything becomes clearly violent.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes. If this involved threats, stalking, harassment, physical contact, discrimination, sexual misconduct, or an assault, later options may include transit police, local police, victim support, workplace or school support, or legal advice, but you do not have to decide any of that while you are still unsafe or shaken.

## Important note
This guide provides general information for immediate safety steps. It is not legal, medical, mental health, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe you or someone else is in immediate danger, prioritize getting to a safer place and contacting emergency services.
