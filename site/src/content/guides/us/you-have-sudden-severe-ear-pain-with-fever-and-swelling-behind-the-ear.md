# What to do if… you have sudden severe ear pain with fever and swelling behind the ear (USA)

## Short answer
Go to the nearest **Emergency Room immediately** or call **911**. Swelling behind the ear accompanied by fever and severe pain is a red-flag sign of mastoiditis, a serious bone infection that requires urgent medical intervention.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for a primary care appointment or call a clinic that cannot see you immediately.
- Do not put anything into the ear canal, including cotton swabs, fingers, or objects meant for "draining."
- Do not use over-the-counter ear drops unless specifically instructed by a clinician today.
- Do not take leftover antibiotics or someone else's prescription.
- Do not apply direct heat or ice to the swollen area behind the ear.
- Do not exceed the daily dosage limits for pain relievers listed on the product packaging.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 immediately if you observe emergency neurological or systemic signs.**
   - These include facial drooping or weakness on one side, a stiff neck, a severe "worst ever" headache, confusion, extreme drowsiness, or a seizure.
   - Call 911 if the person is unable to walk, has a fever over 103°F (39.4°C), or is vomiting uncontrollably.
2. **Go to an Emergency Room now if you have swelling, redness, or tenderness behind the ear.**
   - **Tell the triage staff immediately:** "I have severe ear pain, fever, and swelling behind the ear."
   - Check if the ear itself appears to be pushed forward or sticking out more than the other side.
3. **Bring a record of recent symptoms and medications.**
   - Note the exact time and dose of any fever reducers (acetaminophen or ibuprofen) taken.
   - Include details of any recent ear infections, even if they seemed to be improving.
4. **Isolate the ear from water and irritants.**
   - Do not allow shower water to enter the ear canal.
   - If there is drainage, gently wipe only the outer part of the ear with a clean cloth; do not plug the ear canal.
5. **Arrange for someone else to drive you if possible.**
   - Severe ear infections can cause sudden dizziness, vertigo, or loss of balance that makes driving unsafe.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the specific cause of the infection (e.g., bacterial vs. viral) before seeking care.
- You do not need to gather insurance cards or identification if it delays your departure for the ER; these can be provided later.
- You do not need to research long-term hearing impacts or surgical procedures right now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed or highly anxious when facing sudden, severe pain and swelling. Seeking professional emergency evaluation is the single most effective way to manage this risk and begin the recovery process. Medical teams are trained to stabilize these specific types of infections quickly.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency identification only. It does not replace a clinical diagnosis, imaging, or the specialized care provided by an Ear, Nose, and Throat (ENT) specialist or emergency physician.

## Important note
Severe ear pain with fever and post-auricular (behind the ear) swelling is a medical emergency in the USA. These symptoms may indicate mastoiditis, which can lead to hearing loss or meningitis if not treated immediately with professional care.
