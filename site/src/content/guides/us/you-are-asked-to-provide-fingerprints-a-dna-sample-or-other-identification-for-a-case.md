# What to do if… you are asked to provide fingerprints, a DNA sample, or other identification for a case (USA)

## Short answer
Do not provide a voluntary sample or consent to identification procedures without a lawyer; ask if you are free to leave and request to see a warrant or court order if the officer claims the collection is mandatory.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign any consent forms or "waivers of rights" before a lawyer reviews them.
- Don’t agree to provide an "elimination sample" just to "clear your name" without a court order.
- Don’t physically resist the collection if an officer states they have legal authority; state your lack of consent clearly and let a lawyer challenge the evidence later.
- Don’t leave behind items like water bottles, tissues, or cigarette butts in an interview room, as these may be collected as "abandoned" DNA.
- Don’t answer any questions about the case, your whereabouts, or other people while the identification process is occurring.

## What to do now
1. **Determine if you are currently being detained or are free to leave.** **Ask the officer: "Am I free to go?"** 
   - If they say yes, leave the area immediately without providing any samples or identification.
   - If they say no, ask: "What is the legal basis for my detention?"
2. **Clarify whether the request is voluntary or mandatory.** **Ask: "Are you requesting this voluntarily, or do you have a warrant, court order, or specific legal authority to compel this?"**
   - Note the officer’s exact response.
   - If they claim to have a warrant, ask to see a physical or digital copy and check for your correct name and the specific type of sample authorized.
3. **State your refusal to consent clearly and for the record.** **Use the phrase: "I do not consent to this search or to providing any biological samples or fingerprints voluntarily."**
   - Repeat this if they continue to ask.
   - If you are being booked following an arrest, say: "I will comply with the booking process because you say it is required, but I do not consent to the collection or storage of my DNA."
4. **Invoke your right to legal counsel immediately.** **Say: "I want to speak with a lawyer and I am going to remain silent until they are present."**
   - Once you have said this, stop talking entirely except to provide basic booking information (name, address, date of birth) if required by your state’s "stop and identify" laws.
5. **Request and record identifying information about the request.** **Ask for the case number, the name of the requesting agency, and the officer’s badge number.**
   - Write this down or commit it to memory as soon as you are able.
6. **Verify the scope of the biometrics being requested.** **Ask exactly what is being taken, such as fingerprints, buccal (cheek) swabs, hair samples, facial imagery, or voice prints.**
   - Under 2026 federal guidelines, biometrics may include expanded modalities like voice or iris scans; clarify which are being demanded.
7. **Ask about the storage and expungement policy if the sample is taken.** **Ask: "Will this sample be entered into CODIS or a state database, and what is the process for record destruction if no charges are filed?"**
   - Note that for federal records, you may need a certified court order of acquittal or dismissal to trigger the FBI expungement process.
8. **Request a lawyer specifically trained in immigration if you are a non-citizen.** **If immigration agents (ICE/CBP) are involved, do not sign any documents (such as Form I-214 or I-215) without an attorney.**
   - Federal law requires DNA collection for many non-U.S. persons in detention; state clearly that you are complying under protest and request an immigration attorney.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove your innocence or provide an alibi during the identification process.
- You do not need to research state-specific DNA laws in the moment; your lawyer will handle the jurisdictional nuances.
- You do not need to decide whether to sue for civil rights violations immediately.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel intimidated when an official demands a DNA sample or fingerprints. You are not "acting guilty" by invoking your constitutional rights or asking for a lawyer. These protections exist specifically to ensure that the government follows proper legal procedures before collecting your biological data.

## Scope note
This guide provides immediate stabilization and harm-reduction steps for the moment of request. It does not cover the long-term legal defense or the technical process of filing for expungement after a case is closed.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and is not legal advice. DNA collection laws vary significantly between states and federal jurisdictions. Always consult with a qualified criminal defense or civil rights attorney regarding your specific case.
