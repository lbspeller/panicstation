# What to do if… a hire company offers a different vehicle type than you booked and you do not feel confident driving it (USA)

## Short answer
State clearly that you cannot safely operate the substitute vehicle and refuse to sign the rental agreement until a safe, equivalent vehicle is provided. If no safe alternative is available, demand a cancellation without a "no-show" or "early cancellation" penalty.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign the rental agreement or "click to accept" on the digital pad before seeing the actual vehicle.
- Don’t drive the vehicle off the lot or out of the parking bay if you feel unsafe.
- Don’t accept a "free upgrade" to a significantly larger vehicle (e.g., a 15-passenger van or large SUV) if you are not experienced with its dimensions or blind spots.
- Don’t assume you can "get used to it" while navigating airport traffic or unfamiliar city streets.
- Don’t leave the counter without a written or digital receipt of the cancellation if you are forced to walk away.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific safety concern immediately.** **Determine if the issue is vehicle size, visibility, fuel type (EV vs. gas), or unfamiliar technology/controls.**
2. **State your safety boundary to the agent using clear, non-negotiable language.** **Say: "I am not comfortable or confident operating this vehicle type, and for safety reasons, I cannot accept it."**
3. **Request a vehicle from the original class you reserved.** **Ask the agent to check the "ready" line, the cleaning bay, or the "returns" area for the specific category you booked.**
4. **Ask for a "downgrade" if no equivalent is available.** **If they only have larger "upgrades" available, ask for a smaller vehicle class (e.g., an Economy car if you booked a Midsize) and ensure the rate is adjusted downward.**
5. **Request a location transfer if the lot is empty.** **Ask the agent to call nearby branch locations (including off-airport sites) to see if they can ferry a vehicle of the correct class to your location.**
6. **Wait for a return if time permits.** **Ask for a specific "ETR" (Estimated Time of Return) for a vehicle in your class and stay at the counter or in the designated waiting area until it is processed.**
7. **Refuse the vehicle during the "Pre-Trip Inspection" if the counter agent was unhelpful.** **If you are already at the car and realize you cannot see over the hood or handle the controls, return to the "Exit Booth" or counter immediately and state the vehicle is "unfit for the driver."**
8. **Document the unavailability of your reserved class.** **Take a photo of the "Class" listed on your reservation and the "Class" offered on the screen or paper agreement to use as evidence for later disputes.**
9. **Contact the third-party booking site immediately if you did not book directly.** **If you used an aggregator (e.g., Expedia, Priceline), call their customer support from the counter so they can verify the rental company's failure to provide the reserved class.**
10. **Demand a "Full Waiver of Fees" if you must cancel.** **If no safe vehicle is provided, ensure the agent provides a document showing the reservation was cancelled due to "Vehicle Unavailability" rather than a "Customer No-Show."**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a different rental company immediately; you can use a ride-share service to leave the airport first.
- You do not need to file a formal complaint with the Corporate Head Office while standing at the counter.
- You do not need to initiate a credit card chargeback until the final charge appears on your statement.

## Important reassurance
It is a responsible safety decision to refuse a vehicle that exceeds your comfort level or skill set. Rental companies frequently overbook specific classes, and their "free upgrade" policy is often a logistics convenience for them that does not account for individual driver safety or confidence.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling a vehicle substitution at a rental counter in the USA. It focuses on immediate safety and contract refusal. It does not cover long-term legal litigation or mechanical breakdown procedures.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Rental terms are governed by individual contracts and state consumer protection laws, such as the California CARS Act or New York's FAIR Business Practices Act. If you are charged for a service not provided, follow your credit card issuer's formal dispute process within 60 days.
