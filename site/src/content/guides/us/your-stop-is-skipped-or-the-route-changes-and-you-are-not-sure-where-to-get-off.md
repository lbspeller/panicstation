# What to do if… your stop is skipped or the route changes and you are not sure where to get off (USA)

## Short answer
Stay on until the next normal stop or station that feels safe, then ask the operator, conductor, station staff, or the transit agency for one clear next step. Do not force an exit, step onto tracks or roadway, or get off somewhere isolated just because you feel rushed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force doors, step onto tracks, walk along a roadway shoulder, or exit anywhere other than a normal stop or station.
- Do not use emergency handles, alarms, or intercoms unless there is immediate danger, fire, a serious medical issue, or another true emergency.
- Do not get off alone into an unfamiliar or poorly lit area while panicked if you can safely stay on to a busier stop or station.
- Do not argue with the operator while the vehicle is moving or while they are driving.
- Do not rely only on the first phone route shown if it sends you through isolated streets, long walks, or closed stations.

## What to do now
1. **Choose the next safe stopping point.**  
   Plan to exit at the next normal stop or station that looks public, lit, and usable. If the next stop looks isolated or unsafe, it may be better to stay on to a busier station, transit center, or staffed place.

2. **Mark where you are.**  
   Check the onboard display, announcement, station sign, or map for the next stop name. If your phone works, pin your current location or take a screenshot of the map so you can show it to staff.

3. **Ask for one clear instruction.**  
   Say: “My stop was skipped or the route changed. What stop should I get off at to reach [your stop] safely?”  
   - On a bus, ask when the bus is stopped or when asking will not distract the driver.
   - On a train, subway, or commuter rail service, ask the conductor, station staff, or use a non-emergency help point if available.
   - If there is no staff nearby, use the transit agency’s official app, website, text line, phone line, or station help point shown on signs.

4. **If this is an Amtrak or other reserved intercity trip, speak to staff before improvising.**  
   Tell the conductor or onboard staff what happened and ask where you should detrain. Do not leave early, rebook, or buy a replacement ticket in a panic unless staff or the official service channel tells you that is the best next step.

5. **Pause in the safest public place available.**  
   If you get off and feel overwhelmed, stay near other riders, lighting, cameras, a staffed booth, an open business, or a clearly marked help point while you work out the next move.

6. **If you feel unsafe right now, move toward help.**  
   Move closer to other people or staff. Call 911 if you are in immediate danger, being threatened, or need urgent medical help.

7. **Save the details without delaying safety.**  
   When you are stationary and safe, note the date, time, route or line, direction, stop or station name, vehicle or car number if visible, and what happened. Screenshot service alerts, route changes, ticket messages, or map locations if you can do that safely.

8. **Contact the transit agency once you are safe and still.**  
   Use the official number, app, website, station help point, or customer service route shown on signs. Ask for the safest way back to your intended stop and whether your fare, transfer, or ticket needs any adjustment because of the disruption.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to file a complaint.
- You do not need to solve the whole journey while panicked.
- You do not need to argue about fares, refunds, or blame at the vehicle door.
- Your immediate sequence is: reach a safe stop, confirm where you are, ask for one clear next step, then move.

## Important reassurance
Skipped stops, detours, short turns, missed announcements, and unclear displays can disorient anyone, especially at night or in an unfamiliar area. Slowing down and using a safe stop or staffed place as a reset point is a sensible response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. After you are safe and oriented, later decisions about onward travel, complaints, refunds, accessibility problems, or disrupted reservations may need help from the transit agency, ticket seller, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Transit rules, fare rules, staffing, and emergency systems vary by agency and ticket type. If you are in immediate danger or need urgent help in the USA, call 911.
