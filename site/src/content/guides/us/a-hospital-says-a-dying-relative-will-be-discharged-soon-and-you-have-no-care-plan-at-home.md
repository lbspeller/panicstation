# What to do if… a hospital says a dying relative will be discharged soon and you have no care plan at home (USA)

## Short answer
Request a meeting with the **case manager** and **palliative care** immediately to state that discharge is unsafe due to a lack of caregiver coverage or equipment. If the patient has Medicare, contact the **BFCC-QIO** for your state by midnight on the day of discharge to file an expedited appeal.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign the discharge papers or "Important Message from Medicare" (IM) follow-up if you believe the discharge is unsafe until you have spoken to the case manager.
- Do not take the patient home if symptom-management medications, medical equipment, and an after-hours clinician contact are not confirmed.
- Do not assume home health or hospice services will begin automatically upon arrival home.
- Do not overstate your physical ability to provide care (such as lifting, toileting, or 24-hour monitoring) if you cannot safely perform these tasks.
- Do not wait for the transport vehicle to arrive before raising safety or care-gap concerns.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the discharge planning team and request a palliative care consult.**  
   **Ask the bedside nurse to page the case manager or social worker and the palliative care team immediately.** Use the specific phrase: "We are documenting a safety concern: there is no feasible care plan or caregiver coverage at home."

2. **Request a formal discharge planning meeting to review specifics.**  
   **Ask for a written discharge plan that includes the names and direct phone numbers of the agencies providing care.** Require specific answers to the following:  
   - The medical reason the patient no longer requires acute hospital care.  
   - The exact date and time home health, hospice, or equipment deliveries will arrive.  
   - The name of the agency responsible for symptom management.  
   - The 24-hour "on-call" phone number for medical emergencies at home.

3. **Verify medication and equipment readiness.**  
   **Ensure that all prescriptions for pain and symptom management are sent to a pharmacy that is open and has the stock ready for pickup before discharge.** Confirm that essential durable medical equipment (DME), such as a hospital bed, oxygen, or a commode, has a confirmed delivery window at the home.

4. **Request an urgent hospice evaluation if applicable.**  
   **Ask the medical team for a "hospice evaluation" today to determine if the patient is eligible for the Medicare Hospice Benefit.** If the patient is eligible, ask if they can be transferred directly to a hospice facility or if home hospice can be initiated with a nurse visit on the same day as discharge.

5. **File an expedited discharge appeal through the BFCC-QIO.**  
   **Find the "Important Message from Medicare" (IM) notice provided by the hospital and call the Beneficiary and Family Centered Care Quality Improvement Organization (BFCC-QIO) listed on the form.** You must call by midnight on the scheduled discharge day. Once you appeal, the hospital generally cannot discharge the patient until the QIO makes a decision, which typically takes 24 to 48 hours.

6. **State your physical and logistical limitations clearly.**  
   **Provide the case manager with a "caregiver capacity" statement in writing.** Explicitly list tasks you cannot perform, such as 24-hour supervision, administering injections, or lifting the patient, and request that these limitations be documented in the patient’s medical record.

7. **Escalate concerns within the hospital hierarchy.**  
   **Ask to speak with the Charge Nurse, then the Nurse Manager, and finally the Patient Advocate or Patient Relations department.** State that you are reporting an "unsafe discharge" and require a resolution before the patient leaves the facility.

## What can wait
- You do not need to finalize long-term financial arrangements or permanent nursing home placement today.
- You do not need to coordinate funeral arrangements or long-term estate matters while the immediate discharge is in crisis.
- You do not need to solve the entire trajectory of the illness; focus only on the safety of the next 48 to 72 hours.

## Important reassurance
Feeling overwhelmed or pressured by hospital timelines is a standard reaction to the discharge process. Stating that you cannot provide safe care at home is a responsible safety observation, not a failure of family duty. Hospitals are legally required to ensure a safe transition.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization of a discharge crisis in a hospital setting. It does not cover long-term legal appeals, insurance litigation, or medical diagnosis.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not legal or medical advice. Medicare and insurance regulations are subject to change; always refer to the specific contact information provided on your hospital notices (IM or DND) for current appeal deadlines and procedures in your state.
