# What to do if… you spot a major printing error on your passport details page shortly before travel (USA)

## Short answer
Do not alter the passport or hope the mismatch will be ignored. Check the official U.S. passport correction page now, and if your international travel is close, use the urgent passport agency appointment route before mailing anything.

## Do not do these things
- Do not write on, alter, laminate, tape, trim, or “repair” the passport.
- Do not assume an airline, border officer, or destination authority will accept a passport whose key details do not match your ticket or destination visa or entry authorization.
- Do not mail the passport for correction if your travel is very close until you have checked whether the urgent appointment route fits your timing.
- Do not pay anyone who claims they can sell you a passport agency appointment.
- Do not book duplicate appointments or use someone else’s appointment.
- Do not change ticket or visa details in a rush unless you know which passport details you will actually travel with.

## What to do now
1. Compare the passport details page against your booking, birth certificate or other identity record, and any destination visa or entry authorization. Treat wrong name spelling, date of birth, place of birth, sex marker, passport number, nationality, or photo as a major mismatch.
2. Make a simple secure note of the mismatch: what is wrong, what it should say, the passport issue date, your travel date, and any booking reference. Avoid sending passport images through ordinary email unless you have no safer way to access them.
3. Open the official U.S. Department of State correction instructions for a data or printing error. The correction page currently directs valid passport data or printing error corrections to Form DS-5504 by mail, with the current passport, a passport photo, and evidence of the error.
4. If you are travelling internationally within 14 calendar days, or need a foreign visa within 28 calendar days, try for a passport agency or center appointment. If you have not already applied, use the online appointment system; if you already have an application in process, call the passport line listed on the official appointment page.
5. Put a grab-and-go packet together now:
   - the incorrect passport book, and passport card if affected
   - printed proof of international travel, with a phone copy as backup
   - appointment confirmation if you get one
   - government photo ID for building entry
   - the completed form for your situation, usually DS-5504 for a data or printing error
   - evidence showing the correct details, such as a birth certificate or other document named in the correction instructions
   - one passport photo
   - a payment method in case an urgent service or delivery fee applies
6. If the appointment system offers only distant locations, decide whether you can safely travel there before changing the trip. Appointment availability is not guaranteed.
7. Once you have a correction path or appointment outcome, contact the airline and confirm the exact identity details they need for check-in and boarding on your route.

## What can wait
- Complaints, refunds, reimbursement requests, or blame.
- Updating frequent flyer, trusted traveler, or travel profile details.
- Changing hotels, tours, or non-essential plans until you know whether you can get corrected travel documents in time.
- Deciding whether to cancel the whole trip until you have checked the official urgent route.

## Important reassurance
This is stressful, but it is a known document problem with an official correction process and an urgent appointment pathway for close international travel. The useful next move is to keep the passport unaltered, use only official instructions, and avoid creating a second mismatch.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours only. Later decisions about visas, entry permissions, refunds, insurance, or trip changes may need help from the airline, the destination authority, a travel insurer, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. U.S. passport requirements, forms, fees, processing times, and appointment availability can change, so follow the U.S. Department of State instructions for your exact case.
