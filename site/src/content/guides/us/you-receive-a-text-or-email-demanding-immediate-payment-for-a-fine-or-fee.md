# What to do if… you receive a text or email demanding immediate payment for a fine or fee (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the message as unverified. Do not click, reply, call the number in the message, or pay through its link; check any real fine or fee through an official route you find yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links, scan QR codes, or open attachments from the message.
- Do not call numbers, reply, or use payment buttons inside the message.
- Do not pay with gift cards, crypto, wire transfers, payment apps, or other rushed methods because the message threatens enforcement.
- Do not share your Social Security number, driver’s license number, passwords, one-time codes, card details, or bank details.
- Do not install apps, browser extensions, remote-access tools, or verification tools the message tells you to download.
- Do not keep arguing with the sender to clear it up; back-and-forth can create more pressure and more chances to share information.
- Do not delete the message before you have saved what you need and reported it.

## What to do now
1. **Pause before paying.** Put the phone down for a moment and decide that you will not pay from the message itself.
2. **Save the details without interacting.** Screenshot the message, sender details, link preview if visible, and date and time. Do not tap the link to get more information.
3. **Check the claim through a route you control.**
   - If it claims **unpaid tolls**, use the toll account, app, or website you already know is real, or look up the state toll agency separately and contact it there.
   - If it claims a **traffic ticket, license issue, motor vehicle agency fee, or court fee**, look up the official state or local government website yourself and contact the listed agency or court clerk.
   - If it gives a **case number, citation number, badge number, or warrant warning**, treat those details as untrusted until an official agency or court confirms them through contact information you found independently.
4. **Check the web address carefully before entering anything.** Official federal websites usually end in .gov or .mil, but state and local agencies vary. Do not trust a site just because it uses official-looking logos, urgent wording, or a familiar agency name.
5. **If you clicked but did not enter anything, stop there.** Close the page, do not download anything, and report the message. If your phone prompts you about a downloaded file, app, profile, or permission, do not approve it.
6. **If you entered card, bank, or payment details, contact the financial provider now.** Use the number on the back of your card, the provider’s official app, or a statement. Ask what can be blocked, disputed, replaced, or monitored.
7. **If you paid with a payment app, wire transfer, gift card, or crypto, contact that provider too.** Ask whether the payment can be stopped, reversed, flagged, or documented. Do not pay anyone who says they can recover the money for an upfront fee.
8. **If you shared your Social Security number, driver’s license details, or other identity information, reduce identity-theft risk.** Consider placing a free credit freeze with Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion, and use IdentityTheft.gov for a recovery plan if identity details may have been misused.
9. **Secure the account most likely to unlock others.** Change your email password first if you entered it or used it on the suspicious page. Use a new password and turn on two-factor authentication if available.
10. **Report the message.**
    - Use your phone’s report junk or report spam option if available.
    - Copy the message and forward it to 7726 (SPAM) to report it to your wireless provider.
    - Report it to the Federal Trade Commission.
    - If you lost money, entered sensitive information, or the scam involved online payment or a link, consider filing a report with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center.
11. **Block and delete after saving and reporting.** Once the details are saved and reported, block the sender and delete the message so you do not keep rereading it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether the alleged fine is real; first confirm it through an official route.
- You do not need to pay today just because a text says there is a deadline.
- You do not need to keep searching for similar messages or reading the threat repeatedly.
- A full password audit, device check, and longer monitoring can wait until your bank, card, and identity-risk steps are started.
- You do not need to explain yourself to the sender.

## Important reassurance
Scam messages are designed to feel official, urgent, and frightening, especially when they mention tolls, tickets, licenses, courts, or enforcement. Pausing and checking through official contact details is a protective step, not a delay. Even if a real fee exists, paying through a link in an unexpected message is not the safest way to handle it.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from your bank or card issuer, the relevant agency or court, an identity-theft support service, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, law-enforcement, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Processes vary by state, agency, provider, and situation. When in doubt, do not pay from the message, verify through official contact details you find independently, and contact your bank or card issuer quickly if payment or sensitive information was involved.
