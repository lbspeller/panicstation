# What to do if… you have a sudden severe headache that is unusual for you (USA)

## Short answer
**Call 911 immediately if you experience a headache that peaks in intensity within 60 seconds (a "thunderclap" headache) or any severe headache accompanied by neurological symptoms like weakness, confusion, or vision changes.** Do not drive yourself to the hospital.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if the pain subsides or "sleep it off" if the onset was sudden and severe.
- Do not drive yourself to the emergency room; wait for an ambulance or have someone else drive.
- Do not eat or drink anything, as you may require emergency imaging or surgery.
- Do not take aspirin or other blood-thinning painkillers until a doctor has ruled out a brain bleed.
- Do not ignore symptoms just because they start to improve or "come and go."

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 immediately if the headache is the "worst of your life" or if you notice any "red flag" symptoms.** 
   - These include facial drooping, one-sided weakness, slurred speech, or sudden confusion.
   - Other triggers for 911 include a stiff neck with fever, seizure, fainting, or sudden vision loss.
2. **Sit or lie down in a safe, quiet place while waiting for help.** 
   - Avoid standing or walking, especially if you feel dizzy or unbalanced.
   - If you are with someone, ask them to stay with you and monitor your alertness.
3. **Be prepared to provide a "F.A.S.T." timeline to emergency responders.** 
   - Note exactly what time the pain started and how quickly it reached its maximum severity.
   - Mention if the headache followed a physical injury, intense exertion, or sexual activity.
4. **Disclose your pregnancy or postpartum status immediately.** 
   - If you are currently pregnant or have given birth within the last 6 weeks, you are at high risk for pregnancy-related stroke or preeclampsia.
   - Mention if you have a history of high blood pressure.
5. **List all medications you are currently taking, especially blood thinners.** 
   - Anticoagulants (like warfarin, apixaban, or rivaroxaban) increase the risk of internal bleeding.
   - If possible, keep the medication bottles nearby or have a photo of the labels ready.
6. **Lie on your side if you feel nauseated or need to vomit.** 
   - This prevents choking (aspiration) if your level of consciousness changes.

## What can wait
- Finding your insurance card or identification if it is not immediately reachable.
- Notifying your employer or canceling non-essential appointments.
- Researching your symptoms online or trying to determine if it is "just a migraine."
- Taking your blood pressure at home if it causes any delay in calling for help.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel frightened when a severe physical symptom happens without warning. Emergency departments are equipped to handle these exact "worst-case" scenarios quickly. Seeking help now is the most effective way to protect your long-term health, regardless of what the final diagnosis turns out to be.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency escalation only. It does not replace clinical diagnosis or long-term management of chronic headache disorders.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization purposes and is not a substitute for professional medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Always seek the advice of a physician or other qualified health provider with any questions you may have regarding a medical condition. If you think you have a medical emergency, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room immediately.
