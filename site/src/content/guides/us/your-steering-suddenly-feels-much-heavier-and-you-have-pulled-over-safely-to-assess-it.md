# What to do if… your steering suddenly feels much heavier and you have pulled over safely to assess it (USA)

## Short answer
Assume steering assist may have failed. Stay parked with hazards on, and arrange roadside help or towing instead of driving to test it unless emergency responders direct you.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t merge back into traffic if steering is still heavy or a steering assist warning is on.
- Don’t keep forcing the steering wheel hard against full lock while stopped.
- Don’t reach near belts, fans, pulleys, or hot engine parts.
- Don’t open hot fluid caps.
- Don’t attempt a roadside repair that puts you in traffic, under the car, or distracted.
- Don’t let pressure from yourself or anyone else push you into driving if the steering still feels changed.

## What to do now
1. **Make your stopped position safer.** Keep hazards on. If you can safely roll farther away from moving traffic into a parking lot, exit, wide shoulder, or other safer place, do that slowly and stop again.

2. **Decide whether staying inside or getting out is safer.**
   - If you are on a shoulder or other stopped area and it seems safer to remain inside: stay in the vehicle, seat belt on, hazards on.
   - If you are stuck in an active travel lane, on a very narrow shoulder, around a curve, or you feel at risk of being struck: call 911 and say you are disabled roadside.
   - If there is fire, smoke, or another immediate hazard: exit only if you can do that without stepping into traffic, then move well away from the roadway.

3. **Check the dashboard before touching anything.** Look for a steering wheel, EPS, power steering, battery, or overheating warning. Note any message such as “steering assist reduced” or “service power steering.”

4. **Only if it is safe to step out, do a quick outside check.**
   - Look for a flat or damaged tire, especially a front tire.
   - Look for fresh fluid under the front of the car.
   - Do not touch fluid, belts, fans, pulleys, or hot parts. Just note what you see.

5. **Try one stationary restart check only if the vehicle is safely stopped.** Put the vehicle in Park if automatic, or neutral if manual. Set the parking brake, switch the engine off, wait a short moment, and restart. If the warning returns, the steering stays heavy, or you are unsure, stop there.

6. **Choose towing over driving if anything still feels wrong.** If steering remains heavy, comes and goes, feels unsafe, or any steering assist warning remains, do not drive unless emergency responders direct you. Arrange roadside assistance or a tow and say: “sudden heavy steering, possible loss of power steering assist.” Mention any warning lights, tire damage, fluid leak, or overheating warning.

7. **Wait in the safest place available.** Keep hazards on, keep your phone charged, stay aware of approaching traffic, and avoid standing beside the vehicle near moving traffic.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the cause is hydraulic fluid, a belt, an electric steering motor, a tire, a fuse, software, or a battery issue while you are roadside.
- You do not need to choose a repair shop, approve major work, or compare prices before you are safely off the road.
- You do not need to research recalls or technical bulletins in the moment.

## Important reassurance
Sudden heavy steering is alarming, and pulling over was the right first move. Treating it as a tow situation is a calm, reasonable way to reduce risk and buy time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Diagnosis, repair, recall checks, insurance questions, and later decisions may need help from a qualified technician, roadside provider, insurer, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional mechanical, legal, emergency, financial, medical, or safety advice. If you feel unsafe where you are, call emergency services.
