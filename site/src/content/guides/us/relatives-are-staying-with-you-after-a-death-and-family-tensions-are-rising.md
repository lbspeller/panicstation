# What to do if… relatives are staying with you after a death and family tensions are rising (USA)

## Short answer
Pause the argument and get to a safer, quieter space. Keep your phone, keys, medication, and ID with you; call 911 if anyone is in immediate danger or needs immediate police, fire department, or ambulance assistance.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep arguing if voices are rising. Step away if you can do so safely.
- Do not divide, give away, or remove the deceased person’s belongings while people are upset.
- Do not try to settle funeral choices in the middle of an argument. Ask the funeral home which choices need attention soon.
- Do not handle or move firearms during a conflict. Move away and call 911 if there is immediate danger.
- Do not pressure anyone to settle inheritance questions or old family disputes now.
- Do not use alcohol while tensions are rising.
- Do not keep hosting people in your home if you feel unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Move to a separate room or another safe place if you can. Take your phone with you. If someone is threatening you, blocking you from leaving, or becoming violent, call 911.
2. **Set one temporary boundary.** Say: “We are not deciding anything about money or belongings while people are upset. We will write down concerns and revisit them later.”
3. **Separate urgent funeral questions from decisions that can wait.** Ask the funeral home which arrangement questions need attention soon and which can wait.
4. **Keep your personal essentials with you.** Take your own phone, keys, medication, wallet, and ID. Do not move shared belongings or estate-related papers during the argument.
5. **Reduce friction in the house.** Use separate spaces, quiet hours, and short breaks. Ask people not to drink alcohol while tempers are high.
6. **Choose one informal logistics contact if everyone agrees.** That person can keep a written list of questions and coordinate calls. This does not give them legal authority over the estate or the family.
7. **Use official information once the immediate tension has settled.** USAGov has guidance for reporting a death. Its courts page can help you find official state or territory court information about probate for wills and estates.
8. **Use crisis support if needed.** Call, text, or chat 988 if you or someone with you is in a mental health crisis or emotional distress.

## What can wait
- You do not need to divide belongings or settle inheritance questions during an argument.
- You do not need to resolve old family disagreements now.
- You do not need to decide how long everyone will stay while tensions are high.
- You do not need to work through the full list of agency notifications, tax questions, or probate steps during an argument. Once things are calmer, check official guidance promptly because some tasks may need attention soon.
- You do not need to manage everyone’s emotions or provide constant updates.

## Important reassurance
Grief affects people differently. Anger, confusion, anxiety, and fatigue can occur after a death. Creating a pause is not unkind; it gives everyone a chance to slow down before making decisions.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation only. Later decisions about funeral arrangements, the estate, housing, or family conflict may need help from a funeral home, mediator, attorney, or the appropriate court.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, mental health, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Call 911 for immediate emergency assistance. Call, text, or chat 988 for mental health crisis support.
