# What to do if… an officer asks for your name and address and you are unsure what to say (USA)

## Short answer
Ask: “Am I free to leave, or am I being detained?” Whether you must provide your name, address, or identification depends on the state and the situation. Do not guess, lie, or give a fake name or address.

## Do not do these things
- Do not lie or give a fake name or address.
- Do not argue about the law during the encounter.
- Do not make sudden movements or reach into pockets or bags without saying what you are doing.
- Do not volunteer extra details just because you feel pressured to fill the silence.
- Do not physically resist, even if you disagree with what is happening.

## What to do now
1. Ask one narrow question: “Am I free to leave, or am I being detained?”
2. If the officer says you are free to leave, you can calmly leave.
3. If the officer says you are being detained, ask: “Are you requiring me to provide my name?” Then ask: “Are you requiring my address or an identification document too?”
4. Ask: “What information are you requiring me to provide?” Do not lie. If you provide information, keep it accurate and limited. Do not argue or physically resist during the encounter.
5. If you do not want to answer questions about the incident, say: “I am exercising my right to remain silent.” You can repeat that sentence without explaining yourself.
6. If you are the driver during a traffic stop, provide your driver’s license when requested. Be ready to provide registration and proof of insurance as required by your state’s vehicle laws. Do not assume that the same document rules automatically apply to passengers.
7. During a traffic stop, do not physically resist an order to step out of the vehicle. Keep your hands visible and say what you are doing before reaching for anything. A disagreement about an instruction can be addressed later.
8. If you are arrested and asked questions about the incident, say: “I want a lawyer. I am exercising my right to remain silent.” You may still be asked routine identifying questions. If you are unsure what information is being requested, ask for clarification instead of guessing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide during the encounter whether to complain or take legal action.
- You do not need to persuade the officer that your understanding of the law is correct.
- You do not need to explain your full background, plans, or reasons for being in the area unless you later choose to do so after getting advice.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to freeze when an officer asks for personal information. Short, repeatable phrases can help you slow the moment down and avoid accidental over-sharing. A disagreement about what was required can be addressed later.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Identification rules vary by state and by context, including whether the encounter is voluntary, whether you are detained, whether you are driving, whether you are receiving a citation, and whether you are arrested. Later decisions may need help from a lawyer in your state.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. It is not a substitute for advice from a lawyer who can consider the state law and the facts of the encounter.
