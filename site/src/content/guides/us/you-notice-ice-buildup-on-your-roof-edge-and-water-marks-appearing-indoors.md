# What to do if… you notice ice buildup on your roof edge and water marks appearing indoors (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a likely ice-dam leak: keep people away from wet or sagging ceiling areas, avoid electrical hazards, contain the water, and get roof help without climbing onto the roof yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go onto an icy roof or ladder to chip, hammer, or pull at ice.
- Do not stand under a swollen, sagging, or cracking ceiling.
- Do not try to cut, puncture, or open a bulging ceiling yourself.
- Do not use fans, wet/dry vacs, extension cords, or heaters near standing water, wet outlets, wet fixtures, or dripping water.
- Do not pour boiling water, salt, or chemicals onto the roof edge or gutters unless a roofing professional says it is safe for your roof.
- Do not start painting, patching, or replacing drywall before the leak source is controlled and wet materials are drying.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safer.** Move people, pets, and valuables away from the stained, dripping, swollen, or sagging area. Put a bucket or pan under active drips only if you can place it without standing under a dangerous ceiling.
2. **Reduce electrical risk.** If water is near a light fixture, ceiling fan, outlet, baseboard heater, appliance, breaker panel, or visible wiring, keep people away. Turn off the affected circuit only if you can reach the breaker panel without standing in water or touching wet surfaces. If you cannot do that safely, call an electrician or your utility for urgent guidance.
3. **Protect belongings from water.** Move furniture, electronics, rugs, and stored items away from the leak path. Use towels and plastic sheeting for items that cannot be moved.
4. **Look from the ground only.** From a safe spot outside, take photos of the ice buildup at the roof edge, gutters, downspouts, icicles, and any snow on the lower roof. Do not climb to inspect it.
5. **Remove only what you can reach safely from the ground.** If you already have a roof rake and the ground is not slippery, you can pull loose snow from the lower roof edge while standing on the ground. Do not pull on gutters, wires, shingles, or heavy ice.
6. **Call for roof help.** Contact a roofing contractor or ice-dam removal service and say: “There is ice at the eaves and water marks or dripping indoors.” Ask whether they are insured and how they will avoid damaging the roof.
7. **If you rent, notify the landlord or property manager now.** Send photos, describe the active leak and any electrical concern, and ask for emergency repair.
8. **Document the damage.** Take photos or video of indoor stains, drips, wet items, damaged belongings, and the outside ice from the ground. Keep receipts for buckets, tarps, towels, emergency repair, temporary lodging, or cleanup costs.
9. **Escalate if there is immediate danger.** If the ceiling is rapidly sagging, water is pouring through or near electrical equipment, you smell burning, you see sparks, or someone could be injured, leave that area and call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose today whether the cause is insulation, air leaks, attic ventilation, gutter blockage, or roof detailing.
- You do not need to choose permanent prevention upgrades while water is still coming in.
- You do not need to start cosmetic repairs until the roof edge problem is controlled and wet materials are drying.
- You do not need a perfect inventory before acting; quick photos and saved receipts are enough for the first hour.

## Important reassurance
Ice dams can push meltwater under roof edges even when the roof looks mostly normal from outside. The right first move is not to solve the whole roof problem yourself; it is to keep people safe, slow the indoor damage, and bring in help for the icy roof edge.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about roofing, insulation, ventilation, mold cleanup, electrical repairs, insurance, or landlord obligations may need qualified local help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, insurance, electrical, building, medical, or other professional advice. Follow local emergency instructions and use qualified professionals for work at height, electrical hazards, structural concerns, and water-damage repairs.
