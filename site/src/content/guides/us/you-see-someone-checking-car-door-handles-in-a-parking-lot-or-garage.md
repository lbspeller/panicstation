# What to do if… you see someone checking car door handles in a parking lot or garage (USA)

## Short answer
Put distance between you and the person, and do not confront them. If a crime appears to be happening now, someone may be in immediate danger, or you need an immediate response, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not approach, argue with, follow, chase, or try to physically stop the person.
- Do not go to check your own car alone while the person is nearby.
- Do not film from close range or in a way that makes you obvious.
- Do not touch car doors, handles, bags, tools, broken glass, or anything unusual if police or security may attend.
- Do not assume the person is unarmed or that the situation will stay calm.
- Do not post identifiable images or footage online while the situation is unfolding.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer place now.** Go inside a nearby business, to a staffed desk, to a guard station, or near other people. If you are already in your vehicle, lock the doors and leave or reposition only if you can do that safely.
2. **Treat it as urgent if the person is moving car to car.** Pulling on several handles, looking through windows, using tools, forcing entry, acting aggressively, or approaching you are all reasons to stop watching and get help.
3. **Call the right help.**
   - Call 911 if a crime appears to be in progress, anyone may be in danger, or you need an immediate response.
   - If the person has left and there is no immediate danger, use the local police or sheriff non-emergency number from an official city, county, or department website.
   - If you are unsure whether it is an emergency, say what is happening clearly and follow the dispatcher’s instructions.
4. **Give clear details from safety.** Say the exact lot or garage, level, nearest entrance or store, row or bay if known, and what you saw: for example, “pulling on door handles,” “moving from car to car,” or “looking inside windows.”
5. **Describe the person without getting closer.** Give clothing, approximate height and build, direction of travel, and any vehicle involved. Only give a license plate if you can see it safely without approaching.
6. **Tell on-site security or staff.** Notify a garage attendant, mall or store staff, campus security, building front desk, or property management so they can check cameras, patrol, and warn people without putting you in the middle.
7. **If your own car may be involved, do not return alone while the person is nearby.** Stay in a staffed or public area and ask on-site security for an escort, or follow the dispatcher’s instructions before going back.
8. **Use a remote alarm only from safety.** If you have a remote panic button and using it will not draw the person toward you, it may help draw attention; do not approach to see what happens.
9. **After reporting, stop close watching.** Stay aware of your surroundings, keep your phone available, and update dispatch or security only if the person’s location, direction, or behavior changes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove intent or decide whether it is definitely a burglary right now.
- You do not need to confront the person or personally warn every driver.
- Checking for damage, contacting an owner, making a written statement, or dealing with insurance can wait until you are safe and calmer.

## Important reassurance
Feeling shaky or uncertain is normal in a moment like this. Creating distance, getting to a staffed or public place, and reporting concrete details is a useful and responsible response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on local police procedures, property security rules, whether a vehicle owner reports damage or loss, and whether you are asked for a statement.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, or professional advice. If you think a crime is happening now or anyone could be harmed, prioritize safety and call 911.
