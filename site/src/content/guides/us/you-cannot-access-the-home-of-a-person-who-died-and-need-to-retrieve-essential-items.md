# What to do if… you cannot access the home of a person who died and need to retrieve essential items (USA)

## Short answer
Do not force entry or hire a locksmith unless the person arranging entry has confirmed permission or authority. Start with the narrowest safe request: ask the person who controls access what is needed for a supervised visit to retrieve essential items.

## Do not do these things
- Do not break in, climb through a window, or enter after being told not to enter.
- Do not hire a locksmith to open the home unless permission or authority has been confirmed.
- Do not remove valuables, sort belongings, or start clearing the home during an urgent visit.
- Do not change locks, cancel utilities, or dispose of items unless the authorised person has decided what is needed.
- Do not confront relatives, neighbours, tenants, landlords, or building staff at the door.
- Do not assume that being a relative, beneficiary, or executor named in a will automatically gives you immediate access.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether there is an immediate safety problem.**
   - For fire, a suspected gas leak, or immediate danger to a person, call **911**.
   - For an active building problem such as a water leak, contact the landlord, property manager, building emergency line, or the appropriate local service.
   - If a pet may be in danger, contact the landlord or property manager and ask for urgent help. Local animal control or emergency services may also be appropriate.
   - If medication is inside, contact the pharmacy, prescriber, or urgent medical service and ask about a safe replacement. Do not enter without permission or authority.

2. **Contact the person or organisation that controls access.**
   This may be an owner, co-owner, landlord, property manager, or building manager. Explain that the resident died and ask what they require for a limited, supervised visit to retrieve essential items.

3. **Ask who currently has authority to act for the estate.**
   A person named as executor in a will may still need court appointment. If nobody has confirmed authority, use the state-court lookup route to find the appropriate probate court and ask what process applies. The court may issue letters testamentary, letters of administration, or a similarly named document as proof of authority.

4. **Gather the documents you already have.**
   Keep your photo ID, any written permission, any will or court document, and a certified death certificate when available. Ask the person controlling access or the probate court which documents they need. Do not delay an emergency call while looking for paperwork.

5. **Keep any permitted visit narrow and witnessed.**
   Ask for a supervised visit and retrieve only the essential items agreed in advance, such as medication, pet supplies, keys, or urgent documents. Keep a simple private note of the date, who was present, and what was removed.

6. **If there is conflict, use the local non-emergency route.**
   Ask local law enforcement whether a civil standby or keep-the-peace option is available. Availability and conditions vary. A law-enforcement presence does not itself authorise entry or decide who owns any item. Call **911** only for immediate danger.

7. **Get state-specific help if access remains blocked.**
   Use the state-court route to find the relevant probate court. If the situation is disputed or unclear, ask a probate lawyer or legal-aid service about the next step.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide who inherits belongings today.
- You do not need to clear, photograph, value, or sort the whole home during the first visit.
- You do not need to settle a family disagreement at the door.
- You do not need to solve the full probate process before making a narrow request about urgent items.

## Important reassurance
You only need to deal with the next safe step. A narrow request, written permission where needed, and a limited record of any permitted visit can reduce confusion while formal authority is being worked out.

## Scope note
These are first steps for a safe pause and a limited request for essential items. Ongoing access, control of the home, and decisions about belongings may need state-specific probate advice.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Property access and probate processes vary by state, ownership, tenancy, and building policy. Ask the relevant person controlling access, probate court, or qualified adviser what applies before entering or removing items.
