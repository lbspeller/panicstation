# What to do if… you are asked to identify personal items for return but you are unsure what belongs to whom (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign for or take custody of items you cannot verify. Formally request that the facility (hospital, medical examiner, or funeral home) secure the items in their property office and provide a written inventory until the legally authorized Personal Representative can identify them.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not take the property bag "to sort out later"** if you have doubts or if there is a potential for family dispute; taking custody legally transfers liability to you.
- **Do not sign any property release form** that includes a clause confirming the items are "correct" or "complete" if you have not verified every item.
- **Do not assume all items will be returned;** facilities often discard clothing deemed a biohazard (soiled or saturated) without notifying the family first.
- **Do not expect government-issued IDs** (passports, driver's licenses) to always be in the bag; Medical Examiners may return these directly to the issuing government agency.
- **Do not post photos of sensitive items** (keys, IDs, credit cards, prescriptions) on social media to ask relatives for help identifying them.

## What to do now
1. **Instruct the staff to "hold and secure" all items immediately.** **Tell the property clerk or security officer that you cannot verify the items and they must remain in the facility's safe storage.**
   - Request that the items be returned to the "Security Safe" or "Patient Property Room" rather than left in a general bereavement area.
2. **Request an official itemized inventory list.** **Ask for a written document that lists every item found on or with the person, including descriptions of brand, color, and serial numbers for electronics.**
   - Ensure the list includes the "location of recovery" (e.g., room number, ambulance, or intake area).
3. **Ask for an appointment to view the items in person without taking custody.** **Request to see the items under supervision of a staff member to check for distinctive marks or engravings.**
   - Take your own photos of the items during this viewing for later comparison with family records.
4. **Determine who has the superior legal right to the property.** **Ask the facility for their written policy on property release to a "Personal Representative" versus "Next of Kin."**
   - If a person has been court-appointed as the Executor or Administrator (Personal Representative), they generally have the legal authority to claim property over other family members.
5. **Ask about "disposal timelines" to prevent loss.** **Verify how many days the facility will hold the items before they are considered "unclaimed" and sent to the State Treasurer or destroyed.**
   - Public offices (Medical Examiners/Coroners) often have strict 60 or 90-day limits before property is turned over for disposal or auction.
6. **Separate items being held as evidence.** **If the death involved law enforcement, ask specifically if any items (such as clothing or phones) are being held by the police department rather than the facility.**
   - Obtain the police case number and the name of the detective or property officer in charge of those specific effects.
7. **Obtain a "Hold Receipt" or Reference Number.** **Before leaving without the items, get a written acknowledgement that the items are being held at your request pending proper identification.**
   - This document should include the name and title of the person you spoke with and the date the hold was requested.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide how to distribute sentimental items or "who gets what" today.
- You do not need to provide a final list of missing items until you have reviewed the facility's inventory against your own photos or records.
- You do not need to settle disputes with other relatives while at the hospital or funeral home; the facility's role is storage, not mediation.

## Important reassurance
It is common for personal effects to be mixed up or mislabeled during emergency transport or hospital intake. Feeling confused or unable to recognize items is a normal response to acute stress. Insisting on a formal inventory and a temporary hold is a standard procedure and is the most effective way to protect the estate and prevent permanent loss.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and property preservation only. It does not cover the legal process of probate, estate litigation, or the recovery of items already released to the wrong party.

## Important note
The FTC Funeral Rule entitles you to receive certain disclosures from funeral homes, but hospital and coroner policies are governed by state law and internal security protocols. This information is not legal advice. If you are the court-appointed Personal Representative, you may need to present "Letters Testamentary" or "Letters of Administration" to claim high-value items or sensitive documents.
