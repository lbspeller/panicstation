# What to do if… your card is declined due to a merchant category block you did not set (USA)

## Short answer
Stop repeated attempts, use another safe payment method if you have one, and contact the card issuer using the number on the back of the card or secure in-app support. Ask for the exact decline reason and whether a merchant category or merchant type block can be removed, temporarily allowed, or explained.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep retrying the same transaction over and over; repeated declines may trigger extra security checks or more declines.
- Don’t share one-time codes, online banking passwords, full card numbers, or app login details with anyone who calls, texts, or emails you.
- Don’t click a link in a sudden “bank” text or email to fix the card; open the app yourself or use the number on the back of the card.
- Don’t let a stranger handle your card or unlocked phone.
- Don’t assume the merchant did something wrong; some merchants are coded under categories that do not match what you expected.
- Don’t close the account in the moment unless the issuer confirms fraud or you can see unauthorized transactions.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and write down the basic facts.** Note the merchant name, website or location, time, amount, card used, and the wording of any decline message. Screenshot the issuer app message if it is visible.
2. **Make only one sensible retry.** If you used tap-to-pay at a terminal, insert the chip once and follow the prompt. If it declines again, stop using that card for this purchase until you speak with the issuer.
3. **Use a backup payment method if the purchase is urgent.** Use another card, cash, or a payment method you already trust. Avoid setting up a new money-transfer method from a link or message while stressed.
4. **Check your card controls in the issuer app.** Look for card lock, merchant controls, category restrictions, online purchases, cash-like transactions, international use, ATM access, and daily limits. If you find a setting you did not choose, save the change, then try the purchase only once more or wait for issuer confirmation.
5. **Contact the issuer through an official route.** Call the number on the back of the card or use secure in-app chat. Say: “My card is being declined and I believe it may be a merchant category or merchant type block that I did not set. Please tell me the exact decline reason and whether any category, MCC, merchant type, fraud, or risk rule is involved.”
6. **Ask for a concrete next step.** Ask: “Can this restriction be removed, temporarily allowed for this merchant, or overridden for this purchase?” Also ask: “How was this setting added, and is it set by me, the issuer, or a card program administrator?”
7. **If the card is a work, prepaid, teen, family, benefits, or program card, contact the administrator too.** Some category blocks are set by an employer, card program, benefits program, or account manager, and the issuer’s frontline support may not be able to change them.
8. **If you see unauthorized transactions, treat it as possible fraud.** Tell the issuer you see charges you did not make, ask them to secure the card, and follow their official fraud process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose every possible cause right now.
- You do not need to change all card settings today.
- You do not need to close the account unless the issuer tells you the card is compromised or you see unauthorized activity.
- You do not need to file a complaint while you are still trying to pay. If the issuer will not explain the decline or will not correct a restriction you did not set, keep records of dates, messages, screenshots, and names, then use an official complaint route later.

## Important reassurance
A merchant category decline is often an automated control, coding issue, program rule, or risk decision. It does not mean you did anything wrong, and it is often possible to get a clearer reason or a safe workaround.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce panic, avoid scams, and get a usable payment route. Later decisions, complaints, fraud reports, account changes, or program disputes may need help from the issuer, card administrator, regulator, or another qualified professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not financial, legal, fraud-investigation, or professional advice. Use official issuer contact details and trusted public resources for decisions about your own account.
