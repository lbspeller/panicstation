# What to do if… a friend seems suddenly disoriented at a social event and you worry someone is targeting them sexually (USA)

## Short answer
Move your friend to a private, controlled area immediately, enlist venue security to block third-party access, and call 911 if they are losing consciousness or you cannot maintain their safety.

## Do not do these things
- Don't leave your friend alone for any amount of time, including for bathroom trips or "getting air."
- Don't allow anyone you do not know and trust to assist in moving or "helping" your friend.
- Don't try to confront the suspected person directly if it creates a physical altercation.
- Don't give them food, coffee, or more water, and do not induce vomiting.
- Don't let them leave the venue in a ride-share or taxi alone.
- Don't pressure them to recall what happened or make legal decisions immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Physicalize your support and move them to a safe zone.** **Take your friend by the arm and move them to a quieter, supervised area like a manager's office or a brightly lit vestibule.**
   - Tell them clearly: "I'm staying with you; we're moving somewhere safer right now."
   - Do not let them wander or sit in dark corners.
2. **Alert venue management and request a security perimeter.** **Identify a staff member or security guard and state: "My friend is incapacitated and I believe they are being targeted; I need a safe space and help keeping others away."**
   - Ask staff to monitor the area and prevent any specific individuals from approaching.
   - Request that staff help you coordinate a safe exit when you are ready to leave.
3. **Check for medical red flags and call 911 if necessary.** **Call 911 immediately if your friend is difficult to rouse, having trouble breathing, experiencing seizures, or vomiting repeatedly.**
   - If they are breathing but non-responsive, place them in the recovery position (on their side with the head tilted back to keep the airway clear).
   - Inform the dispatcher you suspect "drug-facilitated incapacitation."
4. **Consult the Poison Help hotline if they are conscious but confused.** **Call the National Poison Help Hotline at 1-800-222-1222 for immediate guidance on symptoms or suspected substances.**
   - This service is free, confidential, and available 24/7.
   - Relay any sensory cues, such as a strange taste in their drink or sudden physical changes.
5. **Transport them to an Emergency Department for a clinical assessment.** **Take your friend to the nearest hospital or wait for EMS if you called 911, and ask specifically if a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) is available.**
   - Tell the medical team: "I am concerned about possible drugging and a risk of sexual targeting."
   - Focus on stabilizing their physical health first.
6. **Preserve physical evidence if it is safe and practical.** **If a suspicious drink or container is still present, keep it in your possession without drinking from it or allowing others to handle it.**
   - Avoid cleaning up any spilled liquids or discarding clothing that may have evidence.
   - Note the time symptoms started and the names of anyone who was hovering nearby.
7. **Contact RAINN for confidential support and local resources.** **Call the National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-HOPE (4673) for assistance with next steps and local advocacy.**
   - You can call on their behalf to find out which local clinics offer the best support for this specific situation.
8. **Ensure a safe handoff to a trusted environment.** **Only leave the hospital or venue when your friend is with a known, trusted family member or friend at a secure location.**
   - If no one is available, involve a hospital social worker or victim advocate to assist with safe discharge.

## What can wait
- Determining exactly what substance was used or how it was administered.
- Deciding whether to file a formal police report or pursue criminal charges.
- Confronting the person you suspect of targeting your friend.
- Identifying everyone who was present at the event.
- Washing clothes or cleaning belongings that may contain evidence.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel panicked or unsure when a friend’s behavior changes suddenly. Your priority is their immediate physical safety, not solving the mystery of what happened. If you feel like something is wrong, trust that instinct—taking proactive steps to protect a friend is always the right choice, regardless of the eventual cause.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Medical evaluation, forensic evidence collection (rape kits), and legal reporting are secondary steps that occur after the immediate threat of targeting or physical collapse has been managed.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not replace professional medical or legal advice. In an emergency, always call 911. Substance-facilitated crimes are complex; healthcare providers and specialized advocates (like RAINN) are the primary authorities for managing these incidents.
