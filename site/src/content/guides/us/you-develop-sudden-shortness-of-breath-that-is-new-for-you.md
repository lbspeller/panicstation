# What to do if… you develop sudden shortness of breath that is new for you (USA)

## Short answer
If you are struggling to breathe, cannot speak in full sentences, have chest pain, feel faint, or have blue or gray skin/lips, **call 911 immediately**. If the sensation is new and persistent but not an immediate emergency, seek **urgent in-person medical evaluation today** at an Emergency Room or Urgent Care center.

## Do not do these things
- Do not **wait to see if it goes away** if the symptom is new, sudden, or severe.
- Do not **drive yourself** to the hospital if you feel dizzy, faint, or significantly breathless.
- Do not **breathe into a paper bag** as this can be dangerous if the cause is a heart or lung condition.
- Do not **lie flat** if sitting up makes breathing easier.
- Do not take **someone else’s prescription** medication, such as an inhaler or oxygen.
- Do not take **sedatives or alcohol** to try to calm down, as these can suppress breathing.
- Do not **eat or drink** while you are actively struggling to breathe.

## What to do now
1. **Check for 911 emergency triggers and call immediately if any are present:**
   - You cannot speak more than a few words without pausing for air.
   - You have **chest pain, pressure, or tightness** (may spread to arms, neck, or jaw).
   - You have a **cold sweat, nausea, or extreme dizziness**.
   - Your lips, face, or nails look **blue, gray, or unusually pale**.
   - You have **sudden swelling** of the tongue, lips, or throat, or a hoarse voice.
2. **If you have called 911, prepare for arrival:**
   - **Unlock your front door** and sit in a comfortable, upright position.
   - **Stay on the line** with the dispatcher and follow their specific instructions.
   - **Loosen tight clothing** around your neck or chest to reduce restriction.
3. **Use your own prescribed rescue medication if applicable:**
   - If you have an **asthma or COPD rescue inhaler**, use it according to your existing medical plan.
   - If you suspect a severe allergic reaction and have a **prescribed epinephrine auto-injector**, use it immediately and **still call 911**.
4. **Position yourself to reduce breathing effort:**
   - **Sit upright** and lean slightly forward, perhaps resting your elbows on your knees or a table.
   - **Avoid all physical exertion** including walking, climbing stairs, or carrying items.
5. **Gather vital information for medical responders:**
   - List your **medications, allergies, and existing health conditions**.
   - Note the **exact time** the shortness of breath started.
   - Mention if you have **recent surgery, long-distance travel, leg swelling, or if you are coughing up blood**.
6. **Stay with the person if you are assisting someone else:**
   - **Monitor their responsiveness** and breathing until help arrives.
   - If they lose consciousness and are not breathing, follow dispatcher instructions for **CPR**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause (e.g., heart vs. lungs vs. anxiety) before seeking help.
- You do not need to find a pulse oximeter or take your own vitals first.
- You do not need to notify your employer or manage non-essential household tasks right now.

## Important reassurance
Feeling startled or overwhelmed is a common response when breathing feels restricted or different. Taking immediate action to get a professional evaluation is the most effective way to address these sensations, regardless of the eventual diagnosis.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and triage during the initial onset of symptoms. Long-term management and diagnosis require a full clinical assessment by a licensed healthcare professional.

## Important note
This information is for general safety purposes and does not constitute medical advice. If you believe you are experiencing a medical emergency, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room immediately.
