# What to do if… you receive a utility bill or notice for an address where you have never lived (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay or share sensitive information until you verify the utility independently. Contact the real utility, say you never lived at the service address and did not open the account, and ask them to treat it as a fraud or identity-theft dispute while you protect your credit.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay “just to stop it” before you have verified and disputed the account; if you already paid, ask the company how it handles refunds or reversals for fraud.
- Do not use the phone number, link, QR code, or payment instructions on the notice until you have confirmed the company from an official source.
- Do not share your full Social Security number, bank details, passwords, or security codes unless you are sure you are speaking with the real utility.
- Do not ignore letters from the utility, a collection agency, or a credit bureau; unanswered notices can make cleanup harder.
- Do not rely on phone-only promises; ask for a case number and written confirmation.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the utility before you engage.**
   - Search for the utility’s official website yourself, or use a bill from your own address if you are already a customer.
   - If the notice demands immediate payment, unusual payment methods, or secrecy, pause and treat it as a possible scam.

2. **Contact the utility’s fraud, customer security, new accounts, or credit and collections team.**
   - Say: “I received a notice for an address where I have never lived. I did not open this account.”
   - Ask them to flag the account as disputed for possible fraud or identity theft.
   - Ask them to pause collection activity while they investigate, if they can.
   - Ask what documents they need, and send copies only.

3. **Ask for written confirmation.**
   - Ask for a case or reference number.
   - Ask for written confirmation that the account is disputed and that you say it is not yours.
   - Ask whether they have reported it to any credit bureau or collector.
   - If anything has been reported, ask them to correct it, remove it, or mark it disputed as appropriate.

4. **Create an identity-theft record if you think your personal information was used.**
   - Use IdentityTheft.gov to make an official report and save the recovery plan or report details.
   - Keep the report number with the utility notice, letters, screenshots, and call notes.

5. **Protect your credit today.**
   - Place a credit freeze with Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion if you want the strongest quick block against new credit accounts.
   - Or place an initial fraud alert with one of the three nationwide credit bureaus; that bureau should tell the other two.
   - Save confirmation numbers or screenshots for each freeze or alert.

6. **Check your credit reports for the address and account.**
   - Get your reports from AnnualCreditReport.com.
   - Look for the unfamiliar address, utility collection accounts, new accounts you do not recognize, and unfamiliar credit inquiries.
   - Dispute wrong items with the credit bureau and with the company that reported the information.

7. **If a debt collector contacts you, respond in writing.**
   - Ask for debt validation information.
   - State that the debt is not yours and relates to an address where you never lived.
   - Keep a copy of your response, the envelope, and any delivery confirmation.

8. **Escalate if the account is not corrected.**
   - Ask the utility for its complaint or executive resolution process.
   - For utility-service problems, check your state utility regulator or state consumer protection office on an official state website.
   - For credit reporting or debt collection problems, consider submitting a complaint to the CFPB.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove who opened the account right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to file a police report unless a company specifically asks for one.
- You do not need to fix every security setting at once; verifying the account, documenting the dispute, freezing or alerting your credit, and checking your reports are enough for the first pass.
- You do not need to argue about the whole bill amount until the account ownership issue is clearly marked as disputed.

## Important reassurance
A utility notice for an address where you never lived can come from identity theft, a scam notice, or a records mix-up. The first goal is not to solve everything at once; it is to stop payment pressure, get the account marked as disputed, and reduce the chance of credit or collection harm.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, especially lawsuit papers, repeated fraudulent accounts, or ongoing credit damage, may need help from a consumer lawyer, identity-theft specialist, legal aid office, or other qualified professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, identity-theft, or professional advice. Processes vary by utility, state, credit bureau, and collector, so use official sources and keep copies of everything you send or receive.
