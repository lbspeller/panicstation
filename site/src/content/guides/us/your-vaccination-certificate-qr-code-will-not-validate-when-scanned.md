# What to do if… your vaccination certificate QR code will not validate when scanned (USA)

## Short answer
Ask what alternative proof they can accept right now, then pull a fresh copy of the vaccination record from the original issuer. A failed QR scan can mean the scanner cannot verify that format, the display is unreadable, or the record needs correction.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the failed scan means your vaccination record is false or useless.
- Do not install unofficial QR scanner apps or enter health details into unfamiliar websites while under pressure.
- Do not edit, crop, filter, screenshot repeatedly, or send the QR through messaging apps if you can avoid it; use the clean original record first.
- Do not hand over your unlocked phone unless you can keep it in sight.
- Do not spend the first minutes arguing the policy; first ask what alternate proof can be accepted now.

## What to do now
1. Ask for the acceptable fallback immediately. Say: “If the QR will not validate, what alternative proof can you accept right now: a printed vaccination record, the record details on the issuer portal, my paper vaccination card if I have one, or another official document?”

2. Check whether the scanner is meant for your QR type. Some verifiers are built for specific formats, such as SMART Health Cards. Ask: “Is your scanner meant to verify this type of QR code, or can you inspect the record details another way?”

3. Re-open the record from the original issuer. Use the pharmacy, health system, state digital vaccine record portal, clinic, or other issuer that created the record. Avoid old downloads, thumbnails, email previews, or compressed copies.

4. If your certificate came from VAMS, retrieve it again through the VAMS Recipient Portal. This only helps if your vaccine dose was documented in VAMS; the VAMS certificate reflects the COVID-19 dose information recorded there.

5. Make the QR easier to scan. Turn screen brightness up, remove glare, zoom only enough to show the full QR, hold the phone steady, and open the QR outside an email preview if possible. If it is printed, flatten the page and check that the QR is not folded, smudged, cut off, or blurred.

6. Compare the certificate details with your passport or photo ID. Check name spelling, date of birth, and vaccine dates. If there is a mismatch, ask whether staff can accept another matching record now, then contact the issuer, vaccination provider, or state immunization registry to correct the record.

7. If you have a paper vaccination card, treat it as a possible fallback, not as a QR replacement. Present it only if staff say they can accept it, and pair it with matching photo ID or another record if requested.

8. If the requirement is destination-specific, ask for the exact document type. For yellow fever, some countries require the International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis, also called the yellow card, validated by the proper stamp. A QR code or ordinary vaccine record may not replace that document.

9. If no proof is accepted and travel is imminent, start official retrieval or correction. Contact the vaccination provider, pharmacy, clinic, health department, or state immunization information system for an official record or correction. CDC can provide information about locating records, but it does not keep individual vaccination records.

## What can wait
- You do not need to troubleshoot every phone setting while you are at the counter.
- You do not need to convert the record into a different system unless the checker specifically says that system is accepted.
- You do not need to decide cancellation, rebooking, or future travel plans until you have asked what proof can be accepted now.

## Important reassurance
A QR code failing to validate is a document-check problem, not proof that your vaccination did not happen. The quickest path is usually to show a clean issuer record, ask for an accepted fallback, and move record corrections to the official issuer or registry.

## Scope note
These are first steps for an immediate document check. Later decisions about travel rules, medical records, registry corrections, or replacement certificates may need help from the issuer, a travel clinic, the airline, the destination authority, or another qualified professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, travel, or professional advice. Travel rules, airline practices, and destination entry requirements vary and can change quickly.
