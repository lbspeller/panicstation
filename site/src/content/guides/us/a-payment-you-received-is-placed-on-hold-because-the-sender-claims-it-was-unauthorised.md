# What to do if… a payment you received is placed on hold because the sender claims it was unauthorised (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the disputed funds in your balance, stop any pending shipments or service access immediately, and submit proof of delivery/fulfillment through the payment platform's official resolution portal.

## Do not do these things
- Don't send a "refund" via a new, separate payment (e.g., Zelle, CashApp, wire, or gift cards) as the original funds are already held and may be reversed.
- Don't click links in emails or texts claiming there is a problem; always log in directly to the official website or app of your payment processor.
- Don't ship any additional items or provide further service access to the sender while the hold is active.
- Don't assume the hold will be lifted if the sender promises to "cancel the dispute" in a private message.
- Don't delete any communication logs, tracking numbers, or order confirmations related to the transaction.
- Don't provide your login credentials or one-time security codes to anyone claiming to be "support" helping to release the hold.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the disputed amount in your financial records.**  
   **Treat the held funds as already gone to avoid overdrawing your account if the reversal becomes permanent.** 
   - Check your balance to see if the hold has created a negative state.
   - Stop any automatic transfers that rely on those specific funds.

2. **Pause all pending fulfillment immediately.**  
   **Verify the status of any physical or digital goods associated with the transaction.** 
   - If shipping a physical item, contact the carrier to see if the package can be intercepted or redirected.
   - If providing a digital service or subscription, suspend the user's access until the dispute is resolved.

3. **Identify the specific dispute reason in your merchant dashboard.**  
   **Log in directly to your payment processor (e.g., Stripe, PayPal, Square) and look for labels like "Unauthorized Transaction," "Fraudulent," or "No Knowledge."** 
   - Note the response deadline; card issuers often give merchants only 10 to 20 days to respond.
   - Check the "Eligibility" status for Seller Protection (common in PayPal/Stripe) to see if the transaction is covered.

4. **Gather and upload specific fulfillment evidence.**  
   **Submit documents through the official portal that prove the item was sent to the address provided at the time of purchase.** 
   - Physical goods: provide a tracking number that shows "Delivered" to the city/zip code on the Transaction Details page.
   - Digital goods: provide server logs, IP addresses, download timestamps, or email receipts showing the recipient accessed the product.
   - Communication: include screenshots of the buyer's initial request or approval of the purchase.

5. **Acknowledge the risk of non-refundable fees.**  
   **Be aware that most processors charge a non-refundable "dispute fee" (typically $15) regardless of the outcome.** 
   - In 2026, many processors also apply an additional "dispute countered fee" if you challenge the claim and lose. 
   - Evaluate if the payment amount is significantly lower than the potential fees before deciding to fight.

6. **Secure your own account credentials.**  
   **Change your password and ensure multi-factor authentication (MFA) is active on the payment account and the linked email.** 
   - Review your "Payout" settings to ensure your bank account information has not been changed.
   - Check for any unfamiliar "linked devices" in your security settings.

7. **Report the incident to federal authorities if you suspect a scam.**  
   **File a report with the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov to help track patterns of payment fraud.** 
   - If the sender used a stolen identity to pay you, reporting helps protect your standing with the processor.

## What can wait
- Contacting the sender's bank directly—they will generally not speak to the recipient of the funds; you must work through your own processor.
- Closing your account—doing so while a dispute is active may complicate the resolution and could be flagged as suspicious behavior.
- Pursuing legal action against the sender—wait for the formal dispute result first to see if you are covered by seller protection.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an "unauthorised" claim is a standard, automated part of the digital payment ecosystem and does not necessarily mean you have done anything wrong. The payment system places an automatic hold to protect the integrity of the network while the facts are verified. Providing clear, timely documentation is the most effective way to resolve the pause.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate steps for merchants or individuals who have received a payment that is now disputed. It does not cover long-term merchant processing strategies or legal defense for high-value lawsuits.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Dispute outcomes are determined by individual bank policies, card network rules (such as Regulation Z), and your processor's Terms of Service. Deadlines are usually final and non-negotiable.
