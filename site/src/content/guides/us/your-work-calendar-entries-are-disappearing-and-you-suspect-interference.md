# What to do if… your work calendar entries are disappearing and you suspect interference (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible work IT or security incident: record what changed, avoid making more changes, and report it through your company’s IT or security channel so logs and recovery options can be checked.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront or accuse a coworker, manager, or assistant in the moment.
- Do not delete more items, empty Deleted Items or Trash, or reinstall calendar apps before IT has checked what can be recovered.
- Do not change sharing, editor, or delegate permissions unless IT or security tells you to, unless you need to stop obvious ongoing unauthorized access.
- Do not move work calendar data to personal email, personal cloud storage, or a personal device.
- Do not post suspicions in team chat, group email, or social media.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the basic facts.** List which entries disappeared, when you noticed, which calendar was affected, and which app or device you were using.
2. **Capture what you can see without trying to fix it first.** Take screenshots showing the missing date range, the calendar name or account, any cancellation notices, and any unusual update messages.
3. **Check for simple calendar-view causes.** Confirm you are viewing the correct calendar, date range, time zone, and view setting, and check whether a filter or hidden shared calendar could be hiding entries.
4. **Check Deleted Items or Trash carefully.** If missing appointments are there, screenshot them before restoring anything. If you restore an item because you urgently need the meeting details, write down exactly what you restored and when.
5. **Look for permission clues.** If you can safely view sharing, editor, or delegate access, take a screenshot of who can edit or manage the affected calendar. Do not change the list unless IT or security instructs you to.
6. **Treat account compromise signs as urgent.** Unexpected MFA prompts, password reset emails you did not request, new sign-in alerts, or unfamiliar devices should be reported immediately through your company’s security incident route.
7. **Open an IT or security ticket.** Ask them to check Microsoft 365 or Exchange audit logs, mailbox auditing, calendar permissions, and recoverable items, or the equivalent records and recovery area for your workplace system.
8. **Tell your manager briefly and factually.** Say that calendar entries appear to be missing, give the ticket number, describe any immediate work impact, and ask for a temporary workaround such as organizers re-sending invitations.
9. **If the pattern may be tied to discrimination, harassment, or retaliation, make a separate factual HR or compliance report.** Keep it focused on dates, missing entries, business impact, and why you believe the pattern may be connected. External discrimination or retaliation options may have deadlines, so check official EEOC or state/local agency information promptly if that context applies.

## What can wait
You do not need to identify who caused it right now. You do not need to decide today whether to escalate outside the company. You also do not need to rebuild your whole calendar before IT has checked recovery options and logs.

## Important reassurance
Missing calendar entries can be caused by sync problems, accidental deletion, shared-calendar permissions, delegate access, organizer cancellations, or policy changes. A calm record of what changed gives IT or security the best chance of finding out what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation, preserve useful records, and trigger the right internal review. Later decisions about HR, compliance, or external complaints may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, technical, employment, or professional advice. Employer systems, policies, and state or federal protections vary.
