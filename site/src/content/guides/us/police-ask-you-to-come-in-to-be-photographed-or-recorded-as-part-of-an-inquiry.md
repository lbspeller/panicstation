# What to do if… police ask you to come in to be photographed or recorded as part of an inquiry (USA)

## Short answer
Ask if the request is voluntary or if you are being detained. If it is voluntary, politely decline to go, be photographed, or give any statement until you have consulted with a lawyer.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume that "just clearing things up" means you are not a suspect or at legal risk.
- Don’t agree to a "quick chat," recorded interview, or written statement without your lawyer present.
- Don’t consent to providing "identification" photos, fingerprints, or DNA samples voluntarily.
- Don’t believe promises that "things will be easier" if you cooperate immediately without counsel.
- Don’t forget that in most states, police are legally permitted to use deceptive tactics or lie to you during an inquiry.
- Don’t delete any digital evidence (texts, call logs, photos) after being contacted, as this can be interpreted as obstruction.

## What to do now
1. **Determine your current legal status.** Ask the officer: "**Am I free to leave, or am I being detained?**" Also ask: "**Is my presence at the station voluntary or required by law?**"
2. **Clearly invoke your constitutional rights.** If you are being questioned or asked to come in, say: "**I am invoking my right to remain silent. I want to speak with a lawyer before saying anything or providing any identifiers.**"
3. **Request to see legal documentation.** If they claim you must comply, say: "**I will comply with a valid warrant or court order; may I see the paperwork?**"
   - Note the name of the judge or court that issued any document shown.
   - Note the specific agency and badge numbers of the officers.
4. **End the interaction if it is voluntary.** If the police confirm you are not being detained and it is a "request," state: "**I am not coming in or answering questions at this time. Please contact my attorney.**" Then hang up or walk away.
5. **Document the contact immediately.** Write down the date, time, location, and the exact words used by the officer.
   - Record the specific "inquiry" or case number if provided.
   - List any physical items or "identifiers" (photos, DNA, recordings) they requested.
6. **Secure legal representation.** Contact a criminal defense attorney or your local bar association's lawyer referral service. 
   - If you are taken into custody and cannot afford a lawyer, explicitly ask for a public defender as soon as questioning begins.
7. **Limit your communications.** Do not discuss the police contact on social media, via text, or with friends. Only discuss the matter with your legal counsel.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you are "innocent" or "guilty"; that is a legal conclusion for later.
- You do not need to provide an explanation for why you want a lawyer; it is a standard constitutional right.
- You do not need to gather evidence or contact witnesses yourself; your lawyer will handle the investigative phase.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel significant pressure, anxiety, or a "need to explain" when law enforcement contacts you. Invoking your right to a lawyer is a standard procedural step in the U.S. legal system and is not a legal admission of guilt, regardless of how an officer may characterize it.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for initial contact only. It does not cover specific state laws regarding "Stop and Identify" statutes or procedures for grand jury subpoenas.

## Important note
Legal procedures vary significantly between federal, state, and local jurisdictions. This information is for general stability and does not constitute legal advice. If you are uncertain of your status, treat the encounter as high-stakes and remain silent until a lawyer is present.
