# What to do if… a client stops responding after you submit an invoice that is now overdue (USA)

## Short answer
Send one calm written reminder with the invoice attached and a clear response date. Ask whether anything is missing, then keep a record of what you sent before deciding on any further step.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send repeated messages to many people at the client. Keep follow-up targeted.
- Don’t threaten, harass, or make unsupported accusations.
- Don’t post public accusations or reviews while you are trying to resolve the payment.
- Don’t automatically keep taking on new work or new costs for the client while the invoice is overdue, unless your contract requires it.
- Don’t add late fees or interest unless your agreement allows them and you have checked any applicable state rules.
- Don’t rely only on phone calls. If you speak by phone, send a short written summary afterwards.
- Don’t shut off access, remove work, or disrupt services as a pressure tactic. If an ongoing service is involved, check the contract and get legal advice before making a change.

## What to do now
1. **Put the key records in one folder.** Save the contract or statement of work, the invoice, proof that you sent it, any purchase order, messages about delivery or acceptance, and the agreed payment terms.
2. **Check for a possible billing blocker.** Ask the client’s billing contact whether they need anything else, such as a Form W-9, vendor onboarding form, purchase order number, billing address, payment details, or portal upload.
3. **Send one written overdue reminder.** Include the invoice number, amount, original due date, invoice attachment, payment method, and a clear date for a reply. Ask: “Is anything missing from me that is preventing payment?”
4. **Check that you are contacting the right entity.** Start with the contract and invoice records. Confirm the client’s legal name and a reliable mailing address. If either is unclear, check the relevant state’s official website.
5. **Keep proof of your follow-up.** Save the sent email and any delivery confirmation. If you later send a formal written demand, state the invoice details, amount owed, what was delivered, how to pay, and the date by which you are asking for a response.
6. **Limit further exposure while you wait.** Avoid starting new work or incurring new costs for this client unless you have a clear reason to do so. Check your contract before changing any ongoing service.
7. **Check the relevant state or local court guidance before escalating.** Check whether the court requires or recommends a written request for payment before filing. Small-claims limits, filing location, service rules, and required steps vary.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to use a lawyer, collection agency, or court process.
- You do not need to negotiate a payment plan before the client has replied.
- You do not need to guess why the client is silent. Focus first on a clear written request and a reliable record.

## Important reassurance
Silence can have administrative causes, such as a missing document or an internal approval delay. It does not by itself show what the client intends to do. A calm written follow-up gives you a useful next step without forcing a larger decision today.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help, especially if the amount is significant, the work is disputed, the client is in another state, or the contract is unclear.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Contracts, state laws, and court procedures differ. If any communication becomes threatening or you feel unsafe, stop direct contact and keep any further communication in writing.
