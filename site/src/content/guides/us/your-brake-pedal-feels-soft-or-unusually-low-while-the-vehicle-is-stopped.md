# What to do if… your brake pedal feels soft or unusually low while the vehicle is stopped (USA)

## Short answer
Do not drive off. Keep the vehicle parked and arrange roadside assistance, a tow, or a mechanic’s direction before any trip.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “try it out” on the road, even at low speed.
- Do not assume it is safe because the car is stopped right now.
- Do not add brake fluid and then drive if you do not know why the level was low.
- Do not ignore a brake warning light, ABS warning light, fresh fluid under the vehicle, smoke, or a strong burning smell near a wheel.
- Do not attempt parking-lot brake bleeding, topping up with unknown fluid, or other improvised brake repairs so you can keep driving.

## What to do now
1. **Keep the vehicle stationary and secure.**  
   Shift to Park, or Neutral with the parking brake set if that is the correct safe position for your vehicle. Keep your foot off the accelerator. Use hazard lights if you are in or near moving traffic.

2. **Do one stationary brake check only.**  
   While still parked, pump the brake pedal 3 times. Then press it firmly and hold for about 5 seconds. If the pedal moves, sinks, feels spongy, or still feels unusually low, treat the vehicle as unsafe to drive.

3. **Check for a simple pedal obstruction.**  
   Look at the driver footwell. Remove any loose floor mat, bottle, bag, or object that could block the brake pedal. Re-check the pedal only while the vehicle remains parked.

4. **Look for obvious brake danger signs without crawling under the car.**  
   Check the dashboard for brake or ABS warnings. Look from a safe standing position for fresh fluid near the wheels or under the vehicle. Notice any smoke or strong burning smell near a wheel. If you see any of these, do not drive the vehicle.

5. **Check the brake fluid only if it is easy and safe.**  
   If you can identify the brake fluid reservoir from the owner’s manual and the engine area is safe to access, check whether the level is below the minimum mark. If it is low, keep the vehicle parked; low brake fluid may mean a leak or another serious brake problem. Do not add fluid and drive away unless the owner’s manual and a qualified person confirm what to use and why it is low.

6. **Arrange help using plain words.**  
   Call roadside assistance, a tow company, or a repair shop. Say: “The brake pedal is soft or low while stopped, and I have not driven it.” Ask whether they want it towed rather than driven in.

7. **If the vehicle unexpectedly starts rolling, focus only on stopping safely.**  
   Keep steering control, take your foot off the accelerator, and turn on hazard lights if you can do so without losing control. Try a lower gear if your vehicle allows it. If the brake pedal sinks, gently pump the brake pedal to see if pressure returns. Use the parking or emergency brake carefully and gradually, then stop in the safest place you can and call for help.

8. **If the place you are parked is unsafe, separate people from the vehicle.**  
   If you are exposed to traffic, get yourself and passengers to a safer place on foot if you can do that safely. Call roadside assistance or emergency services if there is immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact mechanical cause right now.
- You do not need to decide whether it is brake pads, fluid, a leak, ABS, or a master cylinder.
- You do not need to compare repair prices until the vehicle is safely parked and professional help is arranged.
- You do not need to report anything to NHTSA or check recalls before dealing with the immediate safety issue.

## Important reassurance
Noticing a soft or low brake pedal before pulling away is a good catch. You do not have to solve the mechanical problem under stress; the safe first step is to keep the car parked and get help.

## Scope note
These are first steps for a suddenly soft or unusually low brake pedal discovered while stopped. Later decisions may need help from a qualified mechanic, roadside provider, insurer, vehicle manufacturer, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not mechanical, legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Follow your vehicle owner’s manual and the instructions of qualified roadside, repair, or emergency personnel.
