# What to do if… your bank warns an incoming payment may be reversed or is under review (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the money as temporary until your bank confirms it is safe: do not spend it, transfer it, ship goods, or send a separate refund. Contact your bank using a trusted number or the official app and ask what you should avoid while the payment is under review.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t send money back by Zelle, Cash App, Venmo, wire transfer, gift card, crypto, or any refund link.
- Don’t ship items, hand over keys, release files, or provide digital access while the payment is under review.
- Don’t rely on “funds available” in your banking app as proof the payment cannot be removed later.
- Don’t share verification codes, online banking logins, card numbers, or screenshots that show sensitive account details.
- Don’t let a stranger set the pace; urgent refund pressure is a warning sign.
- Don’t move the payment into another account to “keep it safe” unless your bank specifically tells you to.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the transaction connected to the payment.**
   - If this is tied to a sale, job, rental, marketplace order, or private deal, stop delivery, stop work, and do not release anything else that would be hard to recover.
2. **Keep the credited amount untouched.**
   - Leave the full amount in the account.
   - Avoid new withdrawals or transfers that depend on that credit.
   - If you can, keep enough extra money in the account to reduce the chance of overdraft or returned-item problems if the credit is removed.
3. **Contact your bank through a trusted route.**
   - Use the number on your card, the bank’s official website, or the official banking app.
   - Ask what type of payment it is, such as ACH, check deposit, wire, card credit, refund, or peer-to-peer transfer.
   - Ask whether the credit is provisional, held, available only with conditions, or still subject to return.
   - Ask what the bank recommends you do and not do while the review is open.
4. **If it is a check or money order, be extra cautious.**
   - Ask when the bank expects funds to be available under its funds availability policy.
   - Ask whether the bank can remove the credit if the check or money order is later returned unpaid.
   - Do not treat availability as the same thing as final safety.
5. **If someone asks you to send money back, route it through the banks.**
   - Send one calm message if needed: “My bank is reviewing the payment. I can’t send a separate payment back. Please contact your bank.”
   - Do not negotiate a partial refund, alternate payment method, or faster workaround.
6. **If you expected the payment, verify it without using the message that worried you.**
   - Use a channel you already trust, such as an existing account portal, a known phone number, prior verified email, or the sender’s official website if they have one.
   - Confirm the amount, date sent, and payment method.
   - If the details do not match, tell your bank.
7. **If you did not expect the payment, let the bank handle any return.**
   - Tell the bank the payment was unexpected.
   - Say you do not want to initiate an outgoing payment based on it.
   - Ask whether any return or reversal should happen through the normal banking process.
8. **If you already sent money out, act quickly.**
   - Contact your bank’s fraud team and the payment service you used.
   - Ask whether the outgoing payment can be stopped, recalled, disputed, or flagged.
   - Ask what documentation the bank needs; it may ask for messages, receipts, account activity, or a police report.
   - Report suspected fraud through the FTC reporting route.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to continue the sale, job, rental, or relationship with the sender.
- Trying to fix the situation yourself by refunding, offsetting, or forwarding money.
- Arguing with the sender or proving who is right.
- Posting publicly about the payment.
- Filing longer complaints before you have first protected the account and contacted the bank.

## Important reassurance
A bank review does not automatically mean you did something wrong. Pausing, keeping the money untouched, and refusing a separate refund are normal protective steps when an incoming payment may still be reversed.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate financial harm and preserve options. Later, you may need help from your bank’s fraud team, the payment service, the FTC fraud reporting route, the CFPB complaint process, or another specialist depending on what happened.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Payment reversal and finality depend on the payment type, bank rules, network rules, account terms, and the facts of the transaction. Follow instructions from your bank using official contact channels, and avoid sending any new payment based on money your bank says is under review.
