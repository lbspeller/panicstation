# What to do if… your phone or wallet is lost and your cards are exposed to misuse (USA)

## Short answer
Report the exposed cards as lost or stolen now, then secure the phone, carrier account, email, and Apple or Google account from a trusted device. The goal is to stop payments, block account access, and prevent password resets.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait to contact card issuers; debit and ATM card liability can increase if you delay.
- Don’t click links or return calls from “fraud alerts” you did not start; use the issuer’s official app, website, statement, or saved contact.
- Don’t share verification codes, account passwords, device passcodes, or carrier PINs with anyone who contacts you.
- Don’t try to recover a stolen phone from a location shown on a map; contact local law enforcement instead.
- Don’t post photos of cards, IDs, addresses, travel plans, or the loss online.
- Don’t erase the phone before trying lock or lost mode, unless you have decided recovery is unlikely and you accept the data consequences.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the exposed cards first.**
   - Contact each bank, credit union, or card issuer through its official app, website, statement, or saved phone number.
   - Report the card as lost or stolen, ask for the card to be blocked, and request a replacement.
   - Review recent transactions with the issuer and flag anything you did not authorize.
   - Ask whether mobile wallet tokens, virtual cards, or tap-to-pay access linked to the lost device also need to be removed or reissued.

2. **Act fast on debit and ATM cards.**
   - If an ATM or debit card is lost or stolen, federal guidance generally makes your possible loss depend on how quickly you report it.
   - Reporting before unauthorized use generally means no responsibility for unauthorized transactions.
   - Reporting within 2 business days after you learn of the loss generally limits the maximum loss to $50; later reporting can increase the maximum loss.
   - Do not spend time calculating deadlines before calling. Report first.

3. **Check credit cards and dispute unauthorized charges.**
   - For credit cards, federal guidance generally caps responsibility for unauthorized charges at $50, and reporting before use generally means you are not responsible for those charges.
   - Ask the issuer how to confirm the lost or stolen report and any disputed charges in writing.
   - Turn on transaction alerts if the issuer offers them.

4. **Secure the phone service.**
   - Contact your wireless carrier from another phone or trusted device.
   - Ask the carrier to suspend service for the lost or stolen device.
   - Secure the carrier account by adding or confirming a carrier PIN, passcode, or account lock if offered.
   - Ask what device, SIM, or account-blocking options the carrier offers for a lost or stolen phone.

5. **Lock the device and account access.**
   - For an iPhone, use iCloud or Find My to mark the device as lost if available.
   - For an Android phone, use your Google Account or Find My Device options to lock, sign out, or erase if needed.
   - From a trusted device, change the password for the Apple Account or Google Account linked to the phone.
   - Also secure the email account that receives bank, carrier, Apple, Google, or password reset messages.

6. **Decide about remote erase only after locking.**
   - Use lock or lost mode first if it is available.
   - Consider remote erase if recovery seems unlikely, or if sensitive information on the phone creates a serious risk.
   - Be aware that erasing can be hard or impossible to undo. Check the device tool before assuming what you can still track after erase.
   - Do not remove an iPhone from Find My just because it is lost or stolen, because that can remove Activation Lock.

7. **Reduce new-account and identity-theft risk if IDs or personal information were exposed.**
   - If your driver’s license, Social Security card, passport, health insurance card, or other ID was in the wallet, use IdentityTheft.gov for a recovery plan.
   - Consider a credit freeze if enough personal information was exposed for someone to try opening new credit. A credit freeze must be placed with each of the three major credit bureaus: Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.
   - Consider an initial fraud alert if you suspect identity theft. You can contact one of the three major credit bureaus, and that bureau must tell the other two.
   - Keep the confirmation numbers, dates, and any PINs or passwords for freezes or alerts.

8. **Create a simple paper trail.**
   - Write down what was lost, when you noticed, the issuers you contacted, and any case or confirmation numbers.
   - If the phone or wallet was stolen, or a bank, carrier, insurer, or bureau asks for documentation, file a report through the local police non-emergency route.
   - If you later find unauthorized transactions, report them to the issuer promptly and keep copies of messages or letters.

## What can wait
- Replacing loyalty cards, membership cards, and non-financial cards.
- Reinstalling every app on a new phone.
- Choosing paid monitoring services.
- Rebuilding your whole wallet.
- Making long-term decisions about insurance, new accounts, or subscriptions.

## Important reassurance
You do not have to fix every consequence at once. Blocking the cards, securing the carrier account, locking the device, and protecting the email and Apple or Google account are the highest-impact first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stop misuse and buy time. Later decisions may need help from your bank, card issuer, carrier, credit bureaus, insurer, local law enforcement, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, security, or other professional advice. If you believe a crime is happening right now or you feel unsafe, prioritize getting to safety and contacting emergency services.
