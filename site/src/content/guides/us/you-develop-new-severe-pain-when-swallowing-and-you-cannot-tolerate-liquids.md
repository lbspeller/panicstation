# What to do if… you develop new severe pain when swallowing and you cannot tolerate liquids (USA)

## Short answer
Stop trying to swallow and go to an Emergency Department immediately. Call 911 if you have any difficulty breathing, a muffled voice, or are drooling because you cannot swallow your own saliva.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep "testing" your swallow with water or other liquids.
- Do not try to push stuck food down by eating bread, rice, or crackers.
- Do not take any pills, tablets, or capsules as they may become lodged in the swollen area.
- Do not put your fingers, a spoon, or a tongue depressor into the back of the throat to look for the cause.
- Do not lie flat if you are struggling to manage saliva or breathing; stay upright.
- Do not make yourself vomit, especially if you may have swallowed a chemical or caustic substance.
- Do not wait for a primary care appointment or "see how it feels" in the morning.

## What to do now
1. **Adopt a comfortable upright position.** Sit straight up or lean slightly forward with your hands on your knees (tripod position) if it makes breathing easier.
2. **Spit your saliva into a tissue or cup.** If swallowing is too painful or physically impossible, do not force it; allow saliva to exit your mouth to keep your airway clear.
3. **Call 911 immediately if you experience any of these red flags:**
   - high-pitched noisy breathing (stridor) or gasping
   - a muffled or "hot potato" voice
   - inability to speak in full sentences
   - blue or gray tint to the lips or fingernails
   - rapid swelling of the neck or tongue
4. **Go to the nearest hospital Emergency Department (ED) right now.** If you do not have the 911 symptoms above but cannot tolerate liquids, you still require immediate hospital-level evaluation for airway safety and hydration.
5. **Contact Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222 if a chemical ingestion occurred.** Do this while you are on the way to the ED or while waiting for an ambulance. Do not try to "neutralize" the chemical with milk or water unless specifically told to do so by a professional.
6. **Alert the triage nurse to specific triggers.** Use these exact phrases if they apply:
   - "I have a suspected stuck object / food bolus."
   - "I may have a chemical burn in my throat."
   - "I have a high fever and cannot swallow my own spit."
7. **Bring a list of your current medications.** Specifically note if you take blood thinners, steroids, or medications that weaken your immune system.
8. **Have someone else drive you if possible.** If you are feeling faint, dizzy, or are drooling excessively, do not operate a vehicle.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the medical cause (such as epiglottitis, an abscess, or an obstruction) yourself.
- You do not need to try home remedies like gargles, sprays, or honey.
- You do not need to pack a bag or finish chores before leaving.

## Important reassurance
Inability to swallow liquids is a recognized medical emergency that doctors take very seriously. Seeking help immediately is the standard and correct safety procedure to protect your airway and prevent dehydration.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for acute swallowing failure in the next minutes to hours. Long-term diagnostics and specialist follow-up will be handled by hospital staff.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization only and is not a substitute for professional medical diagnosis. If you are in distress or your symptoms are worsening, call 911 immediately.
