# What to do if… your external hard drive will not mount and you need to save your files (USA)

## Short answer
Stop trying fixes that write to the disk. Disconnect the drive, keep it powered off, and treat this as a data recovery problem first, not a “make it mount” problem.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click Format, Initialize Disk, Erase, Convert, Restore, or create a new volume if the files matter.
- Don’t run repair tools such as First Aid, chkdsk, file system repair, partition repair, or rebuild options until you have decided that the risk is acceptable.
- Don’t keep unplugging and replugging the drive if it is clicking, grinding, beeping, or repeatedly spinning up and down.
- Don’t install recovery tools from ads, pop-ups, or sites you do not trust.
- Don’t open the drive enclosure or the drive itself to look inside.
- Don’t send the drive for warranty service or return before checking what will happen to the original device and the data on it.

## What to do now
1. Stop writes immediately. If you can safely eject the drive, do that once. Then unplug it. If it has its own power supply, turn it off.

2. Capture what happened. Take a photo or screenshot of any error message, such as Format, Initialize Disk, Not initialized, RAW, Unallocated, unreadable, or cannot be mounted. Write down whether the drive spins normally, stays silent, clicks, grinds, beeps, or starts and stops.

3. Do one low-risk connection check only. Try a different USB port, a different cable, and, for a desktop-style external drive, the correct power adapter and a different wall outlet. If the drive still does not appear normally, stop cycling it.

4. Check whether the computer can see the device without changing it.
   - Windows: open Disk Management and look only at whether the disk appears and what status it shows. Do not initialize, format, convert, mark active, create a new volume, or run repair.
   - Mac: open Disk Utility and use View, Show All Devices if needed. Look only at whether the device appears in the sidebar. Do not erase, initialize, restore, partition, or run First Aid while the files are the priority.

5. If files are accessible, copy the most irreplaceable ones first. Copy, do not move, the smallest critical folders to a different drive or trusted cloud storage. Start with documents, photos, school files, work files, or records you cannot replace. If errors increase, the drive slows badly, or the drive starts making abnormal sounds, stop.

6. If the drive appears as RAW, Unallocated, Unknown, Not initialized, or visible but not mountable, pause before using recovery software. Software may help in some logical failures, but it can also stress a failing drive or write recovered files to the wrong place if used carelessly. Never save recovered files back onto the problem drive.

7. If the drive clicks, grinds, beeps, was dropped, got wet, smells burnt, or repeatedly spins up and down, keep it powered off and consider a reputable data recovery service early. These signs may point to physical failure, and repeated home attempts can reduce the chance of recovery.

8. If the drive is under warranty or recently bought, separate replacing the device from saving the data. Read the written warranty and ask the seller or manufacturer, in writing, whether service or return will involve replacement, refurbishment, wiping, or loss of access to the original drive. If the data matters, do not send it away until you have recovered what you can or accepted that the data may be lost.

9. If a seller or manufacturer is unhelpful about a defective device or warranty issue, keep the data question separate. Save receipts, warranty terms, serial numbers, photos, and messages. Contact the seller or manufacturer in writing with the problem and the resolution you want, then use FTC consumer guidance if you need to structure a complaint.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a new backup system right now.
- You do not need to reformat, initialize, repair, or run command-line tools to prove anything.
- You do not need to compare recovery software while panicked.
- You do not need to decide today whether the drive itself can ever be trusted again.
- You do not need to argue with a warranty department before protecting the current state of the drive.

## Important reassurance
Pausing is a useful action here. Many permanent losses happen after someone clicks a repair, format, or initialize option just to get past a warning. Keeping the drive unchanged gives you more room to decide.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If the files are high-value, the drive has physical failure signs, or you are unsure what a tool will change, later decisions may need help from a qualified data recovery specialist or an appropriate consumer support route.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional, technical, legal, financial, or other specialist advice. Storage tools and repair steps can change data on a disk, and outcomes depend on the device, file system, and type of failure.
