# What to do if… you receive a notice that a past-due bill will be escalated to enforcement unless paid today (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay only because the message says “today.” Verify the creditor or collector through a route you choose, then use written debt-collector validation or dispute steps if the debt is not clearly confirmed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links or call back the number in the message until you have verified it independently.
- Do not pay until the original creditor, or a legitimate collector in writing, confirms details that match an account you recognize.
- Do not pay by gift card, cryptocurrency, wire transfer to a new account, payment app to an individual, or cash pickup.
- Do not share one-time passcodes, online banking logins, card PINs, or your full Social Security number with an unexpected caller or texter.
- Do not agree to a settlement, consent to judgment, or payment plan on the spot if you are unsure the debt is yours.
- Do not ignore real court papers if they arrive, even if an earlier message looked suspicious.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and keep the evidence.** Screenshot or photograph the notice, including the date, amount, company name, sender, callback number, payment instructions, and any account or case reference.

2. **Verify through a contact route you choose.**
   - If the notice claims to be from a utility, hospital, credit card issuer, lender, landlord, insurer, or other original creditor, use a recent statement, your secure account portal, or the company’s official website to find contact details.
   - Ask: “Is my account past due? What amount do your records show? What action is actually scheduled, and on what date?”
   - Do not use a phone number, link, QR code, or payment portal from the pressure message until it matches an official route.

3. **If this is a third-party debt collector, ask for validation information.**
   - A debt collector generally must give validation information in the first contact or within five days of that first contact, unless an exception applies.
   - Ask for the debt collector’s name, mailing address, the current creditor, the original creditor if different, the account number or reference, and an itemized amount.
   - If the collector will not provide written details, do not make a payment on the call.

4. **If the debt or amount looks wrong, dispute it in writing.**
   - After you receive validation information from a debt collector, you generally have 30 days to dispute the debt in writing.
   - If you send a written dispute or written request for original-creditor information within that window, the collector generally must pause collection of the disputed debt until it responds as required.
   - Keep a copy of what you send, how you sent it, and the date.

5. **Check for identity theft or a mixed-up account if you do not recognize the debt.**
   - Get your credit reports through AnnualCreditReport.com, the federally authorized free credit report site.
   - Look for accounts, addresses, collections, or inquiries you do not recognize.
   - If identity theft is possible, use IdentityTheft.gov to make a recovery plan and keep the report it gives you.

6. **Separate pressure language from real legal action.**
   - A message saying “lawsuit,” “garnishment,” “levy,” or “enforcement” is not the same as official court papers.
   - For ordinary consumer debts, wage garnishment or taking money from a bank account usually requires the collector to sue and get a court order, but some government debts can follow different processes.
   - If you receive court papers, use the court’s official website or clerk’s office contact details to confirm them, and look for legal aid quickly.

7. **If the debt is real and you cannot pay today, ask for a written pause or hardship option.**
   - Ask the original creditor whether a payment plan, hardship program, short extension, or documented arrangement may pause escalation.
   - For a utility shutoff warning, ask exactly what payment arrangement or assistance application prevents disconnection and what confirmation you will receive.
   - Do not rely on a verbal promise; ask for the arrangement in writing or save the confirmation number.

8. **If you suspect a scam or already paid, act through your own bank route.**
   - Contact your bank, card issuer, or payment app using the number on your card or account app, not the message.
   - Say that you may have paid a scammer and ask whether the payment can be stopped, reversed, disputed, or blocked.
   - Report the scam to the Federal Trade Commission at ReportFraud.ftc.gov, and consider a CFPB complaint if a debt collector is involved.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide a long-term repayment strategy today.
- You do not need to negotiate a settlement percentage while you are unsure who is contacting you.
- You do not need to send personal documents or sensitive ID details to an incoming caller or texter.
- You do not need to keep taking repeated calls today; you can ask for communication in writing.
- You do not need to solve every debt issue before verifying whether this notice is real.

## Important reassurance
High-pressure “pay today or enforcement” messages are designed to make you rush. A short verification pause can protect you from paying the wrong party, paying the wrong amount, or giving sensitive information to a scammer.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: verify the notice, protect your information, preserve records, and use written validation or dispute routes where they apply. Later decisions may need help from a consumer-law attorney, legal aid, a credit counselor, the original creditor, or a state consumer protection office.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Debt collection, court, utility, tax, child support, and enforcement processes can vary by state and debt type.
