# What to do if… you receive official paperwork with a “respond by” date that has already passed (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the document's authenticity, then contact the issuing court or agency clerk immediately to request a "Motion for Extension of Time" or to "Set Aside" any default orders already entered.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the paperwork because the date has passed; inaction often results in automatic "Default Judgments" or loss of legal rights.
- Do not pay any fees via gift card, cryptocurrency, or wire transfer; courts and government agencies do not accept these payment methods.
- Do not call the numbers provided in the letter if you suspect a scam; find the official court or agency phone number via a verified `.gov` or `.org` website.
- Do not discard the envelope; the postmark is essential evidence to prove the date the document was actually mailed or delivered.
- Do not alter or backdate any signatures or documents to try and hide the delay.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the envelope and all contents immediately.**
   - **Locate the postmark on the envelope and photograph it.**
   - **Identify the court name, case number, and the specific title of the document (e.g., Summons, Complaint, Notice of Hearing).**
   - **Check if the document was sent via certified mail or if there is a tracking number.**

2. **Verify the case status through the official court or agency clerk.**
   - **Call the Clerk of Court’s office for the jurisdiction listed on the paperwork.**
   - **Provide the case number and ask if a "Default Judgment" or "Order" has already been entered.**
   - **Ask about the "3-day rule" (FRCP 6(e)) if the document was mailed; in federal and many state courts, you may have an extra 3 days added to the deadline.**

3. **Request a late filing based on "Excusable Neglect" or "Good Cause."**
   - **Ask the clerk for the specific form or motion required to file a late response (often a "Motion for Extension of Time" or "Motion to Vacate").**
   - **State clearly that you only just received the document and provide the date of delivery.**
   - **Request a "Notice of Appearance" be filed immediately to ensure you receive all future notices in the case.**

4. **Prepare a factual statement regarding the delay.**
   - **Draft a short paragraph stating when you received the notice and why it was late (e.g., mail delivery issues, incorrect address, or hospitalization).**
   - **Include the postmark date as evidence if it differs significantly from the date on the letter.**
   - **Focus on the fact that you are responding immediately upon actual notice.**

5. **Contact a lawyer or legal aid if the matter involves high stakes.**
   - **Seek a private attorney or local legal aid office if the case involves debt collection, eviction, or lawsuits.**
   - **Contact your Public Defender or the court’s criminal division immediately if the summons relates to a criminal charge or a missed court appearance.**

6. **File your response and obtain proof of filing.**
   - **Submit your documents via the court’s e-filing system or in person at the clerk’s window.**
   - **Obtain a "date-stamped" copy or an electronic receipt for your records.**
   - **Keep a log of everyone you spoke to at the court, including dates, times, and instructions given.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a complete legal defense today.
- You do not need to decide on a long-term trial strategy right now.
- You do not need to pay any alleged debts mentioned in the paperwork until you have verified the judgment status.

## Important reassurance
Missing a deadline due to mail delays or administrative errors is a common occurrence in the legal system. Most courts have specific procedures (motions to vacate or set aside) designed to allow people to respond when they did not receive timely notice. Taking action within the first 24 hours of receiving the late notice is the most effective way to show the court you are acting in good faith.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for civil and administrative deadlines. It does not replace the specific procedural rules of individual state courts or federal agencies. 

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Timelines and rules for "Excusable Neglect" vary by jurisdiction. If the paperwork involves a warrant, immigration status, or child custody, contact a qualified attorney immediately before appearing in person at a court or government office.
