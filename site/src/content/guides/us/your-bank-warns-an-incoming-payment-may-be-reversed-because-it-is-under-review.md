# What to do if… your bank warns an incoming payment may be reversed because it is under review (USA)

## Short answer
Do not spend, withdraw, or transfer any portion of the funds, and do not "refund" the sender yourself. Contact your bank's official fraud department immediately to confirm if the credit is provisional and what specific verification is required to finalize it.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send a "refund" or "return" payment using Zelle, Cash App, Venmo, wire transfer, or gift cards.
- Do not ship items, provide services, or hand over keys until the bank confirms the funds are non-reversible.
- Do not assume "available balance" means the transaction is final; banks can claw back provisional credits weeks later.
- Do not click "refund links" sent via text or email, even if they appear to be from your bank.
- Do not share one-time passcodes (OTP) or login credentials with anyone claiming to be from the bank or the sender.
- Do not allow the sender to rush you with threats of legal action or police reports.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the funds immediately.**
   - **Calculate your "true balance" by subtracting the disputed amount from your total balance.**
   - **Avoid any automated payments or bill pays that might overdraw the account if this credit is removed.**
2. **Contact your bank via a trusted channel.**
   - **Call the number on the back of your physical debit card or the official website.**
   - **Ask if the hold is a Regulation CC "exception hold" or a fraud review.**
   - **Request the specific "finality date" for the type of payment received (ACH, Wire, or Check).**
3. **Verify the deposit details.**
   - **Ask the bank to identify the sender's name and the originating institution.**
   - **Confirm if the payment is a "provisional credit" resulting from a reported error or a standard deposit.**
4. **Pause all related business or personal transactions.**
   - **Stop any pending shipments or digital transfers associated with this specific payment.**
   - **Notify the sender (once only) that the transaction is under bank review and no further action will be taken until it clears.**
5. **Document the communication.**
   - **Save copies of the bank's warning message and any messages from the sender.**
   - **Keep a log of the time, date, and representative name for every call to the bank.**
6. **Report suspected fraud to federal authorities if the sender pressures you.**
   - **File a report at ReportFraud.ftc.gov if you suspect a "send-it-back" scam.**
   - **Submit a complaint to the Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3.gov) for electronic transfer fraud.**
7. **Protect your account access.**
   - **Change your online banking password if you shared any screenshots or account details with the sender.**
   - **Enable two-factor authentication using an authenticator app rather than SMS if available.**

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to complete the sale or relationship with the sender.
- Responding to follow-up questions from the sender about why the "refund" is taking so long.
- Filing a formal consumer complaint with the CFPB (try to resolve it with the bank's fraud team first).
- Closing the account entirely (this may complicate the recovery of funds if the bank is still investigating).

## Important reassurance
It is standard procedure for banks to review large or unusual deposits to protect both the institution and your account. Receiving a warning does not mean you have done something wrong; it is a safety mechanism designed to prevent you from spending money that might not exist. Taking a pause is the most effective way to prevent a permanent financial loss.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for domestic U.S. banking transactions only. It does not cover international wire transfers or complex commercial escrow disputes, which may require specialized legal or financial counsel.

## Important note
Banking regulations including Regulation CC and the Electronic Fund Transfer Act (Regulation E) govern funds availability and error resolution but do not guarantee a payment is "safe" once it appears in your balance. Always follow your specific institution's deposit agreement and fraud protocols.
