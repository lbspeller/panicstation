# What to do if… you notice an unfamiliar vehicle repeatedly parked near your home or workplace at odd times (USA)

## Short answer
Create a safe physical barrier by going indoors or to a staffed location and do not approach or interact with the vehicle. Contact the local police non-emergency line to report the pattern, or call 911 if you observe an immediate threat or believe a crime is in progress.

## Do not do these things
- Do not approach the vehicle, knock on the glass, or attempt to speak with the occupants.
- Do not open windows, doors, or gates to get a better view or take a photograph.
- Do not follow the vehicle or attempt to block it in with your own car.
- Do not flash lights, use car horns, or make gestures toward the vehicle to "scare them off".
- Do not post license plates, photos of occupants, or location details on public social media or neighborhood apps.
- Do not wait to "see what happens" if you feel your physical safety is currently at risk.

## What to do now
1. **Establish a safe pause immediately.**
   - Move into a locked building or a well-lit, populated area where you are not alone.
   - Ensure all entry points (doors, windows, garage) are secured.
   - If you are in your own vehicle, keep the engine running, doors locked, and drive to a police station or staffed public location rather than stopping at your destination.
2. **Notify a person in your immediate environment.**
   - At home: alert housemates or a trusted neighbor via text/call.
   - At work: notify building security, a manager, or the facilities department.
   - In a public space: inform staff or security personnel of the specific vehicle.
3. **Document the vehicle's details from a safe, internal position.**
   - Record the license plate number and state of issue.
   - Note the make, model, color, and any identifying features (dents, stickers, roof racks).
   - Log the exact date, time of arrival, and time of departure.
   - Observe the occupant's behavior, such as taking photos, using binoculars, or exiting the vehicle near specific entrances.
4. **Preserve existing digital evidence.**
   - Download and save clips from doorbell cameras, dashcams, or security systems.
   - Note the timestamps of when the vehicle first appeared and when it returned.
5. **Report the activity to local law enforcement.**
   - **Call 911** if someone exits the vehicle and approaches you, tries door handles, makes threats, or follows you.
   - **Call the local non-emergency line** if the vehicle is loitering without immediate threat.
   - State clearly that you are reporting "suspicious activity" and provide the description and repeat pattern.
6. **Request a case or incident report number.**
   - Keep this number to reference if the vehicle returns, as it helps police link separate sightings into a single investigative file.
7. **Adjust your immediate arrival and departure routines.**
   - Park in well-lit areas as close to entrances as possible.
   - Have keys or access cards ready in your hand before leaving a building.
   - Ask for a security escort or walk with a colleague if you are at a workplace.
8. **Check for relevant signage from a distance.**
   - Observe if the vehicle is parked in a restricted zone, private lot, or "No Parking" area, which provides a secondary legal reason for police or towing services to intervene.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the owner or driver of the vehicle yourself.
- You do not need to determine the motive or reason for the vehicle being there.
- You do not need to purchase or install new security equipment today.
- You do not need to confront the individuals to "gather proof" for a report.

## Important reassurance
Noticing a change in your environment and feeling a sense of alertness is a standard protective response. Taking steps to distance yourself and document observations is a functional way to manage safety. Hesitation or uncertainty about whether a situation is "serious enough" to report is common, but law enforcement prefers early reports of patterns over waiting for an escalation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions. Ongoing patterns of stalking or harassment may require long-term safety planning with law enforcement or victim advocacy professionals.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and does not constitute legal advice or a guarantee of police response. Jurisdictional protocols for suspicious activity reporting vary by municipality and state.
