# What to do if… a finger or toe suddenly becomes cold, pale or blue and painful (USA)

## Short answer
Seek emergency medical evaluation immediately. If the digit is suddenly cold, extremely painful, numb, or turning blue or grey, call **911** or go to the nearest Emergency Room, as this may indicate acute limb ischemia.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if the symptoms improve if the pain is severe or the digit is numb.
- Do not use **hot water or direct high heat** (such as a heating pad on high) to warm the area; use only lukewarm water.
- Do not rub, massage, or apply pressure to the affected digit, especially if it is numb.
- Do not smoke or use nicotine products, as these further constrict blood vessels.
- Do not wear tight shoes, socks, or jewelry on the affected limb.
- Do not allow the digit to become cold again once you have started warming it.
- Do not take aspirin or other new medications without medical advice, but do not stop prescribed medications.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 if the digit shows signs of critical ischemia.** Call **911** immediately if you experience the "6 Ps":
   - **P**ain (severe and sudden)
   - **P**allor (pale, white, or blue-grey skin)
   - **P**ulselessness (cannot feel a pulse in the wrist or ankle)
   - **P**aresthesia (numbness or "pins and needles")
   - **P**aralysis (inability to move the finger or toe)
   - **P**oikilothermia (the digit feels "perishingly cold")
2. **Go to an Emergency Room if 911 is not dispatched.** Sudden, painful discoloration is a time-critical emergency; permanent tissue damage can occur within 4 to 6 hours if blood flow is not restored.
3. **Remove all constrictive items immediately.** Take off rings, watches, tight socks, or shoes before any swelling begins.
4. **Note the exact time symptoms started.** Write down the time the color change or pain began, as this is the most critical information for the vascular team.
5. **Rewarm the area only if symptoms are mild.** If you suspect a standard cold-exposure episode (like Raynaud's) and there is no severe pain or numbness:
   - Use lukewarm water between 98°F and 104°F (37°C–40°C).
   - Place the hands in the axillae (armpits) or between the thighs for body heat.
   - Stop immediately and seek an ER if the color does not return to normal within 15 minutes of warming.
6. **Protect the digit during transport.** Keep the affected limb still and at a level slightly below the heart to encourage blood flow.
7. **Prepare information for triage.** Be ready to tell the nurse if the symptoms are on one side only, if you have a history of heart rhythm issues (like AFib), diabetes, or recent surgeries.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause (clot, spasm, or injury) before seeking help.
- You do not need to perform "tests" to see if you can still feel pain or temperature.
- You do not need to wait for a call back from your primary care doctor.
- You do not need to worry about long-term medication changes until you are stabilized.

## Important reassurance
Sudden changes in circulation are alarming, but emergency departments are equipped to rapidly assess and treat blood flow issues. Quick action is the standard protocol for these symptoms and is the most effective way to protect tissue health.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for acute digital or limb circulation concerns. Long-term management and diagnosis require in-person clinical assessment.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not a substitute for professional medical diagnosis or treatment. Sudden coldness and pain in a digit is a medical emergency that requires immediate professional evaluation.
