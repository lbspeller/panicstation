# What to do if… a finger or toe suddenly becomes cold, pale or blue and painful (USA)
## Short answer
Call 911 now if the color change comes with severe or rapidly worsening pain, numbness, weakness, inability to move the digit normally, rapidly worsening discoloration, or a cold or pale hand or foot. If none of those apply, a new painful cold finger or toe that stays discolored still needs urgent in-person medical assessment now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delay calling 911 if there is severe pain, worsening discoloration, numbness, weakness, loss of movement, or a cold or pale hand or foot.
- Do not use very hot water, high heat, or anything that could burn numb skin.
- Do not force movement or massage through worsening pain or numbness.
- Do not smoke or vape nicotine.
- Do not leave a tight ring, shoe, or sock in place around the affected area.
- Do not start aspirin or another blood-thinning medicine just in case. Do not stop prescribed medicine unless a clinician tells you to.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 for emergency features.** Call now if the pain is severe or rapidly worsening, the discoloration is rapidly worsening, the digit is numb or weak, you cannot move it normally, or the whole hand or foot feels cold or looks pale.
2. **Get urgent in-person care if the symptoms are new or do not settle.** If there are no 911 features but the finger or toe stays cold, painful, pale, or blue, or is not returning to its usual color with gentle warmth, go to an emergency department now. If this is a familiar mild episode that fully settles with gentle warming, follow your clinician’s usual plan. Seek medical advice if the pattern changes, episodes become more painful, or the color does not return to normal.
3. **Remove constriction early.** Take off rings before swelling develops. Loosen shoes, socks, or anything tight around an affected toe or foot.
4. **Warm gently.** Move into a warm room, use a jacket or blanket, and keep your whole body comfortably warm. Place the affected finger or toe in a warm place or use warm, not hot, water. Do not delay urgent care to keep trying home warming if the pain or color change worsens or does not improve.
5. **Note the start time if this does not delay care.** A quick photo in normal lighting may also help show the change to a clinician.
6. **Tell the clinician the key details.** Say when it started, whether it is new or familiar, whether it followed cold exposure, stress, or injury, and whether you have Raynaud phenomenon, diabetes, vascular disease, an irregular heartbeat, a clotting disorder, or a history of clots. Bring or list your medicines.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the cause before getting care.
- You do not need to perform home circulation tests or keep checking the area every minute.
- You do not need to exercise through pain.
- You do not need to make long-term treatment decisions now.

## Important reassurance
Some episodes settle with gentle warmth, including some Raynaud-type episodes. New, persistent, or severe symptoms still deserve prompt assessment because a clinician may need to check the blood flow.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the initial hours only. Diagnosis and later treatment decisions may need an in-person clinician or specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, diagnostic, or other professional advice. Call 911 for emergency features or seek urgent in-person care if symptoms are new, persistent, severe, or worsening.
