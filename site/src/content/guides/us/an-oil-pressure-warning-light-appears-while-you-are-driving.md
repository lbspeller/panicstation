# What to do if… an oil pressure warning light appears while you are driving (USA)

## Short answer
Move out of active traffic and stop as soon as you safely can. Switch the engine off and do not restart it merely to see whether the warning goes away. If the place where you stopped puts you in immediate danger, call 911 and prioritize reaching a safer position.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep driving farther than needed to get out of active traffic.
- Do not repeatedly restart the engine to test whether the warning disappears.
- Do not rev the engine to try to change the warning.
- Do not inspect the vehicle, open the hood, or attempt repairs while exposed to moving traffic.
- Do not cross traffic lanes on foot to reach another location.

## What to do now
1. **Reach the earliest safer stopping place.** Signal, slow smoothly, leave active traffic as soon as safely possible, and turn on the hazard lights. Stop on the shoulder or in another immediately reachable place away from moving traffic.
2. **Switch the engine off and keep it off.** If the engine has already stopped, do not restart it merely to test the warning.
3. **Call 911 if your position is dangerous.** Call immediately if the vehicle is stopped in or very close to an active lane, on a narrow shoulder, or somewhere with poor visibility. Tell the dispatcher that you have a disabled vehicle exposed to traffic and follow their instructions where possible.
4. **Use the safer available waiting position.** If the vehicle is safely away from moving traffic, stay inside with your seat belt buckled and keep the hazard lights on. Do not step into traffic or cross lanes. If remaining in the vehicle appears unsafe because of your exact location, ask the 911 dispatcher what to do.
5. **Only check for an obvious leak or the oil level if you are in a genuinely safe place.** Keep the engine off. If you know how to check the dipstick safely, follow the owner’s manual. Do not restart the engine if oil is visibly leaking, the oil level is very low, or you are unsure.
6. **Arrange roadside assistance or a tow.** Tell the provider that an oil-pressure warning appeared, whether it stayed on or flickered, whether the engine stopped, and whether you noticed a leak or unusual engine noise.
7. **Only move the vehicle again to avoid immediate personal danger.** When possible, call 911 and follow the dispatcher’s instructions before restarting. If there is no safer option and moving the vehicle is essential to leave an immediately dangerous position, move only the minimum distance needed, then switch the engine off again.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause at the roadside.
- You do not need to decide whether the issue is a leak, a sensor fault, or another mechanical problem.
- You do not need to reset warning codes, disconnect the battery, choose parts, or approve repairs now.

## Important reassurance
Stopping and keeping the engine off is a sensible precaution. An oil-pressure warning can have more than one cause, and you do not need to work out which cause applies while you are dealing with traffic and roadside safety.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The vehicle may need inspection before further driving. A mechanic may check the oil level, look for leaks, review the warning history, and carry out other tests appropriate to the vehicle.

## Important note
This guide provides general information, not professional mechanical, emergency-response, legal, or other professional advice. Warning symbols and instructions vary by vehicle. Follow any stricter instructions in the owner’s manual and prioritize emergency services when your stopping position is dangerous.
