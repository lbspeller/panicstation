# What to do if… you are told you must attend an appointment with an official caseworker within 24 hours (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the request through an official, independently sourced phone number for the specific agency (e.g., County Social Services, US Probation, or the local Courthouse). If verified, request the details in writing and confirm the exact location and necessary documents before traveling.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t provide your full Social Security Number (SSN), bank account numbers, or digital payment codes during an inbound call to "verify" your identity.
- Don’t pay any "appointment fees," "processing fines," or "missed meeting penalties" via gift cards, cryptocurrency, or wire transfers; real government agencies never accept these.
- Don’t ignore the demand if you are currently on probation, parole, or pretrial release, as missing a meeting can trigger an immediate arrest warrant.
- Don’t agree to be picked up by the caller or meet in a non-governmental location like a park or coffee shop.
- Don’t panic and provide a detailed statement about a legal case or incident over the phone in a rushed state.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific agency or system that is claiming authority over you.** 
   - Ask for the caller's full name, badge or ID number, office address, and the specific department they represent (e.g., "Department of Social Services," "Adult Probation," or "Pretrial Services"). 
   - Look for a case number or docket number on any text or email received.
2. **Verify the caller using a verified contact path that you initiate.** 
   - Hang up and find the official agency number on a `.gov` website or the blue pages of a phone directory. 
   - Call the main switchboard and ask to be transferred to the officer or caseworker named. 
   - If it is a federal court matter, search the docket on the PACER system (pacer.uscourts.gov) to see if a notice has been filed.
3. **Confirm if the 24-hour window is a legal requirement or a request.** 
   - Check your existing court orders or release papers; federal "Standard Conditions" often require reporting within 72 hours of release or as directed by an officer. 
   - If this is your first meeting after a court appearance, it is likely a mandatory "intake" appointment.
4. **Request the appointment details in a documented format.** 
   - Ask them to send the "Notice to Appear" or "Appointment Confirmation" via an official agency email or to the mailing address they have on file. 
   - Save any voicemails or texts as evidence of when the notice was actually given.
5. **Request a reschedule immediately if a physical barrier prevents attendance.** 
   - If you have a verified medical emergency, mandatory work shift, or lack of transportation, state this clearly to the verified office line. 
   - Keep names and times of everyone you speak to while trying to reschedule.
6. **Protect your rights if the appointment is with law enforcement or investigators.** 
   - Clarify if your attendance is voluntary or mandatory. 
   - If you have a public defender or private attorney, contact them before attending the meeting. 
   - You have the right to decline an interview or request an attorney be present during questioning.
7. **Organize your identification and required documents.** 
   - Prepare a government-issued photo ID (Driver's License, Passport, or State ID). 
   - Bring any specific paperwork mentioned, such as proof of employment, residence, or completion of court-ordered programs.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prepare a full defense or narrative for the underlying case tonight.
- You do not need to provide original vital documents (like a birth certificate) during the first phone call.
- You do not need to hire a private investigator to "check out" the caseworker.
- You do not need to decide on long-term legal strategies until after the initial logistics are confirmed.

## Important reassurance
Sudden demands for 24-hour appointments are common in supervision systems (like probation) and emergency social services. Feeling overwhelmed or suspicious is a standard response. Taking ten minutes to verify the caller's identity is a normal safety procedure that real caseworkers will understand.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for responding to urgent contact from government or legal officials. It does not cover specific legal defense strategies or long-term social service applications.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Rules for mandatory reporting vary significantly between federal, state, and county jurisdictions. If you are under court supervision, a failure to report can have immediate legal consequences.
