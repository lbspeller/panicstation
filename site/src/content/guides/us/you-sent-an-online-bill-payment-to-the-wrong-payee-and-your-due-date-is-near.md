# What to do if… you sent an online bill payment to the wrong payee and your due date is near (USA)
## Short answer
Check whether the wrong payment can still be cancelled, and contact your bank promptly if it cannot. Separately, contact the correct biller and ask for the quickest accepted payment method and when it will be credited.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t wait for the wrong payment to be recovered before contacting the correct biller.
- Don’t send another payment until you have checked the correct biller details, the quickest accepted method, and your available funds.
- Don’t send another payment to the wrong payee hoping it will be forwarded.
- Don’t share login codes, passwords, or remote access to your device with anyone offering to reverse the payment.
- Don’t guess whether it was fraud. Describe exactly what happened: a payment you entered incorrectly, a payment you did not make or allow, or a possible bank processing error.
- Don’t delete the payment confirmation, screenshots, or messages.

## What to do now
1. **Check the payment details and status in your bank’s bill-pay area.**  
   Confirm the payee, amount, scheduled date, status, and whether the payment appears to be electronic or a mailed check. Save a screenshot or confirmation number.

2. **Cancel the payment immediately if the bank’s system still allows it.**  
   Save the cancellation confirmation. If the wrong instruction is part of a recurring series, check whether the future payments also need to be cancelled.

3. **Contact your bank promptly if you cannot cancel it yourself.**  
   Use the contact details in your banking app, on your bank statement, or on the back of your bank card. Say that an online bill payment was sent to the wrong payee and ask:
   - whether the payment can still be stopped,
   - whether a mailed bill-pay check can have a stop-payment request placed on it,
   - what options remain if an electronic payment is already processing or completed, and
   - whether there may be a fee or any further form to complete.  
   Ask for a case number and note what the bank says.

4. **Contact the correct biller now.**  
   Use a contact route from the biller’s official website, statement, or app. Ask:
   - which accepted payment method is quickest,
   - when a payment made today would be credited,
   - whether a late fee or other consequence may apply, and
   - whether they can note the situation on your account.  
   Save any receipt, chat transcript, or email confirmation.

5. **Decide whether to make a replacement payment to the correct biller.**  
   Check the biller details carefully and consider your available funds, because the wrong payment may still leave your account temporarily. Use the method the correct biller confirms is appropriate.

6. **If you can independently verify the wrong payee, ask whether they can return the payment.**  
   Keep the request brief. Do not share login codes, passwords, or unnecessary account information. If you cannot verify the recipient safely, use your bank’s process instead.

7. **If the bank appears to have sent the payment differently from your instruction, say so clearly.**  
   Ask the bank to log the issue and explain any error-resolution process that may apply. Keep screenshots showing what you selected and what the bank processed.

8. **If you did not make or allow the payment, report that separately and promptly.**  
   Tell the bank that you did not authorize the payment and follow its instructions for reporting the transaction. Keep a record of when you reported it.

9. **Keep a short record.**  
   Note the payment amount, dates, payee, status, case numbers, people you contacted, and where your screenshots and receipts are saved.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to solve recovery and the due date in one call. Start the bank process and contact the correct biller first.
- You don’t need to decide today whether to make a formal complaint.
- You don’t need to argue about fault while you are gathering the payment details and case numbers.
- You don’t need to consider legal options now.

## Important reassurance
You can separate this into two manageable tasks: start the stop or recovery request for the wrong payment, and ask the correct biller about the quickest accepted option. You do not need to solve every later question at once.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on the payment type, payment status, bank process, biller process, and whether specialist help is needed.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Bank bill-pay systems, cut-off times, stop-payment options, fees, and biller crediting practices vary. Ask your bank and the correct biller what applies to this payment.
