# What to do if… your bank notifies you that cheques from your account are temporarily blocked for fraud checks (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately using a verified phone number to confirm the restriction, identify which checks are affected, and switch urgent payments to an electronic method to avoid late fees.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use phone numbers or links provided in an unsolicited text or email notice.
- Do not attempt to write additional checks until the bank confirms the block is lifted.
- Do not assume the block is a mistake; treating it as a false alarm may allow fraudulent activity to continue.
- Do not provide one-time passcodes or login credentials to any caller, even if they claim to be from the bank's fraud department.
- Do not close the account immediately unless directed by the bank, as this can prevent the recovery of funds or the investigation of specific items.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the fraud notice using a trusted channel.** Call the number on the back of your physical debit card or log in to the official mobile app to find a secure contact number.
   - Request the specific fraud department or "loss prevention" unit.
   - Confirm if only specific check numbers are blocked or if the entire account is restricted.
2. **Obtain specific details regarding the hold and the required resolution.** Ask the bank representative the following:
   - Which specific check numbers, payees, or amounts triggered the review.
   - What documentation is required to verify the blocked checks as legitimate.
   - The expected timeline for the review to be completed.
   - Request a case number for all correspondence.
3. **Notify payees of urgent pending payments.** Contact any person or business waiting for a check payment (such as a landlord or utility company) to explain there is a processing delay.
   - Do not wait for the check to be returned; offer an alternative payment method like a credit card, ACH transfer, or online portal payment immediately.
4. **Audit your check register against recent account activity.** Compare your physical checkbook or carbon copies against the "pending" or "processed" transactions in your online banking.
   - Identify any missing check numbers or unfamiliar payees.
   - Report any discrepancies to the bank's fraud investigator immediately.
5. **Place stop-payment orders on compromised or missing checks.** If you suspect a check was lost or stolen, request a stop-payment order.
   - Note that under UCC Article 4, the bank must receive this order in a time and manner that gives them a reasonable opportunity to act before the check is processed.
   - Confirm the fee for this service and the duration the stop-payment remains active (typically six months).
6. **Protect your wider financial identity if fraud is confirmed.** If the bank confirms unauthorized activity, take steps to secure your credit profile.
   - **Place a fraud alert:** Contact one of the three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion); they are required to notify the other two.
   - **Place a credit freeze:** Contact each of the three bureaus individually to restrict access to your credit report; this is free under federal law.
   - **Freeze ChexSystems:** Contact ChexSystems to prevent new fraudulent checking or savings accounts from being opened in your name.
7. **Report identity theft to federal authorities.** If personal information was misused, visit IdentityTheft.gov to file an official report with the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) and receive a personalized recovery plan.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a new bank or permanent payment strategy today.
- You do not need to file a police report immediately unless the bank or the FTC recovery plan specifically requires it for your case.
- You do not need to dispute past legitimate checks that have already cleared.

## Important reassurance
A fraud block is a standard procedural tool used by financial institutions to prevent the loss of funds. Banks frequently use automated systems to flag unusual activity, and many restrictions are resolved once the account holder provides verbal or written verification of the transactions.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a financial restriction. It does not cover legal disputes with payees or long-term account recovery for victims of significant identity theft.

## Important note
Banking regulations and fraud protocols vary by institution and state law. This guide contains general information and is not a substitute for professional financial or legal advice. If you believe your bank is acting in bad faith or failing to resolve a legitimate issue, you may file a complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB).
