# What to do if… you develop pelvic pain with fever and unusual discharge (USA)
## Short answer
Arrange urgent in-person medical assessment as soon as possible, preferably today. Go to the nearest ER now, or call 911 if you cannot get there safely, if the pain is severe or rapidly worsening, you faint or feel close to fainting, you cannot keep fluids down, you have heavy vaginal bleeding, or you have a high fever and feel very unwell.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait a day or two to see whether pelvic pain with fever and unusual discharge settles by itself.
- Do not douche.
- Do not start leftover antibiotics or take someone else’s antibiotics.
- Avoid sex until you have been assessed. Follow the clinician’s advice about when it is safe to resume.
- Do not delay care while trying to diagnose the cause yourself or collect a sample at home.
- Do not assume the symptoms must be an STI, or that they cannot be an STI.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency warning signs.**  
   Go to the nearest ER now, or call 911 if you cannot get there safely, if the pain is severe or rapidly worsening, you faint or feel close to fainting, you cannot keep fluids down, you have heavy vaginal bleeding, or you have a high fever and feel very unwell.

2. **Arrange an in-person assessment as soon as possible, preferably today.**  
   Contact your OB-GYN, primary care clinician, an urgent care clinic, or a local public health or STI clinic. Use the clinic finders in `Additional Resources` if needed. Call ahead and ask whether the site can assess pelvic pain with fever and unusual discharge today. If it cannot, or you cannot be assessed promptly, go to the ER.

3. **Check for possible pregnancy without delaying care.**  
   If pregnancy is possible and you have a home pregnancy test available, take it and tell the clinician the result. Do not delay care to take a test. Get medical care right away if the test is positive or pregnancy may still be possible. Go to the ER if prompt assessment is not available or you have any emergency warning signs.

4. **Make a short symptom note for the clinician.**  
   Include when the pain, fever, and discharge changes started; your highest temperature if known; whether the pain is one-sided or across the lower abdomen; any bleeding; pain with urination or sex; your last menstrual period; possible pregnancy; contraception, including an IUD; and any recent antibiotic use.

5. **Tell the clinician about anything that may affect the assessment.**  
   Mention medication allergies, current medicines, a recently inserted IUD, recent antibiotic use, and any possible STI exposure. You do not need to decide what the cause is before you are seen.

6. **Get to care safely.**  
   If you feel faint, very weak, or the pain is escalating, do not drive yourself. Use emergency transport or ask someone else to take you.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether this is PID, an STI, a UTI, a yeast infection, period pain, or another cause before seeking care.
- You do not need to contact current or past partners before you have been assessed.
- You do not need to make long-term decisions about testing, treatment, contraception, or relationships right now.
- You do not need to clean out the discharge or collect a sample at home.

## Important reassurance
These symptoms do not identify one condition by themselves. Prompt assessment is the useful next step, and you do not need to work out the diagnosis on your own.

## Scope note
These are first steps for getting prompt medical assessment and avoiding actions that could delay care. Later decisions may depend on the clinician’s findings and may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice, and it is not a diagnosis. Seek emergency care if symptoms are severe or rapidly worsening.
