# What to do if… you cough up blood or see blood in your mucus (USA)

## Short answer
Call **911** now if you are coughing up a lot of blood, the bleeding will not stop, you have severe shortness of breath, or you do not feel able to get to emergency care safely. Go to the **ER** now for more than a few teaspoons of blood or for chest pain, shortness of breath, dizziness, lightheadedness, or fever; call **911** instead if you need emergency assistance. Even if it was only a small streak and you otherwise feel stable, contact a healthcare professional promptly.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive yourself if you feel faint, dizzy, short of breath, or very unwell.
- Do not assume new or repeated blood-streaked mucus is only irritation.
- Do not change or stop prescribed medicines, including blood thinners, unless a healthcare professional tells you to.
- Do not delay care to test whether exercise or harder coughing makes the bleeding worse.
- Do not spend time trying to diagnose the cause yourself.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency signs.**  
   Call **911** now if:
   - you are coughing up a lot of blood
   - the bleeding will not stop
   - you have severe shortness of breath
   - you feel faint, rapidly worse, or unable to get to emergency care safely

   Go to the **ER** now, or call **911** instead if you need emergency assistance, if:
   - you have coughed up more than a few teaspoons of blood
   - you have chest pain, shortness of breath, dizziness, lightheadedness, or fever

2. **Sit down and note what you can see.**  
   Avoid exertion while you arrange care. If a tissue or container is already available, use it so you can tell the healthcare professional whether it was a streak, a larger amount, or repeated bleeding.

3. **Check whether the blood may be coming from somewhere else.**  
   Look for a nosebleed or bleeding from your gums or mouth. If you are vomiting blood, or the material looks dark and coffee-ground-like, go to the **ER** now or call **911** if you need emergency assistance.

4. **Contact care promptly even if it was a small streak.**  
   If you feel stable and the amount was small, contact your primary care office, urgent care, or another healthcare professional promptly. Go to the **ER** if the bleeding continues, increases, or you develop warning symptoms.

5. **Share the details that help with triage.**  
   Note when it started, whether it happened once or repeatedly, the estimated amount, and the color. Mention fever, chest pain, shortness of breath, dizziness, recent illness, a recent nosebleed, and any blood-thinner medicine.

6. **Bring your medication information if you are being seen.**  
   Bring or show a list of medicines, especially blood thinners, along with allergies and important medical conditions. Ask someone else to drive if you do not feel safe to drive yourself.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to work out the cause before contacting care.
- You do **not** need to decide which tests or treatments you might need.
- You do **not** need to finish work, chores, or errands before seeking help.

## Important reassurance
Seeing blood can feel frightening. A small streak can sometimes happen after forceful coughing, but new or unexplained blood still deserves prompt medical advice. Focus on the amount, any warning signs, and the next safe step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A healthcare professional may need to assess the cause and advise on any later tests or treatment.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice or a diagnosis. If the bleeding is continuing, increasing, or hard to judge, or if you are becoming unwell, call **911** or go to the **ER**.
