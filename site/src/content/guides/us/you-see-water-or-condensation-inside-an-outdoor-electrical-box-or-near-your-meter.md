# What to do if… you see water or condensation inside an outdoor electrical box or near your meter (USA)

## Short answer
Assume the meter area or outdoor electrical box may still be energized. Keep away, keep others away, and call your electric utility’s emergency or outage number to report water or condensation at the meter or service equipment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the meter box further, remove the meter, cut a seal, or break a utility tag.
- Do not touch the box, meter, conduit, cables, or wet wall surface around it.
- Do not stand in water or on wet ground to inspect, photograph, or switch anything.
- Do not try to dry the equipment with towels, heat, compressed air, sprays, or water-displacing products.
- Do not reset breakers repeatedly after rain. If a breaker trips, leave it off until the utility, fire department, or a qualified electrician says it is safe.
- Do not assume it is safe because it looks like “just condensation” or because the lights still work.

## What to do now
1. Back away and block access. Keep children and pets away. If the box is near a walkway, block the route so no one brushes against it.
2. Check only from a distance for emergency signs. If you see or hear sparking, arcing, crackling, buzzing, smoke, flames, or smell burning, call 911 and say there is an electrical hazard at the meter or service equipment.
3. Call your electric utility right away. Use the emergency, outage, bill, app, or website number and say: “There is water or condensation inside the outdoor electrical box near the electric meter after rain.”
4. Let the utility tell you what to leave alone. Ask whether they need to send a crew, shut off power, inspect the meter, or tell you to contact a qualified electrician for customer-side equipment.
5. Turn off power only if you can do it from a dry, safe place. If the main breaker or indoor main panel is reachable without entering standing water, standing on wet ground, or touching wet surfaces, switch the main breaker off. If you are unsure, do not attempt it.
6. Stop runoff only if you can do it without approaching the electrical equipment. For example, redirect a gutter drip with a bucket or move a loose downspout only if you can stay away from the meter, conduit, cables, and wet surfaces. Otherwise, leave it.
7. Take one photo or video from a safe distance. Do not move closer for a better angle.
8. Arrange a qualified electrician, licensed where required, after the utility or emergency responders say it is safe to work in the area. They can check weather seals, conduit entries, corrosion, breakers, panels, and any equipment that got wet.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the cause is a failed gasket, conduit leak, siding problem, roof runoff, or humidity.
- You do not need to decide who pays or which contractor fixes what until the utility confirms what must not be touched.
- You do not need to restore power immediately if there is any doubt about water, buzzing, burning smell, tripped breakers, or wet equipment.
- You do not need to keep rechecking the box while waiting; distance is safer.

## Important reassurance
This is a sensible thing to take seriously, and you do not need to solve it yourself. The safest first move is distance, reporting, and waiting for the utility or a qualified professional.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce shock and fire risk in the moment. Later decisions may need help from your electric utility, a qualified electrician, your local building department, your insurer, or a building repair specialist.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not electrical, legal, insurance, or professional advice. If there are signs of active electrical failure, fire, smoke, or a burning smell, call 911. Never tamper with utility meter equipment.
