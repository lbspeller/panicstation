# What to do if… you hear arguing next door that sounds like it may escalate and you are unsure whether to intervene (USA)

## Short answer
Do not physically intervene or announce your presence. Call 911 immediately if you hear sounds of violence, threats of harm, or a person calling for help.

## Do not do these things
- Do not knock on the neighbor's door, enter the hallway, or attempt to physically separate the individuals.
- Do not shout through the walls or announce "I am calling the police" as a warning to the neighbors.
- Do not record audio or video from your doorway or common areas to share on social media.
- Do not ignore the situation if you hear specific indicators of danger such as breaking glass, repeated thuds, or a child’s distress.
- Do not stand near shared walls or doors where you might be heard or easily identified as the observer.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safe internal location and secure your own premises.** **Go to a room furthest from the noise, lock your door, and keep your phone in your hand.** Avoid using the "peephole" or standing in shared hallways, which increases the risk of being seen if the parties leave their unit.
2. **Listen for specific "objectively reasonable" cues that indicate an immediate threat to life.** **Identify if you hear screams for help, sounds of physical impact, explicit death threats, or the mention of a weapon.** These specific details are required for police to establish an "objectively reasonable basis" for a warrantless entry under 2026 legal standards (Case v. Montana).
3. **Call 911 from a secure location if you believe violence is occurring.** **Provide the exact address, unit number, and the specific sounds you are hearing right now.** Tell the dispatcher if you believe there are children present or if you have seen a weapon in that unit previously.
4. **Use Text-to-911 only if speaking aloud would put you in direct physical danger.** **If you must text, send your exact location and the nature of the emergency first.** Be aware that text responses may be slower than voice calls and may not provide dispatchers with precise location data.
5. **Contact the local non-emergency line for "concerning but not active" disturbances.** **Request a welfare check if the situation sounds tense but does not involve active violence or immediate threats.** Use an official police department website to find the correct administrative number for your specific precinct.
6. **Report the disturbance to on-site security or building management only from a position of safety.** **Use your phone to contact building staff rather than seeking them out in person.** Ensure they understand you are requesting a safety check and not just making a noise complaint.
7. **Document the timeline of the event for your own records once you are safe.** **Write down the time the noise started, the duration, and any specific phrases you heard clearly.** This information provides an accurate record if you are interviewed as a witness later.
8. **Provide clear access instructions to emergency responders without exposing yourself.** **If your building has a gate code or complex layout, give these details to the 911 dispatcher.** Do not open your door to "flag down" officers unless they specifically request it and you feel safe doing so.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the legal nature of the relationship or identify who "started" the argument.
- You do not need to confront the neighbors the next day to ask for an explanation.
- You do not need to decide whether to provide a formal witness statement until you have had time to stabilize.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel hesitant or fearful of overreacting when hearing a disturbance. Choosing to call professional responders when you hear sounds of potential harm is a standard safety procedure, not an escalation. Your primary responsibility is to maintain your own safety while reporting observable facts.

## Scope note
This guide is for immediate stabilization and reporting of a perceived neighbor dispute. It does not cover long-term legal options, restraining orders, or property management disputes.

## Important note
This information is provided for emergency stabilization and is not legal advice. Reporting a suspected crime is a protected activity, but laws regarding privacy and police entry (such as the 2026 Case v. Montana ruling) vary by state. If you are unsure of the danger level, prioritize calling 911 and allow trained dispatchers to make the assessment.
