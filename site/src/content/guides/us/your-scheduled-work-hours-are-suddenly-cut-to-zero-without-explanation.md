# What to do if… your scheduled work hours are suddenly cut to zero without explanation (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible layoff or reduced-hours unemployment situation: get your status in writing and file for unemployment through the state where you worked as soon as you reasonably can.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume you are ineligible for unemployment just because you were not formally fired.
- Do not resign in anger; quitting can complicate unemployment eligibility.
- Do not do unpaid work while your hours and pay are at zero. Ask whether the work is paid before replying to work texts, emails, or tasks.
- Do not wait weeks for the schedule to fix itself before applying for benefits.
- Do not rely only on verbal promises such as “we will put you back on soon.”

## What to do now
1. **Save proof of the change today.** Screenshot the schedule showing zero hours, save texts and emails, and keep recent pay stubs showing your usual hours.
2. **Send one short written status question to your employer.** For example: “My schedule now shows zero hours. Am I still employed, laid off, furloughed, or expected to remain available for shifts? Is there an expected return date? Please also confirm what happens to my benefits if my hours stay at zero.”
3. **File for unemployment with the state where you worked.** Unemployment is run by states, and rules vary. Apply through the state unemployment agency even if your employer has not used the word “fired” or “laid off.”
4. **Use only official unemployment routes.** Start from your state unemployment agency, USA.gov, or the U.S. Department of Labor page if you are unsure which state page is correct.
5. **Ask whether a work-share or Short-Time Compensation plan exists.** Some states have work-sharing programs for reduced hours, but they usually require the employer to have an approved plan with the state. Ask HR or management whether your employer has one.
6. **If you are salaried and treated as exempt, pause before doing any work.** Salary-basis rules can mean different pay consequences depending on whether any work was performed in the workweek. Ask in writing whether you are being paid before answering work requests or doing tasks.
7. **Check health coverage now.** If the hour reduction may end employer health coverage, ask HR or benefits for the coverage end date and any COBRA notice or election paperwork.
8. **If the cut feels targeted or retaliatory, document it quietly.** Write down dates, who said what, and any recent wage complaints, safety concerns, discrimination concerns, leave requests, or other events that may matter later.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to sue, contact a lawyer, or file an agency complaint.
- You do not need to prove the real reason today; first preserve the schedule records, written status, unemployment filing, and benefits information.
- You do not need to negotiate a new schedule immediately if you do not have the facts yet.

## Important reassurance
A zero-hour schedule can feel like being erased overnight. Written status, a state unemployment filing, and a benefits check are practical first steps that can protect income options while you work out what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours and days. Later decisions may depend on your state, your pay type, your employment documents, any union or contract rights, and the reason your hours were cut.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Unemployment, wage rules, health coverage, and worker protections can vary by state and situation.
