# What to do if… you find a locked safe or strongbox and suspect vital documents are inside (USA)

## Short answer
Do not force it open or move it secretly. Secure it, document where it was found, and involve the executor, personal representative, or the person seeking court authority before any opening is arranged.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pry, drill, cut, or use heat; paper documents can be damaged or destroyed.
- Do not remove the safe or strongbox from the home without telling the person handling the estate.
- Do not keep trying guessed combinations; some safes can lock out or delay after failed attempts.
- Do not share suspected codes or keys widely.
- Do not assume being a relative gives you authority to open it.
- Do not sort, remove, or photograph private contents after opening unless the estate decision-maker has agreed how it will be recorded.

## What to do now
1. Stabilize the situation. Leave the safe or strongbox where it is if that is secure, or move it only as far as needed to prevent loss, theft, or accidental access.
2. Note who is present. Write down the date, time, room, and names of anyone who saw the safe before it was opened.
3. Photograph the outside before touching the lock. Include the safe in its surroundings, the lock, hinges, handle, model or serial number, and any signs of damage or tampering.
4. Collect nearby clues without rummaging for long. Look for a labeled key, key tag, envelope, receipt, owner’s manual, safe card, file folder, or written combination near the safe or in the usual important-papers area.
5. Pause if authority is unclear. If there is a will, tell the named executor. If there is no will, or nobody has been appointed, a person may need to ask the local probate court for authority as a personal representative or administrator.
6. If the safe must be opened, make it witnessed and documented. The executor, personal representative, or person with court authority should arrange the opening, ask the locksmith what proof of authority is required, request non-destructive entry where possible, and keep the invoice or job record.
7. Record the opening calmly. Have at least two adults present if possible, photograph the contents as first seen, make a simple inventory, and keep original documents flat, dry, and together.
8. If the safe looks already forced, contents appear missing, or conflict is escalating, stop. Preserve the safe as found and consider using the local police non-emergency route or a local estate attorney before anyone opens or removes anything.
9. If it is a bank safe-deposit box, do not try to bypass the bank. Ask the bank for its estate process; access usually depends on state law, bank policy, proof of death, and proof of authority.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today who gets any valuables, whether something is part of the estate, or how the whole probate process will work. The immediate job is only to prevent damage, loss, confusion, or accusations.

## Important reassurance
Finding a locked safe can make everything feel urgent, especially after a death. Slowing down, keeping witnesses involved, and making a simple record is usually the safest way to protect the documents and protect everyone from misunderstandings.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Probate authority, safe-deposit-box access, locksmith requirements, and estate procedures vary by state, so later decisions may need the local probate court, the bank’s estate department, legal aid, or an estate attorney.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If there is a dispute, suspected theft, unclear authority, or a large estate, preserve the safe unopened and get state-specific guidance.
