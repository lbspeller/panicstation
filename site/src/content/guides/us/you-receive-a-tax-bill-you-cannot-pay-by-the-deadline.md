# What to do if… you receive a tax bill you cannot pay by the deadline (USA)

## Short answer
Do not ignore the notice. If it is from the IRS, verify it through an official route, pay what you safely can, and request a payment plan or temporary collection delay as soon as possible. If it is from a state or local tax agency, use that agency’s official instructions instead.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice and hope it goes away; ignoring it can lead to more notices, added charges, or collection action.
- Do not pay someone who contacts you unexpectedly and demands gift cards, crypto, wire transfer, or immediate payment under threat of arrest.
- Do not use links or phone numbers from random texts, emails, or social media messages about “tax debt.”
- Do not skip filing a required return because you cannot pay; filing and paying are usually separate problems.
- Do not empty money needed for housing, food, utilities, medication, transport, or childcare before asking about payment plans or hardship options.
- Do not send original documents unless the notice specifically requires it.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm who sent the notice.**  
   Check the letterhead, tax year, account type, and notice or letter number. If it is not from the IRS, follow the named state or local tax agency’s official instructions.

2. **Verify the notice before paying.**  
   For an IRS notice, compare it with your IRS online account or use the phone number printed on the mailed notice. Do not trust caller ID, unsolicited links, or pressure from someone who contacted you first.

3. **Check whether the notice matches your records.**  
   Look at the tax year, amount due, payment deadline, and any payments you already made. Gather payment confirmations, canceled checks, bank records, return records, or letters from the IRS.

4. **Pay only what you can safely pay now.**  
   A partial payment can reduce the balance that continues to accrue charges, but do not use money needed for basic living costs. Save the receipt or confirmation with the notice.

5. **If it is an IRS balance, request a payment plan quickly.**  
   The IRS generally offers short-term payment plans for paying within 180 days or less, and long-term payment plans for monthly payments. Use the IRS online payment agreement application if you qualify, or call the number on the notice.

6. **If paying anything would stop you meeting basic living expenses, ask about hardship.**  
   The IRS may temporarily delay collection if it determines you cannot pay. Be ready with a simple list of income, rent or mortgage, utilities, food, medical costs, transport, childcare, and other essentials.

7. **If you think the amount is wrong, respond using the notice instructions.**  
   Keep it simple: say what you disagree with, include copies of proof, and keep a copy of everything you send. If the notice says you already missed a response date, still contact the agency promptly.

8. **Write down every contact.**  
   Keep one note with the date, time, phone number or website used, person or department reached, what was agreed, and any confirmation number.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to sell property, drain retirement savings, take a high-cost loan, or make another irreversible money move.
- You do not need to calculate penalties and interest perfectly right now.
- You do not need to fix every past tax issue in one sitting.
- You do not need to hire someone before you verify the notice and understand the first official options.

## Important reassurance
A tax balance due notice can feel urgent and frightening, but there are standard routes for people who cannot pay in full right away. The most stabilising first move is to verify the notice, keep a record, and engage with the official agency before the situation escalates.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about disputes, penalty relief, appeals, hardship status, or tax representation may need help from a qualified tax professional or a recognised taxpayer assistance service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, accounting, or professional advice. Options depend on the agency that sent the notice, the tax year, the amount owed, your filing status, and your finances.
