# What to do if… you feel food stuck in your throat and you can breathe but swallowing is difficult (USA)

## Short answer
Stop eating, sit upright, and do not try to force the food down. If you cannot swallow saliva, are drooling or spitting, have significant chest or neck pain, or swallowing is not quickly returning to normal, go to the nearest emergency department. If breathing becomes difficult, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep eating to “push it down”.
- Do not chug, force, or repeatedly try liquids to shift it.
- Do not induce vomiting.
- Do not put fingers, utensils, or tools into your throat.
- Do not lie down.
- Do not take more pills right now.
- Do not let anyone do abdominal thrusts if you can breathe, speak, and cough strongly; keep coughing and stay upright. If you cannot breathe, speak, or cough effectively, treat it as choking and call 911.

## What to do now
1. **Sit upright and pause.** Stop eating. Sit or stand upright, loosen anything tight around your neck or chest, and take steady breaths.
2. **Check for airway danger.** If you develop trouble breathing, noisy breathing, cannot speak, weak or no coughing, or blue, gray, or very pale lips or skin, call 911.
3. **Do a saliva check.** Try to swallow your saliva once. If you cannot swallow it, or you are drooling or spitting because it will not go down, go to the nearest emergency department now. Call 911 if you cannot get there safely.
4. **If you can swallow safely, try only small sips.** Take one or two small sips of still water. If that goes down safely, you may try a few small sips of sparkling water or another carbonated drink. Stop if you cough, choke, have significant pain, the liquid comes back up, or the stuck feeling gets worse.
5. **Do not keep testing it.** If swallowing remains difficult, the food feels stuck, or symptoms are not clearly improving after a brief safe pause, go to the emergency department. Urgent care may not be equipped to assess or remove food stuck in the esophagus.
6. **Go to the emergency department immediately** if you have chest or neck pain, severe pain when swallowing, repeated vomiting or retching, blood in saliva or vomit, fever, faintness, or weakness.
7. **If this has happened before, arrange follow-up after the urgent moment is over.** Repeated food-sticking episodes can be linked to narrowing, inflammation, reflux-related injury, eosinophilic esophagitis, rings, or movement problems in the esophagus, and a clinician can help decide what evaluation is needed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to try different foods, bread, rice, pills, or large drinks.
- You do not need to decide today what tests or treatments you might need later.
- You do not need to drive yourself if you feel faint, weak, or unable to manage saliva.

## Important reassurance
This can feel frightening even when you are still breathing. Food can get stuck in the esophagus rather than the airway, and emergency departments can assess this and arrange removal if needed. Getting help promptly when you cannot swallow normally is a reasonable safety-first choice.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation only. Later decisions, including whether you need endoscopy or specialist follow-up, may need help from an emergency clinician, primary care clinician, or gastroenterologist.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice for your specific situation. If you are drooling, unable to swallow normally, in significant pain, worsening, or unsure, seek urgent medical care right away.
