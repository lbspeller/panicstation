# What to do if… a partner keeps waking you up with sexual demands and you want to stay safe (USA)

## Short answer
Create physical distance immediately by moving to a separate room with a lock or leaving for a safe location. If you are in immediate danger or being prevented from leaving, call or text 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel obligated to "give in" to sexual demands to make the situation end or to avoid an argument.
- Do not attempt to have a deep conversation about boundaries or the relationship while the partner is actively making demands or if you feel unsafe.
- Do not take sedative medications or alcohol that may make it harder for you to wake up or respond to your environment tonight.
- Do not leave your phone, keys, or ID in a place where your partner can take or hide them.
- Do not assume you must decide right now whether to report the situation to the police or end the relationship.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a location where you can secure a door between yourself and your partner.** If you can, go to a room with a lock (like a bathroom or spare bedroom) or leave the home entirely to stay with a trusted friend, family member, or in a hotel.
   - Bring your phone, charger, keys, wallet, and any essential medications with you.
   - If staying in the home, use a door wedge or heavy furniture to block the door if there is no lock.
2. **Contact emergency services if you feel you cannot safely leave or if violence is occurring.** Call **911** or use **Text-to-911** if your local area supports it and you cannot speak safely.
3. **Keep your phone on your person at all times, even if you are just moving between rooms.** Ensure your phone is charged and that you have a "help signal" or code word ready to text a trusted contact if the situation escalates.
4. **Use private or "incognito" browsing modes if you are searching for resources on a shared device.** Most advocacy websites, such as The Hotline or RAINN, have a "Quick Escape" or "Safety Exit" button that allows you to close the page immediately if your partner approaches.
5. **Briefly note the time and what occurred in a secure location.** Use a password-protected note app or a draft in a new, private email account to record what was said or done; this offloads the burden of remembering and creates a timestamped record.
6. **Connect with a confidential advocate for immediate safety planning.** Contact the **National Domestic Violence Hotline** at **800-799-7233** or text "START" to **88788** to discuss safe ways to leave or secure your environment tonight.
7. **Seek medical care at an emergency department if you have physical pain, injuries, or concerns about sexual assault.** You can ask for a **SANE (Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner)** or a **SART (Sexual Assault Response Team)**.
   - In the USA, you can receive a medical forensic exam without being required to file a police report under the Violence Against Women Act (VAWA).
   - Ask the provider about "mandatory reporting" rules in your state before disclosing details, as some states require reports for certain physical injuries.
8. **Contact RAINN for specialized sexual violence support.** Call **800-656-HOPE** or use their online chat to find local resources that offer support without requiring you to involve law enforcement.

## What can wait
- You do not need to label the situation as "abuse" or "assault" right now to deserve safety and help.
- You do not need to decide on long-term housing or relationship changes tonight.
- You do not need to justify your "no" or explain why you are leaving the room or the house.
- You do not need to gather evidence or prepare a legal case in this moment.

## Important reassurance
Waking someone up for sex without their prior, enthusiastic consent is a violation of boundaries. Feeling frozen, "going along with it" to avoid conflict, or feeling confused are very common survival responses. Your primary job right now is to reach a state of physical safety so you can rest.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm reduction. Long-term safety planning and legal options should be discussed with a certified domestic violence or sexual assault advocate at your own pace.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal or medical advice. Mandatory reporting laws for healthcare providers vary significantly by state and typically apply to child abuse, elder abuse, or specific physical injuries like those from weapons. Always prioritize your immediate physical safety and call 911 if you are in danger.
