# What to do if… your messaging app says your account is being registered on another device (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible account-takeover attempt unless you started it: do not share any verification code, use only the app’s official sign-in flow, and contact your mobile carrier if your phone service or transfer alerts look wrong.

## Do not do these things
- Do not give anyone an SMS, voice, email, or in-app verification code.
- Do not approve a login, registration, or device-transfer prompt you did not start.
- Do not use support links, callback numbers, or “security team” contacts sent by text, email, or direct message.
- Do not ignore sudden loss of calls, texts, or mobile data.
- Do not wipe your phone in a panic; that usually will not stop a number transfer and may make recovery harder.
- Do not argue with anyone asking for codes, money, or “urgent verification.”

## What to do now
1. **Check whether you caused the alert.**  
   Think only about the last few minutes: a new phone, reinstall, SIM or eSIM change, app transfer, or sign-in attempt could explain it. If you did not start it, treat it as suspicious.

2. **Use the official app only.**  
   Open the app from your phone screen, not from a link in a message. Start the normal sign-in or recovery flow for your number or account.

3. **Use only a code you requested inside the app.**  
   If a person, message, or “support” account asks you to read back a code, stop replying. A real recovery step should not require you to share the code with another person.

4. **Remove unknown linked devices or sessions.**  
   In the app settings, look for names such as linked devices, devices, active sessions, web sessions, or logged-in devices. Log out anything you do not recognise.

5. **Turn on the app’s extra registration protection.**  
   Look for two-step verification, PIN, registration lock, or similar wording. Save the PIN somewhere safe so you do not lock yourself out later.

6. **If calls or texts suddenly stop working, contact your carrier from another phone or official app.**  
   Say: “I think my number may be involved in SIM swap or port-out fraud. Please check for any SIM, eSIM, or number-transfer request and help secure the account.” Ask about an account passcode, transfer PIN, port-out protection, or any similar protection your carrier offers.

7. **Protect money-related accounts.**  
   Open your bank, credit card, payment, and email accounts using official apps or bookmarked sites. Change passwords where needed, sign out unknown sessions, and contact the institution directly if you see anything you did not do.

8. **Secure your email account.**  
   Change the password if you think it may be exposed. Check recovery phone numbers, recovery email addresses, signed-in devices, and any mail forwarding rules.

9. **Warn a few close contacts through another channel.**  
   Send a simple message such as: “My messaging account may be compromised. Ignore requests for money or codes from me for now.”

10. **Start a report trail if fraud or identity theft may be involved.**  
   Use IdentityTheft.gov for identity-theft recovery steps. Use ReportFraud.ftc.gov for scam or fraud reports. If money was stolen through an internet-enabled fraud, IC3 is the FBI’s reporting route. Ask affected banks or companies whether they need a police report for their process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to change your phone number.
- You do not need to rebuild your whole security setup today.
- You do not need to message everyone in your contacts at once.
- You do not need to prove who did it before securing the app, carrier account, email, and money-related accounts.

## Important reassurance
This alert can feel alarming, but the first steps are clear: keep codes private, use official recovery routes, remove unfamiliar sessions, and secure the phone number if service looks wrong. Acting calmly is enough for this stage.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and reduce immediate harm. Later decisions, especially after money loss, identity theft, or repeated account access, may need help from the app provider, carrier, bank, law enforcement, or another specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or other professional advice. Use official contact routes for your app, carrier, bank, and reporting agencies.
