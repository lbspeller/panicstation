# What to do if… you receive legal papers for someone else at your address and you are worried it could affect you (USA)

## Short answer
Do not accept or sign for the papers, return them immediately to the sender or the court marked as "Not at this address," and strike through any automated barcodes on the envelope to prevent re-delivery.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open mail clearly addressed to someone else.
- Do not throw away, destroy, or hide the papers; this can lead to legal complications or federal mail interference charges.
- Do not sign any "Acknowledgment of Service" or receipt on behalf of the named person.
- Do not tell a process server or official that the person "might" be there or "lives here sometimes" if they do not currently reside at the address.
- Do not pay any money or provide your own Social Security number or financial details to anyone listed on the documents.
- Do not ignore papers left at your door; an "unclaimed" service can sometimes result in a default judgment that affects your physical property (e.g., in eviction cases).

## What to do now
1. **Check the names on the documents carefully.** Ensure the papers do not list "John Doe," "Jane Doe," or "All Other Occupants," which could legally include you in an eviction or property-related action.
   - If your name or a variation of it is listed, do not return the papers; seek legal advice immediately.
2. **Handle misdelivered mail through the USPS.** Write **"Not at this address - Return to Sender"** clearly on the front of the envelope.
   - **Strike through the automated barcode** (the series of small vertical lines) at the bottom or back of the envelope with a permanent marker to prevent sorting machines from sending it back to you.
   - Hand the mail to your carrier or place it in a blue USPS collection box.
3. **Handle papers delivered in person or left at the door.** If a process server or sheriff’s deputy is present, state clearly: "That person does not reside at this address."
   - Do not allow them inside your home.
   - If papers are left at the door (substituted service) or taped to the property, do not discard them. 
   - Note the name of the process server or the agency on the documents.
4. **Contact the issuing court clerk.** If the documents have a court seal and case number, find the court's official website or phone number independently.
   - **Call the clerk of the court** and state: "I have received service papers for [Name] at my address. This person does not live here, and I have no contact information for them."
   - Ask the clerk if they can note the incorrect address in the file to prevent further service attempts at your home.
5. **Report suspected fraud or harassment.** If you receive repeated legal mail for many different names or suspect your address is being used for a scam:
   - File a report with the **U.S. Postal Inspection Service (USPIS)** for mail fraud or address misuse.
   - Keep a log of all dates, names, and case numbers associated with the misdelivered papers.
6. **Reseal accidentally opened mail.** If you opened the envelope before realizing the name was wrong, tape it shut.
   - Write **"Opened in Error - Not at this Address"** on the envelope before returning it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to hire a lawyer unless your own name or "All Other Occupants" is listed on the papers.
- You do not need to track down the person named on the documents or attempt to forward the mail to them yourself.
- You do not need to worry about the specific legal allegations in the case, as they do not apply to you if you are not a party.

## Important reassurance
Receiving legal papers for a stranger or former tenant is common and is usually the result of outdated records or a process server's error. Taking the steps to return the documents and notify the court protects you from being mistaken for the intended party and ensures the legal system recognizes the address is invalid for that person.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for receiving misdirected legal papers. It does not cover situations where you are a named defendant, even if the information about you is incorrect.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Service of process rules vary significantly by state and type of law (e.g., civil vs. criminal). If an eviction notice or foreclosure document is posted on your door, consult a local legal aid society or attorney immediately to ensure your residency rights are protected.
