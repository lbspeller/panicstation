# What to do if… your blood sugar reading is suddenly very high and you feel sick (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a medical emergency: check for ketones immediately and call 911 or go to the nearest Emergency Room if you are vomiting, breathing rapidly, or feel confused. High glucose combined with physical illness can lead to rapid-onset life-threatening complications.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to exercise to lower blood sugar if you feel sick or have ketones present.
- Do not take a self-calculated extra dose of insulin unless you are following a pre-written clinician sick-day plan.
- Do not stop taking your basal (long-acting) insulin even if you are not eating.
- Do not drive yourself to a medical facility if you feel weak, dizzy, sleepy, or have blurred vision.
- Do not rely on a Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM) reading alone; always confirm a high reading with a fingerstick test when sick.
- Do not wait for a call back from your doctor if you are actively vomiting or cannot keep fluids down.
- Do not continue taking SGLT2 inhibitor medications (e.g., Jardiance, Farxiga, Invokana, Steglatro) while you are acutely ill with nausea or vomiting.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 or go to the Emergency Room immediately if you have emergency red flags.** Seek immediate emergency care for the following:
   - Repeated vomiting or inability to keep liquids down for more than 2 hours.
   - Deep, rapid, or labored breathing (Kussmaul breathing).
   - Breath that smells fruity or like nail polish remover (acetone).
   - Moderate or high ketones on a home test.
   - Confusion, extreme drowsiness, or loss of consciousness.
   - Severe abdominal pain.
2. **Perform a fingerstick blood glucose test and a ketone test immediately.** Use a standard glucometer to confirm the reading and use a urine strip or blood ketone meter to check for ketones.
   - If your blood sugar is 250 mg/dL or higher, you must check for ketones.
   - If ketones are "moderate" or "large" (on urine strips) or above 1.5 mmol/L (on blood meters), go to the ER.
3. **Stop your SGLT2 inhibitor medication for the duration of this illness if you take one.** If you take medications like empagliflozin, canagliflozin, or dapagliflozin, stop them now and do not restart until you have spoken with a clinician and are eating/drinking normally.
4. **Follow your specific, clinician-provided sick-day protocol if you have one.** Only take supplemental insulin doses according to the exact "correction factor" or "sick-day rules" your doctor has previously put in writing for you.
   - If you do not have a written plan, do not guess; contact your doctor's on-call service or go to the ER.
5. **Check your insulin delivery equipment if you use an insulin pump.** If your sugar is high and you feel sick, assume the pump may not be delivering insulin.
   - Check for kinks in the tubing, a dislodged infusion set, or an empty reservoir.
   - If you suspect pump failure, give a manual injection of fast-acting insulin according to your sick-day plan and contact your doctor.
6. **Drink 4 to 6 ounces of sugar-free fluid every 30 minutes to prevent dehydration.** Sip water or broth slowly. If your blood sugar is high but you cannot keep fluids down, you need intravenous (IV) fluids at a hospital.
7. **Document your readings, symptoms, and the time of your last insulin dose.** Note the exact time and result of your glucose and ketone tests to provide to emergency medical staff.
8. **Ask someone to stay with you or check on you every 30 minutes.** High blood sugar complications can cause sudden confusion or fainting; do not remain alone while waiting for symptoms to improve.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine why the blood sugar rose (e.g., infection, missed dose) until you are medically stabilized.
- You do not need to adjust your long-term medication dosages today.
- You do not need to worry about your long-term A1C or diet while in an acute crisis.

## Important reassurance
It is a normal physiological response to feel significant thirst, fatigue, or anxiety when your blood sugar is dangerously high. These symptoms are your body's way of signaling that it needs external assistance to regain balance. Taking the step to test ketones and seek help now is the standard protocol for managing this situation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for acute hyperglycemia with illness in the USA. It does not replace a clinical sick-day plan or professional medical diagnosis.

## Important note
High blood sugar with ketones or vomiting can lead to Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) or Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS), both of which are life-threatening without hospital treatment. If you are pregnant, have Type 1 diabetes, or take an SGLT2 inhibitor, the risk of rapid escalation is significantly higher.
