# What to do if… your indicators or turn signals stop working and you notice before setting off (USA)

## Short answer
Do not drive the vehicle for a standard trip if the turn signals are non-functional. If you must move the vehicle to a repair facility, you are legally required in all US states to use hand-and-arm signals to communicate every turn and lane change.

## Do not do these things
- Don't assume other drivers can guess your intentions without a signal.
- Don't use hazard lights as a substitute for turn signals while the vehicle is in motion.
- Don't drive at night, in heavy rain, or in fog if you must rely on hand signals.
- Don't use high-speed freeways or complex multi-lane roads.
- Don't attempt to troubleshoot wiring or relays while the vehicle is moving.

## What to do now
1. **Cancel or delay any non-essential travel immediately.** **Driving without functional signal lights increases collision risk and is a citable traffic offense.** 
2. **Verify exactly which lights are affected while the vehicle is parked.** 
   - Test the left signal, right signal, and hazard lights individually.
   - Walk around the exterior to see if the failure is total (both sides), partial (one side), or specific to the front or rear.
   - Note if the dashboard indicators flash rapidly, which often indicates a single burnt-out bulb.
3. **Check the fuse box if all turn signals have failed simultaneously.** 
   - Consult the vehicle manual to locate the "Turn Signal" or "Directional" fuse.
   - Replace a blown fuse only with one of the exact same amperage rating.
4. **Arrange for a mobile mechanic or professional repair before moving the vehicle.** 
   - Many turn signal issues are caused by inexpensive bulbs or relays that can be replaced on-site.
   - Roadside assistance plans may cover a tow to the nearest repair shop for equipment failure.
5. **Use standard hand-and-arm signals if you must drive the vehicle to a shop during daylight.** 
   - Extend your left arm out of the driver-side window for all signals.
   - **Left turn:** Extend the arm straight out horizontally.
   - **Right turn:** Bend the arm upward at a 90-degree angle with the palm facing forward.
   - **Slow or stop:** Bend the arm downward at a 90-degree angle with the palm facing toward the rear.
6. **Initiate all hand signals at least 100 feet before beginning a maneuver.** 
   - In some states, such as Indiana or for high-speed turns, 200 feet may be required.
   - Maintain the signal long enough for following drivers to see it, then return your hand to the wheel to complete the turn.
7. **Exercise extreme caution when signaling for a right-hand turn or lane change.** 
   - Drivers positioned to your right cannot see your left-arm hand signals.
   - Verify the blind spot is clear and that traffic is not attempting to pass you on the right before moving.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the failure is in the flasher relay or the multifunction switch right now.
- You do not need to compare different state statutes if you follow the universal 100-foot signaling rule.
- You do not need to worry about long-term electrical repairs until the immediate trip to the shop is handled.

## Important reassurance
Noticing a signal failure while parked is the safest time to find it. While it is frustrating to delay a trip, choosing not to drive with faulty safety equipment prevents a predictable hazard. Hand signals are a legal and recognized backup, though they require more physical effort and focus than usual.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. It focuses on immediate safety and the legal requirement to communicate intent. Technical diagnostics should be performed by a qualified mechanic.

## Important note
This guide provides general safety information and is not a substitute for legal advice or professional mechanical repair. Motor vehicle laws vary by state, but all states require functional signaling devices or hand signals. Driving with non-functional safety equipment can result in traffic citations and increased liability in the event of an accident.
