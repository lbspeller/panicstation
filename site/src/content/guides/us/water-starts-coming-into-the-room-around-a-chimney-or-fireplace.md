# What to do if… water starts coming into the room around a chimney or fireplace (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the area from electrical hazards and stop using any appliances connected to the chimney immediately. Avoid touching wet surfaces near outlets or switches and contain the water with buckets or towels if safe to do so.

## Do not do these things
- Do not touch any electrical outlets, switches, or power cords if they are wet or near the intrusion site.
- Do not stand in pooled water while attempting to operate a circuit breaker or light switch.
- Do not light a fire or use a gas fireplace to "dry out" the masonry or flue.
- Do not climb onto the roof during rain, high winds, or without specialized fall-arrest equipment.
- Do not attempt to seal the leak from the inside with caulk or spray foam, as this can trap water within the wall structure.

## What to do now
1. **Evacuate the immediate area and put on rubber-soled shoes.** Move people and pets away from the wet wall or fireplace to prevent accidental contact with potential electrical shorts or falling debris.
2. **Shut off power to the affected room only if the breaker panel and the floor beneath it are completely dry.** If you must stand in water to reach the panel, or if the panel itself is damp, do not touch it; call your local electric utility or an emergency electrician immediately.
3. **Deactivate all appliances that vent through the chimney.**
   - Turn off the gas supply valve to gas logs or inserts if a leak is suspected near the flue.
   - If a carbon monoxide (CO) alarm sounds or you smell gas, exit the building immediately and call **911** from outside.
4. **Place containers to catch dripping water.** Use buckets, plastic bins, or heavy towels to manage the liquid and prevent it from spreading toward floor-level outlets or under baseboards.
5. **Protect the floor and furniture from further damage.** Use plastic sheeting or heavy-duty trash bags to channel water running down a wall into a container, ensuring the plastic does not touch hot fireplace components.
6. **Observe the ceiling and walls for signs of imminent collapse.** Stay clear of areas where the drywall is noticeably sagging, soft, or severely discolored.
7. **Document the intrusion for insurance and repair purposes.** Take clear photos or video of the water entry points, damaged drywall, and any pooling on the floor before moisture is cleaned up.
8. **Contact a certified professional for a formal assessment.**
   - If you **rent**, notify the landlord or property management office immediately.
   - If you **own**, contact a CSIA-certified chimney sweep or a licensed roofer to inspect for failed flashing, damaged masonry, or a missing chimney cap.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific source of the leak (such as cracked crown vs. flashing) before calling for professional help.
- You do not need to begin structural repairs or permanent cosmetic fixes while the water is still actively entering.
- You do not need to run high-powered dehumidifiers or industrial fans until an electrician confirms the wiring in the wet area is safe to use.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when water enters your living space, but taking immediate steps to isolate electrical risks and stop appliance use prevents the situation from escalating. Most chimney leaks are manageable once the weather clears and a professional can access the roof.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization only. Water intrusion can damage chimney liners and interior wiring; a professional inspection is required before the fireplace or electrical circuits in the room are used again.

## Important note
This information is for general safety and does not replace a professional inspection. If you suspect structural failure, gas leaks, or electrical arcing, contact emergency services (911) or your local utility provider immediately.
