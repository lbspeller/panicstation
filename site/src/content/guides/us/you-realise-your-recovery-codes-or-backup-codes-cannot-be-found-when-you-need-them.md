# What to do if… you realise your recovery codes or backup codes cannot be found when you need them (USA)

## Short answer
Stop attempting logins to avoid a permanent lockout, then use a currently signed-in device or the provider's official "Account Recovery" path to verify your identity through alternative methods like a trusted phone number or government ID.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay any individual or service claiming they can "hack" or "recover" your account; these are scams.
- Do not click links in "security alert" emails that arrived recently, as they may be phishing attempts.
- Do not repeatedly guess your password or codes, as this often triggers a 24-to-72-hour security cooldown.
- Do not share any one-time SMS or app codes with anyone, even if they claim to be "support."
- Do not factory reset your phone or log out of existing sessions on other computers until access is restored.

## What to do now
1. **Identify any device where you are already signed in.**
   - Check your laptop, tablet, old phone, or other browser profiles.
   - If you have an active session, navigate immediately to **Security Settings** or **Sign-in & Security**.
   - Generate a **new set of backup codes** and save them physically or in an encrypted password manager.

2. **Check the three most likely digital and physical "safe" locations for the codes.**
   - Search your email (including Trash/Archive) for terms like "Backup Codes," "Recovery," or "Security."
   - Check your "Downloads" folder or "Screenshots" for files named `Backup-codes-account.txt` or similar.
   - Check physical notebooks, desk drawers, or safes where you store important documents.

3. **Initiate the official "Try Another Way" recovery flow.**
   - Go to the official sign-in page (e.g., accounts.google.com, appleid.apple.com, or microsoft.com).
   - Select **"Try another way"** or **"I don't have my codes."**
   - Look for alternative verification methods:
     - Trusted phone number (SMS/Voice).
     - Backup email address.
     - Trusted device prompt (a "Yes/No" tap on another device).
     - Passkeys (using FaceID, TouchID, or Windows Hello).

4. **Prepare for identity verification if automated methods fail.**
   - If prompted for "Account Recovery," be ready to provide a valid photo ID (Driver's License or Passport).
   - Use a device and Wi-Fi network you have used frequently in the past to improve the provider's automated trust score.
   - Follow the prompts exactly; Apple and Google may require a waiting period (often 3–7 days) before access is granted.

5. **Secure financial accounts if they are linked to the locked account.**
   - If the locked account is used for banking or has credit cards saved, call the bank's official support line.
   - Request a **"Hold Harmless Letter"** or **"Letter of Indemnity"** if you suspect the codes were stolen.
   - Ask the bank to place a temporary fraud alert on your accounts.

6. **Report the incident if you suspect an "Account Takeover" (ATO).**
   - File a report with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center at **ic3.gov**.
   - **Important:** Use the specific phrase **"Account Takeover"** in the incident description.
   - If your personal data (SSN, ID) was involved, visit **IdentityTheft.gov** to create a personalized recovery plan.

## What can wait
- You do not need to delete the account or start over immediately.
- You do not need to notify everyone in your contact list unless you see evidence of messages being sent from your account.
- You do not need to change your hardware (phone/computer) unless the device itself was stolen.

## Important reassurance
Losing recovery codes is a common technical issue and service providers have built-in verification workflows specifically for this scenario. While the process may take several days of "security waiting," sticking to official channels is the only way to regain access without compromising your data further.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and account recovery steps. It does not provide long-term security auditing or forensic investigation of compromised devices.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and is not a substitute for the official recovery policies of your service provider. Federal reporting via IC3 or IdentityTheft.gov is recommended if you suspect criminal activity. Always verify you are on an official `.gov` or official corporate website before entering sensitive information.
