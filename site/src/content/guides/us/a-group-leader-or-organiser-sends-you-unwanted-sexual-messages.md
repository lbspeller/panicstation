# What to do if… a group leader or organiser sends you unwanted sexual messages (USA)
## Short answer

Take this seriously as a boundary and safety concern. You can stop the private conversation, move away from one-to-one contact, and tell one trusted person outside the leader’s influence.

## Do not do these things

- Do not feel you have to reply sexually, send images, or play along to keep your place in the group.
- Do not meet them alone to explain yourself or settle the situation.
- Do not move the conversation to disappearing messages or another private app.
- Do not feel you have to decide today whether to leave the group, make a complaint, or report anything.
- Do not blame yourself because the person is admired, influential, older, or trusted by others.

## What to do now

1. **Create a safer contact boundary.**  
   Stop replying, mute the conversation, limit their access, or send one short message such as: “Please keep messages about the group only.” Choose the option that feels safest.

2. **Tell one person outside the leader’s influence.**  
   Contact a trusted friend, family member, advocate, or unrelated support person. Tell them that the private messages have become sexual and that you want support reducing contact.

3. **Avoid one-to-one situations for now.**  
   Do not meet the leader alone. Arrange another ride, another place to stay, or another point of contact if the leader currently controls access to group activities.

4. **Keep a copy only if you choose.**  
   If it feels safe and you may want the messages later, save copies before deleting or blocking.

5. **Use a non-private group contact only if that helps.**  
   You can ask a separate organizer, coordinator, board contact, or safety contact to move essential group communication away from private messages. You do not have to make a formal complaint to ask for this.

6. **Use specialist support if that feels useful.**  
   If the messages feel connected to unwanted sexual pressure, sexual abuse, or a wider safety concern, RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline is available 24/7. Call 800.656.HOPE (4673), use the online chat, or text HOPE to 64673.

7. **Use urgent help if the situation becomes dangerous.**  
   If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If you are under 18 or worried about a child, contact a trusted adult or Childhelp for support with the next safe step. Childhelp counselors are available 24/7 by live chat, or by calling or texting 800.422.4453.

## What can wait

You do not need to decide right now whether to stay in the group, leave permanently, confront the leader, tell the whole group, make a complaint, or contact police. You also do not need a perfect label for what happened before asking for support.

## Important reassurance

You are allowed to step back from private contact without defending or explaining your decision. Freezing, feeling unsure, or worrying about the group does not mean you agreed to sexual messages or have to manage the situation alone.

## Scope note

These are first steps only, focused on reducing pressure and buying time. Later decisions about support, complaints, reporting, or continued involvement may need specialist help.

## Important note

This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Choose the steps that feel safest in your situation.
