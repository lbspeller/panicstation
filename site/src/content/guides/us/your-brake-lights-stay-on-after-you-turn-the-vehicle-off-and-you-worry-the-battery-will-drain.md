# What to do if… your brake lights stay on after you turn the vehicle off and you worry the battery will drain (USA)

## Short answer
Get the vehicle to a safe parked position, then stop the drain only if you can do it safely: disconnect the negative battery cable, or pull the correct brake-light fuse if you can identify it from the vehicle fuse diagram. Do not drive again until the brake lights work normally or a qualified roadside technician or mechanic says it is safe.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t disconnect the positive battery terminal first.
- Don’t let a metal tool touch both battery terminals or the positive terminal and vehicle metal at the same time.
- Don’t leave the vehicle with the brake lights on if you need the battery later.
- Don’t drive with brake lights stuck on or with the brake-light fuse removed.
- Don’t keep stomping the brake pedal or cycling the ignition to “test” it.
- Don’t pull random fuses if you cannot clearly identify the brake-light fuse.
- Don’t work under the dash, in traffic, or in poor visibility if it does not feel safe.

## What to do now
1. Get to a safer pause. If you are in traffic or on a risky shoulder, move to a parking lot, driveway, wide shoulder, or other safer place if you can do that without creating more danger. Turn on hazard flashers if the vehicle is stopped where other drivers need warning.
2. Secure the vehicle. Put it in park, or in gear if it is a manual, set the parking brake, turn the ignition fully off, and remove the key or keep the key fob away from the start button area.
3. Try the quick checks that do not involve tools:
   - Pull the brake pedal gently upward with your foot or hand in case it is not returning fully.
   - Check whether a floor mat, object, or trim piece is pressing the brake pedal.
   - Look at the pedal area for a loose small rubber bumper or broken plastic piece on the floor; do not crawl under the dash if the vehicle is not safely parked.
4. If the brake lights are still on and you can safely access the battery, disconnect the negative battery cable:
   - Check the owner’s manual first if the battery is not obvious or the vehicle has special battery instructions.
   - Identify the negative terminal by the minus sign and, usually, a black cable.
   - Loosen the negative clamp, remove it from the terminal, and secure the cable so it cannot spring back onto the terminal.
   - Expect small resets, such as clock, radio, or window settings. If anything seems unusual, stop and use roadside assistance.
5. If battery access is unsafe or unclear, use roadside assistance rather than forcing it. Say: “The brake lights stay on with the ignition off, and I need help preventing battery drain.”
6. If you can clearly identify the brake-light fuse from the fuse box diagram, you may pull that fuse as a parked-only temporary step:
   - Use only the fuse marked for brake lights, stop lamps, or the name shown in your vehicle manual.
   - Put the fuse somewhere safe.
   - Do not drive with that fuse removed.
7. Check for an open safety recall once the immediate drain is controlled:
   - Find the VIN on the lower driver-side windshield, registration, or insurance document.
   - Use the official NHTSA recall lookup.
   - If an open recall appears, follow the manufacturer or dealer instructions.
8. If the vehicle is stopped somewhere unsafe and you cannot make the situation safe, call 911. Be ready to give your location, the vehicle description, and that you are stopped with a lighting or electrical problem.

## What can wait
- You do not need to know tonight whether the cause is the brake-light switch, pedal bumper, wiring, fuse box, or body control module.
- You do not need to buy parts now.
- You do not need to decide whether to keep, sell, or replace the vehicle.
- You do not need to keep testing the lights once you have stopped the drain.

## Important reassurance
This is stressful because a battery can drain while the vehicle is parked, but the immediate problem is usually stabilized by safely stopping power to the brake-light circuit or getting roadside help. Once the lights are off or the battery is disconnected, you have bought time to arrange repair.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A qualified mechanic or roadside technician may still need to inspect the brake pedal switch, pedal return, fuse circuit, wiring, or vehicle-specific battery system before the vehicle is driven.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional mechanical, legal, electrical, or roadside-safety advice. Follow your owner’s manual and use roadside assistance if you are unsure, cannot safely access the battery or fuse box, or are stopped in a dangerous place.
