# What to do if… police contact you saying they want to “take a quick look” at your home as part of an inquiry (USA)

## Short answer
Do not allow entry without a valid search warrant signed by a judge. State clearly and calmly, "I do not consent to a search," and keep the door closed or step outside and close the door behind you.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door wide or stand back, as officers may interpret this as "implied consent" to enter.
- Do not talk to officers through an open door; anything they see or smell from the threshold (plain view) may give them legal grounds to enter or seize items.
- Do not consent to a "quick look" to appear cooperative; once consent is given, it is difficult to limit the scope of the search.
- Do not physically block or struggle with officers if they force entry; state your non-consent loudly and move out of the way.
- Do not sign any "Consent to Search" forms without a lawyer present.

## What to do now
1. **Speak through the door or step outside and close it behind you.** This prevents the "Plain View Doctrine" from applying, where officers can seize evidence they see from a legal standing point.
2. **Request identification from all officers present.** Ask for names, badge numbers, and their specific department or agency.
   - If the contact is via phone, ask for a name and extension, then hang up and call the agency's verified public number to confirm the inquiry is legitimate.
3. **Ask if they have a warrant.** If they say they have one, ask them to slide it under the door or hold it up to a window so you can read it.
4. **Verify the details of the warrant before opening the door.** Check that the document is:
   - Signed by a judge or magistrate.
   - Correctly addressed to your specific home (including apartment number).
   - Currently valid (check the date and time window).
   - Specific about the areas to be searched and items to be seized.
5. **State your non-consent clearly if there is no warrant.** Use the exact phrase: "I do not consent to a search of my home or a search of my person." 
   - You are not required to provide a reason for refusing consent.
6. **Identify the type of warrant if one exists.** A "Search Warrant" allows entry to look for evidence, while an "Arrest Warrant" only allows entry if police reasonably believe the person named is currently inside.
7. **Document the interaction if they enter anyway.** Do not interfere, but use a phone or notepad to record:
   - The time entry was made and the names of the officers.
   - Which specific rooms or containers were searched.
   - Any items that were handled or removed from the premises.
8. **Ask for an inventory receipt if items are seized.** By law, officers must provide a list of everything taken from the property during a search.
9. **Remain silent if questioned during a search.** If you are detained or the situation becomes custodial, say: "I am going to remain silent and I want a lawyer."

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain why you are refusing consent.
- You do not need to provide an alibi or answer questions about an investigation at the door.
- You do not need to decide whether to file a formal complaint or lawsuit while the police are still present.
- You do not need to determine if an "emergency exception" was valid in the moment; that is a matter for a court later.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard police tactic to ask for a "quick look" because it bypasses the need for probable cause and a judge's oversight. Refusing a search is a constitutional right, not a sign of guilt. Feeling intimidated or pressured is a common reaction to authority, but you have the legal power to maintain your privacy boundary.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate first-contact actions at the door or via phone. It does not cover long-term legal defense, suppression hearings, or civil rights litigation.

## Important note
The Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches, but "exigent circumstances" (like chasing a fleeing felon or hearing screams for help) can allow warrantless entry. If police claim an emergency, do not resist, but clearly state that you do not consent to the entry to preserve your rights for future court proceedings.
