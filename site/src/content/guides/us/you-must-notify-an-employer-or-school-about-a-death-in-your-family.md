# What to do if… you must notify an employer or school about a death in your family (USA)

## Short answer
Send one brief message to your immediate supervisor or the school attendance office stating there has been a death in your family and you require immediate time off. Ask for the specific policy name and the deadline for any required documentation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not provide graphic details about the cause or nature of the death.
- Do not apologize for needing time off or for the timing of the event.
- Do not commit to a specific return-to-work or return-to-class date until you have confirmed funeral dates and travel requirements.
- Do not guess which leave type to use; let HR or the administration categorize the absence.
- Do not wait to notify professors or instructors if you have a high-stakes exam or lab within the next 48 hours.

## What to do now
1. **Send a minimalist notification to the primary administrative contact.**
   - **Work:** Email your direct supervisor and CC your HR representative.
   - **K-12 School:** Contact the Attendance Office or the Main Office.
   - **University:** Email your instructors and the Office of the Dean of Students or Student Affairs.
   - **Script:** "I am writing to notify you of a death in my immediate family. I will be absent starting [Date] and will need bereavement leave. I will provide an update on my return date as soon as arrangements are finalized. Please let me know what documentation is required."

2. **Verify if you are in a state with a legal mandate for bereavement leave.**
   - **California:** Employers with 5+ employees must provide 5 days of leave (unpaid, but protected).
   - **Illinois:** Up to 10 workdays of unpaid leave (for employers covered by FMLA).
   - **Oregon:** Up to 2 weeks of unpaid leave per family member (employers with 25+ staff).
   - **Maryland:** Allows use of earned paid leave for bereavement (employers with 15+ staff).
   - **Minnesota/Washington:** Check the state’s Paid Family and Medical Leave programs for 2026 eligibility.

3. **Identify your specific leave category to ensure job protection.**
   - **Ask HR:** "Am I eligible for statutory bereavement leave, company-provided bereavement leave, or should I use PTO/Sick leave?"
   - **Federal Employees:** You are entitled to use up to 104 hours (13 days) of accrued sick leave per year for bereavement and funeral arrangements. 
   - **Federal Employees (Loss of a Child):** Under the NDAA, you may be eligible for 2 weeks of specific paid bereavement leave.

4. **Request immediate academic or workload triage.**
   - **Ask for a pause:** "I am requesting an immediate extension on [Assignment/Project] due by [Date] while I handle family arrangements."
   - **Note for students:** Ask the Dean of Students to send an official notification to all your professors so you do not have to email each one individually.

5. **Ask for the documentation deadline so you can stop worrying about it.**
   - **Clarify requirements:** Ask if they require an obituary, a funeral program, or a death certificate.
   - **Establish a buffer:** Request that any paperwork be submitted within 10–14 days of your return rather than immediately.

6. **Establish a single check-in window.**
   - **Set boundaries:** State, "I will check my email once daily at [Time] for urgent updates, but otherwise will be focused on family matters."

## What can wait
- You do not need to redistribute your workload or find a substitute teacher/worker yourself today.
- You do not need to provide a final return date if travel is involved and not yet booked.
- You do not need to submit the death certificate or obituary until you have returned to your normal schedule.
- You do not need to explain family dynamics or why a specific relative’s death requires your presence.

## Important reassurance
Administrative systems at schools and workplaces are designed to handle family emergencies. Providing a short, factual notice is professionally responsible and fulfills your immediate obligation. Confusion or a "brain fog" response is a standard physiological reaction to loss and is expected by HR and student affairs professionals.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only to secure your immediate absence and protect your job or academic standing. Long-term leave or grief-related disability accommodations may require additional processes.

## Important note
Leave laws vary significantly by state, union contract, and employer size. This guide summarizes general 2026 standards but is not legal advice. If your leave is denied, contact your union representative or a labor law specialist.
