# What to do if… you are planning a funeral or memorial service for families who are in conflict (USA)

## Short answer
Pause nonessential decisions. Ask the funeral home to state in writing which documents, signatures, and approvals it needs before it acts, and use one neutral contact while any disagreement is clarified.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t try to settle the conflict through group texts, social media threads, or reply-all emails.
- Don’t sign a contract, approve charges, or pay a deposit until the funeral home has explained your role and given you a written itemized statement.
- Don’t promise either family that a disputed choice is settled before the funeral home confirms what it can accept.
- Don’t publish an obituary, service location, or livestream link while the sharing plan is still disputed.
- Don’t carry accusations or demands between the families.
- Don’t agree to open-mic tributes if there is a serious risk of confrontation.
- Don’t assume the rules are the same in every state.

## What to do now
1. **Ask the funeral home for a written checklist.**
   - Ask what documents, signatures, and identification it needs.
   - Ask whether there are written instructions, prearrangements, or other records on file.
   - Ask which decisions can proceed now and which may need to pause while a disagreement is clarified.

2. **Use one neutral contact for coordination.**
   - Choose one email thread or shared document.
   - Send one brief message: “To reduce mistakes, please send service requests through **[contact person]**. The funeral home will confirm whose approval it needs for each decision.”

3. **Separate the urgent decisions from the optional ones.**
   - Ask the funeral home which decisions about care, disposition, timing, contracts, and payment need attention first.
   - Pause optional choices such as flowers, printed programs, slideshows, and catering until the urgent decisions are clear.

4. **Ask for a simple low-conflict service structure.**
   - Use a clear start and finish time.
   - Pre-approve speakers and time limits.
   - Avoid surprise tributes or open microphones.
   - Use neutral music or readings when a choice is likely to trigger a dispute.

5. **Offer separate gatherings if a shared event is becoming unworkable.**
   - A brief shared service with separate receptions, or separate memorial gatherings, may reduce pressure without requiring the families to resolve their wider conflict now.

6. **Limit public sharing.**
   - Decide who will publish the obituary, service details, and any livestream link.
   - Share links privately if harassment or disruption is a realistic concern.
   - Keep login details with the agreed contact.

7. **Ask the venue about safety arrangements.**
   - Ask about staff presence, reserved seating, a clear plan for interruptions, and who will respond if someone becomes disruptive.
   - If there is immediate danger, move to a safer place and call 911.

8. **Pause and get state-specific help if authority is genuinely disputed.**
   - Ask the funeral home what it can do while the issue is unresolved.
   - Do not try to decide a contested legal issue yourself.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to repair the family relationship now.
- You don’t need to agree on a shared version of past events.
- You don’t need to decide every detail of the service today.
- You don’t need to finalize photos, flowers, printed programs, catering, or reception plans before the urgent approvals are clear.
- You don’t need to decide your own attendance plan immediately.

## Important reassurance
You do not have to make everyone agree. A smaller number of written decisions, one neutral contact, and a simpler service plan can reduce mistakes and lower the chance of conflict on the day.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from the funeral home and state-specific legal guidance if there are competing claims about who can approve disposition or arrangements.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Funeral-home requirements and state rules can vary, so ask the funeral home to explain what it needs in writing before making irreversible or expensive commitments.
