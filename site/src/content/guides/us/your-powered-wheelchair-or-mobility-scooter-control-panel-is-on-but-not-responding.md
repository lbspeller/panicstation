# What to do if… your powered wheelchair or mobility scooter control panel is on but not responding (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause first. Then check the common lockouts: joystick or throttle not centered at startup, freewheel mode, charger connected, seat not secured, key not fully inserted, or a resettable breaker. If it still will not drive, call your durable medical equipment supplier or service provider, and call 911 if you are stranded in danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not put the chair or scooter into freewheel or manual push mode on a slope, ramp, curb edge, or near traffic; braking may be reduced or disengaged.
- Do not keep forcing the joystick or throttle while you are near traffic, stairs, ramps, drop-offs, or a curb edge.
- Do not open electrical housings, bypass safety switches, hold in a breaker, or try quick wiring fixes.
- Do not keep operating if control returns but movement feels erratic, delayed, or hard to stop.
- Do not accept help that moves you somewhere isolated; choose a visible, well-lit place when possible.
- Do not use the device if the controller, charger port, wiring, or battery area is wet, hot, burnt-smelling, or otherwise unusual.

## What to do now
1. **Make a safer pause.**  
   If you are in a crosswalk, roadway, parking lane, driveway, curb cut, ramp, or doorway, signal for help. Ask someone nearby to help you get to a flat, visible safe spot such as a sidewalk, lobby, store entrance, or staffed counter. Do not use freewheel on a slope.

2. **Let the control return to neutral.**  
   Take your hand off the joystick or throttle and let it fully return to center. Some devices will not drive if the control was not centered when the unit powered on.

3. **Try one simple restart.**  
   Turn the unit off. Wait briefly. Turn it on again without touching the joystick or throttle until the startup lights, beeps, or screen checks finish.

4. **Check freewheel or manual push mode on level ground only.**  
   If the unit was recently pushed, transported, bumped, or handled by someone else, it may be in freewheel. Ask a helper to look for the freewheel, motor release, or motor disengage lever and return it to drive or engaged mode. If your manual says to turn power off before changing modes, do that first. After any pushing, put it back in drive so the brakes can hold.

5. **Check common drive interlocks and simple seating checks.**  
   Look for simple things that can stop driving or make driving unsafe even when the display is on:
   - Charger still connected
   - Key not fully inserted or not fully turned on
   - Seat not secured or not locked into a safe driving position, on models where this applies
   - Battery pack or cable not fully seated after transport
   - Speed control set so low that movement is barely noticeable

6. **Look at the lights and listen for beeps.**  
   Note the exact pattern: number of flashes, scrolling lights, warning symbol, beep pattern, and battery gauge. Do not guess the meaning while you are stuck; record it for the service provider.

7. **Use only a clearly labeled reset or circuit breaker once.**  
   If your manual or the device label clearly shows a reset or circuit breaker button and you can reach it without tools, press it once. Do not bypass it, tape it down, hold it in, or keep resetting it if it trips again.

8. **Call the service pathway.**  
   Call the durable medical equipment supplier, wheelchair clinic, scooter dealer, manufacturer service line, or repair provider that normally supports your device. Tell them your exact location, device make and model if you know it, what the panel shows, whether freewheel was involved, and whether you are outside or blocking a route.

9. **Use emergency help if you are unsafe.**  
   Call 911, or ask someone nearby to call 911, if you are stuck in traffic, extreme heat or cold, a medical risk, a dark or isolated place, or anywhere you cannot stay safely while waiting for help.

10. **Give helpers one clear instruction.**  
   Say: “Only put it in freewheel on flat ground, move me to that safe spot, then put it back in drive so it will not roll.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose whether the controller, batteries, charger, motors, brakes, or wiring caused the problem right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to repair or replace the device.
- You do not need to interpret every error code while you are stuck; capture the pattern and give it to the service provider.
- You do not need to keep testing once you are somewhere safe and the simple checks have not worked.

## Important reassurance
A control panel that is on but not responding can be caused by a safety lockout, freewheel setting, charger inhibit, seat or key issue, low battery, loose connection, or a fault that needs service. The priority is not to solve the whole problem immediately; it is to get to a safer pause, avoid rolling or electrical risks, and contact the right help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Persistent, recurring, wet, hot-smelling, erratic, or unexplained faults may need your device manual, your durable medical equipment provider, the manufacturer, or a qualified repair technician.

## Important note
This is general information for immediate safety and basic harm prevention, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, engineering, or professional repair advice. Follow your own device manual and service instructions when they are available. If you are in danger or becoming unwell, call 911.
