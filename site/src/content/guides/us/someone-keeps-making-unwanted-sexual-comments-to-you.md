# What to do if… someone keeps making unwanted sexual comments to you (USA)
## Short answer
Get to a safer pause and reduce their access to you. You do not have to answer, laugh, confront them, or decide whether to report anything right now.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay alone with them to prove that you can handle the situation.
- Do not feel you must reply, laugh, or keep the tone friendly.
- Do not confront them in private if that could make you less safe.
- Do not make a formal complaint, police-reporting, or legal decision while you are trying to get to a safer pause.

## What to do now
1. **Create space.** Move toward other people, end the interaction, or leave the immediate area when you can do so safely.
2. **Reduce direct access.** For online comments, stop replying and use mute, block, or report tools where available and safe to use. At work, use group settings or official work channels where possible.
3. **Tell one trusted person what is happening.** Ask them to stay nearby, check in with you, or help you avoid being alone with the person.
4. **Use a boundary sentence only if it feels safe.** You can say: “Stop making sexual comments to me.” You do not need to debate or explain.
5. **If this is at work, consider checking your workplace policy.** You may choose to share the concern with a supervisor, another supervisor, HR, or a designated reporting route when you feel ready. Unwelcome sexual comments can be sexual harassment; whether conduct is unlawful depends on the context.
6. **If you may want a record later and it is safe to keep one,** save existing messages or a brief note somewhere secure that the other person cannot access.
7. **If sexual assault or abuse has happened, or you are worried about sexual harm,** you may choose to contact RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline and ask whether the service fits your situation. It offers confidential support 24/7: call 800-656-HOPE (4673), text “HOPE” to 64673, or use the hotline page in the resources below.
8. **If you are in immediate danger or being threatened, call 911.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to make a formal complaint, contact a lawyer, or report to police.
- You do not need to perfectly label the comments before you create distance and ask for support.
- You do not need to prepare a detailed account while you are trying to get to a safer pause.

## Important reassurance
Repeated unwanted sexual comments are enough reason to create distance. Freezing, laughing nervously, or saying nothing can happen under stress; none of these reactions makes the comments welcome.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, reducing access, and getting support. Later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
