# What to do if… you experience repeated brief power surges and you’re worried about wiring or equipment damage (USA)

## Short answer
Unplug all sensitive electronics immediately and check for sensory signs of electrical fire like burning smells or heat; if signs are present, shut off the main breaker and evacuate.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep resetting breakers that have tripped more than once.
- Do not open the electrical panel cover or touch exposed wires.
- Do not leave high-draw appliances like air conditioners or dryers running while power is unstable.
- Do not use surge protectors if the "Protected" or "Status" light is unlit.
- Do not touch outlets or switches that appear discolored or feel hot.
- Do not assume the issue is resolved just because flickering stops temporarily.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate fire or shock hazards.** Look for visible smoke, sparks, or scorch marks around outlets and the breaker panel.
   - Listen for buzzing, popping, or crackling sounds.
   - Sniff for the smell of burning plastic or ozone.
   - Notice if lights are becoming "extra bright," which can indicate a dangerous neutral wire failure.
2. **Shut off power if hazard cues are present.** If you detect heat, smoke, or burning smells, move to the main service disconnect and flip it to the "OFF" position.
   - The main breaker is usually at the top of your indoor panel or located in a separate box outside near the electric meter.
   - If there is active fire or you cannot safely reach the disconnect, leave the building and call 911.
3. **Unplug all sensitive and high-value equipment.** Disconnect computers, televisions, game consoles, routers, and kitchen appliances.
   - Physically pull the plugs from the wall; turning them off via a switch is insufficient to protect against voltage spikes.
4. **Determine the scope of the surge.** Check with neighbors or observe streetlights to see if the instability is localized to your home or affects the block.
5. **Contact the utility for exterior or neighborhood issues.** Call your electric provider's hazard line if you see downed lines, arcing at the meter, or if multiple homes are affected.
6. **Engage a licensed electrician for internal issues.** If the flickering or surges are only occurring in your home, or if you found scorch marks inside, contact a professional to inspect for loose connections or panel failure.
   - Verify the electrician's license via your state's Department of Labor or Contractor Licensing Board website.
7. **Document the events for diagnostic use.** Record the time of surges, which appliances were running, and any specific cues like "lights dimming when the A/C kicks on."
8. **Notify the property owner if you are a tenant.** Report the electrical instability in writing immediately and request an inspection by a licensed professional.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on permanent upgrades like whole-house surge protection or panel replacement during the crisis.
- You do not need to test every individual device for damage until the power supply is confirmed stable by a professional.
- You do not need to contact insurance until a licensed electrician provides a written assessment of the cause and damage.

## Important reassurance
Electrical instability often triggers a strong fear response because the hazard is invisible. Taking the immediate step of unplugging equipment and isolating the power source is an effective way to prevent further damage and reduce fire risk while waiting for professional help.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Resolving the cause of power surges requires professional diagnostic equipment used by a utility company or a licensed electrician.

## Important note
This information is for general safety awareness and does not replace the judgment of emergency responders or licensed electrical professionals. If you suspect an active electrical fire, prioritize evacuation and emergency services over equipment preservation.
