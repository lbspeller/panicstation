# What to do if… you are asked to arrange a funeral but cannot find a will or instructions (USA)
## Short answer
Pause before authorizing cremation, burial, or another final disposition. Ask what must be decided now, what can be held temporarily, and what state-specific authority or documentation the funeral provider needs from the person making the decision.

## Do not do these things
- Do not authorize cremation, burial, or another irreversible final disposition until the decision-maker and required documentation are clear.
- Do not assume that signing for transport is only a temporary step. Ask what the form authorizes and whether it commits you to a provider or final arrangement.
- Do not sign a funeral package or pay a large deposit while you are overwhelmed. Ask for itemized prices first.
- Do not assume that the executor, the nearest relative, or the person who received the first call automatically has authority to decide.
- Do not let pressure force an immediate decision. Ask for the reason, the exact decision needed, and any deadline in writing.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for a pause and identify the immediate decision.**
   - Ask the hospital, facility, medical examiner, coroner, or funeral provider where the person is now and whether temporary holding or transport is needed.
   - Ask which decisions can wait and whether any proposed form authorizes only transport or also commits you to services.

2. **Write down the basic details.**
   - Note the person’s full legal name, date of birth, date and place of death, current location, and the names of close relatives or other people who may have relevant documents.
   - Retrieve the Social Security number later if a funeral director or Social Security needs it, and keep it secure.

3. **Do a short, focused search for instructions.**
   - Check places you are entitled to access, such as labelled files, a home safe, and relevant papers.
   - Look for a will, funeral wishes, burial or cremation instructions, a document naming someone to handle arrangements, and any pre-need or prepaid funeral plan.
   - Contact the person’s attorney if known. Ask the bank what access rules apply if a safe deposit box may contain documents.
   - Set a stopping point. You do not need to search every possession before asking a funeral provider about temporary options.

4. **Ask the funeral provider what authority is required in your state.**
   - State law may identify who can make final-disposition decisions and what documentation is needed. A written designation or pre-need arrangement may affect the answer.
   - If several relatives may have the same priority, ask what documentation is required before anyone signs.
   - If people disagree about who can decide, do not authorize an irreversible step. Seek local legal advice and ask the funeral provider which local process applies.

5. **Ask about prices before choosing a package.**
   - You can ask a funeral provider for price information by telephone. Under the FTC Funeral Rule, the provider must give accurate price information when asked.
   - When a funeral provider discusses arrangements with you face to face, ask for the General Price List to keep.
   - Ask whether the provider offers simpler options, including direct cremation or immediate burial, and request itemized costs.

6. **Ask how to order certified death-certificate copies.**
   - Ordering rules vary by state. Certified copies are generally obtained from the vital-records office for the state where the death occurred.
   - Ask what copies are needed for immediate tasks before ordering more than you presently need.

7. **Confirm the Social Security report once the immediate arrangements are stable.**
   - Funeral homes generally report a death to Social Security if they have the Social Security number.
   - If a funeral home is not involved or does not report the death, contact Social Security by phone or in person. Social Security does not accept death reports online or by email.

8. **Ask about local help if no one can pay or take responsibility.**
   - Local assistance may exist, but the route varies. Ask the medical examiner, coroner, hospital social worker, or local social-services office what applies where the death occurred.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide obituary wording, music, readings, flowers, a reception, or detailed service plans now.
- You do not need to complete estate administration or probate tasks today.
- You do not need to notify every account provider immediately.
- You do not need to settle a family disagreement before asking for a temporary pause and local advice.

## Important reassurance
Missing instructions do not mean that every decision must be made immediately. It is reasonable to pause, ask what is reversible, and confirm who can authorize the next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about the estate, disputed authority, contracts, benefits, or debts may need state-specific specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Rules and local procedures vary by state and by the circumstances of the death.
