# What to do if… you realise you shared your bank login or one-time code and think your account is at risk (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank immediately using the official number on the back of your card to report a compromised account. Inform them you shared a one-time code or login so they can freeze access and attempt to stop or recall pending transfers.

## Do not do these things
- Do not share any further codes, passwords, or PINs, even if the person claims they are from the "fraud department."
- Do not trust caller ID or numbers provided via text; only use verified numbers from your physical card or official app.
- Do not move money to a "safe account" suggested by a caller, as banks do not require you to move funds to secure them.
- Do not download remote-access software or screen-sharing tools (such as AnyDesk or TeamViewer) at the request of a caller.
- Do not delete text messages, call logs, or emails related to the incident; these are required for evidence.
- Do not wait to see if a transaction clears before acting; Regulation E liability protections depend on the speed of your report.

## What to do now
1. **End all contact with the suspected scammer and call your bank’s verified fraud line.**  
   Hanging up is the only way to ensure the line is clear. Dial the number on the back of your debit or credit card or use the "Contact Us" feature within the bank's official mobile application.

2. **State clearly that your "online credentials have been compromised" and request a "hard freeze" on the account.**  
   Use these specific terms to ensure the representative understands the severity. Ask the bank to:
   - **Disable online and mobile banking access** immediately to prevent the scammer from initiating new transfers.
   - **Review all pending transactions**, specifically looking for Wires, ACH transfers, or Zelle payments.
   - **Initiate a wire recall** if a transfer was sent; note that for international wires over $250, banks must follow 2026 FinCEN data-sharing rules which may assist in tracing.
   - **Remove any newly added "Trusted Devices"** or "Travel Notices" that you did not authorize.

3. **Secure your primary email account using a different, clean device.**  
   Scammers often use compromised email access to intercept bank password reset links. Change your email password first, then enable hardware-based Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) if available.

4. **Notify your mobile carrier to place a "SIM Swap" or "Port-Out" lock on your phone number.**  
   If the scammer has your login and a one-time code, they may attempt to hijack your phone number to receive future verification codes. Ask the carrier for an "account-level PIN" for all changes.

5. **File an official report at IdentityTheft.gov to trigger a federal recovery plan.**  
   This creates a formal record that you can provide to the bank if they initially deny a fraud claim. If money was moved electronically, also file a complaint at the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3.gov).

6. **Place a security freeze on your ChexSystems and credit reports.**  
   - **ChexSystems:** This prevents scammers from opening new bank accounts in your name.
   - **Credit Bureaus:** Contact Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion to place a freeze; you must contact all three individually for a full freeze, or one for a renewable one-year fraud alert.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to close the account permanently until the bank has completed its initial fraud sweep.
- You do not need to identify the physical location of the scammer; law enforcement agencies handle the investigative tracking.
- You do not need to provide a typed statement immediately; the verbal report and the case number from your bank are the first priority.

## Important reassurance
Scammers use sophisticated psychological pressure to bypass security protocols; sharing a code does not mean you are at fault for the theft. Taking these steps within two business days significantly increases your legal protections under federal Regulation E, which can limit your liability for unauthorized electronic transfers.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a compromised bank account. It does not cover long-term credit repair, legal action against scammers, or the specifics of business-account liability, which differs from consumer protection.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Consumer protections for "authorized" vs. "unauthorized" transfers vary by bank and the specific circumstances of the disclosure.
