# What to do if… you accidentally triggered a panic alarm (USA)

## Short answer
Do not hang up if you have dialed 911; stay on the line and tell the dispatcher it was an accidental call and you are safe. If a monitored security system was triggered, disarm it via the keypad and wait for the monitoring center to call so you can provide your verbal password.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hang up on 911 before speaking to a dispatcher - this is often interpreted as a "silent call" or a person in active danger, which guarantees a police response.
- Do not send a text to 911 to "cancel" the call - many dispatch centers cannot receive texts, and a text may be viewed as a message sent under duress.
- Do not press the panic button repeatedly to try to stop it - this can send multiple high-priority alerts to the monitoring station.
- Do not run or hide if you see police arrive - stay where you are, keep your hands visible and empty, and calmly explain the error.
- Do not ignore calls from unknown or "No Caller ID" numbers for the next 10 minutes - these are likely the 911 center or your alarm company attempting to verify your safety.

## What to do now
1. **Determine which specific system was activated.**
   - **Phone SOS:** Usually triggered by holding the side/volume buttons or pressing the side button five times.
   - **Monitored Security Alarm:** A home or business system (like ADT, Ring, or SimpliSafe) that is currently sounding or has sent a silent signal.
   - **Standalone Panic Button:** A wall-mounted button or wearable pendant that does not have a keypad.

2. **Stay on the line if 911 was dialed or if your phone is currently in "Emergency SOS" mode.**
   - **If the call is active,** wait for the dispatcher to answer and say: "This is an accidental dial. I am safe and there is no emergency."
   - **If you already hung up,** call 911 back immediately or answer their immediate call-back to confirm your safety.
   - **Stay on the line** until the dispatcher explicitly tells you it is okay to hang up.

3. **Silence and disarm a physical security system immediately.**
   - **Enter your keypad PIN** to stop the siren; this stops the noise but may not cancel the police dispatch.
   - **Keep your phone near you** and wait for the monitoring station to call.
   - **Provide your "Verbal Password"** (this is usually a word or phrase, not your keypad PIN) when the monitoring agent asks for identification.

4. **Alert emergency contacts if a mobile SOS message was sent.**
   - **Check your messages** to see if an automated text with your location was sent to your "Emergency Contacts."
   - **Send a follow-up text** to those specific people saying: "Accidental alarm. I am safe. Please do not call 911."
   - **Check if someone already called the police** on your behalf; if they did, you must stay put and be ready to speak with responders.

5. **Prepare for a possible physical police check.**
   - **Stay in a visible area** if you are at home or work.
   - **Have a photo ID ready** if possible to prove you are the resident or authorized user of the space.
   - **Keep your hands visible** and empty if officers approach the property, and calmly state: "It was a false alarm / accidental trigger. I am safe."

## What can wait
- You do not need to call the police non-emergency line after you have already spoken to 911.
- You do not need to contact your alarm company to "apologize" or explain the mistake until the immediate response window has passed.
- You do not need to troubleshoot why the alarm went off or change your settings right now.
- You do not need to worry about false alarm fines in the next few minutes; focus entirely on stopping the emergency response.

## Important reassurance
Emergency dispatchers and monitoring centers deal with accidental triggers every day. They prefer that you stay on the line and admit the mistake rather than hanging up. Acknowledging the error quickly is the best way to ensure that police and fire resources remain available for actual emergencies.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step guidance only for the immediate minutes following an accidental alarm trigger. It does not cover technical repair of alarm systems or legal disputes regarding false alarm fines.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. 911 protocols and false alarm ordinances vary by city and county. Always follow the direct instructions of the 911 dispatcher or law enforcement on the scene.
