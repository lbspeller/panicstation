# What to do if… you discover a person who died had a co-parenting arrangement and upcoming handovers need immediate planning (USA)

## Short answer
Do not follow the existing visitation schedule without first confirming the legal status of the surviving parent, who usually becomes the sole legal custodian by operation of law in most states. Create a written 48-hour stability plan while you locate the most recent court orders and seek local legal advice.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a will or a verbal wish automatically transfers custody to a non-parent (e.g., a grandparent or stepparent) without a court-issued guardianship or custody order.
- Do not withhold a child from a legal custodian without documented, credible safety reasons that you are prepared to present to a judge.
- Do not attempt a "surprise exchange" or confront family members at their private residence during a time of high emotion.
- Do not ask the child to relay messages about custody, schedules, or legal disputes.
- Do not hand the child to anyone not listed in a court order or authorized in writing by the legal custodian.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the legal custodian under current state law.**  
   **Locate the most recent, signed court order for custody or parenting time.**
   - Determine if the surviving parent has "presumptive custody," which in most states means they automatically regain full custody unless a court has previously terminated their parental rights.
   - Look for specific clauses regarding "death of a party" or "successor guardians" within the existing order.

2. **Draft a minimalist 48-hour stability plan in writing.**  
   **Outline where the child will sleep, who will manage school runs, and the exact time of the next necessary exchange.**
   - Share this plan via email or text with all involved parties to create a paper trail.
   - Limit the plan to the immediate future to avoid long-term disputes while grieving.

3. **Initiate logistics-only contact with the surviving parent or their representative.**  
   **Send a factual communication to confirm the child’s safety and acknowledge the disruption to the schedule.**
   - If direct contact is high-conflict, use a parenting app or have a third-party attorney facilitate the message.
   - Ask for confirmation of the surviving parent's intent for the next scheduled handover.

4. **Alert the school or childcare provider to the situation.**  
   **Inform the administration that a parent has died and that pickup arrangements may change.**
   - Provide the school with a copy of the current custody order.
   - Explicitly state that no one is to pick up the child without photo ID verification and your express written consent.

5. **Select a neutral public location for any immediate exchanges.**  
   **Conduct the handover at a police station lobby, a well-lit public park, or a school foyer to ensure safety and witness presence.**
   - Keep the exchange brief and focused on the child's comfort.
   - If you feel unsafe, request a "civil standby" from local law enforcement, noting that they may only be able to keep the peace and cannot usually decide custody on-site.

6. **Seek an emergency "Ex Parte" order or Temporary Guardianship if a parent is unavailable or unsafe.**  
   **Contact the local family court or probate court to file for emergency relief if the child has no legal guardian available.**
   - Note specific observable cues such as:
     - The surviving parent is incarcerated or unreachable.
     - There is a history of documented violence or active substance abuse.
     - Multiple parties are claiming the child simultaneously.

7. **Contact a local family law attorney or court self-help center.**  
   **Verify state-specific rules regarding "Standby Guardianship" or "Testamentary Guardianship" which vary significantly by jurisdiction.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to finalize a permanent 18-year custody plan today.
- You do not need to settle estate distributions, life insurance claims, or social security survivor benefits in the first 72 hours.
- You do not need to resolve disagreements about the funeral or memorial service during the custody stabilization phase.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed when a legal schedule suddenly fails due to a tragedy. Moving slowly and putting every agreement in writing protects the child and ensures that everyone’s legal rights are respected. Stability and physical safety are the only priorities for the next few days.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate handovers and child safety following a co-parent's death. It does not replace legal advice, nor does it cover long-term probate or custody litigation.

## Important note
In the United States, child custody is governed by state law. While surviving parents generally have a superior right to custody, certain court orders or "unfitness" findings can complicate this. Always verify your specific situation with a local attorney or the clerk of the court that issued your original order.
