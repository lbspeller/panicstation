# What to do if… police ask for access to your vehicle data, dashcam, or connected car app (USA)
## Short answer
Do not volunteer access to your phone, connected-car app, dash system, or vehicle data just because you are asked. Calmly say that you do not consent to a search, ask whether the request is voluntary or based on legal authority, and do not physically interfere with an officer.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t volunteer your phone passcode, pattern, biometric unlock, app password, or account login.
- Don’t delete footage, reset devices, remove SD cards, wipe apps, change logs, power off equipment, or change settings during the encounter.
- Don’t physically interfere if an officer takes property. Keep your hands visible and deal with the issue through the appropriate process later.
- Don’t start explaining what the data shows.
- Don’t sign a consent form you do not understand or feel pressured to sign.

## What to do now
1. **Ask whether you are free to leave.** Say: “Am I free to leave?” If the answer is yes, leave calmly when it is safe to do so.
2. **Clarify whether this is a request.** Ask: “Are you asking for my consent, or are you relying on a warrant or other legal authority?”
3. **State non-consent clearly.** Say: “I do not consent to a search or to access to my phone, car app, dash system, or vehicle data.” Do not argue.
4. **Do not volunteer an unlock or login.** If your phone is the gateway to a connected-car app, do not unlock it or log in for the officer just because you are asked. Police generally may not search digital information on a cell phone seized from an arrested person without a warrant, although exceptions can apply.
5. **If paperwork is shown, keep the roadside response simple.** Note what device, account, vehicle, or data category appears to be named. Do not try to resolve the legal details during the stop.
6. **If you are detained or arrested and questioned, ask for a lawyer and remain silent.** Say: “I want a lawyer. I am going to remain silent.” Stop discussing the incident and the data.
7. **Preserve what already exists.** Do not delete, alter, eject, reset, or disable anything. If dashcam footage may overwrite automatically, seek prompt legal advice once you are in a safe place.
8. **Record the details when safe.** Write down the officer’s name or badge number if known, the agency, the time, the location, what was requested, and what happened. If property was taken, ask whether a receipt or property record is available.
9. **Treat provider-held data as a separate issue.** Some connected-car or telematics data may be held by a manufacturer or service provider. Any later provider-related request can be reviewed separately with a lawyer.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide now whether you will later provide a copy of footage, export logs, or respond through a lawyer.
- You don’t need to interpret speed data, GPS history, trip logs, driver-assist events, or crash-related data on the spot.
- You don’t need to decide whether a document is legally valid while standing roadside.
- You don’t need to contact an insurer or manufacturer immediately unless there is a separate urgent reason.

## Important reassurance
A request for digital access can feel urgent and official. It is reasonable to slow the moment down, avoid volunteering access, state that you do not consent, and keep the interaction calm.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a lawyer familiar with the relevant state and the facts of the encounter, including whether you are a witness or suspect, what type of data is sought, where it is stored, and whether an exception to a general warrant rule may apply.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Laws, police powers, and procedures vary by state and situation.
