# What to do if… you receive notice that your driver’s licence will be suspended for legal reasons (USA)

## Short answer
Stop driving immediately if the effective date has passed, as driving while suspended can lead to arrest. Contact your state's driver licensing agency (DMV, BMV, DOT, or DOL) immediately to confirm the suspension status and the deadline to request an administrative hearing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive your vehicle until you have confirmed your status is "Valid" with the state agency.
- Do not ignore the notice because you think you were not "served"; many states consider mailing a notice to your address on file as legal service.
- Do not assume a criminal court date is your only deadline; administrative suspensions by the DMV often have much shorter, separate windows for appeal.
- Do not pay "license recovery" fees to third-party websites; only pay fees directly to official state portals or offices.
- Do not surrender your physical license to anyone except a law enforcement officer or an official state DMV employee when legally required.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the issuing authority and the effective date.**
   - Determine if the notice is from a state agency (like the DMV) for an administrative reason or from a court for a criminal conviction or unpaid ticket.
   - Locate the exact date and time the suspension begins.
2. **Determine the "Hearing Request" deadline immediately.**
   - Check the notice for a "Request for Hearing" or "Administrative Review" section.
   - Note that many states have extremely short deadlines (e.g., California is 10 calendar days from notice; Texas is 15 calendar days; Florida is 10 calendar days).
   - Verify if the deadline refers to calendar days (including weekends) or business days.
3. **Contact the state driver licensing agency to verify status.**
   - Call the official number or use the agency's "Check Driver License Status" online portal.
   - Ask specifically: "What is the exact reason for the suspension?" and "What is the fastest way to request a stay of suspension pending a hearing?"
4. **Request a "Stay" or "Temporary Permit" if applicable.**
   - Ask the agency if filing a hearing request "stays" (pauses) the suspension.
   - In many DUI-related administrative cases, requesting a hearing allows you to continue driving legally until the hearing date.
5. **Check for 2026-specific state rule changes.**
   - If you are in New York, be aware that as of February 16, 2026, the suspension threshold has dropped to 10 points and the look-back period has extended to 24 months.
   - Check if your state has implemented new "Ignition Interlock" requirements that allow you to keep driving if a device is installed.
6. **Verify identity details on the notice.**
   - Confirm that the Name, Date of Birth, and Driver License Number match your records exactly.
   - If there is a mismatch, inform the agency immediately that this may be an identity error or a clerical mistake.
7. **Secure alternative transportation for the next 72 hours.**
   - Use ride-sharing services, public transit, or a licensed friend/family member.
   - If your employment requires driving, review your contract; you may be required to notify your employer of a change in license status.
8. **Gather required documentation for reinstatement.**
   - Ask the DMV for a "Compliance Letter" or "Requirements List."
   - Common requirements include paying a reinstatement fee, providing proof of SR-22 insurance, or completing a specific safety course.

## What can wait
- You do not need to hire an attorney in the first hour, though you should do so before any scheduled hearing.
- You do not need to pay reinstatement fees until you have confirmed that payment will actually result in a valid status.
- You do not need to sell your vehicle or cancel your standard auto insurance policy immediately.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a suspension notice is a high-stress event, but it is often a resolvable administrative process. Following the specified deadlines is the most effective way to protect your rights. Most states provide a clear path to "restricted" or "hardship" licenses even if a full suspension remains in place.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and administrative protection only. It does not provide legal defense strategies for criminal court or specific advice for commercial driver licenses (CDL).

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Driver license laws vary significantly by state. Always refer to the specific instructions provided on your official state notice and consult with the relevant state agency or a qualified attorney.
