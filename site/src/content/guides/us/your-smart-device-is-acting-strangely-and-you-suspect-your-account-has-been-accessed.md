# What to do if… your smart device is acting strangely and you suspect your account has been accessed (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safe pause, then stop remote access to the affected device if you can do that without creating a safety problem. From a separate trusted phone or computer, secure the account that controls the device: change the password, sign out other sessions, and turn on multi-factor authentication.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep a camera, lock, alarm, thermostat, speaker, or hub online just to “see what happens” if it may still be under someone else’s control.
- Don’t disable a lock, alarm, camera, or thermostat until you have a safe manual fallback, such as a physical key, manual control, or another way to monitor the area.
- Don’t factory reset first unless you are locked out and the provider’s recovery steps tell you to do that.
- Don’t reuse passwords or make a small change to an old password.
- Don’t trust emails, texts, ads, or pop-ups that say they can “verify” or “fix” your account; go to the provider directly.
- Don’t share one-time codes, recovery codes, or password reset links with anyone who contacts you.
- Don’t ignore new users, new routines, changed camera settings, changed lock settings, or linked services you did not add.

## What to do now
1. **Stop remote control if it is safe to do so.**
   - Unplug the affected device, remove its batteries, or disconnect the hub or bridge that controls it.
   - For a smart lock, alarm, camera, garage door opener, or thermostat, first make sure you have a safe manual backup, such as a physical key, manual switch, or another working safety device.
   - If only one device is affected, disconnect that device first rather than taking down your whole home network.

2. **Secure the controlling account from a trusted device.**
   - Use a phone or computer you trust, not the smart device or a device showing suspicious pop-ups.
   - Change the account password to a long, unique password.
   - Use the account’s option to sign out of all devices, log out all sessions, or remove unknown signed-in devices.
   - Turn on multi-factor authentication if available. An authenticator app, passkey, or security key may be a stronger option than SMS if the provider offers it and you can keep access to it.

3. **Secure the email account used for password resets.**
   - Change the email password and sign out other sessions.
   - Turn on multi-factor authentication for the email account.
   - Check for forwarding rules, filters, recovery email changes, recovery phone changes, and unknown logged-in devices.

4. **Remove unknown access inside the smart-home app.**
   - Check household members, home members, shared users, guests, family accounts, and delegated access.
   - Remove any person, phone, tablet, browser session, or old device you do not recognize or no longer control.
   - Check linked services such as voice assistants, home platforms, calendar integrations, delivery integrations, or third-party apps, and disconnect anything unfamiliar.

5. **Check for changed routines, scenes, and privacy settings.**
   - Look for unfamiliar routines, automations, scenes, geofencing rules, schedules, or shortcuts.
   - Delete or disable any rule you did not create, especially rules that unlock doors, turn off cameras, silence alerts, change thermostat settings, or disable notifications.
   - Check camera, microphone, recording, storage, and notification settings and change them back if they were altered.

6. **Secure the router and Wi-Fi basics.**
   - Change the router admin password, not only the Wi-Fi password.
   - Change the Wi-Fi password, then reconnect only devices you still use and recognize.
   - Update router firmware or enable automatic router updates if your router supports that.
   - Disable WPS. If you can find the settings, also consider disabling remote management and UPnP.
   - Use WPA2 or WPA3 encryption if your router supports it.

7. **After the account is secured, reset and re-pair the affected device.**
   - Follow the manufacturer’s recovery and factory reset instructions for the specific device, hub, or bridge.
   - Set it up again under the secured account.
   - Apply device and app updates during setup.
   - Change any default device password, PIN, or admin credential.

8. **Make a simple record.**
   - Write down the date, time, device name, symptoms, login alerts, unknown users, unknown devices, changed routines, and changed settings.
   - Keep screenshots if you already have them, but do not keep the device online just to collect more.
   - Save the record for device support, your internet provider, your bank if money or payment details are involved, or a report.

9. **Escalate if money, identity, threats, or repeated access are involved.**
   - If there is financial fraud or exposed payment information, contact your bank or card issuer through the number on the card or official app.
   - If personal information may have been used to open accounts, create bills, or affect your credit, use IdentityTheft.gov for a recovery plan.
   - If you want to report cyber-enabled fraud or cybercrime to federal authorities, use IC3.gov or complaint.ic3.gov.
   - Be careful of anyone claiming to be FBI or IC3 support who asks for money, passwords, one-time codes, or remote access to your device.

## What can wait
- Buying replacement devices.
- Rebuilding your whole smart-home system.
- Proving exactly how it happened.
- Deciding whether to close every account.
- Deep forensics, unless there is repeated re-entry, serious financial fraud, threats, or evidence that a phone or computer is also compromised.

## Important reassurance
Smart devices can act strangely because of outages, updates, weak Wi-Fi, buggy automations, or accidental setting changes. Treating it as a possible account access problem is still a calm, protective choice because it reduces the biggest immediate risks: continued control, privacy exposure, and being locked out.

## Scope note
These are first steps for containment and recovery only. Later decisions, such as replacing devices, making a formal report, handling identity theft, or investigating repeated access, may need specialist support from the provider, your internet provider, a cybersecurity professional, or another appropriate service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If the device issue is connected to threats, stalking, harassment, domestic abuse, or immediate danger, prioritize personal safety and contact appropriate emergency or specialist support.
