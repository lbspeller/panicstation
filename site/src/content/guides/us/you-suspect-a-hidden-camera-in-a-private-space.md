# What to do if… you suspect a hidden camera in a private space (USA)

## Short answer
Get out of the private area and move to a safer place before doing anything else. If you feel in immediate danger, call 911; otherwise, contact local law enforcement or a confidential victim support service from somewhere safe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not tear the room apart or start removing objects.
- Do not confront the host, roommate, staff member, landlord, manager, or suspected person while you are alone.
- Do not post accusations publicly or send threats.
- Do not throw away, smash, unplug, or take a suspected device with you unless you need to move it to stay safe.
- Do not try to prove what happened before getting support.
- Do not keep using the bathroom, bedroom, or changing area if you can safely leave it.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** Leave the bathroom, bedroom, changing room, shower area, or locker room. Go to a lobby, front desk, common area, public place, trusted neighbour, friend, or another space where you are not alone.
2. **Call 911 if there is immediate danger.** Use 911 if someone is nearby, threatening you, blocking you from leaving, trying to retrieve the object, or you believe you are at immediate risk. If it is not an emergency, call the local police non-emergency line from a safe place.
3. **Leave the suspected item as found if you can.** If a charger, smoke detector, clock, vent, hook, mirror, light, hole, or other object worries you, stop touching it. If you already handled it, do not try to put it back, test it, open it, or delete anything.
4. **Write down only the basics.** Note the date, time, exact location, what you saw, where the object was pointing, who had access, and whether anyone entered the space recently. Save check-in messages, maintenance texts, entry alerts, booking details, or threats.
5. **Ask for a different space immediately.** In a hotel, rental, dorm, gym, workplace, school, clinic, or venue, ask to move rooms or use another private area. Ask management, security, housing staff, or the front desk in writing to preserve access logs, maintenance records, incident reports, and relevant hallway or entry CCTV if they exist.
6. **Use the right on-site safety route.** In a school or college setting, contact campus police, security, residential life, or a trusted staff member if there is an immediate safety concern. Schools, colleges, and universities that receive federal financial assistance must have a Title IX coordinator; contacting that office can wait until you are safe.
7. **Contact confidential support.** RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline can provide support by phone, text, or online chat. VictimConnect can also help with confidential support and referrals for crime victims. Confidentiality can have limits, especially if identifying information is shared and mandatory reporting rules apply.
8. **If someone mentions footage, threatens you, or tries to bargain, do not negotiate.** Save the message, do not reply more than needed for safety, and report the threat to law enforcement or a victim support service.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide right now whether to make a formal report, pursue charges, contact a lawyer, tell family, tell an employer, or confront anyone.
- You do not need to identify the device, confirm whether it recorded, find out who placed it, or search every object before asking for help.
- You do not need to handle this alone. Getting to a safer place and contacting support is enough for the next step.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel shocked, frozen, embarrassed, angry, nauseated, or unsure. You do not need certainty before taking steps to protect yourself, ask for another space, or contact support.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Evidence handling, police reports, school or housing complaints, workplace issues, rental disputes, and legal options can be considered later with qualified support.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, forensic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. Support is available even if you are uncertain about what happened and even if you do not want to report.
