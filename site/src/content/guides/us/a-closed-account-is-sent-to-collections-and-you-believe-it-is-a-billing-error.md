# What to do if… a closed account is sent to collections and you believe it is a billing error (USA)

## Short answer
Send a written dispute to the collection agency within the 30-day validation period and request a full verification of the debt.

## Do not do these things
- Don't pay any portion of the debt or agree to a payment plan before receiving written verification.
- Don't give out your full Social Security number or bank details to an incoming caller.
- Don't rely on verbal "we will fix it" promises from either the collector or the original company.
- Don't ignore court summons or legal notices even if the debt is clearly a mistake.
- Don't delete closure confirmations, final statements, or emails showing a zero balance.

## What to do now
1. **Gather all physical and digital evidence showing the account was closed or paid in full.**
   - Locating the specific account closure date and reference number.
   - Finding the last statement that shows a zero balance or a final "paid" status.
   - Saving emails, cancellation confirmations, and bank records of the final payment.

2. **Locate the "validation notice" sent by the collector and identify the 30-day dispute deadline.**
   - Under federal law (Regulation F), the collector must send you a written notice within five days of first contact.
   - This notice must include an "end date" for the validation period; you must send your dispute before this date to trigger automatic protections.
   - Look for a tear-off dispute form at the bottom of the notice, which is a standard requirement as of 2026.

3. **Send a written dispute via certified mail with a return receipt requested.**
   - **The entire first sentence of this step is bolded because it is the primary action.**
   - State clearly that you dispute the validity of the entire debt because of a billing error.
   - Explicitly request that the collector provide verification of the debt or a copy of a judgment.
   - Demand the name and address of the original creditor if it is different from the current one.

4. **Notify the original creditor in writing that the account they referred to collections is in error.**
   - Provide copies of your closure proof and request that they "recall" or withdraw the account from the collection agency.
   - Ask for a corrected final statement showing a zero balance to be mailed to you.

5. **Dispute the entry with all three major credit bureaus if the error appears on your credit report.**
   - Use the official AnnualCreditReport.com portal to verify which bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) are reporting the debt.
   - File a separate dispute with each bureau and include your evidence of the billing error.
   - Note that credit bureaus generally have 30 to 45 days to complete their reinvestigation under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA).

6. **Monitor collector behavior for signs of illegal harassment.**
   - Under 2026 rules, a collector is generally presumed to be harassing you if they call more than seven times within seven consecutive days about a single debt.
   - Keep a log of every call, including the time, date, and the specific number used to contact you.

7. **File an official complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) if the dispute remains unresolved.**
   - Attach your timeline and copies of all correspondence where the collector or creditor failed to correct the error after being provided proof.

## What can wait
- You do not need to hire a lawyer immediately unless you have been served with a lawsuit.
- You do not need to decide on a "settlement" amount while the debt is still being verified.
- You do not need to call the collector daily; a single certified letter is more legally effective than multiple phone calls.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a collections notice for a mistake is a common administrative error caused by system lags or data processing delays. Confusion and frustration are standard responses to this situation. Following the formal dispute process is the standard way to freeze collection activity and force a review of the records.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a billing error in collections. It does not cover identity theft, complex commercial debts, or legal defense strategy for valid debts.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Federal laws like the FDCPA and FCRA provide specific windows for disputes. If you are sued or if the error persists for more than 90 days, you may need to consult a consumer protection attorney or local legal aid.
