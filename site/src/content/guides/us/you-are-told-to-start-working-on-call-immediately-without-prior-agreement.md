# What to do if… you are told to start working on-call immediately without prior agreement (USA)

## Short answer
Ask for the immediate on-call instruction, restrictions, response expectations, and timekeeping process in writing. Record any calls, messages, remote tasks, or other work you perform.

## Do not do these things
- Do not make a rushed long-term commitment during a verbal exchange.
- Do not quit or send an angry message in the moment.
- Do not leave actual work unrecorded, including calls, messages, troubleshooting, or remote access.
- Do not delete messages, call logs, schedule screenshots, or written instructions.
- Do not ignore a specific fatigue or safety concern in a safety-sensitive job.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the immediate instruction in writing.**  
   Ask for the start and end time, response time, required location, any limits on your movement or activities, who will contact you, and who you should contact if you cannot respond.

2. **Ask how to record time.**  
   Send a brief question such as: *“How should I record each call, message, remote task, and any other work performed while on call? Is there a separate way to record standby time?”*

3. **Keep a simple log from the start.**  
   Record when the on-call period starts, the restrictions you were given, each contact, the work you perform, and when each task starts and ends. Record travel details separately if a call-out requires travel, because whether travel time counts can depend on the circumstances.

4. **Check your classification and workplace documents.**  
   Look at your offer letter, handbook, timekeeping instructions, schedule policy, and any on-call or overtime policy. Note whether your employer classifies you as exempt or nonexempt. Under federal rules, covered nonexempt employees generally receive overtime pay for hours worked over 40 in a workweek; state law may add protections.

5. **Treat restrictive standby requirements as a possible pay issue.**  
   Under federal rules, on-call time can count as working time when you must remain on the employer’s premises or when restrictions are so significant that you cannot use the time effectively for your own purposes. Time spent actually performing work should be recorded even when the full standby period may not count.

6. **Send a calm written message if immediate coverage creates a problem.**  
   For example: *“Please send the on-call expectations and timekeeping instructions in writing. I also need to flag that immediate coverage may be difficult tonight because of [specific issue]. Who should I contact about coverage?”*

7. **Check whether state or local rules may add protections.**  
   Last-minute scheduling rules vary by location and job. Check your state labor office and your city or county labor office if your locality has one before relying on a specific notice rule.

8. **Escalate a pay or timekeeping concern in writing.**  
   Ask payroll, human resources, or your manager how to correct missing time. If the issue is not resolved, you can contact your state labor office or the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division to ask about your situation.

9. **Keep records of any negative response.**  
   Retaliation protections may apply when a worker asks about pay or hours, asserts covered rights, files a complaint, or cooperates with an investigation. Coverage can depend on the law and circumstances.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to stay in the job long-term.
- You do not need to settle every legal question during the first conversation.
- You do not need to file a formal complaint immediately.
- You do not need to argue about labels. Focus first on written instructions and an accurate log.

## Important reassurance
A sudden on-call demand can feel difficult to process. Asking for written expectations and keeping an accurate record is a practical first response.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours or day. Later decisions may need help from a local employment adviser, a union representative if you have one, or a labor agency.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Pay rules, scheduling rules, and retaliation protections can depend on your classification, location, workplace, and circumstances. If fatigue or safety is a concern, prioritize safety and document the concern promptly.
