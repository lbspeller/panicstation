# What to do if… you suspect you have a serious reaction after a vaccine or injection (USA)

## Short answer
If you might be having a severe allergic reaction, such as trouble breathing, swelling of the face or throat, widespread hives with dizziness, or collapse, call 911 now. If it is not immediately life-threatening but you are worried, contact a clinician the same day.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t delay urgent care if symptoms are severe, sudden, or rapidly worsening.
- Don’t drive yourself to urgent care or the emergency room if you feel faint, short of breath, confused, or unsafe; call 911 or ask someone nearby for help.
- Don’t take more than the label dose of any medicine to try to override symptoms.
- Don’t start a new medicine just to test whether it helps a reaction unless a clinician tells you to.
- Don’t get another dose of the same vaccine, booster, or planned non-urgent related injection until you have medical advice about this reaction.
- Don’t treat reporting to VAERS, V-safe, or FDA MedWatch as a substitute for medical care.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency warning signs.**  
   Call 911 now if you have trouble breathing, wheezing, swelling of the lips, face, tongue, or throat, widespread hives with dizziness or weakness, severe dizziness, collapse, severe confusion, severe chest pain, new one-sided weakness or numbness, or you feel like you might pass out. Tell the dispatcher you recently had a vaccine or injection and are worried about a serious reaction.

2. **Use prescribed emergency allergy medicine if you already have it.**  
   If you already have a prescribed epinephrine auto-injector for severe allergic reactions, use it as directed and call 911.

3. **If it is not an emergency, get same-day clinical guidance.**  
   Contact the fastest appropriate option you can access: your primary care doctor’s nurse line, the vaccination clinic or pharmacy, telehealth, urgent care, or the emergency room if symptoms are significant or worsening.

4. **Keep the key facts in one place.**  
   Write down the vaccine or injection name, the date and time it was given, where it was given, when symptoms started, what symptoms changed over time, any medicines you took afterward, and any allergy history or prior reactions.

5. **Find the lot number or product details if you can do it easily.**  
   Check your vaccination card, receipt, patient portal, pharmacy record, clinic paperwork, prescription label, or medication packaging. If you cannot find it quickly, do not delay care to look for it.

6. **Take quick photos of visible symptoms.**  
   If you have a rash, hives, redness, or swelling, take clear photos now. Symptoms may look different by the time you are seen.

7. **If symptoms worsen while you are waiting, upgrade the response.**  
   If you develop breathing trouble, swelling of the face or throat, severe chest pain, confusion, new one-sided weakness or numbness, or feel faint, stop waiting and call 911.

8. **Report a vaccine adverse event after urgent needs are handled.**  
   If the reaction followed a vaccine, VAERS accepts reports from patients, caregivers, clinicians, and others. You can report even if you are not sure the vaccine caused the event.

9. **Use V-safe only if it applies and only after urgent needs are handled.**  
   V-safe is for COVID-19 and RSV vaccination check-ins. If you report seeking medical care after vaccination, V-safe can ask you to complete a VAERS report.

10. **Use FDA MedWatch for many non-vaccine injection problems.**  
   If the reaction followed a non-vaccine medical product, such as an injected medicine, biologic, or device-related product, FDA MedWatch is usually the federal reporting route for serious reactions or product problems. Reporting can wait until urgent medical needs are handled.

## What can wait
- Proving whether the vaccine or injection definitely caused the symptoms.
- Searching forums, debating risk, or trying to interpret adverse-event data.
- Decisions about future doses, boosters, or allergy evaluation.
- Complaints, compensation questions, insurance disputes, or legal steps.
- Getting every record perfect before you ask for medical help.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to be certain that the vaccine or injection caused the symptoms before you seek help. Getting checked is a reasonable safety step when symptoms are intense, unusual, or worsening.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation: getting urgent help if needed, getting same-day clinical guidance, and preserving useful details. Later decisions about future vaccination, allergy evaluation, or specialist review may need help from a qualified clinician.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not medical advice, diagnosis, legal advice, financial advice, therapeutic advice, or a substitute for professional care. If you feel seriously unwell, symptoms are worsening, or you are unsure what is safe, use urgent or emergency services.
