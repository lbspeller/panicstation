# What to do if… your smart lock or keypad shows repeated failed entry attempts while you are inside (USA)

## Short answer
Assume it could be someone trying to get in until you can confirm otherwise. Move everyone away from the entry door, stay inside, and call 911 if you think a break-in may be happening or you feel unsafe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door to see who it is.
- Do not confront anyone through the doorway.
- Do not go outside to check the porch, hallway, or entryway.
- Do not stand in front of windows or glass panels facing the door while you look.
- Do not remotely unlock the door to reset the lock.
- Do not announce that you are alone or say who is inside.
- Do not share an entry code if someone calls or messages saying they need it.
- Do not assume it is only a tech issue if the attempts keep happening like repeated guesses.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** Bring children and pets with you into an interior room, preferably one with a solid door. Take your phone and, if safely reachable, your keys.
2. **Create distance from the entry door.** Close and lock any interior doors between you and the entry if you can do that without going near the front door. Stay away from windows, glass panels, and direct sightlines to the entry.
3. **Check without approaching the door.** Use a doorbell camera, peephole camera, or indoor camera view if you already have one. If you can do it from where you are and it does not delay calling 911, save the clip or screenshot.
4. **Increase deterrence without exposing yourself.** If you can turn on exterior lights from your phone or a nearby switch away from the door, do it. Do not walk into view at the front of the home to reach a switch.
5. **Use lock safety features only if you already know them.** If your lock or app has a clear keypad-disable, lockout, or vacation setting and you can enable it without unlocking the door, use it. Avoid changing settings you are not sure about.
6. **Call 911 if there is any sign of an active attempt.** This includes hearing the handle or door being tested, seeing someone at the door, failed attempts continuing in real time, damage to the door, threats, or feeling at risk. Give your address and unit number first, then say what you are seeing or hearing.
7. **If you cannot speak safely, try text-to-911 if available.** A voice call is usually best if you can make one. If speaking would increase danger, text 911 with your address or unit number and what is happening. Text-to-911 is not available everywhere; if it is not supported, you may receive a bounce-back message telling you to contact 911 another way.
8. **If you are in an apartment or managed building, ask for help without leaving.** Contact building security, the front desk, or management to check hallways or cameras while you stay inside. If you have a trusted neighbor, ask them to call 911 if they see someone at your door; do not ask them to confront anyone.
9. **Keep key details ready.** Have your full address, unit number, the door being targeted, number of people inside, camera description, sounds you heard, and any urgent medical needs ready for the 911 call-taker.

## What can wait
- Resetting the lock.
- Changing codes or adding and removing users.
- Firmware updates or troubleshooting the app.
- Calling the manufacturer or installer.
- Digging through long activity logs.
- Trying to identify the person yourself.
- Posting publicly or coordinating a group response.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel shaken by repeated entry alerts, even if it later turns out to be a false alarm, a mistaken visitor, or a lock problem. Quietly creating distance, staying inside, and calling for help if you feel unsafe are protective first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate risk and buy time. Later decisions, such as police reports, lock changes, landlord issues, or security upgrades, may need specialist help once you are safe.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, technical, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe or unsure, treat it as an emergency and contact 911.
