# What to do if… your calls go straight to voicemail and your texts are delayed or not arriving (USA)

## Short answer
If this could put you or someone else at risk, use Wi-Fi Calling if it already works, a landline, or someone else’s phone to call 911. If it is not an immediate emergency, reconnect the phone to the network, check call-blocking settings, then check whether your carrier has an outage.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t factory reset or wipe your phone in panic.
- Don’t delete an eSIM unless your carrier tells you to; you may make reconnection harder.
- Don’t share verification codes with anyone “helping”; delayed texts can make scams more confusing.
- Don’t assume the phone is broken until you have checked settings, signal, outage status, and your account line.
- Don’t keep making non-urgent test calls over and over during an outage; keep attempts brief and save battery.
- Don’t buy boosters, replace the phone, or switch carriers today just because this started suddenly.

## What to do now
1. **Get a working route for urgent contact first.**
   - For emergencies, call **911** from any working phone.
   - If your mobile line is failing, try Wi-Fi Calling if it is already enabled and working, a landline, or someone nearby’s phone.
   - Text 911 only if calling is not safe or not possible and your local 911 center accepts texts; include your location first.

2. **Check the obvious call-divert and silent settings.**
   - Turn off Do Not Disturb, Focus, Sleep, Driving mode, or any similar quiet mode.
   - Check that the important caller is not blocked or marked as spam.
   - Turn off call forwarding if it is on.
   - Temporarily turn off settings that silence unknown callers or send spam-risk calls away.
   - Check whether Bluetooth headphones, a car system, or a watch is taking the ring alert.

3. **Force a fresh network connection.**
   - Turn Airplane Mode on, wait about 5–10 seconds, then turn it off.
   - Power the phone fully off, wait about 20 seconds, then turn it back on.
   - Wait a minute after restart before testing, because the phone may need time to register on the network.

4. **Test in one different place.**
   - Step outside, near a window, or a short distance away from the building.
   - Ask one trusted person to call and text you once.
   - Note whether the problem happens only indoors, only in one area, or everywhere.

5. **Check whether this is a carrier or account problem.**
   - Use Wi-Fi to open your carrier’s app or outage/status page.
   - Check that your line is active and there is no account, billing, plan, or service block.
   - If you can, ask someone on the same carrier nearby whether their calls or texts are also delayed.

6. **Check the SIM or eSIM without making risky changes.**
   - Physical SIM: power off, remove and reinsert the SIM, then power on again.
   - eSIM: check that the cellular line is turned on and selected for calls and messages.
   - Do not delete, replace, or transfer an eSIM unless your carrier gives you that instruction.

7. **Check the message side if texts are the main problem.**
   - Confirm your messaging app is the default SMS app if you are on Android.
   - Check that the sender is not blocked.
   - If you recently moved from iPhone to Android, check whether your number is still tied to iMessage.
   - If you recently changed messaging apps, check whether RCS or chat features need to be turned off or reset.

8. **Do one deeper fix only after the simple checks.**
   - Install pending phone software or carrier settings updates over Wi-Fi.
   - If the problem still continues, reset network settings. Expect saved Wi-Fi networks, Wi-Fi passwords, VPN settings, and APN settings to be cleared.

9. **Contact your carrier with a short summary.**
   - Say: “Incoming calls go straight to voicemail, and SMS texts are delayed or missing.”
   - Give your ZIP code, nearby cross street or general area, device model, SIM or eSIM type, and the times it happened.
   - Ask them to check local outages, tower issues, account blocks, line provisioning, SIM/eSIM status, and whether the SIM or eSIM needs replacement.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to replace the phone, switch carriers, buy a booster, or change your number.
- You do not need to troubleshoot every advanced setting right now.
- You do not need to file a complaint right away; first write down a few examples with dates, times, locations, and whether calls, texts, or both failed.
- You do not need to keep testing constantly. A few careful tests are enough to describe the problem to your carrier.

## Important reassurance
This pattern can happen when a phone loses network registration, when a carrier has a local issue, when a line or SIM needs attention, or when a setting sends calls away from the phone. Many cases are fixed by simple reconnection steps or by the carrier refreshing the line.

## Scope note
These are first steps to restore calling and texting and avoid irreversible changes. If the problem keeps happening across multiple locations or several days, later decisions may need carrier support, device-maker support, SIM or eSIM replacement, or repair diagnostics.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, technical, or professional advice. If you cannot reliably place or receive calls and someone may be in danger, use another working communication method immediately.
