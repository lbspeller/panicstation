# What to do if… your child may have swallowed a medicine or unknown substance but seems fine so far (USA)

## Short answer
Call **Poison Control immediately at 1-800-222-1222** even if there are no symptoms, and if the child is unconscious, seizing, or struggling to breathe, call **911**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for symptoms to appear before seeking help.
- Do not induce vomiting or use syrup of ipecac.
- Do not give the child activated charcoal unless specifically instructed by a professional.
- Do not give water, milk, or food unless Poison Control tells you to.
- Do not follow first-aid instructions on the product packaging, as these may be outdated.
- Do not delay calling to calculate exact dosages or missing pill counts.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate life-threatening symptoms and call 911 if they occur.** Seek emergency help if the child:
   - Is unconscious or cannot be awakened.
   - Has trouble breathing or is turning blue.
   - Is having a seizure or convulsions.
   - Has an obstructed airway (choking).
2. **Remove the substance from the child and have them spit out any remaining pieces.** Gently clear any visible material from the mouth with your fingers and wipe their face and lips.
3. **Call Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222 or use the webPOISONCONTROL online tool.** If the child is stable, call the national hotline; it is free, confidential, and available 24/7.
4. **Gather the product container and basic information while you are on the phone.** Have the following ready:
   - The exact product name and strength (mg/ml) from the label.
   - The estimated amount missing and the time of ingestion.
   - The child's age and approximate weight.
   - Any current health conditions, allergies, or other medications the child takes.
5. **Rinse any skin or eyes if they were exposed to the substance.** For skin, remove contaminated clothing and rinse with lukewarm water for 15 minutes. For eyes, rinse with a steady stream of room-temperature water for 15 minutes.
6. **Watch for evolving sensory cues while awaiting further instruction.** Note any of the following to report to professionals:
   - Unusual odors on the child's breath.
   - Pupils that are unusually large (dilated) or small (pinpoint).
   - New blisters, burns, or redness around the mouth or on the skin.
   - Sudden drowsiness, irritability, or loss of coordination.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the exact number of pills missing before calling.
- You do not need to decide if an ER visit is necessary; Poison Control will triage the risk for you.
- You do not need to identify the substance if it is unknown; specialists can provide guidance based on symptoms and physical cues.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel panicked when you find a child with a potentially dangerous substance. Many accidental ingestions in children are managed safely at home under professional guidance, provided that help is sought immediately before symptoms escalate.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage guidance only. It is not a substitute for clinical diagnosis or the specific treatment instructions provided by Poison Control or emergency medical services.

## Important note
Accidental ingestion requires immediate professional consultation. Poison Control (1-800-222-1222) is the primary resource for non-life-threatening exposures. Always call 911 for acute physical distress.
