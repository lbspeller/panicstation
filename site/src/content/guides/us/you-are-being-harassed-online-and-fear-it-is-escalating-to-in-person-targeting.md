# What to do if… you are being harassed online and fear it is escalating to in-person targeting (USA)
## Short answer
Avoid replying for now. Preserve threatening messages, reduce exposed information that could reveal where you are, and call 911 if you believe there is an immediate physical danger.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t argue, negotiate, or threaten back. Replying may escalate the situation or create more material.
- Don’t post your location, routine, workplace, school, or travel plans while trying to explain what is happening.
- Don’t delete threatening messages before saving what you can safely preserve.
- Don’t click links, open unknown files, or accept unexpected calls or invitations sent by the person targeting you.
- Don’t assume online targeting is harmless if someone mentions your address, workplace, family, routine, or local area.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether you are physically safe.**  
   If someone says they are nearby or coming now, appears to be following you, or you believe there is an immediate physical danger, move to a safer place if you can do so safely and call **911**.

2. **Tell one trusted person what is happening.**  
   Ask them to stay reachable. If you are at work, school, or a building with a front desk or security team, tell staff that you are concerned about unexpected visitors and ask them not to confirm whether you are there.

3. **Start a simple incident log.**  
   Record the date and time, platform, account name, profile URL, what happened, and any mention of your address, workplace, family, routine, or location.

4. **Preserve electronic threats before changing settings, if you can do so safely.**  
   - Save screenshots that show the sender, date, time, and surrounding context.
   - Save links, profile pages, emails, direct messages, and voicemails.
   - Keep the original messages where possible.
   - Store copies somewhere you control.

5. **Reduce exposed location information, if it is safe to make changes.**  
   - Restrict who can view, message, tag, or comment on your accounts.
   - Remove public posts that reveal your location, workplace, school, routine, or travel plans.
   - Turn off location sharing and geotagging that could reveal where you are.
   - Ask friends and family not to tag you, share your location, or reply to the person targeting you.

6. **Secure key accounts from a device you believe is safe.**  
   - Start with your email account, then secure important social accounts.
   - Use strong, unique passwords.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication.
   - Review recovery phone numbers and email addresses.
   - Sign out of other sessions or devices where the service offers that option.
   - If you think someone may be monitoring a device or account, use a different device or seek specialist support before changing settings.

7. **Report specific posts and messages to the platform.**  
   After preserving what you can, report threats, posted personal information, and impersonation. Use block, restrict, or mute tools if they are appropriate for your situation. Keep a note of what you reported and when.

8. **Treat a swatting threat as urgent.**  
   If someone threatens to make a false emergency report about your home, workplace, school, or another location linked to you, retain usernames, messages, websites, photos, videos, and platform details. Report the threat to your local law enforcement agency. Call **911** for an emergency or immediate threat to life. You may also use the FBI reporting routes listed in the linked FBI swatting guidance.

9. **Use local police routes for threats or signs of escalation.**  
   Call **911** for immediate physical danger. Otherwise, contact your local police department through its non-emergency route. Ask whether a report can be made, how to add later incidents, and whether a reference number is available.

10. **Use VictimConnect when you are physically safe.**  
   VictimConnect offers support, information, and local referrals for victims of crime in the United States and its territories. Call or text **1-855-484-2846**, or use its online chat. If you are in immediate danger, call **911** instead.

11. **Use IC3 for cyber-enabled crime reports.**  
   The FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center collects reports about cyber-enabled crime. You may file a report even if you are unsure whether your complaint qualifies. Use IC3 as an additional route, not instead of local police reporting about threats or escalation, and not instead of calling **911** for immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide right now whether to delete your accounts, change your phone number, move, or seek a court order.
- You do **not** need to reply publicly, explain yourself online, or convince other people that the situation is serious.
- A wider clean-up of old posts, unused accounts, and search results can wait until you feel safer and have support.

## Important reassurance
Feeling exposed or on edge after online targeting is understandable. Taking a few practical steps now can reduce immediate risk and give you more time to decide what to do next.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: reach a safer pause, preserve what you can, reduce exposed information, and connect with support. Later decisions may need specialist help and can vary by state and situation.

## Important note
This guide provides general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Call 911 for immediate physical danger. Otherwise, use your local police department’s non-emergency route for guidance about reporting.
