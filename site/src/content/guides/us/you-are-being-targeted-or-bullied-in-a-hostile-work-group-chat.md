# What to do if… you are being targeted or bullied in a hostile work group chat (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before replying. Mute notifications, preserve a minimal record in a way permitted by workplace policy, and move the issue to a private workplace route such as a manager who is not involved, HR, or another reporting channel.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to win the argument in the group chat. A public back-and-forth may intensify the exchange and make the record harder to understand.
- Do not delete or alter messages.
- Do not send screenshots widely around the workplace or post them online.
- Do not move the exchange into an informal private argument by call, text, or direct message.
- Do not make threats about lawsuits, firing, or retaliation in the chat.
- Do not share confidential workplace information while explaining your position.

## What to do now
1. **Create a pause.** Mute notifications and step away from the thread. Unless there is an urgent safety issue, do not reply until you can write calmly.
2. **Preserve a minimal record.** Keep the clearest messages showing the date, time, participants, and escalating language. Use a method permitted by workplace policy. If you are unsure what you may copy, note the dates and times and ask your workplace contact how to retain the record. Do not delete or alter messages.
3. **Send one neutral line only if a response is needed.** For example: “I am stepping back from this thread and will address the issue through the appropriate workplace channel.”
4. **Move the issue out of the group chat.** Contact a manager who is not involved, HR, another designated workplace contact, or a union representative if you have one. Briefly state what happened, when it happened, who was involved, and that the tone is escalating.
5. **Ask for one immediate practical step.** For example:
   - “Please tell me which workplace channel to use for this issue.”
   - “Please move work instructions out of this group chat.”
   - “Please confirm how I should preserve the relevant messages.”
   - “Please address the escalating conduct in the thread.”
6. **Record exact wording without trying to classify it in the moment.** If messages include slurs, sexual comments, threats, or comments about a personal characteristic, note the exact language, date, time, and sender. Federal employment-discrimination law covers harassment based on specified characteristics, and state or local protections may also apply.
7. **Keep pay and working-conditions discussions factual.** Some employees may have protections under the National Labor Relations Act when acting together about pay or working conditions. Coverage and limits vary, so avoid assuming that every message or role is covered.
8. **Note any negative action after you raise the issue.** Keep a brief factual record if your shifts, duties, access, treatment, or employment status change after you report possible discrimination-related harassment.
9. **Prioritize immediate safety if there is a credible threat.** Leave the area if needed, contact workplace security if available, and dial **911** for an emergency requiring immediate police, fire, or ambulance assistance.

## What can wait
- Writing a long narrative or collecting every message. Start with the clearest examples.
- Deciding whether to make an external complaint. That can wait for the immediate pause, but do not assume any filing deadline stops running.
- Directly confronting the person who escalated the thread.
- Finding the perfect wording or policy citation.
- Big decisions such as quitting or transferring roles.

## Important reassurance
A fast-moving public work thread can feel overwhelming. You do not need to solve the entire situation inside the chat. A calm pause, a minimal record, and a move to a safer workplace channel are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from HR, a union representative, an employment advisor, or a lawyer depending on the situation.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Laws, workplace procedures, and available routes can vary by state, employer type, and role. For an emergency requiring immediate assistance, dial 911.
