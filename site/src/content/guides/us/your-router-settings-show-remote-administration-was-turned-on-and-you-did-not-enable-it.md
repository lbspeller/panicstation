# What to do if… your router settings show remote administration was turned on and you did not enable it (USA)

## Short answer
Physically disconnect the device from power or the internet immediately, then use a separate, secure device to change your account password and log out all active sessions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave the device powered on while investigating the cause or monitoring its behavior.
- Do not perform a factory reset until after you have secured the controlling account and confirmed your own access.
- Do not use the same password for your smart home account and the email account associated with it.
- Do not click links in emails claiming to be "security alerts" or "password resets" you did not request.
- Do not share two-factor authentication (2FA) codes or one-time passwords (OTP) with anyone over the phone or via text.
- Do not ignore "unknown login" notifications even if the device appears to be working normally now.

## What to do now
1. **Cut off the device's connection to your home network.** 
   - Unplug the device from the wall outlet or remove its batteries.
   - If the device controls home access (like a smart lock), ensure you have physical keys in hand before cutting power or disabling the bridge.
   - For hardwired devices, you may need to disable the Wi-Fi signal via your router's administration panel.

2. **Secure the controlling account from a trusted, separate device.** 
   - Log in to the manufacturer's app or website (e.g., Amazon, Google, Apple, Ring) on a device you know is secure.
   - **Change the password** to a unique, long phrase that has never been used before.
   - Select the option to **"Log out of all other sessions"** or "De-register all devices" to force the attacker out.
   - Enable **two-factor authentication (2FA)** if it was not already active, prioritizing authenticator apps over SMS.

3. **Check for persistence in account recovery and sharing settings.** 
   - Review the "Shared Users" or "Household Members" list and remove any unfamiliar names or email addresses.
   - Verify that your **recovery email address** and **recovery phone number** have not been changed.
   - Look for linked third-party services (like IFTTT or older voice assistant links) and revoke access to any you do not actively use.

4. **Verify the security of the associated email account.** 
   - Ensure the email account used for the smart home login is not also compromised.
   - Check for **email forwarding rules** or **filters** that might be sending your security alerts or password reset emails to an attacker's inbox.

5. **Clean up compromised automations and routines.** 
   - Review all scheduled routines or scenes (e.g., "Away Mode," "Nighttime Routine").
   - Delete any automations you did not create, especially those that disable cameras, unlock doors, or change privacy settings.

6. **Secure the home router and network gateway.** 
   - Change the **router administrator password** (different from your Wi-Fi password).
   - Update the router's firmware to the latest version to patch known vulnerabilities.
   - Create a **Guest Network** for your IoT/smart devices to isolate them from your primary computers and sensitive data.

7. **Report the incident to the appropriate federal authorities.** 
   - For general unauthorized access or scams, file a report at **ReportFraud.ftc.gov**.
   - If the breach involved financial loss or significant cybercrime, file a complaint at **www.ic3.gov** (the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center).
   - If you suspect your Social Security number or personal identity was stolen during the breach, visit **IdentityTheft.gov**.

## What can wait
- Replacing the physical hardware or upgrading to a different brand.
- Determining the exact technical method the attacker used to gain entry.
- Closing the account permanently, unless you are unable to secure it after following the steps above.

## Important reassurance
Smart devices frequently exhibit "odd" behavior due to software glitches, cloud outages, or outdated firmware. Taking these steps is a standard safety precaution that stops potential unauthorized access without causing permanent damage to your smart home setup.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and containment for suspected account takeovers. It does not cover deep network forensics or recovery from malware infections on your primary computer or smartphone.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal, financial, or professional cybersecurity advice. If you believe your physical safety is at risk due to compromised security systems or locks, contact local law enforcement immediately.
