# What to do if… a tree has uprooted or is leaning toward your home after severe weather (USA)

## Short answer
Evacuate the fall zone (twice the tree’s height), maintain a 35-foot clearance from any wires, and call 911 if power lines are involved or the structure is hit.

## Do not do these things
- Don't stand or walk under the leaning side of the tree or near a lifted root plate.
- Don't touch, approach, or drive over any downed wires (assume all are energized).
- Don't attempt to "brace" the tree with vehicles, ropes, or DIY supports.
- Don't use a chainsaw or ladder on a storm-damaged tree.
- Don't enter a home that has been structurally impacted until cleared by responders.
- Don't allow pets or children to roam the yard until the area is professionally stabilized.

## What to do now
1. **Establish a safety perimeter of twice the tree's height.**
   - **Identify the "fall zone"** based on the tree's total height and direction of lean.
   - **Clear all people and pets** from this zone immediately.
   - **Avoid "root heave" areas** where the soil is mounded or lifting, as these are unstable.

2. **Isolate electrical hazards and maintain distance.**
   - **Stay at least 35 feet away** from any downed or low-hanging wires.
   - **Call 911 immediately** if the tree is touching a power line or if you see sparking or smoke.
   - **Shuffle your feet** (keeping both feet on the ground) or hop with feet together if you must move away from an area where a wire has just fallen to avoid "step potential" shocks.

3. **Evacuate rooms directly in the potential fall path.**
   - **Move occupants to the opposite side of the house** or leave the building if no area is outside the fall zone.
   - **Listen for popping or cracking sounds**, which may indicate imminent trunk or root failure.

4. **Notify the electric utility and local services.**
   - **Report the specific pole number** if visible from a safe distance when calling the utility company.
   - **Contact 311 or Public Works** only if the tree is in the public right-of-way (street or sidewalk) and not causing an immediate life-safety emergency.

5. **Contact an ISA Certified Arborist for a hazard assessment.**
   - **Verify credentials** through the International Society of Arboriculture (ISA) "Verify an Arborist" tool.
   - **Request an emergency "Level 3" assessment** for trees leaning toward targets (homes, lines, or roads).

6. **Alert 811 if the tree is uprooted.**
   - **Call 811 (utility marking service)** to report an uprooted tree, as the root ball may have pulled up or damaged underground gas, water, or electric lines.

7. **Take photos from a safe distance for insurance.**
   - **Capture the angle of the lean** and any visible damage to the roof or siding without entering the fall zone.
   - **Contact your homeowners insurance carrier** to open a claim for "emergency mitigation" to cover the cost of professional stabilization.

## What can wait
- Liability and responsibility determinations between neighbors.
- Full debris removal and wood chipping.
- Cosmetic pruning or non-emergency landscaping.
- Final structural repair estimates for the home.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel an urgent need to "save" the tree or clear the mess immediately. However, storm-stressed trees are under extreme tension and can shift or snap without warning. Prioritizing distance and professional assessment is the only way to prevent secondary injuries after the storm has passed.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for hazardous trees following a weather event. It does not cover technical tree removal procedures, long-term tree health recovery, or permanent structural repairs.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization only and is not a substitute for an on-site inspection by an ISA Certified Arborist or emergency responders. Always treat power lines as live until a utility representative confirms they are de-energized.
