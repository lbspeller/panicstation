# What to do if… an investigator calls and asks you to clear something up by phone immediately (USA)

## Short answer
You do not have to answer questions on the call just because someone asks you to. Keep it brief, verify the contact through an official public number, and speak with a lawyer before giving an interview or statement.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t try to “clear it up” on the phone because the caller sounds informal, friendly, or rushed.
- Don’t guess what they are asking about or fill gaps with speculation.
- Don’t lie, exaggerate, or smooth over details.
- Don’t agree to a recorded or informal interview before getting legal advice.
- Don’t let “right now” pressure you into answering questions.
- Don’t rely on a badge number, case number, caller ID, or callback number from the caller as proof.
- Don’t give passwords, device access, records, money, payment details, or personal identifying information on the call.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the call and say very little.**  
   Say: “I’m not answering questions right now.”  
   If you have a lawyer, say: “Please contact my attorney to arrange anything further.”  
   If you do not have a lawyer, say: “Please send any request in writing.”  
   Then end the call.

2. **Write down the contact details without discussing the case.**  
   Note the caller’s name, agency, office location, phone number, badge or ID number if offered, case or reference number, and the exact words they used, such as “clear something up” or “just a few questions.”

3. **Verify the contact independently.**  
   Look up the agency’s official public website or main phone number yourself. Call the main switchboard or listed field office and ask whether that person works there and is trying to reach you. Do not use only the number, link, or transfer the caller gave you.

4. **Use one script if they keep pressing.**  
   Repeat: “I’m not answering questions without a lawyer.”  
   You can also say: “I want to remain silent.”  
   Do not explain why, debate the facts, or offer your side of the story on the call.

5. **If they come to your door, ask whether you are free to leave.**  
   Ask: “Am I being detained, or am I free to leave?”  
   If you are free to leave, end the interaction calmly and leave or close the door.  
   If you are detained or arrested, say: “I want a lawyer,” and stop answering questions. Do not argue or physically resist.

6. **Do not consent to searches or access on the spot.**  
   If they ask to look at your phone, computer, home, car, records, accounts, messages, or passwords, say: “I do not consent to that. I need legal advice first.”

7. **Save what you received.**  
   Keep voicemails, texts, emails, call logs, letters, cards, and screenshots. Make a short factual note of the date, time, number used, what they asked for, any deadline or threat, and what you said.

8. **Switch to safety help if the contact feels threatening or fake.**  
   If someone is impersonating law enforcement, demanding money, demanding personal information, or threatening immediate harm, end the contact and verify through a trusted official number. Use 911 only for immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you will do an interview.
- You do not need to assemble your full side of the story right now.
- You do not need to search your phone, computer, messages, or files in a panic.
- You do not need to contact witnesses, friends, coworkers, or family to compare stories.
- You do not need to post online or ask social media for legal takes.

## Important reassurance
A pause is not an admission of guilt. It is normal to feel alarmed when someone with official-sounding authority wants immediate answers, and slowing the interaction down is a standard protective step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for surprise investigator contact by phone or a related in-person follow-up. Later decisions may depend on the agency, state, whether you are a witness or suspect, and whether there is a subpoena, warrant, arrest, or other legal order.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Laws and practices vary by state and situation, especially around identification requirements, searches, recording calls, subpoenas, warrants, and arrests.
