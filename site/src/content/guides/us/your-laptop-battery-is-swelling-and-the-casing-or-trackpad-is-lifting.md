# What to do if… your laptop battery is swelling and the casing or trackpad is lifting (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using the laptop now: shut it down, unplug it, and move it to a cool place away from anything that can burn. Treat it as a damaged lithium-ion battery until the manufacturer, a qualified repair shop, or your local household hazardous waste program tells you what to do next.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using it, charging it, or plugging it back in to check whether it still works.
- Don’t press the trackpad or casing back down, clamp it, tape it shut, or force the lid closed.
- Don’t puncture, bend, crush, pry, or squeeze the battery or laptop casing.
- Don’t open the laptop or remove an internal battery unless the manufacturer says it is user-removable and it comes out without force.
- Don’t put the laptop or battery in household trash, curbside recycling, or an ordinary battery drop box that has not confirmed it accepts damaged batteries.
- Don’t leave it on a bed, sofa, paper pile, carpet, or near curtains, chargers, fuels, or other items that can burn.
- Don’t mail or ship it unless the manufacturer, recycler, or disposal program gives you specific instructions for a damaged lithium-ion battery.

## What to do now
1. **Shut it down.** Use the normal shutdown option if you can do that without pressure on the swollen area. If it is frozen, hold the power button until it turns off.
2. **Unplug it and leave it unplugged.** Disconnect the charger, dock, USB devices, external drives, and anything else attached to the laptop.
3. **Move it only if you can do so safely.** Carry it gently without squeezing the raised casing or trackpad. Put it on tile, concrete, stone, or another hard non-flammable surface in a cool, ventilated area away from people, pets, and things that can burn.
4. **Watch for signs that it is getting worse.** If you notice smoke, hissing, popping, a strong chemical smell, leaking, sparks, flame, or rapid heating, stop handling it and move people away.
5. **Call 911 if there is smoke, fire, rapid heating, or an imminent fire concern.** Tell the dispatcher it is a swollen lithium-ion laptop battery.
6. **Do not turn it back on for data.** If files matter, ask the manufacturer or a reputable repair shop whether they can remove the storage drive or recover data without energizing the swollen battery.
7. **Contact the manufacturer or retailer.** Say: “The laptop battery is swollen and the trackpad or casing is lifting.” Ask for their handling, repair, replacement, recall, and disposal instructions.
8. **Check the correct disposal route before transport.** Contact your local household hazardous waste program, local solid waste agency, state waste management agency, manufacturer, or approved electronics recycler and ask whether they accept a swollen or damaged lithium-ion laptop battery.
9. **Prepare for transport only if the item is stable and accepted.** Keep the laptop powered off. If the battery is already separate and removable without force, cover exposed terminals with non-conductive tape or place the battery in its own plastic bag unless the disposal program gives different instructions. If the battery is internal, do not open the laptop; transport the whole laptop only as instructed by the accepting program.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose why the battery swelled.
- You do not need to run battery health apps, updates, antivirus scans, or backups right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to repair, replace, sell, wipe, or recycle the laptop.
- You do not need to press the casing flat to make it look normal before service.

## Important reassurance
A swollen laptop battery is alarming, but the safest first steps are simple: power off, unplug, isolate, and get instructions from a qualified route. Acting early and not forcing the casing or battery helps reduce the chance of a worse incident.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repair, data recovery, warranty, replacement, shipping, or disposal may need help from the manufacturer, a qualified repair shop, your local household hazardous waste program, or an approved electronics recycler.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, technical, fire-safety, disposal, or other professional advice. If there is smoke, fire, rapid heating, or immediate danger, prioritize people’s safety and contact emergency services.
