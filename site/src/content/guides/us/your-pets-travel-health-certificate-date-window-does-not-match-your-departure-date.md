# What to do if… your pet’s travel health certificate date window does not match your departure date (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before check-in and compare the certificate’s exam, signature, issue, and endorsement dates with your exact departure and arrival dates. If any date may be outside the required window, contact your USDA-accredited veterinarian before travelling.

## Do not do these things
- Do not edit the certificate yourself or ask anyone to change a date informally.
- Do not assume an airline, airport agent, or border official will overlook a date mismatch.
- Do not book a replacement appointment before asking whether the certificate needs USDA APHIS endorsement or whether an existing VEHCS certificate can be corrected.
- Do not rely on social media timelines; date windows vary by destination country and sometimes by travel type.
- Do not send your pet ahead, change the route, or add a transit stop without checking whether the paperwork rules change.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the exact dates and times.**
   - Vet exam date
   - Certificate signature or issue date
   - USDA APHIS endorsement date, if there is one
   - U.S. departure date and time
   - Destination arrival date and time
   - Any transit stops, especially if the pet leaves the airport or clears customs

2. **Check the official APHIS page for your destination country.**
   Look for whether your destination requires:
   - An export health certificate
   - USDA APHIS endorsement
   - A certificate signed within a specific window
   - A window counted from exam, issue, signature, endorsement, departure, or arrival

3. **Call your USDA-accredited veterinarian with the dates in front of you.**
   Ask:
   - “Using the destination country’s rules, is this certificate still valid for my current departure and arrival?”
   - “If it is not valid, is the compliant fix a correction, a VEHCS re-issue request, a new certificate, or a new exam?”

4. **If the certificate is already in VEHCS, ask about re-issue only if the problem is an error.**
   A VEHCS re-issue request is generally for replacing an endorsed or completed certificate because of an error on the health certificate. If the issue is that the certificate date is now outside the destination’s allowed window, ask your veterinarian what the destination requires next.

5. **Ask the airline what it will check at pet check-in.**
   Ask:
   - “Will you check the certificate against departure time, arrival time, or both?”
   - “Do you require proof of USDA APHIS endorsement for this route?”
   - “Do you need any airline-specific pet form in addition to the health certificate?”
   Write down the agent’s name, date, time, and answer.

6. **Choose the lowest-risk compliant fix.**
   If the certificate is outside the window, aim to make the paperwork clearly valid before the airport. That may mean a corrected certificate, a VEHCS re-issue request, a new certificate process, or moving the travel date to fit the rules.

7. **If your pet is a dog entering or returning to the U.S., re-check CDC dog import paperwork.**
   A CDC Dog Import Form is required for each dog being brought into the U.S. Rules can change depending on whether the dog has been in a high-risk country for dog rabies in the 6 months before U.S. entry. If your itinerary added a country or transit stop, treat that as a reason to re-check.

## What can wait
- You do not need to redesign the whole trip right now.
- You do not need to decide between cargo, cabin, or a different route unless the paperwork fix requires it.
- You do not need to call several agencies at once; start with the destination rules, your USDA-accredited veterinarian, and the airline’s check-in requirements.
- You do not need to argue at the airport as the first plan; the goal is to arrive with paperwork that is clearly valid.

## Important reassurance
This is a fixable paperwork problem if you catch it before travel. The next useful step is not panic or blame; it is getting the exact dates checked by the veterinarian who can issue or correct the certificate.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from your USDA-accredited veterinarian, APHIS Veterinary Export Trade Services, the airline, destination authorities, or a specialist pet shipper.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, veterinary, travel, or professional advice. Pet travel requirements can vary by destination, airline, route, species, and travel type, so check the official rules for your exact trip.
