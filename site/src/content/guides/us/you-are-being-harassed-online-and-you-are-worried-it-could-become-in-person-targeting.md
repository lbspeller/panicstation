# What to do if… you are being harassed online and you are worried it could become in-person targeting (USA)

## Short answer
Treat any mention of your physical location or routine as a safety risk, stop all engagement with the harasser, and preserve evidence immediately. If you believe there is an immediate threat to your physical safety, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not argue, negotiate, or threaten the harasser as this creates more material for targeting.
- Do not post your current location, routine, or travel plans to explain your side of the story.
- Do not delete messages or accounts before you have saved a complete record of the evidence.
- Do not click links or open attachments sent by the harasser or unknown accounts.
- Do not assume threats are "just online" if the harasser mentions your workplace, home, or family.

## What to do now
1. **Do a quick safety check to ensure you are not in immediate danger.**  
   If you believe someone is following you, watching your home, or has made a credible "on my way" threat, move to a populated indoor area and call **911**.

2. **Tell one trusted person about the situation and make a plan for the next 24 hours.**  
   Ask a friend or family member to stay reachable; if you live alone, consider staying with someone else.
   - If you are at work or school, notify security or the front desk that you are concerned about an unauthorized visitor.

3. **Start a simple incident log to document the pattern of behavior.**  
   **Record the date, time, platform, and a brief description of why the specific event felt threatening.**  
   - Federal stalking laws often require proving a "course of conduct" (two or more related acts).

4. **Preserve evidence before you change your privacy settings.**  
   **Capture screenshots that show the harasser's username, the date/timestamp, and the full context of the threat.**  
   - Save direct URLs to the profiles and specific posts.
   - Keep original emails, voicemails, and direct messages rather than just screenshots.

5. **Reduce your digital exposure by performing a fast privacy sweep.**  
   **Set all social media accounts to private and restrict who can send you direct messages or comments.**  
   - Remove mentions of your workplace, school, or city from your bios.
   - Turn off location sharing and GPS tagging in your camera and social media settings.

6. **Secure your core accounts starting with your primary email address.**  
   **Change your passwords to unique, complex strings and enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on every platform.**  
   - Use the "Sign out of all other sessions" feature if available to ensure no one else is logged in.

7. **Report the harassment to the platforms where it is occurring.**  
   **Use the internal reporting tools for specific violations like doxxing, impersonation, or violent threats.**  
   - Keep a record of when you reported the content and any confirmation numbers received.

8. **Contact local law enforcement through their non-emergency line for a report.**  
   **Tell the officer you are concerned about online harassment escalating to in-person targeting and request a case number.**  
   - This creates an official paper trail that can be used if you need to seek a protective order later.

9. **Contact VictimConnect for confidential support and referral options.**  
   **Call or text 1-855-484-2846 to speak with a specialist who can help you navigate your rights and local resources.**  
   - Use this service when you are in a physically safe location.

10. **File a report with federal authorities if the situation involves interstate threats or cybercrime.**  
    **Submit a tip at tips.fbi.gov for immediate threats of violence, or use ic3.gov for cyber-enabled crimes like hacking or extortion.**  
    - Federal jurisdiction often applies when the harasser is in a different state.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to permanently delete your social media presence.
- You do not need to respond to the harasser's "accusations" or defend your reputation publicly.
- You do not need to research the harasser's identity yourself; leave investigations to professionals.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a high level of distress when your digital privacy is breached or your physical safety is threatened. Taking these practical steps to distance yourself and document the situation is a protective and appropriate response.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate safety and evidence preservation. Legal requirements for stalking and harassment vary by state, and later steps may require consultation with legal professionals or victim advocates.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance and does not constitute legal advice. If you are in immediate danger or believe a crime is in progress, call 911.
