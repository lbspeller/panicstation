# What to do if… a partner pressures you to take intimate photos or videos you do not want to create (USA)

## Short answer
Physically distance yourself from the person pressuring you and contact a confidential advocate to discuss safety before taking any action that could increase your risk.

## Do not do these things
- Do not create, record, or send any intimate media as a way to "negotiate" or stop the pressure.
- Do not let them hold your phone or gain access to your passcodes, even for a moment.
- Do not delete messages or call logs if you believe you may need evidence of coercion or threats later.
- Do not meet the person in a private location to discuss the situation if you feel unsafe or controlled.
- Do not ignore your gut feeling that something is wrong; discomfort is a valid boundary.

## What to do now
1. **Physically distance yourself from the person and the device if possible.** Move to a separate room, a public space, or a trusted friend's home to break the immediate cycle of pressure. If you are in immediate physical danger, call **911**.
2. **State one final boundary and then stop responding.** Say or text: "I am not doing this. Do not ask me again." Do not engage in an argument or offer justifications, as this often invites more manipulation.
3. **Contact a confidential crisis resource for a safety assessment.**
   - Call the **National Domestic Violence Hotline** at **800-799-7233** or text **"START"** to **88788** for support regarding relationship control and coercion.
   - Contact the **RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline** at **800-656-HOPE** or via online chat if the pressure involves sexual acts or threats.
4. **Use proactive tools to block the sharing of images if you are worried they already exist.** Use **StopNCII.org** (for adults) to create a digital "fingerprint" of the images on your own device; this prevents them from being uploaded to participating major social media and adult platforms.
5. **If you are under 18 or the images involve a minor, use NCMEC services immediately.** Report the threat or the image to the **CyberTipline** at **cybertipline.org** and use the **Take It Down** tool to help remove content from the internet.
6. **Notify platforms of threats or shared content under the TAKE IT DOWN Act.** As of 2026, federal law requires "covered platforms" to remove non-consensual intimate imagery within 48 hours of a valid request. Use the platform’s internal reporting tool and mention the **TAKE IT DOWN Act (TIDA)**.
7. **Secure your digital accounts to prevent unauthorized access.** Change your email password first, then your phone passcode and social media passwords. Turn on **Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)** using an app like Google Authenticator rather than SMS if you suspect they have access to your texts.
8. **Keep a secure log of the pressure if it is safe to do so.** Take screenshots of demands, threats, or harassment. Store these in a hidden, password-protected folder or send them to a trusted person who does not know the partner.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to break up or stay in the relationship right now.
- You do not need to file a formal police report today unless you want to.
- You do not need to "prove" to the partner that their behavior is wrong; focus on your own safety instead.
- You do not need to have all the answers about how the images might be used in the future.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel trapped, confused, or scared when someone you trust ignores your boundaries. Coercion is not your fault, and your refusal to participate in something that makes you uncomfortable is a sign of strength, not a lack of love or trust. You are not alone in this experience.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for responding to digital sexual coercion and pressure. It does not replace legal advice, long-term counseling, or comprehensive safety planning for domestic abuse.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance and does not constitute legal or professional advice. Laws regarding non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) vary by state. If you are in immediate danger or being physically harmed, contact emergency services at **911**.
