# What to do if… you receive a link claiming to be an intimate photo of you and you fear it is real (USA)

## Short answer
Do **not** click the link, preview the image, or reply to the sender. Immediately block the account and use official federal reporting channels like IC3.gov or the 1-800-CALL-FBI line.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click the link to “check” if it is real; clicking can trigger malware, log your IP address, or signal to the sender that you are active and vulnerable.
- Don’t pay any money, send gift cards, or provide cryptocurrency, as this almost always leads to increased demands rather than the deletion of the image.
- Don’t delete the message or the account immediately, as you will need screenshots of the threat and the sender’s profile for law enforcement.
- Don’t engage in negotiations or try to “reason” with the sender, as they are using scripted psychological coercion.
- Don’t forward the link to friends or "reputation management" services that you found via social media ads, as many of these are predatory.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all interaction and do not touch the link.** **The most effective way to prevent escalation is to cease communication immediately without providing any further data to the sender.**
2. **Capture evidence while keeping the link closed.** **Take screenshots of the message, the sender’s username/handle, any profile details, and the visible text of the URL provided.**
   - Do not open the link to take these screenshots.
   - Note the platform, the date, and the time the message was received.
3. **Block the sender on all platforms.** **Use the platform’s built-in block and report tools to prevent them from contacting you or seeing your activity.**
   - If they contact you from a new account, block that one immediately as well without replying.
4. **Change your account security settings if you suspect a breach.** **If you previously clicked the link or entered a password, go to a different trusted device to update your passwords and enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).**
5. **Report the incident to the FBI and specialized services.** **File a formal report with the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) and, if the threat is persistent, contact your local FBI field office.**
   - Visit ic3.gov to file a cyber-enabled extortion report.
   - For specialized support with non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII), contact the Cyber Civil Rights Initiative (CCRI) at 844-878-2274.
6. **Use age-specific resources if the situation involves a minor.** **If you or the person in the photo are under 18, or were under 18 when the photo was taken, report immediately to the NCMEC CyberTipline.**
   - Use the "Take It Down" service at takeitdown.ncmec.org to help prevent the image from being shared on major platforms under the 2026 federal requirements.
7. **Seek immediate emotional or physical safety support.** **If you feel in immediate physical danger, call 911; if you are experiencing a mental health crisis, call or text 988 for the Suicide & Crisis Lifeline.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to tell your family or employer right now.
- You do not need to hunt for the image on the "dark web" or search engines; professionals can help with this later if necessary.
- You do not need to determine if the image is a "deepfake" or a real photo before you report the crime.
- You do not need to apologize to the sender or explain yourself.

## Important reassurance
These tactics are designed to trigger a "fight or flight" response to make you act quickly and bypass your better judgment. Feeling a sense of panic, shame, or dread is a normal biological response to a high-stress threat. By choosing not to click and instead taking a safe pause, you have already disrupted the sender's primary goal.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the first minutes and hours following a threat. It does not replace legal counsel or long-term digital forensic investigation.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. If you are in immediate danger or believe a crime is in progress, contact 1-800-CALL-FBI or 911. Federal laws implemented in 2026 require platforms to have clear notice-and-removal processes for intimate imagery.
