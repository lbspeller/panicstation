# What to do if… someone shares sexual rumours about you in a workplace or community group chat (USA)

## Short answer
Preserve the evidence immediately by taking screenshots of the messages, timestamps, and usernames, then report the conduct to your Human Resources (HR) department, Title IX coordinator, or group administrator to request the content be removed and the behavior stopped.

## Do not do these things
- Do not engage in an argument or "defend yourself" within the group chat while the situation is active.
- Do not delete the messages or leave the group until you have secured copies of the evidence.
- Do not forward the rumors to others to "show them what is being said" as this spreads the harm further.
- Do not attempt to confront the person responsible in a private or unrecorded setting.
- Do not assume that rumors shared on personal devices after hours are "outside work" if they involve colleagues.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause by disengaging from the platform.** Mute the notifications for the specific chat and move to a physical space where you are not being observed.
2. **Preserve evidence exactly as it appears.** Take screenshots or screen recordings that clearly show:
   - The specific text or images containing the rumors.
   - The date and time of the posts.
   - The full names or usernames of the person posting and any people reacting/replying.
   - The name of the group or the channel ID.
3. **Identify the correct reporting channel.** 
   - If at work: Consult your employee handbook for the "Harassment Policy" or "Code of Conduct" to find the designated reporting officer (HR, Compliance, or a 24/7 hotline).
   - If in a community group: Locate the "About" or "Members" section to find the group administrators or moderators.
   - If in a school or university: Contact the Title IX office or the Dean of Students.
4. **Submit a written report to initiate a record.** 
   - Use email or a formal reporting portal to ensure your complaint is timestamped.
   - Stick to the facts: "On [Date], [User] posted a message in [Group] claiming [Content of rumor]."
   - Specifically state that the conduct is "unwelcome," "harassing," and "sex-based."
5. **Request immediate intervention and preservation.** Explicitly ask the recipient of your report to:
   - Order the poster to stop spreading the rumors.
   - Remove the harassing content from the platform.
   - Preserve the platform's internal logs of the conversation for future investigation.
6. **Inquire about temporary safety measures.** Ask for practical adjustments while the matter is investigated, such as:
   - Changes in shift schedules or remote work options to avoid the harasser.
   - Instruction for the harasser to have "no contact" with you.
   - A different reporting line if your direct supervisor is involved in the rumors.
7. **Note federal and state filing deadlines.** 
   - For most private-sector employees, you generally have **180 days** (extended to **300 days** in some states) to file a charge with the EEOC.
   - **Federal agency employees** must generally contact an EEO Counselor within **45 days** of the incident.
8. **Contact a confidential crisis resource.** If you are experiencing high distress or need help navigating your next steps, call the National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN) at 800-656-HOPE (4673).

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a lawsuit or hire an attorney right now.
- You do not need to explain the "truth" of your personal life to your colleagues or community members.
- You do not need to decide if you want to quit your job or leave the community group permanently today.
- You can wait until you have received an initial response from HR or administrators before taking further action.

## Important reassurance
Seeing sexual rumors about yourself in a group setting often triggers a "freeze" or "fight" response, which is a standard biological reaction to a social safety threat. These incidents are recognized as a form of sex-based harassment in the USA, and the responsibility for stopping the behavior lies with the employer or platform owner once notified.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and evidence preservation only. It does not cover the long-term legal processes of defamation suits, EEOC investigations, or internal disciplinary hearings.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. If you are being threatened with physical violence or are being stalked, contact 911 immediately. Laws regarding online harassment vary by state and individual workplace policies.
