# What to do if… sewage starts coming up through a floor drain but has not reached living areas yet (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all water use in the home, keep people and pets away from the drain area, and make the relevant calls now. Contact your local sewer utility or public works department if you use municipal sewer, or a septic service provider if you use septic, and contact an emergency plumber.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t flush toilets or run sinks, showers, the dishwasher, or the washing machine.
- Don’t walk through, touch, or clean up sewage while it is still rising.
- Don’t plug or seal drains, remove drain covers, or try to clear the line yourself unless your local sewer utility or a plumber instructs you to do so.
- Don’t use a chemical drain opener.
- Don’t begin cleanup or use cleanup equipment while the backup is active.
- Don’t use fans or HVAC to blow air from the affected area into the rest of the home.
- Don’t switch electrical equipment on or off while standing in or near contaminated water.
- Don’t let children or pets enter the affected area.

## What to do now
1. **Keep the area separate from the rest of the home.** Stay on a dry route. Close an interior door from a dry location if you can do so without approaching the drain. Keep everyone away from the area.
2. **Stop all water use in the home.** Tell everyone not to flush toilets or use sinks, showers, appliances, or other drains. In a multi-unit building, contact the property manager or emergency maintenance line immediately.
3. **Reduce electrical risk only from a dry, safe location.** If sewage could reach outlets, cords, appliances, a furnace, or a water heater, switch off the affected circuit at the breaker panel only if the panel is safely reachable without approaching contaminated water.
   - Do not cross wet or contaminated flooring to reach electrical controls.
   - Leave the area and call 911 for an immediate danger such as fire, sparking, or someone feeling faint. Otherwise, call an electrician if electrical controls cannot be reached safely.
4. **Make the relevant calls now.**
   - If you use municipal sewer, call your local sewer utility, public works department, or local sewer-backup contact. The number may be on your utility bill or local government website.
   - If you use septic, call a septic service provider and contact your local health department or onsite wastewater regulator for guidance. Leave any tank pumping to the septic service provider.
   - Contact an emergency plumber or drain professional.
   - If you rent, contact your landlord or property manager’s emergency maintenance line.
5. **Protect clean items only from a dry area.** If you can do so without approaching sewage, move clean boxes, rugs, and other items away from the floor near the affected area.
6. **Take a few photos from a safe distance.** Photograph the drain area and any visible wetting. Note when you first noticed the backup and whether recent toilet, sink, shower, dishwasher, or washing-machine use appeared to affect it.
7. **Stay out of the area while help is on the way.** If you must approach for an immediate safety reason and can do so from a dry route, wear waterproof gloves, rubber boots, and goggles. Cover any cuts, avoid touching your face, and wash your hands with soap and water afterward.

## What can wait
- Cleanup, disinfection, drying, and decisions about contaminated belongings can wait until the backup has stopped and the affected area is clear.
- Deciding whether the cause is in the public sewer, a private sewer line, or a septic system can wait for the utility or professional assessment.
- Detailed insurance or landlord paperwork can wait. A few safe-distance photos and brief notes are enough for now.
- Long-term prevention measures, including inspections or backwater-valve discussions, can wait.

## Important reassurance
The sewage has not reached living areas yet, so there is still time to limit exposure. The useful steps right now are stopping water use, keeping your distance, and making the relevant calls.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Cleanup, drying, plumbing work, septic work, repairs, and any permit or inspection questions may need qualified local professionals, your insurer, or your property manager.

## Important note
This is general information, not plumbing, electrical, legal, medical, insurance, or other professional advice. Sewage may contain harmful germs. Rinse exposed skin with safe water, and gently flush the eyes with safe water if needed. Seek medical advice promptly if anyone becomes unwell or has concerns after exposure.
