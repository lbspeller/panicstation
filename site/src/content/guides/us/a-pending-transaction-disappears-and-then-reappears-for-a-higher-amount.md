# What to do if… a pending transaction disappears and then reappears for a higher amount (USA)

## Short answer
Check whether the higher amount is still pending or has posted. Save a screenshot and contact your card issuer promptly if the amount is wrong or unfamiliar.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume there are two charges until you check whether one entry is pending and the other is posted.
- Don’t delay reporting a wrong or unfamiliar amount.
- Don’t throw away the receipt, booking confirmation, order details, or any screenshot showing the earlier amount.
- Don’t assume the higher amount is correct just because the merchant name looks familiar.
- Don’t ignore the available balance if this is a debit card and the higher amount could affect essential payments.
- Don’t replace or cancel the card without checking the issuer’s instructions. If you suspect fraud, use the issuer’s card controls if available and report it promptly.

## What to do now
1. **Check the transaction status and save what you can see.**  
   Note whether the higher amount is pending or posted. Screenshot the merchant name, amount, date, status, and any transaction details shown in the app.

2. **Compare it with your records.**  
   Check the receipt, order confirmation, booking details, or tip amount. Ask the merchant or issuer whether the difference relates to a temporary authorization, tip, deposit, incidental amount, fuel preauthorization, or another adjustment.

3. **Ask the merchant for an itemized explanation if you recognize the transaction.**  
   Ask what final amount was submitted and why it differs from the amount you expected. Ask for an itemized receipt or correction if there is an error. Do not delay contacting the issuer if the amount is unfamiliar or clearly wrong.

4. **Contact the card issuer using its stated reporting route.**  
   Use the number on the back of the card, the issuer’s app, or the issuer’s website. Say whether the amount is still pending or has posted, whether you recognize the merchant, and whether the amount is incorrect or the whole transaction is unfamiliar. Ask what the issuer needs you to do next.

5. **For a posted credit-card billing error, protect the written-notice deadline.**  
   Call the issuer promptly. To protect your billing-error rights, make sure a written notice reaches the billing-inquiries address shown on your statement no later than 60 days after the issuer transmitted the first statement showing the error. Use an electronic billing-error notice only if the issuer states that it accepts electronic notices and tells you how to send one. Keep a copy and the date sent.

6. **For a debit-card transaction, report the problem promptly.**  
   Tell your bank or credit union as soon as you notice an incorrect or unauthorized electronic transaction. Regulation E generally uses a 60-day period after the statement first showing the error, but do not wait for that deadline. If the card, PIN, or security code may have been lost or stolen, report that immediately and follow the bank’s instructions.

7. **If you suspect fraud, review nearby transactions.**  
   Use the issuer’s lock or freeze control if available, report the unfamiliar transaction, and check for any other activity you do not recognize.

8. **Check the practical effect on your balance.**  
   If this is a debit card and the higher amount may cause overdraft fees or affect an essential payment, contact the bank promptly and explain the issue.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide immediately whether to close the account or replace every automatic payment.
- You don’t need to write a long complaint before contacting the issuer.
- You don’t need to argue with the merchant. Save the evidence and use the issuer’s reporting route if the conversation is not helping.

## Important reassurance
A pending amount and a posted amount are not always the same. Checking the status, saving evidence, and reporting a higher-than-expected amount promptly are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A repeated pattern of unfamiliar transactions, identity theft concerns, or an unresolved dispute may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Issuer procedures can vary, and consumer-protection timelines can be strict, so report the issue promptly and keep copies of what you submit.
