# What to do if… two relatives both claim legal authority to make decisions after a death (USA)

## Short answer
Freeze all movement of property and funds immediately. In the USA, legal authority to manage an estate is only granted by a probate court through **Letters Testamentary** or **Letters of Administration**; being named in a will is a nomination only and does not grant power until the court acts.

## Do not do these things
- Do not accept a copy of a will as proof of current legal authority.
- Do not hand over keys, passwords, jewelry, or documents to any person who cannot produce a court-sealed certificate of appointment.
- Do not attempt to "choose" which relative seems more reliable or honest.
- Do not sign any "release" or "indemnity" forms provided by family members without professional review.
- Do not engage in physical confrontations or attempt to block entry to a property if you feel threatened; step away and contact local law enforcement for a civil standby if necessary.

## What to do now
1. **Request the "Letters" from both parties immediately.**
   - Ask for a certified, court-sealed copy of the **Letters Testamentary** (if there is a will) or **Letters of Administration** (if there is no will).
   - If either person says they are "working on it" or "it's in the will," inform them that no assets can be moved or accessed until the court process is complete.
2. **Identify the specific probate court handling the case.**
   - Determine which county the deceased person resided in at the time of death.
   - **Visit the official website for that county's Probate Court, Surrogate Court, or Clerk of Court.**
   - Use the "Case Search" function to look up the deceased person’s name to see if a case has been opened and if a representative has been officially appointed.
3. **Contact the Probate Court Clerk for verification.**
   - If the online search is inconclusive, **call the Probate Division clerk.**
   - Provide the name and date of death and ask: "Has a Personal Representative or Executor been appointed and have Letters been issued?"
   - Ask for the name of the appointed individual and the date the authority was granted.
4. **Notify third-party stakeholders of the dispute.**
   - **Inform banks, landlords, and insurance companies that the legal authority for the estate is currently contested.**
   - Request that they "freeze" or "flag" the accounts to prevent any unauthorized withdrawals or transfers until they receive court-certified Letters.
5. **Differentiate between funeral authority and estate authority.**
   - Be aware that "Right of Disposition" laws (control of the body/funeral) vary by state and often follow a statutory hierarchy (Spouse > Adult Children > Parents) rather than the will’s executor nomination.
   - **Check the state’s "Funeral Agent" or "Right of Disposition" statutes** if the dispute is about burial or cremation.
6. **Maintain a log of all claims and documents provided.**
   - **Record the date, time, and specific claim made by each relative.**
   - Save copies of any "will" or "court paperwork" they provide, but do not treat them as valid until verified with the court clerk.
7. **Secure the physical premises if you are the current occupant or landlord.**
   - **Change locks or restrict access if multiple relatives are attempting to remove items without court authority.**
   - Inform all parties: "Access to the property is restricted until a court-appointed representative provides Letters."

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine who "should" inherit specific items.
- You do not need to pay the deceased person’s bills or debts today.
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family arguments or mediate the dispute.
- You do not need to decide which version of a will is "real."

## Important reassurance
It is normal for financial institutions, hospitals, and courts to require strictly verified paperwork after a death. Refusing to act without court-issued Letters is a standard legal safeguard, not a personal slight against a family member. Requiring this proof protects the estate, the heirs, and you from potential liability.

## Scope note
This guide covers initial stabilization of a dispute over legal authority immediately following a death. It does not cover the long-term probate process, litigation of contested wills, or estate tax filings.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Probate laws vary significantly by state. If assets are being actively removed, if threats are made, or if the estate involves significant value, consult a probate attorney in the state where the deceased person lived.
