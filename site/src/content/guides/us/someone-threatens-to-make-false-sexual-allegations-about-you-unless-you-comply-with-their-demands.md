# What to do if… someone threatens to make false sexual allegations about you unless you comply with their demands (USA)

## Short answer
Identify if you are in immediate physical danger and prioritize reaching a secure location. Treat the threat as a criminal act of extortion or coercion rather than a personal dispute, and stop all communication immediately without complying with demands.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money, gift cards, cryptocurrency, or any form of payment.
- Do not provide sexual images, videos, or passwords to "prove" anything or appease the person.
- Do not meet the person in person or engage in a phone or video call to negotiate.
- Do not delete any messages, call logs, or evidence of the threat from your device.
- Do not send a "forced apology" or any statement that could be interpreted as an admission.
- Do not retaliate by making counter-threats or posting the person’s information publicly.

## What to do now
1. **Identify immediate safety risks.** If the person is physically present, following you, or at your home or workplace, call 911 immediately.
   - Look for signs of stalking, such as the person appearing uninvited or sending photos of your location.

2. **Secure all accounts and devices.** Change your passwords and enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on your email, social media, and banking accounts.
   - Use a separate, secure device if you suspect your current phone or computer has been compromised by the person.

3. **Preserve the evidence exactly as it appears.** Take screenshots of the entire conversation, including the specific threat and any demands made.
   - Ensure the screenshots capture the sender's username, profile link, phone number, and the timestamps of the messages.
   - Do not edit, crop, or blur any part of the evidence.

4. **Cease all contact immediately.** Do not explain your side, argue about the truth of the allegations, or attempt to convince the person to stop.
   - Silence often forces the person to either escalate (creating more evidence) or move on once they realize the tactic is not working.

5. **Report the extortion to federal and local authorities.** Use official channels to document the criminal nature of the threat.
   - File a report with the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3.gov) if the threat occurred online.
   - Contact your local police department to file an extortion or harassment report.
   - Call 1-800-CALL-FBI (225-5324) if the threat involves high-stakes financial extortion or specialized cyber-crimes.

6. **Contact RAINN for confidential support.** If the sexual nature of the threat is causing severe distress, use the National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-HOPE.
   - They can provide guidance on navigating the emotional impact and connect you with local resources.

7. **Consult a criminal defense attorney before making formal statements.** If a school, employer, or police department contacts you regarding an allegation, state that you have been the victim of a threat and wish to have legal counsel present before speaking further.
   - Many states, including West Virginia, Michigan, and North Carolina, have specific "Sexual Extortion" statutes updated as of 2026 that your attorney can use for your defense.

8. **Notify a single trusted person.** Provide them with a copy of the evidence and ask them to act as a witness to the threat.
   - This prevents you from being isolated, which is a key goal of the person making the threat.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to sue the person for defamation or emotional distress.
- You do not need to clear your name with everyone you know immediately.
- You do not need to have a "perfect" explanation for why the person is targeting you.
- You do not need to predict if the person will actually follow through; the priority is the evidence and your safety.

## Important reassurance
A threat of false allegations is a calculated attempt to use fear to gain control. Feeling overwhelmed, panicked, or tempted to comply is a standard physiological response to this type of coercion. Taking a moment to stop and secure your evidence is a protective action, not a sign that the threat is valid.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling a threat of false sexual allegations. It does not provide legal defense strategy for active criminal charges or long-term reputation management.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Laws regarding extortion, coercion, and sexual misconduct vary by state and are subject to specific definitions and evidence requirements. Consult a licensed attorney for advice tailored to your specific jurisdiction.
