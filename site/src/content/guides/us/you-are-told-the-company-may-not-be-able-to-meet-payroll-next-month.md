# What to do if… you are told the company may not be able to meet payroll next month (USA)

## Short answer
Request the original signed agreement and an itemized cost breakdown in writing, and do not authorize any immediate paycheck deductions while you verify the debt's legality under 2026 state "Stay-or-Pay" bans.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign any new document, promissory note, or "settlement" agreement during an exit interview.
- Do not verbally agree to a specific repayment amount before seeing the itemized receipts and the original contract.
- Do not assume the debt is valid just because it is mentioned in an employee handbook or offer letter.
- Do not ignore an actual deduction from your final check; federal and state laws have strict time limits for filing wage theft claims.
- Do not use company email or devices to research your rights; save all relevant documents to a personal device immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Request the "Validation Packet" for the alleged debt.** Send a written request (email or letter) for the following specific items:
   - A copy of the signed agreement that specifically authorizes the repayment.
   - An itemized list of the actual costs incurred by the employer (tuition receipts, third-party invoices).
   - Evidence that the training was optional and not mandatory for your current job duties.
2. **Determine if your state has a 2026 "Stay-or-Pay" ban.** If you are working in California or New York, new laws (like California AB 692) void most training repayment provisions in contracts signed on or after January 1, 2026.
   - In California, check if the training resulted in a "transferable credential" (a degree or certification with value outside the company); if not, the debt may be entirely unenforceable.
   - In New York, the "Trapped at Work Act" prohibits most "employment promissory notes" for training costs.
3. **Check your state's final paycheck deduction laws.** Most states (including California, Illinois, and New York) prohibit employers from deducting anything other than taxes or court-ordered garnishments from a final check without a very specific, contemporaneous written authorization.
   - Ask the employer: "Under what specific state statute are you authorized to make this deduction without a court order?"
4. **Apply the Federal "Free and Clear" test.** Under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA), a deduction or repayment demand is a "kickback" to the employer.
   - If the repayment brings your hourly pay below the federal minimum wage ($7.25/hr) or cuts into your overtime pay for that workweek, it is a federal violation.
5. **Collect all training-related evidence.** Secure copies of:
   - The course syllabus or description.
   - Emails where you were "assigned" or "required" to take the training.
   - Your paystubs showing the hours you worked while in training (to prove you were "on the clock").
6. **File a formal wage claim if money is withheld.** If the employer proceeds with a deduction:
   - Contact your State Department of Labor (or Labor Commissioner) to file a wage claim for "unauthorized deduction."
   - In California, note that workers can sue for actual damages or a $5,000 penalty per violation under AB 692.
7. **Issue a "Validation of Debt" notice if they send a bill.** If you have already left and receive an invoice, respond in writing stating that you dispute the debt and require full validation of the legal basis and the itemized costs before any payment is considered.

## What can wait
- You do not need to pay the bill immediately upon receipt; most employers will not initiate formal collections for several weeks.
- You do not need to decide if the training was "valuable" to you; the focus is on whether the contract is legally void.
- You do not need a private lawyer to file an initial wage claim with a state agency.

## Important reassurance
Training Repayment Agreement Provisions (TRAPs) are increasingly viewed by regulators as "predatory employer-driven debt." Having a bill handed to you does not mean you are legally obligated to pay it, especially with the wave of new state protections enacted in 2026. It is a standard business tactic to demand money; it is your right to demand proof of the lawfulness of that demand.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and documentation steps for US-based workers facing training cost clawbacks. It does not cover specific industry-standard apprenticeship programs (like union-backed programs) which may have different federal exemptions.

## Important note
Employment law is highly specific to the state where you work and the date your contract was signed. This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. If your employer threatens immediate legal action or collections, or if the amount exceeds several thousand dollars, consult with an employment attorney or your state's labor agency.
