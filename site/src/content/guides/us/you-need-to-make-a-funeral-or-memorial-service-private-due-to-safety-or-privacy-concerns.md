# What to do if… you need to make a funeral or memorial service private due to safety or privacy concerns (USA)

## Short answer
If you are unsure, choose private or invitation-only for now and share details only directly with invited people until you have a basic plan with the funeral home or venue.

## Do not do these things
- Do not post the time, date, and location publicly while you are still unsure.
- Do not leave the door, calls, and messages unassigned.
- Do not confront a risky person yourself at the service, visitation, or memorial.
- Do not share addresses, travel plans, full birth dates, or details that suggest a home will be empty.
- Do not give personal or financial information to unexpected callers, texts, or emails. Pressure to act urgently is a reason to pause and verify.

## What to do now
1. **Make a temporary decision for the next day.**  
   Use one sentence: "Private for now. Invitation-only." It is usually easier to widen access later than to pull back details after they spread.

2. **Choose one gatekeeper.**  
   Ask a trusted person to send invitations and answer inquiries with: "The family is keeping details private; invited guests will receive information directly."

3. **Tell the funeral home or venue what you need today.**  
   Say: "We have safety and privacy concerns. What can you realistically do for an invitation-only service?" Ask about a name list at the door, limited entrances, staff briefing, a private family room, seating plan, and a separate arrival or departure route if needed.

4. **Control the service details.**
   - Share details one-to-one by text, email, or phone, not in large public threads.
   - Send the general plan now and the exact location later if forwarding is a concern.
   - Ask invited guests not to post, forward, or screenshot the details.

5. **Prepare for an unwanted person showing up.**
   - Choose one person who will speak to staff or security so you do not have to.
   - Give staff only the information they need to recognize the person, such as a name, brief description, or photo you already have.
   - Agree on a simple signal that means "get staff now", "move us to a private room", or "help us leave by another exit."

6. **If there are threats, stalking, or harassment, treat it as a safety issue.**
   - If there is immediate danger, call 911.
   - Otherwise, contact your local police or sheriff non-emergency number to document the concern and ask what they suggest for safety at a public gathering. Look up the number on an official city, county, police, or sheriff website.
   - Save screenshots, voicemails, call logs, and a brief timeline.

7. **If the risk involves domestic violence or an abusive partner or ex, keep the legal piece cautious.**
   - Protection orders, sometimes called restraining orders, vary by state and local court.
   - You may be able to ask a local court, victim services program, or domestic violence advocate what options exist where you are.
   - You do not have to solve that today unless risk is urgent. Write down who is involved, what happened, when it happened, and what evidence already exists.

8. **If there will be a livestream, make it private by default.**
   - Use an invite-only link, password, waiting room, or registration if the platform allows it.
   - Turn off public sharing or recording if possible.
   - Ask someone else to admit guests and remove anyone disruptive.

9. **If scams or identity theft are part of the concern, slow the contact down.**
   - Go directly to IdentityTheft.gov for federal identity theft reporting and recovery steps.
   - For inheritance, insurance, funeral payment, or "release funds" messages, pause and verify through a known official number or website before responding.
   - Do not pay fees or share banking details because of an unexpected urgent message.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today how to handle long-term family conflict or who deserves to attend.
- You do not need a perfect obituary, public announcement, or social media post now.
- You do not need to choose a court process immediately unless there is urgent risk.
- You do not need to explain the decision to everyone today. The first aim is to reduce exposure and avoid escalation.

## Important reassurance
Choosing privacy after a death is a reasonable safety step. "Private for now" is a protective pause while you are grieving and have limited bandwidth.

## Scope note
These are first steps only for a quick privacy and safety decision about a service. Later decisions may need specialist help from the funeral home, venue, local victim services, law enforcement, a court, or a lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If there is immediate danger, call 911. Court processes and protection orders vary by state.
