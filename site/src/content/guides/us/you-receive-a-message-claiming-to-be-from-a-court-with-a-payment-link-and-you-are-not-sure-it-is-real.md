# What to do if… you receive a message claiming to be from a court with a payment link and you are not sure it is real (USA)

## Short answer
Do not click the link, scan any QR codes, or provide payment. Verify the claim by contacting your local or federal clerk's office directly using a phone number found on an official `.gov` website.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click any link, open attachments, or download "warrants" sent via text or email.
- Do not reply to the message or use any "opt-out" or "STOP" prompts if the sender is unknown.
- Do not call the phone number provided in the message or use the caller ID to verify identity.
- Do not pay using cryptocurrency, Bitcoin, "government kiosks," gift cards, or wire transfers.
- Do not provide your Social Security number, birthdate, or bank details to "verify" your case over the phone or via a text link.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the message and stop interaction.** **Take a screenshot of the message for evidence and then do not click any further.**
2. **Verify the case status using official channels only.** **Look up the official court website yourself through the Federal Court Finder or your specific state's judiciary portal.**
   - Federal courts exclusively send initial jury summons via US Mail.
   - Courts do not impose fines for missed jury duty or violations without a formal hearing before a judge.
   - For federal matters, use the "Central Violations Bureau" (cvb.uscourts.gov) or the local clerk's office.
3. **Report the fraudulent communication.** **Forward the text message to 7726 (SPAM) and report the incident at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.**
   - If the scam involves a fake federal warrant or US Marshals impersonation, report it to your local FBI field office.
4. **Secure your finances if you interacted with the link.** **Contact your bank or credit card issuer immediately to freeze your accounts and request new cards.**
   - If you entered login credentials, change your passwords for that account and all other accounts using the same password.
5. **Protect your identity if personal data was shared.** **Place a fraud alert or credit freeze through the three major credit bureaus.**
   - To place a fraud alert, you only need to contact one bureau (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion); they must notify the others.
   - To place a credit freeze, you must contact all three bureaus individually.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to pay a fine until you have spoken directly with a court clerk.
- You do not need to notify the police in person unless you have suffered a direct financial loss or are in physical danger.
- You do not need to hire a lawyer until you have confirmed that a real legal case exists against you.

## Important reassurance
Courts and law enforcement agencies will not arrest you over a text message or demand immediate payment via a link to avoid jail. Feeling pressured or anxious when receiving "official" threats is a standard reaction to scam tactics; slowing down to verify is the correct safety procedure.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying and responding to court impersonation scams. It does not replace legal advice or the instructions of a verified court official regarding legitimate cases.

## Important note
Jurisdictional procedures for court payments vary by state and county. Federal and state courts do not request financial information or sensitive identifiers like Social Security numbers via unsolicited text or email.
