# What to do if… you receive a message claiming to be from a court with a payment link and you are not sure it is real (USA)

## Short answer
Do not click the link or pay through the message. Verify it through an official court or government website, phone number, or payment portal that you find yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click the payment link, open attachments, or scan a QR code in the message.
- Do not reply, call back, or confirm your name, address, date of birth, Social Security number, card details, or case details through the message.
- Do not trust caller ID, logos, seals, case numbers, official-looking documents, or urgent legal language by themselves; they can be faked.
- Do not pay by gift card, cryptocurrency, payment app, wire transfer, cash deposit, or any method the sender says is the only way to avoid arrest.
- Do not delete the message until you have saved a screenshot or enough details to report it.

## What to do now
1. **Stop interaction with the message.** Take a screenshot showing the sender, date, wording, phone number or email address, and the link preview if visible. Then close it without clicking further.

2. **Check the court claim independently.** Search for the official website of the court, county, city, state, or federal court named in the message. Use only the phone number, clerk contact page, jury-duty page, or official payment portal listed there; do not use the link or number in the message.

3. **Ask a narrow verification question.** Say that you received a message with a payment link and ask whether there is a real case, citation, jury-duty issue, fine, or balance connected to your name. You do not need to pay anything through the message while you are checking.

4. **If it mentions jury duty or arrest, slow it down further.** Do not pay or give sensitive information unless the court confirms the issue through a trusted route. Real court-related problems can be checked through the court; scammers often use missed jury duty, warrants, and immediate-arrest threats to rush payment.

5. **Report the message.** Use your phone’s report-junk option if available, forward a suspicious text to 7726 (SPAM), and report the suspected scam to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.

6. **If you clicked the link but did not enter anything,** close the page, do not download anything, and watch for new suspicious messages. If anything downloaded or your device starts behaving unusually, run your device’s security checks or ask a trusted repair or support provider.

7. **If you entered payment details or paid,** contact your bank, card issuer, payment app, wire service, or crypto platform using a trusted number or app. Tell them it may be fraud and ask what can be blocked, reversed, replaced, or monitored.

8. **If you entered a password,** change that password from the real website or app, not from the message link. Change it anywhere else you reused it, and turn on two-factor authentication where available.

9. **If you entered your Social Security number or other identity information,** use IdentityTheft.gov for next steps. Consider a fraud alert or credit freeze: a fraud alert is placed by contacting one credit bureau, while a credit freeze usually needs to be placed separately with each bureau.

10. **If someone is actively pressuring you right now,** stop replying or hang up. If you believe there is immediate physical danger, call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to pay until the court confirms through a trusted route that there is a real balance or court matter.
- You do not need to argue with the sender or prove to them that you know it is fake.
- You do not need to solve every credit or identity step right now unless you shared money, passwords, card details, or sensitive identity information.
- You do not need to choose a lawyer right now just to check whether the message is real.

## Important reassurance
These messages are designed to create urgency, especially by using words like court, warrant, jury duty, fine, arrest, or final notice. Pausing, saving the message, and checking through a trusted court contact is a normal and safer response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to avoid paying a scammer, verify the court contact, and protect accounts if information was shared. If a real court matter is confirmed, later decisions may need help from the court clerk, legal aid, or a lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Court processes and payment systems vary by state, county, city, and court.
