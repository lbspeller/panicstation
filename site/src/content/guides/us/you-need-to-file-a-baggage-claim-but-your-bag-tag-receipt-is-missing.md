# What to do if… you need to file a baggage claim but your bag tag receipt is missing (USA)

## Short answer
Go immediately to the airline **Baggage Service Office** at the airport to file a **mishandled baggage report** (also called a Property Irregularity Report or PIR); the airline can retrieve your bag-tag number directly from your electronic reservation and check-in records using your name or confirmation code.

## Do not do these things
- Don't leave the airport without a physical or digital copy of the mishandled baggage report and your 8–10 character file reference number.
- Don't assume that a missing physical tag receipt invalidates your claim; the digital record in the airline's system is the primary source of truth.
- Don't guess or invent a tag number on the form; leave the field blank or mark it as unknown so the agent can look it up correctly.
- Don't submit duplicate reports via the airline app if you have already filed one at the desk, as this can create conflicting tracking files.
- Don't make large purchases for replacement items immediately; only buy essential toiletries and clothing and keep all itemized receipts.

## What to do now
1. **File a mishandled baggage report at the airport baggage desk before passing through customs or leaving the terminal.**  
   - Look for the Baggage Service Office (BSO) near the carousels for the airline that operated your final flight.
   - Request a printed copy or a confirmation email containing your file reference number (e.g., ABCXY12345).
2. **Provide your flight confirmation code (PNR) to the agent so they can pull the tag details.**  
   - Give the agent your 6-character reservation code, your government-issued ID, and your flight numbers.
   - They can use these to locate the specific "LPN" (License Plate Number) associated with your checked luggage in their departure control system.
3. **Verify your contact information and delivery address on the official report.**  
   - Ensure the delivery address is a location where someone can sign for the bag (e.g., hotel front desk, residential address with a secure drop-off).
   - Confirm that the phone number listed is one you can answer while traveling.
4. **Submit a detailed physical description of the bag to the tracing system.**  
   - Include the brand, color, material (hard/soft), and any identifying marks like ribbons, stickers, or unique scuffs.
   - If you have a photo of the bag on your phone, show it to the agent so they can add visual descriptors to the WorldTracer file.
5. **Request a copy of the airline's policy on incidental expense reimbursement.**  
   - Ask for a "passenger information leaflet" or a link to their specific baggage liability page.
   - Note that DOT regulations prohibit airlines from setting an arbitrary daily spending limit (like $50 per day) if your actual needs for essentials are higher.
6. **Monitor the 12-hour or 15/30-hour threshold for an automatic baggage fee refund.**  
   - Under DOT rules, if your bag is not delivered within 12 hours of arrival (domestic) or 15–30 hours (international, depending on flight length), the airline must automatically refund your checked bag fee.
   - Keep your mishandled baggage report on file to trigger this process.

## What can wait
- You do not need to provide a full itemized inventory of every item inside the bag right now; this is only required if the bag is not found after 5–14 days.
- You do not need to contact the Department of Transportation (DOT) today; wait until you have a final resolution or a formal denial from the airline.
- You do not need to file a claim with your travel insurance or credit card provider until you have the final status from the airline, though you should check their notification deadlines.

## Important reassurance
Losing a physical bag-tag receipt is a standard issue that airline agents handle daily. Because your baggage data is linked to your electronic ticket and boarding pass, the airline can almost always "re-link" your claim to the correct bag using your passenger name record. Most delayed bags are located and returned within 24 to 48 hours.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate stabilization of a missing baggage situation when documentation is lost. It does not cover the long-term legal process for declaring a bag permanently lost or the specifics of litigation against a carrier.

## Important note
For domestic U.S. flights, the maximum liability limit for lost, damaged, or delayed baggage is $4,700 per passenger. For international travel governed by the Montreal Convention, the liability limit is currently 1,288 Special Drawing Rights (SDR). These limits are subject to change based on inflation adjustments.
