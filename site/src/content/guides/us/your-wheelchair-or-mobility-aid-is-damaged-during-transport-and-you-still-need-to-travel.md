# What to do if… your wheelchair or mobility aid is damaged during transport and you still need to travel (USA)

## Short answer
First, stop and check whether the device is safe to use. Then report the damage before leaving the airport, station, terminal, bus stop, or pickup point if you can, and ask for a written incident or claim number, a safe loaner or assistance, and a plan for continuing your trip today.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using the device if a safety-critical part seems damaged, such as wheels, brakes, frame, steering, controls, seating, battery casing, exposed wiring, or anything dragging or leaking.
- Don’t leave the airport, station, terminal, bus stop, or pickup point without a written report or claim number if you can avoid it.
- Don’t accept a purely verbal promise as the only record; ask for a basic written incident report, claim number, text, email, or receipt.
- Don’t sign anything saying the device was returned undamaged, the problem is resolved, or your claim is closed if that is not true.
- Don’t attempt a quick fix that could fail during movement or transfer, such as taping damaged electrical parts, forcing bent components, or bypassing brakes.

## What to do now
1. **Make the device and your body safe first.**  
   Move to the nearest calmer safe spot if you can. Power the device off if it is powered. Look for loose wheels, brake failure, cracked frame, damaged battery casing, exposed wires, leaks, sharp edges, unstable seating, or anything dragging or rubbing.

2. **Tell staff exactly what happened and what you need today.**  
   Say: “My mobility device was damaged during transport and I still need to travel. I need this documented, I need a claim or incident number, and I need safe assistance or a loaner so I can continue.”

3. **Get quick proof without delaying help.**  
   Take photos of the damage from close up and farther away. Photograph your boarding pass, ticket, baggage tag, claim tag, itinerary, and the make, model, or serial number area if you can reach it safely.

4. **If this happened during air travel, ask for the airline’s CRO.**  
   Ask for a Complaints Resolution Official, called a CRO, in person or by phone. For a mishandled wheelchair or scooter, ask the airline for written notice of your rights, a claim process, an adequate loaner wheelchair or scooter if yours cannot be safely used, and the option to choose a preferred repair or replacement vendor. If the damaged item is another mobility aid, still ask the airline to document the damage and explain the claim process in writing.

5. **Ask for a workable plan for the next part of the trip.**  
   Ask for the safest combination of:
   - a loaner that meets your basic mobility and safety needs
   - wheelchair or guided assistance through the terminal, station, or connection
   - help with safe transfers, boarding, securement, and getting to baggage claim or pickup
   - rebooking or rerouting if you cannot continue safely as planned
   - clear written information about what costs they will cover; keep receipts

6. **If this happened on Amtrak, ask station or onboard staff for immediate accessibility help.**  
   At staffed stations, ask for assistance and whether a station-provided wheelchair is available. If your trip needs to change, ask Amtrak staff for help with accessible seating, wheeled mobility device space, or other travel options.

7. **If this happened on local public transit, ask for the accessibility supervisor or dispatcher.**  
   Ask for safe boarding, securement help, an accessible reroute, or another accessible vehicle if needed. Get the route, vehicle number, time, location, staff names if available, and an incident report or reference number.

8. **If this happened on a private coach, shuttle, or tour bus, ask the operator to document it and arrange safe continuation.**  
   Ask for the company’s accessibility contact, an incident report, and a safe way to continue or stop the trip. If they cannot make the situation safe, move to the nearest safe stop and arrange another accessible option if you can.

9. **If you cannot stay safe, switch to a safe stop.**  
   Ask staff to help you reach a safe waiting area where you are not forced to transfer, stand, or sit in an unsafe position. If you are injured, cannot transfer safely, have urgent pressure injury risk, or feel medically unsafe, ask for urgent medical help or call 911.

## What can wait
- A long written complaint.
- Negotiating final reimbursement amounts.
- Full repair quotes and detailed documentation packages.
- Insurance calls, extended emails, and legal research.
- Deciding whether to escalate beyond the carrier or transport provider.

## Important reassurance
This is a real access and safety problem, not just a baggage problem. It is reasonable to ask for immediate help, written documentation, and a safe way to continue or pause your travel.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on safety, documentation, and continuing or safely pausing travel today. Later decisions about claims, complaints, repairs, replacement, or escalation may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. What is available on the spot can vary by carrier, transport provider, location, device type, and safety situation.
