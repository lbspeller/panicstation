# What to do if… you find signs of sexual activity in your room and you do not remember agreeing to anything (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer location immediately and seek medical care at an emergency room that provides forensic exams. You can receive medical attention and have evidence collected without being required to file a police report.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront anyone you suspect of being involved until you are in a safe, controlled environment.
- Do not blame yourself for memory gaps or for not having a clear understanding of what happened.
- Do not wash your body, brush your teeth, or change your clothes if you intend to seek a forensic exam, unless the discomfort is unmanageable.
- Do not put clothes or bedding in plastic bags; use paper bags to prevent moisture from destroying DNA evidence.
- Do not feel pressured to make a permanent decision about police involvement in the first few hours.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.**  
   **If you believe someone is still in your home or you are in immediate danger, leave the premises and call 911.** If you are not in immediate danger, move to a room where you can lock the door or go to a neighbor or friend's home.

2. **Call a confidential advocate or trusted person.**  
   **Contact the RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-4673 or via online chat.** They can provide immediate stabilization, explain your local rights, and help you find a hospital that has a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) on call.

3. **Seek a Sexual Assault Forensic Exam (SAFE) within 120 hours.**  
   **Go to an emergency room and state that you need a forensic exam or "rape kit."** 
   - Under the Violence Against Women Act (VAWA), the forensic evidence collection portion of the exam is free of charge.
   - In most states, you can have evidence collected and stored anonymously (often for up to 2 years) without filing a police report.
   - Note that associated medical costs (like STI medication or injury repair) may be billed to insurance or the patient, though victim compensation programs may cover these later.

4. **Preserve physical evidence if you are able.**  
   **If you have already changed, place the clothes you were wearing in a clean paper bag.** 
   - Keep different items in separate paper bags.
   - Do not clean the area where you woke up or dispose of any unfamiliar items (such as cups or containers) until you have spoken with an advocate or professional.

5. **Document your current physical state.**  
   **Note any physical symptoms or observable marks without attempting to diagnose them.**
   - Write down your last clear memory, the names of anyone you were with, and the specific signs that caused your concern. 
   - Save any text messages, call logs, or social media interactions from the previous 24 hours.

6. **Prioritize urgent medical symptoms.**  
   **If you are experiencing severe nausea, difficulty breathing, extreme confusion, or loss of consciousness, inform medical staff immediately.** These may be signs of drug-facilitated assault or other acute medical issues that require priority treatment over the forensic exam.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to press charges or pursue a legal case today.
- You do not need to notify your landlord or employer yet.
- You do not need to have a "perfect" or chronological memory of the night to receive help.
- You do not need to tell your family or friends if you are not ready.

## Important reassurance
Memory loss or confusion after a potential assault is a documented physiological response to trauma, shock, or the presence of certain substances. A lack of memory does not mean you consented, nor does it mean your experience is not valid. Medical and advocacy professionals are trained to assist people who are unsure of exactly what occurred.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and evidence preservation only. It does not cover long-term legal options, civil litigation, or ongoing psychological support, which should be discussed with a qualified advocate or attorney.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or medical advice. Procedures and victim rights vary by state. If you are in a medical or safety crisis, call 911 immediately. For specialized support, contact the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline or the RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline.
