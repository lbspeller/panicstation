# What to do if… you receive a message threatening to share intimate webcam recordings of you (USA)

## Short answer
Do not reply, pay, click links, or send anything else. Save a minimal record, secure your email and key accounts, then use official U.S. reporting routes.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay, bargain, or keep chatting to “buy time”; it can create more pressure or reveal more information.
- Do not send more images or videos.
- Do not get on a video call to “prove” anything or “clear it up.”
- Do not click their links, open attachments, scan QR codes, or install anything they send as “proof.”
- Do not erase everything before saving a basic screenshot record.
- Do not give personal, banking, crypto, or login details to anyone who contacts you claiming they can “recover” money or “fix” the situation.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Put the message down for a moment, breathe slowly, and move away from anyone who might pressure you to answer quickly.
2. **Do not respond.** Do not explain, argue, apologize, threaten, or ask for proof.
3. **Save a minimal record.** Take screenshots of the message, sender account, dates or times, payment instructions, usernames, crypto wallet addresses, and any threat to contact your friends, followers, school, family, or workplace. Store the screenshots somewhere safe, then stop rereading the threat.
4. **Report and block on the platform.** Use the app, email provider, or social platform’s report option for extortion, blackmail, harassment, impersonation, or non-consensual intimate content. After reporting, block the sender.
5. **Secure your email first.** Change your email password, especially if the message included a password or claims of access. If you reused that password anywhere else, change it there too.
6. **Turn on two-factor authentication.** Add 2FA to email and major social accounts. Review recovery phone numbers, recovery emails, logged-in devices, and connected apps; remove anything you do not recognize.
7. **Reduce contact exposure temporarily.** Tighten privacy settings, limit who can message, tag, comment, or see your friends or followers list, and turn off contact syncing if it is not needed.
8. **Check basic device access.** Update your operating system and browser, review camera and microphone permissions, and remove access for apps or browser extensions you do not recognize. Use a reputable security scan if you already have one.
9. **Report through official U.S. routes.** If you are in immediate danger, call 911. For internet extortion or fraud, use the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center and FTC ReportFraud. Type official addresses yourself or use saved trusted links, not links from the threat message.
10. **If you are under 18, or the person in the image or threat is under 18, get specialist help.** Report to NCMEC CyberTipline for suspected child sexual exploitation or sextortion involving a minor. If explicit images were taken before age 18 and may be shared online, NCMEC Take It Down may help limit sharing without uploading the image.
11. **If real intimate images or videos exist or are posted, switch to containment and support.** Report the content on the platform where it appears. If the images were taken when you were 18 or older, StopNCII may help participating platforms detect and limit sharing of matching images. You can also contact local law enforcement, especially if you feel unsafe or the threats are continuing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to tell friends, family, school, or work.
- You do not need to figure out exactly how they found you tonight.
- You do not need to delete all accounts or wipe your phone immediately.
- You do not need to keep reading the threats to stay safe.
- You do not need to hire anyone who contacts you offering urgent recovery or takedown services.

## Important reassurance
These messages are designed to create shame, panic, and fast payment. Many are bluffing, and even when a threat feels personal, the safest first moves are still simple: stop contact, save a minimal record, secure accounts, and use official reporting routes.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilise the situation and avoid irreversible actions. If the person keeps threatening you, money was sent, accounts were accessed, or content appears online, later decisions may need help from official reporting channels, a trusted support person, a lawyer, a victim support service, or a qualified security professional.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and immediate safety steps. It is not legal advice, medical advice, financial advice, therapy, counseling, or professional cybersecurity advice.
