# What to do if… your laptop is stolen and it contains sensitive personal documents (USA)

## Short answer
Use a trusted phone or another trusted device to protect the laptop through its official device service, then secure your main email account because it can control password resets for other accounts.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go to a location shown by tracking software yourself; save the information for police or your organization.
- Don’t log in to important accounts on a public or shared computer unless there is no safer option.
- Don’t assume the laptop password alone protects everything; focus on accounts already signed in and files that may not have been encrypted.
- Don’t call numbers or click links from messages claiming they “found your laptop.”
- Don’t post serial numbers, tracking screenshots, exact locations, or a list of documents online.
- Don’t delay securing email, financial accounts, cloud storage, and password manager access while waiting to see what happens.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the key details before they blur.**  
   Note where and when the laptop was stolen, any witnesses, the device name, model, serial number if you can find it, and what sensitive documents were on it, such as Social Security number, passport scan, tax forms, bank statements, medical documents, HR files, or work files.

2. **Use the official laptop-protection route, if it was already set up.**  
   For a Mac, use Apple Find My. For a Windows laptop, use Microsoft’s device page. For a work or school laptop, use the official IT or security channel. Lock the device, mark it lost, or erase it if that option is available and appropriate. If you see a location, do not try to recover it yourself.

3. **Secure your main email account first.**
   - Change the password from a trusted device.
   - Sign out of other sessions and devices in account settings.
   - Turn on multi-factor authentication if it is not already on.
   - Check for forwarding rules, mailbox rules, recovery emails, recovery phones, or connected apps you do not recognize.

4. **Secure the accounts that could be abused fastest.**  
   Prioritize banking, credit cards, payment apps, cloud storage, and any password manager. Change passwords, revoke sessions or trusted devices, and check recent activity. If a password manager may have been open or accessible on the laptop, change the master password and review its device list.

5. **Contact banks, card issuers, and payment apps if financial records or saved access were on the laptop.**  
   Ask them to watch for suspicious activity, remove trusted-device access where possible, and tell you whether they recommend replacing cards or changing account credentials.

6. **File a police report for the theft and keep the report number.**  
   You may need the report number for insurance, employer reporting, device recovery, or later disputes if someone misuses your information.

7. **Use credit protections if Social Security numbers, tax forms, or enough identity details were on the laptop.**
   - A fraud alert can usually be placed by contacting one of the three major credit bureaus.
   - A credit freeze usually gives more direct new-credit protection and usually needs to be placed separately with each of the three major credit bureaus.

8. **Use IdentityTheft.gov if sensitive identifiers were exposed or misused.**  
   If the laptop contained Social Security numbers, tax forms, government ID scans, or enough information to open accounts, use IdentityTheft.gov to make a recovery plan and keep a documentation trail.

9. **Consider IRS identity-theft steps if tax records or Social Security numbers were on the laptop.**  
   If you are worried someone could file a tax return using your information, check the IRS Identity Theft Central page and consider an Identity Protection PIN.

10. **Notify the organization immediately if the laptop was from work or school, or contained other people’s data.**  
   Use the official IT, security, privacy, or manager route. They may be able to disable access, force sign-outs, protect shared systems, and handle any required notifications.

## What can wait
- You do not need to replace the laptop today.
- You do not need to change every password at once; start with email, financial accounts, cloud storage, and password manager access.
- You can handle insurance claims, a new-device setup, and longer-term monitoring after you have contained the immediate account and identity risks.
- You do not need to decide every possible identity-theft step right now; take the strongest steps first where Social Security numbers, tax records, banking details, or government ID scans were on the laptop.

## Important reassurance
This is a lot to face at once, but the first useful moves are limited and practical: protect the device if possible, secure email, secure financial and cloud access, then use credit and identity-theft protections if sensitive identifiers were on it. Acting in that order can reduce the chance of lasting damage.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours after discovering the theft. Later decisions, such as insurance claims, employer reporting, tax identity-theft follow-up, account disputes, or legal questions, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, insurance, tax, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 911.
