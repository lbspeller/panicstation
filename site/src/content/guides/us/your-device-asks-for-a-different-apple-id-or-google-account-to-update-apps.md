# What to do if… your device asks for a different Apple ID or Google Account to update apps (USA)

## Short answer
Do not enter a password for an Apple Account or Google Account you do not recognize. First check which account is being used for app purchases and updates, then secure the account you actually use from a trusted device.

## Do not do these things
- Do not type passwords into surprise pop-ups for accounts you do not recognize.
- Do not erase or reset the phone as your first move; this can make recovery harder and may lock you out.
- Do not remove a work or school management profile until you know what it controls.
- Do not install “support” apps, remote-access tools, or configuration profiles from unsolicited texts, emails, calls, or ads.
- Do not use unofficial app stores or sideloaded apps to get around the update prompt.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and check settings directly.** If this started after a link, attachment, “security alert,” or support call, treat it as suspicious until you have checked inside Settings, the App Store, Google Play, or the official account website.
2. **Work out the pattern.**
   - If only one or a few apps ask for a different account, those apps may have been installed or purchased under another Apple Account or Google Account.
   - If many apps, system prompts, or account pages suddenly show an unfamiliar email address, treat it as possible account compromise or device management.
3. **On iPhone or iPad, check the Apple Account used for purchases.**
   - Go to **Settings → your name → Media & Purchases** and check the account shown there.
   - Also open **Settings → your name** and check whether the main Apple Account email address is one you recognize.
   - If the update prompt is for an old Apple Account you recognize, you can usually sign in with that account for that app update, or delete and reinstall the app under your current Apple Account. Before deleting, assume you may lose local app data, in-app state, or access unless the app is backed up, synced, or restorable.
4. **On Android, check the Google Play account.**
   - Open **Google Play → profile picture or initial → down arrow → select the account**.
   - Check whether the app updates normally after selecting the Google Account you recognize.
   - If an app is tied to another Google Account you recognize, you can usually switch to that account to update it, or uninstall and reinstall under your preferred account. Before uninstalling, assume you may lose local app data, in-app state, or access unless the app is backed up, synced, or restorable.
5. **Check for device management.**
   - On iPhone or iPad, go to **Settings → General → VPN & Device Management**.
   - On Android, look in **Settings** for a **Work profile**, a **Work** tab, or device-management entries. Menu names vary by device and Android version.
   - If this is a work or school device, contact your IT helpdesk before removing anything.
   - If this is a personal device and you do not recognize a profile or management entry, take screenshots first. Removing a profile or work profile can remove associated settings, apps, and data, so secure your accounts before changing it.
6. **Secure the account you actually use, from a trusted device or browser.**
   - Change the password for the Apple Account or Google Account you use.
   - Confirm that the recovery email and phone number are yours.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication or 2-Step Verification if it is not already on.
   - Review signed-in devices and sign out or remove devices you do not recognize.
7. **If you entered a password into a suspicious prompt, act on that first.**
   - Change that account password from the official Apple or Google account page.
   - Change the same password anywhere else you reused it.
   - Check recent account activity, recovery details, and signed-in devices.
8. **If you think your identity, phone number, or accounts may be misused, document and report.**
   - Save screenshots, dates, times, messages, unfamiliar email addresses, and any phone numbers involved.
   - Use **IdentityTheft.gov** if personal information or identity accounts may be at risk.
   - Use **ReportFraud.ftc.gov** if this may be a scam or fraud attempt.
   - If you think someone is trying to take over your phone number, contact your mobile carrier and ask them to secure the line.

## What can wait
- You do not need to merge, rename, or reorganize all your accounts today.
- You do not need to fix every app at once.
- You do not need to wipe the phone as a first step.
- You do not need to decide whether to keep or replace the device until you have checked the purchase account, management settings, and account security.

## Important reassurance
This often has a non-scary cause, such as an app installed under an old account, a shared device, Family Sharing confusion, or a work profile. If it is suspicious, moving slowly and securing the account first is safer than rushing into resets or password prompts.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to reduce lockout risk and help you regain control. Later decisions, such as formal account recovery, device repair, employer IT help, or identity-theft steps, may need specialist support.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Menu names and account options vary by device, operating system version, carrier, employer setup, and app.
