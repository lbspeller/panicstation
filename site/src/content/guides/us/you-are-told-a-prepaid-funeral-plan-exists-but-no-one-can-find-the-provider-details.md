# What to do if… you are told a prepaid funeral plan exists but no one can find the provider details (USA)

## Short answer
Gather the deceased's personal details and search bank records for recurring payments, then use the official NAIC Policy Locator and MissingMoney.com to trace insurance-funded or trust-funded burial assets.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not pay a fee** to any private company or "locator" service claiming they can find a lost plan for a percentage of the value.
- **Do not share the Social Security number** of the deceased with anyone other than verified government agencies or the official NAIC tool.
- **Do not sign an "assignment of benefits" or "transfer" form** at a new funeral home until the original plan is located and its value is confirmed.
- **Do not assume the plan is invalid** if a specific funeral home has closed; many plans are portable or held in state-regulated trusts.

## What to do now
1. **Create a standardized "Information Sheet" for the search.**
   - Include the full legal name, all known aliases (maiden names), date of birth, Social Security number, and every city/state where they lived for the last 40 years.
   - Note the names of any funeral homes or cemeteries the deceased mentioned or attended for family services.
2. **Perform a physical search for "sensory cues" and minor documents.**
   - **Check for small plastic wallet cards** or stickers on the back of calendars or inside old address books.
   - **Look for yellowed envelopes** or brochures from local funeral homes, particularly those with older logos or names that may have changed.
   - **Search for bank-branded folders** or safety deposit box keys that might lead to a trust agreement.
3. **Review five years of bank and credit card statements.**
   - **Search for payments** to entities containing the words "Life," "Assurance," "Indemnity," "Trust," "Preneed," or the name of a local mortuary.
   - **Identify recurring monthly payments** of small amounts ($10–$50), which often indicate a "Final Expense" or "Burial Insurance" policy.
4. **Submit a request through the official NAIC Life Insurance Policy Locator.**
   - **Navigate to the official National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) website** to use their free search tool for lost policies and annuities.
   - **Enter the deceased's Social Security number** and dates exactly as they appear on the death certificate.
5. **Search the multi-state unclaimed property database.**
   - **Visit MissingMoney.com** (the official gateway endorsed by the National Association of Unclaimed Property Administrators) to search multiple states at once.
   - **Perform individual searches** on the official "Unclaimed Property" website for every state the deceased ever lived in, using their full name and last known address.
6. **Contact the state regulator for funeral and cemetery services.**
   - **Identify the specific agency** in your state that oversees "Preneed" or "Prepaid" contracts (this is often the State Board of Funeral Service or the Department of Banking).
   - **Ask for a "Permit Holder" search** to see if the deceased’s name is registered in a state-mandated trust fund or escrow account.
7. **Contact former employers or labor unions.**
   - **Ask the HR department** of any long-term former employer if there was a "death benefit" or "burial fund" included in the pension or retiree package.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the plan was a "bad investment" or a scam right now.
- You do not need to choose a final casket, urn, or service package until the funding source is verified or ruled out.
- You do not need to initiate a formal complaint against a funeral home until your search of bank records and state trust registries is complete.

## Important reassurance
It is extremely common for families to know a plan exists but lack the paperwork. Most states have strict "preneed" laws requiring funds to be placed in trust or insurance, meaning the money is likely still protected even if the original funeral home changed owners or closed. Systematic searching usually uncovers a payment trail.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. It focuses on locating the asset or provider and does not cover the legal process of claiming funds or litigating disputed contracts.

## Important note
This guide provides general procedural information for the USA and is not legal or financial advice. Accessing financial records or filing claims may require legal status as an executor, administrator, or verified next-of-kin.
