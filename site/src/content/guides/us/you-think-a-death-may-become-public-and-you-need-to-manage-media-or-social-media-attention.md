# What to do if… you think a death may become public and you need to manage media or social media attention (USA)

## Short answer
Choose one spokesperson for now and give everyone one short sentence to repeat. Do not confirm details, guess, or argue online; route media and public questions to the spokesperson, and ask any involved agency which media contact handles confirmed information.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t let several relatives answer “just a few questions”; small differences can spread quickly.
- Don’t confirm or guess the cause of death, timeline, witnesses, locations, blame, or family conflict in texts, DMs, calls, or comments.
- Don’t post emotional updates to “set the record straight” while you are overwhelmed; screenshots can travel.
- Don’t send photos, documents, recordings, private messages, or personal details to reporters in the first hours.
- Don’t treat a reporter’s deadline as your family’s emergency.
- Don’t argue with strangers or reply to rumor threads from a personal account.

## What to do now
1. **Pick one spokesperson for the next few hours.**  
   Choose the person who can stay calm, repeat a script, and ignore pressure. Pick one backup only for missed calls or urgent messages.

2. **Send close family and key friends one rule.**  
   “Please don’t comment or post details. If anyone asks, say: ‘Please contact [Name]. We’re not speaking individually.’”

3. **Use a holding statement and do not freestyle.**  
   Keep it to 2–4 sentences. Do not include cause of death, timeline, location, blame, investigation details, or family disagreements. Example: “Our family is grieving and asking for privacy. We are not able to speak about details right now. If we have updates we want to share, they will come through our family spokesperson.”

4. **If law enforcement is involved, ask where confirmed information should be routed.**  
   Use the case contact if you have one. If not, call the agency’s main or non-emergency number and ask: “Who handles media or public-information questions for this case?” The spokesperson can then say: “Please contact that office for confirmed information.”

5. **If a medical examiner or coroner office is involved, ask about its public-information process.**  
   Call the main office and ask: “Who can explain what information may become public and who handles general inquiries?” You are asking about the process, not trying to provide or confirm details.

6. **Set up one intake channel the spokesperson controls.**  
   Use one phone number and one email address. Change voicemail to: “Please email [address]. We are not taking calls right now.” Ask a trusted person to screen messages if the spokesperson is being overwhelmed.

7. **Reduce accidental public signals for the next day.**  
   Ask close family not to post names, “RIP” messages, photos, memorial details, or location details yet. Review tagging and privacy settings. If someone has already posted, ask them to edit or remove it until the family decides what it wants public.

8. **Make a short no-comment list.**  
   Choose the topics the spokesperson will not discuss, such as cause of death, rumors, blame, family conflict, medical history, or investigation details. The response is: “We’re not discussing that.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to write a full statement, obituary, memorial notice, or social media post right now.
- You do not need to correct every rumor today.
- You do not need to agree to interviews, provide photos, or answer follow-up questions.
- You can change the spokesperson later when things are calmer.
- You can ask for help from a victim advocate, attorney, clergy member, funeral professional, or trusted friend later if the situation becomes complicated.

## Important reassurance
A single calm voice and a short repeated script can protect the family from being pulled into confusion while everyone is grieving. Setting a boundary is not the same as going public; it is a way to slow things down.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next hours or day: choosing a spokesperson, keeping relatives from being pressured, and routing media questions away from grieving family members. Later decisions about statements, interviews, memorial details, legal questions, or victim-support options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If there is an active investigation, be especially cautious about details and rely on official public-information channels for confirmed facts.
