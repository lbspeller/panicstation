# What to do if… you suspect someone may have followed you onto a train, bus, or subway after you changed platforms or stops (USA)

## Short answer
Move immediately to a crowded area near transit staff or a Help Point, and use a discreet reporting app or 911 if you feel threatened.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the individual or try to verify their intentions through conversation.
- Do not move into isolated areas such as empty train cars, quiet ends of platforms, or deserted station exits.
- Do not exit at a dark or lonely stop to "test" if they are following you.
- Do not lead the person toward your home, vehicle, or a secluded walking path.
- Do not walk between moving train cars via the end-doors; this is a fatal fall risk.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a high-visibility density zone.**  
   **Position yourself near other passengers, preferably near a station agent booth, an emergency Help Point (often marked with a blue light), or directly behind the bus driver.** In a train, move to the car where the conductor or operator is located (usually the first or middle car).

2. **Use internal emergency communication tools.**  
   **Locate the Passenger Emergency Intercom (PEI) inside the vehicle or the Help Point on the platform to speak directly to transit staff.** If you are on a bus, speak to the driver immediately when the bus is stopped or through the plexiglass barrier if safe to do so.

3. **Report discreetly via text or app.**  
   **Use the system’s "SeeSay" app or text-to-police number if it is displayed on vehicle windows or station signage.** Many major US systems (MTA, WMATA, BART, SEPTA, MBTA) allow silent reporting of "suspicious activity" or "harassment" which goes directly to transit police dispatch.

4. **Exit only at a "Safe Haven" station.**  
   **Wait to exit until you reach a busy, well-lit station with visible staff or police presence.** If the person exits with you, stay in the fare-control area near the station agent or a group of people; do not leave the station into the street until help arrives.

5. **Provide specific locational identifiers to dispatch.**  
   **Identify your exact location using the vehicle number (found above the doors or at the front/rear of the bus), the route name, and the direction of travel.** State clearly: "I am being followed by an individual who matched my platform change at [Station Name]."

6. **Create a "Witness Anchor."**  
   **Stand near a group of people and, if safe, make a phone call to a friend or family member.** Describe your location and the situation loudly enough for others nearby to hear, which can discourage a follower from approaching.

## What can wait
- You do not need to confirm the person's identity or motive before seeking help.
- You do not need to worry about "bothering" staff; reporting suspicious following behavior is a standard safety procedure.
- You do not need to continue to your original destination if it is isolated or unstaffed.

## Important reassurance
Trusting your intuition regarding following behavior is a valid safety response. Transit systems are designed with layered communication tools specifically for these moments. Moving toward light and people is the most effective way to disrupt a follower's proximity.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for immediate transit safety. It does not cover long-term stalking interventions or legal reporting requirements.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and is not professional security or legal advice. In any immediate threat to life or physical safety, call **911**. Availability of transit apps and Help Points varies by city; finding a uniformed employee is the most reliable secondary option.
