# What to do if… your mobility scooter stops working while you are out and walking the distance isn’t realistic (USA)

## Short answer
Move to the nearest safe shelter away from traffic, then contact a specialized roadside service, a trusted person with an accessible vehicle, or your local paratransit dispatch for an emergency "missed trip" return.

## Do not do these things
- Don't keep toggling the power if the scooter is surging or cutting out, as this can lead to unpredictable movement toward traffic.
- Don't attempt to push the scooter yourself across busy intersections or along narrow road shoulders.
- Don't leave your mobility device unattended in a public area unless you have secured all personal medical supplies and keys.
- Don't wait in direct sunlight or extreme cold if an indoor "area of refuge" is visible and accessible.
- Don't assume a standard taxi or rideshare can transport a non-folding scooter; do not book one without confirming a ramp or lift is available.

## What to do now
1. **Navigate to the nearest "area of refuge."** Move off the main path of travel to a wide, level, well-lit area such as a building lobby, staffed store, or transit station.
2. **Perform a high-level mechanical check.** Confirm the following without attempting complex repairs:
   - **The freewheel / neutral lever:** Check the lever (usually near the rear motor) to ensure it is in the "Drive" position and not accidentally bumped into "Neutral."
   - **The key and battery:** Ensure the key is fully inserted and look for a flashing light or "beep code" pattern on the console.
   - **The circuit breaker:** Locate the reset button (often on the battery box or main pillar) and press it once if it has popped out.
3. **Communicate the fault code.** If the console is flashing or beeping in a specific pattern, write down the number of beeps or flashes (e.g., "five beeps") to tell a technician or help-line dispatcher.
4. **Call for a specialized accessible pickup.** Contact sources in this priority order:
   - **Roadside Assistance:** If you have a plan through AAA, Better World Club, or your scooter manufacturer that includes mobility device towing.
   - **Trusted Contact:** Someone with a ramp-equipped van or a vehicle large enough to transport the device.
   - **ADA Paratransit Dispatch:** If you are a registered user, call the "Where’s My Ride" or "Dispatch" line; tell them your device has failed and you are stranded. Request a "Reasonable Modification" if you need the driver to meet you inside the building rather than at the curb.
5. **Request an accessible taxi or "TNC" (Transportation Network Company) vehicle.** Use terms like "Wheelchair Accessible Vehicle" (WAV) or "Rear-entry ramp van." Specifically state that you have a mobility scooter, as some WAVs are sized only for manual wheelchairs.
6. **Request temporary shelter from a nearby business.** If you must wait for an hour or more, ask a staff member if you can wait in a seated area. Mention if you need access to a power outlet for your phone or medical device.
7. **Contact 911 only if there is a life-safety threat.** Call if you are stuck in the path of traffic, experiencing symptoms of heatstroke or exposure, or if you are in a location where you feel physically unsafe from your surroundings. Tell the dispatcher: "I am a person with a mobility disability, my device has failed, and I am in immediate danger because [reason]."

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause of the electrical or motor failure right now.
- You do not need to contact the manufacturer for warranty claims while you are still stranded.
- You do not need to finish your planned errands or appointments.
- You do not need to worry about the cost of the repair while you are coordinating the trip home.

## Important reassurance
Sudden equipment failure is a standard risk for all powered mobility users. It is normal to feel a surge of adrenaline or confusion when your primary means of movement stops. Focus entirely on the logistics of getting yourself and your device to a secure, climate-controlled environment.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for a mechanical or power failure while in public. It does not cover long-term repair, battery maintenance, or legal claims against manufacturers or transit agencies.

## Important note
This information is for situational stabilization and does not constitute legal or medical advice. ADA paratransit policies regarding "curb-to-curb" versus "door-to-door" service vary by jurisdiction; always follow the instructions of emergency dispatchers if you are in a life-threatening situation.
