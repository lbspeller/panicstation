# What to do if… a carbon monoxide alarm sounds and you feel sick or have a headache (USA)

## Short answer
Stay outside in fresh air, keep everyone out, and call 911 now. Tell the dispatcher that a carbon monoxide alarm sounded and that someone has headache or nausea. Do not go back inside until emergency responders say it is safe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not re-enter to silence, reset, or check the alarm.
- Do not go back inside to look for the source or operate, turn off, or check equipment.
- Do not assume it is a false alarm because you cannot smell anything. Carbon monoxide has no smell.
- Do not delay calling 911 while contacting a landlord, utility company, or Poison Help.
- Do not use a generator indoors or in a garage. Keep generators outdoors more than 20 feet from windows, doors, and vents.
- Do not use a grill or camp stove indoors or near building openings.
- Do not run a vehicle in an attached garage, even with the garage door open.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel dizzy, confused, unusually sleepy, faint, or very unwell.

## What to do now
1. **Stay outside and keep everyone out.** Move to a safe spot away from the building, its open windows and doors, garages, generators, and idling vehicles.
2. **Call 911 now.** Say that a carbon monoxide alarm sounded and that someone has headache or nausea. Give the address and follow the dispatcher’s instructions.
3. **Account for everyone who was inside.** Tell the dispatcher immediately if any person or pet may still be inside. Do not re-enter.
4. **Report urgent symptoms clearly.** Tell the dispatcher if anyone has trouble breathing, chest pain, fainting, seizure, confusion, worsening symptoms, or difficulty staying awake.
5. **Mention factors that may matter to responders or clinicians.** Say if anyone exposed is pregnant, a baby or infant, an older adult, or has chronic heart disease, anemia, or a respiratory illness.
6. **Use Poison Help as an additional expert route without delaying 911.** Another adult can call **1-800-222-1222** while the 911 call is underway. Otherwise, call Poison Help after speaking with 911 if you need additional poison-exposure guidance.
7. **Wait for responder permission before re-entering.** If responders identify a malfunctioning appliance, do not use it again until trained personnel have serviced it.
8. **Give responders or clinicians the key details.** Include when the alarm sounded, when you left, who was inside, symptoms, and possible sources such as a furnace, water heater, stove, fireplace, generator, or vehicle near a garage.

## What can wait
- Troubleshooting the detector or deciding whether it needs replacement.
- Finding the exact source yourself.
- Calling a landlord, homeowners association, insurer, or gas utility unless responders tell you to do so now.
- Retrieving belongings.
- Arranging repairs before responders have said the space is safe to enter.

## Important reassurance
Getting outside was the right first step. Headache or nausea after a carbon monoxide alarm is a valid reason to treat the alarm seriously and call for help.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Medical assessment, repairs, inspection, and later prevention decisions may need help from emergency responders, clinicians, or qualified technicians.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical or other professional advice. Call 911 now for a sounding carbon monoxide alarm with headache or nausea, and do not re-enter until emergency responders say it is safe.
