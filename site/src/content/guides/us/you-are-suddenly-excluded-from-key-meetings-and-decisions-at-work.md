# What to do if… you are suddenly excluded from key meetings and decisions at work (USA)
## Short answer
Document the change and ask your manager in writing to confirm your current responsibilities, decision authority, and which meetings you are expected to attend.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send a public or emotional message that may be forwarded or screenshot.
- Do not quit on the spot or threaten resignation while you still lack clear facts.
- Do not accuse individuals of discrimination or retaliation without describing the specific events that concern you.
- Do not delete messages, rewrite past notes, or remove calendar records.
- Do not take, copy, or send confidential company information outside the access and retention rules that apply to your work.
- Do not secretly record conversations while you are unsure whether recording is lawful and permitted. Recording laws vary by state, and workplace rules may also apply.

## What to do now
1. **Write a short timeline.** Note when meeting invitations stopped, which decisions you learned about afterward, whether access changed, whether responsibilities shifted, and any direct statements made to you.
2. **Preserve work-related records without crossing lines.** Keep dated notes of calendar changes, emails, chat messages, task assignments, and missed handoffs that you can legitimately access for your job. Do not export confidential files if you are unsure whether that is allowed.
3. **Send a short, non-accusatory message to your manager.** Ask them to confirm:
   - your current responsibilities and decision authority
   - which recurring meetings you should attend
   - whether the changes were intentional or an error
   - how decisions affecting your deliverables will be communicated
4. **Request written handoffs for blocked work.** Ask for a brief written recap of any decision that affects your deadlines, including what changed, who owns the next step, and what you should do now.
5. **Record the effect on your work.** If exclusion blocks delivery, write a factual message such as: “I cannot complete X until Y is confirmed. Please confirm the owner and next step by [date].”
6. **Use an internal route if your manager does not clarify.** Contact HR, employee relations, or the relevant internal contact with a short factual summary and ask which process to use to confirm role expectations and restore work-critical communication.
7. **Check deadlines promptly if discrimination or EEO-related retaliation may be involved.** The EEOC says a charge generally must be filed within 180 calendar days, sometimes extended to 300 calendar days where a state or local agency enforces a law covering the same basis. Federal employees and applicants for federal jobs generally follow a different process and usually must contact an agency EEO counselor within 45 days. Ask the EEOC which route applies to your situation.
8. **Note relevant timing.** If the exclusion began after you raised a concern about discrimination or harassment, requested accommodation for a disability or religious practice, or took part in an EEO complaint process, add that sequence to your dated timeline.
9. **Slow down when paperwork arrives.** If you receive a performance plan, acknowledgment, separation document, or release, request a copy, ask whether time is available to review it, and avoid signing before you understand what it says.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign, threaten legal action, or confront colleagues one by one.
- You do not need to prove the reason for the exclusion right now.
- You do not need to write a long narrative. Capture the clearest examples and keep them date-stamped.
- You do not need to solve every longer-term employment question before asking for basic role clarity.

## Important reassurance
Being excluded from work that affects your role can feel alarming. You do not need to resolve the cause immediately. A factual timeline, a calm written request, and clear notes about blocked work are useful first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and reduce avoidable mistakes. Later decisions may need specialist help tailored to your state, employer, and role.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Laws, deadlines, processes, and workplace policies can vary.
