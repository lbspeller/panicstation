# What to do if… your payment app account is locked and you have money stuck inside (USA)

## Short answer
Secure the account, move urgent payments away from the app, and create a written complaint trail asking how your remaining balance will be released. If support keeps you in a loop, escalate through official complaint channels such as the CFPB and, where relevant, your state regulator.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay anyone who claims they can unlock the account faster.
- Don’t share one-time codes, passkeys, passwords, or full identity documents through social media messages, texts, or unofficial phone numbers.
- Don’t keep resubmitting unclear verification information if the app is not accepting it; pause and ask support exactly what is missing.
- Don’t ignore your linked bank account, card, email, or phone while you wait.
- Don’t delete emails, chats, screenshots, or ticket numbers.
- Don’t make public posts containing your username, phone number, balance, transaction IDs, or identity documents.

## What to do now
1. **Make the next few days workable without the app.**  
   Move rent, utilities, groceries, transport, subscriptions, and other essentials to another payment method if you can. If wages, benefits, refunds, or regular deposits go to the app, contact the payer and change the destination for the next payment.

2. **Secure the payment app and the accounts connected to it.**  
   Change the payment app password if you can. Change the password on the email account linked to the app, turn on two-factor authentication, and check for new-device, password-reset, or email-forwarding changes. If you suspect someone has control of your phone number, contact your mobile carrier and ask how to secure the line.

3. **Check linked bank and card activity now.**  
   Look for transfers, card charges, withdrawals, or new linked accounts you do not recognise. If anything looks unauthorized, contact the bank or card issuer right away and ask how to report the transaction as an unauthorized transfer, fraud, or error.

4. **Save a simple evidence packet.**  
   Screenshot the lock message, the balance, the transaction list, verification requests, failed withdrawal messages, and support conversations. Write down the date and time access stopped, the amount stuck, the last successful withdrawal, and any support ticket numbers.

5. **Use only official support routes and ask for the payout path.**  
   Contact support through the app or the provider’s official website. Ask:
   - why the account is locked, even if they can only give a general reason,
   - what exact verification or document is needed,
   - whether the remaining balance can be sent by ACH transfer, check, or another clear method,
   - whether any pending transactions are blocking release, and
   - when you should expect the next written update.

6. **Send a formal complaint message to the provider.**  
   Use a clear subject such as “Formal complaint: account locked and funds inaccessible”. Include your account identifiers, the amount stuck, the date access stopped, the support ticket numbers, why the money is urgent, and the outcome you want: restored access or release of the remaining balance to you. Ask for a written response and a case number.

7. **Escalate if the provider stalls or sends repeated generic replies.**  
   Submit a CFPB complaint and upload your evidence packet. Choose the closest product category, such as money transfer, virtual currency, money services, prepaid card, or checking account, depending on the app and account type.

8. **Check whether a state regulator route may apply.**  
   Use NMLS Consumer Access to look up the company if it appears to be a licensed money transmitter. If you find a state regulator listed for your state, file directly through that regulator’s official website or complaint page. If NMLS is unavailable, check the provider’s official legal, licenses, or regulatory disclosures page for your state.

9. **If there is scam activity, report it and keep the confirmation.**  
   Report the scam to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov. Save scammer profiles, phone numbers, emails, payment references, usernames, and screenshots, but do not contact the scammer again just to gather more evidence.

10. **If identity theft may be involved, use a recovery checklist.**  
   If someone opened the account in your name, changed your login, used your identity documents, or took over your email or phone, use IdentityTheft.gov to create a recovery plan.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to sue, hire anyone, or start a long dispute strategy.
- You do not need to open several new payment apps right now.
- You do not need to publicly accuse the company or post private account details.
- You do not need to understand every banking or money-transmitter rule before sending a clear complaint and protecting your linked accounts.

## Important reassurance
A locked payment app can feel urgent and frightening when your money is inside. A calm written record, official support route, and clear request for release of the balance usually gives you a stronger path than repeated chats, public posts, or unofficial “unlock” offers.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: protect access, preserve evidence, ask for release of funds, and use official complaint routes. Later decisions may need specialist help if there is identity theft, a large loss, repeated unauthorized transfers, or urgent financial hardship.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Payment apps are structured differently, and the best route can depend on whether the issue involves a bank account, prepaid account, money transfer, card payment, virtual currency, or a licensed money transmitter.
