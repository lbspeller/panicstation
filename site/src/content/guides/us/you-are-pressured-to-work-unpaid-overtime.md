# What to do if… you are pressured to work unpaid overtime (USA)

## Short answer
Cease all informal or verbal communication regarding the allegation and request all threats or demands in writing. Do not sign any admissions, repayment agreements, or resignations until you have secured an external copy of the specific allegations.

## Do not do these things
- Do not admit fault, offer "context," or apologize in a recorded or written medium.
- Do not delete, modify, or "clean up" any digital records, logs, or emails related to the incident.
- Do not forward confidential company or client data to your personal email or cloud storage to "save evidence."
- Do not sign any document presented as a "statement of facts," "settlement," or "reimbursement agreement" during an initial meeting.
- Do not discuss the threat on internal messaging platforms like Slack, Teams, or company-monitored text threads.
- Do not contact a complaining customer or third party directly to resolve the matter personally.

## What to do now
1. **Request the formal allegation and "demand" in writing.** If the threat is verbal, send a follow-up email: "Following our conversation, please provide the specific legal claims being made, the party pursuing them, and any requested actions or deadlines in writing."
2. **Preserve existing records in their current state.** Leave all work-related communications, logs, and files exactly as they are in the company's systems to avoid allegations of evidence tampering.
3. **Draft a private, off-system factual timeline.** Use a personal device or notebook to record dates, specific instructions you received prior to the mistake, and the names of witnesses.
4. **Invoke Weingarten rights if you are in a union.** If you are called to a meeting you believe could lead to discipline, state: **"If this meeting could in any way lead to my being disciplined or terminated, I request that my union representative be present."**
   - If the request is denied, stay in the room but do not answer questions.
   - For non-union workers, you may still request a witness, but the employer is not currently federally mandated to grant it under 2026 NLRB precedent (IBM Corp.).
5. **Identify the type of legal document received.** Determine if you have a "Demand Letter" (from a lawyer), a "Summons and Complaint" (court papers), or an "Internal Investigation Notice."
6. **Route third-party threats through official channels.** If a customer or vendor threatens to sue you personally, forward the communication to your supervisor and the company’s legal/risk management department as required by policy.
7. **Verify professional liability coverage.** If you have personal "Errors and Omissions" (E&O) insurance or professional liability coverage, notify the carrier immediately according to their "notice of claim" protocol.
8. **Document potential retaliation triggers.** If the threat occurred shortly after you reported a violation (e.g., wage theft, safety hazard, harassment), record the date of your report and the date of the threat separately.
9. **Seek a "pause" on high-pressure demands.** If pressured to sign a document immediately, state: **"I am willing to cooperate with the process, but I require a copy of this document to review with my own advisor before signing."**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to resign or accept a financial settlement.
- You do not need to prove your innocence or "fix" the mistake in the next few hours.
- You do not need to respond to group chats or informal peer inquiries about the situation.
- You do not need to hire a private investigator or perform your own audit of company files.

## Important reassurance
A "threat" of legal action is a common tactic used to create urgency or leverage in workplace disputes; it does not mean a lawsuit has been filed or that you are liable. Taking a moment to document the situation and seek advice is a standard, protective procedure.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization only. Future steps depend on whether you are classified as an employee or independent contractor, and whether the threat originates from your employer or an outside party.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Workplace liability laws vary significantly by state. If you are served with a lawsuit or receive a formal demand letter, consult an attorney licensed in your jurisdiction. You can find legal aid or lawyer referral services through the American Bar Association or your state’s bar association.
