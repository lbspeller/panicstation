# What to do if… you hear a relative has died but cannot reach anyone to confirm the details (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the news through an official local route before you travel, send money, or tell others widely. Start with the place where your relative lived or was last known to be: local emergency dispatch if there may still be danger, otherwise local police, hospitals, or the county medical examiner or coroner.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t post publicly or notify extended family widely until you have confirmed the death through an official source or a close contact you already know is reliable.
- Don’t send money, gift cards, wire transfers, cryptocurrency, or payment-app transfers to anyone claiming to be handling arrangements until you independently verify who they are.
- Don’t trust a number or link provided in a message you do not fully trust; look up official numbers yourself.
- Don’t assume you can get details immediately; hospitals and public offices may need to verify your identity, relationship, or role before sharing anything.
- Don’t rush into long-distance travel before confirming the city, county, state, and which office, hospital, or funeral home is handling the next step.

## What to do now
1. **Make a quick facts note.**  
   Write down who told you, their number or account, the exact wording, where they said it happened, the date or time they gave, and any organization names. This keeps you from chasing conflicting versions.

2. **Pause if there is pressure to act fast.**  
   Scams around deaths and funeral arrangements often use urgency. If anyone asks for immediate payment, gift cards, wire transfers, cryptocurrency, secrecy, or a code sent to your phone, stop and verify through a separate official route.

3. **If someone may still be in immediate danger, call 911.**  
   Use 911 for an emergency that may need immediate police, fire, or ambulance help. Give the person’s name, last known address, phone number, age or date of birth if known, and why you are worried.

4. **If it is not an emergency but you need a welfare check or confirmation route, look up the local police or dispatch non-emergency contact.**  
   Search for the official website for the city or county where your relative lived or was last known to be. Ask how to request a welfare check or death-confirmation call-back, and give the person’s name, address, age or date of birth if known, and your relationship.

5. **Call likely hospitals through the main switchboard.**  
   Use the hospital’s official website or a trusted directory, not a number from a message. Ask whether they can confirm whether your relative is or was there, or whether they can explain what documentation family members need. They may only be able to share limited information.

6. **Contact the county medical examiner or coroner if the death may have been sudden, unattended, or unclear.**  
   If you know the county, search for the official county medical examiner or county coroner office. Ask whether they can confirm whether the case is with them, what information they can share, and what they need from a relative.

7. **If a funeral home is named, call it using independently verified contact details.**  
   Ask whether they are actually handling the arrangements. If they are, ask what family contact is listed and how you can safely share your contact details. Do not pay a caller who says a funeral will be cancelled unless you pay immediately.

8. **Once you know where the death occurred, use that state or local vital records process for the death certificate.**  
   Certified copies are usually ordered through the state or local vital records office where the death occurred. Expect to provide the date and place of death if known, your identity, and your relationship or reason for requesting the record.

9. **If Social Security needs to be notified and no funeral home is involved, use SSA directly.**  
   Funeral homes generally report deaths to Social Security. If a funeral home is not involved or does not report it, call SSA at 1-800-772-1213. Use TTY 1-800-325-0778 if you are deaf or hard of hearing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide about funerals, payments, travel, inheritance, belongings, or notifying everyone today.
- You do not need to contact banks, insurers, employers, landlords, or creditors until you have confirmation and a reliable point of contact.
- You do not need to order multiple certified death certificates immediately; first confirm where the death occurred and who is coordinating.

## Important reassurance
It is common for information to be unclear in the first hours or day. People may be travelling, overwhelmed, offline, or dealing with local officials. Moving slowly and verifying first protects you from misinformation, scams, and painful mistakes.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: confirm the facts, identify the city, county, and state, and establish a reliable point of contact. Later decisions may need help from the funeral home, local officials, an estate lawyer, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Processes vary by state, county, hospital, and the circumstances of the death. If anything feels off or you are being pressured, pause and rely only on independently verified official contacts.
