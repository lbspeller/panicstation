# What to do if… your employer asks to search your personal phone or laptop for a workplace investigation (USA)

## Short answer
Do not make an on-the-spot decision about handing over an unlocked personal device. Ask for the request in writing, ask for a brief pause to review it, and ask whether a narrower method can provide the specified work-related material.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete, edit, wipe, reset, move, or alter requested work-related material after you learn about the investigation.
- Do not unlock the whole device, share a passcode or biometric, or sign a broad consent form in a rush.
- Do not remove a work profile, uninstall work apps, or change device settings before asking what must be preserved.
- Do not send unrelated personal data just to be safe.
- Do not argue about policies or legal rights from memory. Ask for the written request and a brief pause to review it.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the request in writing.** Ask which device is involved, what material is sought, the relevant date range, which apps or accounts are in scope, whether the employer wants possession of the device, and whether any copy will be made.
2. **Ask about a narrower collection method.** Ask whether the employer can use records already held in its systems, collect from a managed work profile, accept a targeted export, or arrange supervised limited collection instead of reviewing the whole device.
3. **Review the policies you agreed to.** Look for bring-your-own-device, acceptable-use, investigation-cooperation, mobile-device-management, and confidentiality terms. Save a copy or note where the current policy is stored.
4. **Ask how personal material will be limited.** Ask who will handle the device or collected material, how unrelated personal information will be excluded where possible, how copies will be stored, and when the device and any collected material are expected to be returned or deleted.
5. **Ask for a union representative before continuing to answer investigatory questions when applicable.** If you are union-represented and reasonably believe questioning about your conduct could lead to discipline, clearly request a union representative before continuing to answer interview questions.
6. **Take extra care if you are a public-sector employee.** Public-sector employees are outside National Labor Relations Act coverage. Ask for the agency policy and seek prompt advice from your union, if any, or an employment attorney familiar with your role before agreeing to broad access.
7. **Create a simple record.** Note who made the request, the date and time, any deadline, what you provided, and whether the employer took possession of the device. Ask for a receipt that identifies the device, who holds it, whether it will be copied, the expected return time, and how to maintain access to essential accounts without changing investigation-relevant material.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to quit, file a complaint, or pursue legal action.
- You do not need to give a full account of every event before you understand the request.
- You do not need to change your longer-term device setup until the immediate request is resolved and any preservation needs are clear.

## Important reassurance
You do not have to solve every policy or legal question in the moment. Asking for written scope, a brief pause, and a limited collection method can reduce avoidable exposure while keeping the situation calm.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may depend on your state, employer policies, any agreement you accepted, your union status, whether you are a public-sector employee, and the exact request. Specialist help may be needed.
