# What to do if… your lights dim noticeably when appliances start and you suspect an electrical fault (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using the specific appliance that triggers the dimming immediately and unplug it if safe to do so. If you notice heat, a burning smell, or buzzing sounds, shut off the main power breaker and seek professional assistance.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t repeatedly turn the appliance on and off to "test" the dimming effect.
- Don’t ignore discolored outlet covers, warm switches, or a "fishy" or "burnt plastic" odor.
- Don’t attempt to open the electrical panel cover or manipulate internal wiring.
- Don’t use extension cords or power strips to move the appliance to a different outlet.
- Don’t touch an outlet or switch that feels hot to the touch.

## What to do now
1. **Turn off the appliance that triggered the dimming.** If the dimming occurs when a specific device starts (e.g., microwave, space heater, or vacuum), switch it off and unplug it from the wall.
2. **Scan the area for immediate sensory warnings.** Look and listen for signs of a more serious fault:
   - A smell similar to burning plastic, ozone, or rotting fish.
   - Visible smoke, sparks, or yellowing / browning on outlet covers.
   - Audible buzzing, crackling, or popping noises from walls or the breaker panel.
3. **Isolate the power if any warning signs are present.** Go to your main electrical panel:
   - **Shut off the individual breaker** for the affected room if you can identify it clearly.
   - **Shut off the main breaker** (the largest switch at the top or bottom) if you smell burning or are unsure which circuit is at fault.
4. **Evacuate and call 911 if smoke or fire is visible.** Do not attempt to investigate the source of smoke inside walls or behind fixtures.
5. **Reduce the total electrical load on the property.** Avoid running other high-power devices simultaneously until the system is inspected.
   - This includes air conditioners, electric dryers, dishwashers, and EV chargers.
6. **Identify if the issue is house-wide or room-specific.** Notice if lights are dimming in just one area or across the entire home. 
   - If the whole house is affected, or neighbors report similar issues, contact your local electric utility provider to report a potential service line fault.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the issue is a "loose neutral" or "voltage drop" right now.
- You do not need to replace light bulbs or appliances as a first step.
- You do not need to decide on a full home rewire or panel upgrade today.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel anxious when electrical systems behave unexpectedly. Dimming lights are a common way for home systems to signal that a circuit is reaching its capacity or that a connection needs tightening. Taking the immediate step of stopping the load is the correct safety response.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and harm prevention only. Technical diagnosis and repairs must be performed by a licensed electrician or utility professional.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and does not constitute professional electrical advice. If you detect heat, smoke, or sparks, prioritize evacuation and contact emergency services. Modern 2026 safety standards require specialized equipment to detect arc faults that may be causing these symptoms.
