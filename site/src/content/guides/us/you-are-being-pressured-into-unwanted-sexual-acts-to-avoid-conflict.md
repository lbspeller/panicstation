# What to do if… you are being pressured into unwanted sexual acts to avoid conflict (USA)
## Short answer
Get to the safest pause you can without arguing or taking extra risks. If you are in immediate danger, call 911; when it is safe, contact a sexual violence support service or a trusted person.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume that you owe anyone sexual acts to prevent conflict, anger, silence, or a breakup.
- Do not keep debating or explaining after you want the sexual activity to stop.
- Do not take extra risks to retrieve belongings, argue, or prove what happened.
- Do not blame yourself if you froze, complied, or chose the safest-seeming option in the moment.
- Do not use a phone or computer that may be monitored when a safer device is readily available.

## What to do now
1. **Reach the safest pause available.** If it is safe to move away, go to a public place, a trusted person, or another place where you are not alone with the person pressuring you.
2. **Call 911 if you are in immediate danger.** Do this as soon as it is safe to call.
3. **Contact one trusted person if you can.** Send a short message such as: “I need you to call me” or “Can I come to you?”
4. **Contact RAINN when it is safe to do so.** The National Sexual Assault Hotline is available 24/7: call **800-656-HOPE**, text **HOPE** to **64673**, or use online chat. Ask about confidentiality limits before sharing identifying details.
5. **Use relationship-abuse support if that fits your situation.** The National Domestic Violence Hotline offers confidential support 24/7: call **800-799-SAFE**, use online chat, or text **START** to **88788**.
6. **Ask about medical care if unwanted sexual contact happened.** RAINN can help you find a local clinic or hospital. You can ask what care is available and whether a **sexual assault forensic exam (SAFE)** is an option.
7. **Keep later options open only if it is safe and manageable.** If you may want medical or forensic support later, avoid deleting messages and ask a clinician or advocate what to do next.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide right now whether to report, confront the person, end the relationship, or choose a label for what happened.
- You do not have to explain everything to anyone you are not ready to tell.
- You do not need to collect evidence, write a detailed account, or make major decisions while you feel overwhelmed.

## Important reassurance
Pressure, threats, repeated asking, guilt, or being worn down can be sexual coercion. Freezing or complying to get through the moment does not make the pressure your fault.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need support from a specialist advocate, healthcare professional, or another appropriate professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If you feel at risk of harming yourself, call or text 988.
