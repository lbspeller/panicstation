# What to do if… you receive notice of conditions you must follow before your first court date (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the conditions as active now unless your lawyer or the court tells you otherwise. Follow the written conditions, avoid risky contact or travel, and contact your lawyer or the listed supervision office for safe clarification.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the conditions can be ignored because your first court date has not happened yet.
- Don’t contact anyone covered by a no-contact or stay-away condition, including through friends, family, social media, shared accounts, or messages passed by someone else.
- Don’t go to a protected address, workplace, school, pickup point, or shared location unless the written order clearly allows it or your lawyer has helped you confirm a safe route.
- Don’t change address, phone number, job schedule, travel plans, or monitoring arrangements without checking whether permission is needed.
- Don’t explain your side of the case to court staff, pretrial services, the protected person, witnesses, or police while trying to fix confusion.
- Don’t miss a check-in, test, monitoring appointment, or reporting deadline because you are waiting for a later court date.
- Don’t decide for yourself that a condition is unfair, unrealistic, or impossible and then break it.

## What to do now
1. **Read the notice once and turn it into a short checklist.**  
   Write down the exact conditions you see: people you cannot contact, places you cannot go, curfew hours, reporting dates, travel limits, testing requirements, device or monitor rules, weapons language, and any passport or travel-document language.

2. **Find the case details on the paperwork.**  
   Look for the case number, court name, issuing judge or magistrate, date signed, next court date, and any listed pretrial services or supervision contact. Take clear photos of every page.

3. **Contact your lawyer first, if you have one.**  
   Send the full notice and ask: “What applies today, what is unclear, and what is the safest way to ask for a change?” Do not rely on friends, the protected person, or online examples to interpret the order.

4. **If you do not have a lawyer yet, ask how to request appointed counsel.**  
   Use the phone number or website on the paperwork, or the official court website, and ask how to request a public defender, appointed counsel, or other court-appointed lawyer for this case. Keep the call practical: ask about the process, not the facts of what happened.

5. **If a pretrial services or supervision office is listed, contact it for logistics only.**  
   Ask where and when to report, what ID or paperwork to bring, how check-ins work, what to do if work or medical care conflicts with a date, and whether they can confirm the answer in writing. Keep the call about reporting, timing, location, and conflicts; do not discuss what happened or ask them for legal advice.

6. **Put guardrails around no-contact or stay-away conditions today.**  
   Block or mute phone numbers and social accounts so you cannot reply impulsively. Avoid shared places for now. If housing, childcare, transport, work, school, pets, medicine, or bills require coordination, ask your lawyer or the court-approved contact route before sending any message.

7. **Keep proof that you are trying to comply.**  
   Put the notice, court papers, reporting logs, appointment confirmations, screenshots of blocked contact attempts, and written clarification requests in one folder. If something goes wrong, this can help your lawyer or the court understand what happened.

8. **If a condition is impossible or unsafe, ask for a change through the proper route.**  
   In federal cases, release conditions can be reviewed or amended by the court. In state cases, the process varies, but it is often handled by a request or motion to modify bond or release conditions. Until a condition is changed in writing, keep following it unless a safety emergency prevents it; if that happens, contact your lawyer, the listed supervision office, or emergency services as appropriate.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to plead, negotiate, testify, or fight the case.
- You do not need to explain your side to the protected person, witnesses, police, or supervision staff.
- You do not need to solve every long-term housing, job, childcare, travel, or relationship problem today.
- You do not need to guess what a judge would probably allow; focus on complying now and using formal channels for any change.

## Important reassurance
This can feel sudden and unfair, especially if the first court date has not happened yet. The safest first move is not to solve the whole case, but to make the next few days more controlled: fewer risky contacts, fewer uncertain places, clear records, and quick legal help.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce the chance of an accidental violation and stabilise the situation. Later decisions about challenging, modifying, or explaining the conditions may need help from a qualified criminal defense lawyer in the state or federal court handling the case.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. Criminal procedure, bond terms, pretrial services, public defender access, and modification rules vary by state, county, court, and whether the case is state or federal. If anything is unclear, follow the plain wording of the notice, avoid contact and location risks, and seek guidance from your lawyer, the court, or the listed supervision contact.
