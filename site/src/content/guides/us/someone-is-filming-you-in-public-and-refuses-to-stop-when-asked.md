# What to do if… someone is filming you in public and refuses to stop when asked (USA)

## Short answer
Move toward safety, witnesses, and staff or security first. Do not try to grab the phone, block the person, or force them to delete anything.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t grab their phone or camera, push them, cover their lens with your hands, or try to delete footage.
- Don’t argue at length about whether the recording is legal while the situation is still active.
- Don’t follow them, block their path, or try to trap them into stopping.
- Don’t say your full name, address, workplace, school, license plate, or threats where they can record it.
- Don’t lead them toward your car, home, hotel room, workplace entrance, or a quieter area.
- Don’t post their face online in the heat of the moment to retaliate.

## What to do now
1. **Create space and witnesses.** Move toward a populated, well-lit area, a staffed counter, a security desk, a front desk, or another place with people nearby. Put distance, furniture, a counter, or a wall between you and the camera if you can do that without cornering yourself.
2. **Use one clear boundary, then disengage.** Say, calmly if possible: “Stop recording me. Do not come closer.” After that, focus on leaving, getting help, and not giving them more material to record.
3. **Ask staff or security for help.** In a store, mall, transit station, school, venue, hotel, or workplace lobby, say: “Someone is recording me and won’t stop. I feel unsafe. Can you take me to a safer area and handle the situation?” Staff or security may be able to enforce venue rules, ask the person to leave, or help you exit safely.
4. **Reduce what they can capture about you.** Turn badges, labels, tickets, packages, screens, and documents away from the camera. Keep your phone screen locked or angled down. Avoid saying personal details out loud.
5. **Call the right level of help.** If you are being threatened, followed, blocked from leaving, touched, cornered, or you think violence may happen, call 911. If you are safe but want to report harassment, intimidation, or repeated behavior, use the local police non-emergency number, or 311 if your city uses it for non-emergency help.
6. **If you call 911, give simple location details first.** Say where you are, what is happening now, whether you can leave, and whether there are threats, weapons, injuries, or a person following you. Follow the dispatcher’s instructions.
7. **Ask for a venue record if there is staff or security.** Ask whether they can make an incident report and preserve or flag relevant CCTV for the time and location. Ask for a report number, the staff member’s name or role, and the safest way to follow up.
8. **Make a short note once you are away.** Write the time, location, description of the person, what they said or did, whether they followed or blocked you, names of staff or witnesses, and any report number. Keep it factual and brief.
9. **If it repeats, keep the incidents together.** Save notes, report numbers, screenshots, messages, usernames, or links if the video appears online. Repeated filming, following, threats, or unwanted contact may need a different safety response than a one-off public recording.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether the recording was legal in your state.
- Searching for the video online while you are still in the situation.
- Contacting social media platforms while you are still shaken.
- Writing a full statement; a short timeline and key details are enough for now.
- Confronting the person again to force an apology, explanation, or deletion.

## Important reassurance
Feeling exposed, angry, or panicked is understandable when someone keeps recording after you ask them to stop. Creating distance, using witnesses, and asking staff or emergency services for help is a protective response, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: immediate safety, de-escalation, and preserving your options. Later decisions about reporting, online removal, workplace or school processes, formal safety options, or legal advice may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, security, mental health, or professional advice. In the USA, photographing or recording things plainly visible in public is often protected, but laws and private-property rules vary, and threats, following, harassment, assault, trespass, or blocking someone from leaving may still be unlawful or grounds for removal.
