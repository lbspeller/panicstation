# What to do if… you accidentally take someone else’s prescription medication (USA)

## Short answer
Do not take any more medication and call **Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222** or use **webPOISONCONTROL.org** immediately. Call **911** if the person is unconscious, having a seizure, or struggling to breathe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to "wait and see" if you feel fine right now; some medications have delayed effects.
- Do not induce vomiting unless specifically instructed to do so by a medical professional.
- Do not drink milk, coffee, or use home remedies to "neutralize" the drug.
- Do not take your own prescribed dose to "balance out" the mistake.
- Do not drive yourself or the affected person to the hospital; call 911 if transport is needed.
- Do not throw away the medication packaging or any loose pills.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency signs and call 911 immediately if any are present.**
   - Unconscious or cannot be woken up.
   - Shallow, slow, or stopped breathing.
   - Gurgling or snoring-like sounds (the "death rattle").
   - Pinpoint (very small) pupils.
   - Blue, gray, or pale lips, skin, or fingernails.
   - Seizures or uncontrollable shaking.
2. **Remove any remaining medication from the mouth.**
   - If the person is conscious, have them spit out any unswallowed fragments. 
   - Wipe out the mouth with a soft, clean cloth.
3. **Contact Poison Control immediately at 1-800-222-1222 or webPOISONCONTROL.org.**
   - This service is free, confidential, and staffed 24/7 by toxicologists.
   - Provide the medication name and strength (mg) from the label.
   - Provide the time the pill was taken and the estimated quantity.
   - State the age and approximate weight of the person involved.
4. **If opioids are suspected and the person is not breathing properly, use naloxone.**
   - Administer one dose of naloxone (Narcan) nasal spray if available.
   - Follow product instructions; more than one dose may be needed every 2 to 3 minutes until 911 arrives.
   - Perform rescue breathing or CPR if trained and the person has stopped breathing.
5. **Place an unconscious, breathing person in the recovery position.**
   - Roll them onto their side to keep the airway clear and prevent choking if they vomit.
6. **Keep the medication container nearby for the responders.**
   - The exact brand, dosage, and prescribing information are critical for clinicians to determine the correct antidote or treatment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to research side effects on the internet; Poison Control provides case-specific data.
- You do not need to notify the pharmacy or the prescribing doctor in the first few minutes.
- You do not need to figure out a new pill-organization system until the immediate medical risk is cleared.

## Important reassurance
Accidental ingestion of the wrong medication is a frequent reason for calls to emergency services and poison centers. These services are designed to handle these specific situations without judgment. Most cases managed early result in better outcomes.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions for the immediate aftermath of a medication error. It does not replace professional medical diagnosis or long-term care plans.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and does not constitute medical advice. Medication effects vary significantly based on individual health factors and drug interactions. In the U.S., always contact Poison Control (1-800-222-1222) or call 911 for personalized emergency guidance.
