# What to do if… you receive an invoice for a purchase or service you never ordered (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay the invoice, do not click any links, and do not use the contact information provided on the document. Verify your accounts through independent, official channels and file a written dispute with your financial institution if funds have already been moved.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay any amount to "stop the calls" or "avoid collections" before verification.
- Do not click links, open attachments, or scan QR codes within the suspicious invoice.
- Do not call the phone numbers or use the "customer support" links printed on the invoice.
- Do not provide your Social Security number, bank details, or one-time passcodes to anyone "confirming" the debt.
- Do not admit the debt is yours or agree to a payment plan during a verification call.
- Do not dispose of the record; digital or physical copies are necessary for reporting.

## What to do now
1. **Preserve the document as evidence.** Save the original email (including headers), take screenshots of digital requests, or keep the physical letter and envelope.
2. **Verify your actual financial records independently.** 
   - Check your bank and credit card statements directly through your bank's official app or website.
   - Do not use any login links provided in the invoice; type the official URL into your browser manually.
3. **Contact the alleged sender using verified information.** 
   - Look up the company's official customer service number on their verified website.
   - Ask if an account or order exists in your name, but do not provide sensitive data to "find" the account.
4. **Initiate a written dispute for unauthorized charges.** 
   - If a charge appears on your statement, you must mail a written dispute to your creditor's "billing inquiries" address (not the payment address).
   - This must be done within 60 days of the statement date to be protected under the Fair Credit Billing Act.
5. **Demand a debt validation notice from collectors.** 
   - If a collection agency contacts you, they are legally required to send a written validation notice within 5 days of their first contact.
   - Dispute the debt in writing within 30 days of receiving this notice to force the collector to pause activity and verify the debt.
6. **Protect your identity if personal info was compromised.**
   - Visit IdentityTheft.gov to create an official FTC Identity Theft Report and recovery plan.
   - Place a free fraud alert by contacting one of the three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion); the one you contact must notify the others.
   - Request a credit freeze individually at each of the three bureaus to prevent new accounts from being opened.
7. **Handle unordered merchandise as a gift.** 
   - Under federal law, if you receive merchandise you never ordered, you may keep it as a free gift and are under no legal obligation to pay for it or return it.
8. **Report the scam to federal authorities.**
   - File a report at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.
   - If the invoice arrived via the U.S. Mail, report it to the U.S. Postal Inspection Service (USPIS) online or at 877-876-2455.

## What can wait
- You do not need to respond to the sender of a fake invoice to "cancel" the order.
- You do not need to hire a lawyer immediately unless you are served with a formal court summons.
- You do not need to close all your bank accounts unless there is evidence they have been directly accessed.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a fraudulent invoice is a common high-pressure tactic designed to trigger a panicked payment. Having the invoice in your possession does not mean you owe money or that your credit is ruined. Following official dispute processes provides strong federal legal protections.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for unrecognized invoices and suspected billing scams. It does not cover long-term credit repair or legal representation for complex identity theft cases.

## Important note
This information is for harm prevention and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Federal protections like the Fair Credit Billing Act have strict time limits; prioritize written communication with your financial institution through their verified channels.
