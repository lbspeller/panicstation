# What to do if… you are contacted by a child protection or safeguarding agency asking to speak with you urgently (USA)

## Short answer
Verify the caller’s identity through an official government directory before sharing information, then exercise your right to remain silent or seek legal counsel before making formal statements.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use the phone number provided by the caller or your "caller ID" for verification; spoofing is possible.
- Don’t admit the worker into your home without a court order, a warrant, or a genuine medical emergency involving the child.
- Don’t volunteer information, "guess" at answers, or provide a full narrative of your family history during the first contact.
- Don’t sign any documents—including safety plans, service agreements, or medical releases—immediately without legal review.
- Don’t interview or "rehearse" answers with your children, as this can be interpreted as witness tampering or coaching.
- Don’t record conversations in "two-party consent" states without notifying the worker first.

## What to do now
1. **Request the caller's identifying information and end the initial call.** Ask for the worker’s full name, their specific agency (e.g., CPS, DCFS, ACS), their office location, a case number if one exists, and their supervisor’s name.
2. **Verify the worker’s identity independently.** 
   - Search for the official state or county government website for child welfare services (.gov).
   - Locate the agency's main public directory or regional office number.
   - Call that main office and ask to be transferred to the specific worker or supervisor you were just speaking with.
3. **Clarify the nature of the contact once verified.** Ask if this is a "Forensic Investigation" (searching for evidence of a crime/neglect) or a "Family Assessment / Differential Response" (a non-adversarial service-focused track).
4. **Invoke your right to silence or counsel politely.** **If they ask for a statement or an immediate interview, say: "I want to cooperate, but I need to consult with an attorney before I answer questions or agree to a home visit."**
   - You are generally not required to speak to an investigator without an attorney present.
   - Note that anything you say can be used in civil "dependency" court or criminal court.
5. **Establish the "immediate safety concern" only.** **Ask the worker: "What is the specific safety concern that makes this matter urgent today?"**
   - Focus only on addressing immediate life-safety issues (e.g., "Is the child fed and supervised right now?") rather than the history of the allegations.
6. **Communicate through written or documented channels.** Request that any further requests for interviews or visits be sent via email or letter. This creates a paper trail and prevents "he-said/she-said" disputes.
7. **Secure your home but maintain routine.** Ensure the child’s basic needs (food, clean bedding, utilities) are met. Do not flee or hide the child, as this can trigger emergency removal orders.
8. **Contact a family defense attorney or legal aid.** Look for "Parental Representation" programs or "Family Defense" specialists in your county. If you are low-income, search for your local Legal Services Corporation (LSC) grantee.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify who made the report (this information is often legally protected/anonymous anyway).
- You do not need to allow a physical inspection of every room in your house during the first hour.
- You do not need to agree to "voluntary" services or parenting classes until you understand the findings.
- You do not need to explain your entire life history or past mistakes during the initial contact.

## Important reassurance
An urgent contact from CPS is a stressful event, but it is a procedural step based on a report, not a final judgment. In many cases, an initial "safety check" results in the case being closed as "unsubstantiated" or "screened out" once immediate safety is confirmed. Taking a moment to verify the worker and seek advice is a standard way to protect your family's rights.

## Scope note
This guide addresses the first 60–120 minutes of contact with a child welfare agency in the United States. It does not cover court procedures, long-term foster care, or criminal defense strategies. Laws regarding "exigent circumstances" for child removal vary by state.

## Important note
Child welfare laws are state-specific and highly complex. This guide provides general procedural stabilization and is not a substitute for legal advice from a licensed attorney in your jurisdiction. If a worker claims they have a court order or an "exigency" to remove a child, you should comply physically while documenting their actions and calling an attorney immediately.
