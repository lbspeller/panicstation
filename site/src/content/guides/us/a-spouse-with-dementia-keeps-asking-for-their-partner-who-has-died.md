# What to do if… a spouse with dementia keeps asking for their partner who has died (USA)

## Short answer
Do not rush into a long explanation. For the next hour, choose a brief direct explanation or a comfort-first response according to what reduces distress and keeps them safe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not tell them in a hectic moment, in public, or with several people talking at once.
- Do not use confusing euphemisms such as “went to sleep” if you decide to tell them.
- Do not argue, demand that they remember, or test their memory.
- Do not automatically repeat the news each time they ask if it causes a fresh painful reaction and is not retained.
- Do not leave them alone if they may try to go out searching for their partner.
- Do not force a permanent decision about what to say every time they ask.

## What to do now
1. **Reduce noise and stay nearby.** Move to a calm room, use one speaker, and make sure someone can stay with them afterward.
2. **Choose an approach for the next hour.** If you decide to explain, keep it brief and plain: “I’m so sorry. [Name] died.” If repeated explanations cause a fresh painful reaction and are not retained, focus on comfort rather than repeatedly breaking the news for now.
3. **Respond to the feeling.** Use a calm phrase such as: “You’re safe. I’m here with you.” Allow time for a response. Do not argue about what they remember.
4. **Offer one familiar activity.** Try tea, familiar music, photographs, sitting together, or folding towels. Keep the choice simple.
5. **Prepare one repeatable response.** Ask household members or the care team to use the same brief response for now. Adjust it if it increases distress.
6. **Contact their clinician about new or rapidly worsening changes.** Call their primary care clinician or dementia care clinician if there is new agitation, sleep disruption, pacing, repeated attempts to leave, or another sudden behavior change.
7. **Reduce immediate leave-seeking risk.** Stay nearby during higher-risk times. If the person is no longer driving, remove access to car keys. If helpful, store visible leaving cues such as coats, hats, keys, and wallets. Ask a trusted neighbor, friend, or family member to call if they see the person wandering or appearing lost.
8. **Use dementia support.** Call the Alzheimer’s Association 24/7 Helpline at **800-272-3900** for support, local resources, crisis assistance, and emotional support.
9. **Call 911 for immediate danger.** Use **911** straight away if there is an urgent safety threat, such as the person entering traffic or becoming violent. If the person is missing, begin looking nearby immediately. If they are not found within 15 minutes, call **911** and say that they have dementia. Call sooner if immediate danger is likely.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today what you will say every future time they ask.
- You do not need to decide right now whether they should attend services or take part in other arrangements.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements today.
- You do not need to make major long-term care decisions unless there is an urgent safety issue.

## Important reassurance
There may not be one perfect script. It is reasonable to adjust the response according to what the person understands, what they retain, and what reduces distress in the moment.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about bereavement support, behavior changes, and care planning may need help from the person’s clinicians or dementia support services.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Use urgent or emergency services when safety is at risk.
