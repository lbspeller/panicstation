# What to do if… you are told you must start working on-call immediately without prior agreement (USA)

## Short answer
Request a written separation summary and a copy of any severance agreement before leaving the premises. Do not sign any waivers or releases during the initial meeting.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a severance agreement or "release of claims" during the termination meeting.
- Do not resign or sign a voluntary resignation letter if you are being laid off.
- Do not delete work files or download company-owned data to personal devices.
- Do not make emotional or aggressive statements to HR or management.
- Do not assume "effective immediately" means you lose your right to final pay or benefits.

## What to do now
1. **Request a written separation notice before exiting the building.** This document should confirm the separation date, the reason for termination (e.g., position elimination or layoff), and contact information for the HR representative managing your file.
2. **Verify the timing of your final paycheck.** State laws vary significantly; some jurisdictions require immediate payment upon involuntary termination, while others allow payment by the next regular payday.
   - Ask if the final check includes accrued but unused Paid Time Off (PTO) or vacation time.
   - Ask for a breakdown of any commissions, bonuses, or expense reimbursements owed.
3. **Secure access to your personal employment documents within the HR system.**
   - Download or print your most recent paystubs, W-2 forms from previous years, and your original offer letter or employment contract.
   - Ensure your personal email and physical mailing address are updated in the system for tax and benefits mailings.
4. **Inquire about the COBRA health insurance election process.**
   - Confirm the exact date your current employer-sponsored health coverage expires.
   - Request the name and contact information of the COBRA third-party administrator who will mail your election notice.
5. **Ask if a federal or state WARN Act notice was issued for this layoff.** The federal WARN Act generally requires 60 days' notice for employers with 100+ employees in certain mass layoff scenarios, but some states (like California) have lower thresholds, such as 75 employees.
6. **Retain any severance or waiver documents for off-site review.** If you are 40 years of age or older, federal law (OWBPA) typically requires that you be given 21 days to consider an individual severance agreement (or 45 days for group layoffs) and 7 days to revoke your signature after signing.
7. **Contact your state's unemployment insurance agency to initiate a claim.** Eligibility is determined by the state where you worked, and most "no-fault" separations like role eliminations qualify for benefits.

## What can wait
- Signing a severance agreement or release of claims.
- Returning non-essential company equipment if a later drop-off is permitted.
- Deciding between COBRA and a Health Insurance Marketplace plan.
- Updating professional social media profiles or resumes.
- Notifying your professional network of the change.

## Important reassurance
The shock of an immediate role elimination often triggers a physical stress response that makes it difficult to process complex legal or financial details. It is standard practice to take paperwork home for review; taking this time is a procedural step, not an indication of conflict.

## Scope note
This guide addresses the immediate stabilization of an employment crisis. It does not cover legal strategies for wrongful termination, detailed tax implications of severance, or long-term career planning.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Employment laws, including final pay requirements and WARN Act thresholds, vary by state and company size. Consult with an employment attorney or your state's Department of Labor for specific legal protections applicable to your situation.
