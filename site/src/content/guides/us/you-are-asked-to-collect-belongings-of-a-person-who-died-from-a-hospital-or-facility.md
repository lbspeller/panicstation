# What to do if… you are asked to collect belongings of a person who died from a hospital or facility (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the facility’s Decedent Affairs or Security office to confirm their specific property release protocol, then present a government-issued photo ID and any required next-of-kin documentation to receive the items and a signed inventory receipt.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t arrive at the facility without a confirmed appointment or confirmation that the property is ready for release.
- Don’t leave the facility without a signed, itemized inventory of every item handed over to you.
- Don’t assume medications will be returned; most facilities are legally required to dispose of prescription drugs.
- Don’t expect government documents (passports, military IDs) to be released if the Medical Examiner is involved; these are often returned directly to the issuing agencies.
- Don’t discard the decedent’s mobile phone, wallet, or keys, as these are often essential for accessing accounts or securing the home later.
- Don’t attempt to collect items if you are not the legally recognized next of kin or authorized representative.

## What to do now
1. **Call the facility and locate the office responsible for property.**  
   **Ask to speak with Decedent Affairs, Patient Relations, or the Security Office.**  
   - Confirm the exact location of the belongings (some are held in a central safe, others on the nursing unit).  
   - Ask if a specific appointment is required for property pickup.  
   - Request a list of the exact documents they require to verify your authority (e.g., proof of kinship, a facility-specific release form, or a letter of administration).

2. **Verify if the Medical Examiner or Coroner is involved.**  
   **Ask staff if the decedent’s property was transported with the body to a county office.**  
   - If the Medical Examiner (ME) has the property, call their office first; they often have stricter "normal business hours" and may require a different "Property Release Form."  
   - Ask the ME office if clothing will stay with the body or if you must pick it up separately.  
   - Note that in cases involving criminal investigations, certain items may be held as evidence indefinitely.

3. **Identify "Resident Trust Fund" accounts in nursing homes.**  
   **Ask the facility administrator for a final accounting of any funds held in the resident's name.**  
   - Under federal law, nursing homes must return the funds and a final accounting to the estate within 30 days of death.  
   - Ensure they have the correct mailing address for the person handling the estate.

4. **Prepare your documentation before leaving home.**  
   **Ensure you have a valid, unexpired government-issued photo ID.**  
   - Bring any specific forms the facility emailed or mentioned over the phone.  
   - If you are not the spouse or child, bring documentation showing your legal authority (such as a Power of Attorney that survives death, though these are rare, or probate court documents).

5. **Perform a physical inventory check at the time of pickup.**  
   **Review the facility's inventory list against the physical items before you sign the release.**  
   - Check specifically for "high-loss" items: jewelry (rings, earrings), watches, hearing aids, dentures, and glasses.  
   - If an item is missing, ask immediately if it was moved to a "Security Safe" or if it was documented as being on the body when transported to the funeral home.

6. **Secure sensitive digital and physical access items.**  
   **Place the decedent’s phone, tablet, keys, and wallet in a secure, centralized location once home.**  
   - Do not attempt to guess passwords or "factory reset" devices, as this may permanently lock access to important records.

## What can wait
- Sorting through clothing or non-essential personal effects can wait until you have the emotional capacity.
- Determining the final distribution of jewelry or valuables among family members does not need to happen today.
- You do not need a full probate court order just to pick up basic clothing and personal effects in most jurisdictions; the facility’s internal "Next of Kin" release form is often sufficient for these items.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel overwhelmed, hesitant, or even numb when performing this task. Taking a second person with you can help manage the paperwork and provide a witness to the inventory process. Asking for a receipt and a clear list is a standard professional procedure and is not seen as "being difficult" by facility staff.

## Scope note
This guide covers the immediate physical collection of personal property from a medical or care facility. It does not cover the legal distribution of assets, the probate process, or the handling of titled property like vehicles.

## Important note
Procedures vary significantly by state law and individual facility policy. If a death is under investigation by law enforcement, different rules for evidence preservation will apply. Always verify specific document requirements with the facility’s legal or security department before traveling.
