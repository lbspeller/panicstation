# What to do if… police ask to take or search your phone or other electronic device (USA)
## Short answer
Say: **“I do not consent to a search of my device.”** Ask whether you are free to leave and whether police have a warrant. Do not agree voluntarily to unlock the device or share a passcode on the spot; ask for legal help before responding to any demand or order.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to a voluntary search just to end the conversation if you are unsure. Consent can affect your legal position later.
- Do not volunteer a passcode, PIN, password, or biometric unlock.
- Do not delete anything, wipe the device, or change its contents.
- Do not physically resist if police take the device.
- Do not argue about legal technicalities during the encounter.

## What to do now
1. **Refuse consent clearly.** Say: **“I do not consent to a search of my phone or any of its contents.”**
2. **Ask about your status.** Ask: **“Am I being detained, or am I free to leave?”** If needed, ask: **“Am I under arrest?”**
3. **Ask what police are requesting.** Ask: **“Do you have a warrant to search the contents of my device?”** Then ask: **“Are you taking the device, or are you asking me to agree to an examination of its contents?”**
4. **If the request is voluntary, pause.** Say: **“I am not agreeing to that. I want legal advice first.”** Do not discuss messages, photos, files, or accounts on the device.
5. **Leave the device locked.** If you still have the device, avoid entering a passcode or using biometric unlock where it can be observed. Do not change settings or delete anything.
6. **If police take the device, do not resist.** Ask for a receipt or property form. As soon as safely possible, note the agency, officer name or badge number, date, time, location, and any case or incident number.
7. **If police ask investigative questions, limit what you say.** Say: **“I am going to remain silent. I want a lawyer.”** Identification requirements can vary by state and situation, so ask for legal advice if you are unsure what information you must provide.
8. **Mention urgent practical needs briefly.** If losing the device creates an immediate problem with medical care, childcare, travel, work access, or account security, ask whether there is a way to retrieve essential information without agreeing to a search. Do not enter credentials or agree to unlock the device as part of that request.
9. **Get legal help promptly.** Contact a criminal defense attorney as soon as safely possible. If criminal proceedings begin and you cannot afford counsel, ask the court or a local defender office about appointed counsel.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to explain your messages, photos, files, or online activity during the encounter.
- You do **not** need to decide immediately whether to challenge the taking of the device.
- You do **not** need to debate exceptions or legal definitions on the street.
- You do **not** need to solve work, account-access, or replacement-phone problems before you are somewhere safe.

## Important reassurance
Being unsure is understandable. You can stay calm, refuse consent clearly, avoid volunteering access, and ask for a receipt if the device is taken. You do not need to resolve every legal question during the encounter.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Taking a device and searching its digital contents are not the same thing, and the rules can depend on the facts, the state, and the type of police contact. Border and port-of-entry inspections, probation, supervised release, and other special settings may involve different rules. Later decisions may need advice from a qualified lawyer.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. A lawyer can advise you about a warrant, court order, seizure, passcode request, biometric unlock request, or any local rule that may apply to your situation.
