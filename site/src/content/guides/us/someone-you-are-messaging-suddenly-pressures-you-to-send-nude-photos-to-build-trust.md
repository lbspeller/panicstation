# What to do if… someone you are messaging suddenly pressures you to send nude photos to build trust (USA)

## Short answer
Do not send a nude or intimate image to prove trust. Stop replying to the request and block the account, or mute it if that option is available and feels safer for the moment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send an intimate image because you feel pressured, rushed, guilty, or afraid of losing the relationship.
- Do not send money or more images if the person threatens to share something.
- Do not argue, bargain, or keep explaining your boundary.
- Do not share your address, school, workplace, other accounts, or location.
- Do not meet the person to try to resolve the situation.

## What to do now
1. **Stop the exchange.** You do not need to decide what the request means before ending the conversation.
2. **Use the platform controls.** Block the account, or mute it if that option is available and feels safer for the moment. You can also use the platform’s reporting option if that feels right for you.
3. **Keep one optional record.** If you may want support later, save the request, the account details, and any threats without replying further.
4. **If you are an adult and the person already has an image or claims to have one, do not pay or send more.** The CCRI Safety Center can help you consider your next step.
5. **If you are under 18, tell a trusted adult who can stay with you while you get support.** If an image or video was taken before you turned 18, NCMEC’s Take It Down service may help limit online sharing on participating platforms. Do not download or forward an image or video to submit it. NCMEC’s CyberTipline is also available if you want to report suspected online child sexual exploitation.
6. **If you are an adult and the situation has become online extortion or blackmail, you can choose to file a report with the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center.**
7. **If you feel physically unsafe or someone is threatening immediate harm, call 911.**
8. **If you feel overwhelmed, contact RAINN for confidential support from a trained support specialist.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to make a public post, warn other people, or confront the account.
- You do not need to build a detailed evidence file before asking for support.
- You do not need to explain why you said no.
- You do not need to work out whether the person was joking, testing you, or trying to manipulate you.

## Important reassurance
You are allowed to stop replying at any point. Feeling embarrassed, frozen, or unsure does not mean you did anything wrong, and sending an image earlier does not mean you have to send another one.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the moment and reduce harm. Later decisions may need specialist support, especially if threats continue, an image has been shared, or you feel unsafe.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911.
