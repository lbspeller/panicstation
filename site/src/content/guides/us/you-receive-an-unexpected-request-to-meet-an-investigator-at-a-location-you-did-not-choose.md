# What to do if… you receive an unexpected request to meet an investigator at a location you did not choose (USA)

## Short answer
Do not go to the unexpected location or discuss the facts yet. Pause, verify the request through an official public route you find yourself, and speak with a lawyer before any interview if you may be questioned about wrongdoing or your role is unclear.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go to an unexpected meeting location until you have independently verified the person, agency, location, and whether the meeting is voluntary.
- Don’t discuss the facts of the situation by phone, text, email, doorstep conversation, or “off the record” chat while the request is unverified or your role is unclear.
- Don’t trust caller ID, a badge number, a real-sounding name, or personal details about you as proof.
- Don’t click links or share personal identifiers because someone claims to be an investigator or law enforcement officer.
- Don’t pay money, “bond”, “fees”, or “fines” because of a call or message, especially by gift card, wire transfer, cryptocurrency, payment app, prepaid card, cash machine, or mailed cash.
- Don’t hand over your phone, unlock devices, or volunteer documents without legal advice or clear written authority.

## What to do now
1. **Create a pause and take control of timing.** You can say: “I can’t meet or answer questions today. I need to verify this and speak with counsel. Please provide the request in writing.”
2. **Treat the unexpected location as unverified for now.** A request to meet somewhere unusual, private, rushed, or hard to verify is a reason to slow down, not a reason to comply faster.
3. **If they ask for money or payment in any form, end the contact.** Hang up or stop replying. Real law enforcement generally will not call to threaten arrest or demand payment to avoid arrest.
4. **Verify independently using an official public route, not the number or link they gave you.**
   - Ask for the person’s full name, agency, badge or ID number, supervisor or unit, and case or reference number.
   - Remember that these details can be faked or copied from real officials.
   - Find the agency’s official website or main public phone number yourself, such as the city police department, sheriff’s office, state agency, court, or federal field office, and ask whether the person works there and whether the request is real.
5. **Clarify what you are being asked to do without discussing facts.**
   - Ask whether you are being contacted as a witness, victim, suspect, person under supervision, or something else.
   - Ask whether the meeting is voluntary.
   - Ask whether they claim there is a subpoena, court order, warrant, or other written authority. If they do, ask for a copy in writing.
6. **If you might be a suspect, or it is unclear, invoke your rights and stop.** Say: “I am not answering questions. I want a lawyer.” Then stop talking about the situation. Even context meant to be helpful may be used against you.
7. **If a real meeting is needed, ask for a safer official setting and counsel.** Ask whether the meeting can happen at the agency office, courthouse, or another clearly official location, and with your attorney present. If they insist on a secluded or unusual location, rush you, or discourage counsel, do not attend before getting legal advice.
8. **If you are on probation, parole, pretrial release, or court supervision, verify through your existing official route.** Contact your supervising officer using the number you already have or the agency’s main line. Ask whether the contact is legitimate and whether you have any reporting requirement. Do not discuss the facts of any new allegation without a lawyer.
9. **If you are incarcerated or the request comes through jail or prison channels, ask for counsel and formal verification.** Use the facility’s process to request counsel and ask staff to verify the investigator’s name, agency, and case number. Do not agree to an “off the record” interview.
10. **Preserve what you received.** Save voicemails, texts, emails, letters, envelopes, phone numbers, usernames, links, payment instructions, and the requested date, time, and location. This can help a lawyer, the real agency, or a fraud-reporting service check what happened.
11. **If someone appears in person and pressures you, end it safely if you can.** Ask: “Am I free to leave or end this conversation?” If yes, leave or close the door. If not, say: “I want a lawyer. I am remaining silent.” Provide only information you are legally required to provide in your state, because those rules vary.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain your side, assemble evidence, or write a statement right now.
- You do not need to decide whether to “cooperate” before you know who is contacting you, what they want, and whether the request is voluntary.
- You do not need to attend a surprise meeting just because someone requested it.
- You do not need to solve the whole legal situation today; the immediate task is verification, safety, and getting advice before irreversible steps.

## Important reassurance
An unexpected official-sounding request can make anyone freeze or comply reflexively. Slowing down, verifying identity through a route you find yourself, and asking for counsel are normal protective steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a qualified attorney in your state, especially if you may be suspected of wrongdoing, are under court supervision, or are being contacted by a federal agency.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. U.S. rules vary by state, agency, custody status, and court order, so a qualified attorney can give advice about your specific situation.
