# What to do if… you notice new vision changes like flashing lights, blind spots, or blurred vision (USA)

## Short answer
Treat sudden or new vision changes as a medical emergency. **Call 911 immediately** for sudden vision loss (even if painless), vision changes accompanied by stroke symptoms, or severe eye pain with nausea and rainbow halos.

## Do not do these things
- Do not **drive yourself** to the hospital or an appointment.
- Do not **wait to see** if symptoms improve or go away on their own.
- Do not **assume a painless change is safe**; many sight-threatening conditions, like eye strokes or retinal detachments, are painless.
- Do not **rub, press, or apply pressure** to your eye.
- Do not use **anyone else’s eye drops** or medications you have at home.
- Do not **ignore symptoms** just because they lasted only a few minutes.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 immediately if you experience sudden vision loss.**
   - This includes a total "blackout," significant dimming, or a sudden "eye stroke" where vision disappears in one eye without pain.
2. **Seek emergency care right away if vision changes occur alongside stroke signs.**
   - Check for the F.A.S.T. signs: Face drooping, Arm weakness, or Speech difficulty.
   - Other red flags include sudden confusion, loss of balance, or a severe "thunderclap" headache.
3. **Go to the Emergency Department for severe eye pain and nausea.**
   - Watch for specific cues like extreme redness, a "hard" feeling in the eye, or seeing rainbow-colored halos around lights.
4. **Contact an ophthalmologist for a same-day urgent appointment if you see new flashes or floaters.**
   - If you see a "shower" of new spots, lightning-like flashes, or a dark curtain/shadow moving across your vision, you must be seen today.
   - If an eye specialist is not available to see you within hours, proceed to the nearest Emergency Department.
5. **Perform a brief, safe check of each eye separately.**
   - **Cover one eye at a time** to determine if the issue is in the left eye, right eye, or both.
   - Do not strain or spend more than a few seconds testing; prioritize getting to professional care.
6. **Gather your basic medical information if time permits.**
   - Note the exact time symptoms started.
   - List your current medications (especially blood thinners) and any recent eye surgeries or injuries.
7. **Prepare for a dilated eye exam.**
   - Bring your glasses and have a driver ready, as your vision will be temporarily blurred and sensitive to light after the exam.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific medical cause (e.g., detachment vs. migraine) before seeking help.
- You do not need to notify your insurance company or employer before getting stabilized.
- You do not need to find your previous eye exam records if they are not immediately at hand.

## Important reassurance
Sudden vision changes are alarming, but prompt medical intervention significantly increases the chance of preserving your sight. Medical teams are trained to prioritize these symptoms; you are not "overreacting" by seeking immediate emergency or urgent care for your eyes.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and triage instructions only. It is intended to help you reach the appropriate level of care and does not replace a professional ophthalmic or neurological evaluation.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute medical advice or a diagnosis. If you are experiencing a medical emergency, **call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room immediately**.
