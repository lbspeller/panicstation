# What to do if… your trunk or hatch will not close and you need to travel with luggage (USA)

## Short answer
Do not start driving until the luggage is secured so it cannot shift or fall out, and until your brake lights, turn signals, reflectors, necessary driver view, and license plate are not blocked. If you cannot make that happen quickly, change the plan instead of forcing the trip.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive with loose suitcases, boxes, or bags that could fly forward in a hard stop.
- Do not rely on one light bungee cord, tape, string, or someone holding the trunk down by hand.
- Do not drive if the trunk, hatch, or tailgate could open farther, or if anything could slide out.
- Do not block tail lights, brake lights, turn signals, reflectors, or the license plate.
- Do not let cargo hang near the exhaust, touch the road, or drag behind the vehicle.
- Do not ignore exhaust smell in the cabin, headache, dizziness, weakness, nausea, chest pain, confusion, or feeling faint.

## What to do now
1. **Pull into a safer place before solving it.** Use a parking lot, service area, driveway, or wide shoulder away from moving traffic if that is the safest available option.
2. **Check for the simple obstruction first.**
   - Remove straps, coat sleeves, fabric, bag handles, suitcase corners, and loose items from the latch area.
   - Press the normal trunk, hatch, or key-fob release in case the latch has clicked shut while open.
   - Try closing it once gently. Do not slam it repeatedly or force a bent latch or striker.
3. **Choose the safest way to carry the luggage.**
   - Best: rearrange the luggage so the trunk, hatch, or tailgate closes and latches normally.
   - Next: move luggage into the passenger area only if it can be restrained and does not block the driver’s view or controls.
   - If neither works: ship the luggage, use a larger vehicle, call roadside assistance, or delay the trip.
4. **If luggage goes inside the cabin, secure it.**
   - Put heavy items low and forward, such as on the floor or behind the front seats.
   - Use seat belts through suitcase handles where possible, then buckle them.
   - Do not stack hard luggage high behind heads, necks, or on top of seats.
5. **If anything would stick out of an open trunk or hatch, treat that as a stop sign unless you know the state rules.**
   - All states and Washington, DC have unsecured-load laws, and projecting-load marking rules vary by state.
   - If cargo could fall, shift, leak, blow out, block required lights, hide the license plate, or leave you unsure whether it is legal, do not continue the trip.
6. **Secure any carried load before the vehicle moves.**
   - Use proper straps, netting, or rope rated for the load, attached to solid vehicle tie-down points.
   - Use more than one securement point so a single failure does not release the load.
   - Tighten everything, then push and pull the luggage firmly. If it moves, it is not secure enough.
   - Keep straps away from hinges, latch mechanisms, hot exhaust parts, tires, and sharp edges.
7. **If you smell exhaust or feel possible carbon monoxide symptoms, stop and get fresh air.**
   - Pull over safely, turn the vehicle off, open doors, and move away from exhaust.
   - Call 911 or another local emergency number for medical help if symptoms occur, especially confusion, faintness, chest pain, severe weakness, or symptoms that do not quickly improve in fresh air.
   - Do not drive yourself for medical help if you may have carbon monoxide symptoms.
8. **Use a safer fallback if the load cannot be made secure.**
   - Ship the luggage with a carrier or courier.
   - Rent or borrow a larger vehicle.
   - Call roadside assistance or a repair shop for help with the latch.
   - Leave non-essential luggage behind and collect or ship it later.
9. **If the current spot is unsafe, only move to the nearest safer place.**
   - Move only as far as needed to get out of immediate traffic danger.
   - Stop again as soon as you reach a safer place, then use the safer fallback options above.

## What can wait
- You do not need to fully diagnose the latch mechanism right now.
- You do not need to decide on permanent repairs today.
- You do not need to solve the whole trip at once; first decide whether this vehicle can move safely with this luggage.
- Booking changes, repair quotes, insurance questions, and replacement parts can wait until the load is secure or the trip plan has changed.

## Important reassurance
This is frustrating because it usually happens when you are already rushed. Slowing down now is not overreacting; it is the step that prevents a loose load, blocked lights, exhaust exposure, or a rushed unsafe drive.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate travel problem. Later decisions about repairs, rental vehicles, roadside assistance, shipping, or state-specific legal requirements may need help from a mechanic, roadside service, carrier, rental company, or local authority.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, financial, insurance, mechanical, or professional advice. Cargo, projecting-load, lighting, and license-plate rules vary by state; if you cannot confidently keep the load secured and required equipment visible, do not drive with that load.
