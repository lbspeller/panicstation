# What to do if… a GFCI or RCD outlet keeps tripping and you can’t restore power safely (USA)

## Short answer
Treat a GFCI that will not reset or trips repeatedly as an active electrical fault: stop attempts to restore power, unplug all devices on the circuit, and leave the power off until it is inspected by a professional.

## Do not do these things
- Don't hold the RESET button down, tape it in place, or force it to stay.
- Don't keep trying to reset the outlet if it trips immediately every time.
- Don't touch any electrical components, cords, or the breaker panel if you are wet or standing on a damp surface.
- Don't use the outlet if you notice a burning smell, sparks, popping sounds, heat, or scorch marks.
- Don't bypass the GFCI using extension cords from another room or circuit.
- Don't open the outlet box, junction boxes, or the main electrical panel covers.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize fire safety immediately.** **If you see smoke, flames, or notice a strong burning smell, evacuate the building and call 911 from a safe distance.**
2. **Stop all reset attempts.** **If the RESET button pops back out immediately after being pressed, stop trying to restore power.** Persistent tripping indicates a "ground fault" (electricity leaking where it shouldn't) or a device failure.
3. **Isolate the circuit by unplugging all devices.** **Disconnect every appliance and cord from the tripped GFCI outlet and any other outlets that have lost power.** 
   - Check nearby bathrooms, kitchens, garages, and outdoor areas, as one GFCI often protects multiple "downstream" outlets.
   - Only touch cords if your hands and the floor are completely dry.
4. **Inspect the outlet for visual failure cues.** **Look for a small indicator light on the face of the GFCI (often red, amber, or blinking).** 
   - A solid red or blinking light on modern self-testing GFCIs typically indicates the internal safety mechanism has failed and the unit must be replaced.
5. **Attempt one careful reset only if the area is dry.** **If there is no moisture, heat, or smell, press the RESET button firmly once.**
   - If it clicks and stays in, plug in one small, low-power device (like a lamp) to verify power.
   - If it trips again, stop immediately.
6. **Identify the trigger device safely.** **If the power holds, plug your original devices back in one at a time.**
   - If the GFCI trips the moment a specific device is plugged in or turned on, that device is faulty and must be removed from service.
7. **Check the main breaker panel once.** **If the GFCI button feels "mushy" or won't click at all, check if a circuit breaker in your main panel has tripped to the middle or "OFF" position.** 
   - Flip the breaker fully to "OFF" then back to "ON" exactly once. 
   - If it trips again immediately, do not attempt to reset it a second time.
8. **Contact a qualified person for inspection.** 
   - **Renting:** Report the issue to your landlord or property manager as an "urgent electrical safety hazard."
   - **Homeowners:** Call a licensed electrician to diagnose if the issue is moisture intrusion, a failed GFCI unit, or damaged "in-wall" wiring.

## What can wait
- You don't need to determine if the fault is in the wiring or the outlet itself.
- You don't need to replace the GFCI unit yourself today.
- You don't need to test every appliance in the house; focus only on the dead circuit.
- You don't need to decide on long-term electrical upgrades right now.

## Important reassurance
GFCIs are designed to be extremely sensitive to protect you from electrocution. If the outlet is tripping, it is performing its safety function. A loss of power is a minor inconvenience compared to a potential shock or fire hazard.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only. Electrical troubleshooting involving circuit testing and component replacement requires specialized tools and professional certification.

## Important note
This guide provides general safety stabilization and does not replace the advice of a licensed electrician. Electrical faults can lead to fire or death if handled incorrectly; always default to professional inspection if power will not stay on.
