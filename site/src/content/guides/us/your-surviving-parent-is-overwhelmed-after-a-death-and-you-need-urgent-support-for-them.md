# What to do if… your surviving parent is overwhelmed after a death and you need urgent support for them (USA)

## Short answer
Ensure your parent is physically safe and supervised, then contact the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline for immediate guidance or their Primary Care Physician (PCP) for an urgent medical assessment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave them alone if they are expressing thoughts of self-harm, are unable to maintain physical safety, or appear severely disoriented.
- Do not force major logistical decisions, such as property sales or funeral contracts, during the first 48 hours of acute distress.
- Do not administer unprescribed medications or alcohol to "calm them down," as these can cause dangerous interactions or respiratory depression in shock.
- Do not attempt to "reason" them out of their feelings or demand they stop crying.
- Do not allow a crowd of visitors if your parent is showing signs of sensory overload or increased agitation.

## What to do now
1. **Assess the immediate physical safety of your parent right now.** Ask clearly: "Do you feel like you can stay safe, or are you thinking about hurting yourself?"
   - If they are in immediate danger, have a medical emergency, or cannot guarantee their own safety, call 911 or go to the nearest Emergency Room.
2. **Call or text 988 for immediate crisis counseling and navigation.** This service is available 24 / 7 and can help you determine if the level of distress requires an emergency mobile crisis team or if it can be managed with home-based stabilization.
3. **Bring another trusted adult to the location immediately.** Do not try to manage an acute grief crisis alone; you need someone to handle incoming logistics while you focus on the parent, or vice versa.
4. **Contact their Primary Care Physician (PCP) office and request an "urgent bereavement assessment."** State clearly that there has been a recent death and the surviving parent is not functioning or is experiencing acute physiological distress.
   - Ask for a same-day telehealth or in-person appointment.
   - Request a referral to a licensed clinical social worker (LCSW) or psychologist specializing in complicated grief.
5. **Call the hospice agency if the death occurred under their care.** Medicare-certified hospices are legally required to provide bereavement support to the family for at least 13 months following the death.
   - Ask for the "Bereavement Coordinator" or the on-call social worker.
   - Inquire about immediate crisis intervention or "grief first aid" visits.
6. **Create a "low-stimulation safety zone" for the next several hours.**
   - Provide small amounts of water and simple, bland foods like crackers.
   - Turn off the television and limit phone notifications.
   - Intercept all non-essential phone calls by saying: "We are focused on [Parent's Name] right now; I will update you when things are stable."
7. **Monitor for "red flag" physiological symptoms that require a medical ER visit.** If your parent experiences chest pain, severe shortness of breath, sudden confusion that prevents them from recognizing people, or an inability to stay upright, seek medical help immediately.
8. **Use the NAMI HelpLine for resource navigation during business hours.** If it is Monday through Friday, 10 a.m. – 10 p.m. ET, call 1-800-950-NAMI (6264) for help finding local support groups and long-term mental health resources.

## What can wait
- You do not need to notify every extended family member or acquaintance today.
- You do not need to decide on the permanent disposition of the deceased’s belongings.
- You do not need to determine if this grief is "normal" or "pathological" in the first few days.
- You do not need to resolve insurance claims or estate legalities immediately.

## Important reassurance
Acute grief can look like a total psychological and physical breakdown. It is common for a parent to experience shaking, memory gaps, intense rage, or periods of total numbness. These are natural responses to a massive nervous system shock. Your presence and your effort to connect them to professional support are the most important actions you can take.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only for the immediate hours following a death when a surviving parent is acutely overwhelmed.

## Important note
This guide provides general information and is not a substitute for professional medical, mental health, or legal advice. If there is an immediate threat to life or safety, always contact 911 or local emergency services.
