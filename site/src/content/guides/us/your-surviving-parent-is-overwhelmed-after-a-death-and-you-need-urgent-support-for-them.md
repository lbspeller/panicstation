# What to do if… your surviving parent is overwhelmed after a death and you need urgent support for them (USA)

## Short answer
Stay with your parent or get another safe adult with them, check for immediate danger, and use 988 or 911 if safety is uncertain. Then contact their doctor, hospice team, or a local crisis service for urgent guidance.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave them alone if they say they want to die, cannot stay safe, seem severely confused, may collapse, or you are afraid they might hurt themselves.
- Do not push decisions about the funeral, money, housing, belongings, or family conflict while they are in acute distress.
- Do not give alcohol, leftover sedatives, extra medication, or someone else’s medication to make them sleep.
- Do not change, stop, or restart their medication without a clinician’s guidance.
- Do not argue with the grief, demand closure, or try to prove that they are “overreacting.”
- Do not fill the home with visitors if that makes them more panicked; choose one or two calm people instead.

## What to do now
1. **Check immediate safety first.** Ask plainly: “Are you thinking about harming yourself, or do you feel unsafe right now?” If the answer is yes, they cannot answer clearly, there is a medical emergency, or you believe there is immediate danger, call 911 or go to an emergency department.
2. **Use 988 for urgent crisis support.** Call or text 988, or use 988 chat, if your parent is in emotional crisis, talking about suicide, cannot calm down enough to stay safe, or you need help deciding what level of support is needed. You can contact 988 about someone else.
3. **Get another safe adult physically present.** Call a trusted relative, friend, neighbor, clergy member, or family friend and say: “I need someone calm here with my parent now. They are overwhelmed after the death and I should not manage this alone.”
4. **Reduce immediate risks around them.** If you can do it calmly and safely, move medication bottles, weapons, car keys, alcohol, and sharp objects out of immediate reach. Do not wrestle, threaten, or escalate; if you cannot do this safely, step back and call 911.
5. **Call their doctor’s office or clinic.** Say: “There has been a recent death in the family. My parent is overwhelmed, not functioning well, and I am worried about safety.” Ask for urgent same-day guidance, the fastest safe appointment, and whether they should be assessed today.
6. **If hospice was involved, call the hospice agency.** Ask whether they can offer bereavement support, a nurse or social worker call-back, grief counseling information, or guidance about what to do tonight.
7. **Create a quiet safety bubble for the next several hours.** Keep phones charged, lower noise, pause repeated update calls, offer water and simple food, and say: “I can handle messages for now.”
8. **Use non-crisis navigation when the situation is calmer.** NAMI’s HelpLine can help with mental health resource navigation, but it is not a crisis line. Use 988 or 911 first if safety is urgent.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today about long-term counseling, medication changes, estate issues, selling property, clearing belongings, or family disagreements.
- You do not need to explain the situation to everyone. One trusted person can send simple updates.
- You do not need to decide whether this is “normal grief” right now. Focus on safety, basic needs, and getting support connected.

## Important reassurance
A surviving parent may panic, cry, rage, go numb, stop eating normally, sleep badly, repeat the same questions, or say frightening things after a death. Intense grief can happen after a major loss, but safety concerns still deserve urgent support. Your role today is not to fix the grief; it is to help them stay safe, less alone, and connected to real help.

## Scope note
This is first steps only for the first hours or days when a surviving parent is acutely overwhelmed after a death. Later decisions may need bereavement counseling, medical, mental health, legal, financial, or practical support.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. If there is immediate danger or a medical emergency, call 911. If you are unsure what level of help is needed, 988 can help you think through the next step.
