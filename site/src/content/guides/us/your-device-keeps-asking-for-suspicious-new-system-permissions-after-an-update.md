# What to do if… your device keeps asking for suspicious new system permissions after an update (USA)

## Short answer
Deny any unexpected permission prompt for now, then use your device’s built-in privacy settings to see which app is asking. If you suspect account takeover or fraud, secure your main email from a different trusted device before using the affected device for banking, email, or password-manager logins.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep tapping “Allow” just to clear the prompts.
- Don’t install a “permission fixer,” “cleaner,” or “security” app from an ad, pop-up, text, or email.
- Don’t enter passwords, payment details, banking details, or identity documents on the device until you have checked the prompts.
- Don’t follow links saying you must “verify” your Apple Account, Google Account, bank, or password manager because of a warning.
- Don’t factory reset in a rush unless you have first tried to identify the app, remove suspicious access, and protect key accounts.
- Don’t ignore a work or school device policy warning; the device owner may need to handle it.

## What to do now
1. **Pause risky use of the device.**  
   Until you have checked the prompts, avoid banking, payments, password-manager unlocks, email-password changes, and uploading photos of ID documents from this device.

2. **Deny the prompt unless you clearly expected it.**  
   If the app name is unfamiliar, the timing feels odd, or the permission does not fit what you were doing, choose **Don’t Allow**, **Deny**, or the closest available option.

3. **Write down or screenshot the app name and permission.**  
   Note what the prompt asks for, such as camera, microphone, location, contacts, photos, files, Bluetooth, or local network. This helps you avoid guessing later.

4. **Check iPhone or iPad app access in built-in settings.**  
   Go to **Settings → Privacy & Security**. Review sensitive categories such as Location Services, Contacts, Photos, Microphone, Camera, Bluetooth, Local Network, and Files and Folders. If **App Privacy Report** is available, review it for recent app activity.

5. **Check Android app access in built-in settings.**  
   Go to **Settings → Security & Privacy → Privacy → Permission manager**. On some devices the wording may be **Settings → Privacy → Permission manager**. Review camera, microphone, location, contacts, files, photos, and call-related permissions.

6. **Remove high-impact access from apps you do not trust.**  
   For any unfamiliar, unused, or unnecessary app, change sensitive permissions to **Don’t allow**, **Deny**, **Never**, or **Ask every time** where those options are available. For location, use **Only while using the app** only when the app genuinely needs location to work.

7. **Uninstall suspicious or unnecessary apps.**  
   Remove apps you do not recognise, apps installed outside the official app store, apps with lookalike names or icons, and apps that keep requesting sensitive access without a clear reason.

8. **Use built-in Android safety checks if this is an Android device.**  
   Open the Google Play Store, go to **Play Protect**, and use the available scan or check option. If Play Protect flags an app, follow the on-screen steps to remove, disable, or review it.

9. **Update only through official routes.**  
   Update apps through the Apple App Store or Google Play Store. Update the operating system through the device’s Settings app. Treat update links in pop-ups, texts, emails, or search ads as suspicious.

10. **Secure your main email if account takeover seems possible.**  
   From a different trusted device, change your main email password, turn on two-factor authentication if it is not already on, review recent activity or signed-in devices, and sign out sessions you do not recognise.

11. **Report cyber-enabled fraud if money, accounts, or identity information were affected.**  
   If you lost money, gave payment details, or believe a cyber-enabled crime or fraud occurred, report it through the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center using the official IC3 site.

12. **Escalate work or school devices.**  
   If the device is managed by an employer, school, or organisation, contact the IT or helpdesk team and say: “After an update, the device repeatedly requested new permissions. I denied unexpected prompts and need security guidance.”

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether this was malware, a bad app update, or a normal prompt shown at a bad time.
- You do not need to contact every account you have; focus first on your main email, password manager, bank, and any account showing suspicious activity.
- You do not need to install extra security tools to “clean” the device.
- You do not need to factory reset immediately if you can stop the prompts, remove suspicious apps, and secure key accounts.
- You do not need to explain everything perfectly before asking manufacturer support, your carrier, or workplace IT for help.

## Important reassurance
Repeated permission prompts can feel pushy and alarming, especially after an update. Denying unexpected prompts and reviewing access in settings is usually a safe, reversible way to slow things down and regain control.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. If prompts continue after you revoke access and remove suspicious apps, or if accounts or money may be affected, later decisions may need help from device support, workplace IT, your bank, or a trusted technical specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, cybersecurity, or professional advice. If you believe cybercrime or fraud occurred, keep basic details you already have, such as dates, app names, prompts, screenshots, account alerts, and transaction records, and use official support or reporting channels.
