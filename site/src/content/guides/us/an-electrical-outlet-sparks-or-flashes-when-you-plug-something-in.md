# What to do if… an electrical outlet sparks or flashes when you plug something in (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using that outlet. If there is smoke, flame, or immediate danger, leave the area and call 911 from a safe place.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep plugging and unplugging the device to test the outlet.
- Don’t touch the plug, outlet cover, or nearby wiring if you see active sparking, melting, scorch marks, or smoke, or if the outlet seems hot or appears overheated.
- Don’t repeatedly reset a breaker or GFCI that trips again.
- Don’t use water on an energized electrical fire.
- Don’t remove the outlet cover plate or attempt a repair.
- Don’t work around the problem with an extension cord, power strip, or adapter.
- Don’t use the same device in another outlet until it has been checked.

## What to do now
1. **Make a safer pause.** Step back. Keep children, pets, and other people away from the outlet and anything plugged into it.
2. **If there is smoke, flame, or immediate danger, leave.** Do not go closer to unplug the device or reach the outlet. Call 911 from a safe place.
3. **If there is ongoing sparking, heat, buzzing, crackling, a burning smell, melting, or visible damage, do not touch the plug or outlet.** If the breaker panel is safely accessible and you can identify the breaker for that circuit without trial and error, switch it off.
4. **If the spark has stopped and there are no warning signs, unplug only if it feels safe to approach.** Switch the device off first if it has a power switch. Keep your hands dry and pull the plug itself, not the cord.
5. **Leave the outlet unused.** Keep the breaker off if the outlet showed heat, odor, buzzing, crackling, repeated sparking, melting, or scorch marks.
6. **Stop using the device too.** Do not try it in another outlet until it has been checked.
7. **Contact a qualified electrician or building maintenance promptly.** Check local licensing requirements. Tell them what you saw, what was being plugged in, and whether a breaker or GFCI tripped.
8. **If you rent or live in a managed building, contact the landlord, property manager, or emergency maintenance line promptly.** Tell them the outlet sparked or flashed and whether you switched off the breaker.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause now.
- You do not need to decide whether the outlet, breaker, wiring, or device needs replacement.
- You do not need to test another outlet.
- You can deal with insurance questions, breaker-panel labeling, and any wider inspection after the immediate risk is controlled.

## Important reassurance
A spark does not automatically mean a fire has started, but it should not be ignored. Stepping back, stopping use, and arranging a qualified check are sensible first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about inspection and repair may need help from a qualified electrician, building maintenance, or another appropriate local specialist.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, electrical, or other professional advice. If there is smoke, flame, or immediate danger, prioritize getting everyone to safety and call 911 from a safe place.
