# What to do if… your child’s travel document details do not match their flight booking at check-in (USA)

## Short answer
Pause repeated check-in attempts and get a human airline agent to correct the reservation or ticket so your child’s passenger details match the travel document you will present. Say: **“This is the same child passenger. I need a typo or data correction, not a transfer to another person.”**

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep trying different spellings, dates, or document numbers; this can create confusion about which version is correct.
- Do not assume a small spelling or date difference will be accepted; airline systems, TSA Secure Flight data, and international document checks may still matter.
- Do not buy a new ticket unless the airline or ticketing seller tells you the existing ticket cannot be corrected or reissued in time.
- Do not rely on changing a frequent-flyer profile to fix today’s reservation; today’s booking or ticket must be corrected.
- Do not leave the airline desk or queue just because you are also calling or using chat support.

## What to do now
1. **Write one clean “correct details” note from your child’s document.**  
   Copy the name, date of birth, passport number, passport expiry, and nationality from the passport or travel document you are using. Copy hyphens, apostrophes, spacing, and middle names as shown, as far as the airline system allows.

2. **Go to the operating airline’s check-in desk or service desk.**  
   Say: “This is the same child passenger. The booking details do not match the passport or travel document. Please correct the passenger data so check-in and the boarding pass can be issued.”

3. **Ask who can make the correction right now.**  
   If it is a codeshare flight, ask whether the operating airline, marketing airline, or ticketing seller has control of the ticket. If you booked through a travel agent or third-party site, call them while staying in the airline line.

4. **Ask for the least disruptive fix first.**
   - Ask for a minor name correction or data correction for the same child passenger.
   - If the agent cannot directly edit it, ask whether the ticket can be revalidated or reissued with the corrected details.
   - Before agreeing to any payment, ask whether fees or fare differences apply and whether there is a later same-day flight option if the correction takes too long.

5. **Keep one source of truth while different agents are helping.**  
   Use the same written details for the desk agent, phone agent, chat agent, and seller. Do not approve two different corrections at the same time.

6. **For a U.S. domestic flight, separate the TSA ID issue from the airline booking issue.**  
   TSA generally does not require children under 18 to show ID for domestic travel, but the airline may still need the child’s booking details to be correct before it can issue a boarding pass. Ask the airline what exact field is blocking check-in: name, date of birth, passport details, Secure Flight data, or ticketing control.

7. **For an international flight, re-check the document fields after the fix.**  
   Ask the airline to confirm the passport number, expiry date, nationality, date of birth, and name in the document section. Airlines may need passenger and passport data for APIS, destination entry rules, and boarding checks.

8. **After the correction, try check-in again with the agent still available.**  
   Ask for a printed boarding pass or updated itinerary showing the corrected passenger name. If the system still rejects the child’s check-in, ask the agent to read back which field is failing so you do not guess.

## What can wait
- You do not need to write complaints, dispute charges, or start insurance claims before you know whether you can still travel.
- You do not need to fix every saved passenger profile today; focus on today’s reservation and ticket.
- You do not need to decide about regulators, refunds, or legal routes at the airport counter; first try to get a usable boarding pass or a clear same-day alternative.

## Important reassurance
This is a common travel-document problem, especially with children’s names, middle names, hyphens, double surnames, and booking-form character limits. A calm, same-passenger correction through the airline or ticketing seller is usually the fastest path.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the check-in moment only. If you miss the flight, lose money, or cannot get a clear answer, later decisions may need help from the airline, ticketing seller, travel insurer, or another specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, travel-agent, immigration, security, or professional advice. Airline rules, ticket-control rules, destination document rules, and boarding decisions can vary.
