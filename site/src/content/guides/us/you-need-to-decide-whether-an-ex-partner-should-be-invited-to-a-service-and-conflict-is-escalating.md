# What to do if… you need to decide whether an ex-partner should be invited to a service and conflict is escalating (USA)

## Short answer
Identify the one individual with the legal "right of disposition" and instruct the funeral director to enforce a single, safety-focused guest policy that prevents disruption to the service.

## Do not do these things
- Don't engage in group text threads or social media arguments regarding the guest list.
- Don't allow multiple people to provide conflicting instructions to the funeral home or venue.
- Don't promise to "handle" a disruptive person yourself without professional assistance.
- Don't use the invitation as a tool for negotiation or leverage in other family disputes.
- Don't assume that the closest biological relative automatically has the final legal word in all states.
- Don't ignore specific threats of violence or harassment in an attempt to maintain a peaceful appearance.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a separate space if a confrontation is physically occurring.**  
   If relatives or ex-partners are currently shouting or behaving aggressively, create physical distance between yourself and the conflict before making any calls or decisions.
2. **Ask the funeral director to identify the "right of disposition" holder.**  
   State laws provide a specific hierarchy for who has the legal authority to control funeral arrangements. Request that the funeral home confirm who this person is based on the deceased's paperwork (such as a designated agent form) or state-specific next-of-kin rules.
3. **Verify if a legally designated agent was appointed before death.**  
   Check for an "Appointment of Agent for Disposition of Remains" or similar state-recognized form. In most jurisdictions, a legally appointed agent takes precedence over all other relatives, including spouses or children.
4. **Designate a single spokesperson to communicate with the funeral home.**  
   To prevent conflicting orders, ensure that only the person with legal authority—or their one chosen representative—speaks with the funeral director about guest restrictions.
5. **Request specific disruption-prevention measures from the venue.**  
   Ask the funeral director or officiant what tools they provide to manage tension, such as:  
   - Requiring a private, invite-only service with no public notice.  
   - Providing staff to act as greeters who check names at the entrance.  
   - Setting staggered arrival times to ensure the ex-partner and grieving family do not meet.  
   - Reserving seating in separate sections of the room.
6. **Select the lowest-risk attendance option.**  
   - **Private service:** Limit attendance to a specific list and do not publish the time or location publicly.  
   - **Public service with boundaries:** If the service must be public, ask the funeral home for a staff member to monitor for disruptive behavior.  
   - **Alternative viewing:** Offer the ex-partner a private time to pay respects before or after the main service.
7. **Notify the ex-partner of the decision through a neutral channel.**  
   If a decision is made to exclude them or set specific rules, communicate this once via email or through the funeral director. Do not engage in a back-and-forth debate.
8. **Contact local law enforcement if immediate threats are made.**  
   If someone threatens to "crash" the service or cause physical harm, call 911 for immediate danger or the local police non-emergency line to request a "keep the peace" presence or advice on off-duty security.
9. **Document all hostile communication.**  
   Save any texts, emails, or voicemails that indicate a risk of disruption. Share these only with the person holding legal authority and the funeral home staff.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve the history of the relationship or the reasons for the divorce now.
- You do not need to settle estate, inheritance, or property disputes before the funeral service.
- You do not need to achieve "closure" or forgiveness between all parties during the bereavement period.

## Important reassurance
Conflict during funeral planning is a frequent occurrence because grief can amplify existing family tensions. Choosing a logistics-first approach to guest management is a standard way to protect the dignity of the service and the safety of all mourners.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only for stabilizing immediate conflict and preventing disruption around a service. Long-term family mediation or legal disputes over remains may require specialist professional assistance.

## Important note
This guide provides general information regarding funeral industry practices and common legal hierarchies in the USA; it is not legal advice. The "right of disposition" varies significantly by state law. If there is a dispute over legal authority that the funeral home cannot resolve, you may need to consult a probate attorney. Always prioritize physical safety if threats are present.
