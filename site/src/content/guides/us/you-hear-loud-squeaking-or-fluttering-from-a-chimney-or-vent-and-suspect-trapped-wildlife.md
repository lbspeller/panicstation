# What to do if… you hear loud squeaking or fluttering from a chimney or vent and suspect trapped wildlife (USA)

## Short answer
Deactivate any appliances connected to the vent or fireplace immediately and contact a licensed wildlife control operator or chimney professional to safely remove the animal and prevent re-entry.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t light a fire or run gas appliances, furnaces, or water heaters that vent through the affected area.
- Don’t attempt to "smoke out" the animal; this causes panic, creates a fire hazard from nesting materials, and may be illegal.
- Don’t block or seal the exterior exit of the vent or chimney while an animal is inside.
- Don’t reach into the flue or fireplace with bare hands or attempt to "poke" the animal out with tools.
- Don’t use poisons, glue traps, or chemical repellents inside the vent system.
- Don’t open the damper if you hear scratching or fluttering directly above it without first sealing the fireplace opening with heavy plastic and tape.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the vent system and check for carbon monoxide risks.**
   - **Turn off any furnaces, boilers, water heaters, or gas fireplaces** that use the affected chimney or vent to prevent exhaust backup.
   - **If anyone feels dizzy, nauseous, or has a sudden headache**, evacuate the building immediately and call 911 from outside.
2. **Contain the area within the home.**
   - **Keep pets and children away** from the fireplace, vent grilles, or wall sections where sounds are loudest.
   - **Close interior doors** leading to the room with the fireplace or vent to prevent a loose animal from accessing the rest of the house.
3. **Handle potential bat sightings as a medical priority.**
   - **Do not touch or handle a bat** under any circumstances, even with gloves.
   - **If a bat is in a room where someone was sleeping**, contact your local health department or animal control immediately to arrange for testing; do not release the bat until instructed.
4. **Identify the sound type and timing for the professional.**
   - **Note if the sounds occur during the day** (likely squirrels or birds) or at night (likely raccoons, bats, or mice).
   - **Differentiate between frantic scratching** (trapped animal in a smooth liner) and chirping / rhythmic fluttering (potential nesting birds).
5. **Contact a licensed professional for removal.**
   - **Call a nuisance wildlife control operator (WCO)** for mammals like raccoons or squirrels; they have the specialized equipment for extraction.
   - **Call a chimney sweep** if you suspect a blockage or debris but cannot confirm if an animal is still alive.
6. **Protect yourself if you must look into the fireplace.**
   - **Wear an N95 mask and safety glasses** before opening any doors or looking up the flue to avoid falling soot, droppings, or nesting debris.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact species before calling for help.
- You do not need to climb onto the roof to inspect the chimney yourself.
- You do not need to decide on permanent chimney caps or vent screens until the animal and any potential offspring are confirmed gone.
- You can wait to clean the area until a professional confirms it is safe and provides guidance on sanitizing zoonotic waste (e.g., raccoon latrines or bird droppings).

## Important reassurance
Wildlife frequently mistakes chimneys and vents for natural nesting cavities. While the sounds can be loud and alarming, most situations are non-emergencies if the house systems are turned off and the animal is contained. Professionals deal with these extractions daily and can resolve the issue without harm to your property or the animal.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. Federal and state laws, such as the Migratory Bird Treaty Act, protect certain species (like Chimney Swifts), meaning active nests with eggs or young cannot legally be moved until the birds migrate.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not replace the advice of public health officials or licensed contractors. If there is a risk of rabies exposure (bat contact) or carbon monoxide poisoning, seek medical or emergency services immediately.
