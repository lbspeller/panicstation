# What to do if… people start arriving at your home after a death and you need boundaries and support immediately (USA)

## Short answer
You have the legal right to refuse entry to your home; designate one trusted "gatekeeper" to manage the door and phone while you isolate in a private room. Use a single, repeated script: "We are not accepting visitors today; please leave a message."

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel obligated to open the door because of the visitor's relationship to the deceased.
- Do not attempt to host, provide refreshments, or explain your need for privacy.
- Do not hand over house keys, digital passwords, or the deceased’s personal effects to "helpful" relatives.
- Do not engage in arguments or justifications regarding your boundaries.
- Do not make any financial or funeral-related commitments under pressure from guests.
- Do not allow anyone to begin cleaning or removing items from the home.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the physical perimeter immediately.** **Lock all external doors and close all curtains or blinds to reduce visibility from the street.**
   - Put your phone on "Do Not Disturb" or "Silent" mode.
   - Move to a room with a lock, away from the front door.
2. **Designate a single gatekeeper.** **Identify one person (friend, neighbor, or calm relative) who is not currently at the house and ask them to intervene.**
   - Call or text: "I am overwhelmed and need you to handle the door and messages. Please come now or manage the group chat for me."
   - If they are on-site, ask them to stand by the door and prevent entry.
3. **Deploy a consistent boundary script.** **Use the same sentence for every person who knocks or calls.**
   - Script: "Thank you for your concern. We are not having visitors today. We will reach out when we are ready."
   - Repeat this exactly if they push back. Do not offer a "why."
4. **Post a physical notice on the door.** **Tape a clear, handwritten note to the front door at eye level.**
   - Text: "PRIVATE TIME. NO VISITORS. Please do not knock or ring the bell. Leave a message on my phone."
5. **Protect sensitive documents and items.** **Gather the deceased’s wallet, phone, laptop, and any medical/legal paperwork into a single bag and keep it with you in your private room.**
   - If people are already inside, explicitly state: "Please do not move or sort any belongings today."
6. **Limit communication to one channel.** **Instruct your gatekeeper to send one broadcast message to the family or social group.**
   - "The family needs immediate privacy and will not be answering the door or phone today. Updates will follow tomorrow. Please respect this space."
7. **Contact non-emergency services for crowd management.** **If people refuse to leave your property, dial 311 (in most cities) or your local police non-emergency number to request a "keep the peace" presence.**
   - If you feel physically threatened or someone is trying to force entry, call **911**.
8. **Access emotional stabilization support.** **Call or text 988 to speak with a crisis counselor if the visitor pressure is causing a mental health emergency.**
   - 988 is available 24/7 for bereavement-related overwhelm and uses georouting to connect you to local support.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a funeral home or service details in the next few hours.
- You do not need to notify every extended family member or acquaintance today.
- You do not need to address "who gets what" or discuss the will.
- You do not need to provide an obituary or photo for the news/social media immediately.

## Important reassurance
The feeling of being "invaded" by mourners is a common response to shock and grief. Protecting your physical space is a necessary step for your own stabilization and is not a sign of coldness or lack of love for the deceased. You are allowed to be "unavailable" while you process the immediate impact of the loss.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization to secure your home and mental space immediately following a death. It does not cover long-term estate law or funeral arrangements.

## Important note
This information does not constitute legal or medical advice. As the legal resident, you generally have the right to exclude anyone from your property. If a situation escalates to harassment or trespassing, contact local law enforcement.
