# What to do if… you find a new “read me” or ransom-style note file on your computer (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately disconnect the device from all networks and power it down if you see files actively changing or renaming.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay the ransom or communicate with the attackers.
- Don’t click any links or download "decryptors" provided in the ransom note.
- Don’t plug in backup drives, USB sticks, or external hardware to the affected computer.
- Don’t attempt to restore your data from backups until the device is professionally cleaned.
- Don’t sign into banking, email, or sensitive accounts using the compromised device.
- Don’t delete the ransom note or the encrypted files yet, as they are evidence.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the computer from all network connections immediately.** 
   Turn off the Wi-Fi using a physical switch or software setting and unplug any ethernet cables.
2. **Power down the device if you see file names changing or a progress bar.**
   If the encryption process is still active, a forced shutdown can preserve the current state of the memory (RAM) for forensic experts and stop further data loss.
3. **Disconnect all peripheral storage and shared network drives.**
   Unplug external hard drives and disconnect from any home servers (NAS) or shared cloud folders to prevent the malware from spreading laterally.
4. **Document the incident using a separate, clean device.**
   Take a clear photo of the ransom note on the screen, the specific file extensions (e.g., .locked, .crypted), and any contact instructions or cryptocurrency addresses provided.
5. **Change your primary account passwords from a different, secure device.**
   Update your passwords for email, banking, and password managers immediately.
   - Choose "Log out of all sessions" or "Revoke all sessions" in your account security settings.
   - Ensure Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is active on all accounts.
6. **Report the crime to the FBI through the Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3).**
   Submit a report at ic3.gov. A single report here notifies relevant federal agencies, including CISA and the U.S. Secret Service.
7. **Notify your employer or school if the device was used for work.**
   Stop all activity on the device and follow your organization’s specific IT security incident protocols.
8. **Contact your financial institutions if you used the device for banking.**
   Alert your bank or credit card issuer that your credentials may have been compromised and request enhanced monitoring on your accounts.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide whether to pay the ransom right now; focus entirely on containment and security.
- You don’t need to wipe the hard drive or reinstall the operating system today.
- You don’t need to notify your entire contact list until you have confirmed whether your email or social media accounts were accessed.

## Important reassurance
Finding a ransom note is a high-stress event designed to trigger a panicked response. Disconnecting the device and moving to a clean machine is a standard, effective procedure that regains control of the situation while you determine the next steps.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. Professional malware removal, forensic analysis, and data recovery are separate processes that typically require specialist assistance.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional cybersecurity, legal, or financial advice. Ransomware often involves data theft (exfiltration); if sensitive personal or corporate data is involved, you may have legal reporting obligations and should consult with a qualified professional.
