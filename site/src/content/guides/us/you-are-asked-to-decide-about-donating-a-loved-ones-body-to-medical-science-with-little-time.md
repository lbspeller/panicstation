# What to do if… you are asked to decide about donating a loved one’s body to medical science with little time (USA)

## Short answer
Ask for a brief pause before authorizing anything. Confirm whether the request concerns organ, eye, and tissue donation or whole-body donation, and ask for the program name, the real deadline, and written terms.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree before confirming which donation pathway is being discussed.
- Do not sign until you understand the scope of the donation, possible transfers or distribution, costs, and final disposition.
- Do not assume that a driver’s license donor designation is the same as whole-body donation paperwork.
- Do not pay under pressure. Ask for written, itemized information.
- Do not let a caller rush you past questions about your loved one’s known wishes or who may authorize the decision.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for a brief pause and write down the basics.** Get the caller’s name, organization, callback number, program name, written information, and the deadline for a decision.
2. **Confirm which pathway is being discussed.**
   - For **organ, eye, and tissue donation**, ask to speak with the donation team and ask them to check the applicable registry and any driver’s license or state ID donor designation.
   - For **whole-body donation**, look for separate program paperwork, a donor card, or a letter from a university, medical school, or donation organization.
3. **Check your loved one’s known wishes.** Share any paperwork or donor card with the relevant coordinator. If there is no clear record, ask: “Who may authorize this decision under the law and policy that apply here, and is anyone else’s agreement needed?”
4. **For organ, eye, and tissue donation, ask the donation coordinator what is being requested.** Ask which decisions are needed now and how much time is available.
5. **For whole-body donation, contact the program directly.** Ask whether it can accept the donation now and what alternative arrangements are needed if it declines.
6. **Before authorizing whole-body donation, ask these questions and write down the answers.**
   - “How may the body or donated material be used?”
   - “Could any donated material be transferred or distributed to another organization?”
   - “Which transport, storage, cremation, or other costs are covered, and which costs may fall to the family?”
   - “How long may the donation last, and what final disposition will occur?”
   - “Can cremated remains be returned, and how will the family be notified?”
7. **If the whole-body donation organization is not a university or medical-school program, do a quick credibility check.** Ask for written terms. Ask whether it is accredited by the Association for Advancing Tissue and Biologics. This accreditation is voluntary and is one useful check for non-transplant anatomical donation organizations.
8. **If a funeral home is involved, reduce pressure around costs.** Ask for price information by phone. If you discuss arrangements or prices in person, ask for the General Price List to keep. After selecting any services, ask for the itemized statement before paying.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle whether donation feels morally right before checking known wishes and written terms.
- You do not need to finalize a memorial, obituary, or service before confirming whether the donation has been accepted.
- You do not need to make detailed funeral purchases while a whole-body donation program is still deciding whether it can accept the donation.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to ask for a brief pause, written details, and a fallback plan. A whole-body donation program may still decline a donation, so asking about alternative arrangements is a normal practical step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. State law, hospital procedures, and donation-program rules differ, so later decisions may need help from the relevant donation coordinator, hospital bereavement team, funeral home, or a qualified professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Whole-body donation acceptance is not guaranteed, and program requirements and timelines vary.
