# What to do if… your employer says you must repay an overpayment immediately (USA)

## Short answer
Do not hand over money immediately. Ask for the overpayment claim in writing, ask them to pause or limit any deduction while you check it, and only discuss repayment terms for an amount you can verify.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay by cash, gift card, wire transfer, cryptocurrency, or a personal payment app because someone says it is urgent.
- Do not sign a wage-deduction authorization you do not understand, especially one that is open-ended.
- Do not agree to fees, interest, or administrative charges without a written explanation and confirmation that they are allowed under the rules that apply to your wages.
- Do not ignore the message; a short written reply helps protect your record.
- Do not assume your employer can deduct any amount they want. Wage-deduction rules are often state-specific.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for a written, itemized overpayment notice today.** Ask payroll or HR to state:
   - the pay periods involved
   - what caused the overpayment, such as hours, rate, leave, commission, bonus, or payroll coding
   - the calculation
   - whether the amount is gross or net
   - how they want to recover it
   - whether they are claiming only the overpaid principal
   - who your payroll contact is

2. **Save and gather the records that show what happened.** Keep the notice, emails, texts, pay stubs, time records, schedules, offer letter, pay-change notices, commission or bonus documents, and any leave records for the pay periods they named.

3. **Reply in writing before paying.** Say that you will review the calculation and will repay any correct amount, but you need the itemized details first. Ask them to pause recovery, or at least cap any deduction, while you check the figures.

4. **Check the amount against your own records.** Compare the employer’s calculation with your pay stubs, hours, rate, taxes withheld, benefit deductions, and any corrected pay records. If something does not match, send the specific line item you dispute and ask payroll to correct or explain it.

5. **If they want payroll deductions, ask for clear limits in writing.** Ask for the deduction start date, amount per paycheck, end date, total amount, and what happens if you leave the job before repayment is complete. Ask which state’s wage-deduction rules they are applying, usually the state where you work, and ask them to confirm the deduction follows those rules.

6. **If the employer will not give details or the deduction seems wrong, contact a wage agency.** Your state labor office can usually tell you what your state requires for overpayment recovery, payroll deductions, written authorization, and final-paycheck deductions. The U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division may be relevant if there are federal wage issues, such as unpaid minimum wage, unpaid overtime, or retaliation for asking about wages.

7. **Handle tax records carefully if the repayment affects a prior year or a W-2.** Ask payroll whether they will issue a corrected W-2, W-2c, or other corrected wage or tax record, and how they will record the repayment. Keep the repayment agreement, proof of each repayment, and any corrected forms.

## What can wait
- You do not need to agree today to a lump-sum repayment before you can verify the amount.
- You do not need to sign a deduction authorization during the first conversation.
- You do not need to decide whether to quit, threaten legal action, or make a long-term career decision right now.
- You do not need to solve the tax paperwork in the first exchange; first get the payroll calculation and repayment method in writing.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to slow this down. Payroll mistakes can usually be corrected, but you are allowed to ask for written details and a repayment method that does not create a new crisis.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you document the claim, verify the amount, and avoid a rushed repayment agreement. If the amount is large, deductions have already started, your final paycheck is involved, or you are being threatened or retaliated against, get state-specific help quickly.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, tax, financial, or professional advice. U.S. wage-deduction rules vary by state, and your options may depend on how the overpayment happened and how your employer is trying to recover it.
