# What to do if… you suspect your pipes are starting to freeze and the taps are slowing or sputtering (USA)

## Short answer
Shut off your home’s main water valve immediately to prevent flooding if a pipe has already split, open the affected faucet to relieve pressure, and apply gentle heat to the frozen section using a hair dryer or heating pad.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use a blowtorch, propane heater, charcoal stove, or any open flame (this is a fire hazard and can cause the pipe to explode).
- Don’t use a high-heat heat gun or boiling water, as extreme temperature shifts can cause the pipe to rupture.
- Don’t leave any electric heating device (hair dryer, space heater, heating pad) running unattended.
- Don’t stand in water while using electrical appliances to thaw pipes.
- Don’t turn the water back on at full pressure once thawed; restore it slowly while inspecting the entire line for leaks.

## What to do now
1. **Shut off the main water supply valve immediately.**  
   - This prevents a major flood if the pipe has already split (cracks often remain hidden by ice until the thaw begins).
   - The valve is typically located where the water line enters the house, in a basement, crawlspace, or utility closet near the water heater.
   - If the indoor valve is stuck or missing, look for the "curb box" (a metal or concrete lid labeled "WATER") at the property line near the street.
2. **Open the faucet served by the restricted pipe.**  
   - Opening the tap relieves the internal pressure that causes pipes to burst.
   - Leave it in the "on" position even if no water is flowing.
3. **Identify the frozen section of the pipe.**  
   - Look for pipes located along exterior walls or in unheated areas like attics, garages, or crawlspaces.
   - Feel the pipes for sections that are significantly colder than others, or look for visible frost or slight bulging on the pipe surface.
4. **Check all other taps in the house.**  
   - If one pipe has frozen, others are likely at the same risk. 
   - Ensure all other faucets are functioning; if they are not, they also require stabilization.
5. **Apply gentle heat to the frozen area.**  
   - Use an electric hair dryer on a low or medium setting, moving it back and forth along the pipe.
   - Alternatively, wrap the pipe in an electric heating pad or towels soaked in hot water.
   - Start heating from the end closest to the faucet and work your way toward the frozen blockage.
6. **Improve ambient heat circulation.**  
   - Open cabinet doors under sinks to let warm room air reach the plumbing.
   - Increase the home's thermostat temperature to at least 65°F (18°C) temporarily.
   - Keep interior doors open to allow heat to move into colder rooms.
7. **Monitor for leaks as the pipe thaws.**  
   - Watch the area closely for any dripping or spraying as the ice melts.
   - If you see a leak, ensure the main water remains off and call a plumber.
8. **Restore water flow slowly.**  
   - Once water begins to flow normally from the open tap, turn the main water valve back on only part-way.
   - Inspect the entire length of the previously frozen pipe for several minutes before fully restoring pressure.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on permanent insulation upgrades or pipe relocation today.
- You do not need to cut into drywall or ceilings to inspect pipes that are not showing signs of moisture or leaks.
- You can delay contacting your insurance company until the immediate risk of water damage is contained and you have identified if a break occurred.

## Important reassurance
Sputtering or slowing water is a clear signal that allows you to take action before a full blockage or a burst occurs. Following these steps for pressure relief and controlled thawing is the standard procedure for stabilizing residential plumbing during a cold snap.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. If the frozen section is inaccessible (inside a wall or underground), or if you discover a split in the pipe, you must contact a licensed plumber.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace professional plumbing or safety advice. Local building codes and plumbing configurations vary. If you are unsure of your ability to safely locate or thaw pipes, or if water is near electrical systems, contact a professional immediately.
