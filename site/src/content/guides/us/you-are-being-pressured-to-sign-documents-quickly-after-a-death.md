# What to do if… you are being pressured to sign documents quickly after a death (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign under pressure. Get the complete document set, step away from the conversation, and have someone independent review it before you agree to anything.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign on the spot because someone says it is standard, urgent, or only a formality.
- Don’t initial pages, click an e-sign link, or approve a payment before you have all pages and attachments.
- Don’t give anyone one-time passcodes, bank login access, card details, or account recovery codes.
- Don’t send money to new wire, Zelle, Venmo, Cash App, or payment details that arrived by text or email without verifying through a number you independently look up.
- Don’t hand over original identity documents, the only original death certificate, or the only copy of estate papers to someone you do not fully trust.
- Don’t let the person pressuring you control who you speak to or stop you from getting independent advice.

## What to do now
1. **Set one clear boundary.** Say or write: “I am not signing anything today. Send me the full documents and I will respond in writing.” Then stop debating the deadline.

2. **Ask for the full document set.** Request:
   - every page, attachment, addendum, fee schedule, and payment authorization
   - the full legal name of the person or business asking you to sign
   - the reason they say it must be signed now
   - what they claim will happen if you do not sign today, in writing

3. **Name the type of document before discussing details.** Treat it as high-risk if it involves:
   - funeral home contracts or payment authorizations
   - assignments of benefits, releases, waivers, or indemnities
   - estate, probate, inheritance, or family settlement paperwork
   - disclaimers, renunciations, executor documents, or personal representative documents
   - bank, insurance, pension, or beneficiary claim paperwork

4. **If this is funeral-related, slow the purchase down.** Under the FTC Funeral Rule, funeral homes generally must let you choose only the goods and services you want, give price information by phone if you ask, and give written itemized pricing when you visit. Ask for the General Price List and itemized costs before agreeing to a package, payment plan, or authorization.

5. **Move contact to a channel you control.** Ask for an email from an official business address. Independently look up phone numbers and websites instead of using links or numbers sent by the person pressuring you.

6. **Get independent review.** If the document could affect inheritance, property, debt, liability, or control of an estate, contact your own probate or estate attorney, not one chosen by the person pressuring you. If you do not have one, use a state or local bar lawyer referral service, legal aid, or court self-help resources and ask for a quick document review.

7. **If money has been requested or sent, contact your bank or card issuer now.** Ask what fraud-report, stop-payment, ACH-revocation, charge dispute, or account-protection options are available. Act quickly, because payment protections and reversal options can depend on the payment type and timing.

8. **Treat power-of-attorney pressure as a red flag.** A power of attorney usually ends when the person who granted it dies. After death, authority usually depends on a court-appointed personal representative, executor, trustee, or another state-law process; it is not a reason for someone else to rush your signature.

9. **Use official reporting routes if you suspect fraud.** Report scams or fraud to the FTC. Use USA.gov to find the right agency for a specific scam. If the issue involves a bank, card, loan, payment app, or other consumer financial product, consider a CFPB complaint. If it happened online or through electronic communication, you can also report it to the FBI’s IC3.

10. **Keep a simple incident log.** Save the date, time, names, contact details, documents, payment requests, and exact pressure phrases used. Keep texts, emails, voicemails, screenshots, envelopes, and receipts.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today who is in charge of the estate.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements before pausing the signature.
- You do not need to answer repeated calls or texts after one clear written response.
- You do not need to choose a final legal strategy, complaint route, refund request, or lawsuit now.
- You can sort out longer-term estate, payment, and complaint questions after the documents have been reviewed.

## Important reassurance
Grief can make urgency feel harder to resist. Pausing a signature, asking for itemized paperwork, and getting independent review is a normal protective step, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about probate, inheritance, contracts, payment disputes, or liability may need help from a qualified attorney or other specialist in your state.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or someone is threatening you, move to safety and call 911.
