# What to do if… your key fob stops working and you cannot unlock or start your vehicle (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a safer pause first, then try the hidden physical key and your vehicle’s backup start method. If that does not work, call any roadside benefit you know you have through AAA, your insurer, a credit card, a new-car warranty, or the vehicle manufacturer, and ask for lockout help, a jump-start, or a tow.

## Do not do these things
- Do not stay in an unsafe place to troubleshoot. Move to a safer, public, well-lit place if you can. If you need immediate police, fire, or medical help, call 911.
- Do not keep pressing the fob after a few failed tries. Stop and work through the manual-key and backup-start steps instead.
- Do not force the door handle, window, or trim with improvised tools.
- Do not pry a hidden keyhole cover unless you can do it gently with the emergency key or the method in the owner’s manual.
- Do not attempt “slim jim” entry unless you are trained; modern doors may contain wiring, sensors, and side-impact components.
- Do not accept a roadside or locksmith dispatch without asking for the service-call fee, labor charge, and likely total price.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** Stand away from traffic, avoid staying alone in an isolated spot if there is a safer public place nearby, and keep your phone charged enough to call for help.

2. **Try one simple reset.**
   - Move the fob away from other electronics, a large key ring, heavy bag hardware, or metal objects.
   - Try the spare fob if you have it with you.
   - Try the door button, remote unlock, and driver-door handle once each, then stop.

3. **Find the physical emergency key.**
   - Most key fobs have a small release tab or button that lets a metal key slide out.
   - Look for a key cylinder on the driver door. It may be visible, or it may sit behind a small cover near the handle.
   - Use gentle pressure only. If the cover does not move easily, check the owner’s manual or call roadside assistance.

4. **Unlock the driver door with the physical key if you can.**
   - Turn the key slowly; the alarm may sound when the door opens.
   - If the alarm sounds, get in, keep the fob with you, and move to the backup start step.

5. **Use the backup start method if the vehicle unlocks but will not start.**
   - Many push-button vehicles can read a weak fob if you hold it against the start button or another marked backup reader area while pressing the brake and start button.
   - Some vehicles require the fob to be placed in a specific cupholder, console pocket, tray, slot, or steering-column area.
   - Check the owner’s manual, vehicle app, or manufacturer website for your exact make, model, and year.

6. **Check whether the vehicle itself has power.**
   - If there are no dome lights, no dashboard lights, and no response from the start button, the problem may be a weak or dead 12V battery rather than the fob.
   - Tell roadside assistance that the vehicle has no dash power and may need a jump-start or tow.

7. **Call for the right help.**
   - If you have roadside assistance through AAA, your insurer, a credit card, a new-car warranty, or the vehicle manufacturer, request lockout service and say whether the vehicle has electrical power.
   - If the vehicle cannot be unlocked or started safely, ask whether they can send a lockout technician, jump-start service, or tow truck.
   - If you are somewhere unsafe and need immediate emergency help, call 911.

8. **If you need a locksmith, slow the decision down.**
   - Ask for the business name, local address, service-call fee, labor charge, and likely total price before dispatch.
   - Ask whether they can work with your vehicle’s make, model, year, and smart-key system.
   - Be ready to show proof of ownership or lawful possession of the vehicle.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether the fob needs reprogramming or replacement.
- You do not need to diagnose the whole electrical system in the parking lot.
- You do not need to buy a new fob tonight unless a qualified roadside provider, dealer, or automotive locksmith says it is necessary to get you moving.
- Replacing the coin-cell battery, checking for water damage, finding the hidden keyhole in daylight, and making a spare-key plan can wait until you are somewhere safe.

## Important reassurance
A dead fob battery, weak vehicle battery, or missed backup-start location can feel like the whole car has failed, but there is usually a built-in fallback. The aim is to get safe, get access without damage, and bring in roadside help if the manual steps do not work.

## Scope note
These are first steps for immediate access and starting only. Later decisions about batteries, programming, replacement fobs, towing, dealership help, or locksmith work may need vehicle-specific help.

## Important note
This is general information, not vehicle-specific mechanical, legal, insurance, roadside, or professional advice. Procedures vary by make, model, year, and location; if a step requires force or uncertain disassembly, stop and use roadside assistance or a reputable automotive locksmith.
