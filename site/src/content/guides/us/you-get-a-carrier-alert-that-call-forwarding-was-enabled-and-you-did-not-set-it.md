# What to do if… you get a carrier alert that call forwarding was enabled and you did not set it (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as an active attempt to hijack your phone number and intercept two-factor authentication codes. Immediately dial ##002# (or *73 for Verizon) to clear all forwarding rules, then contact your wireless carrier's fraud department from a different, trusted device.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the alert, even if you still have cellular service and can make calls.
- Do not dial any codes or "repair numbers" provided by an unexpected caller or text following the alert.
- Do not click links within the alert SMS; use the official carrier app or a verified website instead.
- Do not power off your phone, as you may need it to receive legitimate security notifications or verification codes while you are securing the account.
- Do not share your account PIN, porting PIN, or one-time passcodes with anyone claiming to be from the carrier's "support team" who contacted you first.

## What to do now
1. **Dial the universal deactivation code to clear all forwarding rules immediately.**  
   Open your phone's dialer and enter **##002#** then press Call; this works for most GSM carriers (AT&T, T-Mobile, and most MVNOs). If you are on **Verizon**, dial ***73** then press Call. You should receive a network confirmation that all diverts or forwarding rules have been disabled.
2. **Contact your wireless carrier’s fraud or security department from a separate, trusted line.**  
   Do not rely on the compromised phone to call out if the line behaves strangely; use a landline or a different device to reach the carrier's official support number. Explicitly state that you received an unauthorized call-forwarding alert and suspect an **account takeover or SIM swap attempt**.
3. **Confirm the current status of your SIM card and any pending port-out requests.**  
   Ask the representative to verify if a new SIM (physical or eSIM) was activated, if a device change occurred, or if there is an active request to move your number to a different provider. If any of these are true, tell the carrier to **freeze the account and cancel all pending changes**.
4. **Enable a "Number Lock" or "Account Freeze" on your wireless line.**  
   Per FCC regulations, carriers must provide account locking features to prevent unauthorized transfers. Ask for **Verizon Number Lock**, **AT&T Account Lock**, or **T-Mobile SIM Protection**. Ensure these are active and require a secondary PIN or in-person verification to remove.
5. **Reset your carrier account PIN and update your security questions.**  
   Change your account passcode to a new, unique number that you have never used before. Avoid using easily guessed numbers like birthdays, addresses, or the last four digits of your Social Security Number.
6. **Change your voicemail PIN and disable remote access without a PIN.**  
   Attackers often use compromised voicemail to reset passwords for other services. Ensure your voicemail requires a PIN even when calling from your own number, and verify that no unauthorized "forward to voicemail" rules are active.
7. **Secure your primary email and financial accounts immediately.**  
   Check your email "Sent" folder and "Forwarding Rules" for any activity you did not authorize. Change the password for your email and your primary bank. If possible, switch your two-factor authentication (2FA) from SMS-based codes to an **authenticator app** or a physical security key.
8. **Test your inbound service using a different device.**  
   Have someone call your number or send you a text to confirm that the messages and calls are arriving on your physical device and are no longer being redirected.
9. **Report the incident at IdentityTheft.gov to create an official recovery plan.**  
   This provides you with a government-verified record of the event, which is necessary if the attacker successfully accessed your financial accounts or if you need to dispute fraudulent charges.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the person who initiated the forwarding right now.
- You do not need to replace your physical device or SIM card unless the carrier confirms the SIM itself was deactivated.
- You do not need to file a police report immediately unless you have confirmed financial losses or someone is actively impersonating you.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an alert means your carrier's security system caught the change. By acting within the first few minutes to clear the forwarding rules and lock the account, you can often stop the attacker before they are able to reset your bank passwords or drain your accounts.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for unauthorized call forwarding. It does not cover full identity recovery or the technical cleaning of a compromised device.

## Important note
This information is for situational stabilization and does not constitute legal or technical advice. Federal law requires wireless carriers to protect your account information (CPNI); if your carrier fails to assist you or refuses to secure your line, you can file a formal complaint with the FCC.
