# What to do if… you smell burning from your vehicle and have pulled over safely (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as a possible vehicle fire: turn the engine off, get everyone out, move at least 100 feet away and away from traffic, and call 911 if there is smoke, flame, a strong burning smell, or any sign the situation is getting worse.

## Do not do these things
- Do not restart the vehicle to test it or move it a little if the smell is strong, worsening, or joined by smoke.
- Do not open the hood or trunk if you suspect fire, smoke, or heat from that area.
- Do not reach into the engine bay, wheel area, or under the vehicle at the roadside.
- Do not try improvised ways to put out a possible engine or electrical fire.
- Do not stand in front of the vehicle or in a traffic lane while checking what is happening.
- Do not go back for phones, bags, or other items if there is smoke, flame, crackling, popping, or spreading heat.
- Do not let anyone smoke near the vehicle.

## What to do now
1. Put the hazard lights on if you can do that without delaying exit.
2. Turn the engine off, take the keys with you, and set the parking brake if it is safe to do so.
3. Get everyone out of the vehicle on the side away from traffic if possible.
4. Move everyone at least 100 feet from the vehicle and well away from traffic. If you are on a high-speed road and can safely get behind a barrier, do that.
5. From a safe distance, look for smoke, flame, hissing, sizzling, crackling, popping, heat shimmer from the hood or wheel area, or a smell that is getting stronger.
6. Call 911 if there is any smoke or flame, the smell is strong or getting worse, anyone feels unsafe, or the vehicle is stopped in a dangerous place. Give the route number, nearest exit or intersection, mile marker if visible, direction of travel, and whether you are on a shoulder, curve, or low-visibility area.
7. Do not open the hood or trunk while waiting. Keep people back and let responders handle the vehicle.
8. If there is no smoke or flame and the smell fades, still do not drive on as a test. Arrange roadside assistance, a tow, or advice from a qualified mechanic before the vehicle is driven again.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the cause of the smell right now.
- You do not need to decide on repairs, insurance, recalls, or complaints while the vehicle may still be unsafe.
- You do not need to look under the hood or take photos close to the vehicle.
- You do not need to try one more short drive to see whether the smell comes back.

## Important reassurance
A burning smell does not always mean the vehicle is on fire, but stepping away early is the safest choice. You are not overreacting by creating distance, keeping people out, and asking for help before deciding what happens next.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate roadside pause only. Later decisions about towing, repair, recalls, insurance, or replacement may need help from a mechanic, roadside provider, insurer, dealer, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not mechanical, legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If there is smoke, flame, spreading heat, worsening smell, or danger from traffic, prioritize distance and emergency services.
