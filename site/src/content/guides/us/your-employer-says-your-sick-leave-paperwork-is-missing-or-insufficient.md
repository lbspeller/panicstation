# What to do if… your employer says your sick leave paperwork is missing or insufficient (USA)
## Short answer
Ask HR or payroll in writing for the exact missing document, the deadline, where to send it, and whether your pay is paused or denied. Ask whether any available PTO or temporary pay option can be used while the paperwork is reviewed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not rely only on a verbal explanation. Ask for the missing items and deadline in writing.
- Do not send broad medical records before asking what specific document or form is required.
- Do not ignore a deadline. If you cannot meet it, explain why in writing and ask what extension or next step is available.
- Do not quit or agree to unpaid leave on the spot because you feel cornered.
- Do not assume one nationwide paid sick leave rule applies. The answer may depend on employer policy and state or local rules.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for a written checklist.** Send HR or payroll a short message: “Please confirm the exact document or information that is missing, the deadline, where I should send it, and whether my paid sick leave is paused or denied.”
2. **Ask what type of leave or benefit is being reviewed.** Ask whether this is employer PTO or sick leave, a state or local paid sick leave rule, short-term disability paperwork, or FMLA paperwork.
3. **Ask for the correct form or instructions.** If a particular form is required, ask for the current version or the correct link. Submit the requested document promptly and keep a copy of what you sent.
4. **Ask for confirmation of receipt.** Save the confirmation, the date you submitted the paperwork, and the name or department handling the review.
5. **If FMLA certification is involved, check the deadline carefully.** These deadlines concern FMLA paperwork and protections, not whether paid sick leave must continue. Employers must generally allow at least 15 calendar days after requesting certification. If a submitted certification is incomplete or insufficient, the employer must state in writing what additional information is needed and generally allow seven calendar days to fix it. If meeting a deadline is not practicable despite diligent, good-faith efforts, explain that in writing promptly.
6. **Keep the request limited.** For FMLA leave, you generally do not have to provide broad medical records. Ask what specific certification, form, or limited information is needed for the leave review.
7. **Keep a small record folder.** Save the message saying pay may stop, the written checklist, the policy page or handbook section, your submission, confirmation of receipt, and any pay stub showing a change.
8. **Check the official state or local route if needed.** If the issue may involve a state or local paid sick leave rule, start with the official state labor office directory and check the applicable state, city, or county government site.
9. **Ask for the right internal contact.** Ask for the leave administrator, benefits contact, or HR contact handling the review. If you are represented by a union, you can also contact your union representative.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to file a formal complaint or speak with an attorney.
- You do not need to write a detailed account of your illness unless a specific form requires information.
- You do not need to make long-term work decisions while you are still clarifying the paperwork issue.

## Important reassurance
An “insufficient paperwork” message can mean a missing form, an unanswered field, or a document sent to the wrong place. Getting the exact request in writing, submitting the requested document, and keeping a record can make the issue easier to resolve.

## Scope note
These are first steps for clarifying the paperwork issue and reducing avoidable mistakes. Later decisions may need state-specific or specialist help, depending on the type of leave and where you work.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Paid sick leave rules and documentation requirements can vary by employer policy, state or local law, insurance process, and the type of leave involved.
