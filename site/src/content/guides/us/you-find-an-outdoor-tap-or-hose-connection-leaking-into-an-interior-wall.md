# What to do if… you find an outdoor tap or hose connection leaking into an interior wall (USA)

## Short answer
Shut off the home’s water at the main valve first. Keep away from wet outlets, switches, cords, and appliances, and do not restore power to that area until a qualified person says it is safe.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep using the hose or outdoor spigot “just briefly.”
- Don’t touch wet outlets, switches, cords, appliances, or the breaker panel if you would have to stand in water.
- Don’t turn power back on to the wet area just because the surface looks dry.
- Don’t cut into drywall while the leak is active or while electrical safety is uncertain.
- Don’t run fans, heaters, or a dehumidifier against the wet wall until the leak is stopped and electrical risk is controlled.
- Don’t throw away damaged belongings before taking photos if you may need insurance or rental documentation.

## What to do now
1. **Shut off the water supply to the home.**
   - Close the main water shutoff valve. It is often where the water line enters the home, in a basement, garage, utility area, crawlspace, near the water heater, or on an exterior wall.
   - Open a cold faucet briefly to confirm the pressure drops and water flow stops.

2. **Stop using the outdoor faucet and hose connection.**
   - Turn the outdoor faucet or spigot handle fully off after the main water supply is off.
   - If the hose is pulling on the spigot and you can reach it from a dry, safe position, gently disconnect it. If the area is wet, spraying, icy, or unsafe, leave it alone.

3. **Reduce electrical risk.**
   - Stay away from wet outlets, switches, cords, appliances, and baseboard heaters near the damp wall.
   - If you can reach the breaker panel without stepping in water and the panel area is dry, turn off power to the affected area.
   - If the panel area is wet, blocked by water, or you are unsure which breaker controls the area, do not touch it. Call an electrician or your utility’s emergency contact.

4. **Limit water damage while waiting for help.**
   - Put a bucket or towel under active drips.
   - Blot standing water from floors.
   - Move furniture, electronics, rugs, papers, and valuables away from the wet wall.
   - After the leak is stopped and electrical risk is controlled, use ventilation, fans, or a dehumidifier if you can do so safely.

5. **Get repair help quickly.**
   - If you rent, notify your landlord, property manager, or maintenance emergency line now. Say: “Water is leaking into an interior wall from an outdoor faucet or hose bib.”
   - If you own, call a licensed plumber and explain that the leak appears to involve an outdoor faucet, hose bib, sillcock, or pipe feeding the exterior spigot.
   - If water may have reached outlets, switches, wiring, or electrical equipment, arrange an electrician before restoring power to that area.

6. **Document what happened.**
   - Take photos or video of the outdoor faucet and hose, the wet interior wall, bubbling paint, stained drywall, water on the floor, and affected belongings.
   - Write down when you noticed the leak, when the main water was shut off, and who you contacted.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to file an insurance claim.
- You do not need to know whether the failed part is a hose washer, vacuum breaker, sillcock, valve, or hidden pipe joint.
- You do not need to choose drywall repair, repainting, or remodeling options now.
- You do not need to test for mold immediately; stopping the leak, reducing electrical risk, and starting safe drying are the priority.

## Important reassurance
Water inside a wall is stressful, but the first moves are clear: stop the water, avoid electrical hazards, protect nearby belongings, and get the right repair help. Acting quickly can reduce damage even before the exact failed part is known.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Plumbing repair, electrical inspection, moisture checks, drying, mold prevention, insurance decisions, and rental disputes may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional plumbing, electrical, legal, medical, financial, insurance, or other professional advice. If water may have reached wiring, outlets, switches, appliances, or the breaker panel, prioritize safety and get qualified inspection before restoring power.
