# What to do if… your car key snaps, bends, or breaks and you cannot unlock or start your vehicle (USA)

## Short answer
Stop forcing the key. Move yourself and passengers to the safest pause you can, then use a spare, your fob’s backup method, roadside assistance, or an automotive locksmith instead of risking damage to the lock or ignition.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep twisting, prying, bending, or yanking on a damaged key.
- Don’t push tools, another key, glue, wire, picks, or screwdrivers into the lock or ignition.
- Don’t spray oils, grease, solvents, or random products into the lock or ignition unless your owner’s manual or a qualified professional tells you to.
- Don’t keep troubleshooting on a highway shoulder, live-lane edge, dark isolated spot, or anywhere you feel exposed.
- Don’t let a stranger force the lock or ignition unless you have chosen that help and the situation feels safe.

## What to do now
1. **Make the scene safe first.**
   - Turn on hazard lights if the vehicle is stopped where others need to see it.
   - Move yourself and passengers away from traffic if you can do that safely.
   - If there is immediate danger from traffic, threats, injury, fire, or an unsafe roadside position, call **911**.

2. **Stop using the damaged key.**
   - Put the broken or bent key down where you will not lose it.
   - Do not test it repeatedly. Repeated attempts can push a fragment deeper or damage the lock or ignition.

3. **Use a non-damaged way in or out if one is available.**
   - If the remote buttons still work, use the fob to unlock the vehicle and avoid the damaged blade.
   - If you have a spare key nearby, ask someone safe and trusted to bring it.
   - If this is a rental, work vehicle, dealership loaner, or borrowed car, contact the owner or provider before authorizing key cutting or lock work.

4. **Check the owner’s manual or vehicle app for the backup fob method.**
   - For push-to-start vehicles, many models have a backup reader location or emergency start method for a weak or undetected fob.
   - Use only the method for your exact vehicle. If the key blade is cracked, bent, or broken, do not insert it again.

5. **If a piece is stuck in the lock or ignition, leave it for a professional.**
   - Do not dig, glue, spray, or pull at the fragment.
   - Tell roadside assistance or the locksmith: **“The key broke, and there may be a fragment in the door lock or ignition.”**
   - Ask whether they can send an automotive locksmith or whether towing to a safer place is the better first step.

6. **Prepare the basic details without delaying safety.**
   - Keep both pieces of the key if you have them.
   - Note your exact location, vehicle make, model, year, and whether it is key-start or push-to-start.
   - Have ID available. A locksmith, dealer, rental company, or roadside provider may ask for proof that you are allowed to access the vehicle.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether a dealer, locksmith, insurer, or roadside plan is cheapest.
- You do not need to diagnose whether the key, door lock, ignition cylinder, fob battery, or anti-theft system caused the problem while you are stranded.
- You do not need to try internet extraction hacks before calling for help.
- You do not need to replace every key today unless the professional says the vehicle cannot be secured or used safely without it.

## Important reassurance
A broken vehicle key is a known roadside and automotive-locksmith problem. Stopping early, avoiding improvised tools, and asking for the right help can prevent a small key problem from becoming a larger lock or ignition repair.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the moment, stay safe, and avoid avoidable damage. Later decisions about extraction, cutting, programming, towing, or dealer repair may need a locksmith, roadside provider, dealer, insurer, rental company, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not mechanical, legal, financial, or professional advice. Vehicle designs, anti-theft systems, roadside rules, and service requirements vary. If you are in danger, prioritize emergency services and personal safety over the vehicle.
