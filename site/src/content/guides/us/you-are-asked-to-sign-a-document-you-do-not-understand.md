# What to do if… you are asked to sign a document you do not understand (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before signing. Ask what the document is, request an explanation or interpreter, and ask for a copy or time to review it before deciding what to do.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign blank pages, incomplete forms, or a document you have not been allowed to read.
- Do not sign only because someone says the document is standard or routine.
- Do not rely only on a translation app for an official, legal, financial, or high-stakes document.
- Do not assume consular staff will explain a document or decide whether you should sign it.
- If an official is involved, keep your words brief and repeat your request calmly.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the interaction.** Say: “I do not understand this document. Please explain what it is before I sign.”
2. **Ask for a readable version.** Ask whether there is:
   - an English version,
   - a clear written summary,
   - a staff member who can explain the wording slowly, or
   - an interpreter if one is available.
3. **Ask for a copy or time to review it.** Request a photo, scan, printout, or copy you can keep. If this is refused, ask whether you can delay signing while you get help understanding the document.
4. **Use a translation tool only for orientation.** Check what the document appears to cover, such as charges, waivers, incident reports, consent, or statements about responsibility. Do not treat an automated translation as the final meaning of an important document.
5. **For an airline or hotel document, ask for a supervisor.** Ask the supervisor to show you the exact section that requires your signature and explain what happens if you do not sign immediately.
6. **For a waiver, damage form, or incident report, slow down further.** Ask whether you can contact your travel insurer, a trusted person, or a translator before deciding.
7. **If an official is involved, say clearly that you do not understand.** Ask what the document is, whether signing is required, and whether an interpreter is available.
8. **If you are a U.S. citizen abroad and you are told that you are arrested or detained, ask for consular contact.** Say: “Please notify the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate. I do not understand this document.” Ask whether an interpreter is available. Ask the embassy or consulate what consular help is available. For detained U.S. citizens, consular staff can provide a list of local attorneys who speak English. They cannot provide legal advice, represent you in court, or serve as official interpreters or translators.
9. **Record the basics if safe and permitted.** Note the time, place, names, badge numbers if visible, what you were told the document is, and what you were told would happen if you did not sign. Photograph the document only if safe and permitted.
10. **Treat notarisation as a separate step.** A notary or consular officer may witness your signature. Do not treat notarisation as an explanation of the document or as advice to sign it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to complain, seek a refund, or argue about responsibility.
- You do not need to solve the whole dispute during the first conversation.
- You do not need to sign simply to end an uncomfortable interaction.
- Later decisions about claims, complaints, or legal advice can wait until you understand the document and have reached a calmer pause.

## Important reassurance
Being asked to sign an unfamiliar document under time pressure can make it hard to think clearly. Asking what the document is, requesting an explanation, and slowing the interaction down are reasonable first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A document involving detention, alleged offences, significant financial risk, or a formal waiver may need urgent advice from a qualified local attorney or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information for immediate harm-prevention. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Local laws and procedures vary, especially outside the United States.
