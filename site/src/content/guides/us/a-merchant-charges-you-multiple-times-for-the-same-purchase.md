# What to do if… a merchant charges you multiple times for the same purchase (USA)

## Short answer
Verify if the duplicate charge is "pending" or "posted," then contact the merchant to request a reversal. If the merchant fails to resolve it or the charge "posts" to your statement, initiate a formal dispute with your bank or credit card issuer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a "pending" charge is final; these are often temporary authorizations that disappear within 3 to 7 business days.
- Do not cancel your card immediately unless you believe your card details were stolen rather than accidentally double-processed.
- Do not stop paying your entire credit card bill; pay all undisputed amounts to avoid interest and late fees on legitimate purchases.
- Do not send original physical receipts to the merchant or bank; use copies, scans, or digital screenshots.
- Do not wait longer than 60 days from the statement date to send a formal written notice; legal protections under the Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA) have strict time limits.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the status of each transaction in your banking app or online statement.**
   - Determine if the duplicate is marked as **Pending** or **Posted** (sometimes called Cleared).
   - Record the exact date, time, and merchant name for each entry.

2. **Save evidence of the single purchase.**
   - Locate the original receipt, order confirmation email, or digital invoice showing you only authorized one transaction.
   - Take screenshots of the duplicate entries as they appear on your financial statement.

3. **Request a refund from the merchant.**
   - Contact the business directly via their customer service line or email.
   - Provide the order number and evidence of the double charge.
   - Ask for a "transaction ID" for the refund and a confirmation email stating that a reversal has been processed.

4. **Initiate a formal dispute if the merchant does not resolve the issue promptly.**
   - **Credit Cards (FCBA):** Send a **written** billing error notice to the issuer's "billing inquiries" address (not the payment address) within 60 days of the statement date.
   - **Debit Cards (Regulation E):** Notify your bank immediately; you may do this orally, but the bank can require a written follow-up within 10 business days to provide "provisional credit" while they investigate.

5. **Request fee waivers if the duplicate charge caused a cash shortage.**
   - If the extra withdrawal triggered an overdraft or non-sufficient funds (NSF) fee, ask the bank to waive these charges specifically because of the processing error.

6. **Escalate to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) if the issuer fails to respond.**
   - If the bank or issuer does not acknowledge your credit card dispute within 30 days or resolve it within two billing cycles (maximum 90 days), file a complaint via the CFPB website.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to close your account or change banks.
- You do not need to contact the Better Business Bureau or post public reviews until the financial resolution process is underway.
- You do not need to build a comprehensive legal file; simple receipts and transaction dates are usually sufficient for duplicate charge disputes.

## Important reassurance
Duplicate charges are common technical errors caused by "double-swiping," system glitches, or batch processing delays. Federal laws like the Fair Credit Billing Act and Regulation E are specifically designed to protect you from paying for these errors. Most duplicates are resolved within one or two billing cycles once the formal process is triggered.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and the initiation of formal dispute rights for identical multiple charges. It does not cover unauthorized fraud where your card was stolen, nor does it cover disputes regarding the quality of goods or services received.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Your specific rights depend on the terms of your account agreement and federal regulations. Always follow the specific dispute instructions found on the back of your monthly statement.
