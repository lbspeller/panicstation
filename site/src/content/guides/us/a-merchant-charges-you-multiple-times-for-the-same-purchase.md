# What to do if… a merchant charges you multiple times for the same purchase (USA)

## Short answer
Check whether the extra entry is pending or posted, then report the duplicate to your card issuer or bank promptly. Contact the merchant as well, but do not wait for the merchant before starting the issuer or bank process.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a pending entry is a final posted charge. Record it and ask your issuer or bank what to do next.
- Do not let merchant contact delay notifying your card issuer or bank.
- Do not send original receipts. Keep the originals and provide copies or screenshots.
- Do not stop paying the entire credit card bill. Pay the undisputed amount according to your account terms while the dispute is handled.
- Do not cancel the card in a panic unless you suspect unauthorized use. If you did not make the purchase, tell the issuer or bank that the transaction may be unauthorized.

## What to do now
1. **Check each entry.**
   - Write down the merchant name as shown, each amount, the date, and whether each entry is pending or posted.
   - Take screenshots before anything changes.

2. **Contact your card issuer or bank promptly.**
   - Use the customer-service number on the back of the card or on your statement.
   - Say that the same purchase appears to have been charged more than once.
   - Ask how to submit a dispute, whether you need to monitor a pending entry, and where any written notice must go.
   - Ask for a reference number and note the date of the call.

3. **Contact the merchant as well.**
   - Give the merchant the receipt or order number and the duplicate amounts.
   - Ask whether the extra entry can be voided, reversed, or refunded.
   - Ask for written confirmation of any action the merchant takes.

4. **Keep the issuer or bank process moving.**
   - Save the receipt, order confirmation, screenshots, reference numbers, and messages.
   - For a credit card, send a written billing-error notice promptly so it is received at the issuer’s billing-disputes, billing-errors, or billing-inquiries address no later than 60 days after the first statement reflecting the duplicate was sent. This is usually not the payment address.
   - For a debit card, report the error promptly. Regulation E generally covers oral or written notice received no later than 60 days after the statement showing the error was sent. Your bank may ask for written confirmation within 10 business days after an oral report.
   - Send copies of supporting documents, not originals. Keep a copy of anything you send.
   - For a mailed notice, use certified mail or another trackable mailing method, if possible.

5. **Tell the bank if the duplicate affects money you need now.**
   - Explain if the extra debit is causing an overdraft, a declined payment, or difficulty covering essentials.
   - Ask the bank to review any fees caused by the duplicate and tell you whether it needs more documentation.

6. **Escalate if the issuer or bank is not handling the dispute.**
   - You can submit a complaint to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau.
   - Include the dates, amounts, screenshots, receipt, and the dates you contacted the merchant and issuer or bank.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to close the account or switch payment methods.
- You do not need to work out the technical cause of the duplicate before reporting it.
- Store reviews and broader complaints can wait until the refund or dispute process is underway.

## Important reassurance
A duplicate entry is a specific problem you can report. You do not need to solve the cause before taking the first steps, and keeping a simple dated record is enough to get started.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and start the refund or dispute process. If the merchant or issuer contests what happened, you may need more detailed help later.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Procedures can vary by account type and issuer. Follow your card issuer or bank’s instructions and keep a dated record of calls, messages, and letters.
