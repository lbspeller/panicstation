# What to do if… your laptop is locked by an unknown firmware or BIOS password (USA)

## Short answer
Stop guessing. Treat this as a security lockout, protect any data you can still reach, and use an authorized route: your IT administrator, the seller, the retailer, or the manufacturer’s official support.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep trying random passwords; some systems may impose lockouts or extra restrictions after repeated failures.
- Don’t use “BIOS unlock” generators, paid bypass tools, or forum workarounds; they may be scams, may expose your data, and may create authorization problems.
- Don’t disassemble the laptop or attempt hardware “clears” unless the manufacturer or an authorized service provider instructs you.
- Don’t change firmware security, boot, TPM, Secure Boot, or storage settings if drive encryption may be enabled; this can trigger a recovery-key prompt you may not be able to satisfy.
- Don’t erase, reset, or reinstall the operating system just to “try something” before you have checked backups and recovery keys.

## What to do now
1. **Capture exactly what is on the screen.**  
   Take a photo of the prompt wording and any code, service tag, serial number, lock symbol, or error message shown. Write down the laptop make and model from the case or label.

2. **Make one careful attempt to remember the real password, then stop.**  
   Check only likely legitimate sources: your password manager, a workplace or school IT team, a repair or refurbishing provider, a previous owner, or a family member who set up the laptop.

3. **Identify what kind of prompt it appears to be.**  
   - **BIOS, UEFI, Setup, Admin, Supervisor, or Power-on password:** usually blocks firmware settings or startup.
   - **HDD or SSD password:** may be a drive-level lock; moving the drive to another computer may not help.
   - **BitLocker recovery key:** usually asks for a long recovery key rather than a normal password.

4. **If you can still boot into Windows, macOS, or Linux, back up essential files now.**  
   Copy the most important files to an external drive or a trusted cloud account. If Windows device encryption or BitLocker is on, check that you can access the recovery key through your Microsoft account, your work or school account, a saved printout, a USB drive, or IT support before changing firmware-related settings.

5. **If this is a work, school, or otherwise managed laptop, contact IT first.**  
   The password may have been set by device management or an administrator. Tell them the exact prompt wording and send the photo you took.

6. **If this is your personal laptop, contact the manufacturer’s official support.**  
   Be ready to verify ownership and provide the model, serial number or service tag, and any on-screen code. Use the manufacturer’s own support site, store, or authorized service provider rather than a third-party unlock service.

7. **If you bought the laptop used or refurbished and it arrived locked, contact the seller or platform now.**  
   Say the laptop is locked before normal use and ask for a return, refund, or replacement under the platform’s process. Save the listing, order confirmation, messages, and your photo of the lock screen.

8. **If you paid by credit card and the seller does not resolve it, preserve your dispute window.**  
   For credit-card billing-error protections, you generally need to send a written billing-error notice so it reaches the card issuer within 60 calendar days after the first statement showing the problem charge. Follow your card issuer’s dispute instructions and keep copies of what you send.

9. **If the sale looks fraudulent, reporting is optional but may help.**  
   You can report the seller or scam pattern to the Federal Trade Commission. Keep your report factual: what was advertised, what arrived, what the lock screen shows, and what the seller said.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to decide today whether to replace the motherboard, replace the drive, or buy a new laptop.
- You don’t need to try reset tricks, hardware clears, or unlock utilities.
- You don’t need to change boot, encryption, Secure Boot, TPM, or storage settings until backups and recovery keys are checked.
- You don’t need to argue with the seller in detail while panicked; a short written refund or return request is enough for now.

## Important reassurance
This is alarming, but it does not mean you have to solve the firmware lock yourself. The safest next step is usually to preserve information, protect reachable data, and move through an authorized support, IT, or seller-dispute route.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from the manufacturer, an authorized repair provider, your organization’s IT team, the seller platform, your card issuer, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Some firmware and drive locks are intentionally designed to resist bypassing; if you cannot clearly establish that you are authorized to access the laptop, stop and resolve ownership or authorization first.
