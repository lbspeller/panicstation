# What to do if… you are immunocompromised and develop a fever even if you otherwise feel okay (USA)

## Short answer
Call the clinician or team that manages your immune suppression now and tell them your temperature, when you measured it, and why you are immunocompromised. Follow any written fever plan you have; use an emergency department if the plan says to, your team directs you to, or you cannot get prompt advice, and call 911 for severe symptoms.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see whether the fever passes just because you otherwise feel okay.
- Do not delay the call while repeatedly rechecking your temperature or trying to work out the cause.
- Do not take acetaminophen, ibuprofen, or another fever-reducing medicine just to bring the temperature down before asking your care team, unless you already have instructions to do so. If you already took something, still call and say what you took and when.
- Do not stop, skip, or change chemotherapy, transplant medicines, steroids, biologics, or other immune-suppressing medicines unless a clinician tells you to.
- Do not drive yourself if you feel weak, dizzy, confused, short of breath, or too unwell to drive safely.
- Do not delay urgent care while trying to pack everything or arrange the perfect transport option.

## What to do now
1. **Take your temperature and write it down.**  
   Note the reading, the time, how you measured it, and any symptoms such as chills, sweating, cough, shortness of breath, pain, vomiting, diarrhea, or burning when you urinate.

2. **Call the clinician or team that manages your immune suppression now.**  
   Say: *“I am immunocompromised and I have a fever. My temperature is [reading], measured at [time].”* Use the after-hours number if needed.

3. **Use the right trigger for your situation.**  
   If you are receiving chemotherapy, call immediately for a temperature of **100.4°F (38°C) or higher**. If you have been told that you are neutropenic, have had a transplant, or have a written fever plan, follow that plan because it may use a different or lower trigger. If you are unsure whether your reading meets the trigger, call rather than waiting.

4. **Use an emergency department if you cannot get prompt advice or your team tells you to go in.**  
   At check-in, say immediately that you are immunocompromised and have a fever. Mention chemotherapy, neutropenia, a transplant, immune-suppressing medicines, recent treatment dates, and any port or PICC line.

5. **Call 911 for severe symptoms.**  
   Call 911 now for trouble breathing, new confusion, collapse or fainting, inability to stay awake, blue or gray lips or skin, severe pain, or rapid worsening.

6. **Bring the essentials without delaying care.**  
   Take your medication list, allergies, recent treatment information, and your temperature note if they are easy to grab. Wear a mask around other people if practical, but do not delay leaving to find one.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the cause of the fever before calling.
- You do not need to decide whether it is a cold, flu, another virus, or something else.
- You do not need to make long-term decisions about immune-suppressing medicines now.
- You do not need to keep checking your temperature once you have an urgent reading and are arranging care.

## Important reassurance
Feeling okay does not rule out a serious infection when your immune system is weakened. Calling now is a sensible precaution, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours only. The right next step may depend on why you are immunocompromised, your medicines, recent treatment, transplant status, blood counts, and your care team’s written plan.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice for your specific situation. Contact the clinician or team that manages your immune suppression for personal instructions, use an emergency department if prompt advice is unavailable, and call 911 for severe symptoms.
