# What to do if… you find your name and contact details posted alongside sexual content without your consent (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause, save only basic proof, reduce ways strangers can reach you, and report the post for removal. Avoid confronting the poster, paying demands, or spreading the link.

## Do not do these things
- Do not bargain with, threaten, pay, or keep messaging the person who posted it.
- Do not delete accounts, messages, emails, or call logs in a panic if they are your only record.
- Do not repost or widely share the link to warn people; it can spread the harm.
- Do not download, save, forward, or ask anyone to send sexual content involving someone who may have been under 18.
- Do not assume you must prove a crime before asking a specialist service or hotline for help.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause first.**  
   If someone is nearby, trying to meet you, threatening to come to you, or you feel in immediate physical danger, call 911. If you are not in immediate danger, move somewhere calmer or near a trusted person before you start reporting.

2. **Save basic proof without spreading it.**  
   If it is safe and the content does not involve someone under 18, take screenshots that show your name or contact details, the account or site, the URL, and any visible date or time. If messages are coming in, save a few examples of threats, demands, caller IDs, usernames, or numbers.

3. **If anyone in the content may have been under 18, use the safer child-content route.**  
   Do not download, forward, or ask anyone to send the sexual image or video. Record the URL, account name, platform, and what you saw, then use NCMEC’s CyberTipline or Take It Down.

4. **Reduce access to you for the next few hours.**  
   Turn on spam or unknown-caller filtering if available. Limit direct messages, hide phone and email details from public profiles, and ask a trusted person to help monitor incoming contact if you are overwhelmed.

5. **Secure the accounts most connected to the post.**  
   Change passwords for your email and main social accounts, especially if the post links to them. Turn on two-factor authentication and check for unfamiliar recovery emails, phone numbers, or logged-in devices.

6. **Report the post where it appears.**  
   Use the site or platform report tool. Choose the closest categories, such as non-consensual intimate imagery, sexual deepfake, impersonation, harassment, or personal information. If there are multiple copies, start with the post driving the most contact to you.

7. **Ask for removal and keep the report receipt.**  
   For covered platforms, valid reports of non-consensual intimate images generally must be removed within 48 hours. Keep confirmation emails, report numbers, screenshots of report forms, and any response from the platform.

8. **Get specialist support for prioritising next steps.**  
   Contact the Cyber Civil Rights Initiative image abuse helpline if you are in the U.S. and this involves nonconsensual pornography, recorded sexual assault, sextortion, or image-based sexual abuse. Tell them where it is posted, whether your contact details are included, and whether you are being threatened or extorted.

9. **If strangers are contacting you, treat it as harassment.**  
   Do not argue with them. Block and report the accounts, save a small sample of messages or call logs, and use call or text screening. If you feel safe and it seems useful, send one brief line such as “Do not contact me again,” then stop engaging.

10. **Choose an official report route only if it helps right now.**  
   Use 911 for immediate danger. For a police report, contact local law enforcement through its non-emergency or online reporting route. If the situation includes cyber-enabled threats, extortion, impersonation, or broader cybercrime, you can also file with IC3, knowing it may not respond directly to every complaint.

11. **Use confidential emotional support if you are overwhelmed.**  
   RAINN can provide confidential support and help you think through next steps. You do not need to know exactly what to call what happened before asking for help.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to sue, hire a lawyer, tell your employer or school, go public, identify the poster, or change your phone number and email. Start with safety, reducing contact, basic proof, and takedown reports.

## Important reassurance
This is a recognised form of image-based abuse, online sexual harassment, and doxxing. Feeling shocked, frozen, angry, or panicked is a normal response to a violating situation. The next step is not to solve everything; it is to slow the spread, reduce access to you, and bring in support.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about legal options, school or workplace safety, longer-term digital cleanup, or state-specific processes may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, mental health, digital security, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If sexual content involves someone who may have been under 18, do not download, save, forward, or request the content; use NCMEC’s reporting and removal routes.
