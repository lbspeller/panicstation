# What to do if… you return home and find a window has been broken by weather or impact (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before entering. If there is any chance someone is inside, move to a safer place and call 911; if the home appears empty and safe to enter, keep people and pets away from the glass, document the damage, and secure the opening before sorting out repair or insurance details.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t enter if you suspect an intruder might still be inside.
- Don’t touch glass with bare hands or walk near it barefoot, in socks, or in open shoes.
- Don’t pull, pry, or tape glass that is cracked, hanging, high up, or under tension.
- Don’t make permanent repairs before taking clear photos and checking what your insurer or landlord needs.
- Don’t throw away damaged parts, debris, or receipts until your insurer or landlord says they are no longer needed, unless keeping them would be unsafe.
- Don’t leave the opening unsecured overnight if it can be safely covered or professionally boarded.
- Don’t post real-time details that show your home is currently unsecured.

## What to do now
1. **Stop outside and check for immediate danger.** Look for signs someone could be inside, such as an open door, unusual noises, lights you did not leave on, disturbed entry points, or missing property visible from outside. If you suspect a break-in, someone inside, fire, injury, or another immediate danger, call 911 and stay outside somewhere safer.

2. **If it appears safe to enter, go in carefully.** Use a different door if possible. Turn on lights before walking through the affected area. Keep children, pets, and anyone without sturdy shoes away from the room.

3. **Make a glass no-go zone.** Put on sturdy shoes or boots and, if available, work gloves and eye protection. Close the door to the room, block the doorway, or mark a clear boundary so nobody tracks glass through the home.

4. **Document the damage before moving much.** Take photos or video from inside and outside:
   - the full window and surrounding wall,
   - close-ups of cracks, impact points, frame damage, and loose glass,
   - any branch, stone, hail, object, or other debris that may show the cause,
   - any rain, wind, or interior damage near the opening.

5. **If crime is possible, use the right police route.** If it is happening now or someone may be inside, call 911. If it is not urgent, consider contacting your local police department’s non-emergency line or online reporting system and ask whether they want a report for vandalism, burglary, or attempted entry. Keep any incident or report number.

6. **Secure the opening only as far as you can do safely.** If a loose shard is small, reachable from the floor, and clearly safe to remove, place it in a rigid container or heavy bag. If glass is high, flexing, stuck in the frame, or likely to fall, leave it alone and keep the area blocked off. Use heavy plastic sheeting, a tarp, or plywood as a temporary cover only if you can attach it without climbing unsafely, leaning on broken glass, or forcing a damaged frame. If the job needs a ladder, the frame is damaged, or glass is still falling, call an emergency glass repair or board-up service.

7. **Limit water and electrical risk.** Move electronics, papers, and valuables away from the opening if you can do it without stepping near glass. Use towels, buckets, or plastic to catch water. If water is near outlets, cords, electrical panels, or fixtures, do not touch them; keep clear and contact an electrician, your utility, or 911 if there is active electrical danger such as sparking, smoke, or a burning smell.

8. **If you rent, notify your landlord or property manager right away.** Send photos, say whether the opening is secure, and ask who they want to arrange emergency board-up, glass removal, and repair. Keep copies of messages and receipts.

9. **Contact your homeowners or renters insurer after immediate safety is handled.** Ask what documentation they need, what temporary repairs they expect you to make to prevent further damage, and whether they want damaged items kept for inspection. Save receipts for temporary covers, board-up work, cleanup, and emergency repair. Avoid permanent repair until your insurer or landlord says to proceed.

## What can wait
- Choosing the final window type, glass style, or contractor.
- Deep cleaning every tiny glass fragment after the main hazard is blocked off.
- Deciding whether an insurance claim is worth it.
- Cosmetic work such as curtains, paint, trim, or décor.
- Long-term security upgrades.

## Important reassurance
A broken window can feel alarming because it combines noise, mess, exposure, and uncertainty. For now, the job is only to stay safe, keep people away from glass, document what happened, cover the opening, and make the necessary calls.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours after finding the damage. Repair choices, insurance strategy, landlord disputes, and long-term security decisions may need specialist help later.

## Important note
This is general information for urgent first steps and harm prevention, not legal, insurance, construction, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you believe a crime may be in progress or someone may be inside, do not enter; prioritise safety and contact emergency services.
