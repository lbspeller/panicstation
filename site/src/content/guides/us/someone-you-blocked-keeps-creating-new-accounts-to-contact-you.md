# What to do if… someone you blocked keeps creating new accounts to contact you (USA)

## Short answer
Do not reply. Save a basic record of the new contact, reduce the ways new accounts can reach you, and call 911 if you are in immediate danger or fear a threat of harm.

## Do not do these things
- Do not reply, argue, or send another explanation. A response may encourage further contact or complicate the record.
- Do not move the conversation to another app, email address, or phone number to contain it.
- Do not post about the person publicly or ask others to confront them.
- Do not delete messages or account details before saving a record, when it is safe to do so.
- Do not dismiss threats or repeated unwanted contact because they happened online.
- Do not make a large number of sudden account changes if you think that could affect your safety.

## What to do now
1. **Check immediate safety first.** If you are in immediate danger or fear a threat of harm, call **911**. If you think the person may come to your home, workplace, or current location, move to a safer place if you can do so safely.
2. **Save a basic record, then step away.**
   - Save the message, profile page, username, date, time, and any threat.
   - Save links to profiles, posts, or messages where possible.
   - Keep a simple log: *date, time, platform, account name, what happened, and any safety concern*.
   - Some apps may notify the other person when you take a screenshot or screen recording. When you are unsure, consider photographing the screen with a second, safer device.
   - Store copies somewhere you believe the person cannot access. If you suspect device or account monitoring, use a safer device if possible.
3. **Reduce the ways new accounts can reach you.**
   - Make profiles private where available.
   - Remove unknown followers or friends.
   - Limit who can message, comment, or tag you.
   - Turn off location sharing and avoid posting your location in real time.
   - Review whether people can find your account using your phone number, email address, or synced contacts.
4. **Secure accounts that may be exposed.**
   - Only make changes that feel safe. Use a safer device if you think the person may have access to your current device.
   - Change passwords for accounts the person may know or access.
   - Use a different password for each important account.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication where available.
   - Review recovery email addresses and phone numbers.
   - Review signed-in devices. Sign out of devices you do not recognize, or use a sign-out-all-devices option where appropriate.
5. **Report and block new accounts when it is safe to do so.**
   - Use the platform’s available reporting categories, such as harassment, impersonation, or block evasion where offered.
   - Save a record of the report and any response from the platform.
6. **Tell one trusted person.**
   - Share the basic pattern and ask them not to contact the person on your behalf.
   - If you are worried about escalation, agree on a simple check-in or code phrase that means you need help.
7. **Consider local support if the pattern continues or you feel unsafe.**
   - You can contact your local police department to ask how to report the pattern.
   - You can ask a local victim advocate about options in your area. Protective orders may have different names and requirements depending on the jurisdiction.
   - VictimConnect can help you find local victim services. If this involves an ex-partner or relationship abuse, the National Domestic Violence Hotline also has stalking safety-planning resources.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide or do these things now:
- Changing your phone number, renaming accounts, or deleting profiles.
- Creating a perfect timeline. A basic log and saved messages are enough for now.
- Setting up a separate email address for non-essential accounts.
- Deciding whether to seek a protective order.
- Posting public updates or making a large announcement.

## Important reassurance
Repeated contact through new accounts after you blocked someone can feel frightening and exhausting. You do not need to solve everything at once, and you do not have to engage with the person to justify your boundary.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce contact routes, save a basic record, and stabilize the moment. Later decisions may need support from a local victim advocate or another specialist familiar with your area.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger or fear a threat of harm, call **911**. If you suspect account or device monitoring, make changes from a safer device when possible.
