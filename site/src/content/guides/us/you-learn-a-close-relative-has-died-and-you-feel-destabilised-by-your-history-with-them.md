# What to do if… you learn a close relative has died and you feel destabilised by your history with them (USA)

## Short answer
Stabilize first: reduce incoming messages, contact one steady person, and use 988 for immediate emotional crisis support, or 911 if you are in immediate danger or afraid you may harm yourself or someone else.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to travel, funeral roles, clearing a home, or handling arrangements while you feel flooded, unreal, or unable to focus.
- Do not stay on a call where people push you, interrogate you, or demand immediate decisions; end it and switch to text or email.
- Do not post publicly about the death until you are sure the closest next of kin has notified the people they need to notify.
- Do not try to shut the feelings down by drinking heavily or using substances; this can make panic, sleep, and judgment worse.
- Do not drive a long distance, spend a large amount of money, or send an emotional message while you feel shaky or unsafe to focus.
- Do not assume your reaction must be sadness; trauma-linked grief can include relief, anger, numbness, confusion, guilt, or rapid shifts.

## What to do now
1. **Create a short safety pause.** Sit somewhere physically safe. Silence notifications for a set period. Drink water. Eat something small if you can. Put your feet on the floor and name five things you can see.
2. **Confirm only the minimum facts.** If the news is unclear, ask for one written line with the date, location, and who confirmed the death. You do not need the full story, medical details, or family explanations right now.
3. **Contact one person who helps you stay grounded.** Text: “I got news of a death in my family and I’m destabilized. Can you stay with me or call me for 10 minutes?” If being alone feels unsafe, ask them to come over or help you get to a safer place.
4. **Use U.S. crisis support if you feel unsafe or out of control.**
   - If you might harm yourself, might harm someone else, or are in immediate danger: call 911 or go to the nearest emergency department.
   - If you need emotional crisis support now, including non-suicidal distress: call or text 988, or use 988 chat, and say, “I am bereaved, I have a trauma history, and I feel destabilized.”
5. **Set one boundary for the next 24 hours.** Choose one: “No calls,” “Text only,” or “I’ll respond tomorrow.” Send one message to the main family contact: “I received the news. I’m not able to talk right now. Please text key practical updates only.”
6. **Find out who is handling official tasks without volunteering yourself.** Ask: “Who is the point person with the funeral home?” Funeral homes generally report deaths to Social Security. If you are already the person handling this, ask the funeral home what they need; SSA generally takes death reports by phone or in person, not online or by email.
7. **Avoid being pulled into triggering tasks.** If someone asks you to clear a home, handle documents, contact agencies, or manage money, respond: “I can’t do that today. Please put requests in writing and I’ll review later.” If you must go somewhere connected to the death, bring a neutral support person.
8. **Pause decisions about viewing, attending, travelling, or speaking.** You can say, “I’m not deciding today.” Ask only for dates, times, and locations, then stop.
9. **Do one small anchor action.** Take a short shower, change clothes, step outside for two minutes of air, wash your hands in cool water, or put a familiar object in your pocket. The aim is to reduce impulsive decisions, not to fix the grief.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to attend services, travel, speak at a funeral, view the body, or contact specific relatives.
- You do not need to respond to everyone, explain your history, justify your boundaries, or make anyone understand your reaction.
- You do not need to deal with belongings, wills, estate issues, government benefits, insurance, or financial conversations right now unless you are the only person responsible for an urgent safety matter.
- You do not need to feel a particular emotion, forgive anyone, make peace, or decide what the relationship meant.

## Important reassurance
A death can destabilize you even if the relationship was harmful or complicated. Feeling numb, relieved, angry, guilty, blank, or unable to cry does not mean you are grieving wrong. In the first hours, the goal is safety, reduced pressure, and delaying decisions you cannot easily undo.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on getting you stable and protected from pressure while you are triggered. Later decisions about funerals, family contact, estates, travel, or support may need specialist help, but you do not have to solve them today.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are at risk of harming yourself or someone else, or cannot stay safe, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency department. If you need urgent emotional support, call or text 988, or use 988 chat.
