# What to do if… you are asked to provide a photo and life summary for a funeral or memorial on a tight deadline (USA)

## Short answer
Ask what the photo and summary will be used for, the exact deadline, and how they should be sent. Then send one clear photo and a short factual paragraph; a simple usable version is enough for an urgent request.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t guess uncertain facts. Leave out anything you cannot quickly confirm.
- Don’t add private, disputed, or explanatory details because you feel pressured to fill space.
- Don’t spend the available time searching for a perfect photo if you already have a clear, recognizable one.
- Don’t send several competing versions from different relatives if one person can send the final version.
- Don’t rely on a messaging service for the only copy of the photo if email or an upload link is available. Messaging services may reduce image quality.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the requirements in one message.** Ask for the deadline, what the materials are for, any word or character limit, the preferred delivery method, and the preferred photo file type.
2. **Choose one clear photo.** Use a recognizable, well-lit image. A head-and-shoulders photo is often easy to use. A solo image is simplest, but a photo with other people may work if the requester can crop it.
3. **Send the best version you already have.** Attach the original photo file when possible. If you only have a printed snapshot, scan it if that is practical. Ask whether the requester has a resolution requirement; when none is provided, 300–400 dpi is a reasonable starting point for a snapshot print. If scanning is not practical, take a straight, glare-free photo of the print in even light.
4. **Write a short factual paragraph.** Include the person’s name, a few confirmed life details, and one or two warm details such as a hobby, interest, or quality people remember. Leave out any uncertain detail.
5. **Ask who should send the final version.** When several relatives are involved, use one sender where possible and ask who should receive any correction.
6. **Do a quick check if someone is available.** Ask one trusted person to check names, dates, and anything the family does not want published. Do not delay an urgent basic version solely to arrange a second review.
7. **Send a minimal version first when the deadline is very close.** A clear photo and two or three confirmed sentences can be sent immediately. A fuller paragraph can be prepared later if the requester says there is another place to use it.
8. **Keep the sent version.** Save the exact photo, paragraph, recipient, and time sent so you can resend them quickly if asked.

## What can wait
- You do not have to write a full obituary, eulogy, or complete life story now unless that is specifically requested.
- You do not need to settle family disagreements now. Use neutral, confirmed details.
- You do not need to find every possible photo or build a slideshow unless that is the request.
- You do not need to decide whether a longer version should be written later.

## Important reassurance
Grief and time pressure can make a small task feel much harder. A clear photo and a short, accurate paragraph are a reasonable response to an urgent request.

## Scope note
These are first steps for responding to a tight deadline. Later decisions, longer writing, or sensitive family questions may need more time and specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information for a stressful moment. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. When a requester has specific formatting or submission requirements, ask them to confirm what they need.
