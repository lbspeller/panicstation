# What to do if… your credit card is over the limit and restricted due to interest or fees (USA)

## Short answer
Pause new spending on the card, check what posted, and contact the issuer using the number on the back of the card or in the issuer’s app. Make only a payment you can safely cover, and ask when it will post and when it may affect available credit.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep retrying the restricted card. Check whether any attempt went through before trying another payment method for an essential purchase.
- Do not schedule a payment that could overdraw your linked account or be returned.
- Do not use a cash advance to try to fix the limit problem without checking the cost first. It may add fees and interest.
- Do not describe a legitimate charge as fraud to create temporary room on the card.
- Do not sign up for a debt-relief or debt-settlement service while you feel rushed.

## What to do now
1. **Check the account details.** Note the current balance, credit limit, available credit, pending charges, recent interest, fees, recurring charges, and statement dates. Save a screenshot or write down the amounts.
2. **Contact the issuer.** Use the number on the back of the card or in the issuer’s app. Ask why the card is restricted, what amount is needed before new purchases may be allowed, whether a payment must clear first, and whether recurring charges may still be processed.
3. **Make only a safely affordable payment.** Ask how soon the payment will post and when it may affect available credit. Save the confirmation. Do not assume the restriction has lifted until the issuer confirms it or the account shows available credit.
4. **Check the name and cause of any fee.** If the account shows an over-the-limit fee, ask what caused the balance to exceed the limit and which billing cycle the fee relates to. An issuer generally may not charge an over-the-limit fee for a billing cycle if the balance exceeded the limit solely because of fees or interest charged by the issuer during that billing cycle. If the issuer says a transaction triggered the fee, ask whether you had affirmatively opted in to the issuer paying over-the-limit transactions. Ask the issuer to review and reverse any fee that should not apply. You can also ask the issuer to revoke future consent if you no longer want over-the-limit transactions paid.
5. **Check recurring charges.** Identify subscriptions and bills that may still try to use the card. Cancel or move nonessential charges. For essential bills, check whether another payment method is available before a due date.
6. **Act promptly if the minimum payment is difficult.** Tell the issuer what you can safely pay and ask what short-term options are available. Ask whether written confirmation is available and keep notes of the date, time, and outcome of the contact.
7. **Escalate an unresolved account problem.** If the issuer does not correct an obvious error or explain the restriction, ask for its complaint channel. Keep statements, screenshots, payment confirmations, and contact notes. A Consumer Financial Protection Bureau complaint is an option if the problem remains unresolved.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to close the card, apply for another card, transfer a balance, or consolidate debt.
- You do not need to solve your credit score today.
- You do not need to agree to a long-term repayment plan before you understand the issuer’s options.
- You do not need to choose a credit-counseling service during this first call.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to solve the whole balance today. The immediate goal is to understand what posted, avoid a payment you cannot cover, and ask the issuer what condition must be met before the restriction can change.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist help. Credit counseling may be worth considering after the immediate account issue is stable, but you can review services carefully rather than deciding under pressure.

## Important note
This is general information, not financial, legal, or professional advice. Issuer policies, account terms, payment-posting times, and restriction-removal processes vary. Ask the issuer to confirm what applies to your account.
