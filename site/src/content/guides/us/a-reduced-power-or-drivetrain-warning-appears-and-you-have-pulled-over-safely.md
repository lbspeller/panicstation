# What to do if… a “reduced power” or drivetrain warning appears and you have pulled over safely (USA)

## Short answer
Stop driving as soon as you are safely clear of travel lanes and assume the vehicle requires a tow. This warning indicates the vehicle's computer has detected a fault and has limited engine output to prevent catastrophic damage.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "limp" the vehicle to your final destination if you are on a high-speed road or highway.
- Do not ignore the warning even if the vehicle seems to drive normally; the "reduced power" mode is a protective state that may suddenly worsen.
- Do not stand directly behind or in front of the vehicle while waiting for help.
- Do not attempt to inspect the engine or crawl under the car if you are on a narrow shoulder or near active traffic.
- Do not leave your hazard lights off, even during daylight hours.
- Do not light flares if you smell gasoline or suspect a fuel leak.

## What to do now
1. **Ensure the vehicle is as far away from active travel lanes as possible.** **Pull onto the right-hand shoulder or into a parking lot or rest area immediately.**
2. **Make your vehicle visible to other drivers using all available signals.** **Turn on your hazard lights and, if it is dark or visibility is poor, keep your parking lights on.** 
3. **Decide whether to stay in the vehicle or move to a safer location based on the shoulder width.** **If the shoulder is narrow or you feel the vehicle is at risk of being struck, exit through the passenger-side door (away from traffic) and move behind a guardrail or up an embankment.**
   - If the shoulder is wide and you feel safer inside, stay buckled in your seat with the doors locked.
4. **Try a one-time system reset only if you have reached a safe, non-traffic location.** **Turn the engine off, wait for at least 30 seconds, and restart the vehicle once to see if the warning clears.**
   - If the warning returns immediately or the engine runs roughly, shut it off and do not attempt to drive.
5. **Call for professional assistance based on your level of danger.** **If your vehicle is partially blocking a lane or you are in a high-traffic area where you feel unsafe, call 911 to request a "service patrol" or police standby.**
   - Otherwise, call your roadside assistance provider (such as AAA at 1-800-222-4357, your insurance carrier, or the vehicle manufacturer’s assistance line).
6. **Note the exact wording of the message and any physical symptoms.** **Check for "red flag" cues such as steam, smoke, burning smells, or leaking fluids under the car.**
7. **Deploy warning devices only if you can do so without stepping into a travel lane.** **Place reflective triangles or flares behind the vehicle at intervals (approximately 100, 200, and 300 feet) to give oncoming drivers early notice.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the mechanical cause of the fault (e.g., throttle body, sensor failure, or software glitch) while at the roadside.
- You do not need to choose a specific repair shop or negotiate costs until the vehicle is at a safe staging area or being hooked to the tow truck.
- You do not need to worry about permanent engine damage if you have stopped the vehicle promptly after the warning appeared.

## Important reassurance
Seeing a "reduced power" warning is unsettling, but it means the vehicle's safety systems are working as intended to protect the engine and transmission. By pulling over and stopping, you have already completed the most important step in preventing a more serious mechanical failure or an accident.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a vehicle that has entered "limp mode" or displayed a powertrain warning. It does not cover mechanical diagnostics or long-term repair procedures.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace the specific instructions in your vehicle's owner manual or the direction of emergency responders. Move Over laws in many states (including CA, FL, and PA) now require drivers to slow down or change lanes for any stationary vehicle with hazard lights; however, you should always assume passing drivers may not see you.
