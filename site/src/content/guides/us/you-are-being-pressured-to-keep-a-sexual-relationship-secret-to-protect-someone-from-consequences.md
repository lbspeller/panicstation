# What to do if… you are being pressured to keep a sexual relationship secret to protect someone from consequences (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer pause and contact specialist support from a phone, device, or location the other person cannot access. You do not have to stay silent to protect someone who is pressuring you.

## Do not do these things
- Do not meet them alone to explain, negotiate, or clear things up if you feel intimidated or unsafe.
- Do not send an apology, agreement, or reassurance solely because you feel pressured.
- Do not confront them or make a sudden contact change if that could increase risk.
- Do not assume the situation cannot involve coercion because you were dating, agreed before, or do not have visible injuries.
- Do not pressure yourself to choose a label or make a report now.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause first.** Move away from the person if you safely can. Go to a trusted person, a neighbor, or a public place. Call **911** if you are in immediate danger.
2. **Use a safer phone or device if your activity may be monitored.** Contact the **RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline** by calling **800-656-HOPE (4673)**, texting **HOPE** to **64673**, or using online chat. You can ask about privacy limits before sharing identifying details.
3. **Use relationship-abuse support if the secrecy is part of ongoing control or fear.** Contact the **National Domestic Violence Hotline** by calling **1-800-799-SAFE (7233)**, texting **START** to **88788**, or using live chat.
4. **Tell one safe person.** You can say: “I am being pressured to keep a sexual relationship secret to protect the other person. I need support. Please do not contact them.”
5. **Reduce contact in the safest way available.** This may mean leaving messages unanswered, muting notifications, staying with someone you trust, or blocking contact only when that feels safe.
6. **Keep existing messages only if it feels safe and useful.** If you choose to keep them, store them somewhere the other person cannot access; you do not need to collect anything or make a detailed record now.
7. **Seek medical care promptly if you are injured, feel unwell, or have concerns about pregnancy or sexually transmitted infections.** Use an emergency department for urgent symptoms or injuries.
8. **If you are under 18, contact the Childhelp National Child Abuse Hotline.** Call **800-422-4453**, text **GO** to **800-422-4453**, or use live chat. If the other person has power over you and you are an adult, tell RAINN or the National Domestic Violence Hotline so they can help you consider safer next steps.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether to report anything.
- You do not need to decide what label fits the situation.
- You do not need to confront the other person.
- You do not need to make major decisions about work, school, housing, or the relationship during this first safe pause.

## Important reassurance
Pressure to protect the other person from consequences does not create an obligation to stay silent. Feeling conflicted, scared, numb, guilty, or unsure is understandable. Contacting support does not commit you to reporting or to any other action.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist support from an advocate who understands your situation and local options.

## Important note
This guide provides general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If contacting support could put you at risk, use a safer phone, device, or location first.
