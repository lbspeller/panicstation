# What to do if… you think someone may have put a tracking device in your bag, car, or belongings (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer public place and avoid going straight home or anywhere predictable. If you feel in danger right now, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t drive straight home to check it there or go to a private, isolated place.
- Don’t confront the person you suspect or message them about it.
- Don’t post about it publicly or share your location in real time.
- Don’t immediately smash, throw away, or plant the device somewhere else.
- Don’t do a long search in a secluded area; prioritize being around people.
- Don’t turn off your phone’s tracker alerts while you are still trying to understand what happened.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause.** Go into a busy, staffed place such as a store, café, hotel lobby, or police station lobby. If you are driving, park somewhere public, well-lit, and near people or cameras.

2. **If you feel in immediate danger, call 911.** Tell the dispatcher you believe you may be being tracked, where you are now, and that you do not feel safe.

3. **If it is not an emergency, contact local law enforcement through a non-emergency route.** Ask how they want you to handle a suspected tracking device and ask for an incident or report number if they make a report.

4. **Avoid revealing your next private destination.** If you need somewhere to go now, choose a public place you do not usually visit or ask law enforcement where to go. Do not lead anyone to your home, workplace, school, or another predictable place.

5. **Use your phone to check for unknown-tracker information, if you can do this safely.**
   - **iPhone or iPad:** If you received an alert, tap it and follow the on-screen options, such as map details, **Play Sound**, **Find Nearby**, or disable instructions if offered. To look for recent alerts, open **Find My**, tap **Items**, then scroll down for **Unknown Items Detected with You** if shown.
   - **Android:** If you received an alert, tap it and follow the on-screen options, such as the map, **Play sound**, **Find nearby** if offered, and next steps. On many Android 12-or-newer phones, you can also open **Settings**, tap **Safety & emergency**, tap **Unknown tracker alerts**, then tap **Scan now**. On Android 11 or lower, the path may be **Settings**, **Google**, **Personal & device safety**, then **Unknown tracker alerts**. If those paths are not available, search Settings for **unknown tracker alerts**.

6. **Do a quick, safe check of obvious hiding places only if you are somewhere safe.**
   - **Bag or belongings:** Check outer pockets, lining gaps, clipped-on keyrings, under a base insert, inside rarely used compartments, and inside luggage pockets.
   - **Car:** Check under seats, the center console, seat-back pockets, door pockets, the trunk area near the spare tire, and for any plug-in device in the diagnostic port, often under the dashboard.
   - Stop the search if you feel exposed, rushed, watched, or unsafe.

7. **If you find a device, prioritize safety and document what you can.**
   - Take photos of the device where it was found, including how it was attached or hidden.
   - Write down the time, date, exact place, and what led you to find it.
   - If your phone shows a serial number, device identifier, map, owner information, or disable instructions, take screenshots.
   - If you need to stop tracking immediately for safety, follow the device’s own disable instructions if you can do that calmly.
   - If you do not need to disable it immediately, avoid handling it more than necessary and ask law enforcement how they want you to preserve or bring it in.

8. **Save digital evidence somewhere the suspected person cannot access.** Screenshot notifications, scan results, maps, serial numbers, and identifier screens. Store copies with a trusted person or in an account the suspected person does not know about and cannot access.

9. **If this may be connected to stalking or a controlling relationship, contact confidential support from a safer device if possible.** The National Domestic Violence Hotline or a local victim advocate can help you think through safer communication, account access, phone safety, and next steps without pressuring you to report.

## What can wait
- You do not need to dismantle your car or complete a forensic search right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to move, change jobs, change schools, or take major legal steps.
- You do not need to prove exactly who did it before taking safety steps.
- A deeper inspection by a trusted mechanic, installer, or other qualified person can wait until you are safer and supported.
- Longer-term safety planning can wait until you have had a safer pause.

## Important reassurance
You do not need perfect certainty to act. Taking calm safety steps, documenting what you see, and getting help is appropriate even if the alert or device later has an innocent explanation.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on immediate safety, avoiding irreversible mistakes, and buying time. If this is connected to stalking, harassment, theft, or relationship-based abuse, later decisions may need help from a victim advocate, local law enforcement, a lawyer, or another specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice.
