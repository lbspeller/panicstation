# What to do if… your flight is delayed enough that your planned onward transport may no longer work (USA)

## Short answer
Obtain your **confirmed arrival time**, then immediately contact your onward provider (train, bus, or car rental) to **prevent a no-show cancellation** before attempting to rebook.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t book a new, non-refundable onward ticket until your delayed flight is either boarded or in the air, as delay times often shift multiple times.
- Don’t assume a car rental or train will "wait" for you automatically, even if you provided a flight number during booking.
- Don’t accept a travel voucher or credit if you are entitled to a cash refund and haven't decided your next move.
- Don’t delete airline notifications, text alerts, or emails, as these serve as your proof of delay for insurance or refunds.
- Don’t forget to check if your onward transport (e.g., Amtrak) has its own "service disruption" policy that allows for free changes due to connecting delays.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm the actual estimated arrival time and screenshot it.** **Open your airline’s app or website to find the specific "Estimated Time of Arrival" (ETA) rather than just the departure time.** Save this screenshot immediately, as apps often stop showing delay details once the flight eventually lands.

2. **Determine if your onward travel is "protected" or "separate."** **If your onward leg is on the same ticket (same confirmation number), the airline is responsible for rebooking you to your final destination.** If they are separate tickets (e.g., a flight followed by a separate Amtrak or Greyhound booking), you are responsible for the logistics and costs of the second leg.

3. **Contact the onward provider to prevent a "no-show" cancellation.** **Call or use the app for your car rental, train, or bus to inform them of the delay.**
   - For car rentals: Most desks only hold cars for 2–16 hours; ask them to keep the reservation active for a late-night or next-day pickup.
   - For trains/buses: Check if you can "cancel for credit" or "change departure" now to avoid forfeiting the entire fare once the vehicle departs.

4. **Verify your eligibility for a mandatory refund.** **Under DOT rules, you are entitled to a full refund in your original form of payment if your flight is "significantly changed" and you choose not to travel.** 
   - A significant change is defined as an arrival or departure time shift of 3+ hours (domestic) or 6+ hours (international).
   - This right applies regardless of the reason for the delay (weather, air traffic, or maintenance).

5. **Request specific "controllable delay" amenities if applicable.** **If the delay is due to the airline (maintenance or staffing), ask the gate agent for meal vouchers (usually provided for 3+ hour waits) or hotel vouchers for overnight stays.** Refer to the DOT Airline Cancellation and Delay Dashboard if the agent claims these services are not offered.

6. **Exercise your tarmac rights if you are stuck on the aircraft.** **Airlines must provide food and water no later than 2 hours after a tarmac delay begins.** They must also allow you to deplane before the 3-hour mark (domestic) or 4-hour mark (international), unless safety or air traffic control prevents it.

7. **Identify the "last service" time for ground transport.** **Check the schedule for the final bus, train, or shuttle of the night at your destination airport.** If your flight's ETA is within 45 minutes of the last service, start researching airport-adjacent hotels now rather than waiting until you land and find yourself stranded.

8. **Maintain a timestamped log of all expenses.** **Save every receipt for food, water, hotels, or new transport required by the delay.** If you paid with a major credit card, check your benefits guide for "Trip Delay Reimbursement," which often covers up to $500 in expenses for delays over 6–12 hours.

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a formal DOT complaint while at the airport; you have months to do this later.
- You do not need to decide on a long-term "compensation" claim today; your focus is on the next 12 hours of travel.
- You do not need to argue with gate agents about specific laws if they are busy with rebooking; take their names, keep your receipts, and handle the dispute via corporate customer service later.

## Important reassurance
Managing a missed connection is a logistical task, not a personal failure. While it is frustrating to lose time, focusing on one step—first the arrival time, then the onward notification—prevents the situation from becoming more expensive. Grounding yourself in the fact that your refund rights are federally protected can help reduce the pressure to make an immediate, perfect decision.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for travelers facing disrupted onward connections in the USA. It covers immediate airline interactions and protective steps for ground transport. Long-term legal claims or international "EU 261" style compensation are outside this scope.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Airline policies and DOT enforcement may change. Refund eligibility depends on you choosing not to travel on the disrupted flight or any alternative offered. For car rentals and separate transit bookings, your rights are governed by the specific terms and conditions of those individual providers.
