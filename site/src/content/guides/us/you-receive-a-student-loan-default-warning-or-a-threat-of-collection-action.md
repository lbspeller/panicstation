# What to do if… you receive a student loan default warning or a threat of collection action (USA)

## Short answer
Do not panic-pay a collector. First confirm whether the loan is federal or private using an official record, then contact the official servicer, loan holder, or collector and ask what action can stop the immediate escalation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send money to someone who contacted you out of the blue before you confirm the debt is real, yours, and being handled by the right organisation.
- Do not share your FSA ID password, one-time codes, bank login, or debit card details with an unfamiliar caller.
- Do not agree on the phone to a payment plan you cannot maintain just to make the threat stop.
- Do not refinance, consolidate, rehabilitate, or sign new paperwork under pressure before checking the loan type and the official route.
- Do not ignore an official notice of intent about wage garnishment or tax refund offset; the deadline on the notice may matter.
- Do not assume every student loan collector is acting for the government; scam and unauthorised collection attempts can happen.

## What to do now
1. **Save the notice and capture the essentials.** Keep the envelope, email, text, or voicemail. Write down the sender name, account or reference numbers, the action threatened, the amount claimed, the date on the notice, and any deadline.

2. **Work out whether the loan is federal or private.**
   - For federal loans, log in to https://studentaid.gov and check the loan status, current servicer, and whether the loan has been assigned to the Default Resolution Group.
   - For private loans, check the lender’s own portal, your old loan documents, and your credit reports. Do not rely only on what a caller says.

3. **Use only verified contact routes.** Call or message the servicer, loan holder, or Default Resolution Group using contact details from StudentAid.gov, MyEDDebt, the lender’s official website, or the written notice after you have checked it. Do not use a phone number from an unexpected text or email until you have verified it.

4. **If a debt collector contacted you, ask for validation in writing.**
   - Debt collectors generally must give validation information, usually during the first contact or within five days.
   - The validation information should include the creditor name, the amount, and how to dispute the debt.
   - If you dispute the debt in writing within 30 days after receiving the validation information, the collector must stop collection until they send verification.
   - If the collector cannot provide basic validation information, treat it as a scam risk and do not pay through that contact.

5. **If the federal loan is delinquent but not yet in default, ask what stops default.** Contact the official servicer and ask whether a deferment, forbearance, an available income-driven or other repayment plan, or updated income documentation can stop escalation for your loan type.

6. **If the federal loan is already in default, ask for the official default-resolution options.** The usual routes are rehabilitation, consolidation, or repayment in full. Ask the loan holder or Default Resolution Group which options apply to your loan and what first step is needed today.

7. **If federal wage garnishment or offset is threatened, check the current federal collections status as well as the notice deadline.** As of this review, the U.S. Department of Education has announced a temporary delay of federal involuntary collections, including Administrative Wage Garnishment and the Treasury Offset Program, while repayment changes are being implemented. Still read the notice carefully, because deadlines and hearing rights may still matter.

8. **If federal administrative wage garnishment is specifically threatened, read the hearing instructions before doing anything else.** A federal administrative wage garnishment notice may give you 30 days from the date it was sent to request a hearing. A timely hearing request may pause garnishment until after the hearing process, but it does not by itself resolve the default.

9. **If tax refund or federal payment offset is threatened, verify the loan holder and ask what can still be done.** Use the official federal loan holder or MyEDDebt route. Ask whether any review, dispute, repayment arrangement, or default-resolution step is available before the offset happens.

10. **Escalate if the account looks wrong or you are being bounced around.**
   - For unresolved federal student aid problems, use the Federal Student Aid Feedback Center or FSA Ombudsman Group.
   - For debt collection, servicing, or credit reporting problems, you can submit a CFPB complaint after trying the company or if the collection conduct seems wrong.

11. **Keep a paper trail from this point on.** Save screenshots of official loan status, keep a call log with dates and names, and follow up important points in writing when possible.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to settle the whole balance.
- You do not need to take out new credit, refinance, or pay a third-party debt relief company to make this go away.
- You do not need to solve forgiveness, credit repair, or long-term repayment strategy right now.
- You can wait to gather every old document; first confirm the loan type, the official holder, the current collection status, and the deadline on the notice.

## Important reassurance
A default warning or collection notice is serious, but it is also a process with records, contact routes, and possible next steps. One verified action, such as checking StudentAid.gov, requesting validation, or responding to a garnishment notice, can slow the panic and reduce the chance of paying the wrong person.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next hours or days to verify the debt, avoid scams, protect deadlines, and enter a legitimate process. Later decisions about repayment, settlement, disputes, credit reporting, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. Federal and private student loans follow different rules, and collection options can depend on the loan type, notice, state law, and account history. If the debt may be wrong, the collector may be unauthorised, or a deadline is close, prioritise verification and written records before paying.
