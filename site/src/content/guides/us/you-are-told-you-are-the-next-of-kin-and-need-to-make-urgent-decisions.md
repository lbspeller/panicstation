# What to do if… you are told you are the next of kin and need to make urgent decisions (USA)
## Short answer
Ask what exact decision is needed now, what happens if it waits, and which document or state process the provider is relying on. Make only the smallest necessary decision until you know whether this concerns current care, organ donation, body release, or burial or cremation after death.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume that being called “next of kin” automatically gives you authority for every decision.
- Do not sign an authorization you have not read or do not understand, especially for an irreversible step such as cremation.
- Do not try to resolve every family disagreement before asking what decision genuinely cannot wait.
- Do not agree to large personal expenses in a rush if you are unsure what is required.
- Do not discard documents, messages, or paperwork that may show the person’s wishes.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the immediate request.**  
   Ask:
   - “What exact decision are you asking for?”
   - “Does this concern current medical care, organ donation, release of the body, or funeral arrangements?”
   - “What happens if the decision waits?”
   - “What document or state process are you relying on to identify the decision-maker?”
   - “Can you send the request and any authorization form in writing?”

2. **If the person is alive, speak to the care team before doing anything else.**  
   Ask whether the person can make the decision themselves, whether an advance directive or health care proxy is available, and what decision needs an answer now. Ask for a plain-language explanation and a brief pause where medically possible.

3. **If the person has died, ask what affects the next practical step.**  
   Ask the hospital, facility, or other provider:
   - “Is a medical examiner or coroner involved?”
   - “Does that affect release of the body or timing?”
   - “Who is the release contact?”
   
   If you are arranging a transfer, ask the funeral home what authorization and documents it needs before any irreversible choice is made.

4. **Do a brief search for the most relevant paperwork.**  
   Look for:
   - an advance directive or health care proxy if the person is alive;
   - a document specifically naming an agent for disposition after death;
   - written wishes about burial or cremation;
   - prepaid funeral or cremation plan paperwork.

5. **If an organ-donation organization contacts you, ask what is already recorded.**  
   Ask:
   - “Is there recorded donor authorization?”
   - “What decisions, if any, remain for me?”
   - “What information do you need from me now?”
   
   Ask the donation organization to explain the next step rather than trying to interpret donor-registration rules yourself.

6. **If funeral arrangements are being discussed, separate transfer from final choices where possible.**  
   Ask whether transfer into a funeral home’s care can be arranged before deciding burial or cremation. If you are discussing services or costs, ask the funeral home for price information and choose only what is needed now.

7. **If relatives disagree, ask providers to pause irreversible steps while authority is checked.**  
   Say: “There is a disagreement. Please note the file and tell us what authorization and documents you need before any irreversible step.”

8. **Use one factual family message.**  
   Send: “I am confirming the immediate decision, the paperwork, and who is authorized. Please send photos of any written wishes or relevant documents you have. I will share confirmed information when I have it.”

9. **Ask how to obtain death certificates, but do not order a large batch in a rush.**  
   If the person has died, ask the funeral home or relevant office how certified copies are ordered in the state where the death occurred. Before ordering more copies, ask whether each immediate task needs a certified copy or whether a photocopy is enough.

10. **If a home is unattended after a death, handle only immediate practical risks.**  
   If appropriate and safe, secure keys, pets, and the property. Leave sorting belongings and paperwork for later.

## What can wait
- Full funeral or memorial details, such as music, readings, guest lists, and obituary wording, can usually wait.
- Estate administration, wills, account closures, and most paperwork do not need to be completed today.
- Ordering a large number of death-certificate copies can wait until you know which tasks require certified copies.
- Family debates about every later decision can wait while you establish the immediate request and the relevant authority.
- Sorting belongings, devices, files, and the home can wait unless there is an immediate safety or security issue.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to ask for the request in writing, ask what truly cannot wait, and take a brief pause where possible. You do not need to solve every practical, financial, or family issue before making the smallest necessary decision.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. State law, medical circumstances, and local procedures vary. A later decision may need help from the relevant provider or a qualified local professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Ask the care team, funeral home, medical examiner or coroner office, organ-donation organization, or other relevant provider what documents and process apply to the specific decision.
