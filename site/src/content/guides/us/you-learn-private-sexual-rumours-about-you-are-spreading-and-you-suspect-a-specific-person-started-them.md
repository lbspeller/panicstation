# What to do if… you learn private sexual rumours about you are spreading and you suspect a specific person started them (USA)

## Short answer
Do not confront the suspected person directly or respond to the rumors publicly. Secure evidence of the spread privately and use established reporting channels at your school, workplace, or on the digital platform to isolate the behavior.

## Do not do these things
- Do not post "receipts" or public call-outs of the suspected person, as this often broadens the audience for the rumor.
- Do not engage in back-and-forth arguments in comments or group chats where your reactions can be screenshotted and redistributed.
- Do not forward the rumors to others to "show them what is being said," as this increases the digital footprint and circulation.
- Do not delete the original messages or posts if you intend to report them; instead, take screenshots and then hide or block the content.
- Do not make threats or post the suspected person’s private contact information.

## What to do now
1. **Preserve evidence immediately and quietly.** **Save screenshots of the rumors, including timestamps, URLs, usernames, and the specific platform or group where they appeared.**
   - Ensure the screenshots show the context of the conversation if possible.
   - Store these in a secure, password-protected folder or cloud drive.
2. **Contact a confidential support service.** **Call or chat with the National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN) at 800-656-HOPE for confidential guidance on navigating harassment.**
   - They can help you process the situation without triggering a formal report until you are ready.
3. **Use platform-level reporting and privacy tools.** **Report the specific posts or messages for "harassment" or "sexual content" using the platform’s internal tools.**
   - Block the accounts involved in spreading the rumor.
   - Adjust your account privacy settings to "private" or "friends only" to prevent further monitoring by those outside your trusted circle.
4. **Identify the correct official contact if this involves a school.** **Ask for the Title IX Coordinator and request an immediate meeting to discuss "supportive measures."**
   - You are entitled to supportive measures (such as no-contact orders or class changes) even if you do not file a formal complaint.
   - Clarify that the current governing standard is the 2020 Title IX framework, which requires schools to address "severe and pervasive" sex-based harassment.
5. **Report the harassment to HR if this occurs in a workplace.** **State clearly that "sexual rumors are creating a hostile work environment" and request an immediate, confidential response.**
   - Ask for a written copy of the company's anti-harassment policy.
   - Request interim protections, such as a change in seating, shift adjustments, or a directive for the suspected person to stop communication.
6. **Check for "Image-Based Abuse" or non-consensual imagery.** **If the rumors involve the sharing of private photos or videos, contact the Cyber Civil Rights Initiative (CCRI) at 866-472-3584.**
   - They provide specific technical and legal resources for removing non-consensual intimate imagery from the internet.
7. **Contact law enforcement if you feel physically unsafe.** **Call 911 if you are being followed, threatened with violence, or if the suspected person is appearing at your home or work.**
   - For non-emergencies involving stalking or persistent harassment, contact your local police department's non-emergency line to document the pattern of behavior.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact "source" of the rumor before taking steps to stop the current spread.
- You do not need to provide details about your actual sexual history to "disprove" the rumors.
- You do not need to decide on a civil lawsuit (defamation) today; statutes of limitations usually allow months or years for such decisions.
- You do not need to check every social media platform to see if the rumor has moved; focus only on the platforms you currently use.

## Important reassurance
The spread of sexual rumors is a recognized form of sex-based harassment, not a reflection of your character. It is normal to feel a sense of extreme urgency or shame, but these feelings are often the intended effect of the person spreading the gossip. Using formal channels to contain the situation is a proactive way to regain control.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for managing the immediate spread of rumors and accessing initial support. It does not cover the long-term adjudication of Title IX cases, civil litigation, or criminal prosecution.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not legal or clinical advice. Regulatory frameworks for Title IX and EEOC guidance changed in 2025 and 2026; always verify current institutional policies with a qualified professional or advocate.
