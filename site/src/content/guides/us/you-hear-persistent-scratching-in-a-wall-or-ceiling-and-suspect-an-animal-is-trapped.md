# What to do if… you hear persistent scratching in a wall or ceiling and suspect an animal is trapped (USA)

## Short answer
Do not seal openings or cut into the wall or ceiling. Keep people and pets away from the noisy area, then contact local animal control or a wildlife control operator who is licensed or permitted if your state requires it. Treat any possible bat contact as a public-health issue.

## Do not do these things
- Do not block vents, gaps, soffit openings, chimney flues, or exterior entry points while the animal may still be inside.
- Do not put poison, smoke, bug bombs, or other fumes into walls, ceilings, vents, attics, or crawlspaces.
- Do not cut inspection holes unless a professional has told you exactly where and how to do it safely.
- Do not try to grab, corner, or chase the animal if it enters the living space.
- Do not handle bats or other wildlife with bare hands.
- If a bat might be involved, do not release it until you have spoken to animal control, your local health department, or your state health department.

## What to do now
1. **Make the area safer.** Move children and pets out of the room below or beside the noise. Close the room door if there is one. Stay out of attics, crawlspaces, ceiling voids, and tight utility spaces while you are unsure what is inside.

2. **Do a quick check without opening the wall or ceiling.**
   - Note where the sound is loudest.
   - Note whether it is mainly daytime, nighttime, or constant.
   - Look from a safe distance for droppings, nesting material, stains, smells, loose vents, damaged soffits, roofline gaps, or a missing chimney cap.
   - Look outside near that area for possible entry points, but do not seal them yet.

3. **Treat possible bats as a priority.** If you saw a bat indoors, found a bat in a room with someone asleep, found a bat near a child, found a bat near anyone who cannot clearly say whether contact happened, or think a person or pet may have been bitten, scratched, or touched:
   - Keep people and pets out of that room.
   - Close the door if you can do that without getting close to the bat.
   - Call local animal control or your local or state health department for safe capture and rabies-testing guidance.
   - Contact a healthcare professional promptly if anyone may have had contact.

4. **Call the right local help for a wall or ceiling animal.**
   - Call your city or county animal control office and ask who handles wildlife trapped in buildings.
   - If animal control does not handle it, ask for the usual local route for wildlife in walls, ceilings, attics, vents, or chimneys.
   - If you hire help, look for a wildlife control operator who handles building exclusion, humane removal, entry-point finding, and timing for safe sealing.

5. **If the animal enters the living space and it is not a bat, reduce contact.**
   - Keep your distance.
   - Close interior doors to confine it to one room if you can do so safely.
   - Open an exterior door or window only if you can do this without getting close or cornering it.
   - If the animal seems aggressive, injured, unusually disoriented, or you feel unsafe, leave the area and call animal control.

6. **If the sound stops but there is odor, flies, fluid staining, or a strong smell, assume there may be a dead animal.** Do not tear into the wall yourself. Call wildlife control, pest control, or a qualified contractor who can advise on safe access, removal, and cleanup.

7. **Avoid disturbing droppings or contaminated material while you wait.** Do not sweep, vacuum, or stir up droppings, nesting material, or insulation debris. Keep pets away from those areas.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact species right now.
- You do not need to decide on permanent repairs today.
- You do not need to buy traps, repellents, ultrasonic devices, or poison right now.
- You do not need to seal the outside openings until you know the animal is out or a professional has set a safe exclusion plan.
- You do not need to cut holes just to prove something is there.

## Important reassurance
Persistent scratching is unsettling, but a rushed repair can trap the animal, spread contamination, or make the damage worse. Pausing, keeping people and pets away, and getting local wildlife help is usually the safest first move.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about repairs, exclusion work, cleanup, insurance, tenant-landlord responsibilities, or medical follow-up may need help from a qualified local professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, public-health, veterinary, legal, pest-control, building, or professional wildlife-control advice. Local rules, protected-species laws, animal-control services, and wildlife-control licensing vary by state, county, and city. If a bat might be involved, follow public-health guidance and do not release the bat before speaking with animal control or a health department.
