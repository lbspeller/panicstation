# What to do if… you hear persistent scratching in a wall or ceiling and suspect an animal is trapped (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the area immediately and contact a licensed wildlife control operator for professional removal. If a bat is found in a room where people were sleeping, you must capture it for rabies testing rather than releasing it.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t seal any exterior gaps, vents, or holes until a professional confirms the animal is removed.
- Don’t use poison or chemical fumigants, as this often results in animals dying in inaccessible structural cavities.
- Don’t attempt to handle wildlife, especially bats, with bare hands or without professional-grade personal protective equipment.
- Don’t release a bat found in a bedroom or near a child until you have spoken to public health officials.
- Don’t perform "DIY" exclusion during maternity seasons (typically May through August) as it may be illegal to trap dependent young inside.

## What to do now
1. **Evacuate and isolate the immediate area.** Move all residents and domestic pets to a different room and close the doors to the affected area.
2. **Identify sensory cues and timing without investigating structural cavities.** Note whether the scratching is rhythmic, vocal, or occurs primarily at night (suggesting nocturnal species like raccoons or bats) or during the day (suggesting squirrels).
3. **Check for "unseen" bat exposure protocols.** **If a bat is discovered in a room where someone was sleeping, where a child was unattended, or where a pet was present, do not release it.**
   - Contact your local health department or animal control immediately to arrange for safe capture and rabies testing.
   - If the bat must be captured before help arrives, use heavy leather gloves and follow a "container and cardboard" method to secure it without direct contact.
4. **Locate a licensed Wildlife Control Operator (WCO).** **Contact a specialist rather than a general pest control exterminator.**
   - Request a "humane exclusion" assessment that includes identifying all entry points.
   - Verify that the operator is licensed by the state for wildlife handling and has liability insurance.
5. **Monitor exterior entry points from a safe distance.** **Observe the roofline, soffits, and vents from the ground to see if animals are entering or exiting.**
   - Do not use a ladder to inspect entry points while animals may be active, as they may charge or defend the opening.
6. **Manage potential carcasses if activity ceases.** **If you detect a strong, decaying odor or an influx of flies, treat the situation as a biohazard.**
   - Avoid touching the area or attempting to cut into the wall to remove a dead animal yourself.
   - Professional services are required to handle the carcass and the subsequent decontamination of nesting material.

## What can wait
- Identifying the exact species or number of animals is not required for the initial call to a professional.
- Permanent structural repairs and sealing of entry points must wait until a professional confirms the space is vacant.
- Cleanup of attic insulation or nesting debris can wait until the animal is gone and the area is professionally decontaminated.

## Important reassurance
Hearing animals inside your walls is distressing, but taking a moment to call a professional prevents the risk of an animal dying in an unreachable spot or a rabies exposure going untested. Most wildlife issues are resolvable through non-destructive, humane exclusion methods.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and safety instructions for suspected animal entrapment. It does not cover long-term exclusion strategies, structural repairs, or medical treatment for bites.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not medical, veterinary, or legal advice. Bat exposures carry a significant risk of rabies and must be handled according to local public health department protocols. Federal and state laws protect various wildlife species; unauthorized removal or killing may result in legal penalties.
