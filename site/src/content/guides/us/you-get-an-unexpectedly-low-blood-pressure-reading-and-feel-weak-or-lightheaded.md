# What to do if… you get an unexpectedly low blood pressure reading and feel weak or lightheaded (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all activity immediately and lie flat on your back with your legs elevated above the level of your heart. If you lose consciousness, have chest pain, or symptoms do not improve within two minutes of lying down, call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to walk, climb stairs, or "walk it off" while feeling lightheaded.
- Do not drive or operate any machinery until symptoms have fully resolved.
- Do not take a hot shower or bath, as heat can further lower blood pressure.
- Do not take extra doses of any medication to try to stabilize the reading.
- Do not stand up quickly from a sitting or lying position.
- Do not eat a large meal or consume alcohol while feeling symptomatic.

## What to do now
1. **Get into a safe physical position to prevent a fall.**
   - **Lie down flat on your back immediately** on a floor, rug, or bed.
   - **Elevate your feet and legs** about 12 inches using pillows, a chair, or a cushion to help blood flow to the brain.
   - If you cannot lie down, **sit on the floor and lean forward**, but lying flat with elevated legs is the preferred safety position.
2. **Alert someone in your immediate vicinity.**
   - **Call out to a family member or neighbor** if someone is nearby.
   - **Keep your phone within reach** in case you need to call for emergency services.
3. **Check for emergency warning signs (Call 911 if present).**
   - **Call 911 immediately if you faint** and do not regain consciousness within 60 seconds.
   - **Call 911 if you experience chest pain**, severe shortness of breath, sudden confusion, or difficulty speaking.
   - **Call 911 if your skin feels cold and clammy**, you have a very rapid or weak pulse, or you appear extremely pale.
   - **Call 911 if your symptoms do not improve** or if they worsen after two minutes of resting with your legs elevated.
4. **Hydrate slowly if you are fully alert.**
   - **Take small sips of water** if you have been sweating, are in a hot environment, or have been ill.
   - **Avoid drinking anything** if you feel nauseated, are drowsy, or are having trouble swallowing.
5. **Wait at least five minutes before re-checking your reading.**
   - **Stay in a rested, seated, or lying position** for five full minutes before taking another measurement.
   - **Ensure the blood pressure cuff is placed correctly** on a bare arm at heart level.
   - **Limit yourself to one follow-up reading** to avoid increasing anxiety or "spiraling."
6. **Identify and remove immediate environmental triggers.**
   - **Turn off space heaters** or move to a cooler, well-ventilated area if the room is hot.
   - **Loosen any tight clothing**, such as belts, ties, or restrictive collars.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the exact cause (e.g., dehydration vs. medication) right now.
- You do not need to schedule a long-term follow-up appointment in this exact moment.
- You do not need to review your entire medical history or medication list until you are physically stable.
- You do not need to worry about one-off low readings if you are otherwise asymptomatic and the symptoms resolve with rest.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of panic or "doom" when feeling faint or seeing a low blood pressure number. Most episodes of lightheadedness are temporary and caused by simple factors like standing up too fast, mild dehydration, or being in a hot room. Lying down is a highly effective way to stabilize your circulation and prevent injury from a fall.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute episode of symptomatic low blood pressure. It is not a substitute for clinical diagnosis or chronic management of hypotension.

## Important note
Low blood pressure (hypotension) is generally defined as a reading below 90/60 mmHg, but numbers only matter if they occur with symptoms. If you experience fainting, chest pain, or signs of shock (cold/clammy skin, rapid pulse), seek immediate emergency medical care by calling 911.
