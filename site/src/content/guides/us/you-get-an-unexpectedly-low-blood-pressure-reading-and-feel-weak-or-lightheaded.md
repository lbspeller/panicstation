# What to do if… you get an unexpectedly low blood pressure reading and feel weak or lightheaded (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a fainting risk: sit or lie down safely now, and do not stand back up until you feel steady. Call 911 if you faint, have chest pain, severe trouble breathing, new confusion, stroke-like symptoms, heavy bleeding, or rapidly worsening weakness.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep standing, walking around, or trying to “shake it off.”
- Do not drive, operate machinery, climb, shower, or bathe while you feel lightheaded.
- Do not take extra doses of prescribed blood pressure or heart medicines to “correct” the number.
- Do not stop prescribed medicine suddenly unless a clinician tells you to.
- Do not drink a large amount all at once if you feel nauseated; use small sips only if you are fully awake and can swallow safely.
- Do not dismiss serious symptoms because the monitor might be inaccurate.

## What to do now
1. **Get low and still to prevent a fall.**  
   If you may faint, lie on your back and raise your legs on a chair or pillows. If you cannot lie down, sit and lean forward with your head lowered and supported.

2. **Keep help within reach.**  
   If you are alone, call or text someone nearby to check on you. Keep your phone with you and unlock the door if it is safe to do so.

3. **Call 911 now if any emergency warning sign is present.**  
   Call if you faint and do not wake within about 1 minute, are hard to wake, or are not breathing normally. Also call for chest pain or pressure, severe shortness of breath, new confusion, face drooping, arm weakness, speech trouble, heavy bleeding, very pale or clammy skin with extreme weakness, or symptoms that worsen, return, or do not clearly improve after a few minutes lying down.

4. **If you are fully awake and can swallow, take small sips of water.**  
   This may help if you have been hot, sweating, sick, vomiting, not eating or drinking normally, or standing for a long time. Do not take anything by mouth if you are confused, very drowsy, choking, or actively vomiting.

5. **Remove obvious current triggers.**  
   Stop any hot shower or bath, alcohol, intense activity, overheating, or rapid standing. Loosen tight clothing around your neck or waist. Stay seated or lying down until the faint feeling has passed.

6. **Re-check the reading only after you are steadier.**  
   Rest quietly for about 5 minutes. Put the cuff on bare skin, keep your arm supported at heart level, keep your feet flat if sitting, and do not talk during the reading. Re-check once or twice, then stop repeated checking and pay more attention to how you feel.

7. **Get medical advice today if this is new, unexplained, or not clearly settling.**  
   Contact your clinician’s office, nurse line, telehealth service, or urgent care if you still feel weak or lightheaded after rest and small sips of fluid, if the low readings continue, or if symptoms keep coming back. Treat it as higher priority if you have heart disease, are pregnant, recently started or changed a medicine, take diuretics or blood pressure medicine, have had vomiting or diarrhea, or fainted.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the cause right now.
- You do not need to keep measuring your blood pressure over and over.
- You do not need to decide whether this means a long-term health problem.
- You do not need to change your prescribed medicines in the moment unless a clinician gives you urgent instructions.

## Important reassurance
A low reading can be alarming, especially when you feel weak or lightheaded. Many episodes are related to temporary factors such as heat, dehydration, illness, standing quickly, or medicine effects, but the safest first step is to prevent a fall and watch for emergency warning signs.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, to help you stabilise the moment and decide whether to seek urgent care. Later decisions, including medicine changes or testing for repeated episodes, may need help from a medical professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, or a substitute for professional care. If you faint, have chest pain, severe trouble breathing, stroke-like symptoms, severe confusion, heavy bleeding, or rapidly worsening weakness, call 911.
