# What to do if… you get alerts that someone is trying to recover your account (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible account takeover attempt. Do not reply to the alert or share any code; open the provider’s official app or website yourself and secure the account recovery details, password, MFA, and active sessions.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click links, scan QR codes, or call numbers from the alert message; use the official app or type the known website address yourself.
- Don’t share MFA codes, 2FA codes, backup codes, password reset links, or verification numbers with anyone, including someone claiming to be support.
- Don’t approve a login, password reset, account recovery request, or MFA prompt that you did not start.
- Don’t install remote-access, screen-sharing, or device-management apps because someone says they are helping you secure the account.
- Don’t disable MFA to stop repeated prompts.
- Don’t move money, buy gift cards, send cryptocurrency, or approve transfers because someone says it will protect an account.
- Don’t assume it is safe because you can still log in; recovery attempts can happen before a lockout.

## What to do now
1. Open the real service safely: use the official app, a saved bookmark you trust, or a website address you type yourself.
2. Change the password on the targeted account:
   - Use a unique password you have not used anywhere else.
   - If that password was reused, next secure your email, financial accounts, and any other account using the same password.
3. Check the account recovery settings:
   - Look at recovery email addresses, phone numbers, backup contacts, and backup recovery methods.
   - Remove anything you do not recognize.
   - Re-confirm the email address and phone number that are yours.
4. Sign out other sessions:
   - Use “sign out of all devices,” “log out everywhere,” or similar wording if the provider offers it.
   - Review recent devices, locations, and connected apps.
   - Revoke any app, browser extension, device, or third-party access you do not recognize.
5. Strengthen MFA:
   - Keep MFA on.
   - If the provider offers a stronger option, such as an authenticator app, passkey, or security key, use that instead of only SMS when you can.
   - Regenerate backup codes if the provider offers them, then store them somewhere safer than your email inbox.
6. Secure your email account because it is often the recovery key:
   - Change the email password if you have any doubt.
   - Check for forwarding rules, filters, mail delegation, connected apps, or recovery details you did not add.
   - Remove suspicious rules or access.
7. Contact official support only from inside the real app or website:
   - Tell them you are receiving account recovery attempts.
   - Ask whether they can add extra verification, a temporary lock on recovery changes, or a review of recent support contacts.
8. If the targeted account has saved payment methods or is a bank, card, payroll, benefits, crypto, or money-transfer account:
   - Contact the bank, card issuer, or financial provider using the number on your card or the official app.
   - Ask them to review recent activity and add stronger verification for transfers and profile changes.
9. If you shared a code, allowed remote access, lost money, personal information was misused, or you are locked out:
   - Use the provider’s official recovery process first.
   - Report fraud to the FTC.
   - Use IdentityTheft.gov if personal information was misused.
   - File an IC3 complaint for cyber-enabled fraud or account takeover, especially where financial loss or attempted financial access is involved.

## What can wait
- You do not need to catch the person, argue with them, or keep replying to gather information.
- You do not need to update every account at once; prioritize email, the targeted account, financial accounts, then other reused-password accounts.
- You do not need to decide right now whether this is identity theft; first lock down access and recovery routes.
- You do not need to write a perfect report before securing the account.

## Important reassurance
These alerts are frightening, but they can also be useful early warnings. Securing recovery details, email access, MFA, and active sessions can often stop the takeover before the attacker gets control.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, such as identity recovery, charge disputes, device cleanup, or specialist cybersecurity help, may need support from the provider, a financial institution, or another qualified service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Use official provider, bank, FTC, IdentityTheft.gov, and IC3 channels for your specific situation.
