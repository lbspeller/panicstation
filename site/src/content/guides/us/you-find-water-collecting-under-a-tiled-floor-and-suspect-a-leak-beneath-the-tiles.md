# What to do if… you find water collecting under a tiled floor and suspect a leak beneath the tiles (USA)

## Short answer
Stop the water from spreading: shut off the nearest safe water valve or the home’s main water valve, avoid wet electrical areas, then call a licensed plumber and describe water collecting under a tiled floor.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep running water to test the leak, and don’t keep using the shower, tub, sink, toilet, dishwasher, washing machine, or ice maker near the wet area.
- Don’t turn on radiant floor heat or any floor-heating system, and don’t try to dry the floor by heating it.
- Don’t pry up tiles, drill, cut, or chip into the floor while the leak may still be active.
- Don’t use extension cords, plug-in heaters, fans, or dehumidifiers in or near standing water.
- Don’t touch outlets, switches, appliances, or a breaker panel if you are wet, standing in water, or touching wet surfaces.
- Don’t force a water meter valve or any valve that seems stuck.

## What to do now
1. **Make a safer pause.** Keep people and pets off the wet tile. Treat loose, lifted, or hollow-sounding tiles as a slip and trip hazard.

2. **Stop using likely sources nearby.** Do not run fixtures or appliances that could feed the leak, especially a shower, tub, toilet, sink, dishwasher, washing machine, refrigerator ice maker, or radiant floor heating system.

3. **Shut off the water if you can do it safely.**
   - If the source seems obvious and dry to reach, close the nearest shutoff first, such as a toilet supply stop, sink supply stop, appliance valve, or refrigerator water valve.
   - If the source is not obvious, shut off the home’s main water valve if you can reach it safely. It is often in a basement, crawl space, garage, utility room, near where the water line enters the home, or on an exterior wall.
   - Use a house-side valve if you have one. Do not force a meter valve or any valve that may belong to the utility.
   - Open a faucet briefly to check whether the flow slows or stops.

4. **Reduce electrical risk only from a dry location.**
   - If outlets, wiring, appliances, floor heat controls, or electrical equipment may be wet or near water, turn off power at the breaker only if you can reach the panel without stepping in water or touching wet surfaces.
   - If you cannot reach the panel safely, do not try. Call an electrician or your electric utility for help disconnecting power.

5. **Limit spread if it is safe to do so.**
   - If the area is electrically safe and the water does not look or smell like sewage, mop or towel up surface water.
   - Place towels or a pan where water is emerging, without blocking access for the plumber.
   - Move rugs, boxes, and small belongings away from the wet tile.

6. **Take quick photos or video.** Capture water at grout lines, lifted tiles, wet baseboards, nearby walls, nearby ceilings below, and any appliance or fixture close to the wet area.

7. **Call for urgent professional help.**
   - Call a licensed plumber and say: “Water is collecting under a tiled floor and I suspect a leak beneath the tiles.”
   - Mention whether the floor is over a slab, crawl space, basement, condo unit, or another room.
   - Mention any radiant floor heat, recent plumbing work, recent appliance installation, or recent shower or toilet problem.

8. **Notify others who may be affected.** If you rent, live in a condo, or have an HOA, contact the landlord, property manager, or HOA right away. There may be shared shutoffs, rules for opening floors, or a unit below that needs warning.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now whether tiles, subfloor, cabinets, or drywall must be replaced.
- You do not need to open the floor yourself to locate the leak before a plumber assesses it.
- You do not need to choose a restoration company before the water is off and electrical risk is controlled.
- You do not need to complete an insurance decision in this moment. Keep quick photos and contact your insurer, landlord, or property manager after the immediate risk is controlled.

## Important reassurance
Water under tile feels alarming because the source is hidden, but the first moves are simple: stop using nearby water sources, shut off water if safe, avoid electrical contact, and get a plumber involved. Those steps can meaningfully limit damage while keeping you out of the floor cavity.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The source could be supply plumbing, a drain or waste line, a shower pan, a toilet seal, an appliance line, a slab leak, or a floor-heating system, and later decisions may need a plumber, electrician, restoration contractor, building manager, or insurer.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional plumbing, electrical, insurance, legal, or safety advice. If you cannot safely shut off water, cannot avoid electrical risk, smell sewage, see structural damage, or think anyone is in immediate danger, contact qualified professionals or emergency services as appropriate.
