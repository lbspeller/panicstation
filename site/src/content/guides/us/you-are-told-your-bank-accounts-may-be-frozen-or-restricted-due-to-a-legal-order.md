# What to do if… you are told your bank accounts may be frozen or restricted due to a legal order (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank through the number on your card or its official app. Ask whether an order is active, what kind of order it is, who issued it, and whether there is a deadline you need to act on.

## Do not do these things
- Do not move money or redirect deposits to evade an order before getting situation-specific advice.
- Do not give anyone your password, PIN, one-time code, or remote access to your device.
- Do not use a phone number or link from an unexpected call, text, or email.
- Do not assume every account, deposit, or benefit payment is treated the same way.
- Do not ignore paperwork. Some response windows can be short.

## What to do now
1. Call your bank through a verified channel. Ask for the team that handles legal orders, levies, or garnishments. Ask:
   - whether the account is already restricted or only may be restricted,
   - what type of order the bank received,
   - who issued it,
   - the case, docket, or reference number,
   - the date the bank received it,
   - whether the bank can see a deadline or next action date,
   - which funds are currently unavailable, and
   - whether incoming deposits may be affected.

2. Ask for a copy of the order or notice if the bank can provide one. If it cannot, write down the issuer, reference number, contact details shown on the paperwork, and where the paperwork says to respond. Ask whether the bank has sent or will send any notice or forms.

3. If this is an IRS bank levy, ask the bank for the compliance date. An IRS bank levy has a 21-day waiting period before the bank sends the held funds to the IRS. Funds added after the bank receives that IRS levy normally are not affected by that levy. Contact the IRS promptly using the number on the levy notice or an official IRS route. Ask about a release if there is an error or the levy is causing immediate economic hardship.

4. If the account receives federal benefits by direct deposit, tell the bank. Certain direct-deposited benefits may be covered by an automatic account-review rule, including Social Security, Supplemental Security Income, veterans’ benefits, Railroad Retirement Board payments, and federal civil service retirement payments. Ask whether the bank performed a two-month lookback and established a protected amount that remains available to you. Ask whether the rule applies to your specific order. For an order obtained by the United States or issued by a state child support enforcement agency, the bank generally follows its customary procedures instead of the automatic protected-amount process.

5. Check what you need before you can get clarification: food, medication, housing, utilities, transport, and child care. Identify which payments are due soon and which funds remain accessible. Do not change deposit instructions to evade an order. Ask the bank or legal aid before making changes that could affect the process.

6. If this is a creditor, court, or state-agency order, state law may provide additional exemptions or response procedures. Contact a civil legal-aid organisation or a consumer-law attorney promptly. Keep the order, bank notices, recent account statements showing deposit sources, and a short list of urgent bills.

7. If you think the order concerns the wrong person, an identity mix-up, or a joint account, tell the bank immediately. Write down who you spoke to, when you spoke, and what they said. Keep documents showing the issue and contact legal aid promptly.

8. If the warning came from an unexpected caller, text, or email, stop using that contact route. Call the bank through the number on your card or official app. If the message names a government agency or court, look up that office independently before contacting it.

## What can wait
- You do not need to argue the full underlying dispute before you know what order the bank received.
- You do not need to make long-term financial decisions today.
- You do not need to move every payment or change every account.
- You can decide whether to hire a private attorney after you have the paperwork, but seek legal aid promptly if a deadline is close.

## Important reassurance
You do not have to solve the whole situation at once. Start with the bank’s verified channel, identify the order, and deal with the nearest deadline. Different orders follow different processes, and some funds may remain available.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The next step may depend on the order type, the issuing court or agency, the source of the deposits, and state law. A civil legal-aid organisation or an attorney may need to review the paperwork.
