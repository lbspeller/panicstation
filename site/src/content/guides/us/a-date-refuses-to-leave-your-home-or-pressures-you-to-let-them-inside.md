# What to do if… a date refuses to leave your home or pressures you to let them inside (USA)

## Short answer
Keep the door closed and locked if you can do so safely. End the interaction from inside, and call 911 immediately if you need emergency assistance or feel in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door or step outside to smooth things over.
- Do not debate, justify, or negotiate after you have said no.
- Do not let them inside for a short request, such as using the bathroom, charging a phone, or talking for a minute.
- Do not go somewhere more private with them to avoid conflict.
- Do not keep responding once you have told them to leave.
- Do not place yourself between them and the door if they are already blocking entry. Move toward a safer pause and call 911.

## What to do now
1. **Keep a barrier between you and them.** Close and lock the door if you can do so safely. Keep your phone with you. If you cannot close the door safely, or they try to force entry, move toward a safer pause and call 911 immediately.
2. **Use one clear sentence.** Through the closed door, say: “I am not letting you in. Please leave.” If needed, repeat: “No. Leave.”
3. **Stop engaging.** Do not reopen the door. You do not need to keep explaining or answering messages.
4. **Contact someone you trust.** If it is safe to use your phone, call or text them, give them your address, and ask them to stay connected while the person is outside.
5. **Use nearby support without creating a confrontation.** If you have a roommate, front-desk staff, building security, or a trusted neighbor, ask for support while you remain inside. Do not ask anyone to confront the person.
6. **Call 911 if you need emergency assistance.** Call if the person refuses to leave and you feel in immediate danger, threatens you, or tries to enter. Give your address and say that someone outside your home is refusing to leave or trying to get inside.
7. **After they leave, keep the door locked.** Ask a trusted person to stay connected while you decide where you feel safest. If the person returns, waits nearby, or you feel in immediate danger, call 911.
8. **Use confidential specialist support if it would help.** If sexual pressure or unwanted contact happened, or you want support without pressure, contact RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline. Call 800-656-HOPE, text HOPE to 64673, or use online chat.
9. **Use relationship-abuse support if this is part of an ongoing pattern.** Contact the National Domestic Violence Hotline by calling 800-799-SAFE (7233), using live chat, or texting START to 88788.
10. **Seek medical care if unwanted sexual contact happened or you want medical support.** Contact RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline for help locating a clinic or hospital that offers a sexual assault medical forensic examination, often called a SAFE, or ask a hospital whether it offers one or can refer you. Ask what reporting rules apply before deciding what you want.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report anything to police.
- You do not need to label what happened.
- You do not need to send more messages, block them, post publicly, or make relationship decisions until you feel safer and calmer.
- You do not need to confront them or explain your reasons.

## Important reassurance
Your discomfort is enough reason to keep your door closed. Freezing, becoming very polite, or second-guessing yourself may happen under pressure. You are not required to keep engaging.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reach a safer pause and reduce immediate pressure. Later decisions about support, medical care, reporting, or longer-term safety may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you need emergency assistance or feel in immediate danger, call 911.
