# What to do if… you are presented with a new non-compete or restrictive covenant after you resign (USA)

## Short answer
Do not resign impulsively; instead, request a written clarification of your current responsibilities and start a factual, off-system record of all changes to your role.

## Do not do these things
- Do not quit or send a resignation email in the heat of the moment, as this may forfeit your right to unemployment benefits or legal claims.
- Do not take, download, or forward company files, proprietary data, or client lists to your personal email to "save as evidence."
- Do not use company-owned laptops or phones to record your personal notes or timeline of events.
- Do not sign any new job descriptions, Performance Improvement Plans (PIPs), or demotion letters during a meeting; always ask for time to review them.
- Do not vent to coworkers or post about the situation on social media, as these statements can be used against you later.

## What to do now
1. **Start a detailed timeline of every specific change on a personal device or in a physical notebook (today).**  
   Record exactly which duties were removed, the dates of meetings you were excluded from, when your access to systems changed, and the names of anyone involved.

2. **Send a neutral email to your manager asking for a "role alignment" check (today or next workday).**  
   Request a written summary of your current priorities and how your performance will be measured moving forward.  
   - If the changes were discussed verbally, send a "summary of my understanding" email to create a paper trail.

3. **Ask HR for a copy of your official job description and your recent performance reviews (this week).**  
   Compare your current daily tasks to the official requirements of your role to document the gap between your contract and your actual work.

4. **Secure your permitted personal records from company systems (today).**  
   Save copies of your offer letter, pay stubs, benefits information, and written praise or awards that you are legally allowed to keep under company policy.

5. **Request a formal meeting to ask if these changes are performance-related (this week).**  
   **Ask directly for specific examples of any concerns and document the response.**  
   - If they claim there are no performance issues, document that the removal of duties remains "without explanation."

6. **Check your specific filing deadlines for discrimination or retaliation claims (today).**  
   **Be aware that federal employees generally must contact an EEO Counselor within 45 days of a discriminatory event.**  
   - For private-sector employees, the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) deadline is typically 180 days, which may extend to 300 days if a state agency is involved.

7. **Determine if you are protected under the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) if you are acting with others (this week).**  
   **Note that most private-sector employees have the right to discuss working conditions together, though most supervisors and independent contractors are excluded.**  
   - 2026 rulings have narrowed the "managerial" exception; if you do not have the power to hire, fire, or create company policy, you may still be covered even with a "lead" title.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a lawsuit or a formal agency charge today.
- You do not need to confront your manager about their "true motives" or hidden agendas right now.
- You do not need to update your LinkedIn or start applying for new jobs this second, though you can do so when you feel stable.
- You do not need to justify the situation to your family or friends until you have more clarity.

## Important reassurance
It is a common and high-stress experience to feel "managed out" or sidelined, and your feeling of uncertainty is a logical response to a lack of transparency. Taking small, boring steps to document the facts helps move you from a state of shock to a state of preparation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and documentation strategies for employees in the USA. It does not cover the specifics of union grievances, state-specific "at-will" exceptions, or the details of filing a formal lawsuit.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Employment laws and deadlines vary significantly by state and by the size of the employer. If you suspect your rights are being violated, you should consult a qualified employment attorney or your state's labor department promptly.
