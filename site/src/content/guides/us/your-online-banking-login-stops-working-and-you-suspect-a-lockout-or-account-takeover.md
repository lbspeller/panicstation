# What to do if… your online banking login stops working and you suspect a lockout or account takeover (USA)

## Short answer
Stop trying to log in and contact your bank or credit union through a trusted route now: the number on your card or statement, or the official app or website reached by typing the address yourself. Ask them to secure the account, check recent activity, and tell you what transfers or access they can temporarily block.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep retrying passwords, codes, or multiple login methods.
- Don’t click links in texts, emails, search ads, or pop-ups saying your account is locked or compromised.
- Don’t share one-time codes, security answers, PINs, or passwords with anyone who contacts you.
- Don’t approve an unexpected in-app prompt or device sign-in request.
- Don’t move money because someone tells you to protect it, including to a safe account, crypto wallet, gift card, or cash withdrawal.
- Don’t install remote-access or screen-sharing software because someone says they are from the bank, a payment app, or the government.
- Don’t use a number or link given in the suspicious message to contact the bank.

## What to do now
1. **Pause and record what you can see.** Write down the time, the exact error message, any new-device alert, any unexpected verification code, and any suspicious call, text, or email. Take screenshots if you can do so safely.

2. **Contact the bank or credit union through a trusted route.** Use the number on the back of your debit or ATM card, a recent statement, the official app, or the official website reached by typing the address yourself. Say: “My online banking login stopped working and I’m worried about a lockout or account takeover. Please secure my account and check for unauthorized activity.”

3. **Ask what they can temporarily block or freeze right now.** Ask specifically about online banking access, online transfers, wires, external transfers, new payees, debit-card use, and any linked peer-to-peer payment service such as Zelle.

4. **Review the account with the bank while you are on the trusted call or chat.** Ask them to check recent and pending transactions, new recipients, changed contact details, new devices, password resets, and any recent profile changes.

5. **Start the bank’s unauthorized-activity process if anything looks wrong.** Ask what can still be stopped, reversed, recalled, or disputed. Ask whether they need written or in-app confirmation after the call. Get a case or reference number.

6. **Reset access only through the bank’s verified process.** Use a new, unique banking password. Ask whether they recommend removing unknown devices, re-registering the app, replacing the card, or opening a new account number.

7. **Secure the email account that can reset your banking login.** Change the email password from a safe device and turn on strong two-factor authentication. Check whether forwarding rules, recovery email addresses, or recovery phone numbers were changed.

8. **Secure your phone number if texts or calls are acting strangely.** If you suddenly have no service, receive a SIM-change alert, or cannot receive calls or texts, contact your mobile carrier through its official number or app and ask them to lock the line and check for SIM swap or port-out activity.

9. **Act quickly if you see money missing or transfers you did not authorize.** Tell the bank as soon as you notice the problem. Federal protections for many electronic transfers can depend on how quickly you notify the bank and on the type of transfer.

10. **If identity theft may be involved, start a recovery path after the bank is secured.** Use official identity-theft resources to make a recovery plan. If you are worried someone may open new credit in your name, consider a credit freeze or fraud alert; this is mainly for new-account risk, not for fixing the existing bank login.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to switch banks or close every account.
- Changing every password you own; prioritize bank, email, and mobile carrier first.
- Filing a police report, unless your bank asks for one now or there is another immediate reason.
- Preparing a full document packet; first get the incident logged and the account protected.
- Deciding whether a credit freeze or fraud alert is needed if there is no sign of identity theft or new-account risk.

## Important reassurance
A failed banking login can be a normal security lockout, a technical issue, or a warning sign. You do not need to diagnose it before acting. The safest first move is the same: reach the bank through a verified route, stop further access, and protect the email and phone number that can reset the login.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce further loss, preserve useful information, and buy time. Later decisions may need help from your bank, credit union, mobile carrier, identity-theft recovery services, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Banks, credit unions, payment services, and mobile carriers have different processes. Follow the instructions of your institution once you have reached it through a verified contact method.
