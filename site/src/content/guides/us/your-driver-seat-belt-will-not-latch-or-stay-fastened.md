# What to do if… your driver seat belt will not latch or stay fastened (USA)
## Short answer
If you are moving, reach the earliest safe stopping place and stop. Do not start or continue a trip with the driver’s seat belt unlatched; arrange repair, roadside assistance, or a tow.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t drive onward because the destination is close or the road seems quiet.
- Don’t tie the belt, clip it to another point, wedge objects into the buckle, or use an improvised restraint.
- Don’t use a random extender or adapter to make the belt latch. Check the owner’s manual or contact the vehicle manufacturer for vehicle-specific guidance.
- Don’t insert tools, add liquids, force parts, or dismantle the buckle unless vehicle-specific instructions say to do so.
- Don’t assume a click means the belt is secure if the tongue pulls free, the buckle releases unexpectedly, or the release button behaves abnormally.
- Don’t ignore an airbag or SRS warning light alongside the buckle problem.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safe pause.** If you are already moving, signal and stop at the earliest safe place. If you are already parked, keep the vehicle parked.
2. **Check for an obvious obstruction while the vehicle is stationary.** Look at the buckle opening and the metal tongue. If you can see debris, bending, cracking, or other damage, do not insert tools or try to take the buckle apart.
3. **Try one normal latch attempt.** Insert the tongue normally. If it will not enter, will not click, releases unexpectedly, or pulls free with an ordinary pull, stop testing and treat the driver’s seat belt as not working.
4. **Check the owner’s manual and contact a qualified repair provider.** Ask a dealership or reputable repair shop for vehicle-specific guidance. Mention any airbag or SRS warning light.
5. **Check for an open recall.** Find the 17-character VIN, usually at the lower driver-side windshield, inside the driver-side doorjamb, or on registration or insurance documents. Search the VIN on NHTSA’s recall page. A zero-result does not rule out a defect because some recalls may not appear in the lookup.
6. **Arrange the safe route to repair.** Use roadside assistance or a tow rather than driving the vehicle. If there is an open recall, follow the automaker’s instructions and contact the manufacturer’s dealership about the recall repair.
7. **Consider reporting a possible safety defect.** If the issue is not resolved by a current recall, you may file a vehicle-safety complaint with NHTSA.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether the fault is in the buckle, tongue, retractor, pretensioner, or another component.
- You do not need to negotiate repair costs before the vehicle is safely parked and recall status has been checked.
- You do not need to make a long-term decision about the vehicle today.
- A photo or short video for the repair shop can wait until you are safely parked and have the capacity to do it.

## Important reassurance
Pausing is the right response. You do not need to solve the mechanical cause before arranging a safe repair route.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Diagnosis, buckle replacement, seat removal, and work involving pretensioners, airbags, or SRS warnings may need qualified professional help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, mechanical, or other professional advice. Use vehicle-specific instructions from the manufacturer or a qualified repair provider.
