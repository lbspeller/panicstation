# What to do if… you notice a new damp patch on a wall that is expanding hour by hour (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as an active leak. Keep away from wet electricity, stop the water if you can do that safely, and contact emergency maintenance or a plumber.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait while the patch keeps expanding.
- Do not touch outlets, switches, power strips, cords, or appliances near the wet area.
- Do not cut, drill, or punch into the drywall; there may be wiring or a pressurized pipe behind it.
- Do not paint over it, seal it, or spray cleaners while it is still spreading.
- Do not aim a space heater at the wet wall or place electrical equipment close to the wet area.
- Do not stand in water while touching switches, breakers, cords, plumbing, or appliances.

## What to do now
1. **Check for immediate danger.**  
   Keep people and pets away from the wet area. If you see sparks, smell burning, hear buzzing or crackling from an outlet or switch, or water is reaching electrical equipment, move back. If there is an immediate fire or electrocution risk, call **911**.

2. **Make the electricity safer only if you can do it safely.**  
   If the patch is near outlets, switches, cords, or appliances, avoid using them. If your hands, shoes, floor, and route to the electrical panel are dry, and the panel itself is dry, turn off the affected breaker. If you cannot identify the affected breaker, use the main breaker only from a dry, safe position. If the panel, meter, floor, or your route is wet, do not approach it; keep clear and call **911** if there is immediate danger, or contact a licensed electrician if there is no immediate danger.

3. **Stop water use near the likely source.**  
   Stop using nearby sinks, toilets, showers, tubs, washing machines, dishwashers, ice makers, or water heaters if they could be involved. If there is a room above the patch, stop water use there too.

4. **Shut off the water if the patch keeps growing.**  
   Use a local shutoff first if it is obvious and safe, such as a valve under a sink, behind a toilet, behind a washer, or near an appliance. If that does not stop the spread, or you cannot identify the source quickly, shut off the home’s main water valve. It may be where the water line enters the home, near a water heater, in a basement, crawlspace, garage, utility room, outside wall box, or near the water meter.

5. **Call the right help now.**  
   If you rent, contact your landlord, property manager, or emergency maintenance line and say: “A new damp patch on the wall is expanding hour by hour, and it may be near electricity.” If you own, call an emergency plumber. If the wet area is near electrical equipment, also arrange a licensed electrician.

6. **Limit damage without disturbing the wall.**  
   Move furniture, electronics, papers, rugs, and valuables away from the wall. Put a bucket or towels under any drip if you can do that without touching wet electrical items. Do not open the wall yourself.

7. **Document what is happening.**  
   Take photos and short video from a safe distance, including a wide shot of the room and a close shot of the patch. Note the time, whether the patch is still expanding, what water you shut off, and who you contacted.

8. **After the source is stopped, start safe drying.**  
   Only after the leak has stopped and the area is electrically safe, ventilate if you can and use fans or a dehumidifier from dry outlets, not from outlets near the damp patch. Keep cords away from wet areas. Separate wet items so they can dry. Dry water-damaged areas and items as quickly as possible; official guidance commonly uses 24–48 hours as an important mold-prevention window.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find the exact cause before stopping water and calling help.
- You do not need to decide on repairs, remodeling, repainting, or mold testing right now.
- You do not need to remove drywall yourself.
- You do not need to solve insurance in the moment; stabilize, document, and follow your insurer’s instructions if you contact them.

## Important reassurance
A wall patch spreading in real time can feel alarming, but the first steps are simple: avoid wet electricity, stop water if safe, and get the right help involved. Those actions buy time and reduce the chance of bigger damage.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The repair, drying, electrical safety check, and cleanup plan may need a plumber, electrician, remediation contractor, landlord, insurer, or local code professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, insurance, electrical, plumbing, mold-remediation, or other professional advice. If you are unsure whether an area is electrically safe, treat it as unsafe and get qualified help.
