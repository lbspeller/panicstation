# What to do if… police say they have a warrant and ask you to open the door while you are trying to verify it (USA)

## Short answer
Keep the door closed and locked while you ask to see the warrant through a window or have it slid under the door. If you cannot verify the individuals are real law enforcement, call 911 immediately to confirm that officers have been dispatched to your address.

## Do not do these things
- Don't open the door just because the person outside is shouting or acting aggressive.
- Don't give verbal or written consent to a search even if you believe you have nothing to hide.
- Don't physically resist or block the doorway if officers begin to force entry.
- Don't run, hide, or reach for any objects that could be mistaken for a weapon.
- Don't answer any questions about your activities or the case without a lawyer present.

## What to do now
1. **Communicate through the closed door without opening it.** Speak loudly and clearly so they can hear you, but keep the deadbolt engaged.
2. **Ask for the name, badge number, and agency of the lead officer.** Record this information or write it down immediately.
   - Example agencies include City Police, County Sheriff, State Police, or Federal (FBI / DEA / USMS).
3. **Request to see the warrant through a window or the peephole.** If no window is available, ask them to slide the warrant under the door.
4. **Check the warrant for three specific details.** Ensure the document is signed by a judge, lists your exact address, and describes the specific areas or items to be searched.
5. **Call 911 if you suspect the people are not actual police.** Tell the dispatcher there are people at your door claiming to be police and you need to verify if an official unit is at your location.
6. **State clearly that you do not consent to a search.** Say: "I do not consent to this search, but I will not physically resist your entry."
7. **Contact a witness or lawyer immediately.** If possible, call a neighbor or friend and leave the line open so someone else can hear the interaction.
8. **Keep your hands visible and empty if they enter.** Move slowly and follow all safety commands regarding your physical position.
9. **Invoke your right to remain silent.** Beyond stating your name if required by state law, say: "I am exercising my right to remain silent and I want to see my lawyer."
10. **Observe the search from a safe distance without interfering.** Note what items are taken and ask for a written inventory or receipt before the officers leave.

## What can wait
- You do not need to explain why you were hesitant to open the door.
- You do not need to argue about the legality of the warrant on your doorstep.
- You do not need to decide whether to file a complaint or lawsuit during the encounter.
- You do not need to sign any statements or confessions.

## Important reassurance
It is a normal physiological reaction to feel fear or panic when law enforcement demands entry to your home. Taking a few moments to verify a warrant and the identity of those at your door is a standard safety precaution and does not mean you are "guilty" or "obstructing" in a way that justifies immediate harm.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate interaction at the door. It does not cover long-term legal defense, trial strategy, or specific state-by-state variations in search and seizure law.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not legal advice. Under the "Knock and Announce" rule (18 U.S.C. § 3109), officers must generally wait a reasonable amount of time after announcing themselves before using force, though "no-knock" exceptions exist for officer safety or to prevent the destruction of evidence. If a federal warrant is executed, Rule 41 requires officers to provide a copy of the warrant and a receipt for any property taken.
