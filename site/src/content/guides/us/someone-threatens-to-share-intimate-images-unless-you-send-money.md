# What to do if… someone threatens to share intimate images unless you send money (USA)

## Short answer
Do not pay any money and immediately cease all communication with the extortionist. Document the threat with a single set of screenshots and report the incident to federal law enforcement through official channels.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay the demand, as payment typically leads to repeated and higher demands.
- Don’t negotiate, apologize, or attempt to plead with the extortionist.
- Don’t delete the conversation or your account before capturing essential evidence.
- Don’t send additional images or personal information to "prove" your identity or cooperation.
- Don’t forward or share the explicit images with anyone else, including via email or messaging apps, even for reporting purposes unless specifically directed by law enforcement.

## What to do now
1. **Document the evidence immediately but briefly.**  
   **Save a record of the extortionist's profile name, the specific threat made, the payment instructions, and the timestamps of the messages.**
   - Do not capture more explicit images than necessary for the report.
   - Store these screenshots in a secure, private folder.
2. **Cease all contact and block the account.**  
   **Once you have documented the threat, stop responding to the extortionist and use the platform’s "Block" and "Report" features.**
   - Reporting through the app helps the service provider take down the abusive account.
   - Do not tell the extortionist you are blocking them; simply do it.
3. **Notify your financial institution if money was sent.**  
   **Contact your bank, credit card issuer, or payment app (e.g., Venmo, CashApp, PayPal) to report the transaction as fraud or extortion.**
   - Ask if the payment can be stopped or if your account details need to be changed to prevent further unauthorized access.
   - Do not assume funds can be recovered, but reporting helps protect your account from future charges.
4. **Report the crime to the FBI.**  
   **File a report online at tips.fbi.gov or call 1-800-CALL-FBI (1-800-225-5324).**
   - You should also file a complaint at the Internet Crime Complaint Center (ic3.gov).
   - If you feel you are in immediate physical danger, call 911.
5. **Use proactive image-removal tools if you are 18 or older.**  
   **Visit StopNCII.org to use their free, private tool which helps prevent your intimate images from being shared on participating social media platforms.**
   - This tool uses "hashing" technology to identify and block images without you ever having to upload the actual image to the site.
6. **Follow child-safety protocols if you or the person in the image is under 18.**  
   **If the incident involves a minor, report it immediately to the NCMEC CyberTipline at report.cybertip.org or call 1-800-843-5678.**
   - Use the "Take It Down" tool provided by NCMEC (takeitdown.ncmec.org) to help remove or prevent the spread of images of minors.
   - You can also contact the DHS Know2Protect Tipline at 833-591-KNOW (5669).
7. **Secure your digital accounts.**  
   **Change the passwords for your email and social media accounts and enable two-factor authentication (2FA) if it is not already active.**
   - Check your account settings to ensure no unauthorized recovery emails or phone numbers have been added.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to tell your family or employer right now.
- You do not need to hire a private "reputation management" or "sextortion help" company; many of these are predatory.
- You do not need to determine how the person found you or which specific data they have access to in this first hour.

## Important reassurance
Sextortion relies on creating a sense of isolation and overwhelming shame to force you to act quickly. You are a victim of a coordinated criminal scheme, and your reaction—including any initial panic—is a normal response to an intense threat. Law enforcement agencies deal with these cases daily and have established protocols to help you without judgment.

## Scope note
This is first-step guidance only. Ongoing support for digital privacy, legal advice, or mental health counseling should be sought from qualified professionals after the initial situation is stabilized.

## Important note
This guide provides general procedural information and does not constitute legal or clinical advice. If you are a minor or if the images involve a minor, additional legal protections and reporting requirements apply. If you are experiencing a mental health crisis, contact the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline.
