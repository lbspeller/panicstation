# What to do if… you receive an official notice that you are being charged and you do not understand it (USA)

## Short answer
Do not ignore it. Write down the court date or response deadline, verify the case using independently found official court contact details, and try to get legal help before you plead, pay, miss a required appearance, or send an explanation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not skip the court date or deadline because the notice is confusing.
- Do not call notice-only phone numbers, scan QR codes, or pay through links until you have checked the case through the court’s official website or clerk’s office.
- Do not plead guilty, no contest, or send a written explanation because you feel rushed.
- Do not send detailed accounts of what happened to anyone other than a lawyer until you have legal advice.
- Do not contact anyone involved in the case if the paperwork mentions a no-contact order, protective order, stay-away order, or release condition.
- Do not assume a wrong name, misspelling, old address, or scam concern means you can ignore it.

## What to do now
1. **Pull out the 5 facts you need and write them down.**  
   From the notice, copy the court name and address, case number, date and time you must appear or respond, charge or charges listed, and any instructions about counsel, release conditions, or no-contact restrictions.

2. **Verify the notice through official court channels, not through notice-only links.**  
   Search for the court’s official website yourself, or use an official state or federal court directory. Use the clerk’s phone number or official case lookup from that site. Ask: “Can you confirm this case number, the next required date, and what this document is?”

3. **Save the paperwork exactly as it arrived.**  
   Keep the notice, envelope, mailing label, email headers if it came by email, and every page or attachment. Take clear photos or scans, but do not write on the original unless a court or lawyer tells you to.

4. **Try to get legal help before responding to the charge.**  
   If you can contact a criminal defense lawyer, send clear photos or scans of every page. If you cannot afford a lawyer, ask the clerk, local public defender office, or court website how to request a public defender or court-appointed lawyer in that court. The process varies, but it often starts at or before the first appearance or arraignment and may require financial information.

5. **Plan to attend any listed arraignment, first appearance, or hearing unless the court officially tells you otherwise.**  
   In many courts, an arraignment or first appearance is where the judge confirms the charge, addresses rights, and deals with counsel or release conditions. Bring the notice, ID, and any documents the court told you to bring.

6. **Ask early for an interpreter or accessibility accommodation if you need one.**  
   Contact the clerk through the court’s official website and say what language or accommodation you need, the case number, and the date of the hearing. Ask whether the request must be made on a form.

7. **If the notice may be for the wrong person, still verify it.**  
   Tell the clerk there may be a name, date of birth, address, or identity error and ask how that court handles corrections. Keep notes of who you spoke with, the date and time, and what they told you. Tell any lawyer or appointed counsel about the error.

8. **Treat listed conditions as active until a lawyer or the court clarifies them.**  
   If the paperwork lists release terms, no-contact rules, travel limits, weapons restrictions, testing, check-ins, or stay-away locations, do your best to follow them immediately. If you cannot understand or comply with a condition, ask counsel or the court how to get clarification rather than guessing.

9. **Use one short sentence if you are asked to respond before you understand.**  
   Say: “I do not understand the notice. I want to speak with a lawyer. If I qualify, I am asking for a court-appointed lawyer.” Then stop and wait for instructions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your whole legal strategy tonight.
- You do not need to gather every record, contact witnesses, or explain the facts to friends, family, employers, or the other people named in the paperwork.
- You do not need to work out whether the charge is fair before you have confirmed the next required date and started the process for legal help.
- You do not need to pay anything through the notice until you have verified the case and payment route through official court channels.

## Important reassurance
Legal paperwork can feel frightening because it uses unfamiliar words and may carry serious deadlines. Your immediate job is smaller: confirm whether the notice is real, protect the next date, avoid rushed statements or pleas, and get a lawyer or court-appointed counsel process started if you can.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Criminal court procedures, public defender eligibility, interpreter requests, and correction processes vary by state, county, city, tribal, and federal court, so later decisions may need help from a criminal defense lawyer, public defender, court self-help office, or other qualified local legal resource.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice or professional advice. Court staff can usually provide scheduling and procedural information, but they generally cannot give legal advice. For advice about your charge, rights, plea, release conditions, or deadlines, seek legal counsel as soon as possible.
