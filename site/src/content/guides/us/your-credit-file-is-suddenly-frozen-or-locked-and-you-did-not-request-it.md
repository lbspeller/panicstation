# What to do if… your credit file is suddenly frozen or locked and you did not request it (USA)

## Short answer
Assume identity theft until proven otherwise: get your credit reports from AnnualCreditReport.com, confirm whether there’s a security freeze or credit lock with the bureaus, and file an IdentityTheft.gov report if anything looks unauthorized.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t “unfreeze” using links from unexpected emails/texts — go directly to official bureau pages.
- Don’t keep submitting new credit applications to “see if it works.”
- Don’t give your Social Security number or document images to anyone who contacted you first.
- Don’t pay “credit repair” or “freeze removal” services for urgent steps you can do yourself for free.

## What to do now
1. **Write down what happened and who told you it was frozen/locked.**  
   Note the date/time and whether it was a bank, lender, landlord, employer screening, or your own credit app. If it’s blocking an application, ask which bureau they used so you can focus your calls.

2. **Get your credit reports from the official site and scan for red flags.**  
   Use **AnnualCreditReport.com** to pull reports and look for:
   - accounts you don’t recognize
   - hard inquiries you didn’t authorize
   - new addresses/phone numbers/employers you didn’t add

3. **Confirm what’s on your file: a security freeze or a credit lock (and when it was placed).**  
   Contact the three nationwide credit bureaus and ask:
   - Is there a **security freeze** on my file? (A freeze is a legal right and is free to place/lift.)
   - Is there a **credit lock** on my file? (A lock is typically a product feature and may involve an online account.)
   If you didn’t place it, follow the bureau’s identity verification/account recovery steps so you regain control.

4. **If anything on your reports is unauthorized, create an IdentityTheft.gov report and save it.**  
   The report/plan helps you document what happened and is often accepted by creditors and bureaus when fixing identity-theft issues.

5. **Place a fraud alert to add an extra verification layer.**  
   You can place an initial (or extended) fraud alert by contacting **one** of the three bureaus; the bureau you contact must notify the other two. Fraud alerts are free and tell lenders to take extra steps to verify it’s really you.

6. **Contact the fraud departments of any companies showing unauthorized accounts or applications.**  
   Ask them to stop processing, close/flag the account as identity theft, and tell you exactly what they need from you. Keep a log (date/time/person/summary).

7. **If you’re worried about bank-account fraud too, check specialty reporting.**  
   Consider requesting and/or freezing your **ChexSystems** consumer file (separate from credit reports). Some people also freeze with **Innovis** as an extra layer.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to buy monitoring today to handle an unexpected freeze/lock.
- You don’t need to “fix your score” right now — stopping misuse and correcting records comes first.
- You can wait to decide about filing a local police report unless a creditor/bureau specifically requires it for your case.

## Important reassurance
Finding a freeze/lock you didn’t request is unsettling, but it also gives you a clear next move: verify what’s on your reports and regain control of the freeze/lock accounts. Once you know whether there are unauthorized accounts or inquiries, the path forward gets much clearer.

## Scope note
This is a first-step stabilization guide. Cleaning up fraudulent accounts and disputes can take follow-up with each bureau and each creditor that reported the information.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal advice. Processes vary by state and by company. If you can only do two things right now, do these: pull your reports from AnnualCreditReport.com, and contact the bureaus directly to confirm (and regain control of) any freeze/lock.
