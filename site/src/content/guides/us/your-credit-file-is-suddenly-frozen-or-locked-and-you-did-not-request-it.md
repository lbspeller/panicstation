# What to do if… your credit file is suddenly frozen or locked and you did not request it (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as possible identity theft until you verify it. Check your credit reports through the official free-report site, contact the credit bureaus directly to confirm whether there is a freeze or lock, and use IdentityTheft.gov if you find changes you believe are unauthorized.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t use links from unexpected emails, texts, pop-ups, or calls about the freeze or lock.
- Don’t keep submitting credit, rental, or screening applications just to see whether one goes through.
- Don’t give your Social Security number, ID images, passwords, or one-time codes to anyone who contacted you first.
- Don’t pay a “credit repair,” “freeze removal,” or “identity recovery” service for urgent steps you can usually start yourself.
- Don’t ignore it because the blocked application was inconvenient rather than financially large.

## What to do now
1. **Write down exactly what happened.**  
   Note the date, time, company, application type, and the exact words used: “freeze,” “security freeze,” “lock,” “fraud alert,” or “unable to access file.” If a lender, landlord, employer screener, insurer, or phone provider was involved, ask which credit bureau or screening company they tried to use.

2. **Get your credit reports from the official free-report site.**  
   Use AnnualCreditReport.com directly. Check each available report for accounts you do not recognize, hard inquiries you did not authorize, addresses you did not use, phone numbers you did not add, employers you do not recognize, or name variations that do not belong to you.

3. **Contact the three nationwide credit bureaus directly.**  
   Ask each bureau whether your file has a security freeze, a credit lock, a fraud alert, or another access restriction. Ask when it was placed and what account, phone number, email address, mailing address, or identity-verification route is attached to it.

4. **Regain control of any freeze or lock account you did not create.**  
   Use the bureau’s official website or phone route, not a link sent to you. Follow its identity-verification or account-recovery process, and save confirmation numbers, screenshots, letters, or emails in one place.

5. **If you find unauthorized activity, make an IdentityTheft.gov report.**  
   Use it if you see an account, application, inquiry, address, phone number, or other credit-file change you believe was not yours. Save the report and recovery plan because companies and bureaus may ask for documentation while fixing identity-theft records.

6. **Place an initial fraud alert if identity theft is possible.**  
   Contact one of the three nationwide credit bureaus and ask for an initial fraud alert. The bureau you contact should notify the other two. If you have an FTC identity-theft report or a police report, ask whether an extended fraud alert fits your situation.

7. **Contact any company connected to an unauthorized account or application.**  
   Ask for the fraud department. Say the account or application may be identity theft, ask them to stop processing or close it as appropriate, and ask what documents they need from you. Write down the date, time, person, and summary of each call.

8. **Check bank-account screening reports if bank misuse is also possible.**  
   If you see signs involving checking accounts, debit accounts, or deposit-account applications, request your ChexSystems file and consider a ChexSystems security freeze. You can also check whether an Innovis security freeze is useful for your situation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to buy credit monitoring today to start the urgent steps.
- You do not need to fix your credit score right now; first check whether someone is using your information.
- You do not need to decide every dispute, police-report, or long-term monitoring question in the first few minutes, unless a company specifically asks for it or there is an immediate safety risk.
- You do not need to contact every past lender until you know which report, account, inquiry, or application is affected.

## Important reassurance
An unexpected freeze or lock is alarming, but it also gives you a clear first task: find out whether it is a security freeze, a lock, a fraud alert, or an account-access problem. Once you know which bureau and which record is involved, you can work through the recovery steps one company at a time.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later disputes, account closures, police reports, tax issues, benefits issues, or debt-collection problems may need help from the relevant company, government agency, consumer lawyer, financial counselor, or identity-theft specialist.
