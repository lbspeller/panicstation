# What to do if… your credit file is suddenly frozen or locked and you did not request it (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all active credit applications and contact the three major bureaus immediately to confirm if an unauthorized security freeze or credit lock has been placed in your name. Pull your free weekly credit reports from AnnualCreditReport.com to identify any fraudulent accounts or inquiries.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use links in emails or text messages to "log in" to your credit accounts; always navigate directly to official bureau websites.
- Do not submit additional credit, loan, or apartment applications until you have confirmed the source of the freeze.
- Do not provide your full Social Security number to any caller claiming to be from a credit bureau "investigating" the freeze.
- Do not ignore the freeze; an unauthorized freeze often indicates that an identity thief already has enough of your data to pass verification checks.

## What to do now
1. **Identify exactly which credit bureau reported the freeze to your lender or service provider.** Ask the company that ran the check (bank, landlord, or employer) for the name of the specific credit reporting agency they used and the date the access was blocked.

2. **Pull your credit reports from all three bureaus at AnnualCreditReport.com.** Review the reports for unauthorized activity, specifically looking for new addresses, phone numbers, or credit inquiries you do not recognize.
   - You are entitled to free weekly reports from Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.
   - Look for the "Security Freeze" status on each report to see when it was applied.

3. **Contact the three major bureaus directly to regain control of your files.** Use official phone lines to verify your identity and find out how the freeze or lock was established.
   - **Equifax:** 1-888-298-0045
   - **Experian:** 1-888-397-3742
   - **TransUnion:** 1-888-909-8872

4. **File an official identity theft report at IdentityTheft.gov.** Use the Federal Trade Commission's (FTC) portal to create a recovery plan and a formal affidavit, which is required to remove fraudulent information from your file.

5. **Place an initial fraud alert on your credit file by contacting one bureau.** You only need to contact one of the three; they are legally required to notify the other two. This requires lenders to take extra steps to verify your identity before issuing new credit.

6. **Freeze your specialty consumer reports to prevent bank account fraud.** Contact ChexSystems and Innovis to place security freezes on your non-traditional credit and banking history files.
   - **ChexSystems:** 1-800-887-7652
   - **Innovis:** 1-800-540-2505

7. **Notify the fraud departments of any creditors listed on your report for accounts you did not open.** Request that they close the accounts immediately and provide you with a letter confirming the account was opened fraudulently.

## What can wait
- You do not need to pay for a credit monitoring subscription while you are in the initial stabilization phase.
- You do not need to file a local police report until you have completed the IdentityTheft.gov process and determined if a creditor requires one.
- You do not need to worry about your credit score fluctuations caused by the unauthorized activity; these can be corrected once the fraud is documented.

## Important reassurance
An unauthorized freeze is a defensive signal that your data may be compromised, but it also physically prevents further credit-based identity theft from occurring. Once you verify your identity with the bureaus and reset your access credentials, you can begin the structured process of clearing your record using your FTC recovery plan.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for unauthorized credit freezes only. Long-term dispute resolution for fraudulent accounts requires following the specific steps provided in your IdentityTheft.gov recovery plan.

## Important note
This guide is provided for informational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Security freeze and credit lock procedures are governed by the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) and vary by bureau.
