# What to do if… you receive notice that conditions you must follow have been imposed before any court date (USA)

## Short answer
Assume all conditions are active immediately, comply with every requirement exactly to avoid arrest, and contact your lawyer or the listed pretrial services officer to confirm specific compliance steps.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume conditions only start after your first formal court appearance; they usually take effect the moment you are released.
- Do not contact any protected person listed in a "no-contact" or "stay-away" order for any reason, including through third parties or social media.
- Do not travel outside the permitted geographic area (often the county or state) without written permission from the court or your supervisor.
- Do not miss a scheduled check-in, drug test, or equipment maintenance appointment even if the timing is inconvenient or conflicts with work.
- Do not attempt to "test" or tamper with electronic monitoring equipment or ignition interlock devices.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific restrictions and deadlines listed on your paperwork.**  
   **Carefully list every "must" and "must not" item found in the notice.**  
   - Prohibited contacts (victims, witnesses, co-defendants).  
   - Geographic stay-away zones (specific addresses or distances).  
   - Curfew start and end times.  
   - Mandatory reporting dates, times, and locations.  
   - Prohibited substances (alcohol, controlled medications, illegal drugs).  

2. **Locate the contact information for your supervising agency.**  
   **Find the name and phone number of the pretrial services office or the specific officer assigned to your case.**  
   - In federal cases, this is usually **U.S. Pretrial Services**.  
   - In state or county cases, this may be called **Pretrial Services**, **Bond Supervision**, or a similar local department.  
   - If no phone number is listed, call the clerk of the court that issued the order to ask which agency is supervising your release.

3. **Contact your attorney to review the order for errors or impossible requirements.**  
   **Send a clear digital copy of the entire notice to your lawyer immediately.**  
   - Ask for confirmation on when each condition expires.  
   - Identify any conditions that make it impossible to work or live safely (e.g., a stay-away order from your own residence).  
   - If you do not have a lawyer, contact the local Public Defender’s office or the court clerk to ask how to request court-appointed counsel.

4. **Follow the specific reporting instructions for your first check-in.**  
   **Prepare to provide verification for your address, employment, and any medical needs at your first meeting.**  
   - Ask your supervising officer for the preferred method of communication (phone, email, or in-person).  
   - Request a written copy of any "verbal" instructions or "allowances" the officer gives you regarding your schedule.

5. **Establish physical and digital barriers to prevent accidental contact violations.**  
   **Block or mute the phone numbers and social media accounts of any person listed in a no-contact order.**  
   - Delete saved locations in GPS apps that fall within restricted "stay-away" zones.  
   - Inform family members that they must not pass messages between you and protected persons, as this can be considered a violation.

6. **Create a "Compliance Log" to document your adherence to the rules.**  
   **Keep a dedicated folder or digital log of every check-in, drug test, and communication with pretrial services.**  
   - Save copies of all emails and take notes during phone calls, including the date, time, and name of the person you spoke with.  
   - Retain receipts or confirmation documents for every mandatory appointment.

## What can wait
- You do not need to resolve the underlying criminal case or decide on a plea today.
- You do not need to explain your version of events to your supervising officer; their role is monitoring compliance, not investigating the crime.
- You do not need to worry about the long-term impact of the charges while focusing on the immediate 24-hour compliance window.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a list of restrictive conditions can feel overwhelming and intrusive. These rules are standard administrative requirements designed to keep the case moving without you being held in jail. Most conditions are temporary and can be managed by following a strict schedule and maintaining clear communication with your legal counsel.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for complying with pretrial release orders in the United States. It does not cover specific state laws or federal sentencing guidelines. Later decisions regarding the modification or removal of conditions must be handled through formal legal motions filed by a qualified attorney.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Pretrial procedures vary significantly between federal, state, and local jurisdictions. Violating these conditions can result in the immediate revocation of your bond, a bench warrant for your arrest, and additional criminal charges. Always follow the written order of the court exactly until a judge signs a new order changing those terms.
