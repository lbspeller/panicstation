# What to do if… a merchant threatens to send a large charge to collections unless you pay immediately (USA)

## Short answer
Terminate the communication, refuse any immediate payment, and demand a written validation of the debt before taking further action.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay immediately to avoid a "today only" deadline or threatened credit damage.
- Do not provide a Social Security number, bank login, or one-time passcodes to the caller.
- Do not use payment links or phone numbers provided in a suspicious text or email.
- Do not admit the debt is yours or make a partial payment, as this can restart the statute of limitations.
- Do not pay via gift cards, cryptocurrency, or wire transfers.
- Do not engage with callers who contact you more than seven times in a seven-day period.

## What to do now
1. **End the high-pressure interaction immediately by stating you require all claims in writing.** **Refuse to discuss payment details over the phone and hang up once you have made this request.**
2. **Identify if the contact is the original merchant or a third-party debt collector.** 
   - A merchant collecting its own debt is generally not subject to the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) but must follow state-level consumer laws.
   - A third-party agency is strictly regulated by the FDCPA and Regulation F, requiring specific disclosures.
3. **Verify the existence of the charge through an independent, known channel.** **Log in to the merchant’s official portal or call the customer service number found on a previous verified statement.**
   - Ask for the specific transaction date, service description, and account number.
   - If the merchant claims the debt has been sold, request the name and contact information of the specific agency.
4. **Demand a written "Validation Notice" if you are dealing with a third-party collector.** **Note that under federal law, the collector must provide this information either during the first contact or within five days of the initial communication.**
5. **Submit a written dispute within 30 days of receiving the validation notice if you do not recognize the debt.** **Send this dispute via certified mail with a return receipt to ensure you have proof of delivery.**
   - Once a collector receives a written dispute, they must cease all collection activity until they provide verification of the debt.
6. **Initiate a billing error dispute with your credit card issuer if the charge appears on your statement.** **Follow the Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA) procedures by sending a written notice to the "billing inquiries" address within 60 days of the statement date.**
7. **Secure your financial accounts if you have already shared sensitive information.** **Contact your bank’s fraud department to freeze accounts or issue new cards if card numbers or bank details were disclosed during the call.**
8. **Document the interaction for your records while the details are fresh.** **Record the date, time, caller's name, company name, and the specific threats made (e.g., threat of arrest, immediate credit reporting, or legal action).**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to settle the debt or pay in full today.
- You do not need to respond to subsequent phone calls once you have requested written communication.
- You do not need to perform a full credit report audit until you have verified if the claim is legitimate.

## Important reassurance
High-pressure demands for immediate payment are a standard tactic used to bypass your consumer rights. Federal laws like the FDCPA and the FCBA are specifically designed to give you time to verify claims and dispute errors. Taking a pause to demand documentation is a legally protected action and does not constitute a refusal to pay a legitimate bill.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction for immediate financial pressure only. It does not cover long-term debt management, legal defense for lawsuits, or credit repair strategies.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal or financial advice. Consumer protection laws vary by state. Verification of all claims through official, independent channels is required before transferring any funds.
