# What to do if… you are told you must register your address locally and the deadline is very soon (USA)
## Short answer
Pause before sharing documents or money. Confirm the destination-country requirement through an official local government channel or your lodging provider, then keep a record of what you submit.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the message, but do not rush into an unofficial process because someone is pressuring you.
- Do not send passport, visa, or residence-document images to a contact you have not independently verified.
- Do not hand over your original passport to an unofficial contact. If a verified authority or verified lodging process needs to inspect or temporarily retain it, ask when and how it will be returned.
- Do not pay an unexplained fee through an unverified route. Ask for the official payment method and a receipt or other record.
- Do not confront the person who raised the issue if you feel unsafe.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the claim.** Record the deadline, the name of the rule if one was given, the office or website you were told to use, any requested fee, and who contacted you.
2. **Check your lodging record.** Ask your hotel, host, or accommodation provider whether it submitted any required guest or address registration for you. Ask for written confirmation of what it submitted and when.
3. **Check official destination guidance.** Use the U.S. Department of State destination page for the country you are visiting. Then check the destination country’s official local-authority website or ask that country’s embassy or consulate whether it can confirm the route or direct you to the relevant local authority.
4. **Verify the contact details independently.** Use contact details found on an official website, not only the phone number, address, link, or payment instructions in the message you received.
5. **Use the earliest verified official route available.** This may be an official online portal, your verified lodging provider, or an official local office. Follow the verified instructions for the documents required in your situation.
6. **Keep a record.** Ask for a submission confirmation, registration slip, stamped form, receipt, reference number, or email. Save a copy somewhere you can access if your phone is unavailable.
7. **Get help if the situation feels unsafe.** If someone is coercing you, withholding your documents, or making you feel unsafe, move to a safer place and use the local emergency number if you need immediate help. You can also contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate. If you cannot reach it during an emergency, use the U.S. Department of State Help Abroad page for contact routing and emergency helplines.
8. **Use STEP only if it does not delay the immediate task.** STEP can send updates and alerts from U.S. embassies and consulates abroad, but it does not complete local address registration.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide your long-term residency or visa plans right now.
- You do not need to work out why the issue arose before you confirm the correct official route.
- You do not need to make a complaint or confront anyone before you are safe and have saved your records.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to solve every travel or immigration question at once. Focus on three things first: the correct official route, the actual deadline, and the documents that the verified process requires.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Local registration rules may vary by destination, type of accommodation, and immigration status. Later decisions may need help from the relevant local authority or a suitably qualified specialist.
