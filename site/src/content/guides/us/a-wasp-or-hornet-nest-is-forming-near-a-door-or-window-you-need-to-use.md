# What to do if… a wasp or hornet nest is forming near a door or window you need to use (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using that entrance immediately, keep windows shut, and contact **licensed pest control** for professional removal. If someone is stung and has trouble breathing, swelling of the face / throat, or faintness, call **911** immediately and use an epinephrine auto-injector (e.g., EpiPen) if prescribed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hit, poke, or attempt to knock down an active nest yourself.
- Do not spray the nest with water, gasoline, or unapproved chemicals.
- Do not seal or plug the entrance hole if the nest is inside a wall or attic (this forces the insects to find a new exit, often inside the home).
- Do not stand directly in the flight path (the straight line insects use to enter and exit).
- Do not use a flashlight or bright light near the nest at night, as many species are attracted to light and may attack.
- Do not attempt to "smoke out" a nest.

## What to do now
1. **Switch to a different exit or entrance immediately.** Use a garage, back door, or secondary entrance to avoid the insects' flight path and the nest location. 
2. **Keep the affected window or door closed and locked.** Ensure screens are intact and do not open the window even for a moment, as vibrations can trigger defensive behavior.
3. **Establish a safety perimeter for residents and pets.** Ensure children and animals are kept away from the area; place a physical sign or note on the door to warn visitors or delivery drivers not to approach.
4. **Locate the specific entry point from a safe distance.** Observe where the insects are entering (e.g., a gap in the siding, a vent, or under the eaves) so you can accurately describe the location to a professional.
5. **Contact a licensed pest management professional.** 
   - **Renters:** Notify the landlord or property manager immediately; in many jurisdictions, stinging insect nests near entrances are considered a habitability issue.
   - **Homeowners:** Call a professional pest control service, especially for nests in wall voids or high eaves which require specialized equipment.
6. **Follow emergency medical protocols if a sting occurs.**
   - **Call 911** for signs of anaphylaxis: difficulty breathing, throat tightness, tongue swelling, or collapse.
   - **Position the person safely** by having them lie flat with their legs elevated if they feel faint or dizzy.
   - **Call Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222** for non-emergency first aid guidance or if you are unsure about a reaction.
7. **Protect yourself if you must pass the area.** 
   - Wear thick clothing that covers all skin, including a hat and eyewear.
   - Move slowly and calmly; do not wave your arms or run, as sudden movements can provoke an attack.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact species (wasp vs. hornet) before calling for help; treat all active nests near entrances as a hazard.
- You do not need to clean the area or remove nesting debris today.
- Long-term structural repairs (caulking, sealing gaps, or replacing vents) must wait until a professional confirms the nest is completely inactive.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel trapped or anxious when an entrance is blocked by stinging insects. Most social wasps and hornets are only aggressive when they perceive a threat to their nest; by maintaining distance and using an alternative route, you significantly reduce the risk of injury while waiting for professional help.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for nests near structural openings. It does not provide DIY removal instructions for active colonies, as these require specialized safety gear and professional-grade pesticides.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and is not a substitute for professional pest control or medical advice. Stinging insect allergies can develop at any time; always treat a severe reaction as a life-threatening emergency.
