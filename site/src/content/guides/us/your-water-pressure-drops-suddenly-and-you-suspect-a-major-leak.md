# What to do if… your water pressure drops suddenly and you suspect a major leak (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using water and shut off your home’s main water valve to limit damage. Then check for immediate electrical danger, check the meter if it is safely accessible, and contact the water utility, a plumber, or your landlord depending on what you find.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep running faucets “to test it”; that can worsen flooding if a pipe has failed.
- Don’t enter any area where water is near outlets, power strips, appliances, wiring, or the electrical panel.
- Don’t touch electrical equipment if you are wet or standing in water.
- Don’t try to patch an actively leaking pipe before shutting off the main water valve.
- Don’t cut into walls, pull down ceiling material, or dig outside in a panic.
- Don’t force open a stuck water-meter lid or enter traffic to reach a meter.
- Don’t assume it is only a city pressure issue before you have tried to secure your own shutoff.

## What to do now
1. **Stop all water use immediately.**  
   Shut off faucets, toilets, irrigation timers, the washing machine, dishwasher, and ice maker. Ask anyone else in the home not to use water for now.

2. **Shut off your home’s main water valve.**  
   Look where the water line enters the home: basement, crawlspace, garage, utility room, near the water heater, or on an exterior wall. A round handle usually closes clockwise; a lever valve usually closes when turned a quarter-turn so it is across the pipe. Do not use force if the valve feels stuck or starts leaking.

3. **Check for electrical danger before walking through wet areas.**  
   Keep people and pets away from pooled water, wet appliances, wet cords, outlets, and the electrical panel. If the breaker panel is dry and you can reach it without stepping in water, you may turn off power at the main breaker. If you cannot reach it safely, stay clear and call local emergency services if there is immediate danger, or contact your electric utility or a qualified electrician.

4. **If the water meter is safely accessible, check whether water still appears to be flowing.**  
   Only do this if you can reach the meter safely and without forcing the lid. With the home’s main valve closed and no water being used, look for a leak indicator, often a small triangle, star, gear, or flow display.
   - **If the indicator or dial is moving:** water may still be flowing between the meter and the home shutoff, through an irrigation branch, or through a shutoff that did not fully close. Call a plumber, and ask your water utility who is responsible for the line where the leak may be.
   - **If the indicator or dial is not moving:** your shutoff may be isolating the home for now. Still continue the safety scan and call for help if you saw, heard, or smelled signs of a leak.

5. **Do a fast check for signs of an active or hidden leak.**  
   Listen for rushing water. Look for new wet spots, dripping ceilings, bulging drywall, damp smells, unusual warm or damp floor areas, water around the water heater, or water coming from walls, ceilings, cabinets, or the yard. Do not stand under a bulging ceiling.

6. **Check whether the problem may be outside your home.**  
   Ask a nearby neighbor whether they also lost pressure. Look from a safe place for water bubbling from the street, sidewalk, driveway, yard, or meter box. If more than one home is affected, or you see water in the street, report a possible main break or low-pressure issue to your water utility through its emergency or outage reporting route.

7. **Call the right help now.**
   - **If you see or hear water inside the home, the meter still shows flow after your shutoff is closed, or the shutoff will not close:** call an emergency plumber.
   - **If nearby homes are also affected, water is coming from the street, or the issue appears utility-side:** call your water utility.
   - **If you rent or live in a managed building:** contact your landlord or property manager immediately and tell them the water pressure dropped suddenly, you suspect a major leak, and whether you shut off the main.
   - **If water is near electrical equipment and you cannot safely shut power off:** keep clear and call local emergency services if there is immediate danger, or contact the electric utility.

8. **Limit damage only where it is safe.**  
   Move electronics, papers, medications, and valuables away from wet areas. Place buckets or towels under drips if you can do it without standing in water or under a bulging ceiling. Open cabinet doors under sinks so you can see active dripping.

9. **Document the basics quickly.**  
   Take photos or video of the meter, wet areas, damaged items, and the time the pressure drop started. Do not delay shutoff or safety actions to take photos.

## What can wait
- You do not need to find the exact leak location right now.
- You do not need to decide on repair options, insurance decisions, or contractor estimates until the water is shut off and the area is safe.
- You do not need to start demolition, deep cleaning, or major drying until the leak is stopped and electrical hazards have been checked.
- You do not need to prove who is responsible for the pipe before making the first safety calls.

## Important reassurance
A sudden pressure drop can feel chaotic, but closing the main water valve is a strong first move that reduces damage and buys time. The meter check and neighbor check are only there to help route the problem to the right help, not to make you diagnose it perfectly.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to reduce immediate damage and help you contact the right support. Later decisions about leak location, repairs, drying, mold prevention, insurance, lease duties, or pipe responsibility may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional plumbing, electrical, legal, insurance, or property-management advice. If water may have reached electrical systems, keep clear and get qualified help.
