# What to do if… you receive a legal hold notice at work regarding your emails and documents (USA)

## Short answer
Stop deleting, editing, moving, or “cleaning up” anything that may relate to the hold. Acknowledge the notice, follow its instructions, and ask the designated legal, HR, compliance, or IT contact how preservation will be handled for your accounts and devices.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete emails, chats, texts, files, drafts, calendar items, notes, or attachments that may relate to the matter.
- Do not rename, re-save, backdate, edit, consolidate, or reorganize related files unless the hold notice or designated contact tells you to.
- Do not move work material to personal email, personal cloud storage, a USB drive, or another account.
- Do not empty Trash, Deleted Items, Recycle Bin, archive folders, chat history, or old versions for related material.
- Do not forward the hold notice broadly or discuss details in large group chats.
- Do not decide on your own that something is unimportant enough to delete; preserve first and ask.
- Do not assume backups are enough; routine retention settings may still delete some data unless the right hold is applied.

## What to do now
1. **Pause deletion and cleanup immediately.**  
   Stop deleting, editing, wiping, or tidying anything that may relate to the people, projects, time period, topics, or systems named in the hold notice.

2. **Protect the places most likely to contain related information.**  
   Check your work email, chat apps, shared drives, SharePoint, OneDrive, Google Drive, local work folders, calendar entries, meeting notes, work phone messages, and any approved work apps named in the notice.

3. **Pause any auto-cleanup you personally control.**  
   Turn off or pause mailbox cleanup rules, auto-archive rules, scripts that purge folders, routines that clear downloads, or personal reminders to delete old chats, until IT or the designated contact confirms what is allowed.

4. **Read the hold notice and write down the basics for yourself.**  
   Note the matter name or reference, the time period, the topics, the people or teams mentioned, the systems covered, and the named contact for questions.

5. **Acknowledge receipt through the requested channel.**  
   Keep the reply simple: say you received the notice, are preserving potentially related material, and will ask before taking any action that could alter or delete data.

6. **Ask for clarification if the scope is unclear.**  
   Ask what sources are in scope for you, whether chats, texts, shared drives, local folders, paper notes, or personal-device work messages are covered, and whether an administrator will apply any technical hold.

7. **Flag anything at risk of routine deletion today.**  
   Tell the designated contact if there are short chat-retention settings, mailbox quotas, planned device replacement, offboarding steps, shared folders with deletion schedules, or project tools that auto-delete after a set time.

8. **Make a location inventory, not a self-collection.**  
   List where related information may be stored, such as email folders, chat spaces, shared drives, local folders, work phone messages, or notebooks. Do not export, screenshot, forward, or copy material unless the designated contact gives you an approved method.

9. **Disclose any personal-device or personal-account involvement without moving the data.**  
   For example, say, “Some work-related texts may be on my personal phone.” Then wait for legal, HR, compliance, or IT instructions before copying, forwarding, deleting, or changing anything.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide what the case is about or who is right.
- You do not need to produce documents to anyone unless the official process asks for them.
- You do not need to write a full narrative statement unless HR, legal, compliance, or counsel asks.
- You do not need to contact outside parties yourself unless the designated contact instructs you.
- You do not need to solve the technical hold yourself if that is not your role.

## Important reassurance
A legal hold can be sent to many people and does not by itself mean you did anything wrong. The safest response is steady and narrow: preserve potentially related information, avoid changing or moving it, and route questions through the named contact.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on preventing accidental deletion, alteration, or other irreversible mistakes. Later decisions about scope, collection, production, personal devices, privacy, employment duties, or legal rights may need help from your employer’s legal, HR, compliance, IT teams, counsel, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you are unsure whether something is covered by the hold, ask the designated legal, HR, compliance, or IT contact before taking any action that alters, moves, copies, forwards, or deletes data.
