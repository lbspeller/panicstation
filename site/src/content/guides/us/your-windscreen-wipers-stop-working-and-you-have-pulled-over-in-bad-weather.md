# What to do if… your windscreen wipers stop working and you have pulled over in bad weather (USA)

## Short answer
Stay parked in a safe, visible location and do not attempt to drive until you have a clear, unobstructed view of the road. Activate your hazard lights immediately to remain visible to other drivers and call for professional roadside assistance if the issue cannot be resolved with a simple defrosting.

## Do not do these things
- Don't attempt to "limp" the vehicle to the next exit if you cannot see the lane markings or vehicles ahead.
- Don't stand on the traffic side of the vehicle or between your car and the flow of traffic to inspect the wipers.
- Don't stop in a "blind" spot, such as just over the crest of a hill or on a sharp curve, where approaching drivers cannot see your hazard lights.
- Don't force the wiper arms if they are frozen to the glass, as this can strip the wiper motor gears or tear the rubber blades.
- Don't use your bare hands to wipe the outside of the glass; skin oils will cause severe smearing and further reduce visibility.

## What to do now
1. **Secure your position on the shoulder.**
   - Keep your **seatbelt buckled** at all times while stopped on a high-speed roadway.
   - Turn on your **hazard lights** (four-way flashers) to alert other motorists and trigger "Move Over" law protections.
   - Position your front wheels **pointed away from the road** so that the vehicle does not roll into traffic if hit from behind.
2. **Assess visibility and sensory cues from inside the cab.**
   - Check for the **smell of electrical burning** or a **whirring / grinding noise** when you toggle the wiper switch.
   - Use the **defroster on the highest heat setting** to see if the wipers are simply frozen to the windshield.
   - Look for **physical obstructions** like heavy slush, ice dams at the base of the windshield, or a loose wiper arm nut.
3. **Attempt only low-risk, non-technical recovery steps.**
   - Cycle the wiper switch through **all speeds** (Intermittent, Low, High) and press the washer fluid button.
   - If it is safe to exit on the non-traffic side, clear **heavy snow or ice** from the base of the windshield by hand or with a brush.
   - Ensure the wiper arms are **firmly pressed against the glass**; if they have "popped" up, carefully fold them back down.
4. **Determine if you require emergency or non-emergency assistance.**
   - Call **911** if you are stopped in a live lane of traffic, on a narrow shoulder with high-speed traffic, or if visibility is so low you fear being struck.
   - Call **roadside assistance** or a tow service if you are in a safe spot but cannot restore visibility.
   - Dial **511** for state-specific road conditions and to check if "Motorist Aid" or "Freeway Assist" patrols are active in your specific area.
5. **Manage the cabin environment while you wait.**
   - Keep the **defroster running** to prevent interior fogging and to keep the glass warm.
   - Conserve your **phone battery** by using a car charger; you may need it for GPS location sharing with the tow operator.
   - Stay inside the **locked vehicle** with your seatbelt on until the service vehicle arrives.

## What can wait
- You do not need to open the hood or check the fuse box in the rain or snow.
- You do not need to determine the exact mechanical cause (motor vs. linkage) right now.
- You do not need to choose a repair shop or estimate costs while still on the shoulder.

## Important reassurance
Losing visibility suddenly is a high-stress event, but you have already taken the most important step by stopping the vehicle. Most wiper failures are simple electrical or mechanical issues that can be resolved safely once the vehicle is towed to a shop or the weather clears. Staying put is a professional-level safety decision.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a roadside visibility emergency. It does not cover technical repairs, fuse replacement, or long-term mechanical troubleshooting.

## Important note
This information is for general safety guidance and does not replace the specific emergency procedures found in your vehicle’s owner’s manual. Roadside conditions change rapidly; prioritize your physical safety over the condition of the vehicle.
