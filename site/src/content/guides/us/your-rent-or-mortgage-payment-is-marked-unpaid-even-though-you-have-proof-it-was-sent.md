# What to do if… your rent or mortgage payment is marked unpaid even though you have proof it was sent (USA)

## Short answer
Do not send a second payment in panic. Put your proof in one place and contact the landlord, property manager, or mortgage servicer in writing today asking them to trace the payment, correct the ledger or payment history, and pause late fees or escalation while they check.

## Do not do these things
- Do not send a second payment “just to be safe” unless you can afford it and you have written confirmation of how any duplicate payment will be returned or credited.
- Do not ignore a pay-or-quit notice, eviction filing, foreclosure letter, default letter, or court paperwork.
- Do not rely only on phone calls. If you speak by phone, follow up in writing with the date, time, person you spoke with, and what was said.
- Do not stop making future required payments if you can pay them, because new missed payments can make the problem harder to fix.
- Do not follow unexpected new payment instructions sent by email, text, or portal message until you have independently verified them through a known official contact route.

## What to do now
1. **Make one proof bundle.**  
   Save copies of:
   - The bank statement line, payment confirmation, date, amount, and payee.
   - Any bill-pay confirmation, tracking number, reference number, or confirmation ID.
   - Rent portal or app screenshots showing paid, pending, processing, failed, or reversed status.
   - Money order, cashier check, or check receipts, including images or copies if you have them.
   - Your lease/account details for rent, or your loan number and property address for a mortgage.

2. **Send a short written correction request today.**  
   Use the landlord/property manager email or portal for rent, or the mortgage servicer’s secure message center or official customer-service route for a mortgage. Say:
   - The payment date, amount, and method.
   - The confirmation, tracking, check, money order, or reference number.
   - That proof is attached.
   - That you are asking them to trace the payment, correct the ledger or payment history, and confirm the current amount due.
   - That you are asking them to pause late fees, default steps, eviction steps, foreclosure steps, or credit reporting while they investigate.

3. **If it is rent, ask for the rent ledger and payment trace.**  
   Ask for a current rent ledger showing every charge, credit, fee, and balance. Ask them to check whether the payment was posted to the wrong tenant account, wrong unit, wrong month, old portal account, or suspense account.

4. **If it is a mortgage, consider sending a formal Notice of Error.**  
   A mortgage servicer’s failure to accept, apply, or credit a payment can be a servicing error under Regulation X. Check your mortgage statement or servicer website for a specific Notice of Error or error-resolution address. If the servicer has provided one, use that address. Use an online message or portal only if the servicer identifies it as a way to submit a Notice of Error. Keep a copy of exactly what you sent and proof of when you sent it.

5. **Ask your bank or payment provider what happened to the money.**  
   Ask whether the payment cleared, is pending, was returned, was reversed, or was sent to the wrong payee or account. Ask for written documentation you can add to your proof bundle.

6. **If you received eviction, foreclosure, default, or court paperwork, treat it as urgent.**  
   Do not miss a response deadline or hearing because you believe the payment record is wrong. Save the paperwork, bring or submit your proof bundle, and contact local legal aid, a tenant-help organization, a HUD-approved housing counselor, or a qualified local attorney as soon as you can.

7. **If a late payment appears on your credit report, dispute it in writing.**  
   Dispute the error with the company that reported it and with each credit bureau showing the error. Attach copies, not originals, of your proof bundle and written correction request. Keep records of what you sent and when.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to move, refinance, change banks, or start a lawsuit.
- You do not need to write a long explanation. A short written request with clear proof is enough for the first contact.
- Refunds, fee reversals, and reimbursement can usually wait until the ledger or payment history is corrected.
- Long-term decisions about the lease, loan, bank, or payment app can wait until you know where the payment went.

## Important reassurance
Payment posting mistakes, portal mismatches, and servicing-transfer confusion can happen. Having proof helps you ask for a trace and correction. The safest next move is to keep everything in writing, preserve your records, and respond quickly to any notice or court deadline.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, focused on stabilizing the situation and reducing harm. Rent, eviction, foreclosure, credit reporting, and mortgage-servicing rules can depend on your state, court, loan type, and paperwork, so later decisions may need qualified local help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, housing, credit, or professional advice. If you have eviction papers, foreclosure papers, a court date, or a deadline you do not understand, consider getting qualified local help right away.
