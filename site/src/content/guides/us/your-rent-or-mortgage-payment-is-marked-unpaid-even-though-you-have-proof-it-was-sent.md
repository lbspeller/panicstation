# What to do if… your rent or mortgage payment is marked unpaid even though you have proof it was sent (USA)

## Short answer
Do not issue a duplicate payment immediately. Submit a formal written "Notice of Error" (for mortgages) or a "Payment Dispute" (for rent) alongside your digital or physical proof of payment to force a manual investigation and freeze late fee escalation.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t pay the amount a second time before confirming the first payment was truly cancelled or returned by your bank.
- Don’t delete any email confirmations, text alerts, or browser history related to the payment session.
- Don’t rely on verbal promises from a phone agent or property manager; verbal statements are not legally binding in a ledger dispute.
- Don’t ignore a "Notice to Pay or Quit" or a "Notice of Intent to Foreclose" even if you have proof of payment; these legal clocks do not stop automatically.
- Don’t assume the error is a scam and ignore it; while scams exist, actual processing errors can lead to real eviction or foreclosure filings if not contested.

## What to do now
1. **Gather your sensory proof and transaction data (10 minutes).**  
   **Collect every objective data point showing the transaction was initiated and completed.**  
   - Screen-capture the "Success" or "Confirmed" screen from the payment portal.
   - Download the bank statement or transaction activity showing the funds withdrawn (Status: Cleared / Settled).
   - Locate the specific Trace Number, Confirmation ID, or Money Order serial number.
   - For physical checks, request a "Front and Back" image from your bank to see if it was endorsed/deposited.
2. **Submit a written Correction Request or Notice of Error immediately.**  
   **Send a formal communication that triggers legal response timelines.**  
   - For Rent: Email the landlord or property manager with the subject "Dispute of Unpaid Rent - [Unit Number]."
   - For Mortgage: Send a formal "Notice of Error" as defined by Regulation X (RESPA) to the servicer's designated "Notice of Error" address.
   - State clearly: "I am disputing the 'unpaid' status of my [Date] payment of $[Amount]. Attached is proof of payment. Please update the ledger and acknowledge receipt within 5 business days."
3. **Request a current, itemized transaction history.**  
   **Obtain the internal record the landlord or servicer is using to claim non-payment.**  
   - For Rent: Request a "Rent Ledger" from the first of the year to the present.
   - For Mortgage: Request a "Payment History" or "Life of Loan" transcript.
   - Compare these records to your bank statements to find exactly where the "gap" occurred (e.g., a payment applied to the wrong month or account).
4. **Initiate a trace with your financial institution.**  
   **Ask your bank to verify the exact destination of the funds.**  
   - Request the "ACH Trace Number" for electronic transfers or the "Proof of Debit" for bill-pay checks.
   - If the payment was returned, ask for the specific "Reason Code" (e.g., R01 - Insufficient Funds, R03 - No Account).
5. **Enforce the 60-day credit reporting "Safe Harbor" (Mortgage only).**  
   **Remind the servicer of their legal restrictions during an active dispute.**  
   - Under 12 CFR § 1024.35, once a servicer receives a Notice of Error, they are generally prohibited from reporting that specific payment as delinquent to credit bureaus for 60 days.
   - Include this sentence in your correspondence: "Per Regulation X, I expect no adverse credit reporting regarding this disputed payment while your investigation is pending."
6. **Attend all court dates regardless of payment proof.**  
   **Physically appear or file a formal answer if an eviction or foreclosure is filed.**  
   - Bring three printed copies of your "proof bundle" to court.
   - Present your written "Correction Request" to show the court you attempted to resolve the error in good faith before the filing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to hire a private investigator to find where the money went; the bank and servicer have the tools to trace it once triggered in writing.
- You do not need to update your credit report today; the bureaus will not accept a dispute until the servicer has officially reported the "late" status, which usually takes 30 days.
- You do not need to decide on moving or selling the property until the investigation window (typically 30 business days for mortgages) has closed.

## Important reassurance
Payment systems, particularly automated portals and third-party bill-pay services, occasionally fail or misroute funds. This is a common administrative error and does not reflect your status as a responsible tenant or homeowner. As long as you have a time-stamped record of the payment, you have the legal standing to correct the record and have late fees reversed.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a payment mismatch. It does not cover long-term debt restructuring or defense strategies for cases where funds were actually insufficient at the time of the draw.

## Important note
This information is provided for educational purposes and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Jurisdictional laws regarding eviction and foreclosure vary significantly by state; consult a local housing attorney or HUD-approved counselor if you receive a court summons.
