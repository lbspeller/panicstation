# What to do if… an online bill payment is sent to the wrong payee and the due date is near (USA)

## Short answer
Identify if the payment is a check or electronic transfer, then immediately request a "stop payment" or "ACH reversal" through your bank's bill pay support. Separately, make a manual replacement payment to the correct biller to avoid late penalties while the bank attempts to recover the original funds.

## Do not do these things
- Avoid waiting for a refund before paying the actual bill due.
- Avoid sending a second payment to the wrong recipient to "correct" the first one.
- Avoid providing remote access to your computer or phone to anyone claiming to be "bill pay support" for a reversal.
- Avoid classifying a self-made error as "identity theft" or "fraud" when reporting to the bank, as this may trigger incorrect legal protocols.
- Avoid deleting the original confirmation of the incorrect payment until the funds are recovered.

## What to do now
1. **Verify the payment delivery method and status in your bank's portal.**  
   **Determine if the payment was sent as an electronic ACH (Automated Clearing House) transfer or a mailed paper check.**
   - Save a screenshot or PDF of the payment details, including the confirmation number and recipient address.

2. **Cancel the payment immediately if the status is "Scheduled" or "Pending."**  
   **Use the "Cancel Payment" button in the bill pay dashboard before the bank's daily processing cut-off time.**
   - If the payment is part of a recurring series, pause or delete the entire series to prevent the error from repeating next month.

3. **Request a "Stop Payment" if the bank sent a physical paper check.**  
   **Call the bank's customer service and ask to place a stop-payment order on the specific check number.**
   - Confirm with the agent if the check has already been cashed; if it has not, the stop-payment is usually effective immediately.
   - Note that banks typically charge a fee (often $20–$35) for this service.

4. **Request an "ACH Reversal" if the payment was electronic and sent within the last 5 business days.**  
   **Ask the bank to initiate a reversal for an "erroneous entry" under Nacha rules.**
   - Be aware that for standard one-time "credit-push" payments you initiated, the bank may not be able to pull funds back without the recipient's permission once they have cleared.
   - If the payment was a "preauthorized" recurring debit (auto-pay), you have a legal right under Regulation E to stop it if you notify the bank 3 business days before the scheduled date.

5. **Contact the correct biller to arrange an emergency payment.**  
   **Explain that a payment was misdirected and ask for the fastest way to post funds today to meet the due date.**
   - Ask for a one-time "late fee hold" or "grace period extension" while you resolve the bank error.
   - Use a direct payment method (such as the biller’s own website or a phone payment) rather than the bank's bill pay system for this replacement.

6. **Notify the wrong payee directly if they are a known entity.**  
   **Send a clear, written notice to their customer service department stating that a payment was sent in error and requesting a refund.**
   - Provide the date, amount, and your identifying information (if they were a former biller of yours).

7. **Document the bank's response and your report.**  
   **Record the date, time, and name of the representative you spoke with, along with any case or reference numbers.**
   - Under Regulation E, you must report electronic errors within 60 days of the statement date to maintain your full consumer rights.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on long-term changes to your banking setup or autopay preferences right now.
- You do not need to file a formal CFPB (Consumer Financial Protection Bureau) complaint immediately; this is only necessary if the bank fails to follow their stated error-resolution timeline.
- You do not need to worry about credit score impacts for a few days, as most creditors do not report a payment as "late" to credit bureaus until it is 30 days past the due date.

## Important reassurance
Misdirected bill payments are a common occurrence and banks have established workflows for both stop-payments and reversals. While the recovery of funds is not always instantaneous, taking immediate action to pay the correct biller prevents the most significant consequence: the disruption of your services or credit standing.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for misdirected online bill payments. It does not cover fraudulent transfers where your account credentials were stolen, which requires a different security protocol.

## Important note
This information is provided for general guidance and is not legal or financial advice. Consumer protections under Regulation E and Nacha rules depend on the specific type of transfer and the timing of your report. Individual bank policies for stop-payments and reversal fees vary.
