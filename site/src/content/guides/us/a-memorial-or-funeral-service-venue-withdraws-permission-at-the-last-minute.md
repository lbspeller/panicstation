# What to do if… a memorial or funeral service venue withdraws permission at the last minute (USA)
## Short answer
Pause before starting or continuing the service at that location. Ask one person to speak with the person in charge; if they confirm that the gathering cannot remain, move attendees out calmly and switch to a permitted holding point or a confirmed alternate location.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t argue with the owner, manager, or staff in front of mourners.
- Don’t start or continue the service at that location after the person in charge confirms that the gathering cannot remain.
- Don’t block entrances, refuse to leave, or encourage attendees to confront anyone.
- Don’t announce a replacement location until permission and practical arrangements are confirmed.
- Don’t post accusations online in the moment.
- Don’t call 911 for a routine venue dispute. Use 911 for an emergency involving immediate danger, threats, or violence.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the service and appoint one contact person.**  
   Say: “We’re pausing while we confirm the arrangements. Please move away from the entrances with [name].” Ask one calm person to speak for the group so that the owner, manager, or staff are not approached by several people at once.

2. **Ask who is making the decision.**  
   Ask whether that person is authorized to speak for the home or venue, whether the group must leave now, and whether there is time to collect belongings calmly. If you are told to leave, do not delay the exit to argue about the decision.

3. **Move attendees to a permitted holding point.**  
   Move away from entrances and avoid blocking access. Ask a support person to check whether anyone needs help with mobility, transport, children, belongings, or access to medication.

4. **Contact the people who can confirm an alternative.**  
   Contact the funeral director, the lead family contact, and the officiant or clergy team, where relevant. Ask them to confirm a permitted alternate location and send one clear update to attendees.

5. **Use a brief alternative only where permission is clear.**  
   Where the person in charge expressly permits it, a short reading or moment of silence may be enough for the immediate moment. Otherwise, wait until a permitted location is confirmed.

6. **Give attendees one clear message.**  
   Say: “We cannot hold the service at this location. Please move with us to [permitted holding point]. We will share the confirmed next location as soon as it is ready. Please do not approach the owner, manager, or staff.”

7. **Arrange belongings safely.**  
   Collect belongings only if this is permitted and can be done calmly. If not, ask for a later collection arrangement rather than returning repeatedly or escalating the situation.

8. **Use emergency help only when needed.**  
   Call **911** if there is immediate danger, a threat, or violence. For a non-emergency issue where local law-enforcement assistance is needed, use the relevant local agency’s published non-emergency contact route.

9. **Keep records for later.**  
   Once attendees are settled, save contracts, emails, texts, receipts, and a brief note of what happened. If you think discrimination may have been involved, record the facts and consider specialist advice or an appropriate complaint route later. Applicable protections and routes may depend on the reason for the refusal, the type of venue, and the circumstances.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to decide today whether to seek a refund, make a complaint, or take legal advice.
- You do **not** need to establish fault in front of mourners.
- You do **not** need to create a full replacement service immediately. A clear pause and a simple permitted alternative are enough for now.
- Contracts, extra costs, complaints, and any public statement can wait until people are settled.

## Important reassurance
A last-minute refusal can be upsetting, especially during bereavement. Pausing, leaving calmly, and finding a simpler permitted alternative protects the people attending and gives the family space to decide what comes next.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate disruption only. Later decisions about contracts, refunds, complaints, or legal options may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal or other professional advice. Rules, contracts, and complaint routes can vary by state, locality, venue type, and circumstances.
