# What to do if… you lose access to your financial accounts because your phone number changed or SIM was swapped (USA)

## Short answer
Contact your bank's fraud department from a different phone line immediately to freeze all transfers, then contact your mobile carrier's fraud team to reclaim your number and activate a port-out lock.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to log in repeatedly or request multiple password resets, as this can trigger extended security lockouts.
- Do not use the compromised phone to call your bank if it has "No Service" or "SOS Only" status; use a trusted landline or another person's device.
- Do not trust any incoming calls or texts that claim to be from "Security" or "Technical Support" during this window.
- Do not approve any "push" notifications or multi-factor authentication (MFA) prompts that appear on your screen.
- Do not assume that regaining phone service automatically secures your bank; these are separate security layers.

## What to do now
1. **Call your bank’s fraud department using a different, trusted phone line.** Use the number on the back of your physical debit or credit card.
   - Inform the agent: "I am a victim of a SIM swap or port-out fraud and my mobile number is compromised."
   - Request an immediate "security freeze" on all outgoing transfers, including Zelle, ACH, and wire transfers.
2. **Ask the bank to remove your mobile number as a recovery method.** Request that they switch your identity verification to a physical in-branch ID check or a secondary, secure email address that does not use SMS for its own recovery.
3. **Contact your mobile carrier's specialized fraud or "Porting" team.** Tell them your number was transferred without your consent.
   - Under FCC rules, they must investigate and provide documentation of the fraud.
   - Request that they "Reverse the SIM swap" or "Reclaim the port-out."
   - Explicitly ask to "Activate a Port-Out Lock" and a "SIM Change Lock" on your account moving forward.
4. **Secure the email account linked to your financial services.** Change your email password from a trusted computer. 
   - Check your "Sent" folder and "Deleted Items" for any password reset requests you did not initiate.
   - Review "Forwarding Rules" to ensure the attacker is not BCCing themselves on your emails.
5. **Log into payment apps from a trusted device to revoke sessions.** If you still have access to apps like Venmo, CashApp, or PayPal, go to settings and select "Log out of all other sessions." 
   - Unlink any bank accounts or cards until your mobile number is restored.
6. **Report the incident to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC).** Go to **IdentityTheft.gov** to create an official Identity Theft Report. This document is required to access certain legal protections and extended fraud alerts.
7. **File a complaint with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center.** Visit **ic3.gov** to report the cyber-enabled financial fraud. This helps law enforcement track the "port-out" patterns and may assist in fund recovery.
8. **Place a Fraud Alert on your credit reports.** Contact one of the three bureaus; they are legally required to notify the other two.
   - Equifax: 1-800-525-6285
   - Experian: 1-888-397-3742
   - TransUnion: 1-800-680-7289

## What can wait
- You do not need to buy a new physical phone immediately unless the device itself was stolen.
- You do not need to notify utility companies or non-financial subscriptions today.
- You do not need to file a local police report immediately unless the bank or carrier specifically requests one to process a claim.
- You do not need to change your permanent phone number yet; reclaiming your existing one is usually the priority.

## Important reassurance
Losing control of your phone number is a high-stress event, but it is a recognized form of financial crime with established recovery protocols. Banks and carriers deal with "Account Takeovers" (ATO) daily. Taking the steps to freeze your money movement within the first few hours is the most effective way to prevent permanent loss.

## Scope note
This guide covers the first 24 hours of stabilization after a SIM swap or port-out. It does not cover long-term credit repair or the technical setup of hardware security keys.

## Important note
This information is for crisis stabilization and does not constitute legal or financial advice. If you are in immediate physical danger or suspect a home-invasion-style "SIM swap" involving physical coercion, call 911 immediately.
