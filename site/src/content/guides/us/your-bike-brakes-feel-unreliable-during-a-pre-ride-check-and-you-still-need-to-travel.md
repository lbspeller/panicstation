# What to do if… your bike brakes feel unreliable during a pre-ride check and you still need to travel (USA)

## Short answer
Do not ride the bicycle on public roads if either brake lever feels soft, pulls to the handlebar, or fails to stop the wheel decisively. Secure the bike and use an alternative transportation method immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t attempt to "test" the brakes in active traffic or on a descent.
- Don’t ride the bike if you can squeeze the lever and touch the handlebar.
- Don’t rely on a single working brake for a commute; US safety standards require two independent braking systems for safe operation.
- Don’t attempt complex mechanical adjustments (like bleeding hydraulic lines or over-tightening cables) if you are under time pressure.
- Don’t assume the brakes will "warm up" or improve during the ride.

## What to do now
1. **Decide immediately to use an alternative mode of transport for your trip.** **Commit to not riding the bicycle until a full mechanical inspection is completed, as unreliable brakes pose a high risk of collision.**
2. **Perform a stationary "ABC Quick Check" to confirm the failure.** **Squeeze each brake lever individually with firm pressure and verify that there is at least a thumb-width of space between the lever and the handlebar.**
   - If the lever touches the bar, the system has failed.
   - If the lever feels "spongy" or the pressure fluctuates, there is likely air in the hydraulic lines or a failing cable.
3. **Inspect the wheel and brake pad alignment without using tools.** **Verify that the wheels are fully seated in the dropouts and that all quick-release levers or thru-axles are closed and secure.**
   - Check that brake pads are not missing, significantly misaligned, or worn down past the "wear groove" or to less than 1/4 inch of thickness.
   - For e-bikes, check for the required UL 2849 or EN 15194 safety certification labels on the frame to ensure the system meets 2026 standards.
4. **Walk the bicycle to a secure location or a repair shop.** **Do not sit on the saddle or coast; walk alongside the bike and use any remaining braking power solely to control the bike's walking pace.**
5. **Switch to a safe travel alternative.** **Utilize public transit, a ride-hail service, a taxi, or walk to your destination.**
6. **Communicate your delay to necessary parties.** **Notify your employer or contact that you have experienced a vehicle equipment failure and are switching to a safer transport method.**
7. **Consult the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) for active recalls.** **Search for your bicycle brand and model on the CPSC website to determine if your braking issue is related to a known manufacturing defect, such as the 2026 coaster brake or chainring bolt recalls.**
8. **Schedule a professional inspection at a certified bicycle shop.** **Explain the specific symptoms, such as "lever pulls to bar," "grinding noise," or "intermittent power loss on e-bike motor cut-off," to the mechanic.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the technical root cause (e.g., cable stretch vs. seal failure) right now.
- You do not need to purchase replacement parts or tools today.
- You do not need to feel guilty for being late; a mechanical failure is an objective safety barrier.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel frustrated or pressured when a pre-ride check reveals a problem. Choosing not to ride an unsafe vehicle is a professional and responsible decision that prevents avoidable injury. Equipment failures happen regardless of maintenance history.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction for a pre-ride mechanical concern. It does not replace a professional mechanical diagnosis or legal advice regarding vehicle safety requirements.

## Important note
Bicycle braking systems are safety-critical components. If you are not a certified mechanic, do not attempt to repair hydraulic systems or internal hub brakes. Federal and state laws generally require bicycles to be equipped with brakes that enable the operator to make a banked one-braked wheel skid on dry, level, clean pavement. If your bike cannot do this, it is not street-legal in the USA.
