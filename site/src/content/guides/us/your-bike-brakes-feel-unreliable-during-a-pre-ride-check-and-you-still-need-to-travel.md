# What to do if… your bike brakes feel unreliable during a pre-ride check and you still need to travel (USA)

## Short answer
Do not start the trip by bike if either brake feels weak, inconsistent, or uncertain. Secure the bike and switch to a safer way to travel, such as public transit, a taxi or ride-hail, walking, or getting a ride.

## Do not do these things
- Do not “test it in traffic” or start the trip hoping the brakes improve.
- Do not ride downhill, ride fast, or cross busy intersections if braking is not clearly strong and consistent.
- Do not rely on one working brake when the other brake feels unreliable.
- Do not make unfamiliar brake repairs while rushed.
- Do not keep riding if a lever pulls close to the handlebar, braking power changes from squeeze to squeeze, stopping distance feels longer than normal, or you feel grinding.

## What to do now
1. **Make the travel decision first.** Treat the bike as unsafe for this trip unless you can confidently confirm that both brakes work normally.
2. **Check the brakes away from traffic.** On flat ground in a driveway, parking lot, or other quiet place, walk the bike forward slowly and squeeze one brake lever at a time.
   - Better signs: the lever feels firm, the brake bites predictably, and the wheel stops decisively.
   - Unsafe signs: the lever comes close to the handlebar, the brake feels weak or inconsistent, the bike keeps rolling, or you feel grinding or metal-on-metal contact.
   - If you are unsure, do not ride.
3. **Look for only obvious no-tools problems.** Stop and do not ride if you see any of these:
   - A wheel not sitting straight in the frame or fork.
   - A quick-release lever open or obviously loose.
   - A brake pad missing, badly worn, or not lining up with the rim or rotor.
   - A brake cable visibly frayed, detached, or hanging loose.
   - A brake rubbing heavily while the wheel spins, turns, or moves.
4. **Switch travel mode now.** Use public transit, a taxi or ride-hail, walking, or a ride from someone nearby. If you are late, send a simple message that your bike has an unsafe brake issue and you are changing transport.
5. **Move the bike without riding it.** Walk it to a secure place, home, work, or a bike shop. Avoid coasting, even for a short distance.
6. **Secure the bike.** Put it indoors if you can. If it must stay outside, lock the frame and at least one wheel, and remove lights, bags, and other easy-to-steal accessories.
7. **Arrange repair before riding again.** Call a bike shop and describe the symptom in plain words, such as “front brake weak,” “lever pulls close to the handlebar,” “brake cable looks loose,” or “disc brake squeals and will not bite.” Mention if it is an e-bike.
8. **Check for recalls if the issue seems sudden or unusual.** Search the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission recalls page for your bike, brake, or e-bike brand and model. You can also ask the manufacturer or bike shop whether there is a known recall or safety notice.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact brake fault right now.
- You do not need to choose upgrades, parts, or a full tune-up today.
- You do not need to finish the trip by bike.
- You do not need to explain the whole situation to everyone; a short delay message is enough.

## Important reassurance
Feeling pressure to ride anyway is common when you are already trying to get somewhere. Choosing another way to travel is a practical safety decision, not an overreaction.

## Scope note
These are first steps to keep you out of traffic with unreliable brakes and buy time. Detailed troubleshooting, repair choices, and any later transport decisions may need help from a bike mechanic or another suitable specialist.

## Important note
This is general safety information, not professional mechanical, legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you cannot confidently confirm strong, consistent braking, do not ride on any route; use another transport option and get the bike inspected.
