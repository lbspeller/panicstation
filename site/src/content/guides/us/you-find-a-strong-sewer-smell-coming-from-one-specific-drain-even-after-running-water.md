# What to do if… you find a strong sewer smell coming from one specific drain even after running water (USA)

## Short answer
Treat it as possible sewer gas: get fresh air moving, do not mix or repeat drain chemicals, stop using that fixture for now, and arrange help if the smell does not clear quickly.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep smelling the drain up close if the odor is strong or anyone feels unwell.
- Don’t mix bleach, ammonia products, vinegar, toilet cleaner, drain cleaner, or other cleaning chemicals.
- Don’t pour in repeated drain chemicals to “force” the smell away.
- Don’t dismantle traps, vents, or toilet seals if you rent, are unsure, or see signs of leakage.
- Don’t ignore backups, gurgling, sewage coming up, or the same odor from several fixtures.
- Don’t assume it is definitely sewer gas. If you are unsure whether it could be a fuel gas leak, leave the area for fresh air and contact your gas utility; call 911 if there is immediate danger.

## What to do now
1. Get fresh air in the room. Open a window if you can, use the bathroom fan if you know it vents outdoors, and keep the door closed so the smell does not spread through the home.
2. Move people and pets away from the strongest smell. If anyone has headache, dizziness, nausea, breathing irritation, eye or throat irritation, move them to fresh air.
3. Check whether it is one fixture without deep sniffing. Briefly note whether the odor is strongest at the sink drain, sink overflow, shower or tub drain, floor drain, cabinet under the sink, or near a toilet base.
4. Check for a wider plumbing problem. If several drains smell, drains are slow, drains gurgle, water backs up, or sewage appears, stop using nearby water fixtures and contact a plumber or your property manager.
5. For a floor drain or seldom-used drain, add a small amount of water once to refill the trap. Stop if water backs up or the smell gets worse.
6. Clean only the reachable opening. Use dish soap, warm-to-hot water, and a small brush around the drain opening, stopper, strainer, or sink overflow. Do not add bleach, ammonia, vinegar, toilet cleaner, or drain cleaner.
7. Look for visible signs of a leak without taking plumbing apart. Check under the sink for dampness, drips, staining, swollen cabinet material, or odor strongest inside the cabinet. Check near the toilet base for wetness or a persistent sewage smell.
8. Isolate the fixture if the odor persists. Put in the stopper or plug if available, keep ventilating, and avoid using that fixture until it is inspected.
9. Contact the right help.
   - If you rent, notify your landlord or property manager promptly and describe the exact fixture, odor, any symptoms, and any backup or slow draining.
   - If you own, contact a qualified plumber; use a licensed plumber where your area requires licensing.
   - If multiple fixtures are affected, sewage is backing up, sewage is outside, or neighbors are affected, contact your city or county sewer utility. If you use a septic system, contact your septic service.
10. Get medical or poison guidance if anyone has symptoms. Call Poison Help at 1-800-222-1222 for guidance about possible exposure. Call 911 if someone collapses, has trouble breathing, has a seizure, or cannot be awakened.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether the cause is a dry trap, dirty overflow, partial clog, failed seal, venting issue, cracked pipe, sewer line problem, or septic problem.
- You do not need to buy tools, dismantle plumbing, or choose a repair today if you can ventilate, stop using the fixture, and get help arranged.
- You do not need to make a major repair decision before someone has inspected the fixture and checked for backup, leakage, or venting problems.

## Important reassurance
A sewer smell from one drain is a common household problem and is often fixable once the source is found. Taking it seriously does not mean you need to panic; fresh air, avoiding chemical mixing, and getting the right inspection are the useful first steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce risk, prevent worsening the plumbing problem, and help you decide who to contact. Later decisions about repairs, tenancy issues, sewer utility responsibility, septic work, or medical care may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, environmental, plumbing, tenancy, or professional advice. Follow emergency instructions from your gas utility, sewer utility, landlord, plumber, Poison Help, or emergency services when they apply.
