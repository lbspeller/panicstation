# What to do if… you find a strong sewer smell coming from one specific drain even after running water (USA)

## Short answer
Ventilate the area immediately by opening windows and turning on exhaust fans. Stop using the affected fixture and do not mix cleaning chemicals in the drain while waiting for a professional inspection.

## Do not do these things
- Do not lean over the drain to "test" the strength of the odor if it is already noticeably strong.
- Do not use open flames, lighters, or matches near the drain due to the potential presence of flammable methane or hydrogen sulfide.
- Do not mix bleach with ammonia, vinegar, or any acidic drain cleaners, as this creates toxic gas.
- Do not attempt to dismantle the p-trap or plumbing lines if you are a tenant or lack specialized tools.
- Do not ignore the smell if it is accompanied by symptoms like headache, nausea, or dizziness.

## What to do now
1. **Increase ventilation in the room immediately to disperse any accumulated gases.** Open all windows, turn on the bathroom or kitchen exhaust fan if it vents to the exterior, and leave the door open to allow cross-ventilation.
2. **Perform a brief visual check of other drains in the property to see if the issue is isolated.** Quickly walk to other sinks, tubs, and floor drains to determine if they also emit a smell; if multiple drains are affected, the problem likely lies in the main vent stack or the municipal sewer line.
3. **Run water into the affected drain for 30 seconds to ensure the p-trap is not dry.** If the drain has not been used recently, the water seal that blocks sewer gas may have evaporated; if the smell persists after running water, the seal is not the only issue.
4. **Clean the visible area around the drain stopper and sink overflow safely.** Use a mild mixture of hot water and dish soap to scrub away hair, soap scum, or biofilm from the drain opening and the overflow hole, as bacterial growth here often mimics sewer odors.
5. **Inspect the area under the sink for signs of moisture or visible leaks.** Look for water stains on the cabinet floor or moisture on the exterior of the p-trap and tailpiece, as even a small leak can allow gas to escape the pipe.
6. **Seal the drain temporarily if the odor is overpowering and persists after cleaning.** Use a drain plug or heavy tape to cover the opening and stop using the fixture until a plumber or landlord inspects the system.
7. **Notify the property manager or a licensed plumber to schedule an inspection.**
   - If renting, report a suspected sewer gas leak to the landlord immediately.
   - If owning, contact a professional to check for a compromised vent pipe, a failed toilet wax ring, or a hairline crack in the plumbing.
8. **Contact the local sewer utility 24-hour emergency line if the smell is present outside or affects neighbors.** This indicates a possible blockage or break in the municipal main line rather than a localized fixture problem.
9. **Move to fresh air immediately if you experience physical symptoms.**
   - If someone is unresponsive or having severe difficulty breathing, call 911 from a safe distance.
   - For non-emergency guidance on gas exposure, contact Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the exact mechanical cause, such as siphoning or a cracked vent, before seeking help.
- You do not need to initiate major repairs or pipe replacements immediately if the fixture can be isolated and the room ventilated.
- You do not need to buy specialized gas-monitoring equipment for a one-time household odor.

## Important reassurance
Sewer gas odors are a common plumbing issue usually caused by a dried trap, a local blockage, or a failed seal. Taking immediate steps to ventilate the room and stop using the fixture significantly reduces exposure while you wait for a professional to identify the exact entry point.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for localized odors and is intended for immediate harm reduction. Long-term resolution requires a professional assessment of the building's plumbing and venting systems.

## Important note
This information is for general safety and does not replace professional medical or plumbing advice. If you suspect a natural gas leak (which may also smell like sulfur/rotten eggs), evacuate the building and contact your gas utility's emergency line immediately.
