# What to do if… your travel booking lists the wrong country of citizenship and you cannot edit it online (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the airline or booking agent immediately to update your Advance Passenger Information (APIS) to match your passport, as this data is used for mandatory government vetting before departure.

## Do not do these things
- Don't cancel your entire trip to fix a data field unless the airline explicitly states the ticket is non-correctable.
- Don't attempt to "make it match" by entering incorrect information on government forms like an ESTA or visa application.
- Don't wait until you reach the boarding gate to mention the error; gate agents have limited time and system access.
- Don't ignore the error if you have a domestic connection, as REAL ID requirements (mandatory starting June 1, 2026) require exact identification matches.
- Don't pay third-party "travel fixers" to access your record; only use the airline's official channels.

## What to do now
1. **Identify exactly which field is incorrect in your record.**  
   Check if the error is in the "Citizenship/Nationality" field, the "Passport Issuing Country" field, or the "Secure Flight" data section. Write down the exact text currently shown versus the correct information on your physical passport.
2. **Gather your booking identifiers and passport.**  
   Locate your six-character airline Record Locator (PNR) and your 13-digit ticket number. Ensure you have the passport you intend to use for travel in front of you to read off the document number and expiry date exactly.
3. **Call the airline's international reservations or web support desk.**  
   **Ask specifically to "update the APIS (Advance Passenger Information System) data" rather than requesting a "name change" or "ticket change."** Most airlines can update citizenship, passport numbers, and expiry dates over the phone even if the online portal is locked.
   - If you booked through a travel agency (Expedia, Hopper, etc.), you must contact them first to push the update to the airline.
4. **Verify that your Secure Flight Passenger Data (SFPD) is accurate.**  
   **Confirm that your full name, date of birth, and gender match your ID exactly.** Effective 2026, TSA and CBP systems have stricter validation; for example, gender fields may be restricted to "M" or "F" for system compatibility even if your passport says "X."
5. **Align your travel authorizations with your corrected booking.**  
   **Ensure any required digital permissions, such as a UK ETA (required after Feb 25, 2026) or a US ESTA, match the citizenship and passport number on your airline record.** If these do not match, the airline's check-in system may automatically block your boarding pass.
6. **Arrive at the airport three hours early for a manual document check.**  
   **Go to the staffed check-in counter instead of using a kiosk or mobile check-in.** Ask the agent to "swipe and verify" your passport to ensure the digital manifest transmitted to the government (APIS) is corrected before the flight closes.

## What can wait
- You do not need to worry about being "flagged" for a simple clerical error as long as it is corrected before the manifest is finalized.
- You do not need to research deportation or legal consequences; this is a procedural data mismatch, not a criminal matter.
- You do not need to decide on a refund or compensation claim until after you have successfully boarded your flight.

## Important reassurance
Data entry errors regarding citizenship are common. While airline websites often lock these fields after a booking is confirmed to prevent fraud, airline agents have the tools to update your APIS data. As long as you address this before the manifest is sent to border authorities (usually 30–60 minutes before departure), it is a routine correction.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for a data mismatch on an existing booking. It does not provide legal advice for immigration, visa eligibility, or complex dual-citizenship border crossings.

## Important note
Airlines are required by law to transmit accurate passenger data to U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP). Failure to have matching data can result in denied boarding or secondary inspection. Starting June 1, 2026, REAL ID enforcement is mandatory for all U.S. domestic flights; ensure all identification used for any leg of your journey meets these federal standards.
