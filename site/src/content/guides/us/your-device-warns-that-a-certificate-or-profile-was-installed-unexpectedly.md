# What to do if… your device warns that a certificate or profile was installed unexpectedly (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the warning as a possible trust or device-management risk: get the device offline, check what was installed, remove anything unknown if you can, and use a different trusted device for important sign-ins until this one is checked.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t tap “trust,” “allow,” “install,” or “enroll” just to clear the warning.
- Don’t sign in to email, banking, work systems, or your password manager from that device until you have checked what changed.
- Don’t delete random apps, photos, or files before checking profiles and certificates.
- Don’t factory reset immediately unless removal fails, the profile keeps returning, or trusted support tells you to.
- Don’t follow links in texts or emails that claim to help you “report” or “fix” the warning.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause and take the device offline.**  
   Turn on Airplane Mode, or switch off Wi-Fi and cellular. Leave any public Wi-Fi network you just joined.

2. **Work out whether the warning is about a profile or a certificate.**
   - **Profile, Device Management, MDM, VPN, or Work profile** usually means a settings or management change.
   - **Certificate, CA, root certificate, or certificate authority** means the device may trust something new.
   - If you are unsure, take a screenshot of the warning before changing anything.

3. **iPhone or iPad: review and remove unknown profiles.**  
   Go to **Settings → General → VPN & Device Management**. Open anything you do not recognise, screenshot the name, organisation, and date if shown, then choose **Remove Profile** or **Delete Profile** if that option is available. Restart the device after removal.

4. **iPhone or iPad: check certificate trust if a certificate is mentioned.**  
   Go to **Settings → General → About → Certificate Trust Settings**. If “Enable full trust for root certificates” appears and anything is enabled that you did not intentionally install for a known school, work, VPN, or security purpose, treat it as suspicious. Remove the profile that installed it if you can, then restart.

5. **Android: remove unknown user certificates.**  
   Menu names vary, but start with **Settings → Security & privacy → More security settings → Encryption & credentials**. Under credential storage, remove the unknown entry under **User credentials** if you can identify it. If you cannot tell what is safe and this is your personal device, **Clear credentials** can remove user-installed certificates, but it may break some work, school, VPN, or Wi-Fi access until reconfigured.

6. **If this is a work, school, or managed device, pause before removing managed items.**  
   Contact your helpdesk or administrator and say: “I received an unexpected certificate or profile warning. I put the device offline and have not signed in. Can you confirm whether an MDM, VPN, certificate, or work profile was deployed?”

7. **If this started after a link, app, QR code, or Wi-Fi join, undo that trigger.**  
   Uninstall any new app you do not recognise. Forget the Wi-Fi network you just joined. Do not scan the same QR code or reopen the same link. Update the operating system from the device’s normal settings app, then restart.

8. **Use a different trusted device for account safety checks.**  
   Start with the email account that resets your other accounts, then your Apple Account or Google Account, then financial and work accounts. Change passwords where needed, enable multi-factor authentication where available, and sign out unknown sessions.

9. **Escalate if the profile or certificate cannot be removed, returns, or you entered sensitive information after the warning.**  
   Contact the device maker, your mobile carrier, or your organisation’s IT support from a different device. If money moved, accounts were accessed, or sensitive information was entered after the warning, use the official FTC or IC3 reporting sites by typing the address yourself.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove exactly how it happened right now.
- You do not need to decide on a factory reset unless removal fails, the warning returns, or trusted support recommends it.
- You do not need to contact every account provider at once; start with the accounts that control recovery for the others.
- You do not need to keep using the affected device for urgent messages; use another trusted device if available.

## Important reassurance
Pausing for an unexpected certificate or profile warning is sensible, not overreacting. Getting offline, checking profiles and certificates, and protecting key accounts reduces the immediate risk while you decide what help you need.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce harm and buy time. If the device is managed, the certificate or profile cannot be removed, or account activity looks wrong, later decisions may need help from your organisation’s IT team, device-maker support, or a qualified security professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, forensic, or other professional advice. Use official support and reporting sites that you navigate to directly, and keep screenshots or notes of what you saw if you may need help later.
