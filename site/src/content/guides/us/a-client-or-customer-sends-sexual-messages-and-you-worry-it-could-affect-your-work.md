# What to do if… a client or customer sends sexual messages and you worry it could affect your work (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all interaction with the sender, preserve copies of all messages, and report the incident to your supervisor or Human Resources to trigger your employer's legal obligation to protect you from third-party harassment.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete any messages, call logs, images, or emails.
- Do not respond with jokes, apologies, or personal information to "soften" the situation.
- Do not move the conversation to personal social media or personal phone numbers.
- Do not attempt to "wait it out" or assume the behavior will stop on its own.
- Do not accept gifts or "apology" favors from the client or customer.
- Do not meet with the individual alone or in a non-public setting.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a neutral, private space.** Relocate to an office, breakroom, or restroom to review the situation without being observed by the sender.
2. **Preserve every digital trace immediately.** 
   - **Take screenshots** of all messages, ensuring the sender’s name, handle, or phone number and the timestamp are visible.
   - **Save the original files** or emails in a separate folder; do not rely on the app's "delete for everyone" functions.
   - **Log the details** including the date, time, platform, and a factual summary of what happened before and after the message.
3. **Cease all direct communication.** 
   - **Stop replying** to the messages immediately. 
   - **If a response is required by your job role,** send one factual statement: "I only communicate regarding professional matters. Please keep all messages work-related." Do not engage further after this.
4. **Use company communication tools only.** Ensure all future interactions related to this client occur through work email, recorded phone lines, or company-monitored CRM systems.
5. **Initiate an internal report.** 
   - **Contact your direct supervisor** or HR department via email to create a written record.
   - **State clearly** that you have received unwelcome sexual messages from a client/customer and that it is affecting your ability to work.
   - **Reference your company’s anti-harassment policy** if you have access to it.
6. **Request immediate interim protections.** Ask your employer for specific actions to reduce your exposure:
   - **Assign a different staff member** to handle the client or account.
   - **Move all meetings** to a public or recorded digital format.
   - **Require a second employee** to be present for any necessary interactions.
   - **Block the sender** from your professional accounts if your employer provides the technical means.
7. **Verify your legal standing.** Under Title VII, U.S. employers are liable for harassment by customers or clients if they "knew or should have known" about the conduct and failed to take prompt, effective corrective action.
8. **Prioritize physical safety.** 
   - **Call 911** if the messages contain threats of physical harm or if the individual is at your location and you feel endangered.
   - **Alert building security** or front desk staff to ensure the individual is not permitted unescorted access to your workspace.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to file a formal lawsuit or quit your job today.
- You do not need to confront the client/customer directly about their behavior.
- You do not need to have a "perfect" or "final" version of your statement before making the initial report to HR.
- You do not need to worry about the immediate deadline for an EEOC charge; you generally have 180 or 300 days to file.

## Important reassurance
Worrying about your job security or "making a scene" is a common reaction when a client crosses a boundary. Harassment by a third party is a workplace safety issue that your employer is legally required to manage. Reporting the behavior is a professional action taken to maintain a functional work environment.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization only. It does not cover the long-term legal process of an EEOC investigation or private litigation.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Federal and state laws vary; for specific legal guidance regarding your situation, consult an employment attorney or the EEOC.
