# What to do if… you receive a message from someone you know that feels “off” and asks you to meet urgently in a private place (USA)

## Short answer
Do not go to a private or secluded location. Verify the sender's identity through a completely different communication channel—such as a direct phone call to their known number—and involve others if you feel pressured or unsafe.

## Do not do these things
- Don't go to any secluded area, private residence, parked vehicle, or location where you cannot easily exit or be seen by others.
- Don't accept a ride from the person or agree to a last-minute change to a quieter location.
- Don't share your live location, home address, or photos to "prove" where you are.
- Don't send any one-time passcodes, verification codes, or security screenshots, even if they claim it is to "verify" their own identity.
- Don't ignore your intuition if the tone, grammar, or level of urgency feels uncharacteristic for that person.
- Don't engage in a long debate in the same message thread while you are uncertain of the sender's identity.

## What to do now
1. **Stop and prioritize physical distance.** **Do not move toward the requested meeting location while you are still evaluating the message.** Urgency and a request for secrecy are primary indicators of social engineering or physical safety risks.
2. **Verify the sender’s identity using an independent method.** **Call the person directly on a phone number you have previously saved for them.** 
   - If they answer, listen for signs of AI-generated voice cloning, such as unnatural pauses or a flat tone.
   - Use a pre-established "secret word" or "safety phrase" if your family or friend group has one. 
   - If you do not have a secret word, ask a specific question about a past shared experience that an impersonator or AI would not know.
3. **Offer digital or emergency help instead of a physical meeting.** **If the person claims to be in a crisis, offer to call 911 or a local non-emergency line for a welfare check on their behalf.** 
   - Suggest calling a mutual friend or family member to assist them instead of you going alone.
4. **Insist on a "Safe Exchange Zone" if you choose to meet at all.** **Propose meeting at a local police station parking lot or a designated law enforcement safe exchange zone.**
   - If a police station is not available, choose a high-traffic, well-lit, staffed public lobby like a library, hospital, or busy coffee shop.
   - Arrive and depart in your own vehicle or via independent transport.
5. **Establish a check-in trigger with a third party.** **Tell a trusted person exactly who you are meeting, where you are going, and the specific time they should call 911 if they do not hear from you.**
   - Share your live location with this third party for the duration of the encounter.
6. **Treat any links or files as high-risk.** **Do not click on links, download attachments, or "confirm" anything on your phone at the sender's request.** These are common tactics used to facilitate account takeovers or install spyware.
7. **Document the interaction for reporting.** **Take screenshots of the messages and the account profile before blocking or reporting the user.**
   - Use the in-app reporting tools to flag the account for impersonation or hacking.
   - If you suspect a coordinated scam or crime, file a report at ic3.gov (FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center).

## What can wait
- You do -not- need to respond to the person's messages while you are performing safety checks.
- You do -not- need to worry about "offending" a friend by being cautious; if it is really them, they will understand the need for safety.
- You do -not- need to decide whether to permanently block or delete the contact right this second.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of "social pressure" or guilt when a friend appears to be in trouble. However, slowing the pace of the interaction is a standard safety protocol that protects both you and the person being impersonated. Trusting a "bad feeling" about a message is a functional survival skill, not a lack of loyalty.

## Scope note
This guide focuses on immediate physical and digital stabilization upon receiving a suspicious urgent request. It does not cover long-term account recovery or legal proceedings following an incident.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. If you believe a crime is in progress or you are in immediate physical danger, call 911.
