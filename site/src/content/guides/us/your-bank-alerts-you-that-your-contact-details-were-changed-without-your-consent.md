# What to do if… your bank alerts you that your contact details were changed without your consent (USA)

## Short answer
Call your bank immediately using a trusted number, such as the number on the back of your card, on a statement, or on the bank’s official website. Tell them there was an unauthorized phone or email change and ask them to secure the account before any more money can move.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click links or call phone numbers included in the alert text or email.
- Do not share one-time passcodes, person-to-person payment verification codes, PINs, or online banking login details with anyone.
- Do not reply to the alert to “fix” the issue.
- Do not reset your bank login through a message link.
- Do not keep signing in from the same device if you suspect your email, phone, or device is compromised.
- Do not ignore the alert because no money appears missing yet; contact-detail changes can be used to take over the account.

## What to do now
1. **Call the bank’s fraud department using a trusted number.** Ask them to:
   - Lock or restrict online and mobile banking until you are verified.
   - Undo the phone or email change and confirm the safe contact details now on file.
   - Require extra verification before any future profile, password, payee, card, or transfer changes.
   - Check for new payees, linked external accounts, new devices, new cards, address changes, statement-setting changes, and paperless-setting changes.
   - Remove anything you do not recognize or did not authorize.
   - Give you a case number and tell you the safest way to call back.

2. **Ask the bank to review money movement now.**
   - Ask whether any pending transfers, wires, ACH transfers, debit-card payments, or person-to-person payments can be stopped, cancelled, or reversed.
   - Ask whether your debit card, online banking password, or account number needs to be replaced.
   - If the transfer was an electronic fund transfer, ask how to make an error notice or dispute through the bank’s process.
   - Ask what deadline the bank wants you to meet for any written follow-up, and write that down.

3. **Secure the email account connected to the bank.**
   - From a known-safe device, change the email password.
   - Turn on multi-factor authentication for the email account.
   - Check for unfamiliar recovery email addresses, recovery phone numbers, forwarding rules, filters, connected apps, and recent logins.
   - Remove anything you do not recognize.

4. **Contact your mobile carrier if your phone number is involved.**
   - Ask whether a SIM change, eSIM activation, or number port-out was requested or completed.
   - Ask what account PIN, passphrase, number-lock, or port-out protections they offer.
   - Ask for documentation if the carrier confirms a SIM swap or port-out fraud incident.
   - Reset your voicemail or carrier security PIN if it may have been exposed.

5. **Protect your credit if this may be broader identity theft.**
   - Consider placing a credit freeze with each major credit bureau.
   - If you are not ready for a freeze, ask about placing a fraud alert.
   - Keep the bank case number and any carrier documentation with your records.

6. **Use the official identity-theft reporting route if more than this bank account may be affected.**
   - Go to IdentityTheft.gov to make a report and recovery plan.
   - Save the recovery plan and any report number for bank, credit bureau, or carrier disputes.

7. **Keep one simple record.**
   - Save screenshots of alerts.
   - Write down dates, times, names, case numbers, and what each company said it changed or blocked.
   - Keep a list of every transfer, linked account, payee, device, card, address, or setting you do not recognize.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide today whether to close every account or change banks.
- You do not need to replace your phone immediately unless your carrier confirms a SIM swap or port-out, or you keep seeing signs that the phone is compromised.
- You can come back to other passwords after banking, email, and phone-number access are contained.
- You do not need to understand every rule now; focus on getting the bank, email account, and mobile number back under your control.

## Important reassurance
This alert is frightening, but it also gives you a chance to act early. The immediate goal is simple: use trusted contact routes, lock down access, stop anything pending if possible, and keep a record.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, such as formal disputes, account replacement, credit freezes, complaints, or identity-theft recovery, may need help from the bank, your mobile carrier, credit bureaus, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 911. Follow your bank’s official fraud and error-resolution process and keep copies of everything you submit.
