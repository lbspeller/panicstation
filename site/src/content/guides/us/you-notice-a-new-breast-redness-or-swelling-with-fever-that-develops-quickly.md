# What to do if… you notice a new breast redness or swelling with fever that develops quickly (USA)

## Short answer
Arrange same-day medical evaluation. Rapid breast redness or swelling with fever may be an infection or another breast condition that needs prompt treatment, so do not try to handle it at home for several days.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait multiple days when you have fever, chills, spreading redness, or you feel sick.
- Do not puncture, lance, squeeze hard, or deeply massage a lump or painful area.
- Do not use leftover antibiotics or someone else’s antibiotics.
- Do not add extra pumping, forceful pumping changes, or aggressive “emptying” to try to clear it.
- If you are breastfeeding, do not abruptly stop feeding or expressing just because you are scared.

## What to do now
1. **Arrange same-day care.**  
   Call your OB-GYN, midwife, primary care clinician, health-plan nurse line, or an urgent care clinic and say: “I have rapidly developing breast redness or swelling with fever.” Ask where you should be seen today.

2. **Go in person if you cannot get timely advice.**  
   Use urgent care or an ER if you cannot be seen promptly, the redness is spreading, the pain is increasing, or you feel increasingly unwell. Some cases may need prescription treatment, imaging, or drainage by a clinician.

3. **Use emergency help now if you seem seriously ill.**  
   Call 911 or go to the ER now if you have confusion, fainting, severe trouble breathing, severe weakness, or you are rapidly worsening. These can be warning signs of a severe infection.

4. **If you are breastfeeding or recently breastfeeding, keep milk moving gently while you arrange care.**  
   Continue feeding or expressing as you normally would if you can tolerate it. Keep it gentle; do not deeply massage the breast or add extra pumping to “empty” it unless a clinician tells you to.

5. **Write down the details that help clinicians act quickly.**  
   Note when symptoms started, your highest temperature, whether the redness is spreading, whether there is a firm lump, any nipple discharge, recent skin breaks, piercing, injury, postpartum timing, and medication allergies. A quick photo for comparison can help, but do not delay care to take one.

6. **Follow up if treatment is not matching how you feel.**  
   If you start antibiotics and you are not improving or you are worsening, contact the treating clinician promptly. If redness or swelling persists after treatment, ask for reassessment; you may need follow-up, imaging, or rarely a biopsy because some breast conditions can look similar.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out the exact cause right now; the priority is being assessed today.
- You do not need repeated poking, pressing, or checking to prove whether it is getting worse.
- You do not need to make big decisions about breastfeeding today beyond what you can tolerate while you arrange care.
- You do not need to decide now whether this is mastitis, a clogged duct, an abscess, or something else.

## Important reassurance
Fast breast changes with fever can feel frightening, especially if you feel flu-like. Many causes are treatable, and same-day evaluation is the safest way to reduce complications and get the right next step.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few hours only. After evaluation, later decisions about medicines, imaging, drainage, breastfeeding support, or follow-up may need help from a qualified medical professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, legal advice, financial advice, therapeutic advice, professional advice, or a diagnosis. If you feel very unwell or symptoms worsen quickly, treat it as urgent and use emergency services.
