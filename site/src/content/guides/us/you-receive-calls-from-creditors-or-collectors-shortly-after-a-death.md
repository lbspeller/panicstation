# What to do if… you receive calls from creditors or collectors shortly after a death (USA)

## Short answer
Do not agree to pay or acknowledge the debt over the phone. State that the individual is deceased and request all future communication and debt validation information be sent in writing to the estate's representative.

## Do not do these things
- Don't make any "good faith" payments from your own funds, as this can sometimes be interpreted as an admission of personal liability.
- Don't provide your Social Security number, personal bank details, or specific information about the deceased's assets to an incoming caller.
- Don't admit the debt is valid or yours until you have reviewed a written validation notice.
- Don't assume you are responsible for the debt simply because you are the next of kin or lived with the deceased.
- Don't continue the conversation if the caller is aggressive or refuses to provide their company name and mailing address.

## What to do now
1. **End the call after providing a brief statement.** **Inform the caller that the person has died and state: "Please send all information regarding this matter in writing to the estate representative."** 
2. **Log the details of the communication.** **Record the date, the exact time, the caller's name, the company name, the phone number, and a summary of what they claimed was owed.**
3. **Identify if you are a "protected" contact under the FDCPA.** **Under federal law, collectors may only discuss the debt with the surviving spouse, parents of a deceased minor, or the court-appointed executor / administrator.** 
   - If you do not hold one of these roles, the collector may generally only contact you once to locate the representative and cannot mention the debt itself.
4. **Request a written validation notice.** **Under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA), the collector must send you a written notice within 5 days of their first contact containing the amount owed and the name of the creditor.**
5. **Route all correspondence to the estate representative.** **If an executor or administrator has been appointed by a probate court, provide the collector with that person's professional contact information and instruct them to cease contacting you personally.**
   - If no representative is appointed yet, inform them: "The estate is not yet in probate; all claims must be sent in writing to [Mailing Address] for future review."
6. **Check for state-specific liability triggers.** **Determine if you live in a community property state or a state with spousal medical debt laws, as these are the primary legal exceptions where a survivor may be personally liable.**
   - Community property states include Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, and Wisconsin.
7. **Monitor for frequency violations.** **Note if a collector calls more than 7 times within a 7-day period regarding a specific debt, as this is a presumption of harassment under CFPB rules.**
8. **Submit a written cease-contact or "mail only" request.** **Send a certified letter with a return receipt stating that you wish for the collector to stop calling you and that all future communication must be in writing.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the estate is "insolvent" (having more debt than assets) during the initial phone call.
- You do not need to hire a probate attorney before the first few calls, though one may be helpful later.
- You do not need to verify the accuracy of the debt amount immediately; you have 30 days from receiving the written validation notice to dispute it.
- You do not need to contact other family members to "warn" them unless they are joint account holders.

## Important reassurance
It is common for automated collection systems to continue making calls before a death has been registered in their databases. These calls are often a result of routine computer-generated triggers rather than a personal investigation. You have the legal right to move the entire process to written mail, which allows you to review the claims at your own pace without immediate pressure.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and communication boundaries for survivors in the USA. It does not provide a full guide to the probate process or the final settlement of estate taxes.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Liability for a deceased person's debt is governed by complex state laws and the specific terms of credit contracts. If a collector threatens a lawsuit or claims you are personally responsible in a community property state, consult a probate attorney or legal aid services.
