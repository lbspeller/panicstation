# What to do if… you are placed on a performance improvement plan unexpectedly (USA)

## Short answer
Do not work off the clock and begin documenting every hour worked in a personal log. Under federal law, most workers earning less than $1,128 per week ($58,656 annually) are entitled to overtime pay at 1.5 times their regular rate for hours over 40 in a workweek.

## Do not do these things
- Do not clock out and continue working, even for "quick tasks" or "finishing up."
- Do not agree to "volunteer" your time for tasks that are part of your normal job duties.
- Do not allow a supervisor to edit your time records to remove hours without a written, factual reason.
- Do not sign agreements to waive your right to overtime pay, as these are generally unenforceable under federal law.
- Do not rely on verbal promises that you will be "taken care of" or "given comp time" later.
- Do not delete evidence of work performed, such as sent emails, call logs, or system login timestamps.

## What to do now
1. **Keep all work activities strictly on the clock.** **If you are directed to clock out but continue working, state clearly that you must remain clocked in to ensure your time records are accurate and compliant with federal law.** This establishes your position without being confrontational.

2. **Start an independent personal time log immediately.** **Record the date, the exact start and stop times, any meal or rest breaks taken, and the specific tasks performed.**  
   - Include the name of the person who directed the work.
   - Save copies of schedules, text messages, or emails where unpaid work was requested.
   - Keep these records in a personal location, not on a company device or server.

3. **Check your 2026 salary and classification status.** **Determine if your gross weekly pay is below the federal threshold of $1,128 per week, as most workers below this level are non-exempt regardless of their job title.** If you are in California, New York, or Washington, your state threshold is significantly higher; verify your local rate with your state labor department.

4. **Submit a formal written request for time record correction if hours are missing.** **Send an email to payroll or HR (not just your direct supervisor) stating the specific hours worked that were not recorded and requesting that your pay be adjusted to reflect all time worked.** Attach a copy of your personal log for the period in question.

5. **Contact the Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division (WHD) for guidance.** **Call 1-866-487-9243 to speak with a representative about your specific situation and to learn how to file a formal wage claim if necessary.** If you have a hearing or speech disability, dial 7-1-1 to access telecommunications relay services.

6. **Document any adverse changes to your employment following your inquiry.** **Keep a separate log of any reduced hours, threats, disciplinary actions, or sudden negative performance reviews that occur after you ask about overtime pay.** Federal law prohibits retaliation against employees for inquiring about their wages or filing a complaint with the WHD.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to file a lawsuit or hire a private attorney right now.
- You do not need to confront your manager if you feel unsafe or fear immediate termination.
- You do not need to prove that the company meant to break the law; the focus is solely on whether you worked the hours and whether you were paid.

## Important reassurance
Questioning unpaid work is a standard part of maintaining a professional employment relationship. Federal protections exist specifically because many workers feel pressured to "help out" the company at their own expense. Keeping records is a neutral, protective step that preserves your rights regardless of how the situation is eventually resolved.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and evidence preservation for wage-and-hour disputes under federal law. It does not cover specific union grievance procedures or the nuances of professional/executive exemptions, which may require a detailed legal audit.

## Important note
This information is provided for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Wage-and-hour laws involve complex tests regarding job duties and salary levels that vary by state. If you believe you are being misclassified or are facing retaliation, you should consult with the U.S. Department of Labor or a qualified employment law professional to discuss the specific facts of your case.
