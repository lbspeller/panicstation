# What to do if… a cheque you deposited is returned unpaid and your balance drops (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all outgoing spending from the account immediately and contact your financial institution to identify the return reason and the exact amount needed to bring the balance above $0.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume funds are "cleared" just because they are "available"; federal law (Regulation CC) requires banks to make the first $275 available before they know if the check is valid.
- Do not send money back to the check writer or any third party via wire, Zelle, or gift cards, as this is the primary goal of a fake-check scam.
- Do not attempt to redeposit the check without explicit bank approval, as this may trigger additional "returned item" fees or account restrictions.
- Do not ignore your account balance if it is negative; cascading overdraft fees often post overnight.
- Do not provide your login credentials to anyone claiming they can "fix" the check or "verify" the funds for you.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the specific return code and amount on your statement.**  
   **Look for entries labeled "Returned Deposited Item," "RDI," or "Reversal."** Note the date, the exact dollar amount removed, and any associated fees, which are typically between $10 and $19 per item.
2. **Call your bank or credit union immediately to confirm the return reason.**  
   **Ask the representative to specify if the check was returned for Non-Sufficient Funds (NSF), Account Closed, Stop Payment, or Suspected Fraud.** Request a list of any pending transactions that are likely to fail due to the sudden balance drop.
3. **Request a waiver of the "Returned Deposited Item" fee based on CFPB guidance.**  
   **State that you had no way of knowing the check would be returned and reference the CFPB's stance that blanket fees for returned items are considered unfair.** Ask: "Can you waive the returned item fee and any resulting overdraft fees while I bring the account current today?"
4. **Prioritize covering the negative balance to stop fee escalation.**  
   **Transfer funds from a linked savings account or deposit cash immediately to bring the balance to at least $0.** This prevents "consecutive day" overdraft fees and protects your standing with the ChexSystems reporting agency.
5. **Intercept scheduled payments due within the next 48 hours.**  
   **Contact billers for rent, utilities, or insurance if a payment is about to bounce.** Ask for a one-time extension or change the payment method to a different account to avoid late fees or service interruptions.
6. **Watch for a "Substitute Check" or Image Replacement Document (IRD) in the mail.**  
   **Keep this document as it is the legal equivalent of the original check and contains the reason for the return.** You will need this if you intend to take legal action or re-present the check later.
7. **Contact the check writer only after the bank confirms the return reason.**  
   **Inform them the check was returned and request a secure payment method, such as an ACH transfer or a bank-issued money order.** If the return was for "NSF," ask them specifically when the funds will be available before attempting a second deposit.
8. **File a report if the check was part of a scam.**  
   **Notify the bank's fraud department immediately and file a report at ReportFraud.ftc.gov if you were pressured to send money back to the payer.** Prompt reporting may help prevent the bank from closing your account for "fraudulent activity."

## What can wait
- You do not need to file a small claims court case against the payer in the first 24 hours; stabilize the account first.
- You do not need to close the account immediately unless the bank advises you that the account number itself has been compromised.
- You do not need to wait for the physical check to be mailed back to you before talking to the bank; they have the digital data now.

## Important reassurance
A returned deposit is a common banking occurrence and does not necessarily mean you have done something wrong or that your credit is ruined. While the sudden balance drop is stressful, most financial institutions have standard procedures for these events. Acting quickly to cover the shortfall usually prevents long-term account restrictions.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for a returned deposit. It does not cover long-term debt collection against the check writer or the specific legal requirements for "bad check" criminal notices, which vary by state.
