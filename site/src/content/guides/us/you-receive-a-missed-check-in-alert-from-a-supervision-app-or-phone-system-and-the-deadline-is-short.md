# What to do if… you receive a missed check-in alert from a supervision app or phone system and the deadline is short (USA)

## Short answer
Retry the check-in now. If it does not clear, contact your supervising officer and any monitoring center listed in your paperwork right away, using an approved call, text, email, or app message so there is a timestamp before the deadline.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the alert because you feel panicked or embarrassed.
- Do not turn your phone off, block calls, uninstall the app, wipe call history, or delete messages.
- Do not edit, fake, or backdate screenshots, call logs, confirmations, or messages.
- Do not leave an approved address, curfew area, inclusion zone, or required location while you are trying to fix the alert, unless your officer, the monitoring center, or emergency services instructs you differently.
- Do not send a long emotional explanation first; send a short factual message with times, attempts, and how you can be reached.
- Do not assume the alert will clear itself before a short deadline.

## What to do now
1. **Save the alert and the deadline.**  
   Take screenshots of the missed check-in notice, the deadline, the app time, and any error messages. Write down the exact time you saw the alert.

2. **Retry the check-in in the simplest approved way.**  
   For an app, reopen it, check that location, camera, microphone, and notification permissions are allowed, charge the phone, switch between Wi-Fi and mobile data, move to a place with better signal if that does not break your conditions, and turn off VPN if the app warns about it or your paperwork says not to use it. For a phone system, call again from your registered number, stay somewhere quiet, and complete the prompts slowly. If it works, save the confirmation screen, confirmation code, call time, or completion message.

3. **If it still will not clear, contact supervision immediately.**  
   Call your supervising officer, such as probation, parole, pretrial services, or community supervision. If your paperwork gives an after-hours or on-call number, use it. If you have a separate monitoring center, electronic monitoring vendor, ankle monitor provider, or phone-reporting help line listed in your paperwork, contact that number too.

4. **Leave a clear timestamped message if no one answers.**  
   Say your name, case number, client number, or supervision ID if you have one, the time of the alert, the deadline shown, what you tried, where you are if location is part of the issue, and that you are available now. Do not include your Social Security number. If you have an approved text, email, portal, or app-message channel, send one short follow-up with the same facts.

5. **Stay reachable and keep following your conditions.**  
   Keep the phone charged, keep the app open if required, answer unknown or blocked numbers, and stay at the approved place if you have curfew, home confinement, or location restrictions. Do not tamper with a monitor, base station, phone line, charger, or app.

6. **Write one short incident note.**  
   Record the date, time, alert wording, deadline, each retry attempt, each call or message, who answered, any ticket or reference number, and exactly what you were told to do. Keep screenshots, confirmations, voicemail records, and call logs together.

7. **Get legal help quickly if reporting or enforcement is mentioned.**  
   If you are told to report in person, appear in court, or expect a violation response, contact your lawyer or public defender as soon as you can. Follow the instruction you are given unless it is unsafe or impossible; if it is unclear, ask the officer or monitoring center to repeat the exact next step and document the answer.

## What can wait
- A full explanation of why the alert happened can wait until you have retried, made contact, and saved proof.
- Replacing the phone, changing your number, reinstalling the app, or changing device settings can wait unless your officer, monitoring center, or paperwork tells you to do it now.
- Complaints about the app, vendor, or phone system can wait until the immediate alert and deadline are documented.

## Important reassurance
Some alerts are caused by signal problems, battery issues, GPS or location problems, phone-system errors, or app failures. The most protective first step is not a perfect explanation; it is a clear record that you tried to check in, contacted the right people quickly, stayed reachable, and kept following your conditions.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes and hours only. Supervision rules, monitoring systems, court orders, and consequences vary by state, county, agency, and individual order, so later decisions may need help from your lawyer, public defender, supervising officer, or another qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Use the numbers, apps, reporting instructions, and conditions in your own supervision or monitoring paperwork. If there is immediate danger, call 911.
