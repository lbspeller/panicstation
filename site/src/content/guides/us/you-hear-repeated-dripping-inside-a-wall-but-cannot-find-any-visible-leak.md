# What to do if… you hear repeated dripping inside a wall but cannot find any visible leak (USA)

## Short answer
**Shut off your home’s main water supply valve immediately** and avoid using any electrical switches or outlets in the affected area.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t run faucets, showers, or appliances to "test" the sound—if the leak is pressurized, this increases the volume of water entering your walls.
- Don’t poke holes in the drywall or ceiling to find the source unless there is a visible, sagging bulge that is clearly holding a large volume of water.
- Don’t touch electrical fixtures, outlets, or switches if the wall feels damp or you hear buzzing/sizzling sounds.
- Don’t stand in any pooling water while attempting to reach a circuit breaker or water valve.
- Don’t wait for the water to "show up" on the surface before calling for help; structural saturation happens long before visible staining.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the water supply by closing the main shutoff valve immediately.** **The valve is typically located where the main water line enters the house, often in the basement, a crawlspace, the garage, or near the water heater.**
   - If you cannot find the internal valve, look for the water meter box near the street and use a meter key or pliers to turn the valve off there.
2. **Shut off the electricity to the affected area if there is any sign of moisture near outlets or lights.** **Go to your main electrical panel and flip the breakers for the specific rooms where you hear the dripping.**
   - If the panel itself is wet or you must stand in water to reach it, do not touch it; call your utility provider to pull the meter or disconnect power from the outside.
3. **Listen for a change in the sound after the water is shut off.** **A sound that stops immediately often indicates a pressurized supply line leak, while a sound that continues or slows down gradually may indicate a drainage leak or a roof leak.**
4. **Move furniture and valuables away from the suspected leak site.** **Clear a path for a plumber or restoration professional to access the wall or ceiling without delay.**
5. **Check the water meter's "leak indicator" dial once the main valve is closed.** **If the small triangular or star-shaped dial on the meter is still spinning while your house valve is shut off, the leak is likely between the meter and your home.**
6. **Contact your landlord or building management immediately if you are a renter or condo owner.** **Report that you have a "suspected internal pipe failure" and that you have already performed an emergency water shutoff.**
7. **Call an emergency plumber if you are the homeowner.** **Specify that you hear "active dripping or hissing inside the wall cavity" to ensure they prioritize the call as a potential burst pipe.**

## What can wait
- You don’t need to determine the exact cause (e.g., pinhole leak vs. failed coupling) before calling a professional.
- You don’t need to contact a public adjuster or your insurance agent until the active flow of water is stopped and the site is stabilized.
- You don’t need to start a "mold remediation" plan in the first few hours; your focus is on stopping the water and drying the area.

## Important reassurance
Hearing water without seeing it is a common source of high anxiety. By shutting off the main valve, you have already taken the single most effective step to prevent a total flood. Most modern leak detection tools can locate the source through the wall without needing to demolish the entire room.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a suspected plumbing emergency. It does not replace the diagnostic equipment or repair skills of a licensed plumber or the safety assessment of a qualified electrician.

## Important note
If you smell ozone (a "metallic" or "electric" smell), see sparks, or hear hissing combined with a smell of natural gas, evacuate the premises immediately and call 911. Water contacting high-voltage lines or gas pipes creates an immediate life-safety hazard.
