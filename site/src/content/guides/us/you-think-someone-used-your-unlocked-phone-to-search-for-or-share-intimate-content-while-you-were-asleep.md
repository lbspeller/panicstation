# What to do if… you think someone used your unlocked phone to search for or share intimate content while you were asleep (USA)

## Short answer
Get to a safer, private place first, then secure your phone and main accounts. If you are in immediate danger, call 911; if you feel unwell or are worried you may have been assaulted or drugged, seek urgent medical or sexual assault support.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the suspected person while you are alone or in a place where you do not feel safe.
- Do not hand your phone to the suspected person to “check it” or “explain it”.
- Do not delete messages, photos, browser history, app logs, or account activity before making a brief note or screenshot, unless deleting is needed for your immediate safety.
- Do not post public accusations right now; it may increase risk and make the situation harder to control.
- Do not force yourself to investigate everything at once.
- Do not talk yourself out of support because you are unsure; hotlines and medical care can help you think through uncertainty.

## What to do now
1. **Create immediate safety and distance.**  
   If you are near the person you are worried about, move to a room you can lock, go to a trusted friend or neighbor, or leave if that is safer. If you are in immediate danger or need police, fire, or ambulance help, call 911.

2. **Make a brief factual note.**  
   Write down the time you woke up, where the phone was, what looked wrong, and any body symptoms that concern you, such as pain, bleeding, nausea, unusual drowsiness, or memory gaps. Keep it short.

3. **Secure the phone before doing more checking.**  
   Change the phone passcode to a longer code. Check whether any face unlock, fingerprint unlock, recovery phone number, recovery email, or trusted device was added or changed. Turn off lock-screen message previews if someone nearby could see them.

4. **Do a light-touch check for possible sharing.**  
   Look only at the most likely places first: sent texts and DMs, sent email, recently opened browser tabs, downloads, recent photo shares, recently deleted photos, and newly installed apps or new app permissions for photos, camera, microphone, or contacts. If you find possible sharing, take a screenshot or photograph the screen with another device, then pause.

5. **Stop ongoing account access.**  
   Change passwords for your Apple Account, formerly Apple ID, or Google account first, then your email, then the social media or messaging accounts involved. Turn on two-factor authentication where you can. Review signed-in devices or active sessions and sign out anything you do not recognize.

6. **Use confidential support without deciding what happened.**  
   RAINN’s National Sexual Assault Hotline can provide confidential support and help you find local options. If intimate images may have been shared or threatened, the Cyber Civil Rights Initiative Safety Center and Image Abuse Helpline at 1-844-878-2274 can offer specialized support.

7. **Consider medical care if your body feels wrong or you are worried about assault.**  
   If you have pain, bleeding, injuries, severe distress that feels physically unsafe, unusual drowsiness, memory gaps, or you suspect you were drugged or assaulted while asleep, go to an emergency department or call 911 for urgent help. You can ask the hospital or a hotline whether sexual-assault-trained care is available.

8. **If images were shared or threatened, focus on limiting spread.**  
   Check the FTC image-based abuse page for current information about platform takedown requests and reporting platform problems. If you are an adult and still have a copy of the image or video, StopNCII may help reduce sharing on participating platforms.

9. **Keep reporting optional for now.**  
   If you might want to report later, keep your notes and screenshots somewhere the suspected person cannot access. You do not have to decide about police, legal action, or platform reports right now in order to get support.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to report to police.
- You do not need to confront anyone today.
- You do not need to prove exactly what happened before contacting a hotline or getting medical care.
- You do not need to make public statements.
- You do not need to review every app, message, or account in one sitting.
- You do not need to make relationship, housing, legal, or long-term safety decisions in the next few hours unless there is immediate danger.

## Important reassurance
It is reasonable to feel confused, embarrassed, angry, numb, or unsure when something may have happened while you were asleep. Taking steps to protect your body, phone, accounts, and privacy is valid even when you only have partial information.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: get safer, reduce immediate harm, secure access, and preserve choices. Later decisions about reporting, takedowns, legal options, housing, relationships, or ongoing support may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, investigative, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe or unwell, seek emergency help or healthcare.
