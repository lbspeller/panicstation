# What to do if… you think someone used your unlocked phone to search for or share intimate content while you were asleep (USA)

## Short answer
Move to a private, secure location and immediately update your device and account security settings. If you suspect physical harm or drugging, seek urgent medical evaluation at an emergency department.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront the suspected person if you are alone or feel physically unsafe.
- Do not delete browser history, app logs, sent messages, or "Recently Deleted" photos, as these are critical evidence.
- Do not hand the phone to the suspected person to ask for an explanation or to "fix" the issue.
- Do not post public accusations on social media while the situation is active.
- Do not assume you must have "proof" before contacting a support hotline or seeking medical care.

## What to do now
1. **Prioritize your physical safety and create distance.**  
   **If you are currently near the person you suspect, move to a room with a lock, go to a trusted neighbor, or leave the premises.** If you are in immediate danger or fear for your physical safety, call **911**.

2. **Briefly document the signs that prompted your concern.**  
   **List the specific observations that suggest the phone was used, such as open browser tabs, unrecognized sent messages, or changed app positions.** If you feel physical discomfort, pain, or unusual grogginess, make a brief note of these sensations for a healthcare provider.

3. **Secure the device against further unauthorized access.**  
   **Change your phone’s passcode and review biometric settings (FaceID or Fingerprint) to ensure no new profiles were added.**
   - Remove any unrecognized passkeys or recovery emails in your security settings.
   - Disable lock screen message previews.

4. **Perform a limited review of communication and photo apps.**  
   **Check the "Sent" folders in SMS, email, and social media DMs, and look for recent activity in your photo gallery’s "Recently Deleted" or "Shared" albums.** If you find evidence of sharing, take a photo of the screen using a different device or take a screenshot and send it to a secure, private cloud account you control.

5. **Terminate active sessions and secure core accounts.**  
   **Change the passwords for your primary email, Apple ID, or Google account, and use the "Sign out of all devices" or "Review devices" feature to disconnect any session you do not recognize.** Ensure two-factor authentication is active on all accounts.

6. **Contact specialized support for image-based abuse.**  
   **If you believe intimate images have been shared or are being used for threats, contact the Cyber Civil Rights Initiative (CCRI) Image Abuse Helpline at 1-844-878-CCRI (2274).** You can also use the **StopNCII.org** tool (for adults 18+) or NCMEC’s **Take It Down** (for those under 18) to proactively block the sharing of specific images on participating platforms.

7. **Seek a medical-forensic exam if physical assault is suspected.**  
   **If you suspect you were drugged or assaulted while asleep, go to an Emergency Department and ask for a Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) or Sexual Assault Forensic Examiner (SAFE).** These clinicians provide trauma-informed care and can collect evidence (such as toxicology for drugging) even if you have not yet decided to file a police report.

8. **Access confidential crisis support.**  
   **Call the RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-HOPE (4673) or text "HOPE" to 64673 for confidential assistance.** They can help you navigate local resources, medical options, and safety planning without requiring you to make legal decisions.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to involve law enforcement.
- You do not need to confront the person or end a relationship immediately if you need time to plan a safe exit.
- You do not need to pursue legal remedies or takedown notices in the first few hours.
- You do not need to explain the situation to friends or family members.

## Important reassurance
Waking up to the suspicion that your privacy or body was violated is deeply disorienting. It is common to feel a range of emotions including numbness, anger, or confusion. Taking steps to secure your digital life is a valid way to regain a sense of control, regardless of whether you have complete information.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and harm reduction. Long-term recovery, legal strategy, and formal image removal processes usually require ongoing support from specialized advocates or legal professionals.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal, medical, or professional investigative advice. If you are experiencing a medical emergency or are in physical danger, contact emergency services immediately.
