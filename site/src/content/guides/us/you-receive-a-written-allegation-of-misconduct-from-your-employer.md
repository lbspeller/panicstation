# What to do if… you receive a written allegation of misconduct from your employer (USA)

## Short answer
Do not rush a detailed response. Save the allegation, stay factual, and ask in writing what specific conduct is alleged, what policy is involved, what process is being used, and when a response is due.

## Do not do these things
- Do not quit on the spot or send an angry email.
- Do not delete, alter, or “clean up” emails, chats, files, time records, browser history, or anything on work systems.
- Do not guess, fill gaps, or explain things you have not checked.
- Do not sign an admission, statement, agreement, or “last chance” document you do not understand or agree with.
- Do not pressure coworkers, argue the case with them, or ask them to take sides.
- Do not contact the person you think complained except through the process your employer sets.
- Do not retaliate against anyone connected to the complaint.
- Do not take restricted company data; note what exists and where it is instead.

## What to do now
1. **Save the allegation and create a private record.**  
   Keep the letter, email, attachments, envelopes, message headers, and meeting invites. In a private note, write the date and time received, who sent it, what it alleges, any deadline, and any meeting already scheduled.

2. **Send one calm acknowledgement.**  
   Keep it short: say you received the allegation, you want to understand the process, and you are asking for the details needed to respond accurately.

3. **Ask for the specific allegation and process in writing.**  
   Ask:
   - What exact conduct is alleged, including dates, times, places, and people involved.
   - What policy, rule, handbook section, or instruction they say was breached.
   - Whether this is an investigation, disciplinary meeting, suspension review, or another process.
   - Whether they want a written statement, interview, or both.
   - The response deadline and whether you can have a reasonable extension.
   - What documents, messages, records, or evidence they are willing to share or identify before you respond.

4. **Preserve and map relevant evidence without removing restricted data.**  
   Make a list of where helpful records may exist: email threads, chat logs, ticket systems, schedules, timekeeping, expense records, delivery or location logs, access logs, training records, prior instructions, and relevant policy documents. Do not forward confidential company material to yourself unless you are clearly allowed to do so.

5. **If you are union-represented, ask for representation before an investigatory interview.**  
   If you are a union-represented employee in a workplace covered by the NLRA and you reasonably believe the interview could lead to discipline, you may request a union representative. The employer generally does not have to tell you about this right. Say clearly: “I am requesting union representation before answering investigatory questions.” After that, say you are not answering investigatory questions until the representative is present or the meeting is ended or rescheduled.

6. **Check the written rules that apply to you.**  
   Look at your handbook, conduct policy, disciplinary policy, offer letter, employment agreement, collective bargaining agreement, or professional rules if your job is licensed. Ask HR to identify the exact policy sections and the process they say they are following.

7. **Prepare a short, factual response only after you know enough.**  
   If a response is due before you receive everything you asked for, keep it limited. Confirm what you know, deny or correct only what you can support, identify what you need to review, and avoid speculation, blame, or long background stories.

8. **If suspension or leave is mentioned, get the terms in writing.**  
   Ask whether it is paid or unpaid, whether benefits continue, who your contact person is, what work systems you may access, whether you may contact coworkers, and how you can obtain records needed to respond.

9. **If you think the allegation is linked to protected activity, document that separately.**  
   Make a clean timeline if the allegation followed a discrimination or harassment complaint, accommodation request, wage or hour concern, safety concern, union activity, or discussion of working conditions. Record dates, people, what you reported or requested, and what changed afterward.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to make an external complaint.
- You do not need to write a full defence before you know the allegation and process.
- You do not need to negotiate an outcome immediately.
- You do not need to prove everything in one message.
- You do not need to explain your emotions, motives, or entire work history right now.

## Important reassurance
A written misconduct allegation can feel like a final decision, but it is often the start of a process. The safest early move is to slow the exchange down, protect the record, and avoid statements or signatures you cannot take back.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a union representative, worker center, legal aid clinic, professional licensing adviser, or employment attorney in your state.
