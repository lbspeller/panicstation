# What to do if… you receive a written allegation of misconduct from your employer (USA)

## Short answer
Do not resign or sign any separation documents during the meeting. State clearly that you are not resigning today and request all company proposals and reasons for the requested exit in writing.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign a resignation letter, severance agreement, or "voluntary" waiver on the spot.
- Do not resign verbally to "protect your record" without first confirming the impact on unemployment eligibility.
- Do not believe verbal promises that you can still get unemployment benefits if you quit voluntarily.
- Do not delete work emails, calendar invites, or performance reviews before you have secured copies of your personal records.
- Do not take or download trade secrets, client lists, or proprietary data to use as evidence.
- Do not argue or become hostile; keep the interaction strictly procedural.

## What to do now
1. **Request a written proposal immediately.** If pressured to quit, state: "I am not resigning at this time. Please provide the company's proposal and the specific reasons for this request in writing so I can review them."
2. **Clarify the nature of the exit.** Ask directly: "Is the company terminating my employment today if I do not resign?" Write down the exact response, or note if they refuse to answer.
3. **Invoke your legal review periods if applicable.**
   - If you are age 40 or older, federal law (OWBPA) generally requires that you be given 21 days to review a severance offer (45 days in group layoffs) and 7 days to revoke after signing.
   - If you are in California, state law requires a minimum of 5 business days to review a separation agreement for employers with 5 or more employees.
4. **Document the "resign or be fired" ultimatum.** Note the date, time, attendees, and specific phrases used, such as "it will look better if you quit" or "we will fire you if you don't sign this."
5. **Secure your personal employment records lawfully.** Save copies of your original offer letter, signed handbook acknowledgments, most recent performance evaluations, and any emails praising your work.
6. **Request information on final pay and benefits.** Ask for the date of your final paycheck and information on COBRA health insurance continuation. Most states require final pay within a specific window (e.g., immediately or within 72 hours).
7. **Research your state’s unemployment "good cause" rules.** Unemployment is state-governed; visit your State Workforce Agency website to see if "resignation in lieu of termination" is treated as a discharge for benefits purposes in your jurisdiction.
8. **Check for overbroad gag clauses.** Be aware that under NLRB rulings (like *McLaren Macomb*), private-sector employers generally cannot force non-supervisory employees to sign overly broad non-disparagement or confidentiality clauses in exchange for severance.
9. **Monitor EEOC deadlines if discrimination is suspected.** If you feel forced to quit due to harassment or discrimination (constructive discharge), you generally must file with the EEOC within 180 or 300 days of your notice of resignation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to respond to a performance improvement plan (PIP) or a severance offer in the room.
- You do not need to decide whether to hire an attorney in the first hour.
- You do not need to explain your "side of the story" to HR until you have seen their written allegations.
- You do not need to notify your colleagues or update your LinkedIn status immediately.

## Important reassurance
Being pressured to resign is a high-stress tactic designed to elicit a fast, unconsidered reaction. Taking a pause to ask for written documentation is a standard professional response and does not mean you are being difficult or "guilty" of anything.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for at-will employment situations in the USA. It does not cover specific union grievance procedures, civil service protections, or specialized executive contracts.

## Important note
Employment laws and unemployment eligibility criteria vary significantly by state. Deadlines for filing discrimination or wage claims can be very short. Consult your state labor department or a qualified employment professional for guidance specific to your location and contract.
