# What to do if… your device says the startup disk is not detected and you still need the data on it (USA)

## Short answer
Do not enter your password or click "Yes" yet. Disconnect the device from the internet if the prompt appeared while you were browsing, and verify the "Publisher" name in the prompt details before taking any action.

## Do not do these things
- Don't type your administrator password into a box that appeared while you were simply browsing a website.
- Don't call any phone number displayed inside a pop-up window or on a "blue screen" warning.
- Don't allow anyone who calls you "out of the blue" to remotely access your computer to fix the prompt.
- Don't disable User Account Control (UAC) or security settings just to make the prompt go away.
- Don't ignore a prompt that reappears immediately after you click "No" or "Cancel."

## What to do now
1. **Identify if you initiated the action.** 
   **Recall exactly what you clicked in the three seconds before the prompt appeared.** 
   - Legitimate prompts usually follow an intentional action like installing software, updating an app, or changing system-level settings (like Date / Time or User Accounts).
   - In 2026, Windows may also prompt you just for viewing sensitive areas like "Storage" settings under Baseline Security Mode.

2. **Check the Publisher and Program details.**
   **Click "More details" or "Show details" on the prompt to see who is requesting access.**
   - A legitimate request from the operating system will list "Microsoft Windows" or "Apple" as the verified publisher.
   - If the publisher is "Unknown" or a name you do not recognize, treat the request as high-risk.

3. **Force-close the browser if the prompt is stuck on a website.**
   **Use hardware shortcuts to kill the process if you cannot click away.**
   - On Windows: Press `Ctrl + Shift + Esc` to open Task Manager, right-click your browser (Chrome, Edge, etc.), and select "End Task."
   - On Mac: Press `Cmd + Option + Esc`, select your browser, and click "Force Quit."

4. **Isolate the device if you suspect a scam.**
   **Turn off the Wi-Fi or unplug the Ethernet cable immediately.**
   - This prevents a malicious program from communicating with an attacker while you decide how to proceed.
   - If the device is frozen with a loud audio warning or a "virus detected" screen, perform a hard shut down by holding the power button for 10 seconds.

5. **Route work or school devices through official IT channels.**
   **Stop all troubleshooting and contact your organization's helpdesk via a verified phone number or portal.**
   - Do not attempt to bypass admin restrictions on managed devices, as this can trigger automated security alerts.
   - Provide a photo or screenshot of the prompt to your IT team.

6. **Run a manual security scan on personal devices.**
   **Open your installed security software (such as Windows Security or a third-party antivirus) and trigger a "Full Scan."**
   - Ensure the software is updated before scanning.
   - If the scanner identifies a threat, follow the "Quarantine" or "Remove" recommendations provided by the software.

7. **Secure your accounts if you already provided credentials.**
   **Assume the password you typed is compromised and change it from a different, trusted device.**
   - Update your device login password.
   - Change passwords for sensitive accounts (banking, email, primary ID) if they were the same as the admin password.
   - Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on all critical accounts.

8. **Report the incident to federal authorities.**
   **File a report if you lost money, provided personal data, or gave remote access to a stranger.**
   - Use `ReportFraud.ftc.gov` for consumer-related tech support scams.
   - Use `ic3.gov` (FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center) for serious cyberattacks or financial loss.

## What can wait
- You don't need to determine the exact malware family or "how" the site targeted you right now.
- You don't need to factory reset the device immediately unless a security professional or IT department advises it.
- You don't need to buy new "security cleanup" software recommended by a pop-up; your built-in OS tools are sufficient for initial stabilization.

## Important reassurance
Seeing an administrator prompt does not always mean you have been hacked. Modern operating systems in 2026 are designed to be "loud" and prompt you for many actions that used to be silent. Pausing to check the publisher is a standard, safe habit that prevents most common digital compromises.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for unexpected permission requests. It does not replace a comprehensive forensic audit or professional IT repair if a system is fully compromised.

Administrator privileges allow a user or program to make permanent changes to your operating system; never grant this access unless you are certain of the requester's identity and intent.
