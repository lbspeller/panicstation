# What to do if… you smell gasoline or fuel around your parked vehicle (USA)

## Short answer
Assume it could be a fuel leak: do not start or drive the vehicle, keep people away from the fumes, and arrange a tow, roadside assistance, or a mobile mechanic. If there is visible liquid fuel, a strong or worsening smell, fumes in an attached or enclosed garage, or anyone feels unwell, get to fresh air and call 911.

## Do not do these things
- Do not start the engine or “just move it” while you can smell gasoline or fuel.
- Do not smoke, vape, light matches, use a lighter, or use any open flame near the vehicle.
- Do not plug in chargers, use jump packs, run power tools, or operate anything that could spark close to strong fumes.
- Do not crawl under the vehicle, open the hood, or try to clamp or repair a fuel line while fumes are present.
- Do not stay in a garage or enclosed parking area breathing fuel vapour.
- Do not drive the vehicle to “test it” or to get it to a repair shop.

## What to do now
1. Move yourself, children, pets, and bystanders away from the vehicle. Increase the distance if the smell is strong, the vehicle is indoors, or you can see liquid on the ground.

2. If the vehicle is in a garage or enclosed parking area, leave the space first. If you can do it without walking into strong fumes or operating a switch right next to the vapour, open the main door or exit to let air in.

3. If the vehicle is in an attached garage and fumes are strong or spreading into the home, get everyone outside and call 911. Do not go back inside to investigate.

4. From a safe distance, look for obvious warning signs: a fresh puddle under the vehicle, dripping liquid, a wet trail, or the strongest smell near the fuel door, fuel cap, rear of the vehicle, or engine area.

5. If the smell is faint, you have just refueled, there is no visible liquid, and it is safe to approach briefly, check that the gas cap is properly tightened. Step back afterwards; if the smell remains strong or returns, treat it as a possible leak.

6. Arrange professional help rather than driving. Call roadside assistance for a tow, contact a repair shop, or arrange a mobile mechanic, and tell them you suspect a fuel leak or fuel vapour around a parked vehicle.

7. Call 911 now if there is visible pooling fuel, a flowing leak, strong or increasing fumes, symptoms such as dizziness, headache, nausea, weakness, or trouble breathing, or any concern that vapours could collect inside a building.

8. If there is a significant spill or release and immediate danger is already being handled, the National Response Center can take oil and chemical spill reports at 800-424-8802. Do not delay calling 911 for an urgent fire, health, or building-safety risk.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact failed part right now.
- You do not need to compare repair prices until the vehicle is safe and inspected.
- You do not need to check recalls, warranty coverage, or insurance before dealing with the immediate fumes or leak.
- You do not need to clean up a significant fuel spill yourself; distance and the right responders matter first.

## Important reassurance
A fuel smell can come from something minor, such as a loose cap or recent refuelling, but it can also signal a leak that should not be ignored. The safest first step is simple: do not start the vehicle, move away from fumes, and get the right help.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate situation only. A qualified mechanic, tow operator, emergency responder, insurer, or local environmental agency may need to help with later decisions.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, mechanical, environmental, insurance, or other professional advice. If you believe there is immediate danger, call 911 and follow local emergency instructions.
