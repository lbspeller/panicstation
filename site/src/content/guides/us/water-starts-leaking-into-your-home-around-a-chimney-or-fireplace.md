# What to do if… water starts leaking into your home around a chimney or fireplace (USA)
## Short answer
Keep everyone out of the wet area. If water may be near electrical equipment, turn off power at the main breaker only if the route and panel area are dry; otherwise leave it alone and call a qualified electrician.

## Do not do these things
- Do not touch outlets, switches, cords, appliances, or wet controls in or near the affected area.
- Do not turn power on or off while standing in water.
- Do not use the fireplace, wood stove, gas fireplace, or another appliance venting through that chimney.
- Do not use switches, electronics, or a phone inside if you smell or hear gas leaking. Leave first.
- Do not climb onto the roof during rain, wind, or unsafe conditions.
- Do not stand under a sagging ceiling or swollen drywall.
- Do not try improvised sealing or cleanup that brings you near electrical equipment, unstable materials, or an unsafe wet area.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** Move people and pets out of the room. Keep away from wet flooring, wet walls, outlets, cords, appliances, and the area below any sagging ceiling.
2. **Deal with electrical risk first.**
   - If water is in or near electrical equipment, turn off the main breaker only if you can reach the panel by a completely dry route and the panel area is dry.
   - If you would need to enter water, or the panel area is wet, do not approach it. Call a qualified electrician.
   - If there is fire, sparking, a burning smell, or possible electric shock, leave the area and call 911.
3. **Stop using anything venting through the chimney.**
   - Do not use the fireplace, wood stove, gas fireplace, or another appliance connected to that chimney until it has been assessed.
   - Turn off an appliance only if you can do so without entering the wet area or touching wet controls.
   - If a carbon monoxide alarm sounds, or anyone has possible carbon monoxide symptoms such as headache, dizziness, weakness, nausea, vomiting, sleepiness, or confusion, get outside to fresh air immediately and call 911. Do not go back inside until emergency responders say it is safe.
   - If you smell or hear gas leaking, leave immediately. From outside, contact your gas utility emergency line. Call 911 if there is immediate danger or you cannot reach the utility promptly.
4. **Contain water only if it is safe.** From a dry, stable area, place a bucket or container under a drip and use towels to limit spreading. Keep away from electrical equipment, wet controls, hot surfaces, and sagging materials.
5. **Keep clear of unstable materials.** Close off the area below sagging, swollen, soft, or bubbling ceiling material or drywall. Do not remain in the room if materials are shifting, shedding, or worsening.
6. **Document the visible damage.** Take photos or video from a safe location. If you rent, notify your landlord or property manager promptly and follow up in writing. If you own the home, contact your insurer if you may need to make a claim.
7. **Arrange the right inspection.** Contact a roofer or chimney professional. Possible causes can include flashing, a chimney cap, a chimney crown, or masonry and mortar problems.
8. **Start drying after electrical safety is confirmed.** Once the leak is controlled and the area is safe to work in, begin safe drying or arrange water-damage help promptly. Water-damaged areas should generally be dried within 24–48 hours where possible to reduce the chance of mold growth.
9. **Ask before using the chimney again.** Have a chimney professional assess the affected fireplace, chimney, or venting system before returning it to service.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact entry point before calling a roofer or chimney professional.
- You do not need to choose a permanent repair, settle an insurance question, or start cosmetic work immediately.
- You do not need to repaint, open walls, or run fans before the leak is controlled and electrical safety is confirmed.

## Important reassurance
Water entering around a chimney or fireplace can feel urgent and overwhelming. The useful first steps are limited: stay out of the wet area, deal with electrical risk, stop using the chimney, contain water only when safe, and arrange an inspection.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later repairs may need a roofer, chimney professional, electrician, water-damage contractor, landlord, property manager, or insurer.

## Important note
This is general information, not professional inspection, electrical, structural, insurance, legal, medical, or other professional advice. Call 911 for immediate danger, possible electric shock, fire, suspected carbon monoxide exposure, or urgent symptoms.
