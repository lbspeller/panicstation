# What to do if… your building access badge stops working and you are told to wait outside (USA)

## Short answer
Follow security instructions, move to a safe nearby place if waiting outside is unsafe or exposed, and immediately ask your manager or HR for written instructions about where to wait and how your time should be recorded.

## Do not do these things
- Do not force entry, tailgate behind someone, bypass a turnstile, or argue with security.
- Do not disappear without telling your manager or HR where you are waiting and how to reach you; that could be treated as an attendance problem.
- Do not hand over unlocked personal devices, personal account passwords, or personal files.
- Do not post about it publicly or send angry messages to coworkers, security, HR, or your manager.
- Do not sign documents on the spot while you are surprised or upset; ask for copies and time to read them.
- Do not start packing up your workspace unless you are clearly instructed to do so.

## What to do now
1. **Move to the safest reasonable waiting spot while staying reachable.**  
   If the weather, location, or crowd makes waiting outside unsafe or uncomfortable, tell security: “I am going to wait in the reception area if allowed, or at the nearby coffee shop. I will answer immediately if HR or my manager contacts me.” Then stay where you said you would be.

2. **Ask security one calm clarifying question.**  
   Ask: “Is this just a badge or system issue, or were you instructed not to let me enter?” Write down the time, the names or badge numbers of the people you spoke with if visible, and their exact answer.

3. **Ask who authorized the instruction.**  
   Ask security for the name or role of the person who told them to keep you outside, and whether there is a badge ticket, access-control ticket, or incident number. Do not pressure them if they cannot share it.

4. **Message your manager and HR in one short, factual thread.**  
   Say that you arrived for work, your badge would not open the building, security told you to wait outside, and you are ready and available to work. Include your exact waiting location and ask who is responsible for giving you instructions today.

5. **Ask for written instructions about what to do next.**  
   Ask whether you should stay nearby, wait in reception, work remotely, go home, or report to another entrance. If someone calls you, send a follow-up message afterward saying, “Confirming my understanding of your instruction today…”

6. **Ask how your time should be recorded.**  
   Ask: “Should I clock in or out? How should I report this waiting time? If I am being sent home, how will today be coded?” If you are salaried and exempt, ask payroll to confirm how any employer-directed absence is handled before assuming a deduction is proper or improper.

7. **If you need essential items inside, ask for an escorted retrieval.**  
   Ask specifically for medication, keys, wallet, coat, phone charger, or work equipment you need today. Keep the request narrow and do not remove extra items unless instructed.

8. **If questions may lead to discipline and you are union-represented, request your representative before answering.**  
   Say clearly: “I request my union representative before this interview continues.” If you are not union-represented, you can ask whether company policy allows a support person or a scheduled meeting, but do not assume that request must be granted.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to quit, accuse anyone, or explain your whole side of the story.
- You do not need to contact coworkers to investigate what happened.
- You do not need to debate legal labels such as suspension, administrative leave, termination, or lockout until HR or management gives you written information.
- You do not need to solve the badge system problem yourself.

## Important reassurance
A failed or deactivated badge can happen because of a technical error, expired credential, schedule change, security instruction, or HR decision. The safest first move is to stay calm, stay reachable, and create a clear record that you arrived ready to work and followed instructions.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the immediate moment only. Later decisions may need help from HR, payroll, your union, an employment lawyer, or a wage-and-hour agency depending on what your employer puts in writing.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, employment, therapeutic, or professional advice. Employment rules, pay rules, union rights, and workplace policies vary by state, job classification, contract, and employer.
