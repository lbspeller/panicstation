# What to do if… your water heater pressure relief valve is leaking or discharging water intermittently (USA)

## Short answer
Treat intermittent temperature and pressure relief valve discharge as a safety warning: keep people away from the hot discharge, do not block the pipe, and shut off the water heater’s energy source if you can do that safely. Then arrange a qualified plumber, licensed where required locally, to check the pressure, temperature controls, expansion control, valve, and discharge pipe.

## Do not do these things
- Do not cap, plug, clamp, pinch, or block the relief valve outlet or discharge pipe.
- Do not install or close any shutoff valve on the discharge pipe.
- Do not stand over, touch, or look closely into the discharge pipe; the water or steam may scald.
- Do not test the valve lever while the heater is hot, steaming, or actively discharging.
- Do not assume it is harmless because it only happens sometimes.
- Do not add cold water to an overheating tank or spray the heater with a hose.
- Do not relight or restart the heater until the cause has been checked.

## What to do now
1. **Move people and pets away from the discharge.**  
   Keep clear of the heater, the valve, and the end of the discharge pipe. If it is only dripping and you can do so without blocking the pipe opening, place a bucket under the pipe end to limit water damage.

2. **Shut off the water heater’s energy source if you can do it safely.**  
   For an electric water heater, switch off the breaker labeled for the water heater. For a gas water heater, use the heater’s control or shutdown instructions if you can reach them safely. If you smell gas, leave the area and call 911 or your gas utility from outside.

3. **If water is pouring, steaming, or the area feels unsafe, step back and escalate.**  
   Keep distance from the heater and discharge path. Call an emergency plumber. If you believe there is immediate danger that you cannot safely control, leave the area and call 911.

4. **If it continues to discharge after the heat is off, reduce the incoming water only if you are sure which valve it is.**  
   Turn off the cold-water shutoff valve feeding the water heater, often on the cold line above the heater. If you are not sure, do not guess; wait for help.

5. **Keep the discharge pipe open and visible.**  
   The pipe is there so hot water or steam can discharge safely. Make sure nobody blocks it with a cap, rag, tape, fitting, hose, or closed valve.

6. **Call a qualified plumber and describe exactly what you saw.**  
   Ask them to check house water pressure, thermal expansion control, the expansion tank if present, thermostat and gas or electric controls, the relief valve condition, and whether the discharge pipe is installed safely. If you rent, notify your landlord or property manager promptly and describe it as a water heater safety discharge.

7. **Take quick notes from a safe distance.**  
   Note when it happens, such as during heating, after showers, or after hot water use. Take a photo or short video of the valve area and discharge pipe if you can do that without getting close to hot water or steam.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the exact cause right now.
- You do not need to drain the tank unless a qualified professional tells you to.
- You do not need to decide tonight whether the whole water heater must be replaced.
- You do not need to prove the valve is bad by testing it yourself during an active discharge.
- You can wait to adjust the thermostat setting until the heater is cool, the discharge has stopped, and the manual or a qualified professional confirms the safe way to do it. Many households use about 120°F to reduce scald risk, but household needs and equipment can vary.

## Important reassurance
The relief valve is a safety device, and discharge means it may be responding to heat, pressure, expansion, or a valve problem. The safest first steps are simple: keep clear, keep the pipe open, remove the heat source if safe, and get the cause checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to stabilize the situation and avoid dangerous mistakes. Later decisions may need a qualified plumber, local code requirements, the manufacturer’s instructions, or landlord involvement.
