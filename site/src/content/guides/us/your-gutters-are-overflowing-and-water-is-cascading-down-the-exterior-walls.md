# What to do if… your gutters are overflowing and water is cascading down the exterior walls (USA)

## Short answer
Stay on the ground, maintain a 10-foot distance from wet electrical components, and prioritize diverting water away from the foundation and indoor living areas using temporary ground-level barriers.

## Do not do these things
- Do not climb ladders or access the roof during rain, high winds, or lightning.
- Do not enter any area with standing water if it is in contact with outlets, cords, or the electrical panel.
- Do not touch electrical switches, breakers, or appliances if your hands or the floor are wet.
- Do not use a hose to try to "clear" a clog during the storm, as this adds volume to the flooding.
- Do not attempt to "test" if a surface is electrified; assume all wet electrical areas are live.
- Do not ignore the smell of burning or ozone; evacuate and call **911** immediately.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the immediate electrical hazard zone.** Check the interior of the affected wall for water dripping near outlets, switches, or light fixtures.
   - Maintain a 10-foot safety perimeter from any electrical component showing signs of moisture.
2. **Shut off power only if the path and panel are completely dry.** If you can safely reach the breaker panel without standing in water or touching wet surfaces, turn off the circuits for the affected rooms.
   - If the panel area is damp or the floor is wet, stay away and call your utility provider or **911** if sparking occurs.
3. **Contain water entry inside the home.** Place buckets or heavy towels at the base of the wall to catch drips.
   - Move furniture, electronics, and rugs at least 5 feet away from the wet wall.
4. **Check ground-level downspout outlets for visible blockages.** If it is safe to be outside, clear debris only from the very bottom of the downspout or the yard drain grate where water exits.
   - Do not reach into drains or downspouts if you cannot see the bottom.
5. **Divert the "waterfall" away from the foundation.** Use a splash block, flexible extension, or even a heavy plastic tarp weighted with stones to guide cascading water at least 6 feet away from the house.
   - Focus on moving water away from window wells, basement vents, and door thresholds.
6. **Protect basement window wells if they are filling.** If a window well is filling like a tank, clear any leaves from the grate or use a temporary cover to prevent a sudden burst into the basement.
7. **Report public infrastructure clogs to 311.** If the overflow is caused by a backed-up street drain or municipal ditch, contact your local **311** service or public works department.
8. **Call 911 for life-safety or fire threats.** Escalate immediately if you see smoke, see arcing electricity, or if water is rising into living spaces faster than you can manage.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine why the gutter failed (clog vs. pitch vs. size) until the weather is dry.
- You do not need to clean the upper gutters or downspout entries while it is raining.
- You do not need to start drying or "tearing out" drywall until the active leak is stopped and an electrician has cleared the area.
- You do not need to contact insurance until the immediate movement of water is stabilized.

## Important reassurance
Seeing water cascade down a wall is alarming because roof runoff is highly concentrated. However, as long as you maintain electrical safety and move the water a few feet away from the foundation on the ground, you are preventing the most severe forms of structural and water damage.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization guidance only. Professional assessment of the roof, gutter system, and electrical circuits will be required once the storm has passed.

## Important note
This guide is for general information. If you suspect an electrical hazard or structural compromise, prioritize evacuation and contact emergency services. Water intrusion into electrical systems is a fire and shock hazard that requires professional inspection.
