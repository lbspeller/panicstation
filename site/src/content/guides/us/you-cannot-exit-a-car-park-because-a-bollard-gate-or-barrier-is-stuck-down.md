# What to do if… you cannot exit a car park because a bollard, gate, or barrier is stuck down (USA)

## Short answer
Turn off your engine to prevent carbon monoxide buildup, move to a safe pedestrian area, and use the "Help" button on the exit kiosk or call the facility management number posted on nearby signage.

## Do not do these things
- Do not leave your engine idling in an enclosed or underground garage.
- Do not attempt to manually force, lift, or unscrew the barrier or bollard mechanism.
- Do not ram the barrier with your vehicle.
- Do not attempt to "tailgate" behind another vehicle through a closing gate.
- Do not reverse blindly or aggressively into traffic lanes.
- Do not call 911 for a mechanical delay unless there is a fire, medical crisis, or immediate physical threat.

## What to do now
1. **Turn off your ignition immediately.** Even in large garages, vehicle exhaust can lead to a dangerous buildup of carbon monoxide (CO) if you are stationary for several minutes.
2. **Move your vehicle to a safe pause point.** If the lane is wide enough or if there is a "bypass" area, pull out of the direct exit path to allow other drivers to attempt the exit or to clear the way for emergency vehicles.
   - Activate your hazard lights.
   - Ensure you are not blocking pedestrian walkways or fire exits.
3. **Locate and press the "Help" or "Intercom" button on the exit kiosk.** Most modern US parking systems (like TIBA, SKIDATA, or Amano McGann) have a dedicated button that connects directly to a remote monitoring station.
   - State clearly: "I am at Exit [Number/Location] and the barrier is failing to lift."
   - If the machine has retained your ticket or credit card, report this immediately.
4. **Call the facility management or "After Hours" number.** Look for a sticker on the ticket machine, a sign near the elevator/lobby, or a placard at the entrance. 
   - Provide your exact location, including the street address of the garage and the level you are on.
   - If no number is posted, check your digital parking reservation or app (e.g., SpotHero, ParkWhiz) for a support contact.
5. **Check for a manual release or "breakaway" arm.** Many US parking arms are designed with "breakaway" pins or magnetic releases for emergency egress. 
   - Look for a clearly marked "Emergency Manual Release" lever on the side of the gate cabinet.
   - Only use this if you see instructions provided by the facility; do not use tools or excessive force.
6. **Use non-emergency services if management is unreachable.** If you have made multiple attempts to contact the garage operator over 15–20 minutes with no response:
   - Call **311** (the municipal non-emergency line) or the local police non-emergency phone number.
   - Explain that you are "lawfully on the premises but blocked from exiting due to equipment failure and cannot reach management."
7. **Document the situation for fee disputes.** Take photos of the malfunctioning gate, any error messages on the screen, and the time. 
   - Keep your original ticket or a screenshot of your digital payment/entry time.

## What can wait
- You do not need to settle "overstay" fees or lost-ticket charges while still trapped; focus on exit first.
- You do not need to file a formal complaint or insurance claim against the garage on-site.
- You do not need to worry about "abandoning" the vehicle if you decide to leave it and return later, provided you have contacted the non-emergency police line to inform them of the situation.

## Important reassurance
Malfunctioning parking equipment is a common technical failure, and most facilities have remote override capabilities. While the delay is stressful, following the communication steps usually results in a remote release within a few minutes once you reach a human operator.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization and egress. It does not cover legal disputes regarding parking fines, vehicle damage sustained during gate failure, or long-term parking contract resolutions.

## Important note
In the United States, 911 should only be used for life-safety emergencies. For mechanical issues or being "locked in" without immediate danger, use the 311 system or local non-emergency police dispatch. Carbon monoxide is an odorless, colorless gas; always prioritize turning off your engine in any enclosed structure.
