# What to do if… your visa or entry permission expires before your planned departure date (USA)

## Short answer
Check your I-94 now: the "Admit Until Date" or "D/S" is usually what controls how long you can stay, not the visa expiration date in your passport. If your planned departure is after your authorized stay ends, focus on one immediate path: leave earlier, request a correction if the I-94 is wrong, or use the correct extension/change process before your authorized stay ends if you are eligible.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume the visa expiration date equals how long you are allowed to stay in the United States.
- Don’t overstay "by a day" to match your original itinerary.
- Don’t file an extension or change request you do not qualify for just to delay departure.
- Don’t assume filing a request means it has been approved.
- Don’t leave the United States while an extension or change request is pending unless you have checked the consequences for your situation.
- Don’t rely on unofficial fixers or anyone promising guaranteed immigration outcomes.
- Don’t delete travel emails, airline notices, I-94 screenshots, or filing receipts.

## What to do now
1. Retrieve your most recent I-94 and identify the controlling entry notation.

   Use the official CBP I-94 site to get your most recent I-94. Save a copy as a PDF or screenshot and email it to yourself. Look for:
   - "Admit Until Date" with a specific date; or
   - "D/S", meaning Duration of Status.

2. If you have a specific "Admit Until Date", compare it with your planned departure.

   If your flight, train, cruise, or land-border departure is after that date, contact the carrier or travel agent now and ask for the earliest realistic departure on or before the I-94 date. Keep the old itinerary and the changed itinerary.

3. If you have "D/S", confirm what keeps you in status today.

   D/S can apply to students, exchange visitors, and some other nonimmigrant categories. Contact the person or office tied to your status, such as your DSO, program sponsor, employer, petitioner, or a qualified immigration adviser, and ask what date or event controls your allowed stay and departure timing.

4. If the I-94 date looks wrong, ask CBP about a correction quickly.

   Use CBP Deferred Inspection or the relevant port’s I-94 correction instructions. Have your passport bio page, visa page if any, admission stamp if any, I-94, and travel itinerary ready. Keep copies of messages you send and receive.

5. If you can leave in time, make leaving on time the simplest fix.

   Rebook to depart on or before the I-94 "Admit Until Date", or before the status end point confirmed for D/S. A small buffer is safer than aiming for the final hour of the final day.

6. If you need to stay longer, check the correct extension or change route before your authorized stay ends.

   Many nonimmigrants, including many visitors, may use Form I-539 to request an extension or change of status, but some categories use a different process or are not eligible. Use the official USCIS page and instructions for your category, file before your authorized stay ends if eligible, and save the confirmation, receipt, and a copy of everything submitted.

7. If your authorized stay has already ended, reduce further risk.

   Plan to leave as soon as you realistically can, unless a qualified U.S. immigration attorney advises a different immediate step for your specific facts. Keep basic documents showing what happened, such as medical records, airline cancellations, emergency notices, or filing records. Get qualified advice before filing anything after an overstay.

8. Keep proof of departure and compliance.

   Save boarding passes, itineraries, passport stamps, I-94 screenshots, USCIS receipts, CBP messages, and airline notices. These may help answer later questions or correction requests if a departure record or status record is disputed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether you will apply for another U.S. visa in the future.
- You do not need to write a long explanation right now; secure the I-94, the key date or D/S notation, and basic evidence first.
- You do not need to try to persuade airline staff or border staff to fix an I-94 problem at departure; use the official correction route instead.
- You do not need to choose a long-term immigration plan before handling the immediate date problem.

## Important reassurance
It is common to panic because the visa sticker date and the I-94 date do not match. Checking the I-94, confirming D/S if it applies, and taking one concrete step today can reduce the risk of making an avoidable mistake.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. The correct next move depends on your status category, I-94 notation, travel history, and timing, and later decisions may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. U.S. immigration consequences can be serious and fact-specific, especially if your authorized stay has already ended or you are unsure what D/S means for you.
