# What to do if… your hood release is stuck and you have a dashboard warning light (USA)

## Short answer
Pull over to the safest available place, turn on your hazard lights, shut the engine off, and get roadside assistance instead of forcing the hood open beside traffic.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep driving with an overheating warning, oil-pressure warning, smoke, flames, strong fuel smell, or a burning smell.
- Don’t continue a trip after a charging-system warning unless a safe, qualified person has told you it is okay to do so.
- Don’t pry the hood open with tools or yank the release cable while you are beside moving traffic.
- Don’t open the hood right away if overheating is possible; hot steam and pressurized coolant can burn.
- Don’t remove a radiator or coolant cap while the engine is hot.
- Don’t stand in front of the vehicle or on the traffic side trying to find the latch.

## What to do now
1. **Get to the safest available stop.** Signal and move to the nearest safe shoulder, turnout, exit, or parking lot. The right shoulder is usually best if it is available and safe. Turn on your hazard lights.
2. **Call 911 if there is immediate danger.** Call if the vehicle is in a travel lane, there has been a crash or injury, you see flames or heavy smoke, you smell strong fuel, or you cannot get away from traffic safely.
3. **Shut the engine off.** This is especially important if the warning suggests overheating or low oil pressure.
4. **Choose the safest waiting position.** If you are on a high-speed shoulder and there is no fire, heavy smoke, or fuel smell, it may be safer to stay seat-belted inside with hazards on. If you can clearly move away from traffic to a barrier or safe area, exit on the side away from traffic.
5. **Look without opening the hood.** From a safe position, check for flames, heavy smoke, leaking fuel, or a growing fluid pool. If you see these, move farther away and call 911.
6. **Let the vehicle cool.** If overheating is possible, do not touch the hood area or coolant parts. Wait for roadside help or a technician.
7. **Call roadside assistance or a tow.** Say: “My hood release will not open. I got a warning light. I cannot check under the hood. I need roadside assistance or a tow.”
8. **If you are on a California freeway with Freeway Service Patrol available, use it only for non-emergencies.** You can generally dial 511 and follow the motorist-aid or freeway-assist prompt. Availability, hours, and wording vary by area. For emergencies, call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not need to diagnose the warning right now.
- You do not need to top off oil, coolant, or any other fluid while the hood is stuck.
- You do not need to decide the exact repair today.
- You do not need to force the hood open before the vehicle is in a safer place.

## Important reassurance
A stuck hood release is frustrating, but it is usually a repairable mechanical problem. The safer choice is to treat the warning seriously, stop driving, and let roadside help handle the hood and the warning away from traffic.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the roadside moment only. The hood latch, release cable, and underlying warning may need roadside service, towing, or a repair shop after you are safe.

## Important note
This is general information, not vehicle-specific mechanical, legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or professional advice. Follow emergency instructions from 911, police, highway patrol, fire services, roadside assistance, or your vehicle manufacturer.
