# What to do if… your spare key has been moved or accessed without your permission (USA)

## Short answer
Treat the key as compromised. Get to a safer pause first; if you think someone may be inside or nearby, call 911, and otherwise contact local law enforcement through its non-emergency route and arrange to rekey or change the affected locks.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t go inside to investigate if you feel unsafe, hear movement, see an open door or window, or notice fresh damage.
- Don’t confront or threaten someone you suspect right now.
- Don’t keep using the same outside spare-key setup once it may be known.
- Don’t post your address, spare-key location, lockbox code, routines, or suspicions publicly.
- Don’t tidy, move, or reset a disturbed area if you may want police to take a report, unless you need to do so for immediate safety.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a safer pause first.**  
   Step away from the door or spare-key spot. Go to your car, a neighbor’s home, a lobby, a front desk, or another public place where you can make calls.

2. **Choose 911 or non-emergency.**  
   Call 911 if you think someone may still be inside, you hear movement, someone is nearby and threatening you, or you feel in immediate danger. If it seems suspicious but not immediate, contact the local police or sheriff’s non-emergency line for the city or county where the property is located; use online reporting only if that agency offers it for this type of incident.

3. **Do only an outside check from a safe position.**  
   Look for a door that is not fully closed, a window that is open, fresh marks near a lock, a damaged frame, a moved lockbox, or the spare key being missing, bent, wet, dirty, or in a different position. If you see signs of entry, stay out and call law enforcement.

4. **Treat the affected key as no longer private.**  
   Remove any outside hidden key. If a lockbox or key safe was involved, remove the key and change the code from a safe place. Do not put the same key back in the same hiding spot.

5. **Arrange a rekey or lock change.**  
   Rekeying changes which key opens the lock without necessarily replacing all the hardware. If the lock, door, strike plate, or frame is damaged, ask about repair or replacement instead. Try to do this the same day if practical.

6. **Reduce other ways the same key access could matter.**  
   Check garage entry, side doors, basement doors, gates, storage areas, smart locks, alarm codes, keypad codes, and app access. Change codes or remove unknown users where you can do that safely.

7. **If you rent, contact the landlord or property manager in writing.**  
   Say the spare key may have been accessed or moved, ask whether anyone entered or had reason to handle keys, and request urgent help with key control or rekeying. Entry, notice, and lock-change rules vary by state and lease, so keep the message factual and focused on safety.

8. **Write down a simple record.**  
   Note when the key was last normal, when you noticed the change, what looked different, who legitimately had access, and what you did next. Take photos of the spare-key spot, lockbox, door, lock, frame, and any damage before moving things, if it is safe.

9. **Check for missing or damaged property only after safety is settled.**  
   Once you are safe and the locks are being handled, do a calm walk-through with another trusted person if possible. If anything is missing or damaged, ask local law enforcement how to add it to a report and ask your insurer what documentation they need.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove who did it right now.
- You do not need to decide today whether to move, buy a full alarm system, or replace every door and window.
- You do not need to confront a neighbor, roommate, staff member, vendor, landlord, or property manager before you secure access.
- A full property inventory can wait until you are safe, the locks are handled, and you have a clearer head.

## Important reassurance
A moved or missing spare key can feel violating even when nothing else is obvious. You are not overreacting by pausing, securing access, and making a clear record.

## Scope note
These are first steps only: immediate safety, local reporting routes, lock and key control, and basic documentation. Later decisions about tenancy, insurance, disputes, or security upgrades may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, insurance, security, or other professional advice. If you feel unsafe or in immediate danger, call 911.
