# What to do if… water is overflowing from your gutter and running behind the downspout into your wall (USA)
## Short answer
Move away from any wet electrical area. Contain indoor drips only if it is safe, and arrange urgent help from property maintenance or a gutter contractor without climbing a ladder while the area is wet or the rain is falling.

## Do not do these things
- Do not climb a ladder while rain is falling, footing is wet or slippery, winds are strong, or visibility is poor.
- Do not reach into a gutter or downspout outlet.
- Do not touch wet electrical items, outlets, switches, fixtures, appliances, or the electrical panel.
- Do not approach the electrical panel if water is near it or if you would need to cross standing water.
- Do not plug in fans or dehumidifiers using outlets in the affected area until it is safe to use electricity there.
- Do not seal exterior gaps or open the wall while water is still entering.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safe pause.** Keep people away from the wet area. If you see sparking, smoke, or signs of fire, leave the area and call 911. If the electrical panel is dry and you can reach it with dry hands and dry footing without crossing standing water, switch off the affected circuit only if you can identify it without guessing. Do not remove the panel cover. Otherwise, do not approach the panel; contact an electrician or emergency maintenance.
2. **Limit indoor water spread.** Move belongings away from the wet wall or floor. Use towels, a bucket, or a tray under drips. A plastic sheet may help guide water into a container if you can place it without entering an unsafe area.
3. **Check from ground level only.** From a safe position, note the exact spot where water is spilling over the gutter edge. Look for a visible gap where the downspout meets the gutter outlet, a separated downspout joint, a leaking seam, or a gutter section pulling away from the fascia. Water spilling near the outlet may indicate a blockage or another flow problem.
4. **Leave the exterior repair for a safe pause.** Keep clear of the area below the overflow. Do not try to clear, reconnect, or reseat gutter or downspout parts while rain is falling, footing is wet, or water is pouring down the wall.
5. **Report the problem clearly.** If you rent, contact your landlord, property manager, or emergency maintenance line now. If you own the property, contact a gutter contractor or roofer for urgent help. Say where the overflow is, whether water is entering indoors, and whether any electrical area may be affected.
6. **Start safe drying promptly.** Once water entry has stopped or clearly slowed and electricity is safe to use, begin drying wet materials. Open windows only if the weather allows. Use fans or dehumidifiers only from a confirmed-safe power source. Aim to start drying as soon as safely possible and within 24–48 hours where practical.
7. **Record what happened.** Take photos or video of the overflow point, the downspout, the outside wall, and any indoor damp area. Note when it started and whether rain was heavy. This can help with repair, landlord, and possible insurance follow-up.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause right now, such as a clog, failed seam, poor slope, or another gutter defect.
- You do not need to open the wall or remove drywall during the active leak unless a qualified professional tells you to do so.
- You do not need to decide about an insurance claim during the first safe pause.
- You do not need to plan a permanent gutter upgrade tonight.

## Important reassurance
A concentrated overflow can look alarming. The useful first steps are limited: stay clear of electrical risk, reduce indoor water spread, record the location, and get the gutter or downspout checked.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. A contractor or building maintenance team may need to clear or repair the gutter, reconnect the downspout, correct the water flow, and inspect any wet building materials.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Get qualified help if electricity may be unsafe or water continues to enter the wall.
