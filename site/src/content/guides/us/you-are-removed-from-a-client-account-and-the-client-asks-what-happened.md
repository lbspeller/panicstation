# What to do if… you are removed from a client account and the client asks what happened (USA)

## Short answer
Do not guess or give the client an internal explanation. Pause, route the message to your manager or the account owner, and ask for written direction on who should respond and what you are authorized to say.

## Do not do these things
- Do not speculate about why the account changed, even if the client presses for an answer.
- Do not blame a coworker, your employer, or the client.
- Do not share internal employment information, confidential client information, or anyone’s personal details with the client or publicly.
- Do not try to regain access through someone else’s login, informal workarounds, or shadow systems.
- Do not send a long defensive message, threaten action, or post about the situation publicly.
- Do not forward client information or restricted work records to a personal account or device.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether you have been told not to contact the client.** If you have, do not reply. Forward the client’s message to your manager or the account owner. If you are unsure whether you may reply, ask before sending anything.
2. **If you are authorized to send a holding reply, keep it brief.** For example:  
   “Thanks for reaching out. Your account is being handled by our team. I’ve asked the account owner to follow up with you directly.”
3. **Ask your manager or the account owner for written direction.** Ask who now owns the client relationship, whether you should stop direct client contact, and what wording is approved if the client asks again.
4. **Keep a brief factual note.** Record when access changed, what systems were affected, who contacted you, what the client asked, and any instruction you received. Do not copy confidential client information or restricted records into a personal account or device.
5. **Keep client material in approved work systems.** Do not export information “just in case”. Follow any instruction about equipment, local files, or approved deletion steps.
6. **If you are asked to sign something, ask for a copy.** Ask whether you can have time to review it before signing. Do not assume that any deadline can be extended.
7. **If you are concerned about discrimination, retaliation, or another protected issue, keep permitted records and factual notes.** Use an internal HR, ethics, or compliance route if one is available and appropriate. Do not bypass access controls or forward confidential material to yourself.
8. **Ask for clarification if an instruction seems unusually broad.** Some workers may have protections for certain protected concerted activity, such as acting with coworkers about working conditions. That does not automatically authorize disclosure of confidential client or business information. Ask what specific information and contacts are restricted.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to quit, threaten legal action, or explain yourself to the client in detail.
- You do not need to settle every question about why the account changed before sending a neutral handoff or pausing contact.
- You do not need to decide every next step immediately. If discrimination, retaliation, or whistleblower concerns may apply, promptly check whether any deadline or specialist advice is relevant.

## Important reassurance
An account change does not, by itself, tell you why it happened. A short neutral handoff, or a pause if you have been told not to reply, gives you time to get written clarification without creating a new problem.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first hours or day. If the situation develops into a suspension, termination, allegation, or formal complaint process, later decisions may need advice based on your role, employer policies, contract, and state.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Employment, confidentiality, retaliation, and whistleblower rules can vary by employer, state, industry, role, contract, and the facts. Keep client messages short, neutral, and authorized, and ask for written clarification when needed.
