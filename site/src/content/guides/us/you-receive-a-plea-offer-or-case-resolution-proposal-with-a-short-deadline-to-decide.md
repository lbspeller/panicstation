# What to do if… you receive a plea offer or case resolution proposal with a short deadline to decide (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign, accept, or reject any proposal until you have reviewed the full written agreement with your defense attorney. If you do not have an attorney, request a pause from the court to obtain counsel before making any admission of guilt.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t agree to a plea simply to secure immediate release from custody without reviewing the long-term impact on your record.
- Don’t sign any document containing an "appeal waiver" or "stipulated facts" until your lawyer explains exactly which rights are being surrendered.
- Don’t discuss the details of the offer with other inmates, witnesses, or co-defendants, as these communications are not privileged.
- Don’t assume the judge must follow the prosecutor's sentencing recommendation; many agreements are non-binding on the court.
- Don’t make a final decision if you are currently experiencing medical distress, withdrawal, or extreme sleep deprivation.

## What to do now
1. **Contact your defense attorney immediately to notify them of the deadline.**  
   - Explicitly state the date and time the offer is set to expire.
   - If you are unrepresented, tell the judge or the prosecutor: "I cannot make an informed decision on this offer without first consulting with a lawyer."
2. **Request a complete physical or digital copy of the proposed agreement.**  
   - Ensure you have the full text, including all "fine print" regarding supervised release, fines, and restitution.
   - Confirm if the offer includes a requirement to testify against others or waive the right to see evidence (discovery).
3. **Ask your attorney to clarify the "worst-case" result of a rejection.**  
   - Identify the maximum possible sentence if you lose at trial compared to the plea offer.
   - Ask if the prosecutor intends to add "enhancements" or additional charges if the case proceeds.
4. **Demand a specific explanation of immigration and "collateral" consequences.**  
   - If you are not a U.S. citizen, your lawyer has a constitutional duty under *Padilla v. Kentucky* to advise you on deportation risks.
   - Ask how a conviction will affect your specific employment licenses, housing eligibility, and firearm rights.
5. **Direct your lawyer to request a formal extension of the deadline.**  
   - Attorneys can often secure more time by citing the need to review outstanding evidence or conduct necessary research.
   - If the prosecutor refuses, have your lawyer place the refusal on the record at the next court appearance.
6. **Confirm whether the plea is "binding" or "non-binding" on the judge.**  
   - In federal court, ask if it is a Rule 11(c)(1)(B) recommendation or a 11(c)(1)(C) binding agreement.
   - In state court, ask if the judge has already indicated they will accept the specific terms.
7. **Use only protected channels for all case-related discussions.**  
   - Limit details to attorney-client meetings, legal mail, or official "legal calls" provided by the facility.
   - Assume that any standard phone call or message sent from jail is being recorded and can be used to undermine your defense.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide how to explain the situation to family or employers today.
- You do not need to finalize your post-release housing or job plans before signing.
- You do not need to gather character letters or "mitigation" evidence during the initial offer review.
- You do not need to decide whether you "feel" guilty; the decision should be based on a cold assessment of the evidence and legal risk.

## Important reassurance
The pressure of a short deadline is a common tactical tool used in the legal system, but you have a right to a "knowing and voluntary" plea. Taking time to read the paperwork is a standard procedural step, and your hesitation to sign a complex legal contract is a normal, protective response.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling urgent legal offers. It does not replace the specific legal advice of an attorney who is familiar with your local court rules, the specific prosecutor, and the evidence in your case file.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not legal advice. The Sixth Amendment guarantees the right to effective assistance of counsel; if you feel you are being forced into a plea without understanding it, you must inform the court immediately.
