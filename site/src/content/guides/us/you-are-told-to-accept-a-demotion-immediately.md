# What to do if… you are told to accept a demotion immediately (USA)

## Short answer
Request the specific on-call requirements and pay structure in writing before performing any tasks, then log every minute of restricted time or active work.

## Do not do these things
- Do not verbally agree to new terms without a written record of expectations.
- Do not resign or send confrontational messages in the immediate heat of the moment.
- Do not perform "off the clock" work such as answering texts or troubleshooting without recording the time.
- Do not delete communication logs, schedule screenshots, or digital requests for availability.
- Do not assume that "standby" time is automatically unpaid if your movement is strictly restricted.

## What to do now
1. **Request the on-call instruction and expectations in writing immediately.** 
   Ask for a digital or physical copy of the specific requirements to ensure there is no ambiguity.
   - Start and end times of the on-call period.
   - Required response time (e.g., must answer within 10 minutes).
   - Geographic restrictions (e.g., must remain within 20 miles of the site).
   - Behavioral restrictions (e.g., must remain sober and ready to work).
2. **Clarify the pay rate for both standby time and active work time.** 
   **Ask specifically how to record time spent "waiting to engage" versus time spent "engaged to wait."** 
   - Confirm if a flat stipend is provided for being available.
   - Confirm the hourly rate for any actual work performed during the shift.
3. **Check your current employment classification and handbook policies.** 
   **Identify if you are classified as non-exempt (hourly) or exempt (salaried), as this determines federal overtime eligibility.** 
   - Non-exempt workers must be paid for all hours worked, including on-call time if restrictions are severe.
   - Check for existing "Predictive Scheduling" or "Fair Workweek" policies if you are in Oregon or cities like NYC, Seattle, or Chicago.
4. **Log every interaction and restriction during the on-call period manually.** 
   **Keep a personal record of every call, text, and period where you were unable to use your time for personal activities.** 
   - Note the exact time a call started and ended.
   - Record instances where you had to cancel personal plans due to the short response window.
5. **State your immediate physical or safety constraints if you cannot comply.** 
   **If fatigue, childcare, or health issues make immediate on-call duty unsafe, communicate this fact-based limitation in writing.** 
   - Use neutral language: "I am unable to provide the required response time tonight due to prior childcare obligations."
6. **Inquire about the approval process for overtime hours.** 
   **Ask for written confirmation on whether you are authorized to exceed 40 hours in the workweek while responding to calls.** 
7. **Contact HR or a relevant labor agency if you are pressured to work without pay.** 
   **Reach out to the U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division (WHD) if you are being denied pay for restricted on-call time.** 

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to quit this job today.
- You do not need to file a formal legal complaint or lawsuit in the first few hours.
- You do not need to resolve long-term scheduling conflicts for future months right now.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel pressured or overwhelmed when a schedule is changed without notice. Requesting written instructions and pay details is a standard professional practice and does not constitute a refusal to work. Documentation protects both your time and your eventual compensation.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate workplace change. It does not cover long-term employment litigation or specific union contract negotiations.

## Important note
On-call compensability is highly fact-specific and depends on the degree to which an employee can use the time for their own purposes. While federal law provides a baseline, some states offer additional protections for last-minute schedule changes. If you are in a safety-sensitive role, prioritize safety and fatigue management above all else.
