# What to do if… an investigator shows up at your workplace asking questions about you (USA)

## Short answer
Do not provide substantive answers or sign documents immediately. Obtain the investigator's credentials, state that you will not answer questions without a lawyer, and request to return to work or leave the premises.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to explain the situation or chat to seem cooperative.
- Do not consent to a search of personal items, including bags, lockers, or vehicles.
- Do not provide passwords or unlock electronic devices for investigators.
- Do not sign any "consent to search" or "voluntary statement" forms without legal review.
- Do not discuss the interaction with coworkers, as they may be questioned later.
- Do not physically resist or interfere with investigators if they have a warrant.

## What to do now
1. **Move the interaction to a private area to minimize workplace disruption.**  
   Ask the investigator if you can speak in a private office or conference room rather than at your desk or in a common area.
2. **Verify the identity and authority of the individuals present.**  
   Ask for a business card or official credentials and record the name, agency, badge number, and contact information for each person.
3. **Establish whether the interaction is voluntary or if you are being detained.**  
   Ask: "Am I free to leave or return to my duties?" 
   - If they say yes, you may end the conversation and walk away.
   - If they say no, you are being detained and should move to step 4.
4. **Invoke your right to remain silent and your right to an attorney clearly.**  
   Say: **"I am going to remain silent. I want a lawyer."**  
   Once these rights are invoked, stop speaking entirely; in many states, you must provide your legal name and date of birth if you are being detained, but you do not have to answer any other questions.
5. **Direct investigators to your employer's legal or HR department for company-related requests.**  
   Tell the investigator: **"Please coordinate any further requests through the company's legal counsel or Human Resources."**  
   This ensures that the employer's official channels handle requests for records or access to company property.
6. **Reference your 2026 "Workplace Know Your Rights" notice if you are in California.**  
   Remind your employer of their obligation to notify your designated emergency contact if you are arrested or detained on-site, as required by the 2026 Workplace Know Your Rights Act (SB 294).
7. **Request a representative if the questioning is by your employer and could lead to discipline.**  
   Say: **"If this meeting could result in disciplinary action, I request a union representative (or a witness) be present."**  
   While Weingarten rights apply primarily to union members, requesting a witness or representative is a standard protective step for all employees.
8. **Document the encounter immediately after the investigators leave.**  
   Write down the time, location, names of witnesses, and the specific questions asked, using a personal device or notebook rather than work-owned systems.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide whether to cooperate with an investigation today.
- You do not need to explain why you are requesting a lawyer.
- You do not need to review or respond to accusations on the spot.
- You do not need to apologize for exercising your constitutional rights.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel startled or intimidated when officials arrive at a place of employment. Requesting a lawyer and remaining silent are not admissions of guilt; they are standard legal protections. Your coworkers' reactions are outside of your control, and focusing on clear, verbal boundaries is the most effective way to stabilize the situation.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for unexpected official questioning at a place of employment. It does not provide long-term legal strategy or defense for specific criminal or civil allegations.

## Important note
Rights during workplace interactions vary by state, particularly regarding "Stop and Identify" laws. This information is general and does not constitute legal advice. In some jurisdictions, you may be required to provide identifying information if an officer has reasonable suspicion of a crime.
