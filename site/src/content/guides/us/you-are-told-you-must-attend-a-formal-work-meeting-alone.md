# What to do if… you are told you must attend a formal work meeting alone (USA)

## Short answer
Ask what the meeting is about, whether you will be questioned, and whether discipline could result. If you are union-represented, or you are a federal bargaining-unit employee, clearly request union representation before answering investigatory questions.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume there is a universal right to bring any person you choose. The answer can depend on union coverage, the type of meeting, public-sector rules, a contract, workplace policy, or a reasonable-accommodation request.
- Do not quit, threaten resignation, or send an angry message while you are under pressure.
- Do not guess or fill gaps. If you do not know or do not remember, say so.
- Do not sign a document without reading it and asking whether your signature confirms receipt or agreement. Ask for time to review it where possible, and state any disagreement if you are allowed to add a note.
- Do not delete messages, files, calendar entries, or other records related to the meeting.
- Do not secretly record the meeting unless you have checked applicable law and workplace policy.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the purpose of the meeting.**  
   Ask whether the meeting is fact-finding, whether you will be questioned, what topics will be discussed, who will attend, and whether discipline could result.

2. **If you are union-represented in a private-sector job that may be covered by the NLRA, request representation clearly.**  
   Say: “I request a representative before we continue with investigatory questions.”  
   Under current NLRB guidance, this right applies to union-represented employees and can arise when management questions you as part of an investigation and you reasonably believe the interview could lead to discipline or another adverse consequence. Some workers and employers are outside NLRA coverage, so check the NLRB coverage page or contact the NLRB if you are unsure. In a covered situation, the employer may allow representation and delay the interview, end the interview, or offer you the choice of continuing without representation or ending the interview.

3. **If you are a federal bargaining-unit employee, make the same request.**  
   A federal representation right can apply when an agency representative examines you in connection with an investigation, you reasonably believe discipline may result, and you request representation. If you work for a state or local public employer, ask your union, employee-relations contact, or HR team which contract, policy, or state or local rule applies.

4. **If your request is refused, keep the exchange calm and factual.**  
   Ask: “To confirm, is my request for representation being denied?”  
   Do not argue, walk out, or assume you can refuse an instruction. Ask what the employer expects next. Contact your union representative promptly if you have one, and write down what happened.

5. **If you are not covered by a union representation rule, ask about other available options.**  
   Ask whether workplace policy allows a permitted companion, representative, or note-taking arrangement. If a disability-related adjustment may be relevant, tell HR that you need a change because of a medical condition and ask how to request a reasonable accommodation. Ask what adjustment, if any, is available; do not assume that a particular companion is guaranteed.

6. **Ask for a brief pause or written topics where possible.**  
   Say: “I want to respond accurately. Please tell me the topics I need to address and whether I can have a brief pause or provide a written follow-up.”  
   The employer may still require the meeting to proceed, so answer accurately based on what you know and do not guess.

7. **Make a factual record after the meeting.**  
   Note the date, time, attendees, topics, questions, your answers, any request for representation or accommodation, the response you received, and any deadline. Send a short factual follow-up email if that would help confirm the next step.

8. **Get prompt outside help if the stakes are high.**  
   Contact your union representative if you have one. Consider specific employment-law advice promptly if the meeting may involve termination, discrimination, retaliation, a protected complaint, or a document you are being asked to sign.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide during the meeting whether to resign, negotiate an exit, file a complaint, or take legal action.
- You do not need to give a rushed answer when you genuinely do not know or do not remember.
- You can ask for time to review a document before agreeing with it.
- Some grievance, NLRB, EEOC, and legal deadlines may continue running, so ask promptly which deadlines might apply.

## Important reassurance
Being told to attend alone can feel one-sided. You can still reduce pressure by clarifying the meeting’s purpose, making a clear representation request where applicable, keeping your answers accurate, and documenting what happens.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about grievances, agency processes, accommodations, negotiations, or legal claims may need help from a union representative, an appropriate workplace contact, or an employment professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Workplace rights can depend on the employer, union coverage, public-sector rules, the type of meeting, workplace policy, and the facts.
