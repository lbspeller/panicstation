# What to do if… you are asked to sign paperwork after a death and you do not understand it (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before signing anything you do not understand. Ask for a copy and ask whether the form authorizes cremation, embalming, transfer of the remains, or a personal payment obligation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign because you feel guilty, rushed, or worried that you are causing a delay.
- Do not sign blank or incomplete forms, or forms with sections that may be filled in later.
- Do not assume that being next of kin, an executor, or a personal representative automatically means you should sign every form.
- Do not authorize cremation, embalming, or another action that cannot be undone while you are unsure who may sign or what the form does.
- Do not provide passwords, PINs, or bank logins as part of paperwork after a death.

## What to do now
1. **Create a pause.**  
   Say: “I am not able to sign until I understand this. Please give me a copy and explain what each signature authorizes.”

2. **Ask for the name and purpose of the form.**  
   The paperwork may be called a release or transfer form, cremation authorization, embalming authorization, funeral-services agreement, payment agreement, or death-certificate worksheet. Ask what the specific form does in this case.

3. **Ask these questions before signing.**
   - “Does this allow cremation, embalming, transfer of the remains, or another action that cannot be undone?”
   - “Does this make me personally responsible for payment?”
   - “What role am I being asked to sign in?”
   - “What state rule, document, or provider policy are you relying on to decide who may sign?”

4. **Ask for price information before agreeing to funeral purchases.**  
   If you are speaking face to face with a funeral provider covered by the Federal Trade Commission Funeral Rule and the discussion turns to funeral goods, funeral services, prices, or the overall type of funeral or disposition arrangements, ask for the General Price List. The provider should give you a copy to keep when that discussion begins. After you select goods or services, ask for the written itemized statement before paying.

5. **Treat a death-certificate worksheet separately from an authorization form.**  
   If the form is being used to collect information for the death certificate, check names, spelling, dates, and other personal details carefully. Ask whether signing the worksheet only confirms information or also authorizes something else.

6. **If there is disagreement, ask whether the action can be paused.**  
   Say: “There is uncertainty about who may authorize this. Can you pause any cremation, embalming, or transfer while that is checked and confirm the pause in writing?”  
   If the answer is unclear, ask for a manager. If a medical examiner or coroner is involved, ask that office about its own release process.

7. **Get another person to read the form with you.**  
   Ask for a copy by email or on paper. Ask a trusted person to read it with you. For a dispute about signing authority, payment, or an action that cannot be undone, ask whether a local attorney or legal-aid organization can review the form promptly.

8. **Ask the hospital what support is available.**  
   If the paperwork came from a hospital, ask whether it has decedent-affairs, patient-affairs, bereavement, or social-work staff who can explain its process and which forms are routine.

9. **Keep a simple record.**  
   Save copies of the forms and note who asked you to sign, when they asked, and what they said the form would do.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose every optional funeral detail now.
- You can ask which decisions are needed before a transfer or time-sensitive action and which decisions can wait.
- You do not need to resolve every family disagreement before asking for a pause.
- You do not need to solve probate or estate administration before understanding the form in front of you.
- You can ask which document creates a payment obligation before agreeing to pay.

## Important reassurance
Paperwork immediately after a death can be hard to process. Asking for a copy, asking what a signature does, and taking time to check who may sign are reasonable steps.

## Scope note
These are first steps for slowing the situation down and avoiding a signature you do not understand. Rules about who may authorize cremation, disposition, or release of remains vary by state, and later decisions may need state-specific legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. For a dispute about signing authority, payment, or an action that cannot be undone, seek independent advice for the state where the issue is happening.
