# What to do if… you notice new swelling in both legs and worsening breathlessness at night (USA)

## Short answer
New swelling in both legs together with worsening breathlessness at night or when lying flat can be a sign of a serious heart, lung, kidney, or fluid-balance problem. Get urgent medical assessment now; call 911 now if breathing is severe, sudden, or comes with chest pain, fainting, confusion, blue or gray lips, or coughing blood.

## Do not do these things
- Do not try to sleep it off if breathlessness is worsening at night or when you lie flat.
- Do not drive yourself if you are very short of breath, dizzy, faint, confused, or having chest pain; call 911.
- Do not take extra diuretics, water pills, or heart medicines unless you have a written clinician plan that tells you exactly what to do for this situation.
- Do not rely on a normal-looking home pulse-ox, smartwatch, or blood-pressure reading if you feel worse.
- Do not spend time trying to prove the cause before getting help.
- Do not start new supplements, salt restriction, fluid restriction, or major diet changes as a substitute for urgent assessment.

## What to do now
1. **Call 911 now if any emergency sign is present:**
   - severe difficulty breathing, gasping, or being unable to speak in full sentences
   - chest pain, chest pressure, fainting, severe weakness, or new confusion
   - blue or gray lips, face, or fingertips
   - coughing up blood or pink, frothy mucus
   - sudden severe shortness of breath

2. **If symptoms are not 911-level but are new or clearly worsening, arrange same-day urgent medical assessment.**
   - Say clearly: **“I have new swelling in both legs and worsening shortness of breath at night or when lying flat.”**
   - If your primary care clinician, cardiologist, or nurse line cannot tell you where to be seen right away, the emergency department is usually the safest place for this combination.

3. **Stay upright while help is arranged.**
   - Sit supported upright, loosen tight socks or waistbands, and avoid stairs, carrying bags, or rushing.
   - If you must rest, keep your upper body raised rather than lying flat.

4. **Have someone else help with transport or calls if possible.**
   - Ask them to stay nearby, call 911 if your breathing worsens, and help gather medicines.
   - If you are alone and breathing is getting worse, call 911 rather than trying to manage the trip yourself.

5. **Bring or prepare a quick information pack, but do not delay care to complete it.**
   - Medication bottles or photos of labels, including diuretics, blood-pressure medicines, diabetes medicines, hormones, pain medicines, and over-the-counter medicines.
   - Allergies, major conditions, and any heart, lung, kidney, or liver history.
   - When the swelling started, whether shoes or socks suddenly became tight, and whether pressing a finger into the swelling leaves a dent.
   - Whether you need more pillows than usual to breathe, woke up gasping, gained weight quickly, missed medicines, started a new medicine, or had a recent long trip, surgery, illness, or injury.

6. **Treat blood-clot warning patterns as higher urgency.**
   - Call 911 or go to the emergency department immediately if you have chest pain, fainting, coughing blood, sudden severe breathlessness, or one leg that is much more swollen, painful, red, or warm than the other.
   - Do not wait to compare your legs if your breathing is getting worse.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the exact cause before seeking care.
- You do not need to decide tonight whether this is heart failure, a blood clot, kidney trouble, medication side effects, or something else.
- You do not need to change long-term medicines unless a clinician tells you to.
- You do not need to solve work, travel, family, insurance, or diet decisions before you are safely assessed.

## Important reassurance
This pattern is alarming, and taking it seriously is reasonable. Many possible causes can be treated, and the safest first move is to get checked while the symptoms are happening or worsening.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to reduce immediate risk and help you get assessed quickly. Later decisions about tests, medicines, diagnosis, or follow-up may need an in-person clinician or specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, diagnosis, or professional care. If you are unsure how severe the breathing problem is, choose the safer option and call 911 or seek emergency care.
