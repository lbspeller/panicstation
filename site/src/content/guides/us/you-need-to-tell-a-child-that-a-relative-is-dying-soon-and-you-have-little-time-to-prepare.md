# What to do if… you need to tell a child that a relative is dying soon and you have little time to prepare (USA)

## Short answer
Tell the child as soon as possible using clear, literal words ("is dying" / "will die"), then provide immediate physical closeness and a concrete plan for who will care for them over the next 24 hours.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use euphemisms like "going to sleep," "going away," or "being lost," as children often take these literally and may develop fears of sleeping or traveling.
- Do not make promises about recovery or "getting better" to soften the news, even if the child seems distressed.
- Do not delay the conversation to wait for a "perfect" moment; the child will likely sense the tension and may imagine a situation worse than the reality.
- Do not include complex medical terminology, insurance issues, or adult family conflicts in the explanation.
- Do not force the child to visit, touch the person, or perform a specific goodbye ritual if they express fear or hesitation.
- Do not ask the child to "be brave" or "take care of" the adults.

## What to do now
1. **Find a private space and one primary speaker immediately.**  
   - **Sit at the child’s eye level and ensure you have their full attention before starting.**
   - **Keep the group of adults small—usually just the parent or the person closest to the child—to avoid overwhelming them.**

2. **Establish what the child has already noticed.**  
   - **Ask "What have you noticed about [Name] lately?" to see what they have observed about the person's appearance or the family's stress.**
   - **Correct any immediate misunderstandings, such as the child thinking they caused the person to be tired or sick.**

3. **State the fact that the person is dying in one or two simple sentences.**  
   - **Use direct language: "The doctors have worked very hard, but [Name]'s body is stopping working, and they are dying."**
   - **Pause for at least ten seconds after speaking to allow the child to process the words before you add more information.**

4. **Address the "Three Cs" of childhood grief proactively.**  
   - **Tell the child clearly: "You did not cause this to happen by anything you did or thought."**
   - **Confirm that the illness is not contagious: "You cannot catch this like a cold or the flu."**
   - **Explain the "cure" status: "The doctors and nurses are doing everything they can to keep [Name] comfortable, but they cannot fix the body now."**

5. **Answer the "Who will take care of me?" question immediately.**  
   - **Outline exactly who will be with them today, where they will sleep, and who will be picking them up from school or activities.**
   - **Provide a grounded answer if they ask "Will you die too?" by saying, "I am healthy and I plan to be here for a very long time to take care of you."**

6. **Provide concrete choices for saying goodbye if time allows.**  
   - **Ask if they would like to visit, send a photo, draw a picture, or record a voice message.**
   - **Describe exactly what they will see before a visit, such as tubes, bandages, sleepiness, or the person being unable to speak.**

7. **Contact the child's school or childcare provider today.**  
   - **Ask the school counselor or teacher to be a "safe person" the child can go to if they feel overwhelmed during the day.**
   - **Request that the school notify you immediately if the child shows sudden changes in behavior or distress.**

8. **Request a Child Life Specialist if the relative is in a hospital.**  
   - **Ask the nurse or social worker: "Is there a Child Life Specialist available to help me prepare my child for a visit?"**
   - **Utilize the hospital's social worker or chaplain for age-appropriate books or resources if a Child Life Specialist is not on-site.**

9. **Identify one reliable resource for follow-up questions.**  
   - **Use the American Academy of Pediatrics' parenting website (HealthyChildren.org) for "what to say" scripts for specific ages.**
   - **Call or text 988 if you are an adult experiencing a mental health crisis or need immediate guidance on supporting a distressed child.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on funeral or memorial service attendance right now.
- You do not need to explain the long-term future, such as where you will live or how holidays will change.
- You do not need to have "the perfect talk" in one sitting; expect to repeat these facts many times over the coming days.
- You do not need to hide your own sadness; it is okay for the child to see you crying as long as they know they are still safe.

## Important reassurance
It is normal for children to "puddle jump" through grief, meaning they may cry intensely for a few minutes and then immediately ask to go play or watch a show. This does not mean they didn't understand or that they don't care; it is a natural developmental defense against being overwhelmed. Your presence and honesty provide the safety they need to process the news at their own pace.

## Scope note
This guide is for the first hours and days of an acute family crisis involving terminal illness. Long-term bereavement support and funeral planning are not covered here and should be addressed once the immediate situation has stabilized.

## Important note
This guide provides general information for crisis stabilization and is not a substitute for professional mental health, medical, or legal advice. If you believe a child is at risk of self-harm or immediate danger, call 911. You can call, text, or chat the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline 24/7 in the U.S. for free, confidential support.
