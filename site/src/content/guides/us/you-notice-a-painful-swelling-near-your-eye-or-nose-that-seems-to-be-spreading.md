# What to do if… you notice a painful swelling near your eye or nose that seems to be spreading (USA)

## Short answer
Seek **urgent, same-day medical evaluation** immediately. If you experience vision changes, difficulty moving the eye, a fever over 100.4°F, or a severe headache, **call 911 or go to an Emergency Room (ER) now**.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait to see if the swelling subsides if it is actively spreading or getting warmer.
- Do not attempt to squeeze, lance, or "pop" any bumps or boils near the nose or eye.
- Do not apply heavy pressure to the swollen area or the eyeball itself.
- Do not use leftover antibiotics or apply topical steroid creams to the area.
- Do not wear contact lenses or apply eye makeup until cleared by a clinician.
- Do not drive yourself to the hospital if you have vision changes, dizziness, or confusion.

## What to do now
1. **Screen for emergency red flags (if any are present, go to the ER now).**  
   - **Changes in vision**, including blurring, double vision, or a sudden loss of "red" color perception.  
   - **Pain when moving the eye** or an inability to move the eye in certain directions.  
   - **The eye appearing to bulge** or be pushed forward from the socket.  
   - **Fever (100.4°F / 38°C or higher)**, chills, or a general "toxic" feeling of severe illness.  
   - **Neurological signs** such as a severe headache, stiff neck, vomiting, or confusion.
2. **Access professional care today even if red flags are absent.**  
   **Contact an ophthalmologist or visit an Urgent Care/ER** for a physical examination. Inform the staff: "I have painful, spreading facial swelling near the eye and nose with concern for cellulitis." This location (the "danger triangle") requires immediate assessment to ensure the infection is not spreading toward the brain or deep into the eye socket.
3. **Record the progression for the clinician.**  
   - **Take a clear photograph** of the swelling in bright, natural light to use as a baseline.  
   - **Note the exact time** the swelling was first noticed and how many inches it has moved since then.  
   - **Identify any recent triggers** such as a sinus infection, dental pain, insect bite, or a pimple you may have scratched.
4. **Minimize irritation while awaiting evaluation.**  
   - **Keep your head elevated** with pillows to help reduce fluid accumulation in the face.  
   - **Apply a cool, damp cloth** to the area gently for 10 minutes at a time; do not use ice directly on the skin.  
   - **Avoid touching the area** to prevent further bacterial spread or irritation.
5. **Gather medical history for the intake process.**  
   - **List all medications** and supplements, specifically noting if you take blood thinners or steroids.  
   - **Identify any chronic conditions** like diabetes or immune system disorders that affect how you fight infections.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific type of bacteria or "cause" before seeking help.
- You do not need to wait for a primary care appointment if an urgent care or ER is available.
- You do not need to decide on long-term specialist care (like ENT or Dermatology) until the acute infection is stabilized.

## Important reassurance
It is common to feel concerned when symptoms spread quickly in a sensitive area. Seeking care now is a proactive step that allows clinicians to use standard, effective treatments to stop the spread of infection.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and emergency screening only. A clinician must perform a physical exam and may require imaging (like a CT scan) or blood tests to determine the depth of the infection and the appropriate antibiotic treatment.

## Important note
This information is not a medical diagnosis and does not replace professional medical advice. If you suspect an emergency or your condition worsens, seek immediate emergency medical care.
