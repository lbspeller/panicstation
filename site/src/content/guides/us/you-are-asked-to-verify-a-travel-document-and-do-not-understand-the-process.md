# What to do if… you are asked to verify a travel document and do not understand the process (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before uploading, mailing, or paying anything. Confirm the request through an official contact route that you find independently, and ask exactly what type of verification is required.

## Do not do these things
- Do not use a link from an unexpected message before confirming the request through the organization’s official website or published contact details.
- Do not upload a passport image or send an original document to an unconfirmed private third party.
- Do not pay a verification or rush fee until you know who is requesting it, why it is required, and which process applies.
- Do not assume that every request for verification means an apostille.
- Do not send extra identity documents just because the request feels urgent.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the requirement in writing.** Ask which document is needed, what “verified” means, who the third party is, why that third party is involved, and whether an in-person check or another official route is available.

2. **Confirm the request independently.** Find the organization’s official website yourself and use its published contact details. Do not rely on phone numbers, links, or email addresses in an unexpected message.

3. **Identify the type of request.** Ask whether they need:
   - an identity check using a passport or other identification document
   - a notarized signature or statement
   - a certified copy
   - an apostille
   - an authentication certificate
   - a translation

4. **If the document will be used outside the United States, check the official route.** The country where the document will be used determines whether an apostille or an authentication certificate is needed. State-issued documents are generally handled first through the state that issued them. Federal documents are generally handled through the U.S. Department of State. Use the official pages below to check the route for your document.

5. **If they ask for a notarized or certified copy, ask who may provide it.** Requirements can vary by state, document type, and requesting organization. Check the relevant state authority before paying for a service or mailing a document.

6. **Share only what has been confirmed as necessary.** Use the confirmed secure route. Keep a record of what you sent, when you sent it, and who received it.

7. **If you already shared documents and suspect misuse, stop sending more information.** Save the messages, receipts, website address, and any reference number. If your information may have been misused or you suspect identity theft, use IdentityTheft.gov. Review the FTC guidance on credit freezes and fraud alerts; placing or lifting a credit freeze has no cost.

## What can wait
- You do not need to pay for rush handling while the request is still unclear.
- You do not need to decide whether to challenge the requirement before confirming what it means.
- You do not need to provide extra identity documents to make the process move faster.
- You do not need to choose a private document service before checking the official route.

## Important reassurance
The word “verification” can refer to several different processes. Pausing to confirm the exact requirement is a reasonable safety step and can prevent unnecessary payments, the wrong paperwork route, or sharing sensitive information with the wrong person.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Once the requirement is clear, later decisions may need help from the requesting organization, the relevant government authority, or another appropriately qualified professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Requirements can vary by organization, destination country, state, and document type. Check the official instructions for your specific situation before sending documents or paying fees.
