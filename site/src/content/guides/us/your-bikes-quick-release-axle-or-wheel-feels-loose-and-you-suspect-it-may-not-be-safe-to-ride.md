# What to do if… your bike’s quick-release, axle, or wheel feels loose and you suspect it may not be safe to ride (USA)

## Short answer
Stop riding immediately and do not continue until the wheel is physically secured and verified. If the wheel cannot be tightened or continues to shift, walk the bike or arrange alternative transportation.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to "ride through" the looseness or reach your destination before checking the wheel.
- Do not tighten a quick-release lever by spinning it like a wingnut; it must be clamped shut using the internal cam mechanism.
- Do not touch disc brake rotors or calipers immediately after riding, as they may be hot enough to cause burns.
- Do not assume the issue is a minor brake rub without physically checking the axle security.
- Do not ignore the problem if the looseness disappears temporarily; wheels can reseat themselves poorly and fail later.

## What to do now
1. **Move yourself and the bike to a safe area away from traffic.** Smoothly decelerate without sudden braking if the bike feels unstable. If the wheel feels like it is vibrating or moving significantly, dismount and walk the bike to the sidewalk or shoulder immediately.
2. **Perform a physical check of the wheel security while the bike is stationary.**
   - Firmly grasp the top of the tire and try to rock it side-to-side between the fork blades or frame stays.
   - Look for any visible gap where the axle meets the frame / fork slots (dropouts).
   - Check if the wheel is centered or if it is leaning toward one side of the frame.
3. **Identify your axle type and apply the specific tightening procedure.**
   - **Quick-Release (QR) Lever:** Open the lever fully. Ensure the axle is pushed all the way into the frame slots. Tighten the nut on the opposite side until you feel resistance when the lever is halfway through its closing arc. Close the lever firmly with the palm of your hand; it should leave a temporary impression on your skin.
   - **Thru-Axle:** Ensure the wheel is fully seated. Thread the axle through the frame and hub completely. Tighten according to the markings on the axle (often 6-12 Nm) or until very firm if using a built-in lever. Ensure any lever is tucked away and not pointing forward or down.
   - **Threaded Axle Nuts:** Ensure the wheel is centered and fully seated. Use a wrench to tighten the nuts on both sides evenly and firmly.
4. **Conduct a spin and brake test before remounting.** Lift the wheel and spin it to ensure it rotates freely without catching on the frame or brakes. Apply the brakes firmly while rocking the bike forward and backward to see if the axle shifts in the frame.
5. **Verify the security after a very short test roll.** Ride slowly for 30 feet in a flat, safe area, then stop and re-check the lever or nuts. If they have loosened again, the components may be damaged or incompatible; stop riding immediately.
6. **Check for signs of tampering if the bike was left unattended.** If you find a wheel loose after parking in public, check other safety-critical points including the other wheel, the seatpost clamp, and the handlebar bolts before departing.
7. **Seek emergency care if you have experienced a fall or head impact.** Call 911 or go to an emergency department if you experience a worsening headache, repeated vomiting, confusion, seizures, or trouble staying awake.
8. **Document the issue if you suspect a component failure.** Take clear photos of the axle, the frame slots, and any visible wear or broken parts. Note the brand and model of the bike and the wheel components.
9. **Report potential safety defects to the appropriate authorities.** You can check for existing recalls or report a dangerous product to the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) at SaferProducts.gov or by calling the CPSC Hotline at 800-638-2772.

## What can wait
- Determining the technical cause of the looseness (e.g., worn bearings versus a bent axle) does not need to happen on the side of the road.
- Deciding whether to buy a new bike or upgrade to different wheel standards can wait until a professional inspection is completed.
- Contacting the manufacturer or retailer for a warranty claim can be done once you are in a stable environment.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sudden sense of instability or "drifting" when a wheel is loose. This sensation is a valid signal to stop. Choosing to walk a bike rather than risk a wheel separation is a standard safety protocol used by experienced cyclists.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for a loose wheel. It does not replace a comprehensive mechanical safety check. A professional mechanic should inspect the bike if the wheel has come loose unexpectedly or if there is visible damage to the frame or axle.

## Important note
This information is for immediate safety stabilization only and is not professional mechanical or medical advice. Mechanical failures can lead to serious injury. If you cannot verify that the wheel is safely secured, do not attempt to ride the bicycle.
