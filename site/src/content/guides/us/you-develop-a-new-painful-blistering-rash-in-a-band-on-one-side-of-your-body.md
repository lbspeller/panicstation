# What to do if… you develop a new painful blistering rash in a band on one side of your body (USA)

## Short answer
Seek **same-day medical evaluation** from a primary care provider or urgent care to start antiviral treatment, which is most effective within 72 hours of the first blister. If the rash is on your face or near your eye, seek **emergency care** immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not **wait for the rash to spread or "clear up"** before seeking a professional diagnosis; delay increases the risk of long-term nerve pain.
- Do not apply **unprescribed steroid creams, antibiotic ointments, or adhesive bandages** directly onto open blisters unless instructed by a provider.
- Do not **scratch, pick, or intentionally pop** the blisters, as this can lead to secondary bacterial infections or scarring.
- Do not **have skin-to-skin contact** with anyone who is pregnant, newborn, or immunocompromised until all blisters have crusted over.
- Do not **share towels, washcloths, or clothing** that has touched the uncovered rash area.

## What to do now
1. **Contact a medical provider immediately and specify "possible shingles."**  
   **Call your doctor or go to an urgent care center today.** Antiviral medications (like valacyclovir or acyclovir) significantly reduce the duration of the virus and the risk of postherpetic neuralgia (chronic pain) if started early. Even if more than 72 hours have passed, you should still seek care if new blisters are appearing.
2. **Prioritize face or eye involvement as a medical emergency.**  
   **Go to the emergency room if you have blisters on your forehead, nose, or near your eye.** This may indicate Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus, which can cause permanent vision loss. Seek immediate help if you experience:
   - Redness or pain in the eye itself.
   - Sensitivity to light or blurred vision.
   - Blisters on the tip or side of the nose (Hutchinson’s sign).
3. **Cover the affected area with loose, non-stick dressings.**  
   **Keep the rash clean and dry to prevent spreading the virus to others.** Use a sterile, non-adherent dressing or wear loose-fitting natural fabrics (like cotton) to protect the blisters from friction.
4. **Practice strict hand hygiene after touching the rash area.**  
   **Wash your hands with soap and water for at least 20 seconds** every time you touch the rash or change a dressing. The virus is primarily spread through direct contact with the fluid inside the blisters.
5. **Document the rash progression for your appointment.**  
   **Take clear photos of the rash and note the exact time the pain and the blisters began.** This helps the provider determine the best treatment window and track whether the rash is "disseminated" (appearing outside the initial band).
6. **Use safe, over-the-counter pain management while waiting for care.**  
   **Take acetaminophen (Tylenol) as directed on the label** if it is safe for you to do so. You may also apply a cool, damp (not soaking) compress to the area for 15-20 minutes at a time to soothe burning sensations.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the "source" of the infection; shingles is a reactivation of a previous virus (chickenpox) already in your body.
- You do not need to deep-clean your home; standard laundry and surface cleaning are sufficient once the rash is covered.
- You do not need to determine if you are "contagious" enough to stay home until after your medical assessment; your provider will give you specific work/school return guidelines.

## Important reassurance
While the pain and appearance of a band-like blistering rash can be distressing, it is a very common and well-understood medical event. Seeking prompt care is the most effective way to manage the symptoms and ensure a straightforward recovery.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the first few hours of a suspected shingles outbreak. Long-term pain management and specific medication dosages must be managed by a licensed medical professional.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and does not constitute a medical diagnosis. A new, painful, one-sided rash requires professional assessment to rule out other conditions. If you experience high fever, confusion, or a stiff neck, seek emergency care immediately.
