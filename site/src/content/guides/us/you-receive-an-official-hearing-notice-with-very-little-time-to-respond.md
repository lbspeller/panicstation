# What to do if… you receive an official hearing notice with very little time to respond (USA)

## Short answer
Act today: contact the hearing office or agency listed on the notice, ask the correct way to request more time or a new hearing date, and send a short written request by the required method. Keep preparing as if the hearing is still happening unless you receive clear confirmation that the date or deadline has changed.

## Do not do these things
- Do not ignore the notice or assume the hearing office will know you need more time.
- Do not rely on voicemail alone; use the filing method on the notice, such as a portal, form, email, fax, or mail, and keep proof.
- Do not miss the hearing just because you asked for more time, unless you have clear confirmation that the hearing was moved or cancelled.
- Do not send original documents unless the notice says originals are required; send copies and keep the originals available.
- Do not write a long emotional explanation if time is short; give the case number, the hearing date, the date you received the notice, the problem, and exactly what you are asking for.
- Do not assume rules from another agency, state, or website apply to your notice.

## What to do now
1. **Write down the essentials from the notice.**  
   Copy the hearing date and time, time zone, appearance method, case number, response deadline, hearing office contact details, filing method, and any words the notice uses for postponement, continuance, rescheduling, extension, or response.

2. **Call the hearing office, then send the written request the same day.**  
   Ask: “What is the correct way to request more time or a new hearing date in this case?” Then file by the required channel. Use the notice’s wording if possible. Include your case number, scheduled hearing date, when you received the notice, why the short time makes it hard to respond or prepare, and what you are asking for.

3. **Ask whether anyone else must receive a copy.**  
   Some systems require you to send the request to the agency, opposing party, or another listed person, and may require proof that you did so. Ask the hearing office what is required for this specific case, then follow that instruction.

4. **Ask for the hearing packet or case file if you do not have it.**  
   Use the name the office uses, such as record, exhibits, case file, or hearing packet. Ask how to get it quickly, whether it can be sent through a portal or email, and whether the hearing office will confirm what was sent.

5. **File a short holding response if something is due before you can prepare fully.**  
   Keep it simple: state that you intend to participate, that you are asking for more time or a new hearing date, and that you need time to review the notice, hearing packet, or missing documents. Do not try to argue every point today.

6. **Request interpreter or disability communication support now if you need it.**  
   Ask who handles accommodations for this hearing. Be specific about what you need, such as an interpreter, captions, real-time transcription, accessible documents, a communication aid, or a reliable way to appear remotely if the notice allows it. Ask for written confirmation and a day-of contact if the support fails.

7. **Make a proof folder.**  
   Save the notice, emails, portal receipts, fax confirmations, mailing receipts, call notes, names of people you spoke to, and a short timeline showing when you received the notice and what you filed.

8. **Prepare a minimal day-of packet in case the hearing still goes forward.**  
   Include the notice, case number, your written request for more time, proof you filed it, a one-page summary of what you need the decision-maker to know, and the few documents that matter most.

## What can wait
- You do not need to research every law or rule today.
- You do not need to write a perfect statement before asking for more time.
- You do not need to decide today whether to appeal a future decision.
- You do not need to gather every possible document before preserving your place in the process.
- You do not need to explain your whole life story; the immediate task is to avoid a no-show and create a clear record that you acted promptly.

## Important reassurance
A short-notice hearing can feel overwhelming, but the next steps are narrow. A prompt written request, proof that you filed it correctly, and a basic backup plan for the hearing can reduce the risk of the case moving forward without your side being heard.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Administrative hearing rules differ by agency and by state or federal system, and later decisions may need help from a lawyer, legal aid office, qualified representative, benefits advocate, union representative, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, medical, therapeutic, or professional advice. If the notice could affect benefits, housing, a job license, immigration status, custody, safety, or liberty, seek urgent specialist help if you can and tell the hearing office that you are trying to get help.
