# What to do if… your boot or trunk will not close and you need to travel with luggage (USA)

## Short answer
Do not drive until all cargo is secured, your license plate and rear lights are fully visible, and you have confirmed your load does not exceed federal overhang limits. If you cannot secure the load or the trunk lid safely, you must rearrange items, ship the luggage, or use a larger vehicle.

## Do not do these things
- Do not drive with loose items that could shift or become projectiles during a sudden stop.
- Do not use bungee cords or twine as your primary securement for heavy luggage.
- Do not block your rear-view mirror, tail lights, brake lights, or license plate.
- Do not drive if the trunk lid or hatch blocks your view of traffic behind you.
- Do not allow cargo to drag on the road or hang near the exhaust pipe.
- Do not ignore the smell of exhaust or symptoms like headache and dizziness while driving.

## What to do now
1. **Pull into a safe location and activate your hazard lights.** Move as far off the roadway as possible, ideally into a parking lot or rest area.
   - Be aware that as of 2026, many states (including California) require passing drivers to move over or slow down for any stopped vehicle; however, you must still prioritize getting away from active traffic.
2. **Inspect the latch and strike plate for obstructions.**
   - Check for straps, fabric, or suitcase corners caught in the mechanism.
   - Use a key or screwdriver to see if the latch is "clicked" into the closed position while the trunk is open; if so, pull the interior release or use the fob to reset it.
3. **Rearrange cargo to prioritize a fully closed trunk.**
   - Move smaller, heavier items into the passenger footwells or under seats.
   - Ensure no items are stacked high enough to block your rear visibility or the side-view mirror paths.
4. **Secure any luggage placed in the passenger cabin.**
   - **Use seat belts to anchor suitcases by threading the belt through the handles and clicking the buckle.**
   - Place the heaviest items on the floor behind the front seats to prevent them from sliding forward during braking.
5. **Verify federal and state overhang regulations if the trunk must remain partially open.**
   - **The US DOT (49 CFR 393.87) generally prohibits loads extending more than 4 feet beyond the rear of the vehicle without specific markings.**
   - If the load extends more than 4 feet, you must attach a red or orange fluorescent flag (at least 18 inches square) to the end of the load.
6. **Use ratchet straps or heavy-duty tie-downs for an open hatch.**
   - **Anchor the strap to a solid metal point inside the trunk and the trunk lid itself, not the latch mechanism.**
   - Tighten the straps until the trunk lid is pulled firmly against the cargo so it cannot bounce.
   - Conduct a "shake test" by pulling on the luggage; if it moves independently of the car, it is not secure.
7. **Prevent exhaust gas from entering the cabin.**
   - **Open your front windows slightly to create positive pressure and prevent the "station wagon effect" where exhaust is sucked into the open rear hatch.**
   - Set your climate control to "Fresh Air" mode rather than "Recirculate."
8. **Switch to an alternative plan if the load is not legal or safe.**
   - Ship excess luggage via a courier service (UPS, FedEx, or USPS).
   - Contact roadside assistance to see if a mobile technician can adjust a misaligned striker or latch.

## What can wait
- You do not need to permanently repair the trunk latch or striker until you reach your destination.
- You do not need to diagnose electrical sensor failures or "trunk open" dashboard warnings immediately.
- You do not need to worry about long-term suspension wear from a temporary heavy load.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel rushed when luggage issues happen right before a trip, but taking ten minutes to properly secure a load prevents a much longer delay from a police stop or a highway accident. Most trunk issues are simple mechanical misalignments that can be managed with basic tie-downs or cargo reorganization.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for transporting luggage when a trunk will not close. It does not cover commercial vehicle regulations for loads over 10,000 lbs or permanent mechanical repair of automotive latches.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or mechanical advice. Road safety laws and load securement requirements vary by state and are subject to enforcement by local authorities. If you cannot ensure the load is immobilized and your lights are visible, do not operate the vehicle on public roads.
