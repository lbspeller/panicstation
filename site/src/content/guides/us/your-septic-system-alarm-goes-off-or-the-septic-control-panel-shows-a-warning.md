# What to do if… your septic system alarm goes off or the septic control panel shows a warning (USA)

## Short answer
Silence the alarm using the "Silence" or "Mute" button and **immediately stop all non-essential water use** to prevent an indoor sewage backup.

## Do not do these things
- Don't continue using high-volume appliances (laundry, dishwasher, or long showers).
- Don't open the septic tank covers or look inside the pump chamber (risk of lethal hydrogen sulfide gas, falls, and drowning).
- Don't attempt to adjust float switches or wiring inside the tank yourself.
- Don't ignore a red light even if the beeping has stopped.
- Don't enter a room with standing sewage without professional protective gear.
- Don't force a circuit breaker to stay on if it immediately trips again.

## What to do now
1. **Silence the audible alarm at the control panel.** Locate the "Silence," "Mute," or "Reset" button on the septic control box (usually mounted on the house exterior or in the garage).
   - The red light will typically stay illuminated until the water level drops or the fault is cleared.
2. **Implement a total water-use moratorium.** Instruct all occupants to stop flushing toilets except when absolutely necessary and to avoid all drains until the system is stabilized.
3. **Identify the specific warning indicator on the panel.** Observe which light or code is active to relay this to a technician.
   - **Red Light:** High water level in the pump tank (most urgent).
   - **Yellow / Amber Light:** Aerator failure or air pump issue (common in aerobic systems).
   - **Digital Code:** Note any numbers or letters (e.g., "E1," "H20").
4. **Verify power to the septic pump system.** Check your home's main electrical panel for a tripped breaker labeled "Septic," "Pump," or "Lift Station."
   - **If a breaker is tripped:** Reset it exactly once.
   - **If it trips again immediately:** Leave it off and wait for a professional; this indicates a short circuit or pump motor failure.
5. **Check the area around the septic tank and drainfield.** Look for signs of "surfacing" sewage, such as standing water, unusually lush green grass, or strong odors, without stepping directly into wet areas.
6. **Contact a licensed septic service provider.** Provide them with the specific light color, any error codes, and whether you have experienced heavy rain or a recent power outage.
7. **Contact your local Health Department if sewage is visible on the ground or indoors.** They provide regulatory guidance on public health risks and may require specific reporting for system failures.
8. **Secure professional biohazard remediation for indoor backups.** Do not attempt to clean raw sewage with standard household tools; specialized equipment is required to manage pathogens.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the mechanical cause (e.g., failed capacitor, blocked intake, or broken float) right now.
- You do not need to decide on long-term system replacements or drainfield expansions today.
- You do not need to pump the tank immediately unless a technician confirms it is necessary to reach the components for repair.

## Important reassurance
Modern septic alarms are designed as early-warning systems. In most cases, the tank has a "reserve capacity" that allows for a small amount of time to seek help before an actual overflow occurs, provided you stop adding water to the system immediately.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization only. Septic systems involve biological hazards, toxic gases, and high-voltage electricity. Final diagnosis and repair must be performed by a licensed professional.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and is not a substitute for professional engineering or plumbing advice. Hydrogen sulfide gas in septic tanks can cause sudden unconsciousness or death; never lean into or enter a septic tank.
