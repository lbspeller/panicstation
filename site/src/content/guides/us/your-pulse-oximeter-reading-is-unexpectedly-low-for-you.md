# What to do if… your pulse oximeter reading is unexpectedly low for you (USA)

## Short answer
Treat severe symptoms as a priority over the device number; if you have trouble breathing, chest pain, or confusion, call 911 immediately regardless of the reading.

## Do not do these things
- Do not wait for a "perfect" reading if you are struggling to breathe or feeling faint.
- Do not check the device repeatedly every few seconds; movement and anxiety can cause fluctuating, inaccurate numbers.
- Do not drive yourself to a hospital if you are confused, extremely short of breath, or feel like you may lose consciousness.
- Do not assume a reading of 94% or higher means you are safe if you have dark skin and are experiencing severe symptoms.
- Do not use a smartphone app that claims to measure oxygen through the camera lens for medical decisions.

## What to do now
1. **Assess for emergency "red flag" symptoms that require an immediate 911 call.**  
   Call **911** now if you experience:
   - Severe or rapidly worsening shortness of breath.
   - Bluish, gray, or pale tint to the lips, face, or nail beds.
   - Persistent chest pain or pressure.
   - New confusion or inability to stay awake.
   - Inability to speak in full sentences while resting.

2. **Perform one stabilized recheck to rule out common technical errors.**  
   - Sit upright and remain still for 5 minutes before taking the reading.
   - Ensure your hand is warm; rub your hands together if your fingers are cold.
   - Remove any nail polish or artificial nails from the finger being used.
   - Keep your hand resting still at chest level (heart level) during the measurement.

3. **Verify the device signal by manually checking your pulse.**  
   - Take your pulse manually at the wrist for 60 seconds while the device is on.
   - Compare your manual heart rate to the pulse rate displayed on the oximeter.
   - If the numbers do not match, the device may not have a reliable signal and the oxygen reading should be considered untrustworthy.

4. **Apply standard safety thresholds if you do not have a clinician-provided target.**  
   - If the reading is **89% or lower** and stays there after a stabilized recheck, seek emergency care immediately (call **911** or go to an ER).
   - If the reading is **90% to 94%** and is lower than your usual baseline, contact a healthcare provider or nurse line immediately for urgent advice.

5. **Follow your specific medical action plan if you have a chronic condition.**  
   - If you have COPD, asthma, or heart failure, check your provided "Action Plan" for your specific target oxygen saturation (SpO2) ranges.
   - Use the specific emergency numbers or clinician contacts listed in your personal medical records if your reading falls below your prescribed "red zone."

6. **Prepare essential information for the emergency operator or clinician.**  
   Write down or be ready to state:
   - Your current oxygen reading and your "normal" baseline reading.
   - Your current pulse rate as shown on the device.
   - Exactly when your symptoms (e.g., shortness of breath) started.
   - A list of any chronic lung or heart conditions you have.
   - Whether you are currently using supplemental oxygen or inhalers.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the underlying cause of the low reading (e.g., infection vs. device error) right now.
- You do not need to research long-term oxygen therapy or pulmonary rehabilitation today.
- You do not need to buy a different oximeter model while in a crisis state.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of urgency or alarm when a medical device shows an unexpected result. Pulse oximeters are sensitive to many external factors like temperature, movement, and skin circulation. Taking a moment to sit still and perform one careful recheck is a standard, safe response to an unexpected number.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an acute drop in oxygen readings. It does not replace a clinical diagnosis or a long-term medical management plan for chronic respiratory illness.

## Important note
This information is for stabilization purposes and is not medical advice. Pulse oximeters can overestimate oxygen levels in individuals with darker skin tones (occult hypoxemia); always prioritize how you feel over the number on the screen. If you are in distress, seek professional medical help immediately.
