# What to do if… your heating is stuck on and the indoor temperature is rising beyond what feels safe (USA)

## Short answer
Cut power to the heating system immediately by flipping the furnace service switch or the dedicated circuit breaker. Move all occupants to a cooler environment and monitor for signs of heat-related illness or carbon monoxide exposure.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to troubleshoot the thermostat or internal wiring while the temperature is still rising.
- Do not ignore the smell of gas, a hissing sound, or a carbon monoxide (CO) alarm.
- Do not leave children, elderly individuals, or pets in the overheating rooms.
- Do not use a standard telephone or flashlight in the area if you suspect a gas leak.
- Do not attempt to open the furnace cabinet or touch internal components if you are not a certified technician.

## What to do now
1. **Move all people and pets to the coolest available part of the property or outside if necessary.**
   - Prioritize the lowest floor or rooms on the shaded side of the building.
   - Open windows only if the outdoor air is significantly cooler than the indoor air.
2. **Attempt to shut the system off at the thermostat by switching the mode to OFF.**
   - Move the system switch to "OFF" rather than simply lowering the temperature.
   - If the fan is set to "ON," switch it to "AUTO" to see if the blower stops.
3. **Locate and flip the furnace service switch or the HVAC circuit breaker if the heat continues to run.**
   - Look for a switch that resembles a standard light switch on or near the furnace/air handler unit.
   - If no switch is visible, go to the main electrical panel and turn off the breaker labeled "Furnace," "Air Handler," or "HVAC."
4. **Evacuate the building immediately if a carbon monoxide (CO) alarm sounds or if anyone feels dizzy, nauseated, or has a headache.**
   - Move to fresh air outdoors.
   - Call 911 from a mobile phone once you are outside.
5. **Treat suspected heat stroke as a life-threatening medical emergency by calling 911 immediately.**
   - Look for confusion, slurred speech, seizures, or loss of consciousness.
   - While waiting for help, move the person to a cool area and apply cold, wet cloths to the neck, armpits, and groin.
6. **Report a suspected gas leak by exiting the building and calling the utility company or 911 from a safe distance.**
   - Do not operate light switches, appliances, or phones inside the building if you smell rotten eggs.
   - Do not re-enter the property until the fire department or gas utility confirms it is safe.
7. **Contact a certified HVAC professional or your property manager's emergency line once the system is powered down.**
   - Clearly state that the heating system was "stuck on" and that you have manually disconnected the power.
   - Request an immediate inspection of the relay switches and thermostat.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine if the failure was caused by a stuck contactor, a faulty thermostat, or a control board error right now.
- You do not need to replace thermostat batteries until the main system power is safely disconnected.
- You do not need to decide on permanent equipment repairs or replacement while the house is still cooling down.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sense of urgency when the indoor temperature rises rapidly. Most residential heating systems can be safely deactivated by cutting the power at the service switch or breaker, which stops the heat production immediately.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an overheating home. It does not cover technical repairs or long-term HVAC maintenance. Suspected gas leaks and CO exposure require professional emergency intervention.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and is not a substitute for professional medical or technical advice. Always prioritize evacuation in the event of a CO alarm, gas odor, or severe physical symptoms.
