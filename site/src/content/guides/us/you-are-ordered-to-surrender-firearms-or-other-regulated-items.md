# What to do if… you are ordered to surrender firearms or other regulated items (USA)

## Short answer
Pause in a safe place and follow the written order and any instructions from an officer who is present. Unless an officer is directing you, do not handle, move, or transport firearms or other covered items until you have read the instructions and contacted the relevant agency for its safe surrender procedure.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hide, move, transfer, sell, store, or give anyone access to covered items except as the order, a serving officer, or the receiving agency instructs.
- Do not carry or bring a firearm into a police station, courthouse, or other building unless the receiving agency has given you specific instructions.
- Do not assume that a dealer, family member, friend, or other third party can receive the items unless the order and local procedure allow it.
- Do not miss a deadline because you plan to challenge the order. Follow the order unless a court changes or pauses it in writing.
- Do not rely only on a phone conversation. Keep copies of the order, receipts, filing confirmations, and written instructions.

## What to do now
1. **Reach a safe pause.** If anyone is in immediate danger, call 911. Otherwise, step away from the firearms or other covered items and avoid handling them while you read the paperwork.
2. **Read the order and identify the immediate requirements.** Look for the exact items covered, the surrender deadline, the permitted surrender route, any agency contact details, any hearing date, and any requirement to file proof.
3. **Obtain safe surrender instructions.** Follow instructions from any officer who is present. Otherwise, use the contact information in the order or the relevant agency’s official website to ask where to go, whether an appointment or pickup is required, and what you should bring. Do not move anything until you have instructions.
4. **Ask the issuing court about filing procedure if the paperwork is unclear.** Court staff may be able to explain where and how to file documents. Ask what proof is required, who must file it, and the deadline. For legal interpretation, contact a lawyer or legal-aid service.
5. **Use only a permitted surrender route.** Follow the route stated in the order or confirmed by the receiving agency. Do not improvise a transfer or storage arrangement.
6. **Document what you can without handling the items.** Use existing records or memory to note the items you believe are covered. Do not inspect, unpack, or move anything unless the receiving agency instructs you to do so.
7. **Get written proof and keep copies.** Ask what receipt or confirmation will be provided, whether it lists the surrendered items, and whether you or the receiving agency must file anything with the court.
8. **If you cannot fully comply, say so promptly and factually.** Contact the issuing court and the relevant agency. Explain the obstacle, such as an item not being in your possession or an unclear instruction, and ask what procedure to follow. Do not improvise.

## What can wait
- You do not need to argue your case during the surrender process.
- You do not need to decide long-term storage, return, or disposal questions right now.
- You do not need to decide your long-term challenge strategy right now. Note every hearing, response, and filing deadline stated in the order, and seek legal help promptly if any deadline is close.

## Important reassurance
A surrender order can feel sudden and overwhelming. The immediate task is limited: pause, follow the written order, obtain safe instructions, keep proof, and protect any stated deadlines.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Procedures vary by state, court, order type, and local agency. Later decisions may need help from a lawyer or legal-aid service.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. The exact court order and local procedure control what you must do.
