# What to do if… your pulse oximeter reading is unexpectedly low (USA)

## Short answer
Do one careful recheck, and act on how you feel as well as the number. If the reading stays low or you have severe breathing trouble, chest pain or pressure, pale, gray, or blue lips, skin, face, or nail beds, confusion, fainting, or extreme drowsiness, call 911 now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t ignore severe symptoms because you hope the device is wrong.
- Don’t keep checking every few seconds; movement and panic-checking can make readings less useful.
- Don’t drive yourself to the ER if you feel faint, confused, extremely short of breath, or rapidly worse.
- Don’t assume one “normal” number fits everyone; some people with chronic lung disease or home oxygen have clinician-set targets.
- Don’t make urgent decisions based only on a phone app or wellness device that estimates oxygen.

## What to do now
1. **Check for emergency symptoms first.**  
   Call **911** now if you have severe or rapidly worsening shortness of breath, chest pain or pressure, pale, gray, or blue lips, skin, face, or nail beds, new confusion, fainting, extreme drowsiness, or you cannot speak short sentences while resting.

2. **Do one careful recheck.**  
   Sit upright. Rest quietly for a few minutes. Warm your hands. Use a finger without nail polish or an artificial nail if you can. Keep your hand still and wait until the reading and pulse look stable.

3. **Use your personal target if you have one.**  
   If a clinician has told you what oxygen saturation to maintain, follow that plan and call the number listed in it if you are below your target or unsure.

4. **Use these numbers as a safety backstop if you do not have a personal target.**  
   If your SpO₂ is **88% or lower**, seek emergency medical care now; call **911** if you feel unwell or cannot safely get help another way. If your SpO₂ is **92% or lower** after a careful recheck, contact a healthcare provider right away for same-day advice; if you cannot reach one quickly or you are getting worse, use urgent care or the ER.

5. **Escalate if the pattern is worrying.**  
   If the number is unexpectedly low for you, keeps drifting downward, or comes with new or worse shortness of breath, chest tightness, wheezing, fever, unusual weakness, dizziness, or fast heart rate, call your clinician or nurse line for urgent guidance. If you worsen while trying to get help, call **911**.

6. **Have the key facts ready.**  
   Write down your best stable SpO₂, the pulse shown on the device, your symptoms and when they started, whether the number is dropping, your usual reading if you know it, any lung or heart conditions, and whether you use inhalers or home oxygen.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the cause right now.
- You do not need to keep taking readings once you have one careful recheck and a next step.
- You do not need to decide today about long-term monitoring, tests, equipment, or treatment changes unless a clinician tells you to.

## Important reassurance
Home pulse oximeters can give misleading readings because of cold hands, movement, nail polish, artificial nails, poor circulation, device limits, and skin-pigment accuracy issues. A false low is possible, but a persistent low reading or symptoms deserve prompt attention.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next few minutes to hour. Later decisions may need a clinician’s assessment, especially if you have COPD, asthma, heart disease, sleep apnea, high-altitude exposure, recent infection, or home oxygen.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, therapeutic, or other professional advice, and it is not a diagnosis. If your breathing is worsening, symptoms feel severe, or the reading stays low after a careful recheck, seek urgent medical care.
