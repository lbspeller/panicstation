# What to do if… your arm becomes swollen or heavy after an IV, line, or injection (USA)

## Short answer
New swelling or heaviness in one arm after an IV, line, or injection needs prompt same-day clinical assessment. Call 911 now for trouble breathing, chest pain, coughing up blood, severe lightheadedness, fainting, or passing out.

## Do not do these things
- Do not massage the swollen area unless the treating team specifically tells you to.
- Do not keep using an IV or line that may be causing swelling.
- Do not remove, reposition, or re-tape a line yourself.
- Do not leave rings, a watch, or other tight items on the swelling arm or hand.
- Do not apply heat or cold unless the treating team advises it.
- Do not delay assessment because the swelling might be bruising.

## What to do now
1. Call 911 now if you have trouble breathing, chest pain, coughing up blood, severe lightheadedness, fainting, or passing out.
2. Go to the ER now if the arm or hand is cold, pale, blue, very numb, weak, severely painful, rapidly worsening, blistering, or developing skin breakdown. Call 911 if you cannot get there safely or the symptoms are severe or rapidly worsening.
3. If an IV or line is still present, pause its use using your normal instructions and contact clinical staff immediately. Use the nurse call button if you are in a clinic or hospital. If you are at home, call the team managing the line. Do not remove or manipulate the line yourself.
4. Arrange same-day clinical assessment for new one-sided swelling or heaviness even if you do not have emergency signs. Contact the clinician, infusion center, or office involved. Use the ER if you cannot get prompt advice, the symptoms are worsening, or you were told the medication could injure tissue if it leaked.
5. Remove rings, a watch, and other tight items from the affected arm or hand. Elevate the arm comfortably while arranging care.
6. Tell the clinician what was given, if you know, and whether you have burning, tightness, leaking fluid, redness, warmth, blistering, numbness, weakness, fever, chills, or worsening pain.

## What can wait
- You do not need to work out whether this is a clot, infection, irritation, or a leak from the IV before seeking assessment.
- You do not need to choose a home treatment before speaking with a clinician.
- Paperwork, billing, and record requests can wait until after you are safe.

## Important reassurance
A swollen arm after an IV, line, or injection can feel alarming. Getting prompt assessment is a practical way to reduce risk and avoid guessing at home.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need help from a clinician who can assess the arm and identify what was given through the IV, line, or injection.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical advice, a diagnosis, or a substitute for professional care. Call 911 for emergency warning signs.
