# What to do if… your train, subway, or tram stops unexpectedly and information is unclear about what happens next (USA)

## Short answer
Stay on the vehicle and wait for official instructions. Only use the emergency intercom if there is an immediate threat to life, fire, or a medical emergency.

## Do not do these things
- Do not exit the vehicle onto the tracks or into tunnels without direct orders from transit staff or emergency responders.
- Do not attempt to force, pry, or kick the doors open.
- Do not use the emergency brake or passenger alarm pull for simple information requests or general delays.
- Do not touch the tracks or the "third rail" even if the train is stationary and lights are off.
- Do not move between cars or open end-doors unless specifically told to do so by a crew member.

## What to do now
1. **Assess the immediate environment for physical hazards.**  
   Look for visible smoke, fire, or physical assaults occurring in the car. If these are absent, the situation is likely a technical delay; stay seated or hold onto a rail.
2. **Locate and use the onboard communication system if help is needed.**  
   Identify the difference between the "Passenger Alarm" (which may stop the train or alert the driver) and the "Intercom" (which allows two-way voice communication).
   - Use the intercom to report medical issues or safety threats.
   - Wait for the operator to respond; there may be a delay if they are speaking with central control.
3. **Attempt to contact emergency services only if the intercom fails.**  
   **Call 911** if there is an immediate threat to life and the train crew cannot be reached. 
   - Note that cellular and GPS signals are frequently unavailable in subway tunnels.
   - Use the "text to 911" feature if voice calls fail to connect.
4. **Identify your exact location and vehicle details.**  
   Find the car or unit number (usually found near the ends of the car or above the doors). Note the line name, the direction of travel, and the name of the last station the vehicle departed.
5. **Manage the environment inside the car to reduce distress.**  
   If the car is hot or crowded, encourage others to keep the center of the car clear. Sit on the floor if you feel dizzy or faint, and keep your belongings close to your body to prevent tripping hazards.
6. **Follow evacuation protocols exactly if they are announced.**  
   Wait for the crew to confirm that the power to the tracks (electrification) has been cut. 
   - Step down only at designated doors as directed.
   - Walk only in the direction specified by responders.
   - Leave heavy luggage behind if instructed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine your alternate route or transfer points immediately.
- You do not need to contact the transit agency for a refund or complaint while still on the vehicle.
- You do not need to check social media for news; the crew is the most accurate source of local safety information.
- You do not need to explain the delay to others in detail; a single "delayed on train, safe" message is sufficient.

## Important reassurance
Trains and subways frequently stop due to "spacing" (waiting for a train ahead) or minor signal glitches. A lack of immediate information often means the operator is currently receiving orders from a central dispatcher. The vehicle is generally the safest place to be during a power outage or signal failure.

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for passengers on a stopped transit vehicle. It does not cover long-term service disruptions, legal claims against transit authorities, or transit employee procedures.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not replace the instructions of transit staff or emergency personnel on-site. Transit systems in the USA vary in equipment; always prioritize the specific safety signage and verbal commands provided on your vehicle. If you are in a tunnel, assume all tracks are energized and dangerous until a uniformed official confirms otherwise.
