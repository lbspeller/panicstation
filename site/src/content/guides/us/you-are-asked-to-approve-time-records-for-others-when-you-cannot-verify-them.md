# What to do if… you are asked to approve time records for others when you cannot verify them (USA)

## Short answer
Request a written scope for the documentation, deliver the work using only official company tools, and maintain a private, non-confidential log of your deliveries without removing any proprietary data.

## Do not do these things
- Do not delete, hide, or encrypt files, code, tickets, or work history to prevent "theft" of your knowledge.
- Do not move company data, client lists, or internal manuals to personal email or cloud accounts.
- Do not refuse the request verbally or in writing; this can be cited as insubordination.
- Do not sign a "General Release" or new "Restrictive Covenant" on the spot if presented with one.
- Do not share trade secrets or proprietary business strategies with coworkers when discussing the situation.
- Do not use company devices or networks to research your legal rights or look for new jobs.

## What to do now
1. **Request the documentation requirements in writing.**  
   **Ask for a specific list of processes, the intended audience, and the deadline via email.** 
   - If the request was verbal, send a follow-up email: "To confirm our conversation, you would like me to document [Process A] and [Process B] by [Date] for the purpose of [stated reason]."
2. **Obtain a prioritized list of tasks.**  
   **Ask your manager to rank which workflows are most urgent to document.** 
   - This prevents a "dump everything you know" scenario and creates a record of what management considers critical.
3. **Keep a private, dated log of your output.**  
   **Record the date, the name of the file you created, and where it was saved on company servers.** 
   - Do not copy the content of the notes into your personal log. 
   - Keep this log on a personal device or in a physical notebook at home.
4. **Use company-approved platforms for all documentation.**  
   **Draft and save all notes only on the official company wiki, shared drive, or ticketing system.** 
   - This demonstrates compliance and protects you from accusations of data mishandling or IP theft.
5. **Submit a formal inquiry regarding the organizational change.**  
   **Send a neutral email asking if the documentation is for business continuity or if a role restructure is planned.** 
   - "I am working on the requested process notes. Is this for internal cross-training, or is there an upcoming organizational change I should be prepared for?"
6. **Verify your status under the NLRA.**  
   **Confirm if your role is covered by the National Labor Relations Act for 'protected concerted activity'.** 
   - Most private-sector non-supervisory employees have the right to discuss working conditions and potential layoffs with coworkers. 
   - Supervisors and certain government employees have different or fewer protections.
7. **Monitor for WARN Act triggers.**  
   **Note if the employer has 100 or more employees and if large groups are receiving similar requests.** 
   - The federal WARN Act generally requires 60 days' notice for mass layoffs (50+ people at one site). 
   - States like New York, California, and New Jersey have "mini-WARN" laws with lower thresholds or 90-day notice requirements.
8. **Secure a copy of your current employment agreement.**  
   **Locate your original offer letter or signed handbook acknowledgment from your own physical files.** 
   - Review sections on "Work for Hire," "Confidentiality," and "Termination" to understand what you have already agreed to.

## What can wait
- You do not need to confront leadership with accusations of outsourcing.
- You do not need to resign or stop performing your daily duties.
- You do not need to share your suspicions with clients or vendors.
- You do not need to decide on a severance package until a written offer is actually made.

## Important reassurance
Requests for documentation are often legitimate business continuity steps for audits, software migrations, or simple cross-training. Even if outsourcing is the goal, your most effective protection is a record of professional compliance and a clear boundary between your personal notes and company property. Feeling anxious or defensive in this situation is a standard response to professional uncertainty.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling a documentation request under suspicion of role elimination. It does not cover the specific legalities of severance negotiations, unemployment insurance applications, or trade secret litigation.

## Important note
This information is for general educational purposes and is not legal advice. Employment laws vary significantly by state and are subject to change. The NLRB has recently increased scrutiny on severance "gag clauses," but individual enforcement depends on your specific contract and jurisdiction. If you are threatened with immediate termination or asked to sign a waiver of rights, consult a licensed employment attorney in your state.
