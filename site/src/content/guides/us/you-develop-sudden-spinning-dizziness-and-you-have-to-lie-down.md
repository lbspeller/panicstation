# What to do if… you develop sudden spinning dizziness and you have to lie down (USA)

## Short answer
Lie down immediately with your head propped up and check for "BE FAST" warning signs: Balance loss, Eye changes, Face drooping, Arm weakness, or Speech trouble. If any are present, or if this is a first-time severe attack, call **911** immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Do not attempt to walk, use stairs, or stand without support.
- Do not drive any vehicle or operate machinery.
- Do not close your eyes tightly, as this often increases the sensation of disorientation.
- Do not perform "head maneuvers" or Epley exercises during an active, first-time emergency.
- Do not take any new medications, including anti-nausea or vestibular suppressants, until advised by a clinician.
- Do not ignore the symptoms even if they begin to fade; a Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA) requires the same emergency response as a stroke.

## What to do now
1. **Get to the floor or a low bed immediately.**
   - **Lie down with your head and shoulders propped up by pillows at a 30-degree angle.**
   - **Keep your head as still as possible and keep your eyes open, fixing your gaze on a single stationary object.**
2. **Perform the BE FAST emergency screening.**
   - **Balance:** Check for sudden loss of coordination or an inability to sit upright.
   - **Eyes:** Check for sudden double vision, blurred vision, or loss of sight in one or both eyes.
   - **Face:** Smile and check if one side of the face droops or feels numb.
   - **Arms:** Raise both arms; check if one arm drifts downward or feels weak.
   - **Speech:** Repeat a simple phrase; check for slurring, confusion, or inability to find words.
3. **If any BE FAST sign is present, call 911 immediately.**
   - **State clearly: "I am having sudden vertigo and have [specific symptom, e.g., double vision / one-sided weakness]."**
   - **Note the exact time the spinning started.**
4. **Call 911 for these additional emergency triggers:**
   - **Sudden, severe "thunderclap" headache.**
   - **Fainting, loss of consciousness, or a new seizure.**
   - **Inability to stand even when holding onto a sturdy surface.**
   - **Chest pain or a racing, irregular heartbeat.**
   - **Sudden hearing loss or loud ringing in one ear.**
5. **Check for pediatric-specific signs if the person is a child or teen.**
   - **Look for extreme sleepiness, a sudden severe headache with vomiting, or a new-onset seizure.**
   - **Call 911 immediately if these occur alongside dizziness.**
6. **Position for nausea.**
   - **If you feel the urge to vomit, roll your entire body onto your side while keeping your head aligned with your torso.**
   - **Keep a bucket or basin within reach without reaching or twisting your neck.**
7. **Notify someone else of your location.**
   - **Text or call a friend, neighbor, or family member and ask them to come to you.**
   - **Unlock your front door if you are able to do so safely before the spinning worsens.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the cause (BPPV, migraine, or inner ear infection) right now.
- You do not need to look up long-term vestibular rehabilitation exercises.
- You do not need to decide about work or future travel plans until a medical evaluation is complete.
- You do not need to worry about "testing" your balance to see if it has improved.

## Important reassurance
Sudden vertigo is an intensely physical experience that often triggers a survival-level panic response. Feeling overwhelmed, nauseated, or frightened is a normal physiological reaction to the brain receiving conflicting signals about your position in space. Needing to lie down is the safest response to prevent a fall.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and emergency screening only. It does not replace a clinical diagnosis or professional medical advice.

## Important note
Sudden dizziness can be a primary symptom of a stroke, particularly in the posterior circulation of the brain. Because these symptoms can mimic benign inner-ear issues, any sudden, severe, or new vertigo should be treated as a potential emergency until cleared by a medical professional.
