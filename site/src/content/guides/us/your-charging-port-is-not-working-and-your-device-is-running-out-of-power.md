# What to do if… your charging port is not working and your device is running out of power (USA)

## Short answer
Preserve battery first, secure any must-have access items, then use the safest alternate charging option rather than forcing the port. Stop any charging attempt that causes heat, a liquid warning, a damaged-cable warning, burning smell, corrosion, or melting.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t poke the port with metal, paperclips, pins, needles, cotton swabs, paper towels, or other objects.
- Don’t spray liquid, compressed air, cleaner, or dust remover into the port.
- Don’t use a damaged cable, damaged adapter, loose wall outlet, or unknown charger.
- Don’t use heat, rice, or repeated plug-in attempts to override a liquid or debris warning.
- Don’t take the device apart while you may need warranty or authorized service.
- Don’t erase, reset, or trade in the device while it still contains access codes, tickets, photos, messages, or account keys you may need.

## What to do now
1. **Cut battery drain immediately.**  
   Turn on Low Power Mode, Battery Saver, or the nearest equivalent. Lower brightness, close nonessential apps, turn off Bluetooth and hotspot, and use airplane mode when you do not need a live connection.

2. **Protect the access you may need before the battery dies.**  
   Open and save critical boarding passes, event tickets, maps, hotel details, ride details, emergency contacts, and payment backups. Take screenshots only for items that can safely be stored on the device, and avoid sending QR codes, account codes, or identity documents to another person unless it is clearly necessary.

3. **Secure sign-in options without making rushed account changes.**  
   If you rely on an authenticator app, check whether you already have backup codes, a second signed-in device, a recovery email, or a trusted phone number. Do not reset 2FA or change account recovery details while panicked unless you are following the account provider’s normal recovery flow.

4. **Do one safe charging check.**  
   Use a known-good wall outlet, known-good adapter, and known-good cable. Check that the cable and adapter are not damaged, then connect once and look for a charging symbol or vibration. If it looks safe but slow, leave it connected for up to 30 minutes; if it heats, smells odd, warns, or does nothing, stop.

5. **If there is a liquid or debris warning, stop forcing the port.**  
   For an iPhone liquid alert, unplug the cable and let the phone and cable dry in a dry area with airflow; wireless charging may be safer if the back of the iPhone is dry. For a Pixel-style “Unplug charger” warning, unplug the adapter from the wall first, let the phone cool for a few minutes, then remove the cable carefully. For a Pixel-style “Liquid or debris in USB port” warning, do not put anything into the port; wait until the phone says it is okay to use the USB port.

6. **If you can see debris, avoid digging.**  
   Hold the device with the port facing down and gently tap it against your hand. If debris remains, the port looks bent, burned, melted, corroded, or the cable feels unusually loose, stop and use service support.

7. **Switch to alternate power.**  
   Use wireless charging if your device supports it and the device does not heat unusually. Try a wireless power bank, wireless charging stand, battery case, or reverse wireless charging only if both devices clearly support it. Keep the goal small: enough power to complete the urgent task and arrange support.

8. **Move essentials off the dying device.**  
   Use a second device or computer to sign in where you are already authorized. Print or save tickets, copy travel details, write down urgent phone numbers, and confirm any payment or transport backup that does not require the dying phone.

9. **Document the fault for support.**  
   Take a clear photo of the port and any warning message if you still have enough battery. Note the cable, adapter, outlet, warning text, heat, visible damage, and whether wireless charging works.

10. **Use the lowest-risk service route.**  
   Start with the manufacturer, carrier, retailer, or authorized repair provider. Say plainly: “The device will not charge through the port with known-good accessories; wireless charging works/does not work; these warnings appeared.” Keep proof of purchase and warranty information, but do not argue about warranty rights while the immediate problem is power and access.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether the problem is lint, moisture, the cable, the battery, the port, or the main board.
- You do not need to choose paid repair, replacement, insurance, trade-in, or a new phone until access is protected.
- You do not need to deep-clean the port, reinstall software, factory reset, or run advanced diagnostics while power is critical.
- You do not need to settle warranty or consumer-rights questions before you have stabilized the device and saved what you need.

## Important reassurance
A failed charging port can feel urgent because it threatens access, not because you must solve every cause immediately. The safest first win is to keep the device alive long enough to protect tickets, codes, contacts, and essential information.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to reduce damage risk, preserve access, and buy time. Later decisions about repair, replacement, warranty, insurance, account recovery, or data recovery may need help from the manufacturer, carrier, retailer, repair provider, or another appropriate specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, cybersecurity, repair, or other professional advice. If the device becomes too hot to touch, smells like burning, shows smoke, melting, corrosion, swelling, or repeated liquid or debris warnings, stop charging attempts and use manufacturer or authorized service support.
