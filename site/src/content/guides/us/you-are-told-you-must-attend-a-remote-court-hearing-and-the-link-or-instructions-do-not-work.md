# What to do if… you are told you must attend a remote court hearing and the link or instructions do not work (USA)

## Short answer
Stop troubleshooting after three minutes and **contact the court immediately** by phone to notify the clerk or courtroom deputy that you are ready to appear but having technical difficulties. Request the dial-in number and Meeting ID so you can join by telephone while the video issue is resolved.

## Do not do these things
- Do not record, video, or take screenshots of the remote proceeding unless you have express written permission from the judge.
- Do not assume the court will "see" that you tried to log in; failing to call the clerk can result in a "Failure to Appear" (FTA) warrant or default judgment.
- Do not spend more than a few minutes on tech fixes (like restarting your router) without first calling the court to establish a record of your presence.
- Do not rely on email as your only contact method if the hearing has already started; phone calls are the standard for immediate crisis notification.
- Do not share the link, Meeting ID, or passcode with unauthorized individuals, as this may be a security violation.

## What to do now
1. **Check your "Junk" or "Spam" email folders immediately for the hearing invite.**
   - Search for terms like "Zoom," "Webex," "Teams," "ZoomGov," or the name of the court.
   - Confirm the email address you are checking is the exact one provided to the court or your attorney.

2. **Verify the "Waiting Room" status.**
   - If your screen says "Please wait, the meeting host will let you in soon," the link is working.
   - Stay on that screen; the judge or clerk may be finishing a previous case and will admit you when ready.

3. **Perform one rapid tech workaround (3 minutes maximum).**
   - Switch from a computer to a smartphone (using the mobile app or browser).
   - Turn off your VPN or switch from Wi-Fi to mobile data.
   - Manually type the Meeting ID and Passcode instead of clicking the link.

4. **Call the primary contact number on your hearing notice or court order.**
   - Ask to speak with the **Clerk of Court** or the **Courtroom Deputy** (federal) / **Courtroom Clerk** (state).
   - State clearly: "I am [Your Name], case number [Case Number]. I am ready for my [Time] hearing in Department [Number], but the video link is failing. How should I appear?"

5. **Request the telephone dial-in instructions if video remains unavailable.**
   - Ask for the specific phone number, Meeting ID, and Passcode.
   - If prompted for a "Participant ID" when dialing in, press # to bypass it unless you were specifically assigned one.

6. **Contact your attorney if you are represented.**
   - Inform them you are on the phone with the clerk or that you cannot connect. 
   - Ask them to alert the judge that you are present but experiencing technical issues.

7. **Document your attempt to appear while waiting for instructions.**
   - Write down the exact time you attempted to join and the time you called the court.
   - Take one photo of the error message on your screen as evidence for your attorney or a future motion.

8. **Inform staff immediately if you are in a facility (Jail, Prison, or Hospital).**
   - Alert the officer or coordinator that the link has failed and request they contact the court on your behalf to document your attempt to appear.

## What can wait
- You do not need to fix your internet service or hardware issues permanently during the hearing window.
- You do not need to file a formal "Motion for Continuance" in the first five minutes; the immediate priority is verbal notification to the clerk.
- You do not need to provide a technical diagnostic report of why the link failed.

## Important reassurance
Technical failures are common in remote proceedings. Most courts have established protocols for participants who cannot connect via video. As long as you contact the clerk or courtroom deputy immediately, you are establishing a record of your "intent to appear," which protects you from being marked as a "no-show."

## Scope note
This guide covers first-step stabilization for technical access failures only. It does not cover the legal merits of your case or long-term requests for permanent remote status.

## Important note
This information is for procedural stabilization and is not legal advice. Court rules vary significantly by jurisdiction (State vs. Federal) and case type (Civil vs. Criminal). Always follow the specific instructions on your official court notice as your primary authority.
