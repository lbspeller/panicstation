# What to do if… you discover your address or workplace has been posted publicly online alongside hostile comments (USA)

## Short answer
Prioritize physical safety by moving to a secure location and calling **911** if you observe immediate threats or suspicious activity. Preserve evidence by taking screenshots before reporting the content to the platform for removal and notifying your workplace security or HR.

## Do not do these things
- Don't respond to the post or engage with the commenters.
- Don't post counter-evidence or explanations that include more personal details.
- Don't delete the original post yourself before taking screenshots.
- Don't attempt to contact or "unmask" the person who posted the information.
- Don't ignore the situation because it originated "only online."

## What to do now
1. **Assess your immediate physical environment.**  
   **If you see suspicious people or vehicles near your home or workplace, or if the post contains direct threats of violence, call 911 immediately.**
   - Move to an interior room or a public space with other people.
   - Do not wait for a confrontation to seek help.

2. **Capture and preserve evidence of the exposure.**  
   **Take screenshots that show the full context of the post, including the URL, the date and time, the platform name, and any specific hostile comments or threats.**
   - Save these files to a secure cloud drive or external device.
   - Note any usernames or profile links associated with the original poster.

3. **Submit a removal request to the hosting platform.**  
   **Use the platform's internal reporting tool to flag the content as "harassment," "doxxing," or "sharing private information."**
   - Most major social media platforms have specific categories for "sharing personal contact information."
   - Keep a record of the report confirmation or ticket number.

4. **Use Google’s removal tools to reduce search visibility.**  
   **Access the "Results about you" tool in your Google Account or use the "Remove select personally identifiable info (PII)" form to request the removal of your home address or workplace from search results.**
   - Provide the specific URLs captured in your evidence phase.
   - Understand that this removes the link from search results but does not delete the content from the original website.

5. **Notify your workplace security or HR department.**  
   **Inform your employer that the workplace location has been targeted so they can implement visitor controls and alert front-desk staff.**
   - Ask them to screen incoming calls for your name or department.
   - Discuss temporary safety measures such as remote work or escorted entry and exit.

6. **File a report with local law enforcement and federal channels.**  
   **Provide your saved screenshots to your local police department (non-emergency line if no immediate danger) and file a complaint at the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (ic3.gov).**
   - Mention if the doxxing is accompanied by stalking, hacking, or calls for others to harm you.
   - Request a case number for your records.

7. **Initiate the California DROP process if you are a state resident.**  
   **Access the California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) platform to submit a single deletion request to all registered data brokers.**
   - You can submit the request now; brokers are required to begin processing these requests by August 1, 2026.
   - This helps reduce the availability of your data for future doxxing attempts.

## What can wait
- Deciding on long-term legal action or civil lawsuits against the posters.
- Evaluating whether you need to move or change your legal name.
- Attempting to scrub every mention of your name from the entire internet.
- Responding to friends or family who are asking for details about the situation.

## Important reassurance
Physical safety is the priority, and taking steps to secure your home and workplace is a standard protocol for address exposure. It is common to feel a sense of hyper-vigilance or disorientation when privacy is breached; these are standard physiological responses to an external threat.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for the immediate aftermath of a location-based privacy breach. It does not cover long-term digital footprint management or the legal complexities of harassment litigation.

## Important note
This information is for safety stabilization and is not legal advice. Laws regarding doxxing and digital harassment vary by state and are evolving in 2026. Always prioritize instructions from local law enforcement if they conflict with general safety steps.
