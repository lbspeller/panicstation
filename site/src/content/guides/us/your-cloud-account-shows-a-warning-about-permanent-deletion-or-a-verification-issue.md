# What to do if… your cloud account shows a warning about permanent deletion or a verification issue (USA)

## Short answer
Use the provider’s official website or app, not a message link, to check the warning and start the official recovery or appeal process. If you still have access, begin exporting irreplaceable files now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t click verification links from unexpected emails or texts; urgent account messages may be phishing.
- Don’t send ID documents by email, chat, or social media DM; use only the provider’s secure upload flow.
- Don’t sign out of a device where you are still logged in unless the provider tells you to.
- Don’t create lots of new tickets or repeat rushed guesses; follow the provider’s stated limits and keep one clear record.
- Don’t pay “account recovery” strangers or services that claim they can bypass the provider.
- Don’t delete emails, alerts, receipts, or screenshots connected to the warning.

## What to do now
1. **Check the warning through a clean path.**  
   Open a new browser window or the official app. Type the provider’s address yourself, use a saved bookmark you trust, or search for the provider and choose its official site. If the warning only appears after you click a message link, treat the message as suspicious.

2. **Capture the details before they change.**  
   Screenshot the warning, any deadline, the stated policy or verification reason, the account email, and any case or ticket number. Save related emails, texts, billing receipts, and subscription records.

3. **Start the official recovery, appeal, or request-review flow.**  
   Use the wording the provider gives, such as appeal, request review, recover account, reactivate, or verify identity. Enter details carefully and consistently, especially recovery email, phone number, billing name, recent devices, and approximate last successful sign-in.

4. **Protect the email account used for recovery.**  
   Sign in to that email account through its official site. Change the password if you suspect compromise, turn on multi-factor authentication if available, and check for unknown forwarding rules, recovery-email changes, filters, or signed-in devices.

5. **Use a familiar device and normal connection if you can.**  
   Submit recovery from a device and location you have used with the account before, such as your usual home computer or phone. If the provider tells you to wait before trying again, wait rather than forcing repeated attempts.

6. **Export what you can while you still have access.**  
   Start the provider’s official export or download tool if available. Prioritise photos, original documents, tax or legal records, business files, recovery codes, backup codes, and files needed to sign in elsewhere. Store copies on a local drive and, if possible, a second independent storage location.

7. **Handle sensitive exports carefully.**  
   If you export password-vault data, recovery codes, or identity documents, do it only from a device you trust. Save them somewhere encrypted or otherwise protected, and do not email them to yourself unless there is no safer option.

8. **Contact official support from inside the provider’s help flow.**  
   Provide the screenshots, ticket number, receipts or order IDs, approximate last successful login, device names, and a calm one-paragraph summary. Do not upload identity documents anywhere except the provider’s secure portal.

9. **If this looks like fraud or account takeover, preserve records and report it.**  
   Keep copies of the suspicious messages, sender details, links, transaction records, and support replies. Report scams or bad business practices to the Federal Trade Commission. If identity information was stolen or used, use IdentityTheft.gov for official next steps.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to abandon the provider.
- You do not need to understand every policy detail before starting the appeal or export.
- You do not need a perfect backup system before saving the most important files.
- You do not need to argue with support repeatedly; one clear, documented request is better than many panicked messages.
- You can review state privacy-request options later if ordinary recovery and export routes do not solve the immediate risk.

## Important reassurance
These notices are stressful because they combine urgency, identity checks, and possible data loss. The safest first move is to slow the situation down: use only official paths, keep your current access alive, document the warning, and save the data you can reach.

## Scope note
These are first steps for a sudden account-deletion or verification crisis. Later decisions about privacy requests, legal options, business continuity, or a long-term backup plan may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, cybersecurity, financial, or professional advice. Provider rules and recovery systems vary. State privacy laws may give some people ways to request certain personal information, but they generally are not the fastest way to stop deletion or restore an enforced account.
