# What to do if… someone is outside your home refusing to leave and you are inside alone (USA)

## Short answer
Lock all entry points immediately and call 911 to report a person refusing to leave your property. Stay behind locked barriers and do not engage with the individual until law enforcement arrives.

## Do not do these things
- Do not open the door to speak with the person or ask them what they want.
- Do not go outside to confront, film, or attempt to move the person.
- Do not unlock windows to shout instructions or negotiate.
- Do not mention that you are alone or disclose if you are armed.
- Do not turn off all the lights; keeping external lights on may assist police.
- Do not assume the person is harmless because they appear distressed or familiar.

## What to do now
1. **Secure all physical barriers immediately.** Ensure every door and window is closed and locked.
   - Check the front door, back door, and any garage-to-house entries.
   - Lock sliding glass doors and ensure the security bar is in place if available.
2. **Call 911 and provide your specific location first.** State your address clearly, including any apartment numbers, gate codes, or building letters.
   - Inform the dispatcher that you are inside the home alone.
   - State clearly that there is an individual on your property who refuses to leave.
3. **Describe the individual's actions and appearance.** Use a peephole or security camera to gather details without being seen from the outside.
   - Note clothing colors, approximate height, and any visible items in their hands.
   - Report if they are attempting to turn handles, looking through windows, or shouting.
4. **Request a "silent approach" if you fear escalation.** You can ask the dispatcher if officers can arrive without sirens if you believe noise will cause the person to attempt forced entry.
5. **Move to a room with a secondary exit and a lock.** Choose a space that provides a barrier between you and the person's current location.
   - Bring your phone, a charger , and any essential medication with you.
   - Stay away from windows where your silhouette or movement can be seen from outside.
6. **Use a brief, firm statement only if necessary.** If you must communicate, do so through the closed door without unlocking it: "I have called the police. You must leave now." 
   - After this statement, cease all verbal interaction.
7. **Monitor your security feed if available.** If you have a doorbell camera or external monitors, watch the person’s movements to update the dispatcher.
   - Do not let the screen distract you from listening for sounds of entry at other points in the house.
8. **Stay on the line until an officer is at your door.** Do not hang up with 911 until the dispatcher confirms that the police have arrived and it is safe to open the door.
   - Verify the officer's identity through the door or window before unlocking.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide if you want to press charges immediately; the responding officer will discuss options once the scene is secure.
- You do not need to search for the person's name or identity if they are unknown to you.
- You do not need to review long-term security camera footage until after the immediate threat is gone.
- You do not need to notify neighbors or landlords until you are no longer in active danger.

## Important reassurance
Feeling a sense of panic or hesitation is a standard physiological response to a boundary violation. Taking these steps to maintain a physical barrier and involve authorities is a proactive and appropriate safety measure. You are not "overreacting" by securing your home against an unwanted presence.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an active doorstep or property confrontation. It does not cover long-term protective orders, stalking investigations, or permanent home security installations.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and does not constitute legal or professional security advice. Always follow the direct commands of emergency dispatchers and law enforcement officers on the scene.
