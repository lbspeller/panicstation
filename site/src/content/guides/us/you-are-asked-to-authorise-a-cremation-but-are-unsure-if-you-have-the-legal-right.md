# What to do if… you are asked to authorise a cremation but are unsure if you have the legal right (USA)

## Short answer
Do not sign the cremation authorization while you are unsure. Ask the funeral provider to pause any cremation and the paperwork while it identifies the state rule or document it is relying on.

## Do not do these things
- Do not sign just to keep things moving. Cremation cannot be reversed.
- Do not assume you can sign because you were closest emotionally, paid expenses, or acted as a caregiver.
- Do not assume a power of attorney authorizes you to make cremation decisions after the person has died.
- Do not assume that being named as executor settles the question.
- Do not sign while another person’s possible authority or required consent has not been checked.
- Do not accept pressure to sign immediately without first reading the form and asking why the provider believes you are the correct signer.

## What to do now
1. **Ask the provider to pause any cremation and the paperwork.**  
   Say: “I am not sure I have the legal right to authorize cremation. Please do not proceed with cremation or the paperwork while this is checked.”

2. **Get a copy of the form before signing anything.**  
   Ask: “What role would I be signing as? Please send me the full authorization form and any document you are relying on.”

3. **Ask which state rule applies and why the provider believes you are the correct signer.**  
   Laws and required documents vary by state. Ask:
   - “Which state’s rule are you applying?”
   - “What document or priority rule are you relying on?”
   - “Does anyone else need to consent, sign, or be contacted before cremation can proceed?”

4. **Check for written instructions or a written designation made by the person who died.**  
   Ask whether the provider or family has a document naming a person to control disposition or recording final-disposition instructions. A written document may affect who has authority, but its legal effect depends on the applicable state rule.

5. **Tell the provider about any possible dispute early.**  
   Say: “Another person may have equal or higher priority, or the family may disagree. Please keep the hold in place and tell me what documentation you need while authority is checked.”

6. **Do not rely on executor status or a power of attorney without verification.**  
   Ask the provider to explain whether either document matters under the applicable state rule. If the answer remains unclear or relatives disagree, seek guidance from a lawyer qualified in that state.

7. **If the provider states that a purchase or procedure is legally required, ask for the basis in writing.**  
   The Federal Trade Commission Funeral Rule prohibits certain misrepresentations about legal, crematory, and cemetery requirements. The Funeral Rule does not itself determine who has authority to sign a cremation authorization.

8. **If you already signed and are now unsure, contact the provider immediately.**  
   Ask whether cremation has already occurred. If it has not, request an immediate hold and state clearly that authority to authorize cremation is disputed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide now about urns, scattering, obituary wording, memorial details, or optional services.
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family disagreements today.
- You do not need to argue about who was closest to the person who died.
- The immediate task is only to pause an irreversible step while signing authority is checked.

## Important reassurance
Pausing before signing is a reasonable response when authority is unclear. Taking time to check the applicable rule and documents can reduce the risk of an irreversible decision being made by the wrong person.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. State law, the available documents, and the family circumstances may affect who can authorize cremation. Later decisions may need help from a lawyer qualified in the relevant state.
