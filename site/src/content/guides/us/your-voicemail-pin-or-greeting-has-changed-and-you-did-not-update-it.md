# What to do if… your voicemail PIN or greeting has changed and you did not update it (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as possible phone-account compromise until your carrier checks it. Secure your mobile carrier account first, then reset your voicemail password through official carrier tools.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t keep guessing PINs; repeated attempts can lock the mailbox or waste time.
- Don’t use a reset link from an unexpected text or email; go through your carrier’s official app, website, store, or known support route.
- Don’t rely on voicemail for account recovery until your carrier confirms your line and voicemail are secure.
- Don’t immediately factory-reset your phone or change your number in panic; it can make recovery harder.
- Don’t post screenshots of carrier texts, emails, account pages, or reset messages online.

## What to do now
1. **Check whether your phone line still works.**
   - Place a call and send a text from your phone.
   - If you suddenly have no service, “SOS only,” “SIM not provisioned,” or a message saying your SIM was changed, treat it as urgent and contact your carrier from another phone or a secure internet connection.

2. **Secure your mobile carrier account.**
   - Sign in through your carrier’s official app or website, typed manually or opened from a trusted bookmark.
   - Change the carrier account password.
   - Add or update the account PIN, passcode, number lock, transfer lock, port-out protection, or two-step verification if your carrier offers it.

3. **Contact your carrier’s fraud or security support.**
   - Say: “My voicemail PIN or greeting changed without me. Please check for account takeover, SIM swap, voicemail access, and port-out activity.”
   - Ask the carrier to check for recent SIM changes, eSIM activations, device or IMEI changes, port-out requests, forwarding changes, authorized-user changes, and account email or address changes.
   - Ask them to stop or reverse any unauthorized change they can confirm and to add the strongest available account protections.

4. **Reset voicemail only through official steps.**
   - Use your carrier’s official app, website, support line, or store to reset the voicemail PIN or password.
   - If you use an iPhone and your carrier supports it, you may also be able to change the voicemail password in iPhone settings.
   - Choose a new code that is not a birthday, address, repeated digits, or a simple sequence.

5. **Reduce damage from possible voicemail access.**
   - Change the password on your primary email account, because email often controls other password resets.
   - Check banking, payment, email, and major social accounts for password resets, new devices, changed recovery numbers, or unfamiliar activity.
   - Where available, move important accounts away from phone-call, text-message, or voicemail-based recovery and use an authenticator app, passkey, or security key.

6. **Create a report trail if money, identity details, or phone service were affected.**
   - If personal information, bank details, credit cards, or accounts may have been misused, use IdentityTheft.gov for an official recovery plan.
   - If the problem involved phone-service fraud, number porting, or carrier handling, you can file an FCC consumer complaint and save the confirmation.
   - Keep a simple note of dates, carrier contacts, case numbers, and what each support agent changed.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether to change your phone number.
- You do not need to wipe your phone unless there is a separate sign your device itself was compromised.
- You do not need to notify everyone immediately; focus first on your carrier, primary email, banking, payments, and accounts that use your phone number for recovery.
- You do not need to understand exactly how it happened before taking these first protective steps.

## Important reassurance
A changed voicemail PIN or greeting can have a harmless explanation, but checking it as a possible account takeover is a sensible first response. The goal is to keep control of your phone number and prevent further account misuse, not to solve everything at once.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, especially after confirmed identity theft, account access, or financial loss, may need help from your carrier, financial institutions, credit bureaus, or a qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, technical, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Carrier menus, protections, and recovery routes vary by provider, plan, and device; use your carrier’s official support channels if the steps do not match what you see.
