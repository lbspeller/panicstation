# What to do if… you receive a flood of unexpected password reset or security code messages (USA)

## Short answer
Do not click links in the messages or approve any unexpected login prompt. Secure your email account first, then check directly for any real security alert, purchase, or account change hidden in the flood.

## Do not do these things
- Do not click reset links, “verify” buttons, attachments, or unsubscribe links inside the messages.
- Do not approve any unexpected MFA push notification.
- Do not give a one-time code, password, or recovery code to anyone who contacts you.
- Do not delete everything blindly if you can avoid it; a real password change, login, or purchase notice may be buried in the flood.
- Do not turn off MFA to reduce prompts.
- Do not reuse your email password on any account you are checking.

## What to do now
1. **Create a calmer two-minute window**
   - Silence notifications briefly so you do not act on impulse.
   - Open your email only through the official app or by typing the provider’s address yourself.
   - Keep the messages available for now, but do not use their links.

2. **Secure your email account first**
   - Change the email account password to a long, unique password.
   - Turn on MFA if it is not already on; use an authenticator app or security key where available.
   - Sign out of other sessions or devices if your provider offers that option.
   - Review recent sign-ins, devices, and security events for anything unfamiliar.

3. **Check whether the mailbox has been altered**
   - Review forwarding settings.
   - Review mail rules, filters, labels, blocked senders, and auto-archive or auto-delete settings.
   - Remove any forwarding address or rule you did not create, especially rules that hide, delete, archive, or mark security emails as read.

4. **Search for the real problem inside the flood**
   - Search your inbox for “password changed”, “security alert”, “new login”, “new device”, “purchase”, “order”, “recovery”, “verification code”, and “payment”.
   - Start with accounts that can cause immediate harm: email, bank and card accounts, payment apps and wallets, your mobile carrier, Apple, Google, Microsoft, and any account with saved payment details.
   - Check spam, trash, archive, and recently deleted folders if your provider has them.

5. **Check important accounts directly**
   - Go to each service by typing the address yourself or using the official app.
   - Check recent logins, devices, password changes, recovery email, recovery phone, and saved payment methods.
   - If anything is unfamiliar, change that account’s password, turn on MFA, and remove unknown devices or recovery details.

6. **Report the suspicious messages**
   - Use your email provider’s “report spam” or “report phishing” option.
   - Forward suspected phishing emails to reportphishing@apwg.org.
   - Report phishing or fraud attempts to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.

7. **Act fast if money, codes, or account access were lost**
   - Contact your bank or card issuer using the number on the back of your card or inside the official app.
   - Use the affected provider’s account recovery process if you are locked out.
   - File a report with the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center if you believe you are a victim of cyber-enabled fraud, phishing, or account takeover.
   - Go to IdentityTheft.gov if personal information, financial information, or identity documents may have been misused.

## What can wait
- You do not need to handle every reset email right now.
- You do not need to close old accounts today.
- You do not need to work out exactly how your email address was targeted before securing your email and key accounts.
- You do not need to reply to the senders or prove which messages are real by using their links.

## Important reassurance
A password reset email often only means someone entered your email address. The main immediate risk is being rushed into clicking a link, approving a prompt, sharing a code, or missing a real alert in the noise.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate harm, protect access to key accounts, and buy time. Later decisions, such as account recovery, fraud disputes, identity protection, or specialist cybersecurity help, may need provider support or other specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or other professional advice. If you are unsure whether a message is real, treat it as suspicious and access accounts only through official apps or addresses you type yourself.
