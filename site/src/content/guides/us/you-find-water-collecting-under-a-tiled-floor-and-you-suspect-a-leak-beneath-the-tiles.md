# What to do if… you find water collecting under a tiled floor and you suspect a leak beneath the tiles (USA)

## Short answer
Shut off the home's main water supply valve immediately to stop the flow, keep people and pets away from the wet area, and do not touch any electrical switches or appliances in the vicinity.

## Do not do these things
- Don't run any water at nearby fixtures or flush toilets to "test" where the leak is coming from.
- Don't turn on underfloor heating or use a space heater/hair dryer to attempt to dry the floor while it is still wet.
- Don't walk on the wet tiles more than necessary, as water can cause tiles to de-bond and become unstable slip or trip hazards.
- Don't use any electrical outlets, switches, or plug-in appliances located on the floor or on walls adjacent to the wet area.
- Don't attempt to pry up tiles or drill into the floor to "find" the leak, as you may strike pressurized pipes or electrical conduits.
- Don't step into or stand in water while attempting to access your electrical service panel.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the area immediately.** Keep all residents and pets away from the affected floor to prevent slips and potential electrical contact.
2. **Shut off the main water valve.**
   - **Locate the main shutoff valve** for the entire house (typically found in the basement, garage, utility closet, or at the water meter near the street).
   - **Turn the handle clockwise** (for gate valves) or 90 degrees (for ball valves) until it stops.
   - **Briefly open a faucet** at the lowest level of the home to verify that the water flow has stopped.
3. **Address electrical hazards safely.**
   - **Identify the main breaker panel** and determine if you can reach it while standing on a completely dry surface.
   - **Use a dry, non-conductive tool** (such as a wooden broom handle) to flip the main breaker to "off" if you suspect water has reached any wiring, outlets, or floor-based appliances, provided you are not standing in water.
   - **Contact your electric utility** if you cannot safely reach the panel due to standing water.
4. **Isolate suspected appliances.**
   - **Close the local shutoff valves** for nearby appliances (dishwasher, washing machine, or refrigerator ice line) if they are in the immediate area and can be reached safely without entering water.
5. **Contain the surface water.**
   - **Lay down absorbent materials** such as towels, blankets, or rags around the edges of the wet area to prevent water from spreading to other rooms or under walls.
   - **Do not use a wet-dry vacuum** unless you are certain the outlet being used is dry, GFCI-protected, and you are standing on a dry surface.
6. **Document the damage for insurance.**
   - **Take clear photos and video** of the water pooling, grout discoloration, and any lifting or "hollow" sounding tiles.
   - **Capture any secondary damage** such as wet baseboards, wall staining, or dampness in the ceiling of the room directly below.
7. **Contact a licensed professional.**
   - **Call a licensed plumber** and specify that you have a "suspected pressurized leak under a tiled floor."
   - **Notify your property manager or HOA** immediately if you live in a multi-unit building, as the leak may affect units below yours.

## What can wait
- You do not need to choose a tile replacement or a flooring contractor today.
- You do not need to contact a water restoration company until the plumbing leak is confirmed and stopped.
- You do not need to decide whether to file an insurance claim until a professional has identified the cause and provided a repair estimate.

## Important reassurance
Discovering water beneath a finished floor is a serious situation, but taking immediate action to shut off the water is the most effective way to limit property damage. Professional plumbers use specialized tools to locate leaks through tile without destroying the entire floor, and most household plumbing emergencies are stabilized within the first hour of professional arrival.

## Scope note
This is first-step stabilization only. This guide addresses immediate safety and damage mitigation for suspected plumbing or appliance leaks under tile and does not cover long-term structural drying or mold remediation.

## Important note
This guide is for general information only and does not constitute professional plumbing or electrical advice. Always follow local building codes and manufacturer safety instructions for your utilities. If you see sparks, smell gas, or cannot safely access your water shutoff, evacuate the area and call 911 or your local emergency services for assistance.
