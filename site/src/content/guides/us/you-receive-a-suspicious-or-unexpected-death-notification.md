# What to do if… you receive a suspicious or unexpected death notification (USA)

## Short answer
Do not send money, identification, account details, verification codes, or your Social Security number. Stop the contact and verify through contact details you find independently, not the number, link, caller ID, or transfer offered by the person who contacted you.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t share your Social Security number, date of birth, driver’s license, bank information, account passwords, or verification codes.
- Don’t click links or open “death certificates,” “case files,” invoices, or forms sent by text or email.
- Don’t call back using the number in the message, and don’t let the caller transfer you to a “supervisor,” “detective,” “caseworker,” or “billing office.”
- Don’t send money for funeral costs, transport, release fees, inheritance processing, or urgent paperwork until you have verified independently.
- Don’t post personal details online to “confirm” whether the notice is real.

## What to do now
1. **End the interaction.** Say: “I’m not able to discuss this. I will verify independently.” Then hang up or stop replying.
2. **Save the basic details.** Screenshot texts or emails, note the caller ID or number, the exact claim, any case or invoice number, and anything they demanded.
3. **Verify through a route you choose:**
   - **If they claim to be a hospital or medical facility:** call the facility’s main switchboard using a number you find independently, then ask for the relevant unit, patient information desk, or patient relations. Share only minimal details until you are confident it is legitimate.
   - **If they claim to be law enforcement:** call the agency’s publicly listed non-emergency number and ask whether that officer, unit, or case contact exists.
   - **If they named a funeral home:** look up the funeral home’s public number yourself and ask whether they are handling that person. They may limit what they can say.
   - **If the person is known to you:** contact a close relative, friend, employer, school, or usual caregiver using contact details you already had before the message.
4. **If the message mentions Social Security, benefits, or a Social Security number:**
   - Treat any request for your Social Security number or payment as suspicious until independently verified.
   - Social Security death reports are generally made through a funeral director, by calling Social Security, or by visiting a Social Security office. Social Security does not accept death reports by email or online.
   - If you suspect you or someone you care for has been incorrectly listed as deceased in Social Security records, visit a local Social Security office as soon as possible with current original identification or copies certified by the agency that issued them.
5. **If money was requested:** treat it as a scam until verified. Tell them you will not discuss payment unless you have confirmed the death and the organization through a contact route you found yourself.
6. **Report the contact if it appears suspicious:**
   - Report fraud and scams to the FTC.
   - Report email, website, online payment, app, or cryptocurrency-related scams to the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center.
   - Report Social Security-related fraud or Social Security impersonation scams to the SSA Office of Inspector General.
7. **If you shared financial details, your Social Security number, login details, or paid anything:**
   - Contact your bank, card issuer, payment app, or money transfer service using the number on your card, official app, or official website.
   - Ask whether the payment can be stopped, reversed, traced, or disputed.
   - Start identity-theft recovery through IdentityTheft.gov if your Social Security number or other identity information was shared.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to prove it is a scam before protecting yourself.
- You don’t need to decide funeral, benefit, inheritance, travel, or family-notification issues right now.
- You don’t need to notify every agency immediately; focus first on the route most relevant to what happened, such as money, Social Security information, identity details, or an online scam.
- You don’t need to confront the sender or keep them talking.

## Important reassurance
A sudden death notification can make it hard to think clearly, especially if it might involve someone you know. Slowing down, stopping contact, and checking through channels you control is a reasonable first step.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to prevent immediate harm and help you confirm what is real. Later decisions about family, benefits, records, accounts, or legal matters may need help from the relevant agency, financial institution, funeral provider, attorney, or other specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Scams, reporting options, and next steps can vary by state and situation. When unsure, default to no money, no personal information, and verification through independently found official contact details.
