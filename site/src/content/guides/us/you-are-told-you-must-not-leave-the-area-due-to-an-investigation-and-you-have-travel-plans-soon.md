# What to do if… you are told you must not leave the area due to an investigation and you have travel plans soon (USA)

## Short answer
Verify if the instruction is a formal court order or an informal police request. Do not leave the jurisdiction if you are under a court-ordered bond condition or pretrial supervision until you receive written permission from the court or your supervising officer.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume a verbal "don't leave town" from a police officer is a binding legal order unless you are under arrest or have signed bond paperwork.
- Do not attempt to travel through an airport or across state lines if you have a pending warrant or an active court order prohibiting it.
- Do not discuss your specific travel itinerary or the underlying investigation on social media or with anyone other than your legal counsel.
- Do not ignore the request entirely; leaving without clarifying your status can lead to the immediate issuance of an arrest warrant if the restriction is formal.
- Do not contact investigators directly to "explain" your travel needs; route all requests through an attorney.

## What to do now
1. **Ask for the legal basis of the travel restriction immediately.**
   - If a police officer tells you not to leave, ask: "Am I being detained?" and "Is there a court order or warrant prohibiting my travel?"
   - If you have been arrested and released, locate your **Order of Release** or **Bond Conditions** document to find the exact geographical limits.
2. **Contact a criminal defense attorney to verify your legal status.**
   - **Provide your attorney with your full travel itinerary**, including departure dates, destinations, and return dates.
   - Ask your attorney to confirm with the court or the prosecutor if there is a "stop" or "hold" on your name in law enforcement databases.
3. **Check for "judicial district" or "state-wide" restrictions in your paperwork.**
   - Look for clauses stating you must "remain within the judicial district" or "remain within the State/County."
   - Identify if you were required to **surrender your passport** as a condition of release.
4. **Follow the formal "Travel Pass" or approval process if you are under supervision.**
   - If you are supervised by **Pretrial Services** or a **Probation Office**, contact them to request a formal travel authorization.
   - Submit the request at least two to four weeks in advance, as many offices require this lead time for internal verification.
5. **Seek a formal "Motion to Modify Conditions of Release" through the court.**
   - If your bond paperwork explicitly forbids travel, your attorney must file a motion asking the judge to allow the specific trip.
   - Obtain the **signed court order** allowing the travel before you depart.
6. **Protect your travel investments without admitting fault.**
   - Contact airlines or travel agents to check for "cancellation for any reason" policies or to reschedule your dates.
   - Use neutral language, such as citing "unforeseen legal scheduling conflicts" or "unresolved administrative requirements."
7. **Keep the written approval on your person during travel.**
   - If you receive a travel pass or a court order, carry a physical copy and a digital backup throughout the trip to show to law enforcement or border agents if questioned.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the final outcome of the investigation before dealing with the immediate travel limit.
- You do not need to cancel your trip until your attorney has attempted to negotiate a modification or verified your status.
- You do not need to notify your employer or family about the nature of the investigation while resolving the travel issue.

## Important reassurance
In the United States, the right to travel is a constitutional protection that can only be restricted through due process. If the police simply told you "don't leave town" without a court order or a formal detention, the request may not be legally binding. Identifying your formal status with an attorney is the fastest way to resolve the uncertainty.

## Scope note
This guide focuses on first-step stabilization regarding travel limits during a pending investigation. It does not cover long-term legal defense strategies or international extradition laws.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and is not legal advice. Travel restrictions vary significantly between state and federal jurisdictions. If you are unsure whether you are legally restricted, assume the restriction is in place until a qualified attorney confirms otherwise to avoid an accidental "jumped bail" or "flight from justice" charge.
