# What to do if… an interviewer or recruiter asks sexual or inappropriate personal questions (USA)
## Short answer
You can decline to answer and end the interview. Get to a calmer, safer pause before deciding what to do next.

## Do not do these things
- Do not answer personal sexual questions because you feel pressured to keep the interview going.
- Do not keep debating after you have decided to end the conversation.
- Do not confront the person again to obtain an explanation or admission.
- Do not post identifying details publicly while you are still overwhelmed.

## What to do now
1. **Create a safer pause.** If the interview is in person, leave the room and move to a place that feels safer, such as a staffed or public area. If it is by phone or video, end the call.
2. **Use one short sentence if that feels safe.** You can say: “I’m not comfortable answering that. Can we keep the questions related to the role?” If it continues, you can say: “I’m ending the interview now.”
3. **Reduce further direct contact.** Use email rather than personal messaging where possible. If direct messages feel unsafe or harassing, you can stop replying or block the sender.
4. **Tell one trusted person what happened.** Ask them to stay with you, call you, or help you read any new message before you respond.
5. **Preserve options only if you may want them later.** Keep existing messages and make a brief private note of what happened, but do not pressure yourself to collect more information.
6. **Treat immediate danger as the priority.** If there are threats, stalking, unwanted touching, or an immediate safety risk, move to a safer place and call 911.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide now whether to continue with the application.
- You do not have to decide now whether to contact the employer, staffing agency, HR, a state or local fair employment agency, the EEOC, or an attorney.
- You do not have to explain your reaction or contact the interviewer again.
- EEOC time limits may apply if you later want to explore a discrimination charge. In general, the deadline is 180 calendar days and may be extended to 300 calendar days in some places. Federal employees and applicants for federal jobs generally have a different process and generally must contact an agency EEO Counselor within 45 days. Check the official EEOC time-limits page soon if keeping that option open matters to you.

## Important reassurance
It is understandable to feel unsettled, confused, or unsure what to say after a boundary-crossing question. Ending the conversation and taking a pause is a reasonable response.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions may need specialist support, and you can choose whether to seek that support when you are ready.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Laws, processes, and available options can vary by state, employer coverage, and the facts of what happened.
