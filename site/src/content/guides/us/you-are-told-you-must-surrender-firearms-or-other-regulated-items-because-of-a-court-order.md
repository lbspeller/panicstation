# What to do if… you are told you must surrender firearms or other regulated items because of a court order (USA)

## Short answer
You must surrender all covered items (firearms, ammunition, parts, and permits) within the specific window defined in the order—often 24 hours—and file written proof of compliance with the court immediately afterward. Secure a detailed receipt from law enforcement or an authorized dealer to avoid arrest or contempt charges.

## Do not do these things
- Do not hide or "loan" firearms to friends or family; this is a violation of the order and may result in felony charges for both parties.
- Do not bring firearms inside a police station or court building without calling first to receive specific arrival instructions.
- Do not keep any ammunition, frames, receivers, or magazines if the order covers "firearm parts" or "regulated items."
- Do not miss the filing deadline for your receipt; surrendering the items is not enough—the court must receive the proof by the deadline (often 48-72 hours).
- Do not wait to see an attorney before surrendering; the deadline for relinquishment usually runs from the moment of service regardless of legal counsel status.

## What to do now
1. **Locate and read the "Relinquishment" or "Surrender" section of the order to identify your specific deadline.** Most 2026 state laws for Extreme Risk Protection Orders (ERPOs) or Domestic Violence Restraining Orders require surrender within 24 hours of being served.
2. **If you do not own or possess any firearms, you must still file a "Declaration of Non-Possession" or "Affidavit of Non-Surrender" with the court.** Failure to file this document when you have no weapons to surrender can result in a warrant for your arrest for non-compliance.
3. **Contact the designated law enforcement agency non-emergency line to arrange a safe surrender time.**
   - State clearly that you are complying with a court order to surrender firearms.
   - Ask for the specific location and procedure for intake (e.g., "Do I wait in the parking lot for an officer?").
4. **Prepare the items for transport by ensuring all firearms are unloaded and placed in the trunk or rear of the vehicle.**
   - Keep ammunition in a separate container from the firearms.
   - Do not have any firearms in the passenger cabin or "within reach" during transport.
5. **Verify if the order allows transfer to a Federal Firearms Licensee (FFL) dealer for storage or sale.** Some orders mandate law enforcement surrender only; if FFL is allowed, call the dealer first to confirm they accept "prohibited person" transfers and ask for their storage fee schedule.
6. **Obtain a signed, dated receipt that lists the make, model, and serial number of every firearm surrendered.**
   - Ensure the receipt also quantifies ammunition (e.g., "two boxes of 9mm") and lists any permits or parts turned in.
   - Take a clear photograph of the receipt immediately after receiving it.
7. **File the original receipt (or the required court form with the receipt attached) at the court clerk's office before the deadline.** Ask the clerk for a "conformed" or "file-stamped" copy of your filing for your own records to prove when it was submitted.
8. **Notify the court in writing immediately if a specific item listed in the order is not in your possession.** If a weapon is lost, stolen, or held by another person, you must provide a sworn statement explaining the location or status of the item to the judge.

## What can wait
- You do not need to argue the merits of the case during the surrender process; focus only on the physical transfer and paperwork.
- You do not need to decide on a permanent disposal or sale of the firearms immediately unless the order is permanent.
- You do not need to worry about the "return" process today; the priority is avoiding immediate criminal penalties for non-compliance.

## Important reassurance
Receiving a surrender order is a high-stress event, but following the procedural steps is the most effective way to prevent the situation from escalating into criminal charges. It is common to feel overwhelmed by the technical requirements; following the specific instructions of the court clerk or the law enforcement intake officer is the standard path to stabilization.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate first-step stabilization for firearm and regulated item surrender in the USA as of 2026. It does not cover legal defense, appeals, or the process for the eventual return of firearms.

## Important note
This information is not legal advice. Firearm surrender laws are jurisdiction-specific and may vary by the type of court order issued. Always prioritize the specific instructions written on your court order and confirm procedures with the local sheriff or police department before transporting weapons.
