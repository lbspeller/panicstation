# What to do if… a supervisor suggests your schedule, shifts, or work opportunities depend on sexual attention (USA)

## Short answer
Exit the immediate interaction to reach a safer space, then document the specific event and preserve all existing communications before any work records can be altered or deleted.

## Do not do these things
- Do not feel obligated to agree to sexual requests to protect your livelihood — you have the right to delay your response and seek safety.
- Do not meet with the supervisor alone, in private offices, or at off-site locations while you are processing the situation.
- Do not delete any evidence including text messages, direct messages, emails, voicemails, or screenshots of your current and past schedules.
- Do not confront the supervisor in a secluded area or during a one-on-one meeting where there are no witnesses.
- Do not sign any "agreements," "write-ups," or "confidentiality documents" on the spot without first taking them home to review with support.

## What to do now
1. **Move to a visible or populated area immediately to end the interaction.** You can state that you have a task to complete or need to use the restroom to create a physical exit from the conversation.
2. **Write down a factual record of the event as soon as you are in a safe place.** Include the date, exact time, location, the specific words used by the supervisor, any witnesses present, and the specific work benefits (such as shifts or promotions) that were mentioned.
3. **Preserve all digital and physical evidence on a personal device or account.** Take screenshots of shift schedules, call logs, and messages; forward relevant work emails to a personal address if company policy allows, or take photos of the screen with a personal phone.
4. **Identify the safest person in your organization to notify about the immediate need for protection.** This could be a different manager, an HR representative, a union steward, or an ethics hotline operator.
5. **Request specific immediate safety measures if you choose to report internally.** Ask for a change in who handles your scheduling, the presence of a third party for any necessary meetings, or a temporary shift change to avoid contact with the supervisor.
6. **Check the filing deadlines for the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) and your state's labor agency.** Most private sector employees have **180 or 300 days** to file a charge of "quid pro quo" harassment, but some states like New York now offer longer windows for sexual harassment specifically.
7. **Contact an EEO Counselor within 45 days if you are a federal employee.** This is a strict deadline for federal workers to initiate the formal complaint process; missing this window can waive your right to pursue the claim later.
8. **Contact RAINN for confidential support and guidance on handling workplace sexual misconduct.** You can call the National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-HOPE for anonymous help that does not trigger a workplace investigation.
9. **Seek medical care or contact local law enforcement if any non-consensual sexual contact occurred.** Prioritize your physical health and safety; reporting to your employer or the police remains your choice and can be decided after you have received care.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide right now whether you want to file a lawsuit or a formal government charge.
- You do not need to have "perfect" evidence or a completed timeline before reaching out for confidential support.
- You do not need to explain your situation to coworkers or friends until you feel ready to do so.
- You do not need to quit your job immediately while you are exploring your legal and safety options.

## Important reassurance
A supervisor tying work opportunities to sexual attention is a prohibited form of "quid pro quo" harassment under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act. Feeling anxious about your shifts, your paycheck, or potential retaliation is a common and expected reaction to this type of power imbalance. You are not responsible for the supervisor's behavior, and help is available to help you navigate your next steps.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and information preservation steps only. Decisions regarding legal action, internal complaints, or career changes should be made in consultation with specialists or legal counsel.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Although the EEOC rescinded its 2024 Enforcement Guidance in early 2026, the underlying federal laws prohibiting sexual harassment remain in effect. For specific legal rights in your state, consult an employment attorney or your state's human rights commission.
