# What to do if… you are offered a job, role, or opportunity and the communication turns sexual (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately cease contact, move to a safer location, and preserve all digital or physical evidence without responding to the sexual content. You have the legal right to professional recruitment, and you are not required to continue an interaction that has become sexualized or coercive.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to meet in private residences, hotel rooms, or after-hours bars to "finalize the details."
- Do not provide intimate images or personal videos under the guise of "background checks" or "personality fit."
- Do not delete any messages, emails, or call logs, even if the content is distressing or you feel ashamed.
- Do not attempt to "negotiate" the boundaries or explain why the behavior is inappropriate in real-time.
- Do not assume that your status as an "applicant" means you have no legal protection against harassment.

## What to do now
1. **Discontinue the interaction immediately.** If you are in a physical meeting, leave for a public area; if you are in a digital thread, send one final professional statement.
   - "I am interested in this role only as a professional opportunity. Since the conversation is no longer professional, I am ending this contact."
2. **Document every instance of sexualized communication.** Take full-page screenshots that include the sender’s profile name, phone number or email address, and the exact timestamps of every message.
   - Save copies of job descriptions, offer letters, and any "quid pro quo" statements where the job is conditioned on sexual acts.
3. **Secure your digital and physical perimeter.** Block the individual on all professional and personal platforms to prevent further unwanted contact or monitoring.
   - Adjust social media privacy settings to "private" if the recruiter has access to your personal life details.
4. **Access 24/7 confidential support through specialized services.** Contact the **National Sexual Assault Hotline (RAINN)** to speak with a trained specialist who can help you process the event and identify local resources.
   - Call **800.656.HOPE (4673)**.
   - Text **"HOPE" to 64673**.
   - Use the online chat at **online.rainn.org**.
5. **Verify the legitimacy of the company independently.** Look up the official corporate office or HR department through an external search engine (do not use links provided by the harasser).
   - Report the behavior to the company's internal HR or Ethics department only if you feel safe doing so and want the organization to know their representative is acting inappropriately.
6. **Determine if you need to file a formal report for safety.** If the person is making physical threats, following you, or has possession of your sensitive personal data, call **911** or your local police non-emergency line.
7. **Understand your federal rights as an applicant.** Under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, it is illegal for an employer to harass a job applicant because of their sex.
   - You can initiate an inquiry through the **EEOC Public Portal** to discuss filing a formal "Charge of Discrimination."

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide today whether to pursue a lawsuit or a formal EEOC charge.
- You do not need to notify your current employer or references about the incident.
- You do not need to understand "why" this happened or if you misinterpreted a "joke" before seeking support.

## Important reassurance
It is a common physiological response to feel "frozen" or to try and keep things light when a professional opportunity is suddenly sexualized. This is a survival mechanism used to manage a power imbalance. The illegal and unprofessional nature of the behavior is entirely the responsibility of the person who initiated it.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for sexual harassment during the hiring process. It does not cover long-term legal strategy, civil litigation, or criminal prosecution procedures, which require consultation with an attorney or law enforcement.

## Important note
Federal guidelines regarding workplace harassment interpretations were significantly updated in early 2026. While Title VII protections remain law, the specific administrative procedures and filing windows (typically 180 or 300 days) vary by state. Prioritize immediate physical safety and evidence preservation.
