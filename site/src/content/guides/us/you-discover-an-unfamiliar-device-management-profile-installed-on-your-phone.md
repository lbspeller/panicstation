# What to do if… you discover an unfamiliar “device management” profile installed on your phone (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate the device from all networks, document the profile details for evidence, and remove the configuration or work profile to stop remote access and monitoring.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t enter any passwords, credit card numbers, or MFA codes on the affected phone.
- Don’t follow instructions from any person or website claiming you must install a profile to "fix" a security error or "unblock" an account.
- Don’t use "MDM bypass" software or websites that ask you to connect your phone to a computer; these are often malicious.
- Don’t factory reset the device before taking screenshots of the profile name and organization info.
- Don’t assume a profile is safe because it is named "System Update," "Security Certificate," or uses a bank's name.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the device from all external connections.**
   - **Enable Airplane mode** immediately.
   - **Manually toggle Wi-Fi and Bluetooth to "Off"** in the settings menu, as Airplane mode may sometimes leave them active.
   - Use a separate, known-safe device for all subsequent communications and account changes.

2. **Capture evidence of the unauthorized profile.**
   - **Take screenshots** (or photos with another phone) of the profile details screen.
   - **Include the "Organization" name**, the "Managed by" text, and the list of "Permissions" or "Restrictions" the profile enforces.
   - **Record the date and time** you first noticed the profile or any "Managed" banners on your lock screen.

3. **Locate the profile in your device settings.**
   - **On iPhone/iPad:** Go to **Settings → General → VPN & Device Management**.
   - **On Android:** Go to **Settings → Passwords & accounts** (or **Accounts**) and look for a section labeled **Work** or **Work profile**.
   - **Check for "Supervision":** On iPhone, if the top of the main Settings screen says "This iPhone is supervised and managed by [Organization]," the profile may be permanent and require a factory reset or IT intervention.

4. **Remove the profile and associated accounts.**
   - **On iPhone/iPad:** Select the profile, tap **Remove Management** (or **Delete Profile**), enter your passcode, and confirm.
   - **On Android:** Tap **Remove work profile** and then **Delete**. If this option is missing, select the specific work account and tap **Remove account**.
   - **Restart the device** immediately after removal.

5. **Secure your primary digital identity from a safe device.**
   - **Change the password for your Apple Account (formerly Apple ID) or Google Account.**
   - **Review "Authorized Devices"** in your account security settings and "Sign out" of all sessions.
   - **Update passwords for high-risk accounts** including banking, primary email, and password managers.

6. **Contact your cellular provider to prevent SIM-based attacks.**
   - **Call your mobile carrier** using their official customer service number.
   - **Verify there have been no recent "SIM swap" or "Port-out" requests.**
   - **Add a Port-Out PIN or "Account Protection"** to your cellular account if you do not already have one.

7. **File official reports if the profile was installed via a scam.**
   - **Report cyber-enabled fraud** to the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center at **IC3.gov**.
   - **Create a recovery plan** at **IdentityTheft.gov** (FTC) if you provided any personal information to a scammer before installing the profile.

## What can wait
- You do not need to identify the specific individual who created the profile right now.
- You do not need to replace the physical hardware unless the device is "Supervised" and cannot be wiped.
- You do not need to notify everyone in your contacts list unless you have evidence the device was used to send messages to them.

## Important reassurance
Many management profiles appear because of legitimate work enrollments or used-device history. If this was a scam attempt, isolating the phone and removing the profile usually stops the remote access immediately. You are taking the correct steps to regain your privacy.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for personal devices. If the phone is owned by an employer or school, these steps may violate policy or result in permanent data loss of work files.

## Important note
This information is for educational purposes and is not legal or technical advice. If you suspect your physical safety is at risk due to stalking or domestic surveillance, contact local law enforcement or a specialized victim services agency.
