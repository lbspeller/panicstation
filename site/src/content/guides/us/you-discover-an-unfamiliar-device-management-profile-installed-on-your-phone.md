# What to do if… you discover an unfamiliar device management profile installed on your phone (USA)

## Short answer
Stop using that phone for banking, email, password managers, or other sensitive accounts. Disconnect it from networks, document what the profile says, and remove it if it is not clearly legitimate and you can do so safely, because deleting a profile can also delete settings, accounts, apps, or data tied to it.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t sign into banking, email, password managers, work systems, or recovery accounts on that phone until you have checked what is being managed.
- Don’t follow instructions from a caller, text, chat, pop-up, or “support” person telling you to install, keep, or re-enroll a profile.
- Don’t download “MDM removal” tools from random websites.
- Don’t let a stranger remote-control your phone or computer.
- Don’t factory reset as the first move unless immediate safety or official support requires it; it can cause lockouts, data loss, or make it harder to understand what happened.
- Don’t assume the profile is harmless just because it has an official-sounding name.

## What to do now
1. **Reduce risk immediately**
   - Turn on **Airplane Mode**.
   - Manually switch **Wi-Fi** and **Bluetooth** off as well.
   - Use another trusted device for any urgent logins, password changes, or account checks.

2. **Record what you are seeing**
   - Take screenshots of the profile screen, including the profile name, organization name, “managed by” message, listed VPN, certificates, restrictions, or accounts.
   - Write down the date and time you noticed it.
   - Note anything that happened just before it appeared, such as a link, QR code, app install, “IT help” call, repair visit, used-phone purchase, school setup, or work setup.

3. **Find where the profile or management setting is**
   - **iPhone or iPad:** go to **Settings → General → VPN & Device Management**. Open the profile and read who it claims to be. If you do not see any profiles there, that specific iPhone device-management profile screen is not showing an installed profile.
   - **Android:** look for a **Work profile** or briefcase icon. You can also check **Settings → Passwords, Passkeys & Accounts** or **Settings → Accounts** for a work account or work profile. Names vary by Android version and phone maker.

4. **Try to remove the unfamiliar profile if it is not clearly yours**
   - **iPhone or iPad:** in **VPN & Device Management**, select the unfamiliar profile, tap **Delete Profile**, follow the onscreen instructions, and restart the phone. Removing it can delete settings, apps, accounts, VPN access, certificates, or data associated with that profile.
   - **Android:** if you see **Remove work profile**, use it and confirm deletion. If you do not see that option, tap the work account and choose **Remove account** if available. Removing it can delete work-profile apps, email, contacts, settings, and other data associated with that work account or profile.
   - If removal is blocked, stop using the phone for sensitive accounts and contact the real organization shown on the profile using a known official website, app, or phone number, not a number from a pop-up or message.

5. **Secure the accounts most likely to be exposed**
   - From a trusted device, change passwords for your main email, Apple Account or Google Account, banking, and any password manager.
   - Turn on two-factor authentication or 2-Step Verification where available.
   - Review signed-in devices and recent activity for your Apple Account, Google Account, email, and password manager.
   - Sign out devices or sessions you do not recognize.

6. **Check your phone number account**
   - Call your mobile carrier using the official number from its website, app, bill, or the back of your SIM card packaging.
   - Ask whether there has been any recent SIM swap, number transfer, port-out request, SMS forwarding, account PIN change, or unusual support activity.
   - Ask what stronger account PIN, passcode, port lock, or extra verification option may be available for your carrier account.

7. **Report it if it looks like fraud or identity misuse**
   - If someone tricked you into installing the profile, asked for money, accessed accounts, or used a fake support story, report it to IC3 or ReportFraud.ftc.gov.
   - If your Social Security number, identity documents, tax information, bank details, or other identity information may be misused, use IdentityTheft.gov for recovery steps.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide right now whether you need a new phone.
- You do not need to identify the person behind it before taking protective steps.
- You do not need to reset every account at once; start with network isolation, profile removal if possible, and your most important accounts.
- You do not need to argue with a caller, texter, seller, repair shop, school, or employer while you are panicked.

## Important reassurance
An unfamiliar management profile is serious, but it does not automatically mean everything on the phone has been read. Profiles can appear through scams, work or school setup, used devices, repairs, or account setup mistakes. The useful first move is to slow the situation down, document what is visible, remove what is not yours if possible, and secure key accounts from a trusted device.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions, such as replacing the phone, handling employer or school management, responding to stalking or harassment, or dealing with financial loss, may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, cybersecurity, or other professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 911. If the device belongs to an employer, school, or family plan, confirm through an official support channel before taking steps that could remove required access or data.
