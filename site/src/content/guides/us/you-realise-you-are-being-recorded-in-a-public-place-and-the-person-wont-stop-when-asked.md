# What to do if… you realise you are being recorded in a public place and the person won’t stop when asked (USA)

## Short answer
Disengage immediately and move to a secure or staffed location—do not attempt to physically stop the recording or touch the person's device.

## Do not do these things
- Don't touch the other person, grab their phone, or attempt to delete their footage.
- Don't stay in the area to argue about legality or "rights" while the recording continues.
- Don't block the person's path or follow them to "get a better look" at them.
- Don't shout personal information, such as your full name, home address, or workplace.
- Don't lead the person toward your vehicle or your home.

## What to do now
1. **Create physical distance and find witnesses.** Move toward a populated, well-lit area or a staffed service point such as a security desk, checkout lane, or reception area.
2. **Issue a single, clear boundary statement.** **State "Stop recording me and do not follow me" in a firm voice, then immediately stop all verbal interaction.**
3. **Notify the property authority if you are in a business or private venue.**
   - **Inform staff or security:** "This person is recording me without my consent and I feel unsafe; I need assistance getting to a safe area."
   - **Understand property rights:** Private businesses (malls, stores, theaters) can enforce "no-recording" policies and have the legal right to trespass individuals who refuse to stop.
4. **Identify the level of police response required.**
   - **Call 911** if the person is following you, threatening you, blocking your exit, or if you feel in immediate physical danger.
   - **Call the local non-emergency number** if the situation has stabilized but you wish to report the harassment or intimidation.
5. **Request an incident log and video preservation.** If you are on private property with its own surveillance, ask security to **document the event in an incident report** and **save their CCTV footage** of the interaction.
6. **Obtain identifying information for the report.** Ask for the **security officer's name** or the **police incident/case number** before you leave the scene.
7. **Document the interaction from a safe distance.** Once you are no longer in the person's line of sight, record the **time, specific location, description of the person, and exactly what was said.**
8. **Contact support if this becomes a pattern.** If you are being targeted repeatedly, call the **VictimConnect Resource Center at 855-4VICTIM (855-484-2846)** for guidance on stalking and harassment.

## What can wait
- Determining if the recording violated state-specific "all-party consent" audio laws.
- Searching social media platforms or video sites to see if the footage has been uploaded.
- Filing a formal civil complaint for privacy violations.
- Checking state-specific "Right of Publicity" laws regarding the use of your image for commercial gain.

## Important reassurance
It is a common and normal response to feel a "fight or flight" surge when someone records you without permission. Choosing to walk away and involve authorities is the most effective way to de-escalate the situation and prevent the person from getting more footage of you.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for an immediate encounter. It does not cover long-term legal strategies, digital "take-down" requests for uploaded videos, or state-by-state variations in wiretapping statutes.

## Important note
This guide is for informational purposes and is not legal advice. While the First Amendment generally protects filming in public spaces where there is no "reasonable expectation of privacy," private property owners may prohibit recording. Harassment, stalking, and "disturbing the peace" remain illegal regardless of whether a camera is present.
