# What to do if… a password manager shows unexpected vault changes or missing entries (USA)

## Short answer
Isolate your account by logging out all other sessions and changing your master password from a clean device. Use built-in vault history or trash features to restore data, then secure your primary email and file a report with IdentityTheft.gov if a breach is confirmed.

## Do not do these things
- Don't make new changes or deletions in the vault — this may overwrite the version history you need for recovery.
- Don't uninstall the password manager app — local cache files on your device may be the only remaining copy of missing data.
- Don't use "contact support" links found in unexpected emails or search engine ads — go directly to the official vendor website.
- Don't ignore the issue as a "sync glitch" — treat any unexpected change as a potential security event until proven otherwise.
- Don't share full screenshots of your vault or recovery keys with anyone, including people claiming to be support agents.

## What to do now
1. **Force a global logout of all active sessions.**  
   **Access your password manager's security settings and select the option to "log out of all other sessions" or "deauthorize all devices."** This immediately severs the connection for any unauthorized person currently accessing your vault.

2. **Update your master password from a trusted, malware-free device.**  
   **Create a new, unique master passphrase of at least 16 to 20 characters that you have never used before.** Avoid using personal information or common dictionary words; 2026 CISA guidance recommends a passphrase of four or more random, unrelated words.

3. **Verify and upgrade your Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).**  
   **Confirm that MFA is still enabled and prioritize phishing-resistant methods like Passkeys or hardware security keys over SMS codes.** Review the list of "trusted devices" and remove any you do not recognize. Check for any newly added "Emergency Access" contacts that you did not authorize.

4. **Secure your "root" email account.**  
   **Log into the email account associated with your password manager, change its password, and terminate all other active sessions.** If an attacker has access to your email, they can potentially trigger a master password reset or intercept vault recovery links.

5. **Utilize internal recovery and restoration tools.**  
   **Look for features labeled "Trash / Deleted Items," "Item History," "Vault Rollback," or "Security Dashboard."**  
   - Most major managers (1Password, Bitwarden, LastPass, Dashlane) allow you to view previous versions of individual entries or restore deleted items within a 30-day window.  
   - Check if the changes occurred in a "Shared Vault" or "Family Collection" and consult other members to see if they performed the edits.

6. **Place a fraud alert or credit freeze.**  
   **Contact one of the three major credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion) to place a free fraud alert on your file.** If you confirm that sensitive data like Social Security numbers or financial logins were stored in the compromised vault, a full credit freeze is recommended via each bureau's official website.

7. **Document the specific changes for vendor support.**  
   **Record the exact time you noticed the issue, which entries are missing, and any unrecognized IP addresses or locations in your "Login History" or "Access Logs."** Contact the official support channel of your password manager provider with this data.

8. **Report the incident to federal authorities if fraud is suspected.**  
   **Visit IdentityTheft.gov to create a personalized recovery plan and file a report with the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center at ic3.gov.** Use the keyword "data breach" or "account takeover" in your description to ensure proper routing.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide on a new password manager provider while in the middle of a recovery process.
- You do not need to reset every single password in your vault immediately; focus only on "high-impact" accounts like banking, primary email, and mobile carrier accounts first.
- You do not need to perform a factory reset of your hardware unless a malware scan confirms a deep-level device infection.

## Important reassurance
Missing entries are often the result of sync conflicts, server-side updates, or accidental movements within shared folders rather than a malicious hack. By freezing changes and securing your master credentials now, you are preventing further escalation while you determine the actual cause. Confusion or a sense of urgency is a normal reaction to digital instability.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for individual users. If this involves a corporate or managed work account, you must stop these steps and notify your organization's IT Security or Incident Response team immediately, as they have access to advanced administrative logs.

## Important note
This information is for emergency stabilization and does not constitute legal, financial, or professional cybersecurity advice. Automated vault changes may indicate a technical failure or a security breach; always verify through official vendor channels. If you detect unauthorized financial transactions, contact your bank or card issuer immediately.
