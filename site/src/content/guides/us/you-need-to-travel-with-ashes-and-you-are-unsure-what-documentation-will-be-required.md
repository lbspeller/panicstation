# What to do if… you need to travel with ashes and you are unsure what documentation will be required (USA)

## Short answer
Assume you’ll need a death certificate and a cremation certificate, keep them with you, and use a container that can be screened by TSA. Then confirm your airline and (if international) the destination and any transit country rules.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t bring a container that TSA can’t screen (if they can’t determine what it is, it may not be allowed through).
- Don’t put ashes in checked luggage to “make it easier” unless your airline allows it and you accept loss/damage risk.
- Don’t assume TSA rules cover international borders (countries can require different paperwork to enter/leave with ashes).
- Don’t last-minute ship ashes as a workaround without checking the carrier’s specific rules and timelines.
- Don’t scatter ashes at your destination without confirming you’re allowed to do so there.

## What to do now
1. **Collect the core documents and keep them in your carry-on.**
   - **Death certificate** (original or certified copy if possible).
   - **Cremation certificate/cremation permit** (from the crematory/funeral home).
   Bring 1–2 paper copies separately (and phone photos as backup).

2. **Make the container TSA-screening friendly and spill-safe.**
   - Choose a container that can be **screened clearly** (the key issue is screening, not a specific material).
   - Use a **sealed inner bag** and a **protective outer case/box**.
   - Keep it in your **carry-on** so you maintain custody and can answer questions.

3. **Check your airline’s “cremated remains” policy (don’t rely on general tips).**
   Confirm:
   - Whether they require specific documents (and originals vs copies).
   - Whether ashes are allowed in carry-on, checked bags, or both.
   - Any packaging requirements.
   Save a screenshot/printout of the policy in case you need to show it.

4. **If your trip crosses a U.S. border, separate “screening” from “customs/public health.”**
   - **TSA** is about airport screening.
   - **CBP** is about customs when entering the U.S., and they may expect documentation like a death certificate and cremation permit.
   - **Destination + transit countries** may have their own import/export rules for cremated remains—check them explicitly.

5. **At the airport: name it early and keep it simple.**
   - At TSA, calmly say: **“I’m traveling with cremated remains.”**
   - Present documents if asked.
   - Build in extra time so you’re not forced into rushed decisions at the checkpoint.

6. **If you can’t confirm requirements and travel is imminent, default to “most documented + most conservative.”**
   Travel with both certificates (plus copies), keep ashes in carry-on, and use screening-friendly packaging. If an official asks for something you don’t have, you’ll be in the best position to explain and comply without losing control of the remains.

## What can wait
- You do **not** need to pick a permanent urn today; a secure travel container is enough for many trips.
- You do **not** need to finalize memorial/scattering plans before you travel.
- You do **not** need to contact multiple offices at once—start with TSA + your airline + destination/transit rules.

## Important reassurance
It’s normal to feel on edge about this—airports make uncertainty feel urgent. Most issues come down to missing paperwork or a container that can’t be screened. Address those two risks and the rest usually becomes manageable.

## Scope note
These are first steps for screening and border-readiness. Complex international routes can add requirements, so confirming each border you cross (including transit stops) matters.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal advice. TSA procedures, airline policies, and international border requirements can change and can differ by route and country (including transit). When you cannot confirm a requirement, use conservative documentation and packaging and verify with the airline and relevant authorities as soon as possible.
