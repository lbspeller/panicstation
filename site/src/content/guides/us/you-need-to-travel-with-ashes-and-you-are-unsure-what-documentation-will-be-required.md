# What to do if… you need to travel with ashes and you are unsure what documentation will be required (USA)

## Short answer
Ensure the remains are in a container scannable by X-ray (plastic, wood, or cardboard) and carry an original or certified copy of the death certificate and cremation permit. Keep these items in your carry-on luggage to maintain control and comply with airline-specific rules.

## Do not do these things
- **Do not use a lead-lined or thick metal container** as TSA must be able to see through the contents via X-ray; if they cannot, the item will be denied.
- **Do not ask TSA officers to open the container**, as they are prohibited by policy from opening any urn or cremation container out of respect for the deceased.
- **Do not put remains in checked baggage** unless explicitly required by the airline, as this increases the risk of loss, damage, or delayed inspection.
- **Do not assume TSA rules apply to international customs**; while TSA clears you for the flight, the destination country or U.S. Customs (CBP) may require different health or legal permits.
- **Do not attempt to bypass screening** by hiding the nature of the contents; being transparent with security staff usually speeds up the process.

## What to do now
1. **Secure a certified copy of the death certificate and the cremation certificate.** **These documents are the primary proof required by airlines and border authorities, even if TSA screening does not strictly require them to clear the security checkpoint.** 
   - Ensure the cremation permit/certificate includes the name of the deceased and the crematory's contact information.
   - For pet remains, carry the certificate of cremation and any relevant veterinary records.

2. **Verify that your container is made of X-ray-transparent material.** **TSA must be able to clearly see the contents on their screening monitor to allow the remains through the checkpoint.**
   - Suitable materials include plastic, lightweight wood, cardboard, or non-lead-based ceramic.
   - If your permanent urn is metal or stone, travel with the ashes in a temporary plastic "travel urn" and carry the permanent urn empty.

3. **Check your specific airline’s "cremated remains" policy regarding carry-on status.** **Most major U.S. carriers allow ashes in the cabin, but some treat the urn as a "special item" that does not count toward your carry-on limit, while others require it to fit under the seat.**
   - Call the airline or check their "Special Assistance" or "Restricted Items" webpage.
   - Ask if they require "certified" documents versus photocopies.

4. **Identify the requirements for any international borders you will cross.** **The CDC does not generally require health permits for fully cremated remains entering the U.S., but the destination country's embassy must be consulted for their specific import rules.**
   - Contact the embassy or consulate of the destination country.
   - Check if documents must be translated into the local language.

5. **Notify the TSA officer at the start of the screening process.** **Verbally stating "I am traveling with cremated remains" allows the officer to handle your bag with appropriate care and prevents confusion during the X-ray scan.**
   - You may place the container in its own bin to facilitate a clear scan.
   - Arrive at the airport at least 30 minutes earlier than usual to accommodate potential manual inspections of the outer packaging.

## What can wait
- **You do not need to finalize the permanent urn choice today**; a temporary, sift-proof travel container is sufficient for the flight.
- **You do not need to notify the CDC for domestic travel**, as they only regulate the importation of remains from other countries.
- **You do not need to purchase a separate seat for the remains** unless the container exceeds standard carry-on size dimensions.

## Important reassurance
It is common for travelers to feel anxious about the security process when carrying a loved one. Airport staff and TSA officers are trained to handle these situations with respect and are generally familiar with the required procedures. As long as the container is scannable and you have your certificates, the process is usually a standard part of airport operations.

## Scope note
This guide covers immediate stabilization and preparation for airport security and airline boarding within the USA. It does not cover the legalities of scattering remains or long-term estate administration.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal advice. TSA and airline policies are subject to change; always verify requirements with your carrier and relevant government agencies (CBP, CDC, or foreign embassies) before your day of travel.
