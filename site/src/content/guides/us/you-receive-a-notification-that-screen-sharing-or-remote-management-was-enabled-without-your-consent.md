# What to do if… you receive a notification that screen sharing or remote management was enabled without your consent (USA)

## Short answer
Immediately disconnect the device from all internet and local network connections to terminate any active sessions, then use a separate, trusted device to change the passwords for your primary accounts.

## Do not do these things
- Do not call any phone numbers provided in the notification or pop-up message.
- Do not provide passwords, two-factor authentication codes, or personal details to anyone claiming to be "support" following the alert.
- Do not log into sensitive accounts—such as banking, healthcare, or password managers—on the affected device.
- Do not click "Allow," "Trust," or "Accept" on any follow-up prompts or certificates.
- Do not factory reset the device before capturing images of the specific alert and any listed software names if you intend to report the incident.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the device from all networks.**
   - Toggle Airplane Mode to the "On" position.
   - Disable Wi-Fi and Bluetooth manually in system settings.
   - Unplug any physical Ethernet cables connected to the device.
2. **Capture visual evidence of the notification.**
   - Take a photograph of the screen using an external camera (like a smartphone) or perform a local screenshot.
   - Note the exact wording of the alert, the time it appeared, and any specific application names mentioned (e.g., AnyDesk, TeamViewer, Microsoft Remote Desktop, or Apple Remote Management).
3. **Verify the management status of the device.**
   - On macOS: Go to System Settings > General > VPN & Device Management.
   - On Windows: Go to Settings > Accounts > Access work or school.
   - If the device is owned by an employer or school, contact their official IT helpdesk through a separate verified channel to ask if they initiated a management update.
4. **Identify and disable remote features.**
   - On macOS: Navigate to System Settings > General > Sharing and ensure "Screen Sharing" and "Remote Management" are toggled off.
   - On Windows: Navigate to Settings > System > Remote Desktop and ensure the toggle is "Off." Also, check "Remote Assistance" in the Control Panel under System > Remote Settings.
5. **Review active user accounts and profiles.**
   - Check the list of Users or Administrators in System Settings for any names or accounts you do not recognize.
   - Look for "Profiles" (macOS) or "Management Certificates" (Windows) that were added recently without your knowledge.
6. **Secure primary accounts from a separate device.**
   - Use a different, uncompromised computer or phone to change the passwords for your email, Apple ID / Microsoft Account / Google Account, and financial services.
   - Enable hardware-based Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) where possible.
   - Use the "Sign out of all sessions" or "Remove trusted devices" feature in your account security settings.
7. **Report the intrusion to federal authorities.**
   - File a report at **ic3.gov**. You must type this address directly into your browser's address bar and verify it ends in ".gov" to avoid spoofed reporting sites.
   - If the incident involved a "tech support" representative or pop-up, report it to **reportfraud.ftc.gov**.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the specific technical method of the breach (e.g., specific malware type) immediately.
- You do not need to decide whether to replace the hardware today.
- You do not need to notify your entire contact list until you have secured your primary email and identity accounts.

## Important reassurance
Receiving an unauthorized access notification is a significant privacy event, and feeling a sense of violation is a common reaction. Disconnecting the device from the network is an effective way to stop live remote interaction and provides a safe pause to begin securing your digital identity from a different machine.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for individual users. If the device is part of a corporate or government network, additional forensic requirements or reporting obligations defined by your organization may apply.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute professional cybersecurity or legal advice. If you suspect your Social Security number or financial data has been stolen during the session, consult IdentityTheft.gov for specific recovery steps.
