# What to do if… you are asked to clear a home after someone dies and are worried about losing evidence (USA)

## Short answer
Pause before anything is removed. Make a dated, room-by-room record of the home as it is, then ask the person handling the estate to confirm in writing what, if anything, you should pack, move, store, or discard.

## Do not do these things
- Do not start donating, selling, discarding, or dividing belongings because the clean-out feels urgent.
- Do not assume that everything in a shared home belongs to the estate.
- Do not remove items for safekeeping without clear written instructions from the person with authority and, where relevant, agreement from the occupant or owner.
- Do not mix belongings from different rooms into unmarked boxes or bags.
- Do not throw away papers, mail, keys, or files while authority and ownership are still unclear.
- Do not rely only on verbal instructions. Keep texts or emails that explain what you were asked to do.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the clean-out in writing.**  
   Send a short message: *“I am pausing removal until the contents are recorded and the person with authority confirms the plan.”*

2. **Make a dated record of the home as it is now.**  
   - Take a slow video walkthrough of each room.
   - Take wide photos of visible contents and close-ups of valuables, collections, serial numbers, existing damage, or areas that appear disturbed.
   - Record the date and time.
   - Start with visible contents. Do not open private drawers, cabinets, files, or containers unless the person with authority asks you to do so.

3. **Create a simple room-by-room list.**  
   Use a basic format: **room → item or box number → short description → where it is now**. Keep it factual. You do not need to value or sort everything today.

4. **Ask who is handling the estate and what is included.**  
   Ask in writing:
   - Has a court appointed a personal representative?
   - Is there a will or trust?
   - Are there surviving occupants, co-owners, or other people whose belongings are in the home?
   - What items, if any, should I pack, move, store, or discard?

   A person named in a will may not yet have authority to administer the estate. Procedures vary by state, and the contents of a shared home may not all belong to the estate.

5. **Leave items in place where possible.**  
   If something must be moved because it is perishable, unsafe, at risk of immediate loss, or needed to secure the home, keep the movement minimal and make sure it is safe to do so. Photograph the item first when possible. Note what was moved, why it was moved, who moved it, and where it went.

6. **If you are authorized to pack, keep a clear movement log.**  
   - Pack one room at a time.
   - Number each box and write the room, date, and your initials on it.
   - Photograph the visible area before packing and the sealed, labeled box afterward.
   - List the storage location or the person receiving each box.
   - For valuable items, use a written handoff note and, where practical, have another adult present.

7. **Stop moving items if there are signs of forced entry, suspected theft, or immediate danger.**  
   Contact local law enforcement. Call 911 when immediate assistance is needed.

8. **Tell the person handling the estate about security concerns.**  
   If the home may be vacant or unsecured, ask the policyholder or authorized person to check the insurer’s requirements and arrange any needed security steps.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide who should receive belongings today.
- You do not need to resolve ownership disagreements before making a basic record.
- You do not need to appraise everything now.
- You do not need to choose a clean-out company until the person with authority confirms the plan.
- You do not need to sort private papers while the immediate priority is preventing avoidable loss.

## Important reassurance
Your concern is understandable. A simple dated record can make later conversations clearer and reduce avoidable confusion without forcing you to solve every estate question today.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce the risk of irreversible mistakes. Probate procedures, ownership questions, and the authority to move property vary by state and by the facts. Later decisions may need help from the personal representative, the relevant probate court, or a probate attorney.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, insurance, or professional advice. If authority is unclear, belongings are disputed, theft is suspected, or the shared home contains another person’s property, pause the clean-out and ask for appropriate state-specific advice before removing items.
