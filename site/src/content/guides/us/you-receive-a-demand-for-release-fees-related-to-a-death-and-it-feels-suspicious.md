# What to do if… you receive a demand for “release fees” related to a death and it feels suspicious (USA)

## Short answer
Stop all communication immediately and do not send any form of payment. Independently locate the official phone number for the funeral home or government office mentioned and call them directly to verify the request.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay using wire transfers, cryptocurrency, gift cards, or peer-to-peer apps like Venmo or Zelle.
- Do not trust the name or number on your Caller ID; scammers frequently spoof official funeral home or county numbers.
- Do not click on "funeral livestream" links or "tribute" buttons that require a payment to view.
- Do not share a Social Security Number, death certificate copies, or banking details over text or email.
- Do not respond to threats that a service will be canceled or remains will be "withheld" unless you pay immediately.
- Do not let the sender pressure you into keeping the payment a secret from other family members or the estate executor.

## What to do now
1. **Discontinue the interaction and lock your phone.** If the demand came via call, hang up; if by text or email, do not reply. This stops the immediate pressure and prevents you from accidentally clicking a malicious link.
2. **Locate the original paperwork or the official website for the facility.** Find the "General Price List" (GPL) provided by the funeral home at the start of arrangements. If you do not have it, search for the facility’s official website through a search engine—do not use any link provided in the suspicious message.
3. **Contact the institution using a verified number.** 
   - Call the funeral home or crematorium directly and ask to speak with the funeral director you have been working with. 
   - If the request claims to be from a **Medical Examiner** or **Coroner**, call the county office directly. 
   - Note that under the FTC Funeral Rule, homes must provide price information over the phone and cannot misrepresent local laws to extract fees.
4. **Identify the specific red flags for 2026 scam patterns.**
   - Requests for "security deposits" to keep a pre-need account active.
   - Demands for "facility fees" or "transportation surcharges" not listed in your signed contract.
   - Use of AI-generated voices that sound like a specific funeral director or family member.
5. **Secure your financial accounts if details were shared.**
   - **Call your bank’s fraud department** immediately using the number on the back of your card.
   - Ask for a "freeze" on the account if you provided a routing or account number.
6. **Report the incident to federal authorities.**
   - File a report at **ReportFraud.ftc.gov**; provide the scammer's number and the exact wording used.
   - If a Social Security Number was exposed, contact the **IRS Identity Theft Central** at 1-800-908-4490.
   - Submit a complaint to the **FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center** at IC3.gov.

## What can wait
- You do not need to notify the entire family or post a warning on social media this hour.
- You do not need to verify the authenticity of the death certificate if that was the context of the fee.
- You do not need to figure out how the scammer obtained the decedent’s information; they often pull details from public obituaries or social media.
- You do not need to pay any "late fees" while you are conducting this verification.

## Important reassurance
Scammers target people in grief specifically because the emotional load makes it harder to spot inconsistencies. Falling for the initial pressure or feeling startled is a common reaction and does not mean you have made a mistake. Taking a moment to verify is a standard safety procedure, and no legitimate professional will penalize you for doing so.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for identifying and stopping bereavement-related financial fraud. It does not cover the long-term legal process of probate or the formal resolution of estate debts.

## Important note
This information is for general guidance and does not constitute legal or financial advice. The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) enforces the Funeral Rule, which protects your rights to transparent pricing. Always use official, independently verified contact channels for government and financial transactions.
