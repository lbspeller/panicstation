# What to do if… a person who died left minor children and you need to arrange urgent care (USA)

## Short answer
Make sure the children are safe and with a safe adult. Confirm whether there is a surviving legal parent and whether any custody order already exists before making non-essential moves or treating a temporary arrangement as permanent.

## Do not do these things
- Do not make a non-essential move with the children, especially to another state, until you have checked who has legal authority and whether any existing order affects the decision.
- Do not treat a verbal claim of guardianship as settled proof. Ask to see any court papers or other documents.
- Do not sign documents that appear to make a permanent custody, adoption, or parental-rights change during the first shock without local legal advice.
- Do not create new contact restrictions unless they are needed for immediate safety or required by an existing order.
- Do not post identifying details about the children or the family situation online.

## What to do now
1. **Check immediate safety.**  
   Make sure the children are with a safe adult. If anyone is in immediate danger or needs emergency police, fire, or ambulance assistance, call **911**.

2. **Confirm the surviving-parent situation.**  
   Ask whether there is another legal parent, whether that person can be reached now, and whether any custody, guardianship, divorce, parentage, or child-welfare order already exists. Keep copies or photos of any papers you are shown.

3. **Keep the immediate arrangement simple.**  
   If the children are safe with a non-parent, avoid unnecessary moves while authority is being checked. Write down where the children are staying, who is caring for them, and how that adult can be reached. This record does not transfer custody.

4. **Locate any relevant documents.**  
   Look for a will, written guardian nomination, existing custody order, or recent court papers. A guardian nomination may be important information, but it does not by itself settle the immediate question.

5. **Ask for the local urgent route.**  
   Contact the local court clerk and ask which court handles minor-child guardianship or custody issues in that area and whether an emergency or temporary process is available. Ask whether there is a court self-help service. A local civil legal-aid organisation can also help identify the appropriate route.

6. **Mention facts that may change the route.**  
   Tell the court clerk or legal-aid provider if the children are in another state, if an existing case or order may exist, or if a child may be a member or citizen of a federally recognised Tribe. Also tell them if a child may be eligible for Tribal membership or citizenship and has a biological parent who is a member or citizen of a federally recognised Tribe.

7. **Contact the local child welfare agency when safety is not settled.**  
   If there is no safe adult available, or there are immediate safety concerns, contact the local child welfare agency for direction. If you suspect abuse or neglect, use your state child-abuse and neglect reporting contact. For kinship-care programme referrals, you can also dial **211**.

8. **Handle school and medical needs factually.**  
   Tell the school, childcare provider, or medical provider that a caregiver has died and legal authority is being confirmed. Ask what documentation they need, what pickup instructions they can record, and what they need to provide care safely.

9. **Keep a basic record.**  
   Note who informed you of the death, when you were told, where the children are, who is caring for them, any immediate safety concern, and the names and contact details of people involved.

## What can wait
- Final long-term guardianship or custody arrangements usually do not need to be decided during the first shock, although urgent safety or temporary-authority steps may still be needed.
- Inheritance, life insurance, and long-term financial planning can wait.
- Family disagreements do not need to be settled immediately.
- Non-essential moves, school changes, and other major changes can wait while immediate safety and lawful authority are checked.

## Important reassurance
You do not need to solve the entire future today. The useful first steps are to keep the children safe, confirm whether a surviving legal parent or existing order is involved, and ask for the appropriate local route for any urgent gap in authority.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Longer-term custody, guardianship, and caregiving decisions depend on state law and the children’s circumstances and may need local legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice. If anyone is in immediate danger or needs emergency assistance, call 911.
