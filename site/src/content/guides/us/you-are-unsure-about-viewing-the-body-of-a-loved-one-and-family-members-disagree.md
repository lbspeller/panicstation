# What to do if… you are unsure about viewing the body of a loved one and family members disagree (USA)

## Short answer
Pause the family discussion. Contact the hospital, funeral home, or local Medical Examiner or Coroner office handling the body and ask what viewing options are available, whether any timing issue needs attention now, and who the office needs to hear from about arrangements.

## Do not do these things
- Do not agree to view only because someone is pressuring you.
- Do not pressure another person to view or not view.
- Do not assume every relative has the same wishes or the same role in making arrangements.
- Do not share images or descriptions with someone who has not asked to receive them.
- Do not make a burial or cremation decision only to end an argument.

## What to do now
1. **Pause the discussion.** Say or text: “I am not deciding this in an argument. I am taking a pause and checking the available options.”
2. **Contact the office handling the body.**
   - At a hospital, ask the operator who handles after-death arrangements.
   - At a funeral home, ask for the funeral director handling the arrangements.
   - If a Medical Examiner or Coroner office is involved, ask that office what happens next and where a viewing may be arranged.
3. **Ask for the process in plain language.** Ask:
   - “What viewing options are available?”
   - “Is there any deadline or practical step that needs attention now?”
   - “Who may request or schedule a viewing?”
   - “Do you need any documents before making arrangements?”
4. **Ask whether a gentler option is available.** You can ask about:
   - starting from the doorway,
   - keeping part of the body covered,
   - having a supportive person with you,
   - leaving immediately if you change your mind,
   - a brief private viewing, or
   - separate private times for relatives who make different choices.
5. **Keep each person’s choice separate.** Say: “You can make your choice and I can make mine. We do not need to persuade each other.” Ask staff whether separate arrangements are possible.
6. **If a Medical Examiner or Coroner office is involved, ask only for the next practical step.** Ask whether viewing is permitted there or arranged later through a funeral home, what affects release timing, and when to check back.
7. **Slow down any money pressure.** If you visit a funeral home and discuss arrangements or prices, ask for the General Price List to keep. If you are speaking by phone, ask for price information before agreeing to services.
8. **If relatives disagree about who can make arrangements, do not argue in the lobby or hallway.** Ask the provider which requests they can act on, what documents they need, and whether state or local rules affect the next step.

## What can wait
- You do not need to justify your choice to relatives today.
- You do not need to settle every funeral detail before asking about viewing options. Ask whether any immediate logistical step is required.
- You do not need to resolve long-standing family disagreements now.
- You do not need to decide whether you would attend an open-casket service, if one is planned.

## Important reassurance
This is a personal choice, not a test of love. Some people choose to view and some do not. It is reasonable to ask for time, information, and a supported option before deciding.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce pressure and keep options open. Later decisions about funeral arrangements, control of remains, or serious family disputes may need guidance from the provider and, in some cases, specialist legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, therapeutic, financial, or other professional advice. Viewing procedures and authority over arrangements can depend on state law, local procedure, provider policy, and documents signed before death. Ask the hospital, funeral home, or local Medical Examiner or Coroner office what applies in this case.
