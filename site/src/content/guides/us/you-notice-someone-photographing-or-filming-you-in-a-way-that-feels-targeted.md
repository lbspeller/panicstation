# What to do if… you notice someone photographing or filming you in a way that feels targeted (USA)

## Short answer
Create immediate physical distance by moving to a staffed or crowded area, inform security or employees of the targeted behavior, and call or text 911 if you feel threatened or followed.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t confront the individual alone or attempt to physically seize their device.
- Don’t block the person’s movement or initiate physical contact.
- Don’t follow the individual to "get proof" if it leads you to an isolated or unmonitored area.
- Don’t engage in an argument regarding legal rights or public recording laws in the moment.
- Don’t travel directly to your home or parked vehicle if you suspect you are being followed.
- Don’t upload footage of the individual to social media while still in the immediate vicinity.

## What to do now
1. **Relocate to a high-visibility, staffed environment immediately.** Move into a nearby open business, a building lobby with a reception desk, or a well-populated public area.
   - If outdoors, stand near a group of people or under visible security cameras.
   - Avoid alleys, stairwells, or empty parking structures.
2. **Alert a third party or venue staff to the situation.** Approach an employee or security guard and state clearly: "That individual is filming me in a targeted manner and I feel unsafe."
   - Request that a staff member remain with you until the individual leaves.
   - Ask for a security escort to your transportation or a different exit.
3. **Contact emergency services if you feel threatened, trapped, or followed.** Call 911 or use text-to-911 if you cannot speak safely. 
   - Provide your exact location, a description of the individual, and the direction they are moving.
   - For non-emergency reports where no immediate threat exists, call 311 or the local police non-emergency line.
4. **Identify and report "expectation of privacy" violations.** If the individual is filming under clothing (upskirting), into restrooms, changing rooms, or targeting a minor, treat the situation as an urgent criminal matter and contact 911 immediately.
5. **Activate built-in mobile safety features.** Use your smartphone's "Emergency SOS" or "Safety Check" (found in Settings) to share your live location with trusted contacts or alert local authorities without looking at the screen.
6. **Document specific details once you are at a safe distance.** Note the time, location, physical description of the person, type of device used, and any associated vehicle license plate numbers.
   - Avoid looking directly at the person while doing this; use a notepad or voice memo.
7. **Request the preservation of venue surveillance footage.** Ask management to retain CCTV footage from the specific time and location of the incident, as many systems overwrite data within 24–48 hours.
8. **Specify if the behavior is part of a pattern.** If this individual has targeted you previously, state clearly to authorities: "I am concerned about a pattern of stalking or harassment."
   - Keep a log of dates, times, and descriptions of all related incidents.

## What can wait
- You do not need to determine the specific legality of public recording in your current jurisdiction; safety takes precedence over legal analysis.
- You do not need to successfully demand that the individual deletes the footage in the moment.
- You do not need to file a full written police report before reaching a secure location.
- You do not need to decide on long-term legal action or restraining orders immediately.

## Important reassurance
Noticing targeted filming often triggers a sharp "fight or flight" response or a sense of deep intrusion. These are standard reactions to being monitored by a stranger. While recording what is plainly visible in public is often legal, behavior that crosses into intimidation, following, or capturing images where there is a reasonable expectation of privacy (like restrooms) is a matter for security or law enforcement. You are entitled to take steps to ensure your own safety and seek assistance from those around you.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization and harm-reduction actions for an immediate encounter. If the filming is persistent or involves someone known to you, additional safety planning with a victim advocate or law enforcement is necessary.

## Important note
This information is for safety guidance and does not constitute legal advice. Federal laws like the TAKE IT DOWN Act (2026) provide pathways for removing non-consensual intimate imagery from platforms within 48 hours.
