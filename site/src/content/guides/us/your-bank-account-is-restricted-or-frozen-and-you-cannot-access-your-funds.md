# What to do if… your bank account is restricted or frozen and you cannot access your funds (USA)

## Short answer
Contact the bank only through an official channel, ask what they need in writing, and protect essentials by arranging another way to receive income and pay urgent bills.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t respond to unexpected calls, texts, or emails claiming to be the bank; use the number on your card, the bank’s official app, or the bank’s official website.
- Don’t submit altered, incomplete, or “cleaned up” documents.
- Don’t route money through someone else to work around the restriction.
- Don’t keep sending different versions of the same documents unless the bank asks for them.
- Don’t ignore rent, mortgage, utilities, insurance, car payments, food, or medication costs; contact those providers early and keep notes.

## What to do now
1. **List what must be covered today and in the next few days.** Write down rent or mortgage, utilities, insurance, car payment, food, medication, childcare, incoming pay or benefits, and any cash or other account you can already use.
2. **Contact the bank’s fraud, account review, or compliance team through an official channel.** Ask:
   - “What documents or information do you need to complete the review?”
   - “How do I submit them securely?”
   - “Is this an internal review, or is there a legal or third-party restriction?”
   - “Is any limited access possible for essential living expenses?”
3. **Get the request in writing and get a reference number.** Ask for the checklist by secure message, letter, or another official bank channel. Write down the date, time, department, and representative name or ID.
4. **Send one clear document pack through the bank’s secure route.** The bank may ask for government ID, proof of address, an explanation of recent large or unusual transactions, or proof of source of funds such as pay stubs, benefits letters, invoices, contracts, sale paperwork, or inheritance paperwork. Send only what the bank asks for, and keep proof that you submitted it.
5. **Protect urgent bills today.** Contact your landlord, mortgage servicer, utility providers, insurer, car lender, and any other essential biller. Say: “My bank has temporarily restricted my account during a compliance review. I am working on it. Can you note the account and tell me what short pause, payment arrangement, or fee hold may be available?”
6. **Redirect incoming money if you can.** Ask payroll to switch direct deposit to another account in your name. If the switch cannot happen in time, ask whether a paper check or other employer-approved payment method is available for the next cycle. For benefits or other deposits, contact the payer through its official route to update payment details.
7. **Apply for or use another account in your own name for essentials if you can.** Consider a different bank or credit union account so wages, benefits, and essential payments are not trapped while the review continues.
8. **Send a short written complaint to the bank.** Include when the restriction started, what funds you cannot access, urgent hardship effects, what documents you submitted, and what you need now: a clear checklist, a realistic update route, and whether limited access for essentials is possible.
9. **Use external complaint routes if the bank does not give a workable next step.** The CFPB accepts complaints about checking and savings accounts. You can also look up the bank or credit union’s regulator and file with the regulator that applies. For credit unions, use the NCUA process. For banks supervised by the Federal Reserve, Federal Reserve Consumer Help has a complaint route. If you are unsure where to file, start with CFPB, USAGov, or the bank regulator lookup page.
10. **Keep one proof folder.** Save the restriction start date, affected amounts, bank messages, document requests, what you submitted, complaint copies, confirmation numbers, and a simple timeline of every call or message.

## What can wait
- Deciding whether to switch banks permanently.
- Arguing about why the review happened before you know what the bank needs.
- Posting publicly about the bank before you have your document trail.
- Threatening legal action before you have the bank’s written requests and complaint record.
- Paying non-essential subscriptions or non-urgent debts before food, housing, utilities, medication, transport, and insurance are covered.

## Important reassurance
This is a real financial emergency, and it makes sense to feel alarmed. A restricted account does not automatically mean your money is gone; some reviews move forward once the bank receives clear information and you keep a consistent record.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce immediate harm, keep essentials covered, and push the review forward. Later decisions may need specialist help depending on what the bank or regulator says.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, tax, therapeutic, or professional advice. Banks and regulators may be limited in what they can explain. If you face imminent eviction, utility shutoff, loss of insurance, lack of food, or lack of medication, tell the bank, billers, and any complaint agency that it is urgent hardship.
