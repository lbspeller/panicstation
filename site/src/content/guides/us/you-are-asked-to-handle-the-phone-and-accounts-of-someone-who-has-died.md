# What to do if… you are asked to handle the phone and accounts of someone who has died (USA)

## Short answer
Secure the phone and pause account changes. Do not try passwords, delete anything, or act as the person who died; first identify who is handling the estate and use each provider's official route.

## Do not do these things
- Do not guess passcodes or passwords. Repeated attempts may trigger lockouts or security alerts.
- Do not pose as the person who died when contacting banks, carriers, platforms, or government offices.
- Do not delete emails, texts, apps, photos, or account data to tidy things up.
- Do not share the person's Social Security number, identification documents, or account details in group messages or informal email chains.
- Do not cancel services from inside apps until the person handling the estate has checked what needs to be preserved or transferred.

## What to do now
1. **Secure the phone.** Put it somewhere safe and keep it charged. If it is already unlocked, do not open messages, apps, or account settings. Note only details that are visible without digging, such as the phone number or carrier name.
2. **Record urgent warning signs without exploring the device.** Note the date, who gave you the phone, where it is stored, and whether visible notifications show repeated login attempts, unexpected two-factor codes, or new-device alerts.
3. **Identify the person handling the estate.** Ask whether someone has documents showing that they are the executor, administrator, personal representative, or other person authorized to handle estate matters. Provider rules and state law may affect what they can do.
4. **Contact the mobile carrier through its official support route.** Explain that the account holder died. Ask what options apply for protecting the number, stopping unauthorized use, dealing with billing, and preserving or transferring the number. Ask what documents are required before requesting changes.
5. **Check whether Social Security notification is already being handled.** Funeral homes generally notify the Social Security Administration. If no funeral home was involved, or the funeral home did not report the death, call the Social Security Administration using the official route in the resources below.
6. **Use official provider routes for online accounts.**
   - **Google:** use the deceased-user request route. Do not try to log in as the person who died.
   - **Apple:** if there is a Legacy Contact access key, follow Apple's official Legacy Contact instructions. Otherwise, use Apple's deceased-account request route and check what documentation applies.
   - **Facebook:** use the official memorialization or removal route instead of trying to log in.
7. **Respond to suspected identity misuse carefully.** If visible alerts or incoming notices suggest someone is trying to use the person's identity, save a simple record of what you saw and tell the person handling the estate. They can follow the USAGov guidance for notifying credit bureaus and use IdentityTheft.gov if identity theft appears to be happening.

## What can wait
- You do not need to unlock the phone today.
- You do not need to find every password or close every subscription immediately.
- You do not need to decide today what should happen to photos, messages, social profiles, or the phone number.
- You do not need to take over account access just because relatives are asking for quick action.

## Important reassurance
Keeping the phone safe and making fewer changes is useful progress. It is reasonable to slow down while the person handling the estate checks the correct provider routes.

## Scope note
These are first steps only to prevent irreversible mistakes and buy time. Later decisions may need help from the person handling the estate, the relevant provider, or a lawyer familiar with the state where the estate is being handled.

## Important note
This guide is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or other professional advice. State law and provider rules vary. Preserve the phone and use official support routes rather than attempting account access.
