# What to do if… a pending transaction disappears and then reappears later for a higher amount (USA)

## Short answer
Verify if the transaction status has changed from **Pending** to **Posted**, identify if it is a standard merchant adjustment (like a tip or hotel hold), and if the amount is truly incorrect, initiate a formal dispute with your card issuer immediately.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume it is a fraudulent double charge if one entry is listed as "Pending" and the other as "Posted" / "Settled."
- Don’t wait for the next billing cycle to address a significant discrepancy in the final amount.
- Don’t discard the physical receipt, signed tip slip, or digital order confirmation.
- Don’t cancel the card as a first step for a recognized merchant error, as this can complicate the refund process.
- Don’t ignore the risk of overdraft or "frozen" funds if using a debit card for large holds.
- Don’t ignore the specific "Billing Inquiries" address on your statement when sending formal written disputes.

## What to do now
1. **Identify the transaction status and capture visual evidence.**  
   **Open your banking app and check if the transaction is labeled as "Pending," "Processing," "Posted," "Cleared," or "Settled."**
   - Take a screenshot of the transaction details, including the merchant name, date, and any reference numbers.
   - Note if a previous smaller "Pending" charge has vanished while a larger "Posted" one has appeared.

2. **Evaluate common merchant adjustment triggers.**  
   **Determine if the higher amount matches standard industry practices for temporary authorizations.**
   - **Restaurants:** Final amounts often include the tip, which is added after the initial card swipe.
   - **Gas Stations:** Paying at the pump can trigger a preauthorization hold of up to $175.
   - **Hotels / Rentals:** Initial holds often include a security deposit or "incidental" buffer ($50–$200+ per night).
   - **International Purchases:** Final amounts may fluctuate due to currency exchange rates and bank fees.

3. **Contact the merchant directly to request a correction.**  
   **If the amount is definitely incorrect (e.g., a $50 charge posted as $500), call the merchant’s billing department first.**
   - Request an itemized receipt or a copy of the signed authorization.
   - Ask them to "void" or "reverse" the incorrect amount and issue a new, correct charge.
   - Get a timeline for when the reversal will reflect in your account.

4. **Initiate a formal dispute for credit card billing errors (FCBA).**  
   **For credit card discrepancies, you must act within 60 days of the statement date containing the error.**
   - **Send a written dispute letter to the specific "Billing Inquiries" address listed on your statement (not the payment address).**
   - State your name, account number, the disputed amount, and why it is incorrect.
   - **Pay the undisputed portion of your bill** on time to avoid interest or late fees on the rest of your balance.

5. **Report debit card errors immediately (Regulation E).**  
   **Notify your bank or credit union as soon as you identify a discrepancy to limit your liability.**
   - Inform them that the "Electronic Fund Transfer" (EFT) was for the wrong amount.
   - If the bank takes longer than 10 business days to investigate, ask if you are eligible for a "provisional credit" while the case is reviewed.

6. **Bypass future high holds at gas stations.**  
   **If you need to avoid a $175 hold on your available balance, pay the cashier inside the station for a specific amount.**
   - This authorizes only the exact amount you intend to pump and prevents large, multi-day holds.

7. **Freeze the card if you do not recognize the merchant.**  
   **If a disappearing/reappearing charge is from a merchant you have never visited, use your app to "Lock" or "Freeze" the card immediately.**
   - Follow the issuer's procedures for reporting a stolen or compromised card number.

## What can wait
- You do not need to close your entire bank account unless there is evidence of total identity theft.
- You do not need to replace every autopay service while a single merchant dispute is being investigated.
- You do not need to worry if a hotel hold is still "Pending" for 3–5 days; many take up to 30 days to fully drop off.

## Important reassurance
It is very common for transactions to "disappear" for 24–48 hours while the banking system switches a charge from an authorization hold to a final settled amount. This process is frequently used by hotels, gas stations, and restaurants to ensure funds are available before the final total is calculated.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for financial transaction discrepancies. It does not cover long-term credit repair, tax implications of business spending, or complex identity theft recovery.

## Important note
Federal protections (FCBA and Regulation E) have strict timelines and procedural requirements, such as sending written notice to specific addresses. Policies vary by financial institution; always check your specific cardholder agreement for exact dispute instructions.
