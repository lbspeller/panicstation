# What to do if… a body donation is declined and you need an alternative funeral plan quickly (USA)

## Short answer
Secure immediate care of the body first: confirm where the person is, whether they are refrigerated, and who can release them. Then ask a funeral home for transfer into care and the simplest workable options, such as direct cremation or immediate burial.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t assume pre-registration guarantees acceptance; donation programs usually make a final decision after death based on screening, logistics, legal authority, and current need.
- Don’t authorize embalming just to buy time unless you understand the cost and whether it could affect any remaining donation option.
- Don’t pause body care while you call multiple donation programs; someone still needs to arrange custody, transport, and refrigeration.
- Don’t sign a funeral contract while unclear about transfer, refrigeration, permits, death certificates, crematory or cemetery fees, and what is not included.
- Don’t let several relatives give conflicting instructions; choose one spokesperson who has legal authority under your state’s rules or a written appointment.

## What to do now
1. **Confirm where the person is and whether they are refrigerated.**  
   Call the hospital, hospice, nursing facility, donation organization, or current holder of the body. Ask: “Where are they now, are they refrigerated, and how long can they stay there while we arrange transfer?”

2. **Ask the donation program what happens next, but do not wait on them before arranging care.**  
   Ask for brief confirmation of the decline if they can provide it quickly. Also ask whether they arranged any transport, whether they will cover any transport already started, and whether another program is realistically possible from the current location and timing.

3. **Call a funeral home and use a simple script.**  
   Say: “Whole-body donation was declined. We need transfer into your care, refrigeration, and the simplest options.” Ask for:
   - the earliest transfer time
   - refrigeration arrangements and any daily fee
   - direct cremation and immediate burial prices
   - what permits, death-certificate filing, crematory fees, cemetery fees, or third-party charges are not included
   - an itemized estimate in writing before you agree, if possible

4. **Ask whether a coroner or medical examiner must authorize release.**  
   If the death was sudden, unattended, accidental, suspected suicide, violent, unclear, or otherwise reportable, a coroner or medical examiner may control timing and release. Ask the current holder of the body or the funeral home: “Is this a coroner or medical examiner case, and who can authorize release?”

5. **Make only the minimum decision needed to stop the time pressure.**  
   You do not need to plan a full service today. For now, decide only whether to ask for a basic cremation option, a basic burial option, or a short hold while you confirm one more donation possibility.

6. **If you want to try one more donation program, make it a parallel call.**  
   Have ready: name, age, place and time of death, current location of the body, whether the body has been embalmed, whether an autopsy or medical examiner review is involved, and any known infectious-disease or weight-screening issue. If they cannot clearly say they can take custody now, keep the funeral-home plan moving.

7. **If cost is the immediate blocker, ask for the lowest-cost basic option today.**  
   Ask the funeral home for its lowest-cost direct cremation or immediate burial option and what charges could still be added. Also call the county or city where the death occurred and ask whether any public or indigent disposition assistance exists, what proof is required, and whether it is available in this situation.

## What can wait
- You do not need to plan a memorial service, obituary, flowers, reception, or travel today.
- You do not need to settle every disagreement about what the person would have wanted before arranging safe body care.
- You do not need to handle estate paperwork immediately unless it affects who can authorize disposition.
- You do not need to call every relative now; share the minimum needed to avoid conflicting instructions.

## Important reassurance
A decline can happen even after registration or planning, and it does not mean you failed the person. Moving to a simple backup plan is a responsible way to protect dignity, reduce delay, and avoid rushed expensive choices.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the first day or two after a body donation plan falls through. Later decisions about authority, disputes, interstate transport, benefits, investigations, burial plots, or estate issues may need help from the funeral home, hospital social worker, county office, or a lawyer in your state.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, medical, financial, funeral, therapeutic, or other professional advice. Rules and available services vary by state, county, provider, and the circumstances of the death. Ask the funeral home or relevant local office to explain options slowly and confirm costs and responsibilities in writing.
