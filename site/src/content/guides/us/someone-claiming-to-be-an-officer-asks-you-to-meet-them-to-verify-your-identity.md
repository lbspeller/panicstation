# What to do if… someone claiming to be an officer asks you to meet them to verify your identity (USA)
## Short answer
Do not go to a meeting or share personal details until you have independently verified the contact. End the conversation and use an official agency number that you find yourself. Call 911 if you feel in immediate danger.

## Do not do these things
- Do not go to a meeting place until you have independently confirmed the contact through an official agency number.
- Do not call back the number they used or rely on a number they gave you. Caller ID can be spoofed.
- Do not send photos of your driver’s license, passport, Social Security number, or a selfie with ID.
- Do not confirm your address, date of birth, workplace, bank details, or other personal information because the person already knows some facts about you.
- Do not act on threats of arrest or claims that you must verify yourself immediately without independently checking the contact.
- Do not pay money, buy gift cards, transfer funds, send cryptocurrency, or hand cash to a courier.

## What to do now
1. **End the contact.** Say that you will verify the request independently, then hang up or stop replying.
2. **Save what you already have.** Keep the message, email, phone number, voicemail, or screenshot. Note the claimed name, badge number, agency, and case number if they are already available. Only ask for identifiers if it feels safe to do so.
3. **Find the agency’s official contact details yourself.** Use the agency’s official website or another official government source. For a local police department or sheriff’s office, call the independently found main or non-emergency number and ask the agency to verify the contact.
4. **Use 911 for immediate danger.** If the person is threatening you, waiting outside, following you, or making you feel unsafe right now, call 911.
5. **Keep your door closed while you verify if it is safe to do so.** If someone comes to your home claiming to be an officer, tell them through the closed door that you are verifying the contact. Move away from the door if needed. Call 911 if you feel in immediate danger.
6. **Verify federal contacts independently.** If the person claims to be from the FBI, use the FBI field-office directory to find an official office number. For another federal agency, use that agency’s official website.
7. **Ask whether any meeting is actually needed.** If the agency confirms the contact, ask how to proceed safely and whether an official agency location is appropriate.
8. **Act quickly if you shared information or money.**
   - Report the suspected scam to the FTC.
   - Use IdentityTheft.gov if you shared your Social Security number or suspect identity theft.
   - Contact your bank, card issuer, or payment provider immediately if money or account details were involved.
   - Consider filing an IC3 complaint if the scam involved email, text, online messaging, or another internet-based contact route.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove your identity to an unexpected caller in real time.
- You do not need to decide immediately whether to meet, answer questions, or provide documents.
- You do not need to argue about the claim on the phone. Independent verification comes first.

## Important reassurance
Scammers can use urgency, official-sounding language, real names, and spoofed caller ID. Pausing and checking through an independently found official channel is a reasonable response.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce risk and prevent irreversible mistakes. Later decisions may need help from a lawyer, the relevant agency, or an identity-theft support resource.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, or professional advice. If you feel threatened or unsafe, prioritize immediate safety and contact emergency services.
