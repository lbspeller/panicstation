# What to do if… you receive a notice that unpaid fines will be sent to enforcement agents unless you act immediately (USA)

## Short answer
Stop and do not pay or click any links; instead, independently find the official website of the court or agency named and contact their clerk directly to verify if the case actually exists.

## Do not do these things
- Don't click links, download attachments, or scan QR codes provided in a text or email notice.
- Don't call the phone number provided in the notice or use "click-to-call" features.
- Don't pay using gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfers, Zelle, or Venmo.
- Don't go to a "government kiosk" or Bitcoin ATM as instructed by a caller.
- Don't provide your full Social Security number or bank login details to "lookup" the fine on a site you didn't navigate to yourself.
- Don't stay on a call with someone who uses aggressive threats of immediate arrest.

## What to do now
1. **Isolate the message and identify observable red flags.** Look for sensory cues that indicate a scam, such as a ".com" or ".org" URL for a government notice (which should be ".gov"), the use of high-pressure language ("must pay within 1 hour"), or requests for untraceable payment methods.
2. **Preserve a record of the communication without interacting with it.** Take a screenshot of the text or email, ensuring the sender's phone number or email address is visible.
3. **Navigate manually to the official government or court website.** Use a search engine to find the official site for the specific court or agency (e.g., "Los Angeles Superior Court" or "Central Violations Bureau" for federal tickets).
   - Look for the ".gov" domain extension to ensure the site is legitimate.
   - Find the "Clerk of Court" or "Payments" department phone number directly on that site.
4. **Speak with an official representative to verify the debt.** Call the verified number and provide the case or ticket number from your notice to ask if it is a real entry in their system.
   - If the ticket was for a federal site (military base, national park), contact the Central Violations Bureau (CVB).
   - Ask specifically if the agency ever sends payment demands via text or email (most courts only use U.S. Mail).
5. **Request a written validation notice if the notice came from a private debt collector.** Under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA), you have 30 days from the first notice to dispute the debt in writing.
   - If you do not dispute it within this 30-day window, the collector is legally allowed to assume the debt is valid.
6. **Report the incident to the appropriate fraud authorities.** Use the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) reporting portal at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.
   - If you shared financial info or lost money, file a report at ic3.gov (the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center).
   - Notify your bank immediately if you provided card or account details.

## What can wait
- You don't need to determine if you actually committed the violation right now; the first step is only confirming if the notice itself is a legal reality.
- You don't need to hire an attorney or plan a legal defense until the existence of a legitimate court case is verified.
- You don't need to worry about "immediate" arrest; real warrants are not resolved by telephone payments or Bitcoin transfers.

## Important reassurance
It is normal to feel a sudden surge of panic when threatened with arrest or enforcement agents. Scammers specifically design these notices to exploit your respect for the law. Taking ten minutes to verify through a secondary, official channel is a standard safety procedure, not a delay of justice.

## Scope note
This guide is for first-step stabilization and verification only. It does not provide legal advice for fighting legitimate fines or managing long-term debt collection.

## Important note
Government agencies and courts in the USA do not typically initiate contact about fines via text or email, and they never demand payment via gift cards or cryptocurrency. Always verify court business through the official Clerk of Court in the relevant jurisdiction.
