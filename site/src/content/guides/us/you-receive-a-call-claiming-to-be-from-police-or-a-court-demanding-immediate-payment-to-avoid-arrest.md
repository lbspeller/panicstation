# What to do if… you receive a call claiming to be from police or a court demanding immediate payment to avoid arrest (USA)

## Short answer
Hang up immediately and do not provide any payment or personal information. Contact the official agency directly using a verified number you find yourself on a government (.gov) website to confirm your status.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay using gift cards, cryptocurrency, Bitcoin ATMs, wire transfers (Western Union/MoneyGram), or peer-to-peer apps like Zelle, Venmo, or Cash App.
- Do not stay on the phone if the caller demands you remain connected while traveling to a bank or store.
- Do not trust caller ID; scammers use "spoofing" technology to make official department names and numbers appear on your screen.
- Do not provide your Social Security number, date of birth, or bank account details to "verify" your identity over the phone.
- Do not agree to a video call or "digital arrest" where the caller insists on watching you via camera.
- Do not download "official" attachments or click links sent via text or email.

## What to do now
1. **End the call without further engagement.** **If the caller is aggressive or threatening, disconnect without speaking.**
2. **Write down any details you remember immediately after hanging up.** **Note the agency claimed (e.g., FBI, U.S. Marshals, Local Sheriff), the name or badge number provided, and the specific payment method demanded.**
3. **Locate the official contact information for the agency independently.** **Use a search engine to find the official city, county, or federal .gov website.**
   - For local police or sheriff departments, call the non-emergency line.
   - For federal agencies, look up the local field office (e.g., FBI field offices or U.S. Marshals district offices).
   - For court or jury duty issues, call the Clerk of Court for your jurisdiction.
4. **Ask the official representative to check for active warrants or cases in your name.** **State clearly that you received a suspicious call and want to verify your legal standing.**
5. **Contact your financial institution if you shared any data or sent money.** **Call the number on the back of your card immediately to report fraudulent activity and request a freeze on transfers.**
6. **Report the incident to the Federal Trade Commission (FTC).** **Submit a report at ReportFraud.ftc.gov under the "government impersonator" category.**
7. **File a complaint with the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3).** **Go to ic3.gov to report the details of the communication, especially if digital payment methods were mentioned.**
8. **Call the Elder Justice Hotline if you or the victim is aged 60 or older.** **Dial 1-833-FRAUD-11 (1-833-372-8311) for specialized assistance with government imposter scams.**

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove your innocence to the caller or provide "evidence" that you did not commit a crime.
- You do not need to change your phone number immediately; blocking the specific number is sufficient for now.
- You do not need to visit a police station in person unless directed to do so by a verified official you contacted yourself.

## Important reassurance
It is a standard tactic for scammers to use fear and urgency to bypass logical thinking. Real law enforcement and court officials in the United States do not call citizens to demand immediate payment over the phone to avoid arrest. Hanging up is the recommended safety protocol and does not make you "guilty" or increase your legal risk.

## Scope note
This guide provides first-step stabilization for handling a potential impersonation scam. It does not replace legal advice or formal court procedures if a legitimate legal issue exists.

## Important note
Government agencies will never demand payment via gift card, cryptocurrency, or wire transfer. If you believe there is a physical threat to your safety, call 911.
