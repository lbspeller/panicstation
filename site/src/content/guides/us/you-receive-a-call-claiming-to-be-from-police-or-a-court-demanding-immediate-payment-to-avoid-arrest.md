# What to do if… you receive a call claiming to be from police or a court demanding immediate payment to avoid arrest (USA)

## Short answer
Hang up and do not pay. Verify only by contacting the real police department or court using an official website or number you find yourself.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay by gift card, cryptocurrency, Bitcoin ATM, wire transfer, cash deposit, payment app, or any link sent during the call.
- Do not share your Social Security number, date of birth, address, banking details, passwords, or one-time verification codes.
- Do not treat caller ID, a badge number, a case number, your address, or an official-sounding name as proof.
- Do not stay on the phone while you go to the bank, buy cards, use an ATM, or move money.
- Do not open emailed or texted court documents, warrants, links, QR codes, or attachments from the caller.
- Do not call back the number the caller gives you.

## What to do now
1. **End the call.** Say, “I’m going to verify this through official channels.” Then hang up.
2. **Put a pause between the threat and your money.** Write down: “No payment until verified.” Keep it in front of you while you take the next steps.
3. **Verify through a contact you choose, not the caller.**
   - If they claimed to be local police or a sheriff’s office, find the agency’s official website and call the non-emergency number. Ask how to verify whether there is any real matter connected to you.
   - If they claimed to be a court, jury office, clerk, marshal, or judge, use the official court website or the U.S. Courts court finder for federal courts. Ask the clerk’s office how real notices are delivered and whether there is a legitimate issue you need to handle.
   - If you believe someone is in immediate danger, call 911.
4. **Save details without replying.** Keep the date and time, the number shown, any voicemail, text, email, QR code, payment request, names used, agency or court claimed, and screenshots.
5. **If you paid, contact the payment route now.**
   - For a bank transfer, debit card, credit card, wire transfer, or payment app, contact the bank, card issuer, wire company, or app through its official app or the number on your card. Say it was a scam and ask whether the payment can be stopped or reversed.
   - For gift cards, contact the gift card company, say the card was used in a scam, and keep the card and receipt.
   - For cryptocurrency or a Bitcoin ATM, contact the company or exchange used and say it was a fraudulent transaction. Recovery may be difficult, but report it quickly.
6. **If you shared account access, passwords, or codes, secure the most important accounts first.** Start with email, banking, payment apps, and phone provider accounts. Change passwords from a device you trust and turn on multi-factor authentication.
7. **Report it.**
   - Report the scam to the FTC.
   - Report to the FBI’s IC3 if the scam involved online payments, cryptocurrency, email, text messages, QR codes, websites, apps, or other internet-enabled activity.
   - You can also tell the real police department or court clerk’s office what name, number, and payment demand the scammer used.

## What can wait
- You do not need to prove anything to the caller, send documents, or keep talking to clear it up.
- You do not need to decide today whether to change your phone number.
- You do not need to post publicly, confront the scammer, or answer more calls from them.
- Focus first on verifying through official contacts, protecting accounts, and reporting.

## Important reassurance
These scams are designed to sound official and trigger panic with threats of arrest. Hanging up and verifying through official contacts is the safe move; real courts and law enforcement do not demand immediate payment by gift card, cryptocurrency, payment app, or wire transfer to stop an arrest.

## Scope note
These are first steps only, meant to prevent immediate loss and buy time. If you later confirm there is a real court or legal issue, handle it through verified court or police contacts and consider qualified legal help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, therapeutic, or professional advice. If you believe you are in immediate danger, call 911.
