# What to do if… you suspect a partner is tampering with your contraception or birth control (USA)

## Short answer
Put safety and privacy first, then ask for private reproductive health care as soon as you can if pregnancy or STI risk may have happened. You do not have to prove sabotage before getting medical care or speaking with a domestic violence or sexual assault advocate.

## Do not do these things
- Do not confront them or accuse them if that could increase your risk.
- Do not use shared devices, shared accounts, shared phone plans, or a monitored laptop for help-seeking if that could be unsafe.
- Do not leave birth control, condoms, receipts, insurance papers, clinic paperwork, or pharmacy bags where they can find them.
- Do not rely on “clearing history” as a full safety measure; internet and phone use can sometimes still be visible.
- Do not delay care because you are unsure whether it “counts.”
- Do not risk your safety to collect proof.

## What to do now
1. **Get to a safer pause.** If you are in immediate danger or need urgent emergency help, call **911**. If you can, move to a place where you can think and communicate more privately, such as outside, a bathroom, a clinic, a trusted person’s home, or another public place.
2. **Use safer communications.** Use a phone or device they cannot access, such as a trusted friend’s phone, a work phone, a clinic phone, or a library computer. If you must use your own device, assume it may be monitored and choose the safest contact method available to you.
3. **Ask for private reproductive health care.**
   - Contact a reproductive health clinic, OB-GYN, family planning clinic, urgent care clinic, or another trusted health care provider.
   - Say: “I’m worried my partner may be interfering with contraception, and I need private help with emergency contraception, STI risk, and birth control options.”
   - Ask how they can protect your privacy, including appointment reminders, portals, prescriptions, billing, insurance notices, after-visit summaries, and phone messages.
4. **Ask about emergency contraception as soon as possible if pregnancy risk may have happened.** Some options are time-sensitive. A clinic or pharmacist can help you choose what is available and appropriate for you.
5. **Ask about STI care if condom interference, condom removal, or forced unprotected sex may have happened.** A clinician can advise on testing and any time-sensitive prevention or treatment options.
6. **Reduce access if it is safe.** Keep contraception, condoms, medication, test kits, and clinic paperwork somewhere your partner cannot access, such as with a trusted person, in a locked bag, at work, or outside the home. Avoid sudden changes if they might notice and escalate.
7. **Contact specialist support.**
   - National Domestic Violence Hotline: call **1-800-799-SAFE (7233)**, text **START** to **88788**, or use online chat if that is safer.
   - RAINN National Sexual Assault Hotline: call **800-656-HOPE (4673)**, text **HOPE** to **64673**, or use online chat.
8. **If you may want a record later, keep it private and simple.** You can write down what you noticed and when, but only in a place they cannot access. Do not use a monitored phone, shared cloud account, or shared notebook.

## What can wait
- You do not have to decide right now whether to end the relationship, move out, confront them, or report to law enforcement.
- You do not have to gather proof before getting care or support.
- You do not need to explain everything perfectly. You can say, “I’m not sure, but I’m worried.”
- You can make longer-term decisions after you have had a safer, private conversation with a clinician or advocate.

## Important reassurance
Feeling unsure, frozen, embarrassed, or afraid does not mean you are overreacting. Advocates and clinics can help even when you are not sure exactly what happened.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation, protect your health, and connect with specialist support. Later decisions may need tailored help from a clinician, advocate, or legal professional.

## Important note
This guide is general information only. It is not legal, medical, financial, therapeutic, or other professional advice.
