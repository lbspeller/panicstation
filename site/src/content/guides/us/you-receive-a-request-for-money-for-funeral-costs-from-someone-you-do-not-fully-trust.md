# What to do if… you receive a request for money for funeral costs from someone you do not fully trust (USA)

## Short answer
Do not send money yet. Pause, verify the death and funeral details independently, and only help through a route that is harder to redirect, such as paying the funeral provider directly through its official payment method.

## Do not do these things
- Do not pay because the message is urgent, emotional, or threatening, such as “the service will be canceled unless you pay now.”
- Do not use wire transfer, gift cards, cryptocurrency, cash pickup, or peer-to-peer payment apps with someone you do not fully trust.
- Do not click payment links, invoice links, shortened links, or login links sent by the requester.
- Do not share one-time passcodes, bank login details, Social Security number, photos of ID, or personal details “to prove it’s you.”
- Do not keep negotiating in real time if the person is pressuring you. Pressure is a reason to pause.

## What to do now
1. **Send one calm boundary line, then stop the back-and-forth.**  
   Example: *“I’m sorry for your loss. I can’t send money immediately. I verify funeral payments before I pay.”*

2. **Ask for details you can check in writing.**  
   Ask for the deceased person’s full name, the funeral home name, the funeral home city and state, the service date if known, and a written invoice or account statement from the funeral provider.

3. **Verify the funeral home independently.**  
   Do not use the phone number or link in the request. Search for the funeral home yourself, call its main public number, and ask whether there is a legitimate account or payment route for that person’s arrangements. The funeral home may be limited in what it can confirm, but it can tell you how legitimate payments are usually made.

4. **Check whether the story matches public or family information.**  
   Look for a funeral home obituary page, a notice from a known family member, or confirmation from someone you already trust. Do not rely only on a social media post, a copied obituary, or a screenshot.

5. **If you still want to help, choose a safer payment route.**
   - Pay the funeral home directly through its official website, phone line, or in-person payment route.
   - Ask the funeral home whether you can apply the payment to a named account without sending money to an individual.
   - If it is a fundraiser, check who created it, who receives the funds, whether the platform explains fees and refunds, and whether the organizer is someone you can independently identify.
   - A credit card may give you a clearer route to ask about a dispute than cash, gift cards, wire transfer, cryptocurrency, or peer-to-peer payment apps.

6. **If you already sent money, act quickly.**  
   Contact your bank, card issuer, payment app, wire transfer company, or cryptocurrency platform immediately. Say you suspect fraud and ask whether the payment can be canceled, reversed, disputed, frozen, or traced. Save any case numbers, timestamps, and names of people you speak with.

7. **Preserve the evidence without engaging further.**  
   Screenshot the messages, usernames, phone numbers, email addresses, payment handles, links, invoices, and transaction receipts. Do not keep clicking links or downloading files.

8. **Report it if it seems suspicious or you lost money.**  
   Report fraud to the FTC. If the request used the internet, email, social media, a payment app, or another online route, you can also report it to the FBI’s Internet Crime Complaint Center. If you are being threatened, extorted, or told someone will come to your home, contact local law enforcement using a verified non-emergency number, or call 911 if there is immediate danger.

## What can wait
- You do not need to decide tonight whether the person is lying or whether the relationship is over.
- You do not need to explain yourself beyond “I verify before I pay.”
- You do not need to post publicly, accuse anyone, or investigate the whole situation before making the safe choice to pause.
- You can still help later if the request is legitimate.

## Important reassurance
A request connected to death can feel urgent because grief makes people want to help quickly. Pausing to verify is not cruel or suspicious; it protects your money and makes it more likely that any help reaches the right place.

## Scope note
These are first steps to reduce pressure and prevent an irreversible payment. Later decisions about family conflict, legal options, bank disputes, or identity protection may need specialist help.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, security, or professional advice. If you feel unsafe, stop contact and use verified routes to reach your bank, payment provider, local law enforcement, or a trusted support person.
