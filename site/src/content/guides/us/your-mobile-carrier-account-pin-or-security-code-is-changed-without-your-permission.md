# What to do if… your mobile carrier account PIN or security code is changed without your permission (USA)

## Short answer
Treat this as a possible phone-number takeover. Use another phone or trusted Wi-Fi to contact your wireless carrier through an official route, tell them your account PIN or security code was changed without your authorization, and ask them to stop any SIM change or number transfer now.

## Do not do these things
- Don’t call a phone number from a suspicious text or email; use your carrier’s official website, app, bill, or store locator.
- Don’t keep requesting SMS verification codes for important accounts right now; if your number is being taken over, those codes may go to someone else.
- Don’t share one-time codes, transfer PINs, account PINs, passwords, or security answers with anyone who contacts you unexpectedly.
- Don’t repeatedly guess the PIN or push reset flows if they are failing; it can lock you out and slow the carrier’s fraud review.
- Don’t assume it is harmless because your phone still works; a PIN or security-code change can happen before a SIM change or port-out is completed.

## What to do now
1. Get to a safer way to communicate. If possible, connect to trusted Wi-Fi and use a different phone, such as a friend’s phone, landline, or work phone, so you are not relying only on the possibly compromised mobile line.

2. Contact your carrier immediately through an official route. Say: “My account PIN or security code was changed without my authorization. I need fraud or account security support for a possible SIM swap or port-out.”

3. Ask the carrier to stop changes on the account. Ask them to place any available port-out, number-transfer, SIM-change, eSIM-change, or account-takeover protections on the line, and ask them to reverse the unauthorized PIN or security-code change if they can verify you safely.

4. Ask what happened and what is pending. Ask whether there has been a SIM change, eSIM activation, number transfer request, transfer PIN request, email change, address change, device order, or new authorized user. Ask for the date, time, case number, and what the carrier needs from you to regain control.

5. Secure the accounts that can unlock other accounts, from a trusted device. Start with email, banking, Apple ID or Google account, and your password manager. Change passwords to unique passwords, sign out other sessions where offered, and move two-step verification away from SMS to an authenticator app or security key where available.

6. Contact banks, card issuers, and payment apps if the number is used for money access. Tell them your phone number may be compromised, ask them to watch for unusual transfers or new payees, confirm your contact details were not changed, and ask what extra verification can be placed on the account.

7. Start a short written log. Record the date and time you noticed the change, carrier case numbers, who you contacted, what was changed, and any account access or money movement you find.

8. Use IdentityTheft.gov if accounts were accessed, money moved, or new accounts were opened. It can help you build a recovery plan and documentation trail for identity theft.

9. If identity misuse is possible, consider a credit freeze or fraud alert after the carrier and financial accounts are stabilized. A credit freeze or fraud alert can help reduce new-account identity theft, but it will not by itself fix the mobile account takeover.

## What can wait
- You don’t need to secure every account in one sitting; carrier, email, banking, and main device accounts come first.
- You don’t need to decide today whether to change your phone number permanently.
- You don’t need to identify or confront the attacker.
- You don’t need to file every possible report before stabilizing the number and your money access.
- You don’t need to replace all devices unless there are separate signs that a device itself is compromised.

## Important reassurance
This is a serious warning sign, but it is also exactly the kind of situation where quick, narrow steps can reduce harm. Focus on stopping carrier changes, protecting email and money access, and keeping a simple record.

## Scope note
These are first steps to stabilize the situation and buy time. Later decisions, such as longer-term account hardening, disputes, reimbursement, credit protection, or complaints, may need help from the carrier, financial institutions, official recovery resources, or a qualified specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, financial, cybersecurity, or professional advice. Use official carrier, bank, FTC, FCC, and IdentityTheft.gov routes for decisions about your specific accounts.
