# What to do if… your passport is held by an embassy or consulate and you have urgent travel plans (USA)

## Short answer
Find out exactly who has your passport, then ask that office for urgent return instructions. At the same time, check the fastest official U.S. passport route that fits your situation, because waiting on only one process can leave you with no usable travel document.

## Do not do these things
- Do not assume the passport can be pulled from processing instantly; some offices may require a formal withdrawal or cancellation.
- Do not rely on a photo or scan of your passport for international travel unless the airline, carrier, and destination authorities have confirmed it will be accepted.
- Do not pay anyone for a passport agency appointment or “special access”; U.S. passport agency appointments are not sold through third parties.
- Do not make tight, non-refundable travel changes until you know what document you will have in hand.
- Do not report a passport lost or stolen unless it is actually lost or stolen; once reported, it may be cancelled and no longer valid for travel.

## What to do now
1. **Identify who has the passport.**  
   Write down which of these applies:
   - a foreign embassy or consulate has it for visa processing or stamping
   - it is already inside a U.S. passport application or renewal process
   - you are outside the United States and do not have your U.S. passport available

2. **If a foreign embassy or consulate has it for a visa, ask for urgent passport return.**  
   Use that embassy or consulate’s official contact route. Include your name, passport number if you have it, application or reference number, travel date, and a clear request for “urgent passport return.” Ask whether return is possible without cancelling the visa process, whether withdrawal is required, and whether in-person pickup is possible.

3. **If you already applied for a U.S. passport, contact U.S. passport services through the urgent travel route.**  
   If you are traveling abroad in 14 days or less, call the National Passport Information Center. You must have an appointment to go to a passport agency or center, and an appointment is not guaranteed.

4. **If you have not applied for a U.S. passport and your trip is soon, check whether you qualify for a passport agency appointment.**  
   U.S. passport agencies and centers handle urgent international travel in the next 14 days, or a foreign visa needed in the next 28 days. Do not pay a third party to book the appointment.

5. **If the travel is because an immediate family member abroad has died, is dying, is in hospice care, or has a life-threatening illness or injury, check the life-or-death emergency route.**  
   Keep one piece of evidence ready, such as a death certificate, mortuary statement, or hospital letter. If the document is not in English, a professional translation may be needed.

6. **If you are outside the United States without your U.S. passport, contact the nearest U.S. embassy or consulate.**  
   Tell them your travel date and that you need passport help. If there is not enough time for a regular passport, consular staff may be able to issue a limited-validity emergency passport, but some destinations may not accept it.

7. **Make the trip reversible while you wait for a definite answer.**  
   Contact the airline or carrier and ask what change, refund, or hold options exist if the passport is not returned in time. Check destination entry rules before relying on any emergency or limited-validity passport.

## What can wait
You do not need to decide today whether to reapply for the visa, complain, pursue refunds, or redesign future travel plans. First get a clear answer on whether you can have a valid passport or emergency travel document in hand before the trip.

## Important reassurance
This is stressful because your passport is inside a process you do not fully control. The useful move is to narrow it to two tracks: get a clear answer from the current holder, and check the fastest official U.S. backup route that applies to you.

## Scope note
These are first steps only. Later decisions about cancelling travel, withdrawing a visa application, replacing a limited-validity passport, or dealing with costs may need specialist help from the relevant embassy, consulate, carrier, or travel professional.

## Important note
This is general information, not legal, immigration, financial, or professional travel advice. Rules, appointment availability, visa procedures, and entry requirements can change quickly, so use official U.S. government guidance and the instructions from the office currently holding your passport.
