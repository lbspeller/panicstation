# What to do if… your wheelchair tyre goes flat or a wheel locks up while you are away from home (USA)

## Short answer
Get out of vehicle danger if you can do so without forcing the chair. If you are stuck in traffic, a parking-lot lane, or another place where you may be hit or fall, call 911 and say you are a wheelchair user stranded and need help getting to safety.

## Do not do these things
- Do not keep forcing the chair to move on a flat tire, dragging wheel, jammed caster, or locked wheel.
- Do not attempt a repair in the street, a parking-lot lane, a curb cut, or anywhere drivers may not see you.
- Do not let anyone lift you or the chair unless there is immediate danger or you have clearly agreed to a safe transfer plan.
- Do not switch a power chair into freewheel or manual release mode unless you know your model, you are on level ground, and someone can steady the chair.
- Do not put your fingers, loose clothing, bags, or straps near spokes, forks, axles, belts, or pinch points while a wheel may move.
- Do not rush to remove wheels, tubes, batteries, covers, or panels while you are stranded.

## What to do now
1. **Make the place safer first.**
   - Move only as far as you safely can: inside a building, onto a wide sidewalk, into a lobby, to a bus stop pad, or to the edge of a parking lot away from moving vehicles.
   - If moving the chair makes you unstable, stop and ask someone nearby to stand where they are visible while you call for help.

2. **Tell someone nearby exactly what help you need.**
   - Say: “My wheelchair has broken down. Please help me get to a safer waiting spot and stay nearby while I call for assistance.”
   - Ask building staff, security, transit staff, store staff, campus staff, or a bystander to help keep space around you clear.

3. **Stabilize yourself before checking the chair.**
   - Set wheel locks or brakes only if they can be used safely.
   - Keep your body centered and avoid leaning down between the wheels if the chair feels unstable.
   - If you have pain, numbness, dizziness, a fall, or a near fall, pause troubleshooting and ask for medical help; call 911 if symptoms feel urgent or you may be injured.

4. **Check only what you can see without tools.**
   - For a flat tire, look for a soft, squashed, or rim-low tire.
   - For a locked or jammed wheel, look for debris caught in the caster fork, between the tire and frame, around the axle, or against the brake.
   - For a power chair, note whether it will not drive, one side drags, or there are error lights, beeps, or a controller message.

5. **Try only low-risk fixes from a stable position.**
   - If debris is loose and reachable without putting fingers into pinch points, remove it slowly.
   - If a wheel lock or brake is partly engaged and you can release it safely, do that once and test gently.
   - If a power chair may be in freewheel or manual release mode and you already know the control for your model, return it to drive mode on level ground. If you are unsure, stop and get help.

6. **Use transit or venue staff if you are in their system.**
   - On a bus, train, paratransit vehicle, platform, or station, ask the operator or station staff to contact dispatch or a supervisor.
   - Ask for the ramp or lift, securement help, and enough time to board, exit, or wait safely.
   - If you cannot safely continue the trip, ask staff to help you wait in the safest available accessible place while you arrange pickup or repair.

7. **Arrange transport or recovery.**
   - Call your wheelchair supplier, repair provider, manufacturer support line, seating clinic, or usual durable medical equipment provider and ask for urgent guidance.
   - If you need to leave before repair, call an accessible taxi or ride service where available, a trusted person with a suitable vehicle, or paratransit if you are already eligible and it is available soon.
   - If the chair cannot roll safely, ask for help moving only the shortest distance needed to reach a safer waiting place.

8. **Use a repair kit only if the setting is safe.**
   - If you are safely indoors, you know the tire is pneumatic, and you know the correct pressure from the tire sidewall or manual, you may inflate it within that stated range.
   - If the tire is solid or foam-filled, do not inflate it.
   - If you do not normally remove wheels, tubes, batteries, or panels confidently, leave that for a repair person or someone trained to help.

## What can wait
- You do not need to fully diagnose whether the cause is a tube puncture, valve issue, bearing problem, axle problem, controller fault, or brake alignment issue right now.
- You do not need to choose replacement parts, make warranty decisions, or sort out insurance while you are stranded.
- You do not need to keep your original schedule; the goal for now is safety, a calm waiting place, and a way home or onward.

## Important reassurance
A wheelchair failure away from home can feel exposing and urgent. Treating it like a mobility breakdown, not a personal failure, helps you avoid falls, avoid further damage, and get practical help sooner.

## Scope note
These are first steps for the next minutes to a couple of hours. Later decisions about repair, parts, maintenance, funding, complaints, or replacement may need help from a wheelchair repair professional, your usual provider, a transit agency, or another specialist.

## Important note
This is general information, not medical, legal, financial, therapeutic, or professional repair advice. If you are in immediate danger, call 911. If moving the chair could cause a fall or injury, treat it as unsafe and get assistance.
